Summary
This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T, rather than maintaining C and recomputing its square root. The main algorithmic change is to replace the CSA term C^{-1/2}(m_{t+1}−m_t)/σ_t by A^{-1}(m_{t+1}−m_t)/σ_t in step-size adaptation, enabling quadratic-time O(µ d^2) rank-one updates directly on A while avoiding cubic-time decompositions. The authors justify the approximation by analyzing the RQ-decomposition C^{1/2} = A E, showing that the sequence of rotations E_t stabilizes when the (shape-normalized) covariance converges, hence the omission of E_t has vanishing effect on the CSA norm in the limit. Empirically, the proposed method matches standard CMA-ES in number of function evaluations while achieving substantial wall-clock speedups (e.g., ~20× at d=64), and it reduces memory by storing only a triangular factor. The paper compares against a reference CMA-ES with delayed decompositions, a variant with d-step delays, and a prior non-triangular Cholesky approach.

Strengths
- Practical impact: Reduces both time (to quadratic) and memory while preserving solution quality (same evaluations) on standard benchmarks across dimensions up to 256. This is directly useful to the many users of CMA-ES in ML and beyond.
- Clear algorithmic contribution: A concrete, numerically stable rank-one update for the triangular Cholesky factor for both rank-1 and rank-µ covariance updates; efficient inversion of A when needed.
- Theoretical justification: A clean argument via convergence of rotations in the RQ decomposition to support replacing C^{-1/2} by A^{-1} in CSA, clarifying when the approximation should be accurate (small, slowly varying rotations).
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation: Rotated and unrotated testbeds, multiple CMA-ES baselines, and careful wall-clock timing using a standard implementation (Shark/LAPACK).

Weaknesses and questions
- Correctness issue: The paper states that “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix” and concludes that “we get the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free.” This is not correct in general. For C = L L^T, the diagonal entries of L are not the square roots of the eigenvalues of C (they are related to pivoted variances/leading principal minors), and eigenvalues of C are the squares of the singular values of L. Please correct this claim; what you do get “for free” includes log det(C) = 2∑ log diag(L), access to triangular solves/inversion, and inexpensive conditioning proxies, but not exact eigenvalues.
- Theory scope: The lemma assumes convergence of the shape-normalized covariance sequence. While this is standard in simplified models, it leaves a gap to full CMA-ES. A bound on the CSA norm error as a function of recent rotation drift ‖E_t^T E_{t−1} − I‖ (or an empirical assessment across runs) would strengthen the justification. The supplementary material hints at measuring rotations; summarizing these results in the main text would help.
- Invariance: CMA-ES’s near-invariance to affine transformations is a key property. The modified CSA drops the rotation term E_t and therefore is not strictly equivalent. The experiments indicate similar function evaluations, including on rotated problems. It would be valuable to (i) report success rates and evaluation counts under several random rotations, and (ii) quantify any systematic bias in step-size dynamics (e.g., distribution of ‖p_σ‖/E{χ} across iterations).
- Experimental reporting: For Rosenbrock, runs that did not converge are excluded; please report success rates and, ideally, time-to-target conditionally and unconditionally to avoid bias. Clarify whether function evaluation time is negligible (it appears so for these benchmarks). Consider adding a more aggressive delayed-decomposition baseline to ensure fairness for larger d, as practitioners sometimes delay more than default.
- Numerical stability: The rank-one update (Algorithm 2) is central. Please discuss stability (e.g., references to stable Cholesky rank-one update routines), accumulation of roundoff over many updates, and any safeguards (e.g., periodic re-factorization) used in practice. Also state explicitly that all β in updates are non-negative in your use, hence no downdates.
- Reproducibility: You note integration into Shark. Please provide a link or commit hash, and list exact parameter settings and hardware/BLAS details used for timing.

Quality
- The algorithmic development is sound, with the caveat above regarding eigenvalues and the limited scope of the convergence argument. Empirical results convincingly demonstrate the claimed speedups without degrading evaluation counts.
- The paper is careful to position the work relative to delay strategies, restricted models (diagonal, limited-memory), and prior Cholesky-based updates.

Clarity
- Overall clearly written, with helpful algorithms. Consider tightening the theoretical section by moving assumptions upfront, clarifying the role of E_t, and bringing key empirical diagnostics (rotation drift, step-size statistics) into the main text. Please correct the eigenvalue claim.

Originality
- Extends rank-one triangular Cholesky updates to the full CMA-ES with rank-µ and provides a principled modification of CSA. The idea of replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} is simple but nontrivial due to rotation; the convergence argument is a useful contribution. This appears distinct from Suttorp et al. and related variants.

Significance
- High practical significance for users of full-covariance CMA-ES in ML (policy search, hyperparameter optimization) and black-box optimization, particularly for moderate-to-large d where decomposition costs are material. The method is likely to be adopted in libraries and practice.

Questions for the authors
- Can you report quantitative bounds or empirical statistics on the CSA norm error introduced by omitting E_t (e.g., distribution of ‖p_σ‖/E{χ} compared to the reference)?
- Do you ever re-factorize C to A from scratch to control numerical drift? If so, how frequently?
- How sensitive are results to µ (e.g., µ/d or µ ~ log d)? Any adverse interactions with larger µ?
- Can you provide success rates on Rosenbrock and other multi-modal functions, including rotated versions?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong practical contribution with significant speed and memory benefits.
- Simple, drop-in algorithmic change compatible with existing CMA-ES implementations.
- Reasonable theoretical motivation and thorough empirical support.

Cons for acceptance
- Incorrect claim about eigenvalues; needs correction.
- Theoretical justification of CSA approximation remains heuristic; tighter analysis or broader empirical diagnostics would strengthen the paper.
- Some experimental reporting choices (excluding failures) should be supplemented with success rates.

Overall, this is a useful and timely contribution to CMA-ES methodology with clear benefits and generally solid support. Pending correction of the eigenvalue claim and minor clarifications, I lean in favor of acceptance.
