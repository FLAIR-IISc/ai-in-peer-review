This paper presents a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) algorithm, dubbed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aiming to improve computational efficiency by updating and storing the covariance matrix using its Cholesky factorization instead of directly working with the covariance matrix itself. The core idea is to leverage the quadratic time complexity of Cholesky updates, while addressing potential issues arising from replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor in the step-size adaptation mechanism. The authors provide theoretical justification for the modification and empirical results demonstrating improved runtime, especially in higher dimensions.

The paper is well-motivated. CMA-ES is a powerful but computationally expensive algorithm, and reducing the cost of covariance matrix updates is a significant concern, especially as dimensionality increases. The paper clearly articulates the problem and positions the proposed solution within the existing landscape of approaches to address it, referencing relevant work like restricting model complexity (Poland & Zell, 2001), updating Cholesky factors directly (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), and delayed updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001). The explanation of the theoretical justification, relating to the convergence of the rotation matrix (Lemma 1), is a notable strength and sets this work apart from previous attempts.

The strengths of the paper are several. First, the proposed approach offers a theoretically sound and potentially significant improvement in computational efficiency. The analysis regarding the convergence of the coordinate system rotation is compelling and provides confidence in the validity of the approximation. Second, the empirical evaluation is thorough, covering a range of benchmark functions and dimensions. The comparison with CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, and Suttorp-CMA-ES provides a good baseline for assessing the performance gains. Third, the authors clearly explain the algorithm and its complexity, making it accessible to readers familiar with CMA-ES. Finally, the code availability through the Shark library is a valuable contribution.

However, there are also some weaknesses. The discussion of the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor, while theoretically justified, could benefit from more detailed empirical investigation. Specifically, quantifying the impact of this approximation on the algorithm's convergence behavior in different scenarios would strengthen the claims. While the experiments show comparable performance in terms of function evaluations, a more in-depth analysis of the solution quality or robustness could be beneficial. The experiments primarily focus on standard benchmark functions. Evaluating the algorithm on more challenging, real-world problems relevant to the applications mentioned in the introduction (reinforcement learning, hyperparameter tuning) would enhance the impact of the work. The discussion on the impact of the cache size (d=256) is briefly mentioned but could be explored further. It's unclear if this is a fundamental limitation or something that can be mitigated with different implementation strategies. Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge that the complexity remains quadratic with dimensionality, a brief discussion of potential approaches to address scalability for extremely high-dimensional problems would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant practical challenge in CMA-ES—the computational cost of covariance matrix updates.
*   The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm offers a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a substantial improvement in runtime, particularly in higher dimensions.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly explains the algorithm, and provides a thorough comparison with existing methods.
*   The availability of the implementation through the Shark library is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The analysis of the error introduced by the approximation could be more detailed.
*   The evaluation could be expanded to include more challenging, real-world problems.
*   The discussion of scalability to extremely high-dimensional problems is limited.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm is a valuable improvement to a widely used method, and the theoretical justification and empirical results are convincing. While some further investigation into the error analysis and scalability would be beneficial, the paper is of high quality and deserves to be accepted.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
