**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) to reduce its computational complexity. The standard CMA-ES algorithm's runtime is dominated by the O(d^3) cost of decomposing the d x d covariance matrix in each iteration, which is necessary for sampling and for the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. The proposed algorithm, named Cholesky-CMA-ES, avoids forming the covariance matrix explicitly. Instead, it maintains and directly updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the matrix. This reduces the time complexity of the update to O(µd^2) and the storage complexity to O(d^2), which are both asymptotically optimal for a full covariance matrix.

The central challenge addressed is the CSA path update, which requires the inverse of the matrix square root (C^{-1/2}). The proposed approach approximates this term using the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor (A^{-1}). The paper provides a theoretical justification for this approximation, arguing that the error introduced is related to a rotation matrix that changes slowly and converges to the identity as the algorithm's covariance matrix shape converges. This ensures that the approximation does not significantly alter the algorithm's behavior. The work is positioned relative to prior attempts that either delayed updates, restricted the covariance matrix structure, or used non-triangular Cholesky factors that lacked the stability and uniqueness properties of the proposed method. Empirical results on a suite of standard benchmark functions demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a significant wall-clock time speedup over the standard implementation without increasing the number of function evaluations required for convergence.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed modification is well-motivated and the algorithmic details are clearly presented. The theoretical justification for the core approximation in the CSA update (Lemma 1 and the surrounding discussion) is sound and provides a strong argument for why the performance of the algorithm is not expected to degrade. This theoretical reasoning is convincingly supported by a thorough empirical evaluation. The experiments are well-designed, using standard benchmark functions, appropriate baseline algorithms (including the state-of-the-art reference implementation), and clear metrics. The results robustly demonstrate the two key claims: the number of function evaluations remains unchanged, while the runtime is substantially reduced, especially in higher dimensions.

**Clarity**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively frames the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The background section provides the necessary context on CMA-ES, and Section 3 clearly details the proposed method and its justification. The experimental setup and results are presented in a way that is easy to follow and interpret. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 aids in understanding the implementation details. The paper provides sufficient information for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

**Originality**

The work presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of evolutionary computation and derivative-free optimization. While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor to reduce complexity has been explored before for variants of CMA-ES (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), this paper is the first to successfully and rigorously adapt this concept to the standard, state-of-the-art CMA-ES. The key original contribution lies in the analysis of the approximation needed for the CSA mechanism and the theoretical argument justifying why using a unique triangular Cholesky factor is crucial and effective. This solves a long-standing challenge in making the standard CMA-ES algorithm more efficient.

**Significance**

The significance of this work is high. CMA-ES is a benchmark algorithm for black-box optimization and is widely used in various machine learning applications, such as reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning. Its cubic time complexity has been a major barrier to its application in higher-dimensional problems. By reducing the complexity to quadratic time without sacrificing performance, this work makes CMA-ES practical for a broader range of problems. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is presented as a drop-in replacement for the standard algorithm, and the strong empirical evidence supports this claim. It is highly likely that researchers and practitioners will adopt this more efficient version, and it could become the new standard implementation.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is strong, but a few points could be clarified or corrected to improve it further.

*   **Essential Correction:** The paper claims at the end of Section 3 that "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix". This statement is incorrect for general symmetric positive-definite matrices; it is only true for diagonal matrices. The eigenvalues of the covariance matrix C are the squares of the singular values of its Cholesky factor A, which are not directly available from the diagonal elements of A. This point is used to claim that eigenvalues are obtained "for free", which seems not to be the case. Correcting this statement is essential for the technical accuracy of the paper. The other advantages mentioned in that paragraph (optimal storage, efficient inversion) are valid and sufficient to motivate the approach.
*   **Minor Clarification:** The theoretical argument hinges on the convergence of the covariance matrix shape. It would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss the implications of this assumption or to include a note in the main text about the empirical behavior of the rotation matrices E_t (which is mentioned as being in the supplementary material). This would give the reader more confidence in the robustness of the approximation in practice.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that presents a valuable and practical improvement to a cornerstone optimization algorithm. The contribution is well-supported by both theory and experiments. The work is significant and is a strong candidate for acceptance, provided the technical inaccuracy regarding the eigenvalues is addressed.
