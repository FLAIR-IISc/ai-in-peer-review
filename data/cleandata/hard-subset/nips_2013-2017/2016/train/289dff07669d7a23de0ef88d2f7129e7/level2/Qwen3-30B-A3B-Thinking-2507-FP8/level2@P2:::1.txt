

This paper proposes a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called Cholesky-CMA-ES that replaces the matrix square root computation with a triangular Cholesky factorization, aiming to reduce time and space complexity while maintaining performance. The authors claim to achieve O(µd²) time complexity for covariance matrix updates (compared to O(d³) for standard CMA-ES) with minimal impact on optimization performance.

The paper's main strength is its clear theoretical justification for the proposed modification. Lemma 1 establishes that the error introduced by replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ (where Aₜ is the Cholesky factor) diminishes as the algorithm converges, providing a solid foundation for the approach. The authors properly contextualize their work by discussing prior attempts to use Cholesky factors in CMA-ES variants (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) and explaining why their approach differs by providing theoretical guarantees. The empirical results demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as standard CMA-ES but achieves significant runtime reductions (up to 20x for d=64), with the speedup increasing with dimensionality.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with helpful pseudocode for the proposed algorithm. The authors make a strong case for the practical benefits of their approach, particularly for high-dimensional problems where covariance matrix updates dominate runtime. The experimental design is sound, using standard benchmark functions across multiple dimensions and evaluating both function evaluations and wall-clock time.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the theoretical justification (Lemma 1) assumes Ct converges to a certain shape, but as the authors acknowledge, CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm where Ct does not necessarily converge. The paper provides no analysis of how the error behaves during the optimization process, especially in the early stages when Ct changes rapidly. The claim that "the error introduced by replacing st by ŝt is small" needs stronger empirical or theoretical support.

Second, the paper doesn't clearly define what "optimal" means in the context of their complexity claims. While O(µd²) is better than O(d³), it's not clear if this represents the theoretical minimum complexity for full covariance adaptation. The authors should clarify this and discuss whether their approach is optimal in the information-theoretic sense.

Third, the numerical stability claim is unsubstantiated. The paper states that their approach allows "numerically stable computation" but provides no evidence or analysis to support this claim. Given that Cholesky decomposition can be numerically unstable for ill-conditioned matrices, this is a significant omission.

Fourth, the experimental comparison is limited. The paper compares to CMA-ES-Ref (with delayed decomposition), CMA-ES/d (delayed update every d steps), and Suttorp-CMA-ES, but doesn't compare to other state-of-the-art approaches like LM-CMA (Loshchilov, 2015) or the more recent CMA-ES variants that use low-rank approximations. The paper mentions these approaches but doesn't include them in the empirical comparison.

The paper also has minor issues with presentation. For instance, the reference to "Figure 3 plots the trials with runtimes closest to the corresponding median runtimes for d = 128" is confusing since the paper only includes Figure 1-2. The supplementary material reference is vague, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient detail on how the rotation matrix Et was measured.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that addresses a genuine practical problem in CMA-ES implementation. The theoretical justification is a significant improvement over prior work, and the empirical results are convincing for the specific use case of high-dimensional optimization. However, the theoretical justification could be strengthened, and the numerical stability claim needs proper support. The paper would be significantly improved by including comparisons to other recent CMA-ES variants and providing more detailed analysis of the error introduced by the approximation.

The paper meets the quality criteria with sound technical content and proper experimental validation. It's clearly written and well-organized. The originality is moderate - the approach builds on previous work but provides the crucial theoretical justification that was missing. The significance is high, as efficient CMA-ES implementations are valuable for real-world applications, especially in machine learning where high-dimensional optimization is common.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical justification, numerical stability claim, and experimental comparison limitations. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and has the potential to improve practical implementations of CMA-ES.
