

This paper proposes a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, introducing Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the matrix square root computation with a triangular Cholesky factor in the step-size adaptation mechanism. The authors claim this modification achieves optimal time complexity O(µd²) for covariance updates while maintaining comparable optimization performance to the standard CMA-ES.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution that addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES. The theoretical justification provided in Lemma 1 is particularly strong, showing that the error introduced by using the Cholesky factor instead of the matrix square root vanishes as the algorithm converges. The authors effectively demonstrate through experiments that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but achieves significant runtime improvements (up to 20x faster for d=64), with the speedup increasing with dimensionality.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a thorough background on CMA-ES and the necessary mathematical foundations. The implementation details are precise, and the authors have made their algorithm available in the Shark library. The experimental setup is comprehensive, including multiple benchmark functions with varying dimensionality, rotation invariance tests, and comparisons with relevant baselines (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, and Suttorp-CMA-ES).

The main strengths of the paper are:
1. The theoretical justification for the Cholesky-based modification, which is more rigorous than previous attempts
2. The clear demonstration of the asymptotic time complexity improvement
3. The practical implementation with real-world performance gains
4. The careful comparison with existing approaches

The paper has a few areas for improvement:
1. While the authors show that the number of function evaluations remains the same, it would be helpful to provide more detailed analysis of the optimization trajectories to better understand how the modification affects the convergence path, particularly for non-spherical functions.
2. The theoretical analysis assumes convergence of Ct, but CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm. It would strengthen the argument to provide empirical evidence of how often the error remains small during the optimization process before convergence.
3. The paper mentions that for very large dimensions (d=256), cache effects impacted performance, but it doesn't discuss how this might affect the practical utility of the method in real-world applications with limited memory resources.
4. The comparison with Loshchilov's (2015) low-dimensional approximation methods could be more extensive, as these are commonly used when full covariance updates become infeasible.

The significance of this work is substantial for the optimization community. CMA-ES is widely used in machine learning for hyperparameter tuning and reinforcement learning, and this modification makes it more scalable to larger problems without sacrificing performance. The authors successfully address a practical limitation of an important algorithm, which could enable its application to new domains where computational resources are constrained.

The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and significance. It's a well-executed technical contribution that advances the state of the art in evolutionary optimization. The method is novel enough to be of interest to the community, and the theoretical and empirical results are compelling.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the points about convergence analysis during the optimization process and provide a more detailed discussion of the practical limitations when memory constraints become severe. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of how this method might integrate with other CMA-ES variants or extensions that have been proposed in recent years.
