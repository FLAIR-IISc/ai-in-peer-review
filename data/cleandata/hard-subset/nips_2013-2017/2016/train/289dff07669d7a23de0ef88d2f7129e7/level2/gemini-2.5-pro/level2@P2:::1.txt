This paper addresses the primary computational bottleneck of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a widely used and powerful algorithm for derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a time complexity of O(d^3) per iteration due to the need to compute an eigenvalue decomposition of the d-dimensional covariance matrix `C`. This computation is required for sampling and, critically, for the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which relies on the inverse matrix square root `C^(-1/2)`.

The authors propose a "Cholesky-CMA-ES" that reduces the complexity of the update to O(µd^2), which is asymptotically optimal for a full covariance matrix update. The core idea is to maintain and directly update a triangular Cholesky factor `A` of the covariance matrix (`C = AA^T`) using efficient rank-1 updates, thus avoiding the expensive re-decomposition of `C`. While similar ideas have been explored previously, this work provides a novel and principled solution to the main challenge: adapting the CSA mechanism. The authors propose approximating the required `C^(-1/2)` term with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor, `A^(-1)`. They provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) that this approximation is valid. The reasoning is that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the orthogonal transformation `E` relating the matrix square root to the Cholesky factor (`C^(1/2) = AE`) also converges. Because the CSA path is an exponentially weighted average of past steps, the error introduced by ignoring this rotation diminishes over time. The paper validates this approach empirically, showing that the proposed method requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES while being significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known and important limitation of a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization. CMA-ES is frequently used in machine learning for tasks like policy search and hyperparameter tuning. A method that dramatically reduces its computational cost without degrading its performance is a major practical contribution that is highly likely to be adopted by practitioners and researchers. The proposed algorithm makes CMA-ES with a full covariance matrix feasible for significantly higher dimensions.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The key contribution is the well-justified approximation for the CSA update. Previous attempts to use Cholesky factor updates struggled with this step. The paper's insight, formalized in Lemma 1, that the rotational component of the decomposition stabilizes as the covariance matrix shape converges, is novel and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the approximation. This elevates the work from a simple heuristic to a principled algorithmic modification. The overall approach is technically sound.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It clearly introduces the problem, explains the relevant details of the standard CMA-ES, presents the proposed modification and its justification, and follows with a clean experimental section. The logical flow makes the contribution easy to understand and appreciate.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental comparison is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against the standard reference implementation and another accelerated variant on a suite of standard benchmark functions across a range of dimensions. The results in Figures 1 and 2 clearly support the main claim: the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a substantial speedup in wall-clock time (e.g., 20x at d=64) while exhibiting virtually identical convergence behavior in terms of function evaluations.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarification on Eigenvalues:** In the "Properties of the update rule" section and the Conclusion, the paper claims that the proposed method provides the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free. This is incorrect. The diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor `A` are not the eigenvalues of the matrix `C = AA^T`. Computing the eigenvalues of `C` still requires an O(d^3) operation. This claim should be corrected. The authors could instead state that the diagonal elements of `A` provide information about the variances along the coordinate axes, which can be useful for diagnostics, but this is distinct from the principal components defined by the eigenvectors.

2.  **Convergence Assumption in Lemma 1:** The proof of Lemma 1 relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix `C̄t` converges. The authors correctly note that for a stochastic algorithm this convergence is not guaranteed. While the argument that the error is small as long as `C` changes slowly is still valid and persuasive, it would be beneficial to slightly expand on this point. Perhaps citing literature on the convergence of the expected value of `C` or discussing the behavior when the algorithm has converged to a stationary distribution would further strengthen the theoretical argument.

3.  **Details of the Proof:** The proof of Lemma 1 is very concise. It states that the RQ-decomposition is continuous. While this is true, adding a reference for this property would make the proof more self-contained and rigorous.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant practical and theoretical contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. It solves a long-standing problem of computational complexity in CMA-ES with an elegant, well-justified, and empirically verified solution. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are easily correctable. The work is of high quality, originality, and significance, and it is highly relevant to the NIPS community, particularly for applications in reinforcement learning and automated machine learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
