Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T, rather than C itself or its symmetric square root. This yields quadratic time and storage complexity per iteration (O(µ d^2), where µ is the number of selected elites), while the standard implementation incurs cubic cost due to repeated eigendecompositions or Cholesky decompositions. The main technical difficulty is that the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) in CMA-ES uses C^{-1/2}, whereas the factorization provides A^{-1}. The authors replace C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} and justify the approximation by showing that the orthogonal factor E in the polar/RQ decomposition of C^{1/2} changes smoothly when C’s shape stabilizes, making the resulting CSA error small and vanishing asymptotically. Empirically, Cholesky-CMA-ES matches the function-evaluation performance of the reference CMA-ES while substantially reducing wall-clock time, and outperforms a prior “non-triangular” Cholesky update approach [Suttorp et al., 2009]. The work builds on and extends rank-one Cholesky updates [Krause & Igel, 2015] to the general CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and standard settings, and is positioned among alternatives such as delayed decomposition, diagonal/low-rank simplifications, and limited-memory CMA variants.

Strengths:
- Addresses a real and important bottleneck in CMA-ES: per-iteration matrix decomposition, which often dominates runtime for moderate-to-large d when objective evaluations are cheap.
- Clean algorithmic idea: maintain a triangular factor and implement the rank-1 and rank-µ covariance updates via efficient, numerically stable rank-one updates on A. Complexity and memory usage are near-optimal for full-covariance schemes.
- Thoughtful treatment of CSA: identifies the mismatch between C^{-1/2} and A^{-1}, provides a rationale (via an asymptotic rotation argument) for why the approximation should not harm performance, and supports it empirically.
- Solid empirical evidence on standard benchmarks across a range of dimensions, including rotation tests. The method achieves large speedups in wall-clock time without increasing the number of function evaluations.
- Practical contributions: computation of A^{-1} in O(d^2) when needed; good software engineering (LAPACK; integration into Shark).

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Theoretical justification is limited. Lemma 1 is a continuity argument contingent on convergence of the (shape-normalized) covariance. It does not quantify the approximation error to CSA during the transient regime, which is often most critical in practice. No bounds on the impact on σ dynamics or convergence rates are provided.
- A significant technical inaccuracy: the paper claims “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general. For C = A A^T, eigenvalues(C) are the squares of the singular values of A, not the squares of diag(A). Only for diagonal A (or orthogonally diagonalizable C with diagonal A) does this hold. The claim that “we get the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free” should be corrected; at best one gets cheap surrogates/diagnostics (e.g., diagonal entries) but not eigenvalues.
- Experimental protocol has a potential bias: on Rosenbrock, runs that did not converge to the global optimum are excluded from analysis. This can inflate perceived efficiency. A fair comparison should report success rates and time-to-target at fixed budgets, or include all runs using performance profiles.
- The comparison set omits some strong baselines for large-scale use cases (e.g., LM-CMA and other limited-memory variants) in wall-clock metrics, even though the authors correctly note that these restrict the model class and may need more evaluations on non-separable functions.
- Clarity: some details are relegated to supplementary material (rotation change metric Et); the description of the rankOneUpdate could benefit from stability remarks and constraints on β (here β ≥ 0, but this is not emphasized). A few typos and formatting issues remain.

Quality:
Technically sound overall for the update mechanism and complexity claims. The CSA approximation argument is plausible but not rigorous; still, experiments support the main claim that evaluation counts are unaffected. The incorrect statement about eigenvalues should be fixed.

Clarity:
Generally well written and organized. The algorithmic steps are clear, and the motivation is understandable. Some statements (eigenvalues from diag(A)) are misleading and should be corrected; experimental reporting on failures should be clarified.

Originality:
Extends prior Cholesky-update work to standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and provides a coherent CSA workaround. The combination and its practical performance gains feel novel and useful.

Significance:
High practical impact for users of CMA-ES in ML and reinforcement learning when evaluations are cheap/moderate-cost and d is moderate-to-large. Likely to be adopted in libraries. Theoretical impact is modest but sufficient for a NeurIPS application/algorithms paper.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Correct the eigenvalue claim. If you intend to monitor conditioning, clarify what can be obtained from A cheaply (e.g., diag(A) as rough scale indicators) and what cannot without extra computation. If principal component variances are needed, suggest occasional eigendecompositions or incremental SVD techniques.
- Strengthen the CSA analysis by quantifying the deviation introduced by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}, e.g., a bound on |‖pσ‖approx − ‖pσ‖true| in terms of the rotation drift ‖Et E^T_{t-1} − I‖ and the memory factor (1 − cσ). Even a worst-case inequality would improve confidence.
- Report success rates and include failed runs in statistics (e.g., ECDFs/performance profiles) to avoid bias, especially for Rosenbrock. Consider fixed-budget and fixed-target metrics.
- Add comparisons with LM-CMA and other scalable variants in terms of wall-clock time and evaluations on both separable and non-separable functions, to better scope when full-covariance Cholesky-CMA-ES is preferable.
- Discuss numerical stability of Algorithm 2 explicitly: constraints on β, conditioning issues, and any regularization (e.g., jitter) used in practice.
- Provide code pointers (commit hashes, parameters) to improve reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance:
- Significant practical speedups with no loss in evaluation efficiency on standard benchmarks.
- Simple, well-motivated algorithm likely to see immediate use.
- Extends an important line of work (CMA-ES acceleration) with optimal storage/time for full covariance.
- Empirical results are convincing across dimensions.

Cons for acceptance:
- Theoretical justification for the CSA approximation is qualitative and asymptotic; no finite-time guarantees.
- Misstatement regarding eigenvalues of C from the Cholesky factor.
- Experimental protocol excludes failed runs in at least one benchmark; broader baselines could be included.

Recommendation: Leaning accept, contingent on correcting the eigenvalue claim and clarifying experimental reporting; the practical contribution and speedups are compelling.
