Summary
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix to achieve quadratic-time and memory-optimal updates while preserving the search behavior in terms of objective function evaluations. The key change is to approximate the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}, where C = A A^T is the Cholesky factorization. This avoids eigen/square-root decompositions and enables O(µ d^2) per-iteration updates (with µ = O(log d) in default CMA-ES). A theoretical argument (Lemma 1) shows that the rotation between A^{-1} and C^{-1/2} vanishes as the normalized covariance converges, implying the CSA approximation error should diminish over time. Experiments on standard rotated benchmark functions compare Cholesky-CMA-ES with a reference CMA-ES (with delayed decompositions), a variant with fixed delay d, and a non-triangular Cholesky update (Suttorp et al., 2009). Results indicate essentially identical numbers of function evaluations for Cholesky-CMA-ES and the reference, but substantially reduced wall-clock time, with speedups increasing with dimension.

Relation to prior work
The work is grounded in the extensive CMA-ES literature (Hansen and Ostermeier, 1996/2001; Hansen, 2015). It builds on Cholesky-based updates for specific CMA-ES variants ((1+1)-CMA-ES; multi-objective) by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), extending them to the standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates. It is contrasted with complexity-reduction strategies such as infrequent matrix updates (Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001), structure-restricted models (diagonal/separable or limited-memory; Ros and Hansen, 2008; Loshchilov, 2014/2015; Sun et al., 2013; Akimoto et al., 2014; Poland and Zell, 2001), and sample reuse. The contribution targets the regime where full covariance adaptation is needed (non-separable, ill-conditioned functions), arguing that previous restrictions can harm evaluation efficiency there.

Strengths
- Practical impact: The proposed update yields asymptotically optimal time (O(µ d^2)) and storage (O(d^2)) complexity for full-covariance CMA-ES, translating to large wall-clock speedups without increasing function evaluations.
- Sound algorithmic design: Rank-one Cholesky updates are carefully assembled to implement the standard rank-1 and rank-µ covariance updates, avoiding explicit decompositions. Inversion of a triangular factor is O(d^2), enabling efficient CSA computation with A^{-1}.
- Theoretical motivation: While not a full convergence proof, Lemma 1 provides a clear rationale for why replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} in CSA should not materially affect behavior near stationarity.
- Empirical validation: On rotated benchmarks across d = 4–256 with 100 trials, evaluations-to-target are unchanged relative to the reference CMA-ES, while wall-clock time is substantially reduced (e.g., ~20× at d = 64). The study includes a comparison to a non-triangular Cholesky update and delayed updates.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Algorithms are presented explicitly; implementation uses Shark and LAPACK; default CMA-ES parameters are used; rotation-invariance checks are discussed.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Theoretical guarantees are limited. The central assumption for Lemma 1 (convergence of normalized C_t) is not established for the full algorithm; no quantitative bound on the CSA approximation error or its impact on step-size dynamics is provided. A brief empirical bound or sensitivity analysis of ||p_σ|| differences would strengthen the case.
- Rotation-invariance and CSA: The original CSA is already not fully affine invariant; the modification further departs from the ideal normalization. More direct empirical evidence on invariance (e.g., distribution of EtE_{t-1}^T angles over time; sensitivity of σ dynamics to fast-changing C) would be valuable in the main text (some is deferred to supplementary).
- Experimental scope and reporting: 
  - Excluding non-convergent Rosenbrock runs potentially biases results; reporting success rates and time-to-target conditional/unconditional would be more informative.
  - No tests under noise or constraints; robustness of the CSA approximation in noisy settings is unknown.
  - Comparisons to strong, widely used complexity-reduction baselines (e.g., sep-CMA-ES, LM-CMA) are not included, even if acknowledging different modeling capacity; a brief comparison in regimes where full covariance matters would contextualize practical trade-offs.
  - Only CPU runtimes are reported; memory usage and cache effects are noted but not quantified.
- A technical inaccuracy appears: “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix” is generally false. The diagonal of A contains square roots of the pivots of C under Cholesky, not eigenvalues. If principal-component-wise variance control is needed, an eigendecomposition is still required (or alternative estimates).
- Minor clarity issues: The use of ω(d^2) to describe decomposition cost is unusual; consider stating O(d^3). Some references have formatting artifacts.

Quality
Overall technically sound with a correct and carefully implemented rank-one Cholesky update. The CSA approximation is well motivated but lacks formal guarantees or tight empirical quantification of its impact. Experimental methodology is standard for this area; reporting could be expanded as noted.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized, with helpful algorithms and context. A few statements should be corrected (Cholesky diagonal vs eigenvalues), and some experimental details (success rates, memory/runtime profiles) could be added.

Originality
Extends prior Cholesky-update ideas to the full, standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and provides a novel CSA approximation with a theoretical rationale. The combination and its careful analysis appear new and practically important.

Significance
High practical significance for derivative-free optimization in regimes needing full covariance adaptation. The speedups can enable larger-scale applications without sacrificing evaluation efficiency, relevant to ML tasks such as policy search and hyperparameter optimization.

Suggestions for revision (prioritized)
- Correct the statement about eigenvalues and clarify what information the Cholesky diagonal provides; adjust claims about “getting eigenvalues for free.”
- Provide empirical quantification of the CSA approximation error (e.g., ||p_σ^{C^{-1/2}}|| vs ||p_σ^{A^{-1}}||, σ trajectories) across benchmarks and over iterations; optionally a small bound under a “slowly varying C” assumption.
- Report success rates and include the previously excluded Rosenbrock trials; add noise experiments to assess robustness.
- Include brief comparisons or discussion versus sep-CMA-ES and LM-CMA in dimensions where full covariance is still feasible, to position the method for practitioners.
- Add memory and cache sensitivity measurements; clarify decomposition cost as O(d^3).

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong practical contribution with significant speedups and unchanged evaluation efficiency.
- Simple, elegant modification with a plausible theoretical justification.
- Good fit for NIPS audience interested in scalable black-box optimization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees for the CSA approximation; lack of error bounds.
- One incorrect technical claim and some incomplete experimental reporting.
- Missing comparisons to other fast CMA variants may leave practitioners uncertain about method choice.

Overall assessment
A solid and practically impactful contribution that addresses a well-known bottleneck in CMA-ES with a principled and effective solution. With minor corrections and expanded empirical analysis of the CSA approximation, the paper would be a strong addition to the program. Recommended for acceptance.
