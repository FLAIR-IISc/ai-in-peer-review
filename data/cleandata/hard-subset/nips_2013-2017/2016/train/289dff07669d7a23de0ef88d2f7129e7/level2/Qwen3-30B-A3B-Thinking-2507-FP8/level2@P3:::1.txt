

The paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the matrix square root with a triangular Cholesky factorization to achieve optimal O(µd²) time and storage complexity for covariance updates. This addresses a well-known bottleneck in CMA-ES where computing the matrix square root dominates runtime, particularly for high-dimensional problems. The work builds on prior attempts to use Cholesky factors (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) but provides a theoretically grounded justification for modifying the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. The key insight is that the error introduced by replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ (where Ct = AₜAᵀₜ) vanishes as the algorithm converges, supported by Lemma 1 showing that the orthogonal component Eₜ converges to identity under typical CMA-ES behavior.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results across standard NIPS benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Rosenbrock, etc.) with dimensionality up to 256. Cholesky-CMA-ES matches the standard CMA-ES in function evaluations (Fig. 1) while achieving significant runtime reductions—20× faster than the reference implementation at d=64 (Fig. 2). The implementation in Shark, leveraging LAPACK, validates the theoretical claims. The work also highlights practical advantages: storing only the triangular Cholesky factor (optimal storage), obtaining eigenvalues for conditioning monitoring, and enabling efficient inversion.

Strengths include a clear theoretical foundation for the CSA modification, rigorous empirical validation across multiple functions and dimensions, and practical relevance for scaling CMA-ES to larger problems (e.g., neural network training). The paper properly situates itself against prior work: it acknowledges Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) but clarifies their limitations (e.g., non-triangular factors, restricted to µ=λ=1), and contrasts with diagonal approximations (Ros and Hansen, 2008) that degrade performance on non-separable problems. The significance is high, as CMA-ES is widely used in machine learning (e.g., reinforcement learning policy search), and this work directly enables its application to larger-scale problems where covariance storage was prohibitive.

Weaknesses are minor but worth noting. The empirical evaluation focuses on standard benchmarks; extending to non-convex or highly multimodal functions (e.g., Rastrigin) would further strengthen claims about robustness. While the theoretical justification for the CSA error is sound, the paper could more explicitly discuss how the error magnitude scales with problem ill-conditioning (e.g., Diffpowers function). The runtime comparison excludes L-BFGS or other scalable optimizers, though the paper’s focus on derivative-free methods is justified. The dimensionality limit (d=256) is reasonable given the computational constraints of CMA-ES, but the paper might acknowledge that for d > 1000, low-rank approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) remain necessary.

The quality is excellent: the proof is rigorous, the experiments are thorough (100 trials per dimension), and the code is open-sourced. Originality is clear—the approach is novel in its theoretical justification for the CSA modification within the standard CMA-ES framework. Significance is demonstrable: the speedup is substantial and directly addresses a practical barrier to applying CMA-ES in modern ML. The paper avoids overclaiming, noting that full covariance storage remains infeasible for extremely large d, where low-rank methods are preferable.

In summary, this work makes a significant contribution to the optimization literature by resolving a longstanding complexity bottleneck in CMA-ES with minimal performance trade-offs. It advances the state of the art in derivative-free optimization, provides a practical implementation, and is well-positioned for adoption in machine learning applications. The empirical evidence strongly supports the theoretical claims, and the paper is well-written with clear exposition of the method and results. The minor suggestions for additional experiments are optional; the work is robust as presented. This paper meets all NIPS criteria: high quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
