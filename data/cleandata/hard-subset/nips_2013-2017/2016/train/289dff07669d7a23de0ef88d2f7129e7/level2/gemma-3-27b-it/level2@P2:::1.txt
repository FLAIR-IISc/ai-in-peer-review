This paper presents a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) focusing on a more efficient covariance matrix update and storage. The core idea is to work directly with the Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix instead of the matrix square root, offering potential quadratic time complexity and reduced memory footprint, particularly beneficial for high-dimensional problems. This builds upon prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015) but extends it to the full CMA-ES, offering a theoretical justification for why the approximation doesn’t deteriorate performance.

The paper is well-structured, clearly outlining the problem, related work, the proposed method, and experimental results. The motivation for improving CMA-ES efficiency, especially for large dimensions, is well-established, and the connection to applications in machine learning (RL and hyperparameter tuning) is highlighted. The authors thoroughly explain the theoretical background and present a convincing argument for why using the Cholesky factor shouldn't significantly alter the algorithm's behavior, supported by Lemma 1. The experiments are well-designed, comparing the Cholesky-CMA-ES against standard and modified versions of CMA-ES across various benchmark functions and dimensions.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical justification for the method, particularly the argument relating to the convergence of the Cholesky decomposition and the minimal impact on step-size adaptation, seems solid. The connection to the polar decomposition and the discussion of rotation invariance are thoughtful.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are clear enough for someone familiar with CMA-ES to grasp the core ideas.
* **Originality:** While building on existing work using Cholesky decomposition, extending it to the standard CMA-ES with a theoretical foundation and demonstrably good empirical performance represents a significant contribution. The analysis of the impact on step-size adaptation is novel.
* **Significance:** The demonstrated speedup, particularly in higher dimensions, is substantial. This directly addresses a major bottleneck in CMA-ES, making it more practical for larger-scale problems, especially those arising in machine learning. The reduced memory footprint is also a valuable benefit.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors rigorously evaluate their method against several baselines (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES) using a diverse set of benchmark functions and varying dimensionality.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Detail:** While the experimental setup is well-described, more specific details regarding computational resources (CPU, memory) and implementation details (e.g., LAPACK version) would strengthen the reproducibility of the results.  The discussion of why performance degrades at d=256 due to exceeding the L2 cache is a good start, but a more detailed analysis of the memory usage would be beneficial.
* **Lemma 1 Caveats:**  The assumption of convergence mentioned following Lemma 1 could be elaborated on further. The paper acknowledges it's not always guaranteed, and a more nuanced discussion of the scenarios where the approximation might be less accurate would be helpful.
* **Comparison to other efficient CMA-ES Variants:** While the comparison to Suttorp et al. is good, mentioning and comparing against other recent approaches aimed at reducing CMA-ES complexity (e.g., those relying on low-rank approximations) would position the work more strongly within the broader context of CMA-ES research.  For example, work on subspace-based CMA-ES could be mentioned.
* **Limited Discussion of Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions default parameters are used. A brief discussion on how sensitive the performance is to the CMA-ES control parameters (e.g., `cσ`, `dσ`) in the Cholesky-CMA-ES would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated improvement to a widely used algorithm (CMA-ES).
* The proposed method offers a significant speedup, particularly in high dimensions, without sacrificing performance.
* The theoretical justification is convincing and provides insights into why the approximation works.
* The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates the effectiveness of the Cholesky-CMA-ES.
* The work has the potential to broaden the applicability of CMA-ES to larger-scale problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed experimental configuration could hinder reproducibility.
* The discussion on the limitations of Lemma 1 could be expanded.
* A more comprehensive comparison to other existing efficient CMA-ES variants would strengthen the contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a valuable improvement to the standard CMA-ES, offering a significant performance boost without compromising accuracy. The theoretical justification and thorough experimental evaluation lend strong support to the claims made in the paper.  Despite a few minor weaknesses, I recommend **acceptance** with the suggestion that the authors address the points raised regarding experimental details, the limitations of Lemma 1, and comparison to related work in a revised version.
