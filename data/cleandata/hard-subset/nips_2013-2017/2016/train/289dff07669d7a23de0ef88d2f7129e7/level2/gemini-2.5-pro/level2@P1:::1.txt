This paper presents Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) designed to have optimal time and space complexity for the covariance matrix update. CMA-ES is a leading algorithm for derivative-free real-valued optimization, but its per-iteration time complexity is dominated by the O(d^3) cost of decomposing the d x d covariance matrix C. This work builds upon previous efforts to reduce this cost by directly maintaining and updating a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix (where C = AA^T). This update can be performed in O(µd^2) time. The primary technical challenge, and the paper's main contribution, is adapting the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which relies on the inverse matrix square root C^{-1/2}. The authors propose replacing C^{-1/2} with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor A^{-1}. They provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) suggesting that the error introduced by this approximation vanishes as the shape of the covariance matrix converges during optimization. The paper empirically validates this new algorithm, showing that it requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but is significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, particularly for larger problem dimensions.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithm is clearly described and the motivation for the key approximation is sound. The theoretical argument in Section 3, based on the convergence of the normalized covariance matrix, provides a solid justification for why replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} in the CSA path does not significantly alter the algorithm's behavior. While this relies on an assumption of convergence that may not hold universally, the reasoning is plausible and is strongly supported by the empirical results.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare their Cholesky-CMA-ES against standard implementations on a representative set of benchmark functions that test various properties like conditioning and separability. The results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method achieves a substantial speed-up in wall-clock time without any measurable loss in optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations). This rigorous validation supports the paper's central claims.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The background on CMA-ES is concise but provides the necessary context. The core contribution—the modification to the CSA update and its justification—is explained with excellent clarity. The inclusion of pseudo-code (Algorithm 1 and 2) is helpful for understanding the implementation details. The figures are informative and effectively convey the experimental results, making the performance gains of the Cholesky-CMA-ES immediately apparent.

### Originality

The work is original and makes a novel contribution. While the idea of using Cholesky updates to improve the efficiency of evolution strategies has been explored before (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), this paper is the first to successfully integrate this approach into the standard, state-of-the-art CMA-ES. The key originality lies in addressing the challenge posed by the CSA update path's reliance on the matrix square root. The proposed approximation and its theoretical justification via the convergence of the RQ-decomposition is a novel and important step that enables the quadratic-time update for the complete algorithm, something prior work had not achieved for this specific CMA-ES variant.

### Significance

The contribution is highly significant. CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm for black-box optimization with numerous applications in machine learning, from hyperparameter tuning to direct policy search in reinforcement learning. The high computational cost of the covariance matrix update has been a major barrier to its application in higher dimensions or in settings where function evaluations are cheap. By providing a "drop-in" replacement that is asymptotically faster and empirically proven to be much more efficient without sacrificing performance, this work substantially broadens the applicability of CMA-ES. Researchers and practitioners are very likely to adopt this improved version, making it a valuable and impactful contribution to the field.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Practical Impact:** The paper provides a significant speedup for a widely used, state-of-the-art optimization algorithm, making it more practical for high-dimensional problems.
2.  **Soundness:** The proposed method is well-motivated and supported by both a plausible theoretical argument and strong empirical evidence across multiple benchmark problems.
3.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and presents a complete piece of work from motivation to validation.
4.  **Novelty:** The work presents a novel solution to a long-standing challenge in applying Cholesky updates to the standard CMA-ES algorithm.

### Arguments against Acceptance:

1.  The theoretical justification relies on an assumption of convergence of the normalized covariance matrix. A brief discussion of the algorithm's behavior if this assumption is violated would strengthen the paper.
2.  A minor technical claim that the diagonal of the Cholesky factor provides the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix appears to be incorrect and should be revised for accuracy.

In summary, the strengths of this paper are substantial, and the weaknesses are minor and addressable. The paper presents a valuable, well-supported, and significant contribution to the field of optimization.
