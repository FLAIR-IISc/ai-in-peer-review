## Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes a modification to the standard CMA-ES algorithm, termed “Cholesky-CMA-ES,” aiming to reduce computational time and memory requirements during covariance matrix updates by directly operating on the Cholesky factorization. The core idea is to avoid explicit computation of the matrix square root and instead leverage updates on the triangular Cholesky factor, building on previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015). The authors claim this results in a quadratic-time complexity with minimal memory usage, without sacrificing performance in terms of objective function evaluations.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a known bottleneck in CMA-ES – the covariance matrix update, particularly in high-dimensional spaces. The proposed approach is intuitively sound, and the theoretical justification relating to coordinate system rotations (Lemma 1) is a valuable contribution. The experiments demonstrate a significant speedup in wall-clock time, particularly as dimensionality increases, confirming the practical benefits of the proposed approach. The inclusion of results on standard benchmark functions adds to the credibility of the findings. 

However, several weaknesses need to be addressed. The naming "Cholesky-CMA-ES" is problematic, directly conflicting with the established nomenclature introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009). This must be resolved. Crucially, the paper lacks a direct empirical comparison with *both* Suttorp (2009) and Krause et al. (2015). The authors acknowledge these works, but simply stating the advantage of their method is insufficient; a head-to-head comparison is essential to demonstrate genuine improvement.  The algorithmic contribution feels incremental – essentially looping the rankOneUpdate for all individuals – and this needs to be explicitly stated, or further justification provided for it being more than a trivial modification. 

Furthermore, the claim of "optimality" is overstated. While the method is efficient for full covariance adaptation, it's not optimal for problems where diagonal or low-rank approximations suffice. The reported ~2x speedup over Suttorp’s method, while significant, also needs further contextualization. The assertion that Suttorp’s method is numerically unstable is contentious and contradicts the original paper, requiring strong justification. Finally, the scalability remains quadratic; the method doesn’t fundamentally resolve the large-scale issues of CMA-ES, merely mitigating them for scenarios where full covariance maintenance is still feasible. The plotting of results for the Cigar function needs clarification, as the plateau difference may be an artifact of the plotting method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a key computational bottleneck in CMA-ES.
*   Provides theoretical justification for the proposed method.
*   Demonstrates a significant speedup in practical experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Misleading naming convention.
*   Missing crucial comparisons with Suttorp (2009) and Krause et al. (2015).
*   Incremental algorithmic contribution.
*   Overstated claims of “optimality” and numerical stability.



**Recommendation:** Major Revision. The paper has potential, but requires significant revisions addressing the naming, comparative experiments, and clarification of its contribution before it can be considered for acceptance.
