Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a CMA-ES variant that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = AA^T, rather than explicitly storing and decomposing C. The covariance update corresponding to the standard rank-1 and rank-μ terms (Eq. 2) is implemented as a sequence of rank-one updates on A (Algorithm 2), reducing per-iteration time to O(μ d^2) and storage to O(d^2). For cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA), the paper replaces the usual C^{-1/2} by A^{-1} (Algorithm 1), which avoids computing matrix square roots. A lemma and accompanying discussion argue that the rotation error introduced by this approximation diminishes as the algorithm stabilizes. Experiments on standard benchmarks suggest similar numbers of function evaluations as the reference CMA-ES and substantially faster wall-clock time, increasingly so with dimension.

The idea of updating factorizations instead of recomputing decompositions is well established in the CMA-ES literature. Suttorp et al. (2009) maintain a non-triangular factor; Krause & Igel (2015) derive a numerically efficient rank-one Cholesky update for the (1+1)-CMA-ES and multi-objective variants. The present paper aims to extend such updates to the standard CMA-ES with rank-μ covariance updates, and to justify the CSA modification.

Strengths
- Clear, readable presentation with a practical algorithm that is easy to implement and integrate (code in Shark).
- Sound motivation: avoiding cubic-time eigen/square-root computations is important when function evaluations are cheap and d is large.
- Good empirical evidence on standard benchmarks that function-evaluation counts match the reference CMA-ES, while wall-clock time improves substantially and scales favorably with d.
- The triangular factor has useful byproducts (eigenvalues on the diagonal; cheap triangular solves and inversion).

Weaknesses
- Limited originality: the contribution appears to be a straightforward generalization of known Cholesky-update ideas (e.g., Krause & Igel 2015) to rank-μ CMA-ES, plus a heuristic CSA modification. The novelty over prior work is not crisply delineated.
- Missing equivalence proof: Algorithm 2 is claimed to implement the rank-1 and rank-μ covariance updates in Eq. (2) when applied in sequence, but a formal derivation/proof is not provided. Given the centrality of this claim (and its restriction to triangular updates), a correctness and stability argument is essential, even if similar results exist for μ=1.
- Complexity claims are underspecified. The paper states O(μ d^2) but does not use Θ-notation nor carefully compare amortized costs against standard CMA-ES with delayed decompositions. The practical speedups depend on constants and on λ; a full cost breakdown (sampling O(λ d^2), update O(μ d^2), CSA O(d^2)) and comparison to CMA-ES-Ref’s amortized costs is needed.
- CSA approximation is insufficiently validated. The claim that the path error introduced by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} is small and diminishes over time is only supported by final-performance comparisons. This contradicts known sensitivity of CSA to the normalization (e.g., Auger 2015 notes invariance issues), and calls for time-resolved diagnostics.
- Empirical scope: comparison is missing to more scalable CMA-ES variants (e.g., LM-CMA) on higher dimensions to contextualize when full-covariance O(d^2) methods remain practical.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The overall idea is reasonable, and the empirical results are convincing for the tested regimes. However, two technical gaps remain: (i) a formal derivation/stability argument that Algorithm 2 correctly produces the Cholesky factor after the rank-μ update of Eq. (2), and (ii) a quantitative analysis/measurement of the CSA path error over iterations and its effect on invariance and step-size dynamics.
- Theory: Lemma 1 assumes convergence of normalized covariance shapes; this is a strong assumption for a stochastic algorithm and yields only an asymptotic, qualitative statement. More compelling would be finite-time bounds or empirical measurements of rotation drift (e.g., ||E_t E_{t-1}^T − I||) and path-length bias.
- Experiments: Solid evaluation on rotated benchmarks; speedups are clear. Please add: (a) time-series of the norm of the CSA path and its deviation from the χ expectation, (b) direct measurement of the discrepancy between C^{-1/2}(m_{t+1}-m_t)/σ and A^{-1}(m_{t+1}-m_t)/σ, (c) ablations comparing exact CSA (using polar decomposition) on small d to validate that the approximation preserves dynamics, and (d) amortized cost comparison to CMA-ES with different decomposition delays.

Clarity
- Generally well written and easy to follow. However:
  - Add a precise, self-contained proof sketch that a sequence of rank-one Cholesky updates yields the same C_{t+1} as Eq. (2), including conditions ensuring SPD is preserved for all steps.
  - Provide a clear complexity table with Θ-notation and all per-iteration costs (sampling, update, CSA, inversion frequency).
  - Explicitly discuss invariance properties of the modified CSA and how they differ from the standard algorithm.
- Minor issues:
  - Algorithm 2: “wk” should be α_k.
  - Table 1: “n” should be d.
  - Line 185: “F” should be “f”.

Originality
- Moderate. The main novelty is extending known Cholesky rank-one updates to rank-μ CMA-ES and proposing a practical CSA approximation. The paper would benefit from a sharper contrast to Krause & Igel (2015) and a formalization of what is new (e.g., a general proof for rank-μ updates on triangular factors).

Significance
- Practical significance is potentially high: if broadly adopted, the method could be a new default in full-covariance CMA-ES implementations when function evaluations are cheap. That said, the contribution is incremental and lacks some validation needed to establish reliability of the CSA modification across problems.

Requested revisions/questions
1) Provide a formal proof (or a reference with explicit adaptation) that Algorithm 2, applied as in Algorithm 1, yields a Cholesky factor whose product equals the covariance update in Eq. (2), including stability considerations.
2) Add a thorough complexity analysis with Θ-notation and an amortized comparison against delayed-decomposition CMA-ES across d and λ; report constant factors and BLAS/LAPACK usage.
3) Empirically quantify the CSA path error over time: plot ||p_σ||/E{χ}, the discrepancy between exact and approximated normalized steps, and rotation drift ||E_t E_{t-1}^T − I||; include small-d runs with exact CSA via polar decomposition for calibration.
4) Optionally, compare against LM-CMA on larger d to position the proposed method’s sweet spot.

Pros
- Clear, practical algorithm; integrates well with existing codebases.
- Strong wall-clock speedups with preserved evaluation counts on standard tests.
- Memory optimality and useful byproducts (eigenvalues, cheap triangular solves).

Cons
- Incremental novelty; close to prior Cholesky-update work.
- Missing formal equivalence proof and limited analysis of CSA approximation.
- Incomplete complexity discussion; lacks amortized comparisons.
- Insufficient diagnostics validating the claimed small and diminishing path error.

Overall recommendation
Recommendation: Reject in current form. The paper is promising and practically relevant, but requires a formal equivalence/stability argument for the Cholesky updates, a more rigorous and time-resolved validation of the CSA approximation, and a clearer complexity analysis to meet NIPS standards.

Reviewer confidence: High.
