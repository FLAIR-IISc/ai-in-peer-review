Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T, instead of updating C and recomputing its square root via eigendecomposition. This yields an O(μ d^2) per-iteration update (with typical μ = O(log d)), versus the ω(d^2)–O(d^3) costs incurred by recomputing C1/2. The key change is in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA): the standard CMA-ES uses C^{-1/2} to normalize steps, whereas Cholesky-CMA-ES uses A^{-1}, omitting the orthogonal factor Et that would align A with C^{1/2}. The authors argue theoretically that when the normalized covariance stabilizes, the orthogonal factor changes diminish (Et^T E_{t-1} → I), making the approximation asymptotically exact in norm for the CSA signal. Empirically, on standard synthetic benchmarks (including rotated versions), Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves substantial wall-clock speedups without increasing the number of function evaluations, compared to a reference CMA-ES with delayed decompositions and to a non-triangular Cholesky-update variant (Suttorp et al., 2009).

This contribution sits alongside prior attempts to (a) amortize or delay decomposition (Hansen & Ostermeier; Hansen, 2015), (b) restrict model complexity (diagonal, low-rank, or limited-memory updates; Ros & Hansen 2008; Loshchilov 2014/2015), and (c) update factors instead of full C (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015). Relative to these, this paper extends triangular Cholesky updates to the standard multi-parent CMA-ES and provides a justification for approximating CSA without explicitly tracking the changing orthogonal factor.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic idea with solid engineering: rank-one updates on a triangular factor yield O(μ d^2) time and optimal storage.
- Theoretical rationale: under convergence of the normalized covariance, the CSA approximation error vanishes in norm due to exponentially decaying weights.
- Comprehensive synthetic evaluation: rotated and unrotated standard benchmarks, multiple dimensions up to d = 256, reporting both function evaluations and wall-clock time.
- Practical benefits: large speedups in wall-clock time with no observed loss in evaluation efficiency; improved numerical stability and easy access to A^{-1} in O(d^2).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theory relies on restrictive assumptions (convergence of the normalized covariance and smooth factor evolution). No non-asymptotic error bounds or guarantees for non-stationary or noisy settings are provided.
- Invariance properties: standard CMA-ES is largely affine-invariant, while CSA already weakens this; the proposed modification may further impact rotational invariance. Experiments suggest robustness, but a theoretical characterization is missing.
- Empirical scope: results are limited to synthetic functions; no noisy, constrained, or real-world tasks where runtime matters are included.
- Baselines: the most relevant large-scale alternatives (e.g., LM-CMA / LM-MA-ES) are not compared; these trade model complexity for speed and are important for high-d regimes.
- A technical inaccuracy: the paper claims “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general; Cholesky diagonals are not eigenvalues unless C is diagonal. The method still has merit without this claim.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The factor-update derivation follows prior work; Algorithm 2 gives a stable rank-one update in quadratic time. The CSA approximation is motivated carefully and backed by a convergence lemma; however, the lack of finite-time bounds and missing analysis under non-convergent dynamics limit theoretical strength.
- Empirical support: The benchmarks and measurements (evaluations vs. time) are appropriate and show clear gains. It would help to quantify the CSA approximation error directly (e.g., angle or norm difference to the exact CSA signal) across iterations and conditions.

Clarity
- Generally well organized and readable. The presentation of the RQ/polar decompositions and their roles is helpful. However:
  - Fix the incorrect statement about Cholesky diagonals and eigenvalues.
  - Clean up several reference formatting issues.
  - Explicitly state the per-iteration costs, including the A^{-1} application to update pσ, and whether A^{-1} is recomputed or applied via triangular solves.
  - Clarify invariance properties of the modified CSA (what is preserved, what is not).

Originality
- Extends prior Cholesky-update ideas to the standard CMA-ES with multi-parent updates and integrates a justified CSA approximation. While conceptually incremental, the combination and its careful argumentation are novel and practically meaningful.

Significance
- For regimes where objective evaluations are cheap and d is moderate-to-large, the speedups are significant and likely to be adopted in practice (e.g., policy search inner loops, algorithm tuning, ML hyperparameter sweeps with synthetic objectives). For very large d, limited-memory variants remain necessary, and for expensive objectives, the impact is smaller. Nevertheless, replacing the standard full covariance update with this variant seems a worthwhile change in standard toolboxes.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative diagnostics of the Et-induced error: track ∥Et^T E_{t-1} − I∥ and the discrepancy between the exact CSA norm and the approximation on representative problems (including cases where covariance changes rapidly).
- Add comparisons to LM-CMA (Loshchilov 2014/2015) and other limited-memory/low-rank variants to position speed/quality trade-offs.
- Include at least one real-world or noisy benchmark where computational savings matter (e.g., real-time control or black-box tuning with strict time budgets).
- Study quadratics with controlled spectra (as in Stich & Müller) to relate performance and CSA error to condition number and spectral spread; report how quickly the Et error decays as C stabilizes.
- Discuss invariance explicitly and, if possible, provide partial guarantees (e.g., bounds under small per-iteration rotations).
- Correct the Cholesky/eigenvalue claim and clarify how eigenvalues (if needed) would be obtained (they are not “free” from A).

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, implementable algorithm with clear speedups and no empirical loss in evaluations.
- Theoretical justification for the CSA approximation, aligned with observed behavior.
- Optimal storage and improved numerical stability; practical relevance for widely used CMA-ES.

Cons against acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees are asymptotic and rely on strong assumptions; no finite-time bounds.
- Missing comparisons to key large-scale baselines; no real-world experiments.
- Minor but important technical misstatement about Cholesky diagonals; several reference/formatting issues.

Overall assessment
A strong engineering contribution with a plausible theoretical rationale and convincing synthetic results. With the corrections and additional experiments suggested above—especially clarifying invariance and adding comparisons to limited-memory CMA variants—this work would make a valuable addition to the NIPS community’s toolbox for derivative-free optimization.
