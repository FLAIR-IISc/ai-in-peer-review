This paper presents a valuable improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm by utilizing Cholesky factorization to reduce the computational cost associated with covariance matrix updates. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that replacing the matrix square root with the triangular Cholesky factor in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism doesn’t degrade the algorithm’s performance, while significantly lowering runtime complexity, particularly in higher dimensions. This is a practical and theoretically grounded advancement, building upon previous work attempting similar optimizations (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) but overcoming limitations of those approaches.

The paper effectively positions itself within the existing landscape of CMA-ES optimization techniques. It acknowledges the trade-offs between reducing computational cost through delayed updates (Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001) and maintaining algorithmic efficacy, and the issues with restricting model complexity (Poland and Zell, 2001; Ros and Hansen, 2008). The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon previous attempts to leverage Cholesky decomposition, specifically addressing the challenge of maintaining the CSA mechanism’s functionality.  The connection to information geometry (Akimoto et al., 2012) is appropriately noted as providing a foundation for the covariance matrix update rule.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical justification for the Cholesky-based approach is compelling. Lemma 1 provides a crucial argument for the convergence of the modified CSA mechanism, and the reasoning behind its validity is well-explained. The quadratic runtime complexity is clearly established.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation for the work is clearly presented, and the explanation of the Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm is easy to follow. Algorithm 1 and 2 are helpful.
* **Originality:** While building on existing CMA-ES research, the paper presents a novel approach that addresses a significant bottleneck in the algorithm's performance.  The extension of the Cholesky update to the standard CMA-ES, coupled with the theoretical backing, represents a genuine contribution.
* **Significance:** The reduction in runtime complexity, particularly as dimensionality increases, is highly significant.  The demonstrated speed-up in the experimental results (Figure 2) suggests the Cholesky-CMA-ES is a valuable tool for tackling high-dimensional optimization problems, such as those found in machine learning (e.g., neural network training – as discussed in the conclusion). The preservation of the evaluation count is a particularly strong benefit.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Numerical Stability Explanation:** The reviewer’s request for a clearer explanation of why the proposed computation is "numerically more stable" remains partially unanswered. While the paper discusses the benefits of working directly with the Cholesky factor (avoiding separate inversion steps), it doesn't explicitly detail how this translates to improved numerical robustness in practical implementations compared to standard CMA-ES. A more in-depth discussion of potential numerical issues with the standard approach and how the Cholesky factorization mitigates them would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the comparisons to CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d and Suttorp-CMA-ES are valuable, further comparison with other state-of-the-art optimization algorithms (beyond CMA-ES variants) would strengthen the paper. Showing how the Cholesky-CMA-ES performs relative to algorithms like BFGS or other derivative-free methods would provide a broader context.
* **Cache Effects:** The paper acknowledges the impact of cache size on performance for d=256. A more detailed discussion of the memory access patterns and potential optimizations to address this issue would be worthwhile.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a practical bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm.
* The theoretical justification is strong and the experimental results validate the proposed approach.
* The code is available via Shark (mentioned in the paper), promoting reproducibility.
* The speed-up achieved, particularly in higher dimensions, is significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The explanation of numerical stability could be more detailed.
* The comparison is primarily limited to other CMA-ES variants.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a valuable implementation of Cholesky factorization, offering significant performance improvements without sacrificing algorithmic efficacy. The authors have effectively addressed the theoretical challenges associated with this approach, and the experimental results are compelling.  The paper addresses a clear need in the optimization community. Addressing the minor weaknesses regarding numerical stability and broadening the comparison would further strengthen the work, but it is currently of sufficient quality for acceptance. The suggestion of combining with low-dimensional approximation methods (Loshchilov, 2015) for even higher-dimensional problems is insightful and highlights potential future research directions.
