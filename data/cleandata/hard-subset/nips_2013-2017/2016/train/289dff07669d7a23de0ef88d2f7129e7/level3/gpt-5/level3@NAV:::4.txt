This paper proposes a Cholesky-based reformulation of CMA-ES that replaces the expensive matrix square root and inverse in CSA with operations on a triangular Cholesky factor. Building on prior ideas that update decompositions directly (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), the authors extend these to standard CMA-ES and provide a theoretical argument that substituting A−1 for C−1/2 in CSA does not alter performance in terms of objective evaluations. The result is an O(µ d^2) update with optimal storage that retains CMA-ES behavior while materially reducing wall-clock time, particularly as d grows. The work is well-situated with respect to CMA-ES and related accelerations (diagonal/structured variants, delayed decompositions, low-memory approximations such as Loshchilov, 2015).

Quality: The algorithmic development is sound, with clear pseudocode and a useful lemma explaining why the rotation error in CSA diminishes as the covariance stabilizes. Experiments on rotated benchmarks show essentially unchanged evaluation counts versus a strong reference while substantially reducing runtime; the scaling trend is convincing. Two aspects merit strengthening: (1) the claim of numerical stability would benefit from a more explicit rationale (e.g., backward-stability of Cholesky rank-one updates, conditioning of triangular solves, and safeguards when Ct is ill-conditioned); (2) a quantitative bound on CSA error accumulation from using A−1 instead of C−1/2 would reinforce the theoretical section.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and organized. The distinction between triangular and non-triangular updates and its implications (uniqueness, smoothness) is insightful. Please clarify the practical tolerance/regularization used in Algorithm 2 and articulate when reconditioning is triggered.

Originality: The extension of Cholesky updates to the full CMA-ES with a principled CSA modification and associated justification is novel relative to earlier non-triangular updates and delayed decompositions.

Significance: High. This is a valuable contribution for practitioners constrained by matrix operations; the speedups without extra evaluations are compelling. It also provides useful byproducts (eigenvalues “for free,” cheap triangular inversion).

Suggestions: Evaluate on noisy objectives and higher-dimensional tasks (e.g., policy search, NN hyperparameters). Compare against low-memory/structured variants (LM-CMA, sep-CMA) in d ≥ 1000. Explore hybridization with low-dimensional approximations (Loshchilov, 2015) for regimes where full covariance remains too costly.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled algorithm with strong empirical wins in runtime; evaluation counts unchanged.
- Theoretical argument for CSA approximation; optimal time/memory for full covariance CMA-ES.
- Practical impact and easy drop-in replacement.

Cons for acceptance:
- “Numerically more stable” claim under-explained; lack of quantitative CSA error bounds.
- Limited evaluation on very high-d and noisy/real-world problems.
