### Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm widely used in machine learning. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a time complexity dominated by the O(d^3) cost of decomposing the d x d covariance matrix in each iteration, which is prohibitive for high-dimensional problems. The authors propose "Cholesky-CMA-ES," a variant that directly maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. This approach reduces the per-iteration complexity to O(µd^2), which is asymptotically optimal for a full covariance matrix update.

A key technical challenge arises in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the inverse of the matrix square root (C^{-1/2}). The authors' main contribution is to replace this term with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor (A^{-1}). They provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) arguing that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the error introduced by this approximation vanishes. This work builds upon previous efforts to reduce CMA-ES complexity (e.g., delayed updates or non-triangular Cholesky updates by Suttorp et al., 2009) but provides a more direct and efficient implementation for the standard CMA-ES algorithm, complete with a rationale for its performance equivalence. Experimental results on benchmark functions demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a significant reduction in wall-clock time without degrading convergence performance in terms of function evaluations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Practical Impact:** The paper tackles a critical and long-standing limitation of one of the most effective black-box optimization algorithms. By reducing the computational complexity from cubic to quadratic, the proposed method makes CMA-ES significantly more practical for moderately high-dimensional problems (d > 50), which are common in areas like policy search and hyperparameter tuning. The presented algorithm is a valuable contribution that is likely to be adopted as the default implementation in many software libraries.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The use of rank-one updates on the Cholesky factor is a well-established numerical technique, and its application here is elegant. The theoretical argument in Lemma 1 provides a convincing, albeit non-rigorous, justification for why replacing the inverse matrix square root with the inverse Cholesky factor in the CSA path does not harm the algorithm's performance. This justification is a key element that distinguishes this work from previous attempts.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and effectively support the paper's claims. The comparison against a standard reference implementation and a delayed-update variant clearly shows that Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a substantial speedup in wall-clock time while preserving the number of function evaluations required for convergence. The results convincingly show that the performance advantage grows with the dimensionality of the problem, as predicted by the complexity analysis.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. It provides sufficient background on CMA-ES, clearly articulates the problem, and presents the proposed solution and its justification in a logical manner.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Clarification on Numerical Stability:** The abstract and conclusion state that the proposed computation is "numerically more stable." This is an important claim, as direct updates to Cholesky factors are indeed often preferred for maintaining positive definiteness. However, the paper does not elaborate on why this is the case in this specific context. Is the improved stability due to avoiding the explicit formation of the covariance matrix C (which can suffer from precision loss), or is it related to the inversion of a triangular matrix being a better-conditioned operation? A brief discussion of the source of this improved numerical stability would strengthen the paper's claims.

2.  **Scalability to Very High Dimensions:** The paper rightly concludes by positioning the Cholesky-CMA-ES for problems where maintaining a full covariance matrix is still feasible. While the improvement from O(d^3) to O(d^2) is significant, this quadratic complexity remains a barrier for truly large-scale problems (e.g., d >> 1000) such as optimizing deep neural networks. The authors mention low-dimensional approximation methods (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) as an alternative for such cases. A valuable addition would be a short discussion on the potential of combining the proposed efficient Cholesky update mechanism with such low-rank or limited-memory approximations. This could pave the way for a new class of algorithms that are both computationally efficient and capable of capturing important covariance structures, further broadening the impact of this work.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper presenting a valuable and practical improvement to a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization. The main contribution is a well-justified and empirically verified method to reduce the runtime complexity of CMA-ES without sacrificing its excellent performance characteristics. The work is original, significant, and presented with clarity. The identified weaknesses are minor and relate to requests for clarification and discussion of future directions rather than fundamental flaws. The paper represents a solid and important contribution to the field and is well-suited for NIPS.
