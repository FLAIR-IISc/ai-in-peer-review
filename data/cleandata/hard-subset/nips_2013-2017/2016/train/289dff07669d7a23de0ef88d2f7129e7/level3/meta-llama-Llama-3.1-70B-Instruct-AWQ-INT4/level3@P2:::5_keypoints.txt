- Proposal: Cholesky-CMA-ES replaces the full covariance matrix with a lower-triangular Cholesky factor and omits estimating the random orthogonal matrix E_t.  
- Complexity: Reduces runtime from O(d^3) to O(μ d^2).  
- Theoretical: Provides convergence justification under restrictive assumptions, arguing the approximation error decreases as the algorithm converges.  
- Empirical: Demonstrates substantial speed-up over two CMA-ES variants that delay full covariance updates on standard benchmarks without losing optimization performance.  
- Practical impact: Speed-up is most useful when evaluations are cheap and dimensionality is high; limited relevance when evaluation time dominates or dimensions are modest.  
- Major concern: Theoretical assumptions are restrictive and formal guarantees are limited.  
- Suggested validation: Track evolution/error of E_t (e.g., in various norms) by running full CMA-ES and the approximation simultaneously.  
- Suggested reporting: Include actual wall-clock runtimes and performance of full CMA-ES without delay strategies.  
- Suggested experiments: Test on quadratics with continuously varied spectra to study solution quality and E_t error versus spectral distribution.  
- Suggested real-world test: Include an application where runtime matters (e.g., real-time robotics policy gradients) to demonstrate practical relevance.  
- Clarity: Well-written and easy to follow with a clear explanation of the algorithm and theory.  
- Presentation: Empirical results are clear but would benefit from additional plots and tables for more detail.  
- Overall assessment: Significant contribution with potential usefulness in certain applications, but needs further experiments and analysis to fully understand limitations.  
- Scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 8/10.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, well-motivated modification with sound theory and convincing empirical results.  
- Arguments for rejection: Restrictive theoretical assumptions, limited practical impact in many real-world settings, and insufficient experimental analysis.
