

# Review for NIPS 2016

This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the matrix square root with a triangular Cholesky factor in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. The authors claim this modification maintains the same optimization performance as the standard CMA-ES while reducing the computational complexity from O(d^3) to O(d^2) for covariance matrix updates and storage. The paper builds on previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), which considered Cholesky factorizations for specific CMA-ES variants, extending their approach to the standard CMA-ES with rank-1 and rank-μ updates.

**Strengths**

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the optimization literature. The authors provide a clear theoretical justification (Lemma 1) for why replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor in the CSA mechanism does not significantly alter the algorithm's behavior, particularly when the covariance matrix converges to a certain shape. The empirical results are well-presented and demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same number of objective function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES (with delayed matrix square root computation) while significantly reducing wall-clock time, especially as dimensionality increases. For d=64, the speedup is already 20x, which is substantial for practical applications. The implementation details are clear, and the paper correctly acknowledges related work, including the limitations of previous approaches like Suttorp-CMA-ES.

**Weaknesses**

The main weakness is the limited novelty of the approach. As noted in the key points, extending Krause and Igel's (2015) rank-1 update to the standard CMA-ES with rank-μ updates is a straightforward generalization rather than a novel contribution. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution from previous work, particularly Suttorp et al. (2009), who also proposed using Cholesky factors in CMA-ES variants. While the authors claim their approach "comes with a theoretical justification," this justification relies on the convergence assumption of the covariance matrix (Lemma 1), which is not fully established for the standard CMA-ES. The authors could strengthen their argument by citing Beyer's convergence analysis (2014) of CMA-ES, which would provide better theoretical grounding for their assumption.

The paper contains minor technical errors that should be corrected: in Equation (1), the σ should be \tilde{σ} (as noted in the key points), and there's a typo in line 94 ("which can by be motivated" → "which can be motivated"). While these are minor, they affect the paper's technical credibility.

**Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance Assessment**

*Quality*: The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments showing the expected computational advantages. However, the theoretical justification for the modification (Lemma 1) relies on an assumption about covariance convergence that is not thoroughly established for the standard CMA-ES. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not provide sufficient evidence that the assumption holds in practice.

*Clarity*: The paper is well-written and clearly explains the CMA-ES algorithm and the proposed modification. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are particularly clear. However, the minor notation errors and typo reduce the clarity of the presentation.

*Originality*: The contribution is limited. The extension from the (1+1)-CMA-ES to the standard CMA-ES with rank-μ updates is a straightforward generalization of prior work, not a significant conceptual advance. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from Suttorp et al. (2009) or Krause and Igel (2015), which weakens the novelty claim.

*Significance*: The work addresses a practical computational bottleneck in CMA-ES, which could be valuable for applications where the covariance matrix is still tractable but updating it is expensive. However, for very high-dimensional problems, low-dimensional approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) are typically used, which the authors acknowledge. Thus, the significance is somewhat limited to intermediate-dimensional problems where full covariance matrices are still feasible but updating them is costly.

**Recommendation**

The paper makes a technically sound contribution that addresses a practical computational bottleneck in CMA-ES. While the theoretical justification could be strengthened, and the novelty is limited, the empirical results are convincing and demonstrate a significant practical improvement in runtime. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how it differs from previous Cholesky-based CMA-ES approaches and stronger theoretical grounding for the convergence assumption. 

Given the practical significance of reducing the computational cost of CMA-ES (a widely used optimization algorithm) while maintaining performance, I recommend acceptance. However, the authors should address the minor technical errors and strengthen their discussion of related work to clarify the novelty of their contribution. This is a good paper that makes a useful improvement to an important optimization algorithm, though it does not represent a major theoretical advance.
