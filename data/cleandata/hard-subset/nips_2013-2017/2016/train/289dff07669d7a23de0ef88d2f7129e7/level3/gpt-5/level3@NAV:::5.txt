This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, an approximation to CMA-ES that maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance instead of the full covariance/square-root. The update decomposes the rank-1 and rank-µ steps into a sequence of rank-one Cholesky updates, yielding O(µ d^2) time and optimal storage, versus the cubic cost of recomputing square roots. The key departure from standard CMA-ES lies in the CSA: the authors replace C−1/2 by A−1 (triangular inverse), omitting the per-iteration random orthogonal rotation E_t. They justify this by showing that, under restrictive convergence assumptions, E_t → I (up to scaling), implying asymptotic equivalence. Empirically, on standard synthetic benchmarks with rotations, the method achieves substantial wall-clock speed-ups without degrading optimization performance compared to two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that delay full covariance updates. The work connects to prior Cholesky updates in (1+1)- and MO-CMA-ES and to runtime-reduction strategies (delayed updates, diagonal/limited-memory), but is novel in integrating a triangular factor with standard CMA-ES and a theoretically motivated CSA modification.

Quality: The algorithmic contribution is technically sound and the implementation appears careful. However, the theoretical guarantee relies on strong convergence-of-shape assumptions; the lemma is plausible but provides limited non-asymptotic control on the CSA error. The empirical study is solid but could be more comprehensive (see below).

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. Assumptions behind Lemma 1 should be stated more prominently, and the consequences for non-convergent or slowly rotating regimes clarified. Reporting exact hardware, BLAS/LAPACK settings, and memory footprints would aid reproducibility.

Originality: The combination of triangular-factor maintenance with a principled argument for omitting E_t in CSA is new and clearly differentiated from Suttorp et al. and Krause & Igel.

Significance: The speedup is meaningful when evaluations are cheap and/or d is high; otherwise, practical impact may be limited because evaluation time often dominates. Still, this is a useful step toward scalable full-covariance CMA-ES.

Pros:
- Clear, principled algorithm with asymptotically optimal update/storage.
- Strong empirical runtime gains with no loss in evaluations.
- Good positioning relative to prior acceleration strategies.

Cons:
- Restrictive theory with limited finite-sample guarantees.
- Practical impact may be modest in evaluation-dominated settings.
- Missing comparisons and diagnostics.

Suggestions:
- Track the evolution of E_t (e.g., ‖E_t − I‖, ‖E_tE_{t−1}^T − I‖) by running full CMA-ES and Cholesky-CMA-ES in parallel.
- Report wall-clock times for full CMA-ES without delay and include LM-CMA as a baseline.
- Evaluate on quadratics with continuously varied spectra (cf. Stich & Müller) to relate solution quality and E_t error to spectral distributions.
- Include at least one real-time or cheap-evaluation task (e.g., robotics policy gradients) where the speedup matters.
- Provide ablations on CSA modification and numerical stability, and report memory usage.
