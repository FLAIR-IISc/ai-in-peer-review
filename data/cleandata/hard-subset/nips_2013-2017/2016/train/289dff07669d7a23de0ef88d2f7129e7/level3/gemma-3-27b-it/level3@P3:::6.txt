This paper proposes a modification to the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) focusing on the covariance matrix update, aiming for improved computational efficiency and reduced memory footprint. The core idea is to operate directly on the Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix, avoiding explicit computation of the matrix square root. The authors argue this approach maintains the performance characteristics of the original CMA-ES while offering significant practical benefits, particularly in high-dimensional spaces. This builds upon prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), extending their Cholesky-based updates to the standard CMA-ES framework.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach, with a clear justification for why the proposed modification *should* not deteriorate performance. The theoretical arguments around the convergence properties (Lemma 1) are relevant and reasonably well-explained. However, the empirical evaluation is incomplete (see weaknesses below). The claim of "optimal" asymptotic complexity is somewhat overstated (addressed below). The numerical stability claim regarding Suttorp et al. (2009) is questionable and unsupported. The correction regarding eigenvalue decomposition frequency is valuable. Overall, the work is more than a position paper but stops short of being a complete, thoroughly validated contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithm is clearly presented, and the motivation for each step is explained. However, the connection between equation (6) and the justification for the approximation could be made more explicit. The discussion around the impact of coordinate rotations needs further elaboration.

**Originality:** While the idea of using Cholesky factorization within CMA-ES isn’t entirely novel (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015), extending it to the standard CMA-ES and providing a theoretical justification represents a genuine incremental contribution. The main originality lies in the systematic application of rank-one updates to the Cholesky factor, and the analysis of the resulting approximation error. However, the fundamental algorithmic innovation – looping the rankOneUpdate – feels limited.

**Significance:** The potential significance is moderate. If the empirical results robustly confirmed the performance claims and the speedups observed in experiments are maintained in more comprehensive testing, this could be a useful practical improvement for CMA-ES, particularly for large-scale problems. However, the limited speedup (stated as ~2x) and the quadratic complexity prevent it from being a truly breakthrough advance. The approach is unlikely to fundamentally address the scalability issues inherent in full covariance matrix adaptation.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear and concise presentation of the algorithm.
*   Sound theoretical justification for the approximation.
*   Demonstrated speed improvements in the experiments, particularly at higher dimensions.
*   Identifies and addresses potential numerical stability concerns.
*   Useful factual correction regarding eigenvalue decomposition frequency.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Misleading Naming:** The term "Cholesky-CMA-ES" is already established in the literature (Suttorp et al., 2009) and should be avoided. A different name is crucial.
*   **Missing Prior Work Comparison:** The paper fails to adequately address and empirically compare against Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause et al. (2015). This is a major omission. An algorithmic comparison detailing the differences would be valuable.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** The core algorithmic novelty appears to be simply iterating the `rankOneUpdate` for all individuals – a single "for" loop. This seems insufficient justification for a full paper.
*   **Incomplete Experiments:** The experiments *must* include a direct comparison to Suttorp’s Cholesky-CMA-ES and Krause’s variant. Figures should be updated to reflect these comparisons.
*   **Plotting Concern:** The observed difference on the Cigar function plateau requires further clarification. The authors need to explain if the median result plotting contributes to this observation.
*   **Overstated Optimality Claim:** The algorithm isn’t "optimal" for Sphere or separable functions where a diagonal covariance matrix is sufficient. The ~2x speedup compared to Suttorp’s method is modest.
*   **Questionable Numerical Stability Claim:** The assertion that Suttorp’s method is numerically unstable contradicts the original paper, which explicitly claims the opposite. The authors must provide evidence for this claim.
*   **Unchanged Scalability:** The quadratic complexity remains unchanged. This doesn't solve the fundamental scalability issues of CMA-ES, although it offers some advantages for full covariance matrix adaptation.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Technically sound with a reasonable justification.
*   Clear presentation and potential for practical improvement.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in CMA-ES optimization.

**Con:**

*   Significant omissions in experimental comparison.
*   Incremental algorithmic contribution.
*   Overstated claims of optimality and numerical instability.
*   Misleading naming convention.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The paper has potential, but the missing experimental comparisons, questionable claims, and incremental contribution are significant weaknesses. Addressing the naming issue is also critical. The authors should:

(a) Resolve the naming conflict.
(b) Run comprehensive experiments including Suttorp and Krause variants (move full plots to the supplement if needed).
(c) Explicitly state that the main algorithmic change is the "for" loop in Algorithm 1 or provide a more compelling justification for its significance.
(d) Substantiate the numerical instability claim or retract it.
(e) Re-evaluate and moderate the "optimality" claims.
