## Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper presents a compelling modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) aimed at reducing its computational complexity. The core idea – maintaining and updating a Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix instead of the full matrix – is well-motivated, given that covariance updates often dominate runtime, especially in high dimensions. The authors correctly identify the key bottleneck in standard CMA-ES and propose a theoretically sound approach to address it, achieving a complexity of O(μd²) compared to the standard O(d³). 

The paper builds logically upon previous work in this area, acknowledging prior attempts to use Cholesky factors (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) and clearly differentiating their approach by providing a theoretical justification for its efficacy. The authors’ argument, based on the convergence of the orthogonal matrix E<sub>t</sub> to the identity matrix, is insightful and provides a strong rationale for the approximation. 

Empirically, the results are convincing. The paper demonstrates a substantial speed-up on standard benchmark functions, with no apparent loss of optimization performance. The comparison against state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants with delayed updates is well-chosen and highlights the benefits of the Cholesky-based approach. The inclusion of tests with rotated functions further strengthens the validity of the method.

However, the paper’s practical significance is somewhat limited by the acknowledged fact that evaluation time often dominates runtime in real-world applications. While the speed-up is valuable, its impact may be marginal in many scenarios. Furthermore, the theoretical assumptions regarding the convergence of E<sub>t</sub> are restrictive, and more rigorous guarantees would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical foundation and justification for the proposed approximation.
*   Significant speed-up demonstrated on benchmark functions.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the method and results.
*   Addresses a known bottleneck in CMA-ES, potentially enabling its application to higher-dimensional problems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Practical impact may be limited due to the dominance of evaluation time in many applications.
*   Theoretical assumptions are restrictive and lack broad applicability.
*   Limited exploration of real-world applications where speed is critical.

**Suggested Improvements:**

I strongly encourage the authors to address the following points:

1.  **Track E<sub>t</sub> Evolution:** As suggested by the area chair, track the evolution of E<sub>t</sub> (e.g., using various norms) by running both full CMA-ES and the approximation simultaneously. This would provide empirical evidence supporting the theoretical claim.
2.  **Report Baseline CMA-ES Performance:** Include the actual wall-clock runtimes and performance of full CMA-ES *without* any delay strategies for completeness.
3.  **Quadratic Benchmarks:** Run experiments on quadratics with continuously varied spectra (as suggested by Stich & Müller, 2012) to study solution quality and E<sub>t</sub> error versus spectral distribution.
4.  **Real-World Application:** Include a real-world experiment where speed matters, such as real-time robotics policy gradients, to demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of evolutionary computation. While the practical impact may be limited in some cases, the theoretical insights and demonstrated speed-up make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS program. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the suggested improvements.
