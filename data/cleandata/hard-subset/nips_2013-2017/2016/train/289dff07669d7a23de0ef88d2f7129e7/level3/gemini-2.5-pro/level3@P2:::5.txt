This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a highly effective algorithm for derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES has a time complexity dominated by the O(d^3) eigenvalue decomposition or computation of the matrix square root of the d x d covariance matrix. The authors propose the "Cholesky-CMA-ES," a variant that reduces the complexity of the covariance matrix update to O(µd^2), which is asymptotically optimal. The core idea is to maintain and directly update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, avoiding the need for repeated decompositions.

The main challenge in previous attempts to use a Cholesky factorization has been the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the inverse of the matrix square root (C^{-1/2}). This paper's key contribution is to approximate this term with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor (A^{-1}). The authors provide a theoretical justification for this approximation, arguing that the orthogonal matrix E_t relating the two decompositions (C^{1/2} = A_t E_t) changes slowly and converges as the algorithm proceeds, meaning the error introduced by this substitution diminishes over time. This work builds upon previous efforts to speed up CMA-ES, such as delaying updates or using non-triangular factorizations (Suttorp et al., 2009), but presents a more stable and theoretically motivated approach for the full CMA-ES algorithm.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithm is a complete and well-justified piece of work. The derivation of the Cholesky update is sound, building on the work of Krause and Igel (2015). The theoretical argument in Lemma 1 provides valuable intuition for why the approximation in the CSA step is reasonable, although it relies on the fairly strong assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing the proposed method against state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants on a suite of standard benchmark functions. The results convincingly demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a significant speed-up in wall-clock time without sacrificing performance in terms of function evaluations.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a concise but clear background on CMA-ES, precisely identifying the computational bottleneck they aim to solve. The proposed modification is explained logically, and the accompanying pseudocode makes the implementation clear. The figures are effective at visualizing the main results, particularly the trade-off between function evaluations and wall-clock time.

**Originality:** While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor to speed up CMA-ES has been explored before, this paper's primary novelty lies in its successful adaptation to the standard CMA-ES algorithm, particularly in handling the CSA step. The approximation C^{-1/2} ≈ A^{-1} and its theoretical justification based on the convergence of the associated orthogonal matrix is a novel and important contribution that overcomes a key hurdle in this line of research. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from prior work.

**Significance:** This work represents a significant practical and algorithmic contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. CMA-ES is a workhorse algorithm in many areas, including machine learning for policy search and hyperparameter tuning. By drastically reducing the per-iteration complexity, this paper makes CMA-ES applicable to higher-dimensional problems where function evaluations are relatively cheap. This could broaden the applicability of the algorithm. A potential limitation to its practical impact is that in many machine learning applications, the cost of objective function evaluation (e.g., training a model) far outweighs the O(d^3) matrix operations, especially for moderate dimensions. Nonetheless, for problems where this is not the case, the proposed method is a clear and immediate improvement over the standard implementation.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

The theoretical justification for the core approximation is compelling but rests on an assumption of convergence. It would be very powerful to empirically validate this claim.
*   Consider an experiment where you run the standard CMA-ES and explicitly compute the orthogonal matrix E_t at each step. You could then plot a metric of its change over time, such as ||E_t^T E_{t-1} - I||_F, to show that it indeed converges towards the identity matrix as the optimization progresses. This would provide strong empirical backing for your theoretical argument.
*   The baseline "CMA-ES-Ref" uses a delay strategy. For completeness and to provide a clearer picture of the speed-up, it would be beneficial to include results for a standard CMA-ES implementation that performs a full O(d^3) decomposition at *every* iteration. This would serve as a true upper bound on runtime for the baseline.
*   To further probe the robustness of the approximation, consider testing on problems designed to be challenging for covariance matrix adaptation, such as the quadratic functions with continuously varied spectra from Stich & Müller (2012). This could reveal how the error from omitting E_t behaves as a function of the problem's conditioning.
*   The paper's conclusion mentions applications in training neural networks for policy search. Including even a simple, real-world experiment from this domain (e.g., a classic reinforcement learning control task) would substantially strengthen the paper by demonstrating the practical impact in a scenario where the speed-up is claimed to be important.

**Summary of Pro/Con Arguments**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Addresses a long-standing and important computational bottleneck in CMA-ES, a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization.
2.  The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES provides a dramatic, empirically verified speed-up with no measurable degradation in optimization performance on standard benchmarks.
3.  The method is algorithmically sound, numerically stable, and has optimal storage requirements.
4.  The paper is very clearly written, well-motivated, and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The theoretical justification, while intuitive, relies on strong convergence assumptions that may not always hold.
2.  The practical impact is primarily for problems where function evaluations are cheap relative to O(d^3) computation, which may limit its utility for some common machine learning tasks.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a strong and valuable contribution. The proposed algorithm is a clear improvement over the standard CMA-ES in terms of computational efficiency and should be adopted as the new standard for relevant problem classes. I recommend acceptance.
