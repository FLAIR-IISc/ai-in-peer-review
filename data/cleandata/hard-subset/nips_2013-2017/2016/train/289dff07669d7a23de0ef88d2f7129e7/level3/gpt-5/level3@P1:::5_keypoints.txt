- Proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES that stores and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A (C = A A^T) instead of C or its symmetric square root.  
- Replaces C^{-1/2} in CSA with A^{-1} and omits the per-iteration random orthogonal factor Et, enabling cheap inversion and eigenvalue access.  
- Achieves O(μ d^2) time and optimal O(d^2) space per iteration (μ typically O(log d)), avoiding repeated O(d^3) eigendecompositions.  
- Justifies the CSA approximation by arguing Et “stabilizes” as the normalized covariance converges and exponential forgetting reduces accumulated error (Lemma 1 / continuity argument).  
- Implements a rank-μ Cholesky rank-one update on A (Algorithm 2) that is numerically conscious and composes usual covariance updates directly.  
- Empirically matches standard CMA-ES in function-evaluation efficiency on rotated benchmarks while delivering large wall-clock speed-ups (e.g., ~20× at d=64).  
- Evaluation setup is appropriate (random rotations, multiple dimensions up to 256, 100 trials) and supports the main claims; cache effects at higher d are discussed.  
- Technical soundness: Cholesky updates appear correct and numerically careful; CSA approximation is plausibly justified under the stated assumptions.  
- Scope/limitations: theoretical justification relies on restrictive convergence and smoothness assumptions; no finite-time bound on bias in σ updates or during non-stationary phases.  
- Affine invariance is no longer exact due to ignoring Et; the loss of affine/rotation invariance should be made explicit and empirically characterized.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well written, self-contained, and explains RQ/QR and Et roles clearly.  
- Suggestions for clarity/stability: explicitly state affine-invariance status, summarize rotation-invariance tests in main text, and add numerical-stability guards for Algorithm 2 (e.g., jitter).  
- Originality: extending triangular Cholesky updates to standard multi-offspring CMA-ES with CSA approximation is novel and fills a gap relative to prior non-triangular or restricted works.  
- Significance: practically important when objective evaluations are cheap/moderately expensive and full covariance adaptation is needed at moderate-to-large d; impact is lower when evaluations dominate cost.  
- Suggested additional experiments: track Et and pσ differences between algorithms; test spectra-controlled quadratics; include a cheap-real-world task (e.g., direct policy search); try occasional Et corrections via polar decomposition.  
- Baseline clarity: include forced eigendecomposition-every-iteration runs to fully rule out implementation subtleties and characterize default delay settings.  
- Robustness suggestion: evaluate performance under noisy objectives where covariance may not stabilize quickly.  
- Pros: principled algorithmic idea, optimal asymptotic time/memory, strong empirical speed-ups with unchanged evaluation counts, practical advantages (cheap A^{-1}, eigenvalues for free, stable triangular updates).  
- Cons: theoretical guarantees depend on convergence assumptions with no finite-time error bounds; temporary loss of affine invariance; limited impact when evaluation cost dominates; minor presentation/reference issues.  
- Recommendation: leaning toward acceptance, contingent on addressing suggested empirical analyses (especially Et tracking and a real-world/cheap-evaluation task).
