This paper proposes a CMA-ES variant that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, yielding optimal O(µ d^2) update complexity and minimal storage, while preserving the behavior of standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluations. The key idea is to replace the CSA normalization by C^{-1/2} with the inverse Cholesky factor A^{-1}, arguing (via a convergence lemma on the RQ factors) that the induced rotational mismatch vanishes as the covariance stabilizes. This ties into prior CMA-ES acceleration attempts: delayed decompositions; restricted models (diagonal/limited-memory CMA); and earlier Cholesky-style updates (Suttorp et al.; Krause & Igel), but extends them to the standard CMA-ES with theoretical justification and practical efficiency. The approach remains relevant across ML applications (RL policy search, hyperparameter tuning) and connects to information geometry–motivated updates.

Quality: The algorithmic derivation and numerical considerations are sound; the rank-one updates for the triangular factor are clearly specified, and the argument for CSA’s approximation is reasonable and empirically validated. Experiments on rotated benchmarks across d up to 256 show matched evaluation counts vs. reference CMA-ES and large wall-clock speedups, aligning with the claimed complexity. Two minor concerns: runtime fairness details are underspecified; and some plots (e.g., Cigar/Discus at d=256) do not show the expected widening.

Clarity: The paper is well organized, with helpful algorithm boxes and a clean narrative. Reproducibility is strong, though a clearer statement of parameter defaults, seeding, BLAS/LAPACK usage, and code availability would help.

Originality: Using triangular Cholesky updates to obtain both optimal storage/time and “eigenvalues for free,” while adapting CSA to avoid cubic costs, is a novel and nontrivial integration beyond prior Cholesky-based CMA variants.

Significance: The empirical and asymptotic gains are substantial, especially in medium/large dimensions. This is a compelling candidate default when full covariance adaptation is feasible.

Pros:
- Optimal update/storage complexity; significant wall-clock gains.
- No degradation in function evaluations.
- Eigenvalues available “for free” and numerically stable inversion.
- Thorough evaluation on rotated benchmarks; strong relevance to ML practice.

Cons:
- Need stronger clarity on runtime comparability (same language/compiler, BLAS, threading, cache effects).
- Explain why Figure 2 shows limited differences for Cigar/Discus at d=256 (cache misses? delayed decompositions? step-size behavior?).

Recommendation: Strong accept, pending clarification of implementation details and a discussion of the d=256 anomalies.
