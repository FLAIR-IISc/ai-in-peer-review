

This paper proposes a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called "Cholesky-CMA-ES" that aims to reduce the time and space complexity of covariance matrix updates by using Cholesky factorization instead of matrix square root computation. The authors claim their approach achieves optimal asymptotic complexity while maintaining the same performance in terms of objective function evaluations. The main technical contribution is replacing the matrix square root in the cumulative step-size adaptation with the inverse of the Cholesky factor, which they theoretically justify as having negligible impact on algorithm performance.

The paper builds upon the well-established CMA-ES framework (Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001) and previous work on Cholesky-based updates (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015). The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck of CMA-ES as the O(d³) covariance matrix square root computation, which is particularly problematic for high-dimensional problems. Their proposed approach, which maintains the Cholesky factor and updates it via rank-one updates, theoretically achieves O(µd²) time complexity for the covariance update, which is asymptotically optimal.

The paper has several strengths: the theoretical justification for the Cholesky approximation (Lemma 1) is sound, the algorithmic description is clear, and the experimental results generally support the claim that the proposed method maintains similar performance to the standard CMA-ES while reducing runtime. The authors provide a good discussion of the storage complexity benefits and the practical advantages of obtaining eigenvalues for free from the Cholesky factor.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that undermine its contribution and impact. First, the naming "Cholesky-CMA-ES" is misleading and conflicts with the established terminology from Suttorp et al. (2009), who proposed a similar Cholesky-based approach. This creates confusion in the literature and fails to properly acknowledge prior work. The authors should either adopt a more distinctive name or clarify the relationship with Suttorp's work.

Second, the paper omits critical comparisons with both Suttorp's method (2009) and Krause's variant (2015), which are directly relevant to their approach. The experiments only compare with a delayed CMA-ES variant (CMA-ES-Ref) and CMA-ES/d, but not with the actual Cholesky-based methods they're building upon. This omission makes it impossible to evaluate whether their method offers any real advantage over existing approaches.

Third, the algorithmic contribution appears incremental. The main novelty seems to be simply applying the rankOneUpdate function (from Algorithm 2) in a loop for all individuals (as shown in Algorithm 1), which is a straightforward extension rather than a novel insight. The authors should clearly articulate why this loop constitutes a meaningful contribution beyond existing methods.

The claims about "optimal" complexity are overstated. The paper only achieves optimal complexity for non-separable ill-conditioned problems, but for separable functions (like Sphere), diagonal covariance updates suffice and the full covariance approach is unnecessary. The paper fails to acknowledge this limitation, making the "optimal" claim misleading.

The numerical stability claim is questionable. The authors assert that Suttorp's method is numerically unstable, but Suttorp et al. (2009) explicitly claim their method is numerically stable. This contradiction needs to be resolved with proper analysis or citation.

The experimental section has several issues. The results for the Cigar function show a plateau difference that isn't adequately explained, raising questions about whether the observed differences are meaningful. The paper should clarify how median results are computed and address potential sampling artifacts. The claim of "20 times faster" for d=64 appears exaggerated when compared to the theoretical complexity of Suttorp's method (which should be similar).

The paper's conclusion that this approach "scales up CMA-ES to large optimization problems" is misleading. The method still has quadratic complexity in dimensionality, so it doesn't fundamentally solve the scalability problem for very high-dimensional optimization. It only improves the constant factors for problems where full covariance adaptation is necessary.

In terms of significance, the paper's contribution is marginal for the NIPS community. While the algorithmic improvement is technically sound, it's incremental and lacks sufficient comparison with the most relevant prior work. The practical impact is limited because the method doesn't address the fundamental scalability limitations of CMA-ES for very high-dimensional problems.

To improve the paper, the authors should: 1) resolve the naming conflict and properly contextualize their work relative to Suttorp and Krause; 2) include direct comparisons with Suttorp's and Krause's methods in their experiments; 3) clarify the relationship between the Cholesky-based methods and the claimed "optimality"; 4) address the numerical stability claims with proper analysis; and 5) temper their claims about scalability.

Overall, while the paper presents a technically sound algorithmic modification, the incremental nature of the contribution, lack of proper comparison with relevant prior work, and overstated claims prevent it from making a significant contribution to the field. The paper would be much stronger with the recommended revisions.
