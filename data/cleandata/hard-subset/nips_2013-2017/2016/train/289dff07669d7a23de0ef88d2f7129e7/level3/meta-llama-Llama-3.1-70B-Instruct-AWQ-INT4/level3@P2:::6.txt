The paper "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity" proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) algorithm, which is a powerful real-valued derivative-free optimization algorithm. The authors aim to reduce the time and space complexity of the algorithm by using a triangular Cholesky factorization instead of the square root of the covariance matrix. This approach is based on maintaining triangular Cholesky factors, which requires a modification of the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism in the CMA-ES.

The paper provides a clear and concise introduction to the CMA-ES algorithm and its applications in machine learning. The authors also discuss the limitations of the standard CMA-ES, particularly in terms of its time and space complexity, and propose a solution to address these limitations. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a detailed description of their approach, including the theoretical justification and empirical results.

The main contribution of the paper is the proposal of a new algorithm, referred to as Cholesky-CMA-ES, which achieves a better, asymptotically optimal time complexity of O(µd2) for the covariance update and optimal memory complexity. The authors also provide empirical results comparing the Cholesky-CMA-ES with other CMA-ES variants, including the standard CMA-ES with delayed covariance matrix decomposition.

However, there are some concerns with the paper. Firstly, the naming of the variant "Cholesky-CMA-ES" conflicts with the established Suttorp et al. (2009) name and must be resolved. Secondly, the paper omits discussion and empirical comparison with Suttorp (2009) and algorithmic comparison with Krause et al. (2015), which are relevant prior works in this area. Thirdly, the main novelty of the paper appears to be simply looping rankOneUpdate for all individuals (one "for" loop), which may not be a significant contribution.

Additionally, the experiments presented in the paper are incomplete, as they do not empirically compare to Suttorp’s Cholesky-CMA-ES and Krause’s variant. The plots presented in the paper also raise some concerns, particularly the difference on the Cigar function plateau, which may stem from how median results are plotted and needs clarification.

The paper also makes an overstated "optimality" claim, as the approach is not optimal for Sphere or separable functions (where covariance/diagonal suffice) and only ~2× cheaper than Suttorp’s method. Furthermore, the paper asserts that Suttorp’s method is numerically unstable despite the original paper claiming the opposite, which is questionable.

Finally, the paper does not fundamentally solve large-scale CMA-ES issues, as the complexity remains quadratic, although it saves time/memory for full covariance.

In conclusion, while the paper proposes a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm that achieves better time and space complexity, there are some concerns with the paper that need to be addressed. The authors should resolve the naming conflict, provide a more comprehensive discussion and empirical comparison with prior works, and clarify the contribution of their approach.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm that achieves better time and space complexity.
* The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the CMA-ES algorithm and its applications in machine learning.
* The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a detailed description of their approach, including the theoretical justification and empirical results.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The naming of the variant "Cholesky-CMA-ES" conflicts with the established Suttorp et al. (2009) name and must be resolved.
* The paper omits discussion and empirical comparison with Suttorp (2009) and algorithmic comparison with Krause et al. (2015), which are relevant prior works in this area.
* The main novelty of the paper appears to be simply looping rankOneUpdate for all individuals (one "for" loop), which may not be a significant contribution.
* The experiments presented in the paper are incomplete, and the plots raise some concerns.
* The paper makes an overstated "optimality" claim, and the scalability of the approach remains unchanged.

Recommendations:

* Resolve the naming conflict and provide a more comprehensive discussion and empirical comparison with prior works.
* Clarify the contribution of the approach and provide more details on the algorithmic changes.
* Run experiments including Suttorp and Krause variants and move full plots to supplement if needed.
* Address the concerns with the plots and provide more details on the experimental setup.
* Revise the paper to address the overstated "optimality" claim and provide a more accurate description of the contribution of the approach.
