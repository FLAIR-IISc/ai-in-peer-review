This paper presents the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy designed to reduce computational complexity. The core idea is to replace the standard O(d³) eigen-decomposition of the covariance matrix with a direct O(µd²) update of its Cholesky factor. This modification requires an approximation in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, where the inverse of the matrix square root (C⁻¹ᐟ²) is replaced by the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor (A⁻¹). The authors argue that the rotational error introduced by this substitution is negligible and vanishes as the algorithm converges. Empirically, the proposed method achieves significant wall-clock time speedups over standard CMA-ES implementations on several benchmark functions, without increasing the number of function evaluations required to find a solution.

This work is situated within a line of research aimed at improving the scalability of CMA-ES. The concept of directly updating a matrix factorization is not new and has been explored by Suttorp et al. (2009). The specific technique of using rank-one updates for the Cholesky factor appears to be a direct extension of the method proposed by Krause and Igel (2015) for a simpler (1+1)-ES to the more general rank-µ update of the standard CMA-ES.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is strong: reducing the computational bottleneck of the covariance matrix update is a significant practical goal for making CMA-ES more applicable to high-dimensional problems where function evaluations are relatively inexpensive.
*   **Practical Impact:** The proposed method is sound from an implementation perspective, and the experimental results are compelling. The significant reduction in runtime without degrading optimization performance suggests that this approach, if its theoretical underpinnings are fully validated, could become a new standard for CMA-ES implementations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Originality:** The primary concern is the paper's novelty. The core mechanism—updating a Cholesky factor through a sequence of rank-one updates—appears to be nearly identical to the technique presented by Krause and Igel (2015) in "A More Efficient Rank-one Covariance Matrix Update for Evolution Strategies." The contribution here seems to be the application of this known technique to the rank-µ update of the standard CMA-ES, which feels more like a straightforward extension than a novel conceptual advance. The authors should more clearly articulate what distinguishes their contribution from this closely related prior work.
*   **Missing Equivalence Proof:** The algorithm relies on decomposing the rank-µ update from formula (2) into a series of rank-one updates. However, the paper does not provide a proof that applying these updates sequentially to the Cholesky factor `A` is mathematically equivalent to computing the Cholesky factor of the correctly updated covariance matrix `C_t+1`. This proof is critical for the algorithm's correctness. Even if this has been established elsewhere, it is essential to cite it and, for a self-contained paper, to at least sketch the argument.
*   **Unclear Complexity Claim:** The paper claims a complexity of O(µ d²), contrasting it with the standard CMA-ES. This comparison is somewhat misleading, as the reference implementation (CMA-ES-Ref) also achieves an amortized O(d²) complexity by delaying the expensive O(d³) decomposition. The authors should use more precise Θ-notation and clarify that their advantage lies in providing a consistent per-iteration quadratic cost, thereby avoiding the periodic cubic-time spikes of the delayed update strategy.
*   **Unvalidated Path-Error Claim:** A central justification for the method is that the approximation in the step-size adaptation path `p_σ` (replacing C⁻¹ᐟ² with A⁻¹) introduces a negligible error that decreases over time. This is a very strong claim, as the matrix square root is considered fundamental to the invariance properties of CMA-ES. The theoretical justification provided in Lemma 1 rests on the assumption that the covariance matrix shape converges, which is not guaranteed. This claim requires direct experimental validation. A simple comparison of final performance is insufficient; the authors should provide evidence, such as a plot of the error term over the course of a run, to demonstrate that the approximation path does not significantly diverge from the true path.
*   **Notation and Typos:** There are several minor errors that should be corrected: in Algorithm 2, `wk` should be `α_k`; in Table 1, the dimension header `n` should be `d`; on line 185, `F` should be `f`.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper addresses an important practical problem and demonstrates impressive performance gains, its significant weaknesses regarding originality and the lack of rigorous validation for its core claims are too substantial for acceptance at NIPS in its current form. The work feels more like an incremental engineering improvement than a fundamental scientific contribution at this stage.

Therefore, my recommendation is to **reject** the paper.

To improve the paper for a future submission, the authors should focus on:
1.  Clarifying the novelty of their contribution relative to Krause and Igel (2015).
2.  Including a formal proof that their sequential update scheme is equivalent to the standard covariance matrix update.
3.  Providing direct empirical evidence to validate the claim that the error introduced by their approximation in the CSA path is negligible and diminishes over time.
4.  Refining the complexity analysis to provide a more accurate and transparent comparison with existing methods.
