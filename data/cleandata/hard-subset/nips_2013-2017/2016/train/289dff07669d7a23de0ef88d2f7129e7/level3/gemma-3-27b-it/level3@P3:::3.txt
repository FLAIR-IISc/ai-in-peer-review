This paper proposes the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a modification of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that utilizes a Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix instead of the matrix square root, aiming to reduce computational complexity and memory requirements. The core idea is to update the triangular Cholesky factor directly, leveraging previous work by Krause and Igel (2015) and extending it to the full CMA-ES framework. The authors provide a theoretical justification based on the convergence of rotation matrices and empirical results demonstrating comparable performance with reduced runtime.

The CMA-ES is a well-established, powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001; Beyer, 2007). Existing strategies to address the computational cost of covariance matrix updates include delaying updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) or restricting model complexity (Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008). More recent approaches focus on updating the Cholesky factor directly (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015). This work builds upon this line of research, specifically extending the rank-1 update approach of Krause & Igel (2015) to the standard CMA-ES.

**Strengths:**

*   **Practical Improvement:** The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES demonstrably reduces runtime, particularly in higher dimensions, without sacrificing performance in terms of function evaluations. This is a valuable contribution for applications where computational cost is a bottleneck.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a theoretical argument based on Lemma 1, connecting the convergence of rotation matrices to the validity of approximating the step-size adaptation. Although relying on an assumption (covariance convergence), this provides a reasonable basis for the proposed modification.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core ideas and algorithmic details. Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 provide a concise description of the method.
*   **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors conduct experiments on a range of benchmark functions and dimensionality levels, providing a reasonably comprehensive evaluation of the algorithm’s performance.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The primary weakness of this paper is its limited novelty. The extension of Krause and Igel (2015) to the full CMA-ES, while not trivial, feels incremental. The authors acknowledge this, but the contribution doesn’t represent a significant leap forward. The core idea of updating the Cholesky factor directly was already explored in prior work.
*   **Reliance on Assumption:** While the theoretical justification is present, the reliance on the assumption of covariance convergence (mentioned in the discussion of Lemma 1) feels insufficiently supported within the paper. Citing Beyer (2014) would strengthen this argument and acknowledge relevant work on convergence analysis.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:**  The paper contains a minor error in Eq. (1) where σ should be \tilde{σ} (also present in footnote 2 on p.3). Additionally, a typographical error exists in line 94 ("which can by be motivated…"), which should be “which can be motivated…”. These are easily fixable but detract from the paper's polish.
*   **Discussion of Alternative Methods:** The comparison to Suttorp et al. (2009) feels somewhat superficial. A more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs between the Cholesky-CMA-ES and other Cholesky-based approaches would be beneficial.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear description of the algorithm and supporting theoretical analysis. The experimental results are well-presented and support the claims made by the authors. However, the limited novelty and reliance on an assumption affect the overall quality.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its underlying principles.

**Originality:** The originality is limited. The paper extends existing work in a reasonable manner but does not introduce fundamentally new concepts.

**Significance:** The significance of the work is moderate. The improved runtime efficiency is valuable, but the core idea is not groundbreaking. The paper is unlikely to radically change the field, but it provides a useful optimization for practical CMA-ES implementations.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Offers a practical speedup for CMA-ES, especially in higher dimensions.
*   Provides theoretical justification (albeit based on an assumption).
*   Well-written and thoroughly evaluated.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty; extension of existing work.
*   Theoretical justification relies on an unsupported assumption.
*   Minor technical errors.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a useful improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm. While the novelty is limited, the practical benefits of reduced runtime are significant, particularly for large-scale optimization problems. Addressing the concerns regarding the theoretical assumption and correcting the minor technical errors would strengthen the paper. I lean towards **acceptance**, but with the expectation that the authors will address the weaknesses outlined above.
