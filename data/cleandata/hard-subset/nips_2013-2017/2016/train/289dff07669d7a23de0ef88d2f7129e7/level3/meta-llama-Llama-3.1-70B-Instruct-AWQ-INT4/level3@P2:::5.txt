This paper proposes a modified version of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) algorithm, called Cholesky-CMA-ES, which reduces the runtime complexity of the original algorithm from O(d^3) to O(μ d^2) by maintaining and updating a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix. The main difference between the proposed algorithm and the original CMA-ES is the omission of estimating a random orthogonal matrix E_t at each iteration.

The authors provide a theoretical justification for the convergence of the new algorithm under certain restrictive assumptions, showing that the error caused by replacing the full covariance matrix with the Cholesky factor decreases over time as the algorithm converges to the optimum. They also present empirical results on standard benchmarks, demonstrating a substantial speed-up of the proposed algorithm compared to two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that delay full covariance updates, without sacrificing optimization performance.

The paper makes a solid contribution to speeding up CMA-ES, which is useful when evaluations are cheap and dimensionality is high. However, the practical impact of this speed-up may be limited in most real-world problems, where objective evaluation time dominates and typical dimensions are modest.

A major concern is that the theoretical assumptions are restrictive, and formal guarantees are limited. To address this, it would be useful to track the evolution of E_t (e.g., in various norms) by running full CMA-ES and the approximation simultaneously. Additionally, reporting actual wall-clock runtimes and performance of full CMA-ES without delay strategies would provide a more complete picture.

To further evaluate the proposed algorithm, it would be interesting to run experiments on quadratics with continuously varied spectra (cf. Stich & Müller) to study solution quality and E_t error versus spectral distribution. Moreover, including a real-world experiment where speed matters (e.g., real-time robotics policy gradients) would demonstrate the practical relevance of the proposed algorithm.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed algorithm and its theoretical justification. The empirical results are also clearly presented, although some additional plots and tables would be helpful to illustrate the performance of the proposed algorithm in more detail.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of optimization, and the proposed algorithm has the potential to be useful in certain applications. However, further experiments and analysis are needed to fully understand the strengths and limitations of the proposed algorithm.
