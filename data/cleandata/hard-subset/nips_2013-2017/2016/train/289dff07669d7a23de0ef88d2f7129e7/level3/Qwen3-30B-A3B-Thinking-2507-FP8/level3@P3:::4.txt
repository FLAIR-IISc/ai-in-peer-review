

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the standard matrix square root computation with a triangular Cholesky factorization. The authors demonstrate that this modification reduces the computational complexity of covariance matrix updates from O(d^3) to O(d^2) while maintaining the same number of objective function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES. Previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) explored Cholesky factorization for specific CMA-ES variants but did not provide theoretical justification for why their approaches would maintain performance. This paper addresses this gap with a rigorous theoretical analysis.

The main contribution is the theoretical justification that the Cholesky-based approach does not degrade algorithmic performance. The authors provide Lemma 1 showing that the error introduced by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} (where A is the Cholesky factor) vanishes as the algorithm converges, due to the exponential weighting in the cumulative step-size adaptation. This is a significant advancement over previous attempts at Cholesky-based CMA-ES variants.

The paper demonstrates that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same performance as standard CMA-ES in terms of objective function evaluations while significantly reducing wall-clock time, with the speedup growing as dimensionality increases. The authors provide comprehensive experiments across multiple benchmark functions and dimensions (d = 4 to 256), showing that for d = 64, Cholesky-CMA-ES is already 20 times faster than the reference implementation. The theoretical analysis is supported by empirical results, and the implementation is made publicly available in the Shark library.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for why the Cholesky-based approach does not degrade performance, which is a major contribution over previous work.
2. The runtime complexity is shown to be asymptotically optimal (O(µd^2)) for covariance updates, with minimal memory requirements.
3. The implementation is well-executed, with clear comparisons to standard CMA-ES variants and thorough experimental validation.
4. The paper highlights additional benefits of the Cholesky factorization, such as obtaining eigenvalues for free and enabling numerically stable inverse computation.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper could provide a clearer theoretical explanation of why the proposed computation is "numerically more stable" as requested in the reviewer comments. While the authors mention numerical stability, they do not provide a detailed analysis of the error bounds or compare condition numbers between the standard and Cholesky-based approaches.
2. The connection to low-dimensional approximation methods (Loshchilov, 2015) is mentioned but not explored in depth. The authors suggest combining Cholesky-CMA-ES with such approaches for handling extremely high-dimensional problems, but this remains a promising direction for future work rather than a demonstrated contribution.
3. The paper could better explain how the error introduced by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} diminishes over time, particularly for non-convergent or unstable optimization trajectories.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough experimental validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach and provide a clear comparison to related work. The experimental setup is appropriate and the results are convincing.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical justification to experimental validation. The algorithm descriptions are precise, and the implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproduction.

Originality: This work makes a novel contribution by providing a theoretical justification for using Cholesky factorization in standard CMA-ES, which previous work lacked. While Cholesky-based approaches have been explored for specific CMA-ES variants, this paper extends them to the standard CMA-ES with theoretical guarantees.

Significance: The paper addresses a significant computational bottleneck in CMA-ES, which is widely used in machine learning applications. The demonstrated speedups with increasing dimensionality make this work particularly valuable for real-world applications where computational efficiency is critical. The approach should be adopted whenever updating and storing the covariance matrix is feasible, which covers many important applications.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper makes a significant contribution to a widely used optimization algorithm with clear theoretical justification, experimental validation, and practical implementation. The runtime improvements are substantial and grow with problem dimensionality, making this work particularly relevant for modern machine learning applications.

Arguments against acceptance: The paper's main weakness is the lack of detailed explanation regarding numerical stability, which is a key claim. However, this is a minor issue that could be addressed in a revision, and the overall contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The Cholesky-CMA-ES should replace standard CMA-ES whenever computational efficiency is a concern, and the theoretical justification provided is a significant advancement over previous work. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
