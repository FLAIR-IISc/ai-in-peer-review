Summary and relation to prior work
The paper targets a well-known bottleneck in CMA-ES: updating and decomposing the full covariance matrix, which often dominates runtime and memory for medium-to-high dimensional problems. The authors propose to maintain the triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance C = AAT and to apply covariance updates via a sequence of rank-one updates to A, yielding an O(µ d^2) update per iteration (with µ = O(log d) typically). The key algorithmic change for standard CMA-ES is to modify CSA: replace C−1/2 by A−1 in the normalized path pσ, arguing that the induced error is a rotation that vanishes as the algorithm stabilizes. A lemma formalizes this by showing that successive rotations approach the identity when the normalized shape of C converges, so the norm used by CSA is asymptotically unaffected. Empirically, the method maintains the same function-evaluation efficiency as standard CMA-ES while achieving large wall-clock speedups and lower memory use.

This builds on prior Cholesky-based CMA-ES variants that avoided recomputing decompositions (e.g., non-triangular factor updates in Suttorp et al., 2009; applications to (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES), and on a more efficient rank-one update for the (1+1) setting (Krause & Igel, 2015). It also complements lower-complexity approximations (diagonal/separable CMA, NES variants, limited-memory CMA-ES; Ros & Hansen, 2008; Sun et al., 2013; Loshchilov, 2014, 2015), which trade model richness for scalability.

Strengths
- Practical impact: For full-covariance CMA-ES, the proposed factor-update reduces wall-clock time substantially without sacrificing evaluations to solution, and saves memory by storing only the triangular factor.
- Sound implementation detail: The rank-one update for triangular factors is clear and uses only O(d^2) time and storage; inversion of A is also O(d^2).
- Theoretical rationale: The rotation-vanishing argument for replacing C−1/2 with A−1 in CSA is simple and persuasive; it isolates the approximation to CSA while preserving the core covariance adaptation.
- Empirical results: On standard benchmarks across d up to 256, evaluation counts match the reference CMA-ES, with strong runtime gains that grow with d. Rotation invariance is probed.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Naming conflict: “Cholesky-CMA-ES” has been used in the literature (Suttorp et al., 2009) for a different factorization/update scheme. Please adopt a distinct name (e.g., Triangular-Cholesky-CMA-ES, tChol-CMA-ES) and clarify the relationship early.
- Incomplete prior-work positioning: The relation to Suttorp et al. (non-triangular factor) and to Krause & Igel (2015) (efficient rank-one update in (1+1)-CMA-ES) needs a crisper algorithmic comparison. What exactly prevents a similar CSA modification in Suttorp’s setup? What is new beyond (i) looping rankOneUpdate across the rank-µ terms and (ii) the CSA approximation with analysis? Please make this explicit.
- Experimental baselines: While a Suttorp-style baseline is included, the empirical comparison remains thin. Please:
  - Ensure parameter parity and report per-iteration computational cost breakdowns (sampling, ranking, factor update, inversion).
  - Include Krause & Igel’s update where applicable (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES) to isolate the benefit of the CSA modification and triangular constraint.
  - Add separable variants (sep-CMA-ES) and LM-CMA-ES on separable or near-separable problems to contextualize the “optimality” claim.
- “Optimality” is overstated: The update/storage is asymptotically optimal among full-covariance adaptations, but not globally optimal for all problem classes (e.g., Sphere and separable problems where diagonal models suffice). Please temper claims and specify the regime of interest (ill-conditioned, non-separable targets where full covariance is beneficial).
- Numerical stability claim: The paper states that Suttorp’s approach is numerically unstable, while the original reports stability. Please substantiate with empirical evidence: failure rates, divergence, condition numbers, and sensitivity to finite precision; otherwise soften this statement.
- Scalability: Complexity remains quadratic in d; this does not solve truly large-scale CMA-ES. The paper should frame the method as the preferred full-covariance variant up to the regime where O(d^2) is still feasible, and defer beyond that to low-rank/limited-memory approaches.
- Plotting/analysis details: The visible difference on Cigar’s plateau could be an artifact of median-of-runs plotting or success-run selection. Please clarify statistics (mean/median, success criteria, handling of timeouts), and provide confidence intervals. Consider showing evaluation-based progress as well as wall-clock time.
- Minor: Many implementations decompose periodically rather than every iteration; while you adopt a delayed decomposition baseline, clarify this point earlier to avoid implying that EVD is always performed every iteration in practice.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The CSA approximation and its justification are the main nontrivial element; a more quantitative bound on the induced error (e.g., in terms of recent rotation drift) would strengthen the theory. Experimental methodology is solid but needs broader baselines and deeper analysis of numerical stability and cost breakdowns.

Clarity
Generally well written. Algorithmic presentation is clear. Please resolve the naming issue and sharpen the related-work section to position the contribution precisely. Add explicit statements that the main algorithmic change beyond standard CMA-ES is (i) the triangular rank-µ factor updates and (ii) the CSA modification.

Originality
Incremental on the covariance update side (looping rank-one factor updates is a natural extension of prior work), with more novelty in adapting CSA without C−1/2 and arguing its benign impact. The contribution is practical but not conceptually radical.

Significance
High practical significance for users who need full covariance on medium to large d; the speedups are compelling. Limited significance for truly large-scale optimization where O(d^2) is already prohibitive.

Pros for acceptance
- Meaningful speed and memory improvements for full-covariance CMA-ES with unchanged evaluation efficiency.
- Simple, robust implementation; easy to adopt in existing codebases.
- Plausible theoretical explanation for CSA modification.

Cons against acceptance
- Naming conflict and incomplete positioning vs. Suttorp (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015).
- Overstated optimality and unsubstantiated numerical-stability claims.
- Missing baselines/ablations to fully support the empirical case, especially on separable/large-scale regimes and (1+1) settings.

Questions for authors
- Can you report quantitative drift of Et (rotation) over time and correlate it with CSA error and performance?
- How often do you recompute or reorthogonalize A (if ever) to mitigate numerical drift?
- What are the precise conditions under which the Suttorp baseline becomes unstable in your implementation?

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The contribution is primarily engineering but addresses a pain point for CMA-ES practitioners with strong empirical benefit. I would support acceptance if the final version resolves the naming, tempers claims, and adds the requested comparisons and analyses.
