

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the computationally expensive matrix square root in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism with a triangular Cholesky factor. The authors theoretically justify that this modification preserves the algorithm's behavior in terms of required objective function evaluations while reducing both time and space complexity for covariance updates from O(d³) to O(µd²) (with µ = O(log d)). Crucially, the Cholesky factorization provides eigenvalues of the covariance matrix at no extra cost, enabling better monitoring of optimization conditioning. The paper is well-positioned within NIPS' scope: CMA-ES is a widely used derivative-free optimizer in machine learning (e.g., for reinforcement learning policy search and hyperparameter tuning), and the work extends foundational information-geometric principles from Akimoto et al. (2012) and Hansen (2015).

**Quality**: The theoretical contribution is strong. Lemma 1 rigorously demonstrates that the error from replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ vanishes as the algorithm converges, leveraging the continuity of the RQ-decomposition. The empirical evaluation is thorough: 100 trials across 8 benchmark functions (including rotated, non-separable, and ill-conditioned problems) for dimensions d=4 to 256, with comparisons against state-of-the-art variants (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES). Results confirm no degradation in function evaluations (Fig. 1) and significant wall-clock speedups (20× at d=64, Fig. 2), with the gap widening for larger d. The implementation in Shark with LAPACK ensures high efficiency, though minor clarification is needed on whether all variants use identical codebases (e.g., CMA-ES-Ref’s delay strategy vs. Cholesky-CMA-ES’s full update).

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written. Algorithms 1–2 are precise, the theoretical justification is logically developed, and the experimental design (including rotation invariance tests) is transparent. The connection between Cholesky factors and eigenvalue monitoring is clearly articulated. A minor improvement could be explicitly addressing why Figure 2 shows less pronounced speedups for Cigar/Discus at d=256 (likely due to L2 cache effects, as noted in the paper, but not elaborated).

**Originality**: The work builds on Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015) but makes a critical advance: extending Cholesky-based updates to the *full* CMA-ES with theoretical guarantees, rather than restricted cases like (1+1)-CMA-ES. The key novelty is the CSA modification that preserves convergence properties while optimizing complexity—this is distinct from prior approaches that either delayed updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) or restricted model complexity (Ros & Hansen, 2008). The authors adequately contextualize their contribution against NIPS proceedings (e.g., Loshchilov, 2014, 2015).

**Significance**: The contribution is highly significant for practical ML applications. CMA-ES is a workhorse for derivative-free optimization in RL and hyperparameter tuning, but its O(d³) covariance updates limit scalability. Cholesky-CMA-ES enables efficient scaling to higher dimensions (e.g., for neural network training) without sacrificing optimization quality. The quadratic speedup (Fig. 2) directly addresses a bottleneck in real-world deployments. This is a candidate default for medium/large-scale CMA-ES due to its clear practical advantages, advancing the state of the art in optimization algorithms.

**Strengths**:  
- Theoretical justification for CSA modification (Lemma 1) is rigorous and novel.  
- Empirical results confirm no function-evaluation degradation and substantial speedups.  
- Eigenvalue extraction via Cholesky factors provides a valuable diagnostic tool.  
- Well-motivated by real-world ML applications (RL, hyperparameter tuning).  

**Weaknesses**:  
- Minor implementation detail: Clarify whether CMA-ES-Ref and Cholesky-CMA-ES use identical codebases beyond the covariance update (e.g., same LAPACK calls, random seed handling).  
- Figure 2’s Cigar/Discus behavior at d=256 merits a brief note (e.g., "cache effects dominate at this dimensionality, masking the asymptotic speedup").  

**Conclusion**: This is a high-quality, impactful contribution to optimization algorithms for machine learning. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and demonstrates significant practical value. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES should replace standard CMA-ES in applications where covariance update costs are prohibitive. The minor suggestions do not detract from the paper’s strong merits. I recommend acceptance as a top-tier contribution to NIPS 2016.
