

This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy that replaces the standard matrix square root computation with triangular Cholesky factor updates. The authors build upon previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), but provide a theoretically grounded approach that avoids deteriorating the algorithm's performance while achieving optimal O(μd²) time complexity for covariance updates. The key innovation lies in their justification for replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, supported by Lemma 1 showing the error vanishes as the algorithm converges.

The paper excels in quality and significance. The theoretical analysis is sound, with a clear demonstration that the modification does not increase function evaluations (empirical results confirm this across benchmark functions), while delivering substantial wall-clock speedups—particularly for high-dimensional problems (20× faster at d=64). The approach also yields covariance eigenvalues for free, a valuable practical benefit. The paper is well-written and thoroughly evaluated, with comprehensive experiments on standard benchmark functions across dimensions up to 256.

The work demonstrates strong originality by addressing a fundamental computational bottleneck in CMA-ES with a theoretically justified, practically effective solution. It advances the state of the art in derivative-free optimization with clear practical advantages, making it highly relevant to ML, reinforcement learning, and information geometry communities.

Minor concerns: The implementation details for CPU comparisons should be clarified to ensure fair comparisons (e.g., same language/implementation). Additionally, Figure 2's limited differences for Cigar/Discus at d=256 warrants explanation, as these functions are particularly sensitive to covariance adaptation.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that should be accepted. The paper provides a theoretically sound, practically superior alternative to standard CMA-ES with no performance penalty in function evaluations, making it a compelling candidate for default use in medium/large-scale applications. The minor suggestions do not detract from the paper's substantial merits.
