### NIPS 2016 Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that reduces the computational complexity of the covariance matrix update from O(d^3) to O(µd^2). The core idea is to maintain and directly update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, rather than forming the full matrix and re-decomposing it in every iteration. The main challenge lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the inverse matrix square root C^(-1/2) for normalization. The authors propose approximating this term with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor A^(-1). They provide a theoretical justification for this approximation, arguing that under the assumption of convergence of the covariance matrix's shape, the orthogonal matrix E_t in the decomposition C^(1/2) = A_t * E_t converges to the identity matrix, making the approximation asymptotically exact. The paper's empirical evaluation on standard benchmark functions demonstrates a significant wall-clock time speed-up compared to state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants with delayed updates, without any discernible loss in optimization performance in terms of function evaluations.

This work builds upon a line of research aimed at improving the scalability of CMA-ES. It directly addresses the computational bottleneck identified in the original work (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001), offering a more efficient alternative to the commonly used strategy of delaying covariance updates. It also extends previous work on Cholesky updates (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) by applying it to the full (µ,λ)-CMA-ES and providing a novel justification for the necessary modification to the CSA step.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Performance Improvement:** The paper presents a solid and practical contribution by substantially speeding up a widely-used and powerful optimization algorithm. Reducing the complexity from O(d^3) to O(µd^2) makes CMA-ES more viable for problems where function evaluations are computationally cheap, but the dimensionality is high (e.g., d > 50).
2.  **Well-Justified Approximation:** The key modification—approximating C^(-1/2) with A^(-1)—is not merely presented as a heuristic. The authors provide a plausible theoretical argument (Lemma 1) that under certain convergence assumptions, the error introduced by this approximation should vanish. This provides a principled foundation for the proposed algorithm.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The convergence plots (function value vs. evaluations) clearly show that the Cholesky-CMA-ES performs identically to the reference implementation. The runtime plots demonstrate the dramatic speed-up, which aligns perfectly with the theoretical complexity analysis. The comparison against two relevant baselines (CMA-ES with different delay strategies) effectively highlights the superiority of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses and Concerns**

1.  **Limited Practical Impact in Many ML Scenarios:** The primary weakness is that the addressed problem—the internal algorithmic complexity of CMA-ES—is often not the main bottleneck in real-world machine learning applications. For tasks like hyperparameter tuning of large models or direct policy search in complex simulations, the objective function evaluation cost dominates by several orders of magnitude. In such cases, the speed-up from O(d^3) to O(d^2) would have a negligible impact on the total wall-clock time. The paper would be stronger if it acknowledged this limitation more explicitly.
2.  **Restrictive Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical justification relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix C̄t converges. While this is a reasonable intuition, it is a strong assumption for a stochastic algorithm for which formal convergence proofs are still an open area of research. The provided argument does not offer a formal guarantee or a bound on the error introduced by the approximation, especially during the transient phases of the optimization where the covariance matrix may change rapidly.
3.  **Need for Deeper Experimental Analysis:** While the current experiments are good, they could be extended to provide more insight and strengthen the paper's claims.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

To further strengthen the paper, I would suggest the following:

1.  **Directly Validate the Core Assumption:** The central theoretical claim is that the orthogonal matrix E_t approaches the identity matrix. This could be tested empirically. I suggest running the full CMA-ES (with its O(d^3) eigenvalue decomposition) and simultaneously tracking the evolution of E_t from the RQ decomposition of C_t^(1/2). Plotting a suitable norm (e.g., ||E_t - I||_F or ||E_t * E_{t-1}^T - I||_F) over the course of an optimization run would provide direct evidence for or against the paper's central justification.
2.  **Expand Baseline Comparisons:** For completeness, it would be valuable to report the performance and actual wall-clock runtimes of the "vanilla" full CMA-ES, which performs the O(d^3) decomposition at every single iteration without any delay strategies. This would provide a clearer baseline for the full extent of the speed-up.
3.  **Investigate the Impact of Problem Structure:** The approximation's quality might depend on the problem's structure (e.g., its conditioning). An experiment on quadratic functions with continuously varied eigenvalue spectra (as in Stich & Müller, 2012) could be very insightful. This would allow for a systematic study of how the approximation error and solution quality correlate with the spectral distribution of the Hessian.
4.  **Include a Real-World Use Case:** The paper's significance would be greatly enhanced by a demonstration on a real-world problem where the algorithmic speed-up truly matters. An example could be a problem from reinforcement learning or robotics (e.g., direct policy search for a controller with many parameters) where evaluations are fast enough for the O(d^2) complexity to make a tangible difference in training time.

**Detailed Comments on Review Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed algorithm is well-described, and the experimental results robustly support the primary claims of speed-up without performance loss on the tested benchmarks. The main weakness is the limited scope of the theoretical guarantees.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is strong, the background is concise, and the proposed method is explained logically. The figures and tables effectively communicate the results.
*   **Originality:** While building on prior work on Cholesky updates, the paper's main original contribution is the successful adaptation to the standard (µ,λ)-CMA-ES via a novel and theoretically-justified modification to the CSA mechanism. This is a non-trivial and important step.
*   **Significance:** The paper is a significant contribution to the evolutionary computation community. Cholesky-CMA-ES is likely to become the default implementation for high-dimensional problems where function evaluations are cheap. Its broader significance to the NIPS community is slightly more limited due to the dominance of function evaluation costs in many machine learning applications, but it remains a valuable algorithmic improvement for a cornerstone black-box optimizer.
