## Review of “CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity”

This paper presents a compelling modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) by employing a triangular Cholesky factorization to avoid the computationally expensive square root operation during covariance matrix updates. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that this Cholesky-based approach maintains the algorithmic performance of standard CMA-ES while significantly reducing runtime, particularly in higher dimensions. The authors provide a theoretical justification for this claim, arguing that the smoothness of the Cholesky factor ensures minimal impact on the algorithm’s behavior. 

The work builds upon prior attempts to optimize CMA-ES’s computational cost, like delaying updates or using low-rank approximations, but distinguishes itself by offering a theoretically grounded and practically effective alternative.  Previous works (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) explored Cholesky factorizations, but this paper extends the approach to the full, standard CMA-ES, addressing limitations of prior methods. The paper clearly articulates the advantages – reduced complexity, lower memory footprint, and maintained performance – and positions itself well within the landscape of derivative-free optimization techniques.

A key strength is the empirical validation. The experiments demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable function evaluation counts to the standard CMA-ES, while delivering substantial speedups, which grow with dimensionality.  The clear presentation of results, including the runtime comparisons in Figures 1 & 2, is highly effective.  

However, the theoretical justification regarding “numerical stability” could benefit from further elaboration. While the authors mention the smoothness of the Cholesky factor, a more detailed explanation of *why* this translates to improved or comparable numerical stability, especially in ill-conditioned problems, would strengthen the argument.  

Furthermore, while the runtime improvements are significant, the authors acknowledge the quadratic scaling with dimensionality. The suggestion to combine this method with low-dimensional approximations (Loshchilov, 2015) for very high-dimensional problems like neural network training is insightful and should be explicitly discussed as future work. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Significant runtime improvement over standard CMA-ES without sacrificing performance.
*   Well-grounded theoretical justification for the proposed modification.
*   Clear and comprehensive experimental evaluation.
*   Valuable implementation contributing to the CMA-ES toolkit.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The explanation of improved numerical stability could be more detailed.
*   The quadratic scaling with dimensionality limits applicability to extremely high-dimensional problems.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a practical and theoretically sound improvement to a widely used algorithm. I recommend acceptance.
