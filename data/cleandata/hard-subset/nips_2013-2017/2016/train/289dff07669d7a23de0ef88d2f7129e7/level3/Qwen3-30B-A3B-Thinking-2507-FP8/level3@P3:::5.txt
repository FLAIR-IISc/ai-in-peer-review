

# Review: CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that approximates the standard algorithm using a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix. The authors demonstrate that maintaining and updating the Cholesky factor rather than the full covariance matrix reduces computational complexity from O(d³) to O(μd²) per iteration (with μ typically O(log d)), while maintaining equivalent optimization performance. This work extends prior attempts at Cholesky-based CMA-ES variants (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) by providing a theoretical justification for why omitting the orthogonal matrix E_t in the cumulative step-size adaptation does not degrade performance.

The key insight is that E_t converges to the identity matrix under restrictive convergence assumptions, making the Cholesky approximation asymptotically equivalent to the standard CMA-ES. The authors prove this convergence property (Lemma 1) and provide empirical results showing that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same number of function evaluations as standard CMA-ES but with substantially reduced runtime, especially for high-dimensional problems (e.g., 20x faster at d=64). The paper compares against two state-of-the-art baselines: CMA-ES-Ref (with delayed matrix square root computation) and CMA-ES/d (delaying updates every d steps).

**Strengths:**
1. **Solid technical contribution:** The paper successfully reduces CMA-ES's computational complexity while maintaining optimization performance, which is valuable for high-dimensional problems where covariance updates dominate runtime.
2. **Theoretical justification:** The authors provide a convergence argument for the approximation, which is stronger than previous heuristic approaches.
3. **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-structured with clear pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and thorough experimental evaluation on standard benchmarks.
4. **Practical utility:** For problems with cheap objective evaluations and high dimensionality (e.g., certain neural network training scenarios), this method offers significant speed improvements.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Restrictive theoretical assumptions:** The convergence claim relies on strong assumptions about covariance matrix convergence (Lemma 1), which may not hold in practice for many optimization problems.
2. **Limited practical impact:** The paper acknowledges that in most real-world applications, objective evaluation time dominates, and typical dimensions are modest (d < 100), making the speed-up less relevant. The authors do not provide compelling evidence of real-world scenarios where this speed-up matters.
3. **Incomplete empirical evaluation:** The authors should have reported actual wall-clock runtimes for standard CMA-ES without delay strategies (CMA-ES-Ref) for comparison. The suggested experiments (tracking E_t evolution, testing quadratics with varied spectra, and real-world robotics experiments) would strengthen the evaluation.
4. **Overstated significance:** The paper claims "optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity," but this is only optimal when full covariance updates are required, which is not the case for many practical problems where dimensionality reduction is preferred.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported empirical results, though the theoretical justification is limited by restrictive assumptions. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, but could have provided more thorough analysis of the approximation error.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the algorithm and its theoretical basis. The pseudocode is particularly helpful for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by providing a theoretically justified extension of Cholesky-based CMA-ES to the standard algorithm, distinguishing it from prior work that either required delayed updates or lacked theoretical justification.

**Significance:** The contribution is significant for specific high-dimensional optimization scenarios where covariance updates dominate, but the practical impact is limited due to the dominance of objective evaluation time in most real-world applications. The method is most relevant for synthetic tests or extremely high-dimensional problems (d > 100) with cheap evaluations, which are not common in typical machine learning applications.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Provides a theoretically grounded method for reducing CMA-ES complexity
- Demonstrates significant speed-up without performance loss on standard benchmarks
- Offers a useful tool for high-dimensional optimization when covariance updates matter
- Clear presentation and well-structured experiments

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Theoretical assumptions are too restrictive for practical guarantees
- Limited practical impact given the dominance of objective evaluation time in real applications
- Lacks compelling real-world examples where speed matters
- Does not address the most common use cases of CMA-ES in machine learning

Overall, this is a solid technical contribution that advances the state of the art in CMA-ES optimization for specific scenarios. While the practical impact may be limited, the theoretical justification and performance gains on high-dimensional problems make this a valuable addition to the literature. The paper would benefit from more thorough empirical evaluation addressing the suggested experiments, but as presented, it makes a meaningful contribution to the optimization literature. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the method's practical utility is context-dependent.
