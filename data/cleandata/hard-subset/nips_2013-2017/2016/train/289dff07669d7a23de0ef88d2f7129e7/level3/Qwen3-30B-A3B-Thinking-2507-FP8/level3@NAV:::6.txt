

This paper proposes a "Cholesky-CMA-ES" variant claiming quadratic-time covariance updates with minimal memory. While the core idea—using Cholesky factors instead of matrix square roots—is technically sound, the work suffers from critical flaws that undermine its contribution and validity.  

**Major issues:**  
1. **Misleading naming**: The term "Cholesky-CMA-ES" directly conflicts with Suttorp et al. (2009), who first introduced Cholesky-based CMA-ES. The paper must rename to avoid confusion (e.g., "Loop-Cholesky-CMA-ES" or similar).  
2. **Missing prior comparison**: The paper omits empirical comparison with Suttorp (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015), despite claiming to extend their work. Figure 1’s experiments exclude these baselines, making performance claims unsubstantiated.  
3. **Overstated claims**: The paper asserts "optimal" complexity and performance, but:  
   - The method is *not* optimal for separable functions (e.g., Sphere), where diagonal covariance suffices.  
   - The runtime saving (~2×) over Suttorp’s method is marginal, and the approach *does not solve* scalability (quadratic complexity remains).  
4. **Contradicted stability claim**: The paper falsely claims Suttorp’s method is "numerically unstable," despite Suttorp et al. (2009) explicitly demonstrating stability.  

**Minor concerns**:  
- The Cigar function plateau discrepancy likely stems from median-plotting artifacts (needs clarification).  
- Eigenvalue decomposition is *not* performed at every update (typical implementations use periodic updates).  
- The "novelty" is merely looping `rankOneUpdate` (Algorithm 1), which is algorithmically trivial.  

**Strengths**: Clear theoretical justification for the step-size adaptation approximation, and well-structured algorithm description.  

**Recommendation**: **Reject**. The paper fails to properly situate its work against existing literature (Suttorp 2009, Krause 2015), omits critical experiments, and makes unsubstantiated claims about optimality and stability. The contribution is incremental at best—replacing one for-loop in a known technique without empirical validation against the cited work. To salvage the paper, the authors must: (1) rename the method, (2) run experiments against Suttorp and Krause variants, (3) revise claims to reflect the marginal gains (e.g., "reduces runtime by ~2× for full covariance updates"), and (4) clarify the stability argument. Without these fixes, the paper does not advance the state of the art.
