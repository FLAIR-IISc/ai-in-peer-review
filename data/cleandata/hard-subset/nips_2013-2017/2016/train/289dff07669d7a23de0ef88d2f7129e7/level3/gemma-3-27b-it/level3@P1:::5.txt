## Review of CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

This paper presents the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel approximation to the standard Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) designed to reduce computational complexity, particularly in high-dimensional optimization problems. The core idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix instead of the full matrix or its square root, leading to a time complexity of O(μd<sup>2</sup>) compared to the standard CMA-ES’s O(d<sup>3</sup>).  The authors provide a theoretical justification for this approximation based on the convergence of a related orthogonal matrix, and demonstrate significant speedups on standard benchmark functions.

The CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization and has seen widespread application in areas like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001; Gomez et al., 2008). Existing efforts to reduce the computational cost of covariance matrix updates have included delayed updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) and restricted model complexity (Ros & Hansen, 2008), each with trade-offs. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES builds upon prior work utilizing Cholesky factorization (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) but crucially addresses the incompatibility with the standard CMA-ES’s reliance on the matrix square root.  The presented approach offers a theoretically grounded alternative that aims to preserve performance while drastically reducing computational cost.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-defined algorithm and provides a clear theoretical motivation for its approximation. The connection to Cholesky decomposition and the discussion of the orthogonal matrix E<sub>t</sub> provide a solid foundation.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The reduction in computational complexity from O(d<sup>3</sup>) to O(μd<sup>2</sup>) is a significant advancement, particularly for high-dimensional problems. This is a well-justified and potentially impactful improvement.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results demonstrate substantial speedups on a range of benchmark functions, supporting the claim that the Cholesky-CMA-ES maintains comparable performance to the standard CMA-ES in terms of convergence, with clear gains in runtime.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and algorithmic steps. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Restrictive Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical justification hinges on the convergence of the orthogonal matrix E<sub>t</sub> to the identity matrix. This convergence is argued under restrictive conditions and may not hold for all optimization landscapes. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but a more thorough discussion of the conditions under which the approximation is most accurate would be valuable.
* **Limited Real-World Impact:** While the speedup is significant, the paper acknowledges that in many real-world applications, the cost of objective function evaluation dominates runtime, diminishing the practical benefit. The current experimental setup focuses primarily on synthetic benchmarks.
* **Missing Baseline:**  The comparison lacks a direct performance measurement of the original CMA-ES *without* any delay strategies. Including this would provide a clearer picture of the true speedup achieved by the proposed method.
* **Incomplete Exploration of Error:** The paper argues for the validity of the approximation but doesn’t thoroughly explore the magnitude of the error introduced by neglecting the orthogonal matrix.  



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in the optimization community – improving the scalability of CMA-ES.
* The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a theoretically grounded and empirically validated approach to achieving this improvement.
* The algorithm is clearly presented and well-motivated.
* The potential impact on high-dimensional optimization problems is significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical assumptions underlying the approximation are restrictive, limiting the generality of the results.
* The practical benefit may be limited for applications where objective function evaluation is the dominant cost.
* The empirical evaluation could be strengthened by including additional baselines and exploring a wider range of test problems.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution that presents a valuable improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm.  The Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a promising approach to scaling CMA-ES to higher dimensions. While the theoretical limitations and potential for limited practical impact are concerns, the demonstrated speedup and clear presentation of the algorithm outweigh these weaknesses.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* **Investigate E<sub>t</sub> Evolution:**  Conduct experiments to track the evolution of the orthogonal matrix E<sub>t</sub> (e.g., by monitoring its Frobenius norm or spectral distribution) to provide empirical evidence for its convergence behavior. Running full CMA-ES and the approximation simultaneously would allow for a direct comparison.
* **Expand Baseline Comparisons:** Include the performance of the standard CMA-ES without delay strategies as a baseline.
* **Explore Problem Structure:** Test the algorithm on quadratics with continuously varied spectra (inspired by Stich & Müller, 2012) to assess solution quality and correlate it with the error in the approximation.
* **Real-World Application:** Demonstrate the benefits of Cholesky-CMA-ES on a real-world problem where computational efficiency is crucial (e.g., real-time robotics policy gradients).




**Confidence:** 8/10.  I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While further experimentation and analysis would strengthen the conclusions, the current work is well-executed and presents a compelling case for the proposed algorithm.
