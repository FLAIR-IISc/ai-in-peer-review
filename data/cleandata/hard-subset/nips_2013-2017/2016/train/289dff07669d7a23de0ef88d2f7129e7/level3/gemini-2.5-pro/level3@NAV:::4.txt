This paper introduces the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the well-established Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy that addresses its primary computational bottleneck. The main contribution is an efficient covariance matrix update scheme that replaces the expensive matrix square root computation (often an O(d^3) operation) with a direct update on the triangular Cholesky factor, achieving an optimal O(µd^2) time complexity. While others have explored Cholesky updates (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), this work provides a novel theoretical justification, arguing that the necessary modification to the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) does not degrade algorithmic performance in terms of function evaluations as the covariance matrix shape converges.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper presents a significant practical improvement to a widely used optimization algorithm. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate a substantial reduction in wall-clock time, with speed-ups growing with dimensionality, making CMA-ES more viable for moderately high-dimensional problems.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. The central claim—that runtime can be reduced without increasing the number of function evaluations—is well-supported by both a plausible theoretical argument (Lemma 1) and thorough experiments on benchmark functions.
*   **Clarity & Originality:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its novel contribution in the context of previous work. It successfully presents a complete and valuable implementation of Cholesky factorization for the standard CMA-ES.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**
*   The authors claim their method is "numerically more stable," but this point is not sufficiently elaborated. A clearer theoretical explanation or empirical evidence demonstrating this improved stability would strengthen the paper's claims.
*   While the proposed method effectively scales CMA-ES, its quadratic complexity remains a barrier for extremely high-dimensional problems. The conclusion could be strengthened by discussing future research directions, such as exploring a hybrid approach that combines this efficient update with low-dimensional approximation techniques like LM-CMA (Loshchilov, 2015) to better handle challenges like large-scale neural network training.

In summary, this is a strong paper presenting a well-motivated and empirically validated improvement to a key algorithm in the field. The reduction in computational overhead is a significant and highly useful contribution for both researchers and practitioners. I recommend acceptance.
