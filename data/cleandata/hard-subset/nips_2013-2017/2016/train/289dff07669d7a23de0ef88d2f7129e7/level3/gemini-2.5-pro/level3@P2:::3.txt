This paper proposes an efficient implementation of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm. The main computational bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES is the O(d^3) complexity of decomposing the d x d covariance matrix C in each iteration, which is necessary for sampling and for the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. The authors propose "Cholesky-CMA-ES", a variant that avoids this cost by directly maintaining and updating the triangular Cholesky factor A (where C = AA^T). This reduces the per-iteration complexity for the matrix update to O(µd^2), which is asymptotically optimal.

The key challenge addressed is that the CSA mechanism in the standard CMA-ES requires the inverse of the matrix square root (C^{-1/2}), which is not equivalent to the inverse of a Cholesky factor (A^{-1}). The authors' main contribution is to modify the CSA update by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} and providing a theoretical justification for this approximation. They argue via Lemma 1 that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the error introduced by this change (which amounts to a rotation) vanishes. This work builds upon prior efforts to use Cholesky updates for CMA-ES variants, such as Suttorp et al. (2009) for a non-triangular factorization and Krause & Igel (2015) for a rank-1 update on the triangular factor. This paper extends the update to the full rank-μ case (implemented as a series of rank-1 updates) and analyzes the crucial CSA approximation. The paper empirically demonstrates that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same performance as the standard algorithm in terms of function evaluations but is significantly faster in wall-clock time, especially for higher dimensions.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Practical Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known and important practical limitation of CMA-ES. The proposed algorithm provides a significant speed-up, making CMA-ES more scalable to higher-dimensional problems, particularly when the objective function evaluation is relatively cheap. Given the widespread use of CMA-ES in machine learning (e.g., for policy search and hyperparameter tuning), this is a valuable contribution that practitioners are likely to adopt.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. The proposed algorithm is well-described, and the complexity analysis is correct. The experimental validation is thorough, comparing the proposed method against relevant baselines on a standard set of benchmark functions across a range of dimensions. The results clearly support the authors' claims: the number of function evaluations remains unchanged, while the runtime is drastically reduced.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the problem with the standard CMA-ES, detail their proposed solution, and provide a lucid justification for the key approximation in the CSA mechanism. The inclusion of pseudocode and detailed experimental figures enhances the paper's clarity.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Originality:** The main weakness of the paper is its limited novelty. The idea of updating a Cholesky factor directly to improve CMA-ES efficiency is not new (Suttorp et al., 2009). More specifically, the quadratic-time rank-1 update for a *triangular* Cholesky factor was presented by Krause & Igel (2015). The current paper's primary contribution is extending this to the rank-μ update required by the standard CMA-ES and analyzing the resulting approximation in the CSA. While this is a necessary and useful extension for making the approach practical for the standard algorithm, the generalization of the rank-μ update as a sequence of rank-1 updates is relatively straightforward. The analysis of the CSA approximation is the most novel part, but it is more of a justification than a rigorous proof.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical argument in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the shape of the covariance matrix converges (i.e., C̄t → C̄). While this is a reasonable heuristic, the paper would be stronger if it discussed the conditions under which this assumption is expected to hold. Citing existing theoretical work that supports this convergence (e.g., Beyer, ECJ 2014, shows convergence of the expectation of C in simplified settings) would lend more weight to the authors' argument and increase confidence in the approximation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  The paper provides a significant practical improvement to a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a drop-in replacement for the standard implementation that offers substantial speed-ups.
2.  The work is a complete and high-quality contribution, including a clear algorithm description, theoretical justification, and extensive empirical validation.
3.  The results are convincing and will be of immediate use to practitioners and researchers using CMA-ES.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

1.  The novelty over recent work, particularly Krause & Igel (2015), is incremental. The core technical components (quadratic-time rank-one Cholesky updates) have been previously published.
2.  The theoretical justification for the CSA approximation relies on an unproven assumption of covariance matrix convergence.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a very good paper that presents a valuable and well-executed engineering contribution. While the conceptual novelty is somewhat limited, the practical significance is high. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a clear improvement over the standard implementation and should become the new default. The strengths in significance, quality, and clarity outweigh the weakness in originality. I recommend acceptance.
