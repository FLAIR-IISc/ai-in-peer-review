This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) aiming to reduce computational cost by replacing the standard covariance matrix update based on eigenvalue decomposition with an update using the Cholesky factorization. The core idea is to maintain and update the triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix, reducing runtime and memory complexity. The authors argue this modification doesn't significantly alter the algorithm’s performance, supported by empirical results.

The CMA-ES is a well-established derivative-free optimization algorithm, frequently employed in machine learning for tasks like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning.  The computational bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES lies in updating and storing the covariance matrix, typically requiring O(d^3) time. Previous attempts to address this include delayed updates or restricting model complexity, with varying degrees of success. This work builds on earlier efforts to use Cholesky factors but addresses a key limitation: the original CMA-ES relies on the matrix square root, which isn’t directly replaceable by a Cholesky factor. The authors cleverly modify the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism to accommodate this change, providing a theoretical justification for its convergence.

**Strengths:**

*   **Potential for Significant Speedup:** The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a potentially substantial improvement in runtime, particularly in higher dimensions. The theoretical analysis suggests a reduction in complexity to O(μd^2), which, while still quadratic, represents a significant gain over the cubic complexity of full covariance matrix updates.
*   **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, method, and experimental setup. The explanation of the Cholesky decomposition and its relation to the covariance matrix is helpful for readers unfamiliar with the technique.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide experimental results on several benchmark functions demonstrating that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable performance (in terms of function evaluations) to the standard CMA-ES while being significantly faster.
*   **Addresses a Practical Bottleneck:** The work targets a genuine computational bottleneck in the CMA-ES, making it potentially impactful for applications involving high-dimensional optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Originality:** The core idea of using Cholesky factorization for covariance matrix adaptation isn’t entirely novel.  The paper doesn’t sufficiently differentiate itself from prior work, specifically “A More Efficient Rank-one Covariance Matrix Update for Evolution Strategies” by Krause and Igel (2015), which implements a similar idea and builds upon earlier work by Suttorp et al. (2009). A more thorough comparison and discussion of the contributions relative to these existing approaches is needed.
*   **Missing Equivalence Proof:** The authors claim the modification doesn’t deteriorate performance. While they provide a theoretical justification, a formal proof demonstrating equivalence (or a quantifiable bound on the difference) between the original CMA-ES update (equation 2) and their Cholesky-based update is crucial.  Without this, it’s difficult to assess the impact of the approximation.
*   **Ambiguous Complexity Claim:** The claim of O(μd^2) complexity requires clarification.  Simply stating the complexity is not enough; the authors must explain *why* their method offers a practical speedup over the standard CMA-ES. The standard approach with delayed updates *also* has an effective complexity that can approach O(μd^2). Using Θ-notation to precisely define the complexity in various scenarios would strengthen the argument.
*   **Unvalidated Path-Error Assertion:**  The claim that the introduced error due to replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor is small and decreases over time, contradicting concerns raised by Hansen, lacks empirical support. The authors should present experiments specifically designed to track the error introduced by this approximation over the course of optimization, demonstrating its convergence.
*   **Minor Errors and Notation Issues:** Several minor issues detract from the paper’s polish. For example, the variable ‘wk’ in Algorithm 2 should be ‘αk’, ‘n’ in Table 1 likely refers to ‘d’ (dimensionality), and there’s a typo where ‘F’ should be ‘f’ on line 185.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The algorithm offers a potential speedup for CMA-ES, especially in high-dimensional problems.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.
*   Empirical results demonstrate comparable performance to standard CMA-ES, with faster runtime.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited originality – the approach is a relatively straightforward extension of existing work.
*   Lack of a formal equivalence proof for the modified update rule.
*   Insufficient justification for the claimed error bound and its convergence.
*   Minor technical errors and notation inconsistencies.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a potentially useful modification to the CMA-ES. However, the limited originality, lack of a formal proof of equivalence, and unsubstantiated claims regarding error behavior prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form.  I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing a rigorous theoretical analysis, a more thorough comparison with related work, and experimental validation of the error behavior, before resubmitting.  A demonstration of practical speedups beyond merely stating the complexity would also be highly beneficial.
