Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T, instead of storing and decomposing C directly. The key technical modification is to implement the standard rank-1 and rank-μ covariance updates as a sequence of O(d^2) rank-one updates to the triangular factor (Algorithm 2), yielding per-iteration complexity O(μ d^2) and minimal O(d^2/2) storage.

A second change concerns cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA). The original CSA computes pσ using C^{-1/2}. Since maintaining C^{-1/2} or the polar factor of A in O(d^2) is not known, the authors replace C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}. This introduces a rotation Et that changes over time. The paper provides Lemma 1 showing that if the normalized “shape” matrices C̄t converge, then Et becomes asymptotically constant and the resulting rotation error in the CSA norm ‖pσ‖ vanishes. The authors argue this change does not degrade performance in terms of function evaluations.

The work builds directly on: (i) Suttorp et al. (2009), who updated a non-triangular factorization; and (ii) Krause and Igel (2015), who gave an efficient triangular rank-1 update for special cases ((1+1)-CMA-ES, μ=1). This paper extends the efficient triangular update to the standard CMA-ES with general rank-μ and adapts CSA accordingly. The authors compare their method against a reference CMA-ES with delayed eigendecomposition, a variant with fixed-delay updates, and an adaptation of Suttorp et al.’s approach. On standard noiseless test functions (with random rotations) up to d=256, Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable function evaluations and large wall-clock speedups (e.g., ~20x at d=64).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The triangular rank-one update scheme is well-motivated and numerically standard; the decomposition of the full rank-μ update into a sequence of rank-1 updates is correct. The CSA modification is justified by Lemma 1 under a convergence assumption on the shape of C. While exact convergence of C in full CMA-ES is not established, related analyses (e.g., Beyer 2014; Auger 2015) support that shape matrices tend to stabilize in expectation. The empirical results corroborate the claim that evaluation counts are unchanged while runtime is substantially reduced.
- Empirical evaluation: The benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) with random rotations and a range of dimensions are standard and relevant for full-matrix CMA-ES. Reporting both evaluations-to-target and wall-clock time is appropriate. It would further strengthen the case to include noisy objectives and a task closer to typical ML applications (e.g., a policy search problem), given the paper’s ML motivation. The baselines are reasonable; however, the reference variant recomputes decompositions frequently for the examined d, which may penalize it—this is partially mitigated by including a fixed-delay baseline and the Suttorp-style factorization baseline.
- Numerical considerations: The paper argues for good numerical stability, notes efficient inversion of triangular A, and the availability of eigenvalues from the diagonal. It would help to discuss safeguards near degeneracy (e.g., jitter, minimum variance enforcement), especially under noise.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with useful background and pseudocode. The discussion around the CSA approximation, RQ/polar decompositions, and the impact of the rotation Et is careful and informative. Minor issues noted below (σ notation, a typo). A brief complexity table contrasting per-iteration costs (baseline eigendecomposition, delayed, Suttorp, proposed) would improve readability for non-experts.

Originality
The algorithmic core (triangular Cholesky rank-one update) follows Krause & Igel (2015); extending it to the general rank-μ CMA-ES is a natural but nontrivial engineering step. The main conceptual novelty is the CSA modification with a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) explaining why replacing C^{-1/2} by A^{-1} should not harm performance. Overall, the work is an incremental but useful contribution that consolidates prior ideas into a practical, general-purpose variant of CMA-ES.

Significance
Given the ubiquity of CMA-ES in derivative-free optimization and ML applications, an O(μ d^2) update with optimal storage that preserves evaluation efficiency is practically significant. The reported speedups are substantial and grow with dimension, making the method attractive whenever full covariance is warranted. The contribution is unlikely to change theory, but it can influence practice and library implementations.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, practical improvement: same evaluation efficiency, much faster wall-clock time, optimal storage.
- Sound implementation of rank-μ triangular updates; straightforward to integrate.
- CSA modification is justified and empirically validated; rotation-invariance approximately preserved.
- Careful experiments with rotated benchmarks and multiple baselines; code integrated into Shark.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Novelty is limited: primary advance is an extension/generalization of known triangular updates and an approximate CSA.
- Theoretical justification relies on convergence of shape matrices; no new analysis specific to standard CMA-ES is provided beyond Lemma 1’s conditional argument.
- Evaluation is confined to noiseless synthetic benchmarks; missing tests on noisy objectives and ML-relevant tasks.
- Baseline settings for the reference CMA-ES may be on the unfavorable side for the examined d; more thorough amortized-cost comparisons would help.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen the theoretical section by explicitly connecting Lemma 1’s assumption to existing convergence/stability results for CMA-ES (e.g., Beyer 2014; Auger 2015), or provide empirical diagnostics of shape stabilization.
- Quantify the CSA approximation error during runs (e.g., measure Et^T E_{t-1} deviation, reported partially in the supplement; bring a concise summary into the main paper).
- Include noisy and/or RL policy search experiments to match the stated motivation.
- Report per-iteration time breakdown (sampling, ranking, update) and memory usage; compare against LM-CMA/limited-memory variants to delineate the intended regime.
- Clarify numerical safeguards (e.g., minimal eigenvalue floor, damping) in Algorithm 2.

Minor comments
- Equation (1): the denominator should be \tilde{σ} (same issue in footnote 2 on p.3), to be consistent with the standard notation you use elsewhere.
- Typo: line 94 “which can by be motivated” → “which can be motivated”.
- Please define E{χ} explicitly (dimension-dependent expected norm of a χ-distributed random variable).

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject (leaning reject on originality grounds), but I find the work practically valuable. With strengthened theoretical connections and a broader empirical validation (including noisy/ML tasks), this could be a strong contribution to NIPS and a default recommendation for implementing full CMA-ES.
