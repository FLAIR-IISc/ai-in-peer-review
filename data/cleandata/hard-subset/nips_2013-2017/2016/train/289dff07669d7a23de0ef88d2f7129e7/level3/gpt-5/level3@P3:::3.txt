Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a Cholesky-based implementation of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance C rather than C itself. The covariance update combines a rank-1 evolution path term and a rank-μ weighted-sample term, and is implemented as a sequence of O(μd2) rank-1 updates to A using a stable Cholesky rank-one update routine. This generalizes the rank-1 Cholesky update of Krause and Igel (FOGA 2015) to the standard CMA-ES with rank-μ updates.

A key challenge is the step-size control (CSA): standard CMA-ES uses C−1/2 in the path update pσ, which cannot be replaced with A−1 without introducing an unknown rotation. The paper modifies CSA by replacing C−1/2 with A−1 and analyzes the impact of the missing rotation via an RQ/polar factorization argument. Under a covariance “shape” convergence assumption (normalized covariance C̄t = Ct / det(Ct)1/d converges), the associated rotation matrices converge to identity; hence the error in ‖pσ‖ vanishes asymptotically. Empirically, the authors observe that the modified CSA does not alter the number of function evaluations needed.

The contribution is positioned relative to prior speed-ups that (i) delay decompositions, (ii) restrict the model (e.g., diagonal or limited-memory CMA variants), or (iii) update non-triangular factors (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009). Compared to these, the proposed method aims to retain full-covariance performance while achieving optimal O(μd2) update complexity and reduced memory via a triangular factor.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The Cholesky rank-1 update formulation is standard and appears correctly adapted to handle both the rank-1 evolution path term and the rank-μ sample covariance term by sequencing rank-1 updates. The CSA modification is plausible: the lemma about asymptotic rotation cancellation is correct under the stated convergence assumption and explains why using A−1 instead of C−1/2 should have limited impact on ‖pσ‖ late in the run.
- Assumptions and guarantees: The central justification relies on convergence (in shape) of the covariance. While this is a common working assumption for CMA-ES analyses, the paper would benefit from stronger theoretical backing or references that make the assumption more concrete. In particular, leveraging results such as Beyer (ECJ, 2014) on expected covariance convergence in simplified settings would increase confidence that Lemma 1 applies in practice, and clarifying conditions (e.g., on objective class and parameter settings) under which shape convergence is expected would be helpful.
- Empirical support: Experiments compare the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES with a reference CMA-ES that recomputes the square root each iteration, a delayed-decomposition variant (CMA-ES/d), and an adaptation of Suttorp et al.’s non-triangular Cholesky approach. Benchmarks include standard convex-quadratic families, Rosenbrock, and Diffpowers, with random rotations, dimensions up to 256, and 100 runs. Results indicate essentially identical evaluation counts to the reference CMA-ES and substantial wall-clock speedups that grow with dimension, consistent with the O(μd2) update cost. This supports the headline claim that performance in evaluations is preserved while runtime drops.
- Missing analyses: Since the CSA change is the only behavioral difference, it would strengthen the case to (i) report a direct diagnostic of the per-iteration rotation magnitude (EtETt−1) and its decay over time in the main text (the supplement is mentioned), and (ii) include ablations or occasional polar decompositions to measure how much ‖pσ‖ differs from the C−1/2-based reference during runs, especially early on or on functions where C changes quickly (e.g., Rosenbrock). Performance under noise would also be informative, as CSA is known to be sensitive there.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. Algorithmic details (Algorithms 1–2) are clear, and the discussion of why A−1 is a practical replacement for C−1/2 is carefully motivated. Suggestions:
- Make the covariance convergence assumption explicit at the start of Section 3 and summarize precisely how it feeds into Lemma 1 and Equation (6).
- Briefly contrast the proposed triangular update with Suttorp et al.’s non-triangular factorization, highlighting the one-to-one mapping and numerical stability aspects earlier.
- Minor fixes listed below.

Originality
The main algorithmic contribution—extending the Cholesky rank-1 update machinery to the full rank-μ CMA-ES and coupling it with a modified CSA based on A−1—is a natural and technically straightforward generalization of Krause and Igel (2015). The CSA analysis under shape-convergence provides some new theoretical perspective but is limited in scope and contingent on assumptions. Overall, the novelty is modest.

Significance
For practitioners who need full-covariance CMA-ES on moderate-to-high dimensional, nonseparable problems, a drop-in implementation that delivers the same evaluation efficiency with much lower wall-clock time and memory is valuable. The result is practically significant, though not conceptually groundbreaking. The approach should see uptake in libraries and applications where the covariance update is a bottleneck.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Practical and impactful speedup with preserved evaluation performance on standard benchmarks.
- Clean integration of rank-μ updates via a sequence of rank-1 Cholesky updates; optimal O(μd2) time and memory.
- Plausible theoretical explanation for why the modified CSA works, plus rotation-invariance tests.

Cons:
- Limited novelty beyond a straightforward extension of prior rank-1 Cholesky CMA-ES work.
- Theoretical support hinges on covariance shape convergence; stronger linkage to existing convergence theory (e.g., Beyer, ECJ 2014) would help.
- Early-iteration behavior of the modified CSA and robustness under noise are not thoroughly quantified.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Theory: Please cite and discuss Beyer (2014, ECJ) or related analyses to substantiate the shape-convergence assumption and delineate when it is reasonable. If possible, provide empirical traces of EtETt−1 norms and of the discrepancy between the A−1-CSA and the C−1/2-CSA path lengths to illustrate the transient error.
- Experiments: Consider adding BBOB-style results or a broader set of non-convex, ill-conditioned, and/or noisy functions. Including LM-CMA or other limited-memory baselines would contextualize trade-offs versus sub-quadratic approaches (even if they may lose accuracy on nonseparable functions).
- Implementation: Clarify numerical safeguards in the rank-1 updates (e.g., jitter on the diagonal, conditioning checks) and how often A−1 is explicitly formed versus solved via triangular solves.
- Minor issues:
  - In Eq. (1), the denominator should be \tilde{σ} (and the same correction applies to footnote 2 on p.3).
  - Typo line 94: “which can by be motivated ...” → remove “by”.
- Presentation: Move key diagnostics from the supplement (rotation measures) into the main paper, and briefly summarize memory savings and practical constants in O(μd2) (e.g., how μ scales with d in defaults).

Overall assessment
A solid, carefully implemented engineering improvement with clear practical benefits and a reasonable, if assumption-dependent, theoretical rationale. Novelty is limited, but the contribution is useful to the community. I lean toward acceptance if space allows, especially if the authors strengthen the theoretical discussion and add the suggested diagnostics; otherwise, it remains a strong workshop/engineering contribution.
