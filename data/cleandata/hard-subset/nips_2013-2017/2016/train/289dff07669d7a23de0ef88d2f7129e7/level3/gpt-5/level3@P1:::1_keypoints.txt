- Problem: CMA-ES is bottlenecked by storing/updating the full covariance and recomputing its square root, costing > quadratic time in high dimensions.  
- Contribution: Proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES that maintains the triangular Cholesky factor A and uses rank-one updates to achieve O(µ d^2) per-iteration cost and O(d^2) memory.  
- CSA modification: Replaces C−1/2 in the pσ update with A−1 and argues the rotation error vanishes as the algorithm converges.  
- Theoretical support: Provides a continuity lemma on the RQ decomposition to justify diminishing rotation error between A and C1/2.  
- Empirical results: Function-evaluation counts remain essentially unchanged versus strong CMA-ES baseline while wall-clock time is significantly reduced, especially at higher dimensions.  
- Relation to prior work: Positions method relative to delayed decompositions, restricted covariance models, and prior Cholesky-like updates; retains full covariance modeling power unlike low-rank/limited-memory methods.  
- Advantage of triangular factor: Bijective tie to C, eigenvalues accessible from A’s diagonal, stable quadratic-time inversion/solves.  
- Strength: Clear, impactful algorithmic contribution achieving optimal memory and reduced runtime without sacrificing full covariance expressiveness.  
- Strength: Sound reasoning for CSA approximation based on convergence of rotation factor and exponential forgetting of old steps.  
- Strength: Practical benefits—fast, stable rank-one updates, efficient triangular solves, and free conditioning monitoring.  
- Strength: Empirical evaluation includes rotation-invariant tests and demonstrates scalable speedups.  
- Relevance: Highly pertinent to derivative-free optimization tasks in ML, such as policy search and hyperparameter tuning.  
- Weakness: Theoretical guarantees are limited to CSA approximation; no full convergence proof for the modified algorithm and potential early-iteration transients are a concern.  
- Weakness: Experimental reporting needs statistical significance (medians, confidence intervals) where differences are small.  
- Weakness: Missing runtime baseline LM-CMA for large-scale comparisons between full-model and approximate-model approaches.  
- Reproducibility concern: Require clarification on implementation parity (codebase, compiler flags, BLAS/LAPACK, threading, CPU) and whether triangular solves are used instead of forming A−1.  
- Empirical anomaly: d=256 Cigar/Discus show smaller speedups—need quantification of cache effects, memory footprint, and explanation of function-dependent behavior and µ differences.  
- Evaluation bias: Rosenbrock non-convergent trials were excluded—report fraction excluded and include wall-clock results with budget-capped all-trial analyses.  
- Clarity: Paper is well written; pseudo-code is helpful. Suggest explicitly stating triangular solve use, numerical stability properties, conditioning safeguards, and implementation timing details.  
- Originality: Novel combination of triangular Cholesky updates with a justified CSA modification; empirical confirmation of unchanged evaluation counts is new.  
- Significance: High — retains full CMA-ES modeling while substantially improving speed and memory, likely useful for medium-to-large dimensions.  
- Pros for acceptance: Strong practical impact, solid theoretical motivation for CSA change, careful experimental validation, and likely code/library integration.  
- Cons for acceptance: Limited theoretical guarantees for whole algorithm, unclear runtime comparison details, missing large-scale baselines, and some experimental exclusions/anomalies.  
- Minor camera-ready requests: Clarify implementation/timing details, explain d=256 behavior, report statistics on excluded runs, emphasize triangular solves over explicit inversion, and discuss early-iteration CSA behavior.  
- Recommendation: Recommend acceptance after addressing the listed clarifications and additional experimental/statistical reporting.
