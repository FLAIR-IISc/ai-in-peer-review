

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm that replaces the matrix square root with a Cholesky factor for covariance matrix adaptation, claiming to achieve optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity while maintaining similar performance to the standard CMA-ES. The authors present theoretical justification for why replacing C^(-1/2) with A^(-1) (where C = AA^T) does not significantly affect performance, and provide empirical results showing speed improvements.

The paper builds on prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), who also proposed using Cholesky factors for covariance matrix adaptation in evolution strategies. However, the paper's naming convention ("Cholesky-CMA-ES") conflicts with Suttorp et al.'s established terminology, creating confusion in the literature. The authors claim to extend Suttorp's approach to the standard CMA-ES, but their main algorithmic change appears to be simply looping the rankOneUpdate operation for all individuals (the "for" loop in Algorithm 1), which is a relatively minor modification.

The paper suffers from several critical weaknesses. First, it omits comparison with Suttorp's original Cholesky-CMA-ES and Krause's variant, which are directly relevant to the proposed method. The empirical results only compare against a "CMA-ES-Ref" implementation with delayed matrix square root computation, not against the actual Cholesky-based variants from prior work. This omission makes it impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers any meaningful improvement over existing approaches.

Second, the paper overstates its contribution. The claimed "optimal" complexity is asymptotically O(µd^2), which is not fundamentally better than Suttorp's approach (which also achieves O(µd^2) complexity). The speedup is only approximately 2× compared to Suttorp's method, not the dramatic improvement claimed. The paper also incorrectly claims that their method is "optimal" for non-separable ill-conditioned problems, but for Sphere or separable functions, a diagonal covariance matrix (which is simpler and faster) suffices, making the full covariance approach unnecessary.

Third, the paper makes questionable claims about numerical stability. It asserts that Suttorp's method is numerically unstable, despite Suttorp et al. (2009) explicitly claiming the opposite in their paper. This is a significant factual error that undermines the paper's credibility.

The experiments are incomplete and misleading. The paper does not include Suttorp's or Krause's variants in their empirical comparison, and the plot differences on the Cigar function plateau may stem from how median results are plotted rather than actual algorithmic differences. The paper also incorrectly claims that eigenvalue decomposition is performed at every update, when in practice it is typically done periodically.

Strengths of the paper include a clear presentation of the algorithm, reasonable theoretical justification for the approximation (Lemma 1), and empirical evidence of speed improvements for larger dimensions. The paper also correctly identifies the importance of covariance matrix adaptation in CMA-ES and the need for efficient implementations.

In terms of the review criteria:

**Quality**: The paper's technical claims are not fully supported. The numerical stability claim is contradicted by prior work, and the empirical comparison is incomplete. The paper fails to properly evaluate its claims against relevant prior work.

**Clarity**: The algorithm is clearly described in Algorithm 1, and the theoretical justification is reasonably well-explained. However, the presentation of results is misleading due to missing comparisons.

**Originality**: The main algorithmic contribution appears incremental (a simple "for" loop extension), not novel. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from prior work by Suttorp and Krause.

**Significance**: The claimed speedup is modest (only ~2×), and the method does not fundamentally solve the scalability issue of CMA-ES (which remains quadratic in dimension). The paper's significance is overstated.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper correctly identifies a problem (covariance matrix update complexity) and proposes a technically sound approach. The algorithm is well-described, and the speed improvements for large dimensions are valid.

Arguments against acceptance: The paper's contribution is significantly overstated, it omits critical comparisons with prior work, contains factual errors about prior work, and makes misleading claims about numerical stability and optimality.

Recommendations: The paper should be rejected in its current form. The authors should: (1) resolve the naming conflict by using a different name for their variant; (2) include empirical comparisons with Suttorp's and Krause's variants; (3) clarify that the main contribution is the "for" loop extension (not a fundamentally new approach); and (4) correct the factual errors about numerical stability and eigenvalue decomposition frequency.

The paper has potential but requires substantial revision to properly position itself in the literature and accurately represent its contributions. As written, it fails to meet the quality and originality standards expected at NIPS.
