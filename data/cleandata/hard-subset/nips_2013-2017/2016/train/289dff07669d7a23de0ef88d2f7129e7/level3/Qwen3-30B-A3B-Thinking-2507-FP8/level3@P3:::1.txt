

# Review: CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

This paper proposes a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the standard matrix square root operations with triangular Cholesky factor updates, reducing both time and space complexity from O(d³) to O(d²) for covariance matrix adaptation. The authors provide a theoretical justification for why this modification does not degrade the algorithm's performance in terms of function evaluations, which is critical since the covariance update dominates the computational cost in each iteration of CMA-ES.

The paper builds upon previous attempts to reduce CMA-ES complexity (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008; Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), but makes a significant contribution by addressing the standard CMA-ES with its matrix square root in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. The key insight is that Lemma 1 establishes the error introduced by replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ vanishes as the algorithm converges, making the modification theoretically sound.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and thoroughly evaluated. The authors compare their Cholesky-CMA-ES against state-of-the-art variants (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES) across multiple benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) and dimensions (4-256). The empirical results are compelling: the Cholesky-CMA-ES requires identical numbers of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES while delivering substantial wall-clock speedups (20x at d=64, increasing with dimensionality). The approach also provides covariance eigenvalues for free (via the Cholesky factor diagonal), which is valuable for monitoring optimization problems.

This work has clear practical significance for machine learning applications. CMA-ES is widely used in reinforcement learning (for direct policy search) and supervised learning (for hyperparameter tuning), and the Cholesky-CMA-ES directly addresses a major computational bottleneck. The authors make a strong case that this variant should replace standard CMA-ES for medium/large-scale problems where covariance updates matter, as it offers clear practical advantages without performance degradation.

The paper excels in all four review criteria:
- **Quality**: The theoretical justification is solid, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the authors carefully address limitations.
- **Clarity**: Well-organized with clear pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and explanations; the algorithm is straightforward to understand and implement.
- **Originality**: While previous work used Cholesky factors for some variants, this is the first to provide a theoretically justified approach for the standard CMA-ES.
- **Significance**: The contribution has immediate practical value for many machine learning applications, particularly high-dimensional optimization.

Minor concerns:
1. Implementation details for CPU comparisons should be clarified (e.g., same programming language, optimization flags, and hardware).
2. Figure 2 shows relatively modest differences for Cigar and Discus at d=256, which the authors attribute to cache effects. A brief explanation of why these functions don't show more pronounced differences would strengthen the paper.

This is a strong contribution to the field of optimization algorithms. The paper is technically sound, well-presented, and addresses a practical bottleneck in a widely used algorithm. The authors provide both theoretical justification and empirical evidence for their approach, making it a compelling candidate for adoption in practice. The work is highly relevant to NIPS as it directly impacts optimization methods used in machine learning.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper makes a significant contribution to a widely used optimization algorithm with clear theoretical and practical advantages. The empirical results are thorough and compelling, demonstrating substantial speedups without performance degradation. The paper is well-written and addresses a practical bottleneck that affects many machine learning applications.

Arguments against acceptance: The speedup is most dramatic for high-dimensional problems, but for small dimensions, the improvement may be less pronounced. However, this is not a weakness given the paper's focus on medium/large-scale problems.

Overall, this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. It represents a significant practical improvement to a fundamental optimization algorithm with clear relevance to machine learning research and applications.
