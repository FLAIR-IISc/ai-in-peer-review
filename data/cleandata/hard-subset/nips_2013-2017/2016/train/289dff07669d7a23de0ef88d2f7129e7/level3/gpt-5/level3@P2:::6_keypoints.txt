- Targets reducing the O(d^3) cost of CMA-ES by maintaining a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A and updating it in O(µ d^2) time and O(d^2) memory.  
- Replaces C−1/2 in CSA with A−1 to cheaply approximate step-size adaptation; Lemma 1 argues induced rotational error vanishes asymptotically.  
- Empirically reports comparable function evaluation counts to standard CMA-ES and substantial wall-clock speedups that grow with dimension.  
- Relates closely to prior Cholesky-update work (Suttorp et al., 2009) and Krause & Igel (2015); complements O(d) large-scale approximations.  
- Strength: clear, relevant problem statement addressing time/memory bottleneck without changing evaluation complexity.  
- Strength: simple, implementable triangular rank-one update with O(d^2) inversion and easy monitoring of eigenvalues on A’s diagonal.  
- Strength: theoretical intuition (Lemma 1) that CSA approximation error diminishes as covariance normalizes.  
- Strength: promising empirical speedups in wall-clock time while preserving evaluation performance.  
- Strength: good discussion of numerical considerations and algorithmic complexity.  
- Weakness: naming conflict — “Cholesky-CMA-ES” is already used by Suttorp et al. (2009), causing potential confusion.  
- Weakness: incomplete positioning vs prior work; needs clearer contrast with Suttorp (triangular vs non-triangular) and Krause & Igel (rank-one quadratic update).  
- Weakness: novelty appears limited—mainly extending rank-one triangular update to µ>1 via a loop; requires stronger justification.  
- Weakness: empirical comparison insufficient; needs head-to-head experiments with Suttorp (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015) under matched settings.  
- Weakness: “optimality” claims are overstated and ignore separable/LM variants where O(d) methods win; improvement over Suttorp may be only a small constant factor.  
- Weakness: scalability remains quadratic, so method is not suited to truly large-scale problems where O(d^2) is infeasible.  
- Weakness: experimental clarity issues (e.g., Cigar plateau may be plotting artifact); request for ECDFs, error bars, and explicit median aggregation definitions.  
- Minor factual: paper overstates frequency of eigen-decomposition in practice — many implementations do it periodically; reconcile with baseline choices.  
- Quality: core algorithms and complexity claims are sound; CSA approximation plausible but lacks finite-time quantitative bounds or diagnostics.  
- Empirical rigor: needs stronger baselines and ablations (Suttorp, Krause, diagonal/LM-CMA, periodic eigendecomp, CSA ablation, time breakdown).  
- Clarity: paper is readable but should rename method, state incremental nature explicitly, justify non-triviality, clarify plotting choices, and reconcile eigendecomp frequency claims.  
- Originality: moderate — known ideas (triangular factor, avoiding explicit covariance) integrated with CSA modification and asymptotic justification.  
- Significance: practically useful for moderate-to-high d where O(d^2) is viable, but limited impact on truly large-scale optimization.  
- Suggested fixes: rename method (e.g., tChol-CMA-ES), add controlled comparisons to Suttorp/Krause, provide CSA finite-time diagnostics, ECDFs/error bars, and quantify constant-factor speedups.  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses an important bottleneck, simple to implement, strong empirical wall-clock speedups without harming evaluations.  
- Cons for acceptance: incomplete comparisons/positioning, limited novelty, overstated claims, and lacking robust diagnostics — needs revision before acceptance.
