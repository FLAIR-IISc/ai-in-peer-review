

# Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm that replaces the matrix square root in the step-size adaptation mechanism with the inverse of a lower-triangular Cholesky factor. The authors claim this reduces the computational complexity of covariance matrix updates from O(d³) to O(μd²) while maintaining the same optimization performance. The paper builds upon previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), which explored Cholesky-based updates for specific CMA-ES variants, but provides a theoretically justified approach for the standard CMA-ES. The authors argue that the orthogonal matrix E_t in the RQ-decomposition converges to the identity as the algorithm approaches convergence, making the approximation valid asymptotically.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach to reducing computational complexity. The theoretical justification (Lemma 1) establishes that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor vanishes asymptotically under reasonable convergence assumptions. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing Cholesky-CMA-ES against state-of-the-art variants (CMA-ES-Ref and CMA-ES/d) on multiple benchmark functions across dimensions from 4 to 256. The results consistently show significant speedups (up to 20× for d=64) with identical optimization performance. However, the theoretical analysis relies on restrictive assumptions about covariance matrix convergence that may not hold in practice for non-convex or non-separable functions. The authors could strengthen their theoretical claims by including more detailed analysis of the error bounds and convergence rates.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The background on CMA-ES and the motivation for the Cholesky-based approach are well-explained. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise and implementable. The authors could improve clarity by explicitly discussing the practical implications of their theoretical assumptions and providing more details about the implementation of the Cholesky update. The comparison to previous work is well-motivated, though the paper could better highlight how their approach differs from Suttorp et al. (2009) beyond the triangular factorization constraint.

**Originality:** The core idea of using Cholesky factors to reduce computational complexity is not entirely novel, as Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) explored similar approaches. However, the authors provide a theoretically justified extension to the standard CMA-ES that addresses a key limitation of previous attempts. The paper clearly differentiates itself by providing convergence guarantees under specific assumptions, which previous works lacked. This represents a meaningful contribution to the literature, though the novelty is incremental rather than transformative.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a genuine computational bottleneck in CMA-ES, particularly relevant for high-dimensional problems where objective function evaluations are relatively inexpensive. The demonstrated speedups could be valuable in specific applications such as real-time robotics policy search or large-scale hyperparameter tuning. However, as noted in the key points, the practical impact may be limited because in most machine learning applications, the objective evaluation time dominates the computational cost, and typical problem dimensions are modest (d < 100). The authors would strengthen the significance claim by including a real-world experiment where speed matters (e.g., real-time robotics policy gradients), as suggested in the key points.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Solid contribution to speeding up CMA-ES for high-dimensional problems where evaluations are cheap
- Theoretical justification for why the approximation does not impair performance
- Empirical results show consistent speedups with no loss of optimization performance
- The approach has optimal storage complexity and provides eigenvalues for free

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Theoretical assumptions are restrictive and formal guarantees are limited
- Practical impact may be limited since objective evaluation time typically dominates
- The paper does not include a real-world experiment where the speedup matters
- The authors do not report actual wall-clock runtimes of full CMA-ES without delay strategies
- The paper does not track the evolution of E_t (the orthogonal matrix) as suggested in the key points

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a clear contribution to the CMA-ES literature. While the practical impact may be limited for most machine learning applications, the theoretical and algorithmic insights are valuable for the optimization community. The authors should address the suggested improvements to strengthen their theoretical claims and practical relevance. Given the incremental but meaningful nature of the contribution, I recommend acceptance.
