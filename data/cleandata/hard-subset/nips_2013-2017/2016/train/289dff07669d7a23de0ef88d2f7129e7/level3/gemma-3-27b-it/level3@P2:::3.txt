This paper proposes the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that utilizes the triangular Cholesky factorization instead of the matrix square root for updating and storing the covariance matrix. The authors argue this leads to improved computational efficiency, particularly in high-dimensional problems, while maintaining the core performance characteristics of the original CMA-ES. The key idea is to modify the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, justifying this modification under the assumption of covariance convergence. Empirical results demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable performance to standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluations, but with significantly reduced runtime, especially as dimensionality increases.

The CMA-ES is a well-established and powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm, widely used in various fields, including machine learning for tasks like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001; Gomez et al., 2008). The computational bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES lies in the updating and storage of the covariance matrix, typically involving eigenvalue decomposition. Several approaches have been explored to address this, including delayed updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) and restricting model complexity (Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008). Recent work has focused on updating the Cholesky factor directly (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015). This paper builds upon this line of research, extending the work of Krause and Igel (2015) to the full CMA-ES framework, incorporating both rank-1 and rank-µ updates.

The strengths of this paper are severalfold. First, the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a promising approach to improving the scalability of the CMA-ES. The reduction in runtime complexity, particularly for high-dimensional problems, is a significant advantage. The theoretical justification, linking the modification to the CSA mechanism and the assumption of covariance convergence (Lemma 1), adds credibility to the approach. The experimental validation, demonstrating comparable performance in terms of function evaluations with a substantial reduction in runtime, provides empirical support for the claims. The algorithm’s properties – optimal asymptotic storage complexity, numerically stable computation of the inverse, and free access to eigenvalues – further enhance its appeal. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, and the connection to existing work is well-established.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The novelty of the contribution feels somewhat incremental. Extending the Cholesky update from the simplified case in Krause and Igel (2015) to the full CMA-ES with rank-µ updates, while non-trivial, appears as a fairly direct generalization. The authors acknowledge this, but a stronger argument for the originality and impact of this extension would be beneficial. The assumption of covariance convergence, crucial for the theoretical justification, is stated without substantial supporting evidence beyond citing Beyer (2014). While Beyer’s work provides a foundation, a more detailed discussion or analysis of this assumption in the context of the Cholesky-CMA-ES would strengthen the argument. A minor technical issue exists: the σ in equation (1) appears to be a typo and should be \tilde{σ}, consistent with the footnote on page 3. Additionally, a minor grammatical error exists on line 94 ("which can by be motivated...").

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practical limitation of CMA-ES (computational cost of covariance matrix updates).
*   Provides a theoretically justified modification to the CSA mechanism.
*   Demonstrates significant runtime improvements through empirical evaluation.
*   Offers an algorithm with desirable properties (optimal storage, numerical stability).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution: the extension from Krause & Igel (2015) is relatively straightforward.
*   Reliance on the covariance convergence assumption without strong supporting evidence.
*   Minor technical and grammatical issues.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the concerns regarding the incremental nature of the contribution, the Cholesky-CMA-ES represents a valuable addition to the CMA-ES toolkit. The potential for significant runtime improvements, particularly in high-dimensional optimization problems, is compelling. The theoretical justification, while reliant on an assumption that could be more thoroughly addressed, provides a reasonable basis for confidence in the algorithm’s performance. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the technical issues and strengthening the discussion around the covariance convergence assumption. The authors should also consider expanding the discussion on the limitations of their method and the scenarios where it might be less effective.
