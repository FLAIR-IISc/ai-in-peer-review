This paper proposes a modification to the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) aimed at reducing computational cost by replacing the covariance matrix square root with its Cholesky factorization during step-size adaptation. The authors argue this leads to a faster, asymptotically optimal update procedure with minimal memory requirements, while maintaining the performance characteristics of the original CMA-ES. The paper is clearly written and presents a well-motivated problem.

The CMA-ES is a highly successful derivative-free optimization algorithm, finding applications in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning, as the authors correctly point out, referencing relevant work by Hansen and Ostermeier [2001], Beyer [2007], and Eiben & Smith [2015]. The bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES is the repeated computation of the covariance matrix square root. Existing strategies to address this, such as delayed updates [Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001] or restricting model complexity [Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008], are also well-described. The authors correctly position their work within this context, referencing recent approaches utilizing Cholesky factors [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015].

The core idea – utilizing the Cholesky factorization directly in the step-size adaptation – is intuitively appealing. The authors provide a theoretical justification based on the convergence of the Cholesky factor and its relation to the matrix square root (Lemma 1). However, a crucial aspect is missing: a formal demonstration, or at least a detailed explanation, of how the proposed update rule applied to the Cholesky factor is *equivalent* to the original update rule (equation 2) when operating on the covariance matrix. The claim that the modification doesn’t deteriorate performance hinges on this equivalence, and a more rigorous justification is required. While Krause & Igel [2015] demonstrate this for a simplified case (µ=λ=1), extending this proof to the general CMA-ES is critical.

A significant weakness is the lack of clarity regarding the claimed complexity improvement. The authors state a complexity of O(µd²) for their method, but this is essentially the same asymptotic complexity as the standard CMA-ES with delayed updates. The authors should utilize Θ-notation and provide a detailed breakdown of the computational costs involved in both approaches, explicitly highlighting where the speedup arises. The theoretical claim of “asymptotically optimal” requires more careful justification.

The experimental evaluation is a strength, demonstrating comparable performance (in terms of function evaluations) to the standard CMA-ES and a noticeable speedup in wall-clock time, particularly as dimensionality increases. However, the claim regarding a small and decreasing path error introduced by the approximation (replacing *s<sub>t</sub>* with *ŝ<sub>t</sub>*) is not convincingly supported by the experiments.  Hansen [2015] discusses the importance of accurate step-size adaptation; simply showing that final performance is comparable isn’t sufficient.  The authors should include experiments that directly track the evolution of *E<sub>t</sub>* over time, or measure the impact of this approximation on the algorithm’s convergence behavior. A more rigorous validation of this claim is crucial.

Several minor issues detract from the paper’s polish. There are some notational inconsistencies (e.g., *w<sub>k</sub>* in Algorithm 2 should be *α<sub>k</sub>*), and typographical errors (e.g., 'F' should be 'f' on line 185, and 'n' in Table 1 should be 'd').

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The proposed method is a sensible and potentially impactful modification to the CMA-ES.
*   The writing is clear, and the problem is well-motivated.
*   The experimental results suggest a tangible speedup in runtime.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of a rigorous proof demonstrating equivalence between the proposed update rule and the original CMA-ES update.
*   Unclear justification of the claimed complexity improvement.
*   Insufficient validation of the claim regarding the small path error introduced by the approximation.
*   Minor notational inconsistencies and typos.
*   The method appears to be a relatively straightforward extension of existing work (Suttorp et al. [2009]; Krause & Igel [2015]), lacking substantial novelty.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising modification to the CMA-ES, but it falls short of NIPS acceptance standards in its current form. The lack of a rigorous equivalence proof and the insufficiently validated claims regarding path error are significant concerns. While the empirical results are encouraging, they don’t fully compensate for these theoretical weaknesses. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address these points in a revised version. Specifically, the authors should focus on providing a formal justification for the approximation, clarifying the complexity analysis, and experimentally validating the impact of the approximation on convergence behavior.
