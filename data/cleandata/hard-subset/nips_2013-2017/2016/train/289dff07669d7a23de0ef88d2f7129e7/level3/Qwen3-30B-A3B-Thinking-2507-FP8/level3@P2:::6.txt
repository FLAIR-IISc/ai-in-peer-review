

The paper proposes a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called "Cholesky-CMA-ES" that replaces the matrix square root with a Cholesky factor in the cumulative step-size adaptation mechanism. The authors claim this approach achieves optimal asymptotic time and space complexity (O(µd²)) while maintaining similar optimization performance to the standard CMA-ES. The key insight is that the Cholesky factor can be updated in quadratic time while maintaining the same algorithmic behavior as the standard CMA-ES, with a theoretical justification that the error introduced by this change diminishes as the algorithm converges.

The paper builds on the foundational work of Hansen and Ostermeier (2001) who introduced CMA-ES, and connects to recent work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) who also explored Cholesky-based approaches. However, the paper fails to adequately compare with these works, which is a significant oversight given their direct relevance.

Strengths:
1. The paper correctly identifies a genuine computational bottleneck in CMA-ES (matrix square root computation) and provides a theoretically sound justification for their approach.
2. The empirical results demonstrate significant speed improvements (up to 20x faster at d=64), which is valuable for practitioners.
3. The paper correctly notes that the Cholesky factorization provides eigenvalues for free, which is a useful byproduct.

Weaknesses:
1. The naming is misleading and conflicts with Suttorp et al. (2009), who already proposed a Cholesky-based CMA-ES. The authors should either adopt a different name or clearly differentiate their approach.
2. The algorithmic contribution appears incremental. The core idea is simply looping the rankOneUpdate procedure for all individuals (as seen in Algorithm 1), which is a straightforward extension of existing work.
3. The experimental section is incomplete. The authors compare to a "reference" CMA-ES variant (CMA-ES-Ref) and a delayed update variant (CMA-ES/d), but omit direct comparison to Suttorp's and Krause's Cholesky-based methods. This undermines the claim of "optimal" complexity.
4. The paper overstates the optimality of their approach. For separable functions (like Sphere), the standard CMA-ES can be reduced to diagonal updates, which is more efficient than the full Cholesky update proposed here.
5. The numerical stability claim is questionable. The authors assert that Suttorp's method is numerically unstable, but Suttorp et al. (2009) explicitly claim the opposite.
6. The paper misrepresents standard CMA-ES implementation. Eigenvalue decomposition is typically performed periodically, not at every update, as claimed.

Quality: The paper is technically sound in its core contribution but suffers from inadequate experimental comparison with prior work. The theoretical justification is strong, but the experimental validation is incomplete.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, but the naming conflict and lack of clear differentiation from prior work create confusion. The authors should clarify the distinction between their approach and Suttorp's.

Originality: The main contribution appears to be an incremental extension rather than a fundamentally new approach. The paper fails to clearly articulate how their method differs from or improves upon Suttorp and Krause's work.

Significance: The proposed method provides a practical speedup for non-separable optimization problems, which is valuable. However, the quadratic complexity means it doesn't fundamentally solve large-scale CMA-ES issues, as claimed. For truly large-scale problems, dimensionality reduction approaches would still be necessary.

Pro/Con Acceptance:
Pro: Practical speedup for non-separable optimization, theoretically justified approach, useful eigenvalue byproduct.
Con: Misleading naming, incomplete comparison with prior work, overstated optimality claims, questionable numerical stability claims.

Recommendation: The paper has merit but requires substantial revision. The authors should:
1. Resolve the naming conflict with Suttorp et al. (2009)
2. Add direct experimental comparison with Suttorp's and Krause's methods
3. Clarify the scope of "optimality" (only optimal for non-separable problems)
4. Revise numerical stability claims with stronger justification
5. Correct the factual error about eigenvalue decomposition frequency

Without these revisions, the paper's claims are overstated and it fails to adequately situate itself in the existing literature. The contribution is valuable but not sufficiently novel or well-validated as presented. With these revisions, this could be a valuable contribution to the field.
