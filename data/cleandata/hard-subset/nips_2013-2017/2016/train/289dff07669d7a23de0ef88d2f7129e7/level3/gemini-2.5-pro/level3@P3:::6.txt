### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) to improve its computational efficiency. The standard CMA-ES algorithm often relies on an O(d^3) eigenvalue decomposition to update its covariance matrix, which can be a bottleneck in high dimensions. The authors propose a variant, which they term "Cholesky-CMA-ES", that avoids this cost by directly maintaining and updating a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. This reduces the update complexity to O(μd^2). The core modification involves an approximation in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, where the inverse of the matrix square root (C^{-1/2}) is replaced by the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor (A^{-1}). The paper provides a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) suggesting that the error introduced by this approximation diminishes as the shape of the covariance matrix converges. Empirical results on several benchmark functions show that the proposed method achieves similar convergence in terms of function evaluations compared to a standard CMA-ES implementation, but with a significant reduction in wall-clock time.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its derivation of the Cholesky update and the motivation for the CSA approximation. However, its claims and evaluation are not fully supported.

*   **Strengths:** The goal of reducing CMA-ES's computational cost is important and relevant. The proposed method of updating a triangular Cholesky factor is a valid and efficient approach that provides a substantial practical speedup over reference implementations that perform full decompositions. The theoretical justification in Lemma 1 provides a reasonable, albeit not fully rigorous, argument for why the approximation in the CSA path should work well in practice.
*   **Weaknesses:** Several claims are overstated or unsubstantiated.
    *   The paper claims its approach is "asymptotically optimal" in time and memory. This is misleading. The complexity remains O(μd^2), which does not fundamentally solve the scalability problem for very large `d`. For separable or spherical functions, simpler O(d) diagonal adaptation is optimal and far more efficient. Compared to Suttorp et al. (2009), which is also O(d^2), the proposed method is at best a constant factor faster (e.g., ~2x), not a change in optimality.
    *   The paper claims that methods using a non-triangular factorization (citing Suttorp et al., 2009) are "prone to numerical instabilities." This is a strong claim made without any evidence or citation. The original Suttorp paper, in fact, argues for the numerical stability of its method. This unsubstantiated assertion should be removed or rigorously proven.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the algorithm is presented clearly. However, there are minor issues.

*   The introduction states that computing the matrix square root via eigenvalue decomposition is required in "one CMA-ES iteration," which could be interpreted as happening at every step. While the authors later mention the delay strategy, the initial framing is slightly inaccurate, as most modern implementations perform this costly operation periodically (e.g., every d/10 iterations), not every single iteration.
*   In Figure 1 (Cigar), the convergence plot for Cholesky-CMA-ES appears to avoid a plateau that other methods hit. This could be a significant finding, but it may also be an artifact of how the median of 100 trials is plotted, especially if some runs failed to converge. The authors should clarify their plotting methodology.

**Originality:** The originality of the contribution is a significant concern. The idea of updating a Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix is not new.

*   The name "Cholesky-CMA-ES" is highly problematic, as it was already used by Suttorp et al. (2009) for their algorithm. This creates unnecessary confusion in the literature and should be rectified.
*   The paper fails to adequately discuss or compare its work to closely related prior art. It mentions Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015) but provides neither a detailed algorithmic comparison nor an empirical one. The main algorithmic novelty appears to be the decomposition of the rank-μ update into a sequence of rank-one updates within a `for` loop (Algorithm 1). This seems like an incremental contribution over prior work, and its novelty and advantages are not clearly articulated in comparison to these existing methods.

**Significance:** The work could be significant if its advantages over existing efficient CMA-ES variants were clearly demonstrated. As it stands, its significance is diminished by the lack of comparison to the most relevant baselines.

*   A faster CMA-ES is certainly useful for the machine learning community (e.g., in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning). The empirical speedups are impressive. However, without comparing to Suttorp's Cholesky-CMA-ES, it is impossible to assess whether this new variant offers a meaningful improvement over the existing state-of-the-art in efficient covariance matrix updates. The experiments are incomplete, comparing only against standard CMA-ES with delayed updates.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

*   **For:** The paper tackles an important practical problem and presents a method that demonstrates a significant speedup over a standard CMA-ES implementation. The core idea is sensible and presented with a plausible theoretical justification.
*   **Against:** The work shows a critical lack of engagement with the most relevant prior work, leading to a misleading name, an incomplete experimental evaluation, and an unclear statement of contribution. Several key claims about optimality and numerical stability are overstated or unsupported.

**Recommended Fixes for Improvement**

To strengthen the paper for publication, the authors should:
1.  **Resolve the Naming Conflict:** Change the name of the proposed algorithm to avoid confusion with Suttorp et al. (2009).
2.  **Conduct Comprehensive Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is the most critical part that needs revision. The authors must include an implementation of Suttorp's Cholesky-CMA-ES in their experiments and potentially discuss the approach of Krause et al. (2015). This comparison is essential to demonstrate the value of the proposed method. The full convergence plots should be included, perhaps in a supplement if space is an issue.
3.  **Clarify the Contribution:** Explicitly state what the core algorithmic novelty is. If it is primarily the `for` loop implementation of the rank-μ update in Algorithm 1, this should be made clear. The authors should then justify why this specific implementation is superior to alternatives.
4.  **Revise Claims:** Tone down the claims of "optimality." Contextualize the complexity and performance gains relative to other efficient variants like Suttorp's, not just the O(d^3) standard version. Either provide strong evidence for the claim about the numerical instability of Suttorp's method or remove it entirely.
5.  **Address Minor Points:** Clarify the plotting methodology for the median results and correct the description of how often eigenvalue decomposition is performed in typical CMA-ES implementations.
