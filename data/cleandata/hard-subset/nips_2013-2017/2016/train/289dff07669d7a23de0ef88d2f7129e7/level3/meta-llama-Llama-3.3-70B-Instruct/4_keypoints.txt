- Introduces Sparse Access Memory (SAM) to scale memory-augmented neural networks to large memories and long sequences.
- Main technical contribution: uses triangular Cholesky factorization to remove the square root in CMA-ES, lowering computation cost.
- Provides theoretical justification that the modification preserves algorithm performance and reduces runtime complexity.
- Shows SAM achieves asymptotic lower bounds in space and time complexity.
- Demonstrates SAM requires the same number of objective function evaluations as standard CMA-ES but reduces wall-clock time, with larger gains in higher-dimensional search spaces.
- Implements an approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) index to reduce forward-pass complexity to sublinear time.
- Empirical results: SAM learns with comparable efficiency to dense models and outperforms them in some cases.
- Strength: scalability to large memories and long sequences, relevant to language modeling and machine translation.
- Strength: practical implementation and demonstrated runtime improvements.
- Weakness: insufficient theoretical explanation for observed numerical stability.
- Weakness: lacks detailed analysis of trade-offs between sparse and dense approaches.
- Weakness: inadequate analysis of the effectiveness of the exponential curriculum used to scale tasks.
- Originality: SAM and the use of ANN indexes are novel contributions and well situated within related work.
- Significance: potential to enable training of neural networks with much larger memories, impacting real-world applications.
