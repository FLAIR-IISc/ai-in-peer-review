This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a widely used derivative-free optimization algorithm. The primary contribution is to reduce the computational complexity of the covariance matrix update step. In the standard CMA-ES, this step involves an O(d³) eigenvalue decomposition. The authors propose replacing this with a direct update of the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, which has a complexity of O(µd²). This approach is based on prior work that developed similar updates for simpler variants of CMA-ES. A key challenge in adapting this to the standard CMA-ES is the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the inverse of the matrix square root (C⁻¹/²), not just the inverse of a Cholesky factor (A⁻¹). The authors address this by using A⁻¹ as an approximation for C⁻¹/². They provide a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) suggesting that the error introduced by this approximation should diminish as the algorithm converges and the shape of the covariance matrix stabilizes. Empirical results on several benchmark functions demonstrate that the proposed "Cholesky-CMA-ES" requires a similar number of function evaluations as the standard algorithm but is significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, especially in higher dimensions.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically plausible, but key claims are not sufficiently supported.
*   **Strength:** The empirical results are compelling. The wall-clock speedup shown in Figures 1-3 is significant and clearly demonstrates the practical benefit of avoiding the full matrix decomposition in every step. This is a valuable practical contribution.
*   **Weakness:** A crucial claim is that the proposed sequence of rank-one updates to the Cholesky factor is equivalent to the standard rank-µ update given in Equation (2). This is essential for the method's validity. While similar derivations exist for simpler ES variants (e.g., Krause and Igel, 2015), this equivalence is not proven or explicitly derived for the general (µ, λ)-CMA-ES in this paper. This should be included for the work to be self-contained and verifiable.
*   **Weakness:** The central theoretical argument revolves around the approximation used in the CSA path update (Equation 5). The authors claim the error introduced by replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ is small and decreases over time as the matrix shape converges. This is a strong claim that requires direct empirical validation. The current experiments only compare final performance (function evaluations to convergence). To properly support this claim, the authors should present results that explicitly track the magnitude of the rotational error (e.g., the Frobenius norm of Eᵀt-₁Eₜ - I) over the course of optimization runs. Without this, the justification for the approximation remains speculative.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Strength:** The motivation for the work is clear, and the background on CMA-ES is presented concisely. The proposed algorithm is laid out well in Algorithm 1.
*   **Weakness:** The discussion of time complexity is not as precise as it could be. The authors claim an O(µd²) complexity, which is an improvement over the O(d³) per-iteration decomposition. However, they compare against a reference implementation that already achieves an amortized O(d²) complexity by delaying updates. For clarity, the authors should use Θ-notation and explicitly state that their method has a per-iteration complexity of Θ(µd²), which avoids the need for a delay strategy and provides a more consistent runtime, unlike the reference method's Θ(d³) cost incurred every `d` steps.
*   **Weakness:** There are several minor typos:
    *   In Algorithm 2, `wk` should likely be `αk`.
    *   In Table 1, the dimension is referred to as `n`, but as `d` everywhere else in the text.
    *   In the text around line 185 (not numbered, but in Section 4), `f(xi,t)` is referenced, but the figure captions refer to `F`. Consistency should be maintained.

**Originality:** The originality of the work is limited.
*   **Weakness:** The core idea of performing efficient rank-one updates on a Cholesky factor for evolution strategies is not new and was previously explored by Suttorp et al. (2009) and, more directly, by Krause and Igel (2015). This paper appears to be a direct and somewhat incremental extension of Krause and Igel (2015) from the (1+1)-CMA-ES to the more general (µ, λ)-CMA-ES. The main novel contribution is the specific handling and justification of the CSA path approximation. While a useful extension, it does not represent a fundamentally new approach.

**Significance:** If the technical weaknesses were addressed, the work would be highly significant.
*   **Strength:** CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization with many applications in machine learning. A faster, numerically stable, and memory-efficient implementation would be a welcome contribution and could easily become the new standard for practitioners. The presented speedups are substantial enough to enable applications of CMA-ES to higher-dimensional problems than were previously practical.
*   **Weakness:** The significance is currently undermined by the lack of technical validation mentioned under "Quality." Until the equivalence of the update rule is formally shown and the CSA path approximation is validated empirically, practitioners may be hesitant to adopt this variant over the well-established original.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a clear and important computational bottleneck in a widely used algorithm.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a significant and practical speedup in wall-clock time without degrading optimization performance in terms of function evaluations.
*   The method is well-motivated and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The originality is limited, as the work is an incremental extension of Krause and Igel (2015).
*   The paper lacks a formal proof showing that its covariance update is equivalent to the standard CMA-ES update (Equation 2).
*   The core theoretical claim—that the error from the CSA path approximation diminishes over time—is not backed by direct empirical evidence.
*   The complexity analysis could be more precise.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper presents a promising and practical improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm with impressive empirical speedups, its contributions are undermined by limited originality and, more critically, a lack of sufficient technical validation for its core claims. The missing proof of equivalence for the covariance update and the absence of direct empirical support for the CSA approximation argument are significant weaknesses. For these reasons, the paper is not ready for publication at NIPS in its current form. I would encourage the authors to strengthen the paper by providing the necessary proofs and experimental validation.
