## Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper presents a compelling modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), dubbed Cholesky-CMA-ES, aimed at reducing computational complexity. The core idea – maintaining and updating a Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix – is elegant and well-motivated. By circumventing the need for repeated square root computations, the authors achieve a runtime complexity of O(μd²) compared to the standard CMA-ES's O(d³), offering substantial potential speedups, particularly in high-dimensional spaces. 

The paper effectively positions its contribution within the existing landscape of CMA-ES acceleration techniques, such as delayed updates and restricted model complexity, highlighting the limitations of those approaches. The theoretical justification, based on the asymptotic equivalence to standard CMA-ES as iteration progresses (under the assumption that the orthogonal matrix E_t converges to the identity), is a strong point.  The empirical results on benchmark functions demonstrate consistent speedups with no performance degradation, supporting the theoretical claims. The comparison to state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants with delay strategies strengthens the validity of the results. 

However, the paper acknowledges a crucial practical limitation: in many real-world problems, objective function evaluation time dominates, diminishing the benefit of faster covariance updates. Furthermore, the restrictive convergence assumptions underlying the theoretical equivalence are a concern. While the empirical results are encouraging, formal guarantees remain limited. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significant Speedup:** Addresses a key bottleneck in CMA-ES, offering substantial computational savings in high dimensions.
*   **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** Provides a rationale for the modification and establishes asymptotic equivalence to the original algorithm.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** Demonstrates consistent performance gains on standard benchmarks.
*   **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Practical Impact Questionable:** The benefits may be limited in scenarios where function evaluations are expensive.
*   **Restrictive Assumptions:** The theoretical guarantees rely on restrictive convergence conditions.



I strongly recommend accepting this paper. While the practical impact needs to be considered, the theoretical contribution and demonstrated speedups represent a valuable advancement in CMA-ES, particularly for applications where computational cost of covariance updates is a limiting factor. Addressing the reviewer suggestions – tracking the evolution of E_t, reporting full CMA-ES runtimes, exploring quadratic benchmarks with varied spectra, and including a real-world robotics example – would further strengthen the paper.
