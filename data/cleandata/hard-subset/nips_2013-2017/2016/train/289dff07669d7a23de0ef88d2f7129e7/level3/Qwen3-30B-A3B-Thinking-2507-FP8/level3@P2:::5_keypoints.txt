- Proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES: replace matrix square root with inverse lower-triangular Cholesky factor to cut covariance update complexity from O(d^3) to O(μ d^2) while retaining performance.  
- Builds on Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015) but provides a theoretically justified approach for the standard CMA-ES.  
- Argues orthogonal matrix E_t from RQ-decomposition converges to identity, making the Cholesky approximation asymptotically valid.  
- Lemma 1 shows the approximation error vanishes asymptotically under reasonable convergence assumptions, supporting technical soundness.  
- Theoretical analysis depends on restrictive covariance convergence assumptions that may not hold for non-convex or non-separable problems.  
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive (benchmarks, dims 4–256) and shows consistent speedups (up to 20× for d=64) with identical optimization performance.  
- Lacks more detailed theoretical error bounds and convergence rate analysis to strengthen claims.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly structured, and provides precise, implementable algorithm descriptions.  
- Needs clearer discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical assumptions and more implementation details for the Cholesky update.  
- Comparison to prior work is well-motivated but should better emphasize differences beyond the triangular factorization constraint.  
- Using Cholesky factors is an incremental novelty; main contribution is providing convergence guarantees that previous works lacked.  
- Addresses an important computational bottleneck for high-dimensional problems with cheap evaluations, relevant to real-time robotics or large-scale tuning.  
- Practical impact may be limited for typical ML settings where objective evaluation dominates and dimensions are often <100.  
- Should include a real-world experiment (e.g., robotics policy search) to demonstrate practical speed benefits.  
- Does not report actual wall-clock runtimes for full CMA-ES without delay strategies, which weakens practical claims.  
- Does not track the evolution of E_t empirically to support the convergence-to-identity claim.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solid speedup for high-dim cheap-eval problems, theoretical justification, strong empirical results, optimal storage, and free eigenvalues.  
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive theoretical assumptions, limited practical relevance for many ML tasks, missing real-world tests and wall-clock timings, and not tracking E_t.  
- Overall: technically sound, incremental but meaningful contribution; recommend acceptance if suggested theoretical and empirical clarifications are addressed.
