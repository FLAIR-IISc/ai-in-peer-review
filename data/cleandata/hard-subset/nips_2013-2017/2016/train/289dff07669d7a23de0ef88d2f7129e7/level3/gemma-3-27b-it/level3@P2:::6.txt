## Review of CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) aimed at reducing the computational cost of updating and storing the covariance matrix. The authors leverage the Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix, presenting an update scheme with quadratic time complexity and reduced memory requirements. They argue that this approach maintains the performance characteristics of the original CMA-ES, making it a viable replacement when covariance matrix handling is a bottleneck.

The CMA-ES is a well-established and powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001), widely used in machine learning for tasks like reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning (Gomez et al., 2008; Igel, 2010). The computational cost associated with covariance matrix manipulation is a known limitation, motivating research into efficient update schemes. Prior work includes reducing update frequency (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001), restricting model complexity (Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008), and updating the Cholesky factor directly (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015). 

The core contribution of this paper lies in proposing a specific method for updating the Cholesky factor within the standard CMA-ES framework, accompanied by a theoretical justification for its convergence properties. The authors demonstrate how to rewrite the CMA-ES update rules to operate directly on the triangular Cholesky factor, avoiding the need for explicit covariance matrix computation. This is achieved by carefully substituting the matrix square root with its Cholesky factor representation and addressing the resulting discrepancies in the step-size adaptation mechanism.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly identifies the computational bottleneck in CMA-ES – the covariance matrix update – and proposes a targeted solution.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a compelling argument for why their modification should not deteriorate the algorithm's performance, citing Lemma 1 and discussing the convergence of coordinate systems.
* **Practical Improvements:** The proposed approach demonstrably reduces runtime complexity, particularly in higher dimensions, offering a potential speedup for applications where covariance matrix handling is a significant cost.
* **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-structured and presents the material in a logical and understandable manner. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 and 2 enhances clarity.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Misleading Naming:** The name "Cholesky-CMA-ES" is already used to describe the work of Suttorp et al. (2009). This creates confusion and should be addressed with a new, distinct name.
* **Incomplete Comparison to Prior Work:** A crucial weakness is the omission of a direct empirical comparison with Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015). The authors acknowledge these works but do not present a comparative performance analysis, hindering a thorough assessment of the contribution.
* **Incremental Algorithmic Contribution:** The primary algorithmic novelty appears to be the straightforward application of rankOneUpdate in a loop for all individuals. While correct, the significance of this seemingly minor change requires more justification.  Is this merely an implementation detail, or does it offer benefits beyond a simple code optimization?
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** While the experiments demonstrate performance gains, expanding the comparison to include Suttorp’s and Krause’s methods is essential. The current experimental setup does not fully establish the superiority of this approach over existing Cholesky-based CMA-ES variants.
* **Overstated Optimality Claim:** The claim of "optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity" is too strong. While an improvement, the complexity remains quadratic, limiting scalability to very high dimensions. Furthermore, the approach is not optimal for problems where a diagonal or low-rank covariance matrix suffices (e.g., separable functions).
* **Questionable Stability Claim:** The assertion that Suttorp et al.’s method is numerically unstable contradicts the original paper’s claims and requires substantial justification or retraction.
* **Factual Imprecision:** The statement about eigenvalue decomposition being performed at *every* iteration is inaccurate. Implementations typically employ a delay strategy, as the authors themselves mention later in the paper.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in the CMA-ES literature.
* The theoretical justification provides a reasonable basis for the proposed modification.
* The empirical results demonstrate a tangible performance improvement in terms of runtime.
* The code is integrated into a widely used machine learning library, increasing accessibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The naming convention is confusing due to overlap with prior work.
* The lack of a comprehensive comparison with Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015) weakens the claims of novelty and superiority.
* The algorithmic contribution appears incremental and lacks sufficient justification.
* The overstated optimality claim and questionable stability assertions require clarification.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile, though incremental, improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results are encouraging. However, the lack of a thorough comparison with existing Cholesky-based CMA-ES variants and the misleading naming convention significantly detract from the overall impact. 

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with Request for Major Revisions.** The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically: (a) rename the algorithm to avoid confusion, (b) conduct a comprehensive empirical comparison with Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015), and (c) provide a more compelling justification for the algorithmic contribution. Clarifying the optimality claim and revisiting the numerical stability assessment are also crucial. A revised version addressing these points would be much stronger and more deserving of acceptance.
