This paper proposes the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) designed to reduce computational cost and memory requirements, particularly when updating and storing the covariance matrix. The core idea is to operate directly on the triangular Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix, avoiding the explicit computation of the matrix square root.  The authors provide a theoretically justified modification to the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism and demonstrate empirically that the Cholesky-CMA-ES maintains the performance of the standard CMA-ES while offering significant speed improvements, especially in higher dimensions.

The CMA-ES is a well-established derivative-free optimization algorithm with applications in machine learning, including reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001; Beyer, 2007; Eiben & Smith, 2015). A major bottleneck of the standard CMA-ES is the computational complexity of updating and storing the covariance matrix, which scales as O(d<sup>2</sup>), where *d* is the dimensionality of the search space. Previous attempts to address this issue have included delaying updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) or restricting model complexity (Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008), but these approaches can compromise performance. More recent work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015) explored updating the Cholesky factor directly, but were limited in scope.  This paper builds on this prior work, specifically extending the approach of Krause & Igel (2015) to the full CMA-ES algorithm.

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear presentation of the Cholesky-CMA-ES and the theoretical justification for its performance. By demonstrating that the modification to the CSA mechanism doesn’t fundamentally alter the algorithm’s behavior, the authors provide confidence in its effectiveness. The empirical results convincingly show that the proposed algorithm achieves significant speedups, particularly as dimensionality increases, without sacrificing the number of objective function evaluations required for convergence. This is a valuable contribution, as the computational cost of the covariance matrix update can become prohibitive in high-dimensional problems. The algorithm is also well-integrated into the Shark machine learning library, making it readily accessible for researchers.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The originality of the approach is somewhat limited. While the extension of the Cholesky factor update to the full CMA-ES is a useful contribution, it's presented as a relatively straightforward generalization of Krause & Igel (2015). The paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight the novelty or address why this extension wasn’t obvious or readily achievable.  The reliance on the covariance convergence assumption is also a potential concern. While the authors cite Beyer (2014) as supporting this assumption, a more thorough discussion of the conditions under which this assumption holds, and the potential implications if it's violated, would strengthen the theoretical foundation.  The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, could be further improved by including comparisons to more recent state-of-the-art optimization algorithms, especially those designed for high-dimensional spaces. Furthermore, the paper contains minor errors such as typos (e.g., “which can by be motivated” on page 3) and inconsistencies in notation (e.g. σ in eq. 1).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Significant speed improvements over the standard CMA-ES, particularly in higher dimensions.
*   Clear and well-motivated theoretical justification for the proposed method.
*   Practical implementation readily available in the Shark library.
*   Addresses a practical bottleneck in CMA-ES, making it more scalable to larger problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty, as the work primarily extends a previous result.
*   Reliance on the covariance convergence assumption without sufficient discussion of its limitations.
*   Minor presentation issues (typos, inconsistencies in notation).



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable extension to the CMA-ES algorithm that addresses a significant practical limitation. While the novelty is somewhat limited, the clear presentation, theoretical justification, and empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The speed improvements, particularly in high dimensions, make this a worthwhile contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the presentation issues and to further discuss the covariance convergence assumption. The authors should clarify the novelty of their contribution in relation to Krause & Igel (2015) and potentially broaden the comparative experimental analysis.
