Summary
This paper proposes a CMA-ES variant that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C instead of storing/updating C and periodically computing its square root. The key change is to (i) rewrite the rank-1 and rank-μ covariance updates as a sequence of quadratic-time rank-one updates on A, and (ii) modify CSA by replacing C−1/2 with A−1 in pσ to avoid cubic-time decompositions. A lemma argues the CSA approximation error vanishes as the covariance shape stabilizes. Experiments on standard benchmarks show similar evaluation counts to a reference CMA-ES, with substantial wall-clock speedups that grow with dimension. The method preserves full-covariance modeling but keeps quadratic time and memory.

Relation to prior work
The idea of updating a Cholesky factor within CMA-ES goes back at least to Suttorp et al. (2009), who used non-triangular factors for (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES, and to Krause & Igel (2015), who derived an efficient quadratic-time rank-one update. This paper’s contribution is to apply such rank-one updates in the standard multi-λ CMA-ES, using A−1 to approximate CSA’s C−1/2. While related work is mentioned, the discussion and empirical comparison with Suttorp (2009) and algorithmic comparison with Krause (2015) are incomplete.

Strengths
- Practical speedups for full-covariance CMA-ES with similar evaluation counts; implementation appears simple and robust.
- Clear algorithmic description; a plausible argument for the CSA approximation via convergence of the rotation component.
- Useful engineering: reduced memory footprint, quadratic-time inversion of A, and a clean rank-one update primitive.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Misleading naming: “Cholesky-CMA-ES” has been established by Suttorp et al. (2009) for a different approach. Reusing the name creates confusion and priority conflict.
- Incremental algorithmic novelty: The core change is effectively looping the known rankOneUpdate over all terms in the rank-μ update and replacing C−1/2 by A−1 in CSA. The paper should be explicit about this or justify a deeper innovation.
- Missing prior-work comparison: The paper omits a direct, careful algorithmic and empirical comparison with Suttorp’s Cholesky-CMA-ES and with Krause & Igel’s rank-one update. Given the title and claims, these are essential baselines.
- Incomplete experiments: Figures should include Suttorp’s and Krause’s variants (evaluation counts and wall-clock), not only the reference CMA-ES/delay. If space is tight, move full plots to the supplement.
- Plotting/aggregation concern: The reported difference on Cigar near the plateau could be an artifact of how medians are computed/plotted (per-run medians over time vs median at fixed target; handling of time-to-target censoring). Please clarify methodology and update plots if necessary.
- Overstated “optimality”: The approach is not optimal for classes like Sphere/separable functions where diagonal/low-rank variants suffice; it also remains quadratic and does not address large-scale CMA-ES fundamentally. Relative to Suttorp’s non-triangular Cholesky update, the compute/memory advantage appears modest (around a factor of two), not a qualitative leap.
- Questionable numerical-stability claim: The paper asserts Suttorp’s method is numerically unstable, while Suttorp et al. claimed the opposite and reported stability. Provide evidence (stress tests, condition numbers, failure cases) or soften the claim.
- Scalability unchanged: Complexity is still O(μd^2); this saves constants for full covariance but does not enable the truly large-scale regime where LM-CMA and related low-memory schemes shine.
- Minor factual correction: In practice, eigenvalue decompositions are performed periodically in CMA-ES implementations, not every iteration; the intro should reflect this common practice when discussing per-iteration costs.
- Clarifications: The paper states “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues.” This is incorrect in general; Cholesky diagonals are not eigenvalues except in special cases (e.g., diagonal C).

Quality
Technically sound overall with a reasonable approximation argument for CSA, but key empirical and algorithmic comparisons are missing, and some claims (stability, optimality) are overstated without sufficient evidence.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. Needs clearer positioning vs. Suttorp (2009) and Krause (2015), explicit statement of the main algorithmic change, and clarification of plotting/aggregation and numerical stability claims.

Originality
Incremental. The main novelty is applying known rank-one Cholesky updates in the standard CMA-ES with a pragmatic CSA approximation. The naming suggests a larger conceptual leap than is substantiated.

Significance
Useful engineering contribution for practitioners who need full-covariance CMA-ES faster; significance would be stronger with thorough comparisons to prior Cholesky-based variants and clearer quantification of gains.

Requested fixes before acceptance
- Resolve naming to avoid conflict with Suttorp et al. (2009). Consider a distinct name (e.g., Triangular-Cholesky CMA-ES).
- Add experiments including Suttorp’s Cholesky-CMA-ES and Krause & Igel’s update as baselines (eval counts and runtime), and update figures; put extended plots in the supplement if needed.
- Clarify that the main algorithmic change is looping rankOneUpdate across all rank-μ terms and using A−1 in CSA, or argue why it is more than that (e.g., new analysis, stability guarantees).
- Provide evidence for the numerical-stability claims or soften them; report conditioning and failures across tests.
- Clarify plotting methodology (median definition, handling of censored runs) and the Cigar plateau discrepancy.
- Tone down “optimality” claims (scope limited to full-covariance updates; quadratic complexity remains).
- Correct the statements on EVD periodic updates and the Cholesky diagonal/eigenvalue issue.

Pros
- Simple, implementable change yielding strong speedups for full-covariance CMA-ES.
- Empirically preserves evaluation efficiency; theory plausibly supports CSA approximation.
- Practical memory/time savings without restricting the model class.

Cons
- Naming conflict and insufficient engagement with closely related prior work.
- Incremental contribution; some overstatements and a few factual inaccuracies.
- Missing critical baselines and methodological clarifications.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject in current form due to naming, missing comparisons, and overstated claims; potentially acceptable after addressing the above issues with stronger empirical evidence and clearer positioning.
