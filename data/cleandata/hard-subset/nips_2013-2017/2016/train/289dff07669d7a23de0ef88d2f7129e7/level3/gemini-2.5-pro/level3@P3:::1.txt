This paper presents Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that significantly reduces its computational and memory complexity without sacrificing optimization performance. The standard CMA-ES is a cornerstone of derivative-free optimization, but its per-iteration complexity of O(d³) due to covariance matrix decomposition has limited its application to high-dimensional (large `d`) problems where function evaluations are cheap. This work tackles this bottleneck by proposing a method to directly update the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, rather than the full matrix itself. This lowers the update complexity to an asymptotically optimal O(µd²).

The key technical challenge lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism of CMA-ES, which requires the inverse of the matrix square root, C^(-1/2), for normalization. This cannot be directly replaced by the inverse of a Cholesky factor, A^(-1), as they differ by a rotation matrix. The authors cleverly navigate this by arguing and providing theoretical justification (Lemma 1) that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the error introduced by this substitution vanishes. This insight allows them to build a practical, fast, and numerically stable algorithm that retains the excellent performance characteristics of the original CMA-ES. This work builds upon previous efforts to improve CMA-ES efficiency (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), but provides a more complete and well-justified solution for the standard, widely-used CMA-ES algorithm.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known and highly practical limitation of one of the most powerful black-box optimization algorithms. By drastically reducing the wall-clock time for medium-to-large dimensional problems, this work makes CMA-ES accessible to a wider range of applications in machine learning, such as direct policy search in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning, where the cost of an iteration's overhead can dominate the cost of function evaluations. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a strong candidate to become the new default implementation.

*   **Quality:** The technical contribution is sound. The theoretical justification for approximating the CSA path update is plausible and well-reasoned, establishing that the coordinate system defined by the Cholesky factor changes smoothly and converges. The empirical evaluation is thorough, using a standard set of benchmark functions across a range of dimensions. The results compellingly demonstrate that the proposed method achieves massive wall-clock speedups (e.g., 20x at d=64) with no statistically significant degradation in the number of function evaluations required for convergence compared to the standard implementation.

*   **Clarity and Originality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The motivation for the work is well-established, the technical details of the proposed modification are explained precisely, and the experimental results are presented effectively. The originality lies in the specific, justified modification to the CSA path, which successfully enables the use of efficient rank-one updates on a triangular Cholesky factor within the full CMA-ES framework, a problem that previous works had not fully solved. Furthermore, the approach yields valuable secondary benefits, such as obtaining the eigenvalues of the covariance matrix (via the diagonal of the Cholesky factor) for free, which is useful for monitoring problem conditioning.

**Weaknesses / Points for Clarification:**

The paper is very strong, and the following points are minor requests for clarification that would further solidify its contribution:

1.  The wall-clock time comparisons are central to the paper's claims. To ensure the comparisons are entirely fair, it would be helpful for the authors to explicitly state whether all compared algorithm variants (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES, and Cholesky-CMA-ES) were implemented in the same language and linked against the same high-performance linear algebra library (e.g., LAPACK, as mentioned for their own implementation). This would eliminate any potential confounds from implementation differences.

2.  In Figure 2 (Function Evaluations), the performance difference on the ill-conditioned Cigar and Discus functions between the proposed method and the delayed-update CMA-ES/d is surprisingly small at d=256. One might expect that using an outdated covariance matrix for `d` steps on such functions would lead to a more pronounced increase in function evaluations. Could the authors offer some intuition as to why this difference isn't larger? Is it because the total number of evaluations is already so high that the penalty is less significant in relative terms, or is there another dynamic at play?

**Overall Evaluation:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant and highly practical contribution to the field. It solves a major computational bottleneck in a foundational algorithm, backing up its claims with solid theoretical reasoning and extensive empirical evidence. The work is of high quality, clearly presented, and directly relevant to the NIPS community, with immediate applications in reinforcement learning, model selection, and other areas requiring robust derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers clear advantages in speed and memory with no apparent downside, making it a compelling replacement for the standard algorithm in many use cases. I strongly recommend acceptance.
