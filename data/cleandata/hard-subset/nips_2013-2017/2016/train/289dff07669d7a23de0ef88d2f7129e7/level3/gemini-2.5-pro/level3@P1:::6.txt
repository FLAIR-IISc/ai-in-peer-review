### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm. The standard CMA-ES requires an O(d³) operation (typically an eigenvalue decomposition) to compute the matrix square root needed for the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA). The authors propose a variant, which they name "Cholesky-CMA-ES", that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix directly. This reduces the time complexity of the update to O(µd²) and the storage complexity to O(d²).

The core modification lies in the CSA mechanism, where the inverse of the matrix square root (C⁻¹/²) is replaced by the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor (A⁻¹). The authors provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) arguing that this approximation is sound because as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the rotation matrices involved in the decomposition stabilize, causing the error introduced by this substitution to vanish. Empirical results on several benchmark functions show that the proposed method achieves the same convergence in terms of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but is significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, especially for higher dimensions.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound in its derivation of the Cholesky update and the supporting theoretical argument in Lemma 1 is plausible. The experimental results clearly demonstrate a significant speedup in wall-clock time, which is a valuable practical contribution.

However, there are several weaknesses regarding the claims and evaluation:
1.  **Unsupported Claim of Numerical Instability in Prior Work:** The paper asserts that its approach is preferable to non-triangular Cholesky updates (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009) because the latter is "prone to numerical instabilities." This is a strong claim made without any supporting evidence. The original Suttorp et al. paper, in fact, discusses the numerical stability of their method. This unsubstantiated claim should be either proven or removed.
2.  **Overstated Optimality:** The claims of "asymptotically optimal time complexity" and "optimal memory complexity" are misleading. While O(µd²) is a significant improvement and is efficient for adapting a full matrix, it is not optimal in a general sense. For simple problems like the Sphere function or separable functions, diagonal or identity covariance matrices are sufficient, leading to linear complexity. The proposed method's complexity is only about a factor of 2 better than the Suttorp et al. method, which is also quadratic. The claims should be more carefully qualified.
3.  **Scalability:** The paper correctly notes that for very large `d`, one must resort to low-dimensional approximations. However, the framing could be clearer that this work, while improving efficiency, does not fundamentally change the quadratic scaling limitation of full-covariance CMA-ES.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and organized. Algorithm 1 clearly presents the proposed method. However, there are two major points of confusion:
1.  **Misleading Name:** The name "Cholesky-CMA-ES" has already been used in the literature to refer to the method proposed by Suttorp et al. (2009). Using the same name for a different variant creates unnecessary confusion and makes it difficult to situate this work correctly. A different name is strongly recommended.
2.  **Experimental Details:** In Figure 1, the plot for the Cigar function shows the Cholesky-CMA-ES plateauing at a slightly higher function value than the reference implementation. It is unclear if this is a genuine, albeit small, performance degradation or an artifact of plotting the median of the runs. This should be clarified.
3.  A minor factual clarification: the abstract and introduction imply that the O(d³) EVD is performed at every iteration in standard CMA-ES. In practice, most implementations use a delay strategy (as mentioned in the experiments section), performing the expensive decomposition only periodically. This should be stated more clearly upfront.

**Originality:**

The paper builds directly on previous work aiming to reduce the complexity of the covariance matrix update, particularly Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015). The main algorithmic novelty appears to be the extension of the rank-one update from Krause and Igel (2015) to the rank-µ case by applying it iteratively inside a `for` loop. While this is a useful extension, it feels incremental.

The most significant weakness is the lack of comparison to closely related work.
1.  **Suttorp et al. (2009):** This work proposed a non-triangular Cholesky update with O(d²) complexity. It is the most direct competitor, yet it is only briefly mentioned and not included in the empirical comparison. A thorough comparison is essential to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed triangular approach.
2.  **Krause and Igel (2015):** This paper introduced the efficient rank-one update for a triangular Cholesky factor. The current paper's primary contribution is generalizing this to the rank-µ update. The relationship and precise novelty should be made more explicit.

**Significance:**

The work is significant from a practical standpoint. The CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization and machine learning applications like policy search and hyperparameter tuning. A method that substantially reduces its runtime without sacrificing performance is a valuable contribution that practitioners are likely to adopt. The provided speedup is impressive and makes CMA-ES more feasible for moderately high-dimensional problems where the function evaluation is cheap.

However, its scientific significance is diminished by the failure to properly compare against and differentiate from the most relevant prior art. Without a clear comparison to Suttorp et al. (2009), the real-world advantage of this specific approach over other quadratic-time variants remains unclear.

### Summary of Arguments and Recommendations

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-known and important computational bottleneck in the widely used CMA-ES algorithm.
*   The proposed method provides a significant and well-documented speedup in wall-clock time.
*   The theoretical argument for the validity of the CSA approximation is reasonable.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The contribution appears incremental over Krause and Igel (2015).
*   The paper fails to empirically compare against its most direct and relevant competitor, the method of Suttorp et al. (2009).
*   The chosen name "Cholesky-CMA-ES" conflicts with established terminology, causing confusion.
*   The paper makes overstated claims about optimality and unsubstantiated claims about the numerical instability of competing methods.

**Recommendation:**

The paper presents a practically useful improvement to the CMA-ES but in its current form, it lacks the scientific rigor expected for NIPS. The omission of a comparison with Suttorp et al. (2009) is a major flaw. I would recommend rejection at this stage, but I believe the paper could be a strong candidate for acceptance if the following issues are addressed:

1.  **Rename the Algorithm:** Choose a new name to avoid conflict with Suttorp et al. (2009), for example, "Triangular Cholesky CMA-ES" or "TCMA-ES".
2.  **Expand Experiments:** Conduct a new set of experiments that include the Suttorp-CMA-ES as a baseline. This is crucial for validating the claims of superiority. A comparison with the Krause and Igel (2015) variant would also strengthen the paper.
3.  **Clarify Contribution:** Be more explicit about how the algorithm extends Krause and Igel (2015) and how it differs from Suttorp et al. (2009) algorithmically.
4.  **Revise Claims:** Temper the claims about optimality and either provide evidence for the numerical instability of other methods or remove the claim.
