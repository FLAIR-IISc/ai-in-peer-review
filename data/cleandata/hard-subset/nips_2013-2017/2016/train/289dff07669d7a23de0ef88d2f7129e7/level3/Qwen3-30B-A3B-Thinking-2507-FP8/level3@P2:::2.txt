

The paper proposes "Cholesky-CMA-ES," a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the matrix square root with a Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors claim their method achieves O(μd²) time complexity for covariance matrix updates while maintaining performance comparable to standard CMA-ES. This work builds upon Hansen and Ostermeier's foundational CMA-ES (2001) and recent extensions like Krause and Igel (2015), which proposed a similar Cholesky-based update for a simplified CMA-ES variant.

Strengths:
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to method to experiments.
- The implementation is available in the Shark library, enhancing reproducibility.
- The empirical results demonstrate promising speedups (up to 20x for d=64) without increasing function evaluations.
- The authors correctly identify the need to reduce memory requirements for high-dimensional optimization.

Weaknesses:
- Limited originality: The core idea is a straightforward extension of Krause and Igel (2015), which already proposed a Cholesky-based covariance update for a different CMA-ES variant. The authors fail to sufficiently distinguish their contribution from this prior work, making the novelty questionable for NIPS.
- Missing theoretical justification: The authors claim their modification "does not change the behavior of the CMA-ES," but provide no proof of equivalence to the standard CMA-ES (equation 2). This is essential for establishing that the algorithm maintains the same theoretical properties.
- Unclear complexity claims: The authors state their method achieves O(μd²) complexity but fail to explain how this differs from the standard CMA-ES with delayed matrix square root computation (CMA-ES-Ref). They should use Θ-notation and provide a detailed complexity analysis showing why their method is actually faster.
- Unvalidated path-error claim: The paper claims the error introduced by replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ "decreases over time" (Lemma 1), but this is not supported by experimental evidence. The authors only show final performance, not the claimed convergence of the error term.
- Notational errors: The paper contains several inconsistencies (e.g., "wk" vs. "α_k" in Algorithm 2, "n" vs. "d" in Table 1, "F" vs. "f" on line 185) that undermine clarity.

Quality: The method appears numerically sound but lacks theoretical justification for the key equivalence claim. The experimental validation is limited to final performance metrics rather than the claimed time complexity advantages.

Clarity: The writing is generally clear, but the notational errors and lack of theoretical proof reduce overall clarity. The algorithm description is mostly precise but could benefit from more rigorous notation.

Originality: The contribution is a minor extension rather than a significant advance. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from Krause and Igel (2015), which already addressed a similar problem for a different CMA-ES variant.

Significance: While the speedup claim is potentially valuable for high-dimensional optimization, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support it. The experiments only demonstrate wall-clock time improvements, not the claimed algorithmic complexity advantages.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear presentation of a potentially useful optimization technique
- Promising empirical results showing speedups
- Practical utility for large-scale optimization problems

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient novelty compared to prior work
- Critical theoretical gap in the equivalence proof
- Unclear complexity claims without detailed analysis
- Unvalidated theoretical claim about path error
- Notational errors requiring correction

The paper addresses an important problem in derivative-free optimization, but the lack of sufficient novelty, theoretical justification, and clarity in complexity claims makes it unsuitable for publication at NIPS. The authors should address these issues before resubmission, particularly by providing a proof of equivalence to standard CMA-ES and a more detailed complexity analysis. The current form does not represent a significant contribution to the field.
