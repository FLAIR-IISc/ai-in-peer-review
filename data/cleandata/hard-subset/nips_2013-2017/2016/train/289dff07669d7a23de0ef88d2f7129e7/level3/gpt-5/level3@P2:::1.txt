Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a CMA-ES variant that maintains and updates the triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T, rather than C itself or its symmetric square root. This change enables an O(µ d^2) per-iteration update with minimal memory, while preserving the behavior of the standard algorithm in terms of function-evaluation efficiency. The main technical challenge is that the CSA step-size update uses C^{-1/2}; the authors replace it with A^{-1} and justify the approximation by analyzing the RQ decomposition of C^{1/2} and showing that the sequence of rotations converges (under mild shape convergence assumptions), making the error in the CSA norm vanish over time.

This builds on and extends prior work that (i) restricted model complexity (e.g., diagonal/separable CMA-ES: Ros and Hansen 2008; low-memory/limited-memory variants: Loshchilov 2014, 2015), (ii) delayed decompositions to amortize cubic costs (the reference CMA-ES implementation), and (iii) updated Cholesky factors directly in specific CMA-ES variants ((1+1)-CMA-ES, MO-CMA-ES; Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015). In contrast, the present paper addresses the standard, full CMA-ES with rank-1 and rank-µ updates and provides a theoretical rationale for why the CSA approximation should not degrade behavior. Empirical results on standard and rotated test functions up to d = 256 demonstrate equal function-evaluation counts compared to the reference CMA-ES and substantial wall-clock reductions (e.g., ~20× at d = 64), with stronger benefits as d grows.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The rank-1/µ updates on A achieving O(µ d^2) are consistent with prior Cholesky-update literature. The CSA replacement C^{-1/2} → A^{-1} is the key approximation; the RQ-based argument (Lemma 1) suggests vanishing error when the covariance “shape” stabilizes, and the exponentially weighted CSA attenuates older rotations. This is reasonable and aligns with observed parity in evaluation counts. It would be valuable to add a finite-time bound (e.g., in terms of max rotation drift ||E_t E_{t-1}^T − I|| and CSA horizon) to complement the asymptotic argument.
- Empirical evaluation: Solid, with multiple functions (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers), randomized rotations, multiple dimensions, 100 trials, and both function-evaluation and wall-clock metrics. Baselines include the reference CMA-ES with delayed decompositions, a d-step delayed variant, and an adapted Suttorp-style factorization.
- Reproducibility: Algorithmic details and pseudo-code are provided, and an implementation in Shark with LAPACK is used. Please clarify whether all baselines use identical code paths, compiler flags, BLAS/LAPACK backends, and threading to ensure apples-to-apples runtime comparisons.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} and the implications for invariance and CSA are explained succinctly. Minor clarifications:
- The claim that “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix” is generally not correct. diag(A) contains square roots of the Cholesky pivots, not eigenvalues. If you meant that singular values of A equal sqrt(eigenvalues(C)), that requires an SVD of A and is not “for free.” Please correct and restate what quantities you use for monitoring conditioning and enforcing variance floors.
- For Fig. 2, the reduced speedup at d = 256 for Cigar/Discus is briefly attributed to L2 cache effects. Could you quantify this (e.g., memory-bandwidth profiling) and explain why it is more pronounced for these functions? Is the number of updates or the proportion of rank-µ updates different across functions at high d?

Originality
The idea of working with Cholesky factors in CMA-ES is not new per se, but the specific extension to the standard rank-µ CMA-ES with a CSA-compatible, theoretically motivated approximation and a practical, numerically stable O(µ d^2) update appears novel and substantively advances the line of work beyond Suttorp et al. (non-triangular updates) and Krause & Igel (µ = 1). The combination of theory and strong empirical validation adds originality.

Significance
This is an impactful contribution for practitioners who rely on full CMA-ES on non-separable, ill-conditioned problems where full covariance adaptation is essential. Achieving the same function-evaluation performance with far less wall-clock time and memory is highly valuable, and the method is a plausible candidate default for medium to large d where full C is still maintainable. The work is broadly relevant to ML areas using derivative-free optimization (policy search in RL, hyperparameter tuning).

Questions and suggestions
- Robustness: How does the CSA approximation behave during transient phases with rapidly changing covariance (e.g., Rosenbrock early iterations) or in noisy settings? You report equal evaluation counts overall; a breakdown by phases, and noise experiments, would strengthen confidence.
- Numerical stability: Do you ever re-factorize (from scratch) to control round-off error in repeated rank-1 updates? If so, how often? What diagonal-jitter strategy is used to guarantee SPD?
- Fair runtime comparison: Please specify hardware details, compiler flags, BLAS vendor/threads, and whether all algorithms use exactly the same sampling/evaluation pipelines and memory layouts. If Suttorp-CMA-ES requires extra passes or different memory traffic, note that explicitly.
- Baselines: While full-covariance methods are the target, including a discussion on when LM-CMA (Loshchilov 2014/2015) might be preferable (very large d) would help practitioners choose appropriately.

Pros
- Clear, principled algorithmic change with strong practical upside (O(µ d^2) time, optimal storage).
- Theoretical justification for CSA approximation; preserves evaluation efficiency in practice.
- Comprehensive experiments with rotations, multiple functions, and dimensions; consistent speedups.
- Implementation details and accessible pseudo-code; integration into a known library.

Cons
- The eigenvalue claim is inaccurate and should be corrected; conditioning monitoring needs clarification.
- No finite-time bound on CSA error; only asymptotic argument.
- Runtime-comparison methodology could be more thoroughly documented to preempt concerns.
- Limited discussion of robustness to noise and early-transient behavior.

Overall assessment
A strong, well-motivated, and practically important contribution that improves the computational profile of full CMA-ES without sacrificing optimization performance. With minor clarifications and corrections (eigenvalue claim, runtime methodology, Fig. 2 behavior), I recommend acceptance.
