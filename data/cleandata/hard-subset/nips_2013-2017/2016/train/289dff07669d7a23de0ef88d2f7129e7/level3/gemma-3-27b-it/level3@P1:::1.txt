This paper introduces the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a modification to the widely used Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) designed to reduce computational complexity and memory requirements during covariance matrix updates. The core idea is to operate directly on the triangular Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix instead of computing and updating the matrix itself. This approach is motivated by the observation that the matrix square root, crucial to the standard CMA-ES, can be effectively replaced by its Cholesky factor without significantly altering the algorithm's behavior. The authors provide a theoretical justification for this claim and demonstrate empirically that the Cholesky-CMA-ES maintains comparable performance in terms of function evaluations while achieving significant speedups in wall-clock time, particularly as problem dimensionality increases.

The CMA-ES is a powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm with broad applications, notably in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter tuning. However, its computational cost scales quadratically with the dimensionality of the problem, primarily due to the covariance matrix updates. Previous attempts to address this issue have involved either delaying updates (reducing runtime at the cost of potentially more function evaluations) or restricting the covariance matrix structure (potentially hindering performance on non-separable problems). This work builds on recent approaches utilizing Cholesky factorization, but importantly, provides a theoretical basis for its use within the *standard* CMA-ES framework—something lacking in prior work. The proposed method achieves optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity, making it a potentially valuable improvement for large-scale optimization tasks.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated approach. The justification for replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor is clearly articulated, and the theoretical analysis, while not exhaustive, provides a convincing argument for the algorithm’s convergence properties. The inclusion of Lemma 1 is particularly helpful in understanding the error introduced by the approximation.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The presentation of the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings is clear and concise.  The authors effectively connect their work to existing literature and provide sufficient detail for a knowledgeable reader to understand and potentially reproduce their results.
* **Originality:** The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES represents a novel contribution. While Cholesky factorization has been used in the context of CMA-ES variations before, this work presents the first theoretically justified and practically demonstrated adaptation for the *standard* CMA-ES algorithm.
* **Significance:** The demonstrated speedups, particularly in higher dimensions, are significant. Reducing the computational burden of covariance matrix updates will make the CMA-ES more accessible for larger, more complex problems, widening its applicability in fields like machine learning and robotics. The algorithm’s ability to provide covariance eigenvalues as a byproduct is a further, useful feature.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Implementation Details:** While the experimental results are compelling, the paper could benefit from more detail regarding the implementation. Specifying the programming language, compiler, and libraries used (beyond mentioning LAPACK) would help ensure the fairness of the runtime comparisons. It's important to confirm that differences in speed are attributable to the algorithm itself, rather than implementation-level optimizations.
* **Figure 2 Interpretation:** The results presented in Figure 2, while generally supportive of the authors' claims, show a less dramatic difference between the algorithms for the Cigar and Discus benchmark functions at d=256 than for others. A brief discussion of this observation and potential explanations (e.g., specific characteristics of these functions that might mitigate the benefits of the Cholesky factorization) would strengthen the analysis.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** The authors acknowledge the impact of cache size at d=256. A more detailed discussion of the practical limitations of the algorithm as dimensionality continues to increase (and potential strategies for mitigating these limitations) would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*  The paper addresses a significant bottleneck in the CMA-ES algorithm, offering a concrete and effective solution.
* The theoretical justification and empirical validation are strong.
* The potential impact on the broader optimization and machine learning communities is substantial.
* The paper is well-written, clear, and presents a significant advancement over existing approaches.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of detailed implementation information could raise concerns about the reproducibility and generalizability of the results. (This is a minor concern.)
* The observation in Figure 2, if unexplained, could suggest limitations of the approach for certain problem structures. (Also a minor concern.)

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a promising improvement over the standard algorithm, offering a compelling trade-off between computational efficiency and performance. While addressing the minor weaknesses mentioned above would further strengthen the paper, I **strongly recommend acceptance** to NIPS 2016. This work has the potential to become a standard component in CMA-ES implementations and will likely be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners alike.
