This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a highly effective derivative-free optimization algorithm. The primary cost in each iteration for high-dimensional problems often stems from the O(d^3) complexity of decomposing the covariance matrix C, which is required for sampling and for the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism. The authors propose the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant that avoids this expensive decomposition by directly maintaining and updating the triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix (where C = AA^T). This reduces the update complexity to O(µd^2). The core technical challenge lies in adapting the CSA update, which requires the matrix square root C^{-1/2}. The authors propose replacing this with the inverse of the triangular factor, A^{-1}, and provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) arguing that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the error introduced by this approximation vanishes. This work builds upon previous efforts to improve CMA-ES efficiency, such as delayed updates (Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001) or other Cholesky-based updates for CMA-ES variants (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015), but offers a novel and well-justified approach for the standard CMA-ES algorithm.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The proposed algorithm is a complete and practical contribution. The central claims—that the Cholesky-CMA-ES significantly reduces wall-clock time without impairing optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations)—are convincingly supported by extensive experiments on a suite of standard benchmark functions across various dimensions. The comparison against reference implementations is fair and clearly demonstrates the benefits of the proposed approach.

One minor weakness is the claim that the proposed method is "numerically stable" and that separate updates for A_t and A^{-1}_t are "prone to numerical instabilities." While intuitively plausible, this claim is not substantiated with a detailed explanation or evidence. A clearer theoretical or empirical argument for the improved numerical stability would strengthen the paper. For instance, is this due to avoiding the explicit formation and inversion of the full covariance matrix, or specific properties of the rank-one update on the Cholesky factor?

**Clarity:**
The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the background is presented concisely, and the derivation of the Cholesky-CMA-ES is easy to follow. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 makes the implementation details transparent. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, allowing for easy interpretation and reproducibility by an expert in the field.

**Originality:**
The idea of using Cholesky factorization for covariance matrix updates in evolution strategies is not new, and the authors correctly cite relevant prior work. However, the key originality of this paper lies in its successful and well-justified application to the *standard* CMA-ES algorithm. Previous attempts struggled to reconcile the efficient Cholesky update with the CSA mechanism's requirement of a matrix square root. The authors' approximation (replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1}) and the theoretical argument in Lemma 1 that justifies it is a novel and important contribution. This distinguishes the work from prior art and provides a robust solution to a long-standing problem.

**Significance:**
The work is highly significant. CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization with numerous applications in machine learning, from reinforcement learning to hyperparameter tuning. The computational cost of the covariance matrix update has been a major barrier to its application in higher dimensions. This paper provides a practical, drop-in replacement for the standard CMA-ES that substantially lowers this barrier. The impressive speed-ups shown in the experiments (e.g., 20x faster at d=64) will enable researchers and practitioners to apply CMA-ES to larger problems than were previously feasible. The fact that this speed-up comes with optimal storage complexity and without a performance penalty makes the contribution particularly valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper solves a well-known and significant computational bottleneck in one of the most important derivative-free optimization algorithms.
*   **Pro:** The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves asymptotically optimal time and space complexity for the full covariance matrix update, making it a valuable practical tool.
*   **Pro:** The claims are backed by strong theoretical motivation and convincing empirical results, demonstrating a substantial reduction in wall-clock time without loss of optimization performance.
*   **Pro:** The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and places the contribution well within the context of existing literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The claim regarding improved numerical stability is asserted but not sufficiently explained or demonstrated.
*   **Con:** The theoretical justification relies on the assumption that the covariance matrix's shape converges, which might not always be true, though the empirical results suggest the approximation is robust in practice.

**Constructive Suggestions:**
The conclusion rightly points to applications like training neural networks. However, for very high-dimensional problems (e.g., deep neural networks), the O(d^2) complexity of the Cholesky-CMA-ES would still be prohibitive. The paper would be even more forward-looking if it included a brief discussion on how this efficient update machinery could potentially be combined with low-dimensional covariance matrix approximations (e.g., the work of Loshchilov, 2015) to tackle even larger-scale black-box optimization problems.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a significant and practical improvement to a widely used algorithm. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses.
