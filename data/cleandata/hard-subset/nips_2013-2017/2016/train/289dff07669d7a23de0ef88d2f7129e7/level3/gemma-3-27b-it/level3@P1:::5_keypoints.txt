- Paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES: maintain/update Cholesky factor to reduce complexity from O(d^3) to O(μ d^2), justified via convergence of an orthogonal matrix and validated on benchmarks with substantial speedups.
- Strength — Technical soundness: well-defined algorithm with clear theoretical motivation via Cholesky decomposition and orthogonal matrix E_t.
- Strength — Computational efficiency: significant reduction in computational complexity, beneficial for high-dimensional problems.
- Strength — Empirical validation: experiments show substantial runtime speedups and comparable convergence on benchmark functions.
- Strength — Clear presentation: paper is well-written and includes helpful pseudocode (Algorithm 1 and 2).
- Weakness — Restrictive theoretical assumptions: convergence of E_t to identity is argued under limiting conditions that may not hold generally.
- Weakness — Limited real-world impact: in many applications objective evaluation dominates runtime, reducing practical benefit; experiments focus on synthetic benchmarks.
- Weakness — Missing baseline: lacks comparison to the original CMA-ES without delay strategies to quantify true speedup.
- Weakness — Incomplete error analysis: does not thoroughly quantify the approximation error from neglecting the orthogonal matrix.
- Argument for acceptance: addresses an important scalability problem with a theoretically grounded, empirically supported, and well-presented solution.
- Argument against acceptance: restrictive assumptions, potentially limited practical benefit, and a need for stronger empirical evaluation with more baselines and problem types.
- Overall assessment: solid and promising contribution that improves scalability of CMA-ES despite theoretical limitations and the need for broader empirical validation.
- Recommendations — Investigate E_t evolution: empirically track E_t (e.g., Frobenius norm/spectrum) and compare with full CMA-ES run in parallel.
- Recommendations — Expand baselines: include standard CMA-ES without delays as a baseline.
- Recommendations — Explore problem structure: test on quadratics with varied spectral properties to assess solution quality vs. approximation error.
- Recommendations — Demonstrate real-world impact: evaluate on a real-world application where computational efficiency is critical (e.g., real-time robotics).
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10.
