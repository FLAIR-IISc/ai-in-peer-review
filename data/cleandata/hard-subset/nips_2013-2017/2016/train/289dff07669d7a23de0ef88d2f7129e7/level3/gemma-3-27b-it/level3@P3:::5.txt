This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a modification to the well-established Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) aimed at reducing computational complexity. The core idea is to maintain and update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix instead of the full matrix itself, leading to a runtime complexity of O(μd²) compared to the standard CMA-ES’s O(d³). This builds upon prior work exploring Cholesky factorizations within CMA-ES variations (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) but crucially provides a theoretical justification for its application to the full CMA-ES algorithm, hinging on the asymptotic equivalence to the original algorithm when the orthogonal matrix *E<sub>t</sub>* approaches the identity matrix.

The paper is well-motivated. CMA-ES is a powerful optimization algorithm, but its computational cost, particularly with increasing dimensionality, is a significant limitation. Several attempts to address this—delaying updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001), restricting model complexity (Poland & Zell, 2001)—have trade-offs. The proposed approach directly tackles the computational bottleneck of covariance matrix updates. The connection to information geometry (Akimoto et al., 2012) and the careful analysis of the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism are strong points.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significant Contribution:** The paper presents a solid contribution to speeding up CMA-ES, a widely used and effective optimization algorithm.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Providing a theoretical argument for the equivalence under specific conditions (convergence of *E<sub>t</sub>* to *I*) adds rigor and distinguishes this work from previous Cholesky-based approaches.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate a substantial speed-up, particularly in higher dimensions, without compromising optimization performance on synthetic tests.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the key ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Restrictive Assumptions:** The theoretical guarantee relies on the convergence of *E<sub>t</sub>* to the identity matrix, which is a strong assumption unlikely to hold in many real-world problems. The paper acknowledges this, but its practical implications deserve further emphasis.
*   **Limited Practical Impact:** While the speed-up is substantial, the paper acknowledges that evaluation time often dominates runtime in practical applications, especially with modern, cheap function evaluations, and typical problem dimensions are often moderate. This reduces the immediate usefulness of the optimization.
*   **Incomplete Baseline:** The comparison lacks a report of the actual wall-clock runtimes and performance of the *full* CMA-ES implementation (without any delay strategies) for completeness. This makes it difficult to assess the true overhead introduced by the standard delay strategy and the benefit of the Cholesky approximation.
*   **Lack of Real-World Application:** The experiments are primarily conducted on standard benchmark functions. A demonstration on a real-world problem where speed is critical (e.g., real-time robotics policy gradients) would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Track *E<sub>t</sub>* Evolution:** As suggested, tracking the evolution of *E<sub>t</sub>* (e.g., in various norms) by running both full CMA-ES and Cholesky-CMA-ES simultaneously would provide valuable empirical insight into the validity of the theoretical assumption.
*   **Spectral Variation Study:**  Investigating the impact of continuously varied spectra (following Stich & Müller, 2012) on solution quality and *E<sub>t</sub>* error would help understand how the algorithm behaves under different covariance structures.
*   **Real-World Case Study:** Including a real-world experiment where optimization speed is essential would demonstrate the practical relevance of the approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of evolutionary computation. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a clever and theoretically sound optimization that demonstrably speeds up a widely used algorithm. However, the theoretical limitations and potentially limited practical impact warrant careful consideration. 

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant computational bottleneck in CMA-ES.
*   Provides a rigorous theoretical justification for the approximation.
*   Demonstrates substantial speed-up in experiments.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   Theoretical assumptions are restrictive.
*   Practical impact may be limited by evaluation time.
*   Baseline comparison is incomplete.
*   Lacks a real-world application.



I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily addressing the suggestions for improvement outlined above. Specifically, including the missing baseline performance and a discussion of the limitations in a real-world scenario would significantly strengthen the paper.
