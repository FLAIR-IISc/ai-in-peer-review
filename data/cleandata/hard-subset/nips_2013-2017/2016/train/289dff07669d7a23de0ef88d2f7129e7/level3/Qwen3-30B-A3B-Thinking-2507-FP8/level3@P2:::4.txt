

Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm that replaces the matrix square root computation with a triangular Cholesky factorization, reducing the computational complexity from O(d³) to O(d²) for the covariance matrix update while maintaining the same number of objective function evaluations. The authors build upon previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), who explored Cholesky factorization for CMA-ES variants, but provide a crucial theoretical justification for why their approach does not degrade performance in the standard CMA-ES framework. This is significant because previous attempts to use Cholesky factorization did not establish that the algorithm's behavior would remain unchanged.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the optimization literature by addressing a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES. The theoretical foundation (Lemma 1) demonstrates that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor vanishes as the algorithm converges, which justifies why the performance remains unchanged. The empirical results are thorough, using standard benchmark functions across dimensions from 4 to 256, and clearly demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as standard CMA-ES but achieves significantly faster wall-clock times (20x faster at d=64), with the speed-up increasing with dimensionality.

The paper is technically sound and well-organized. The algorithm is presented clearly in Algorithm 1, and the theoretical arguments are logically structured. The authors adequately reference previous work on CMA-ES variants, including Hansen and Ostermeier (2001), Ros and Hansen (2008), and Suttorp et al. (2009). The paper provides additional practical benefits beyond computational efficiency: the Cholesky factorization provides eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free, which can be useful for monitoring problem conditioning, and the triangular structure enables numerically stable matrix inversion in quadratic time.

The main strength of this paper is its theoretical justification for why the Cholesky-based approach maintains performance, which is a significant advancement over previous attempts. The empirical evidence convincingly demonstrates the computational benefits, particularly for high-dimensional problems where the covariance matrix update becomes a bottleneck. The paper also makes a valuable contribution by showing that the Cholesky-CMA-ES can be implemented with optimal storage complexity, which is important for memory-constrained applications.

The main weakness is the insufficient explanation of the "numerical stability" claim. The paper states that the approach "reduces numerical instability" but does not provide concrete evidence or detailed analysis of why this is the case. A more thorough discussion of the numerical properties, perhaps with specific examples of numerical issues avoided by the Cholesky factorization, would strengthen the paper.

The significance of this work is high for the machine learning community, as CMA-ES is widely used in hyperparameter tuning and reinforcement learning. By addressing a major computational bottleneck, this paper enables more efficient optimization in high-dimensional settings. The proposed approach should be adopted as a standard improvement to CMA-ES when computational efficiency is a concern.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Theoretical justification for a practical computational improvement
- Clear demonstration of performance benefits through extensive experiments
- Practical implementation benefits (eigenvalues provided for free, optimal storage)
- The approach maintains the same optimization performance

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient explanation of numerical stability claim
- Limited discussion of combining with low-dimensional approximation methods (Loshchilov, 2015) for extremely high-dimensional problems

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful practical problem. The theoretical justification is novel and the empirical results are convincing. With a more detailed explanation of the numerical stability aspect, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the numerical stability claim.
