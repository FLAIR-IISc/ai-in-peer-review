This paper presents Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) designed to reduce its computational complexity. The standard CMA-ES has a complexity of O(d^3) per iteration due to the eigenvalue decomposition required to compute the matrix square root of the covariance matrix. This can be a bottleneck for problems where the objective function evaluation is fast or the dimensionality d is high. The proposed method reduces this complexity to O(µd^2) by directly maintaining and updating the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, instead of the full matrix. This builds upon previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015) who proposed similar updates for other ES variants.

A key challenge in applying this to the standard CMA-ES is the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the inverse of the matrix square root (C^{-1/2}) for normalization. The authors propose an approximation, replacing C^{-1/2} with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor, A^{-1}. They provide a theoretical justification for this approximation in Lemma 1, arguing that if the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the rotational error introduced by this substitution vanishes over time. The paper supports this theoretical argument with a comprehensive empirical study on standard benchmark functions, showing that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves a significant speed-up in wall-clock time compared to the standard implementation, without a discernible loss in performance regarding the number of function evaluations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Practical Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known and important limitation of CMA-ES, one of the most powerful algorithms for derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES provides a significant performance improvement in terms of wall-clock time, making the algorithm more practical for higher-dimensional problems. This is a valuable contribution for practitioners in machine learning, particularly in areas like policy search in reinforcement learning.
*   **Clear Methodology and Justification:** The proposed modification to the CMA-ES is clearly explained. The theoretical argument in Lemma 1, which justifies the crucial approximation in the CSA step, provides a solid rationale for why the method should work. Although it rests on an assumption, it insightfully connects the convergence of the covariance matrix shape to the validity of the approximation.
*   **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed algorithm against relevant baselines (including the standard implementation with delayed updates) across a range of dimensions and benchmark problems. The results are presented clearly and convincingly demonstrate the claimed speed-up while preserving the convergence properties of the original algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Originality:** The main weakness of the paper is its incremental nature. The idea of updating the Cholesky factor directly is not new (Suttorp et al., 2009). The core update mechanism is a straightforward generalization of the rank-1 update from Krause & Igel (2015) to a sequence of rank-1 updates for the rank-µ case. The primary novelty lies in successfully adapting this to the standard CMA-ES by tackling the CSA mechanism and providing a justification for the necessary approximation.
*   **Reliance on Unproven Assumption:** The theoretical justification provided in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges (C̄t → C̄). The paper mentions that in simplified algorithms the *expectation* of Ct converges (citing Beyer, 2014), but it does not fully bridge the gap to show why this assumption should hold for a single stochastic run of the standard CMA-ES. Strengthening the theoretical support for this assumption would increase confidence in the formal justification.

**Quality and Clarity:**

The paper is of high quality. It is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by the extensive experimental results. The work is presented as a complete and usable algorithm. The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized, making it easy for a reader familiar with CMA-ES to understand the contribution.

**Minor Issues:**

*   There appears to be a minor inaccuracy in the simplified update rules presented. In Eq. (1) and footnote 2, the term `(mt+1 −mt) / σt` is used. In the standard CMA-ES formulation, this term is typically divided by a temporary step-size `σ̃t` that is not adapted within the iteration. While this is likely a simplification for didactic purposes as stated by the authors, it could be a point of confusion.
*   There is a minor typo in the text describing Eq. (2) (around line 94 of a typical single-column format): "which can by be motivated" should be corrected to "which can be motivated".

**Significance:**

Despite the limited theoretical novelty, the practical significance of this work is high. The CMA-ES is a foundational algorithm in both the evolutionary computation and machine learning communities. By providing a faster, numerically stable, and memory-efficient implementation that does not sacrifice performance, this work represents a valuable engineering contribution that is likely to be adopted as the new standard for many applications. It demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art in the practical application of CMA-ES.

In summary, this paper presents a well-executed and practically important improvement to a widely used optimization algorithm. While the conceptual novelty is modest, the careful implementation, theoretical justification, and thorough empirical validation make it a strong and useful contribution.
