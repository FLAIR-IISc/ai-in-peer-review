

This paper proposes a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that uses a triangular Cholesky factorization for covariance updates instead of the standard matrix square root. The authors argue that this approach reduces both time and space complexity to O(µd²) while maintaining the same number of objective function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES. The key novelty lies in extending the Cholesky factorization approach from the (1+1)-CMA-ES (Krause and Igel, 2015) to the full CMA-ES with both rank-1 and rank-µ updates.

The paper makes a theoretically motivated argument for why replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism does not significantly alter the algorithm's behavior. The authors prove Lemma 1, showing that the error introduced by this modification vanishes as the covariance matrix converges to a stable shape. This convergence assumption is supported by references to Beyer (2014), though the paper could have more explicitly connected to this theoretical foundation. The experimental results on standard benchmark functions demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable performance to the standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluations but with significantly reduced wall-clock time, especially for higher dimensions (up to 20x faster at d=64).

The main strengths of the paper include a clear technical contribution with well-justified theoretical claims, thorough experimental validation across multiple benchmark functions, and practical implementation details that demonstrate the method's effectiveness. The algorithm is presented as a straightforward extension of previous work, with a clear description of the rank-1 update procedure for the Cholesky factor (Algorithm 2). The paper also highlights important practical advantages, such as optimal storage complexity and the ability to obtain eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free.

However, the main weakness is the limited novelty. The extension from (1+1)-CMA-ES to full CMA-ES with rank-µ updates is relatively straightforward, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution from Krause and Igel (2015) or Suttorp et al. (2009). The theoretical justification for the convergence assumption (Lemma 1) would be strengthened by more explicit connections to existing literature on CMA-ES convergence. Additionally, the paper contains minor technical errors: σ in the denominator of Equation (1) should be \tilde{σ}, and there's a typo in line 94 ("which can by be motivated" should be "which can be motivated").

The paper is well-written with clear organization and adequate technical detail for reproducibility. The experimental design is appropriate, with comprehensive benchmark testing across multiple dimensions and problem types. The results clearly demonstrate the computational advantages of the proposed method, which is significant for real-world applications where optimization speed matters.

In terms of significance, the contribution is valuable for practitioners dealing with high-dimensional optimization problems where covariance matrix updates become computationally prohibitive. The method provides a practical way to scale CMA-ES to larger dimensions without sacrificing optimization quality. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the fact that the method only addresses the computational bottleneck of covariance updates while maintaining the same number of function evaluations, which is already well-established in the literature.

The paper meets the quality criteria with sound technical content, proper theoretical justification (though could be strengthened), and convincing experimental results. The clarity is excellent, with well-structured presentation and implementation details. The novelty is marginal, as the main contribution is an extension of existing work rather than a fundamentally new approach. The significance is moderate, providing a useful computational improvement for a widely-used algorithm.

In summary, the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The Cholesky-CMA-ES is a valuable practical improvement that reduces computational costs without sacrificing performance. However, the novelty is limited, and the paper would benefit from stronger theoretical grounding and clearer distinction from prior work. The minor technical errors and the straightforward nature of the extension are notable weaknesses.

Pro: Clear technical contribution with strong experimental validation; practical computational improvement for high-dimensional optimization; theoretically justified approach to covariance update.

Con: Limited novelty as straightforward extension of existing work; theoretical justification for convergence assumption could be strengthened; minor technical errors in paper.
