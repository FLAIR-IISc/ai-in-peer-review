Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T, thereby avoiding explicit square-root computations and expensive eigendecompositions. The key change is in cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA): the original CSA uses C^{-1/2} to normalize steps, while the proposed method replaces it with A^{-1}, which is available in quadratic time. Because A and the matrix square root differ by a rotation (via an RQ/QR relation), the authors analyze the error incurred by this replacement and argue it is small and vanishes as the algorithm approaches stationarity. They provide a lemma showing that the sequence of rotation matrices converges when the normalized shape of C converges, implying the CSA norm discrepancy decays due to exponential forgetting.

The covariance update itself is implemented as a sequence of rank-one updates to the Cholesky factor, extending ideas used in special CMA-ES variants (e.g., (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES by Suttorp et al., Krause & Igel) to the standard, weighted rank-1 and rank-µ update regime. This yields O(µ d^2) time per iteration (typically µ = O(log d)) with optimal O(d^2) storage, compared to ω(d^2) with periodic cubic decompositions in the standard implementation. The approach is compared against (i) a reference CMA-ES with delayed decompositions, (ii) a variant with decomposition every d steps, and (iii) a non-triangular Cholesky-factor update scheme (Suttorp et al.). On rotated and standard benchmarks up to d = 256, the proposed method matches the number of function evaluations of the reference and is substantially faster in wall-clock time (e.g., ~20× at d = 64), with differences increasing in d.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: a principled, triangular Cholesky-factor update for the full CMA-ES that preserves sampling correctness and yields asymptotically optimal O(µ d^2) update time and O(d^2) memory.
- Theoretical insight: a clear argument for why replacing C^{-1/2} by A^{-1} in CSA does not materially affect performance, leveraging the RQ decomposition, smoothness of the triangular mapping, and exponential forgetting in CSA.
- Engineering value: the algorithm removes cubic decompositions and uneven runtime, simplifies access to A^{-1} in O(d^2) time, and integrates cleanly with BLAS/LAPACK; code is placed in an open-source library.
- Empirical validation: across standard and rotated test functions, function-evaluation counts are essentially unchanged relative to standard CMA-ES, while wall-clock reductions are large and grow with dimension.

Weaknesses and questions
- Numerical stability claims need elaboration. While triangular solves and updates are generally stable, explicit inversion of triangular matrices is typically discouraged in favor of solves. The paper should clarify whether A^{-1} is formed explicitly or used via triangular solves, and provide quantitative stability evidence (e.g., backward/forward error, conditioning of A and A^{-1}, drift in symmetry/PSD).
- The statement that the diagonal of a triangular Cholesky factor yields the eigenvalues “for free” appears incorrect in general; Cholesky diagonal elements are not the square roots of the eigenvalues of C unless C is diagonal in that basis. Please clarify what spectrum-related quantities are obtained inexpensively and how they are used (e.g., bounds on principal variances).
- Theory scope. The lemma addresses convergence of rotations when the normalized shape converges. It would help to bound the CSA norm error in finite time as a function of the rate of change of C, and to discuss regimes with non-stationary or noisy objectives where C may not stabilize quickly.
- Invariance. CSA in standard CMA-ES already relaxes full affine invariance; the proposed approximation may further affect invariance to rotations. The rotation experiments are reassuring, but additional diagnostics (e.g., angle changes of E_t vs. optimization progress) could be summarized in the main text.
- Experimental scope. Results are on synthetic testbeds. Given the paper’s motivation (RL policy search, hyperparameter tuning), at least one practical task with cheap-to-moderate evaluation cost would strengthen significance claims. Also report absolute runtime breakdowns (sampling, ranking, updates) and sensitivity to µ and population size.
- High-dimensional regimes. While the method is optimal for full-matrix adaptation, performance will still be O(d^2). Discussion and experiments combining with limited-memory/low-rank approximations (e.g., LM-CMA, Loshchilov 2014/2015) would broaden applicability to thousands of dimensions.

Quality
- The algorithmic development is sound and consistent with prior work on rank-one Cholesky updates. The CSA approximation is justified by a reasonable argument; adding finite-time bounds or empirical error plots would improve rigor. Empirical methodology is solid (multiple dimensions, rotations, multiple runs), though additional real-world tasks would help.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, with useful algorithm summaries. Please clarify numerical stability, the spectrum claim, whether A^{-1} is computed or applied via solves, and add a brief complexity table contrasting time/memory per step across variants.

Originality
- The idea to port triangular Cholesky updates to standard, weighted CMA-ES with a justified CSA modification appears novel compared to Suttorp et al. and Krause & Igel (rank-one or special-case settings). The theoretical angle on the CSA replacement is a nice addition.

Significance
- For practitioners using CMA-ES where function evaluations are not dominant, this is a meaningful speedup with minimal behavior change. The method is likely to be adopted in libraries and can serve as a new default for full-covariance CMA-ES.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative stability analysis and recommend using triangular solves instead of explicitly forming A^{-1} where possible.
- Correct or justify the claim about obtaining eigenvalues “for free.”
- Add finite-time error bounds (or empirical plots) for the CSA norm discrepancy.
- Include at least one application experiment (e.g., policy search on a standard RL benchmark or hyperparameter tuning).
- Explore or discuss combining Cholesky-CMA-ES with low-rank/limited-memory covariance models for very high dimensions.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong practical impact: large, dimension-dependent runtime reductions without extra function evaluations.
- Clean and well-motivated modification with theoretical backing.
- Extends and unifies prior Cholesky-update ideas for the full CMA-ES.

Cons for acceptance
- Some claims (numerical stability, eigenvalues from Cholesky diagonal) need clarification/correction.
- Limited real-world validation; theory could include finite-time bounds.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a valuable, implementable improvement to a widely used algorithm, with solid theory and convincing empirical results; addressing the noted clarity and stability points would make it even stronger.
