This paper proposes a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that aims to reduce the computational cost of the covariance matrix update. The core idea is to maintain and directly update a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, thereby avoiding the cubic complexity of repeated decompositions. This approach builds upon prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015), who also explored Cholesky updates for CMA-ES variants. The authors’ main adaptation involves approximating the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism to work with the triangular factor instead of the true matrix square root.

The primary strength of the paper lies in addressing the well-known computational bottleneck of the standard CMA-ES. The empirical results demonstrate a significant wall-clock speedup over a reference implementation, which is a valuable practical contribution. Furthermore, using a triangular Cholesky factor is memory-efficient compared to storing the full covariance matrix.

However, the paper suffers from several major weaknesses that undermine its contribution.

**Originality and Quality:** The paper’s most significant flaw is its engagement with prior work. It fails to provide any algorithmic or empirical comparison to the most relevant baselines: the "Cholesky CMA-ES" introduced by Suttorp et al. (2009) and the rank-one update technique from Krause et al. (2015). The main algorithmic novelty appears to be extending the rank-one update to a rank-µ update by iterating it within a "for" loop, which seems incremental. The authors must explicitly clarify what their contribution is beyond this. Furthermore, the claim that Suttorp's method is "prone to numerical instabilities" is unsubstantiated and contradicts the claims in the original paper.

**Clarity and Claims:** The chosen name, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," is misleading as it is already established by Suttorp et al. (2009), and this conflict must be resolved. The claims of "optimality" are overstated; the method's complexity remains quadratic, which does not solve the fundamental scalability issue for large dimensions, and it is not optimal for separable functions where a diagonal covariance suffices.

**Experiments:** The experiments are incomplete without comparisons to the Suttorp and Krause variants. Additionally, the plateau observed for the Cigar function could be an artifact of plotting median results, which warrants clarification.

In summary, while the work addresses an important problem, its current form is not acceptable. The limited novelty, critical omission of relevant comparisons, misleading naming, and unsupported claims prevent a fair assessment of its scientific merit. A major revision is required to address these issues.
