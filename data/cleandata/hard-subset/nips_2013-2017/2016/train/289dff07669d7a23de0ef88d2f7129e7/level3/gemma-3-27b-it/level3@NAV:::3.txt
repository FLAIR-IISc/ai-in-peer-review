## Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper presents a modification to the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) focusing on updating the triangular Cholesky factor instead of the covariance matrix itself. The authors argue that this approach, coupled with a specific adjustment to the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, reduces runtime and memory complexity without significantly affecting algorithm performance. They provide a theoretical justification for this claim based on the convergence of the Cholesky factor and validate it with empirical results.

The work builds on previous attempts to efficiently update the covariance matrix in CMA-ES, notably Krause and Igel (2015), and extends the approach to the standard CMA-ES setting. The motivation is clear: the covariance matrix update is a computational bottleneck, particularly in high dimensions, and this work directly addresses that limitation. The authors correctly identify the need to address the impact on the CSA mechanism when switching to a Cholesky factor update, which is a crucial contribution. 

However, the core contribution feels incremental. Extending the rank-1 update approach from Krause et al. (2015) to the full CMA-ES, while not trivial, seems like a straightforward generalization and lacks significant novelty.  While the theoretical justification through Lemma 1 is valuable, the reliance on the covariance convergence assumption, without stronger theoretical support (e.g., referencing Beyer, ECJ 2014 as suggested), weakens the confidence in this claim. The experimental results demonstrate a significant speedup, which is positive, but don't necessarily showcase a fundamental advancement in CMA-ES's capabilities. 

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a practical bottleneck in CMA-ES: covariance matrix update.
*   Clear motivation and well-defined problem statement.
*   Provides a theoretical justification for the proposed modification.
*   Empirical results demonstrate a significant runtime improvement.
*   Well-written and generally easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty: the extension from Krause et al. (2015) feels incremental.
*   Theoretical support for Lemma 1 is somewhat weak, relies heavily on an assumption.
*   Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive (e.g., explore different parameter settings and problem characteristics).



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

*   **Against:** The paper lacks a compelling level of novelty to warrant acceptance at a highly competitive conference like NIPS. It’s more of an engineering optimization than a fundamental algorithmic breakthrough.
*   **For:** The practical improvement in runtime is substantial and could benefit researchers and practitioners using CMA-ES, particularly in high-dimensional problems. The theoretical justification, though not airtight, provides some confidence in the approach.

**Minor Issues:**

*   σ in the denominator of Eq. (1) should be \tilde{σ}. (Same error in footnote 2 on p.3).
*   Delete extra "by" in line 94 ("which can by be motivated ..." → remove "by").



**Overall:** I recommend **rejection**. While the work is solid and demonstrates a practical improvement, the lack of substantial novelty and the relatively weak theoretical foundation do not justify acceptance to NIPS. The paper would be a good fit for a more specialized evolutionary computation conference.
