- Proposes a Cholesky-based reformulation of CMA-ES that replaces matrix square root/inverse in CSA with operations on a triangular Cholesky factor.  
- Extends prior decomposition-update ideas to standard CMA-ES and argues substituting A−1 for C−1/2 in CSA does not change evaluation performance.  
- Achieves O(µ d^2) updates with optimal storage, materially reducing wall-clock time as dimensionality increases.  
- Situates work well with existing accelerations (diagonal/structured variants, delayed decompositions, low-memory approximations).  
- Algorithmic development is sound, with clear pseudocode and a lemma showing rotation error in CSA diminishes as covariance stabilizes.  
- Experiments on rotated benchmarks show essentially unchanged evaluation counts but substantially reduced runtime; scaling trend is convincing.  
- Claim of improved numerical stability is under-explained; needs explicit rationale (e.g., backward-stability of Cholesky updates, conditioning of triangular solves, safeguards for ill-conditioned Ct).  
- Theoretical section would be strengthened by a quantitative bound on CSA error accumulation from using A−1 instead of C−1/2.  
- Paper is generally clear; the triangular vs non-triangular update distinction is insightful but practical tolerance/regularization in Algorithm 2 and reconditioning triggers should be clarified.  
- Originality: novel application of Cholesky updates to full CMA-ES with a principled CSA modification.  
- Significance: high — valuable for practitioners constrained by matrix ops; provides speedups without extra evaluations and yields eigenvalues and cheap triangular inversion.  
- Suggested experiments: noisy objectives, higher-dimensional tasks (policy search, NN hyperparameters), and comparisons to LM-CMA/sep-CMA at d ≥ 1000.  
- Suggested future work: explore hybridization with low-dimensional/low-memory approximations for regimes where full covariance is still costly.  
- Pros for acceptance: clear, principled algorithm with strong empirical runtime wins, theoretical justification, optimal time/memory, and practical drop-in applicability.  
- Cons for acceptance: insufficiently justified numerical-stability claims, lack of quantitative CSA error bounds, and limited evaluation on very high-dimensional or noisy/real-world problems.
