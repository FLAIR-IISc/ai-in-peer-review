This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) algorithm, which is a widely used derivative-free optimization method. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm reduces the computational complexity of updating and storing the covariance matrix from O(d^3) to O(μd^2), where d is the dimensionality of the problem and μ is the number of samples used in the update. This is achieved by maintaining and updating a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix.

The main idea behind the Cholesky-CMA-ES is to avoid the computation of the matrix square root, which is the dominant operation in the original CMA-ES algorithm. Instead, the Cholesky factor is updated directly, which can be done in quadratic time. The authors also provide a theoretical justification for the convergence of the Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm, showing that under certain assumptions, the error introduced by the approximation vanishes as the algorithm converges.

The empirical results presented in the paper demonstrate the effectiveness of the Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm in reducing the computational time while maintaining the optimization performance. The authors compare the Cholesky-CMA-ES with two state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that delay full covariance updates and show that the proposed algorithm achieves a substantial speed-up without compromising the optimization performance.

The strengths of the paper include the solid contribution to speeding up the CMA-ES algorithm, which is useful when evaluations are cheap and dimensionality is high. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the algorithm's complexity and convergence properties.

However, there are some concerns and limitations that need to be addressed. The theoretical assumptions made in the paper are restrictive, and formal guarantees are limited. Additionally, in most real-world problems, the objective evaluation time dominates, and typical dimensions are modest, so the speed-up achieved by the Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm may have limited practical impact.

To further improve the paper, the authors could consider the following suggestions:

* Track the evolution of the random orthogonal matrix E_t (e.g., in various norms) by running the full CMA-ES and the approximation simultaneously.
* Report the actual wall-clock runtimes and performance of the full CMA-ES without delay strategies for completeness.
* Run quadratics with continuously varied spectra (cf. Stich & Müller) to study the solution quality and E_t error versus spectral distribution.
* Include a real-world experiment where speed matters (e.g., real-time robotics policy gradients).

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and provides a promising approach to speeding up the CMA-ES algorithm. However, further work is needed to address the limitations and concerns mentioned above.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's complexity and convergence properties. However, the theoretical assumptions made in the paper are restrictive, and formal guarantees are limited.

Clarity: The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the algorithm and its properties. However, some of the mathematical derivations could be improved for better readability.

Originality: The paper proposes a novel modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, which reduces the computational complexity of updating and storing the covariance matrix. The idea of using a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix is original and valuable.

Significance: The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization, providing a promising approach to speeding up the CMA-ES algorithm. However, the practical impact of the algorithm may be limited due to the restrictive theoretical assumptions and the dominance of objective evaluation time in most real-world problems.
