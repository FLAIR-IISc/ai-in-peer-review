This paper proposes a practical and theoretically motivated variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T, and adapts the step-size by replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} in CSA. The covariance update decomposes the usual rank-1 plus rank-µ update into a sequence of rank-1 updates that can be applied directly to A in O(µ d^2) time per iteration with optimal O(d^2) storage. The key challenge is that standard CMA-ES uses C^{-1/2} in CSA; replacing it with A^{-1} introduces a changing orthogonal rotation E from the RQ decomposition C^{1/2} = A E. The paper argues (Lemma 1) that as the normalized covariance converges in shape, the rotations stabilize (E_t^T E_{t-1} → I), making the CSA approximation error vanish under the exponential forgetting of CSA. Experiments on standard continuous black-box benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) across d = 4…256, with random rotations, compare the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES to a reference CMA-ES with delayed decompositions, a version with d-step delays, and a non-triangular Cholesky update (Suttorp et al.). Results show essentially identical numbers of function evaluations to reach a target value (1e-14) for Cholesky-CMA-ES vs. the reference, but with substantially improved wall-clock time—e.g., ~20× faster at d = 64—while maintaining invariance properties to within small empirical deviations.

Relation to prior work. The paper builds on (i) the CMA-ES framework [Hansen & Ostermeier, 1996; 2001; Hansen, 2015], (ii) structured/limited-memory covariance models [Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008; Sun et al., 2013; Loshchilov, 2014; 2015], and (iii) Cholesky-based rank-1 CMA-ES updates [Suttorp et al., 2009] and the efficient rank-one update of Cholesky factors [Krause & Igel, 2015]. The contribution is to extend the factor-update approach to the general rank-1 + rank-µ CMA-ES and to modify CSA so that the algorithm can operate entirely on a triangular factor while retaining empirical sample efficiency. The information-geometric perspective on CMA-ES [Akimoto et al., 2012; 2014] and invariance discussions [Auger, 2015] provide context for the CSA modification and its potential impact on invariance.

Quality. The algorithmic development is sound, and the Cholesky rank-1 update is implemented carefully (Algorithm 2), with attention to numerical stability. The complexity claims (O(µ d^2) update, O(d^2) storage) are credible and practically important. The theoretical argument for CSA’s approximation error being small rests on convergence of the normalized covariance shape and continuity of the RQ decomposition; for the full stochastic CMA-ES this is heuristic, but plausible and supported by empirical evidence showing unchanged evaluation counts. Experiments are well chosen, include rotation tests, and report both evaluations and wall-clock time. Some experimental details that affect reproducibility are missing (hardware, BLAS/LAPACK configuration, compiler flags), and excluding non-convergent Rosenbrock runs could bias results; reporting failure rates would be preferable.

Clarity. The paper is generally clear and well organized. Presenting the CSA modification and the role of the rotation E early helps the reader follow the motivation. The algorithms are explicit. Minor notation and typographical issues remain (see Minor comments).

Originality. The main engineering idea—extending Cholesky rank-1 updates to the full rank-µ CMA-ES and adapting CSA to A^{-1}—is incremental relative to Suttorp et al. (non-triangular factors) and Krause & Igel (rank-1 triangular updates). The novelty lies in integrating these for standard CMA-ES with a simple CSA approximation and providing a convergence-based justification. While not conceptually deep, the combination is nontrivial and practically valuable.

Significance. For practitioners using full CMA-ES on moderate-to-high dimensions where function evaluations are not overwhelmingly dominant, the reported speedups without loss in evaluations are compelling. The ability to obtain eigenvalues “for free” and invert A in O(d^2) is useful for diagnostics and constraints. The contribution will likely be adopted in libraries and workflows, though it does not change the state of the art in evaluation efficiency or theory.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong practical impact: identical evaluation counts with large wall-clock speedups and reduced memory.
- Clean integration with existing CMA-ES, preserving key invariances empirically.
- Solid engineering and numerically stable factor updates; optimal asymptotic storage/runtime for full C updates.
- Clear presentation with code availability (Shark), and comprehensive benchmark coverage with rotations.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty; extension to rank-µ is a straightforward generalization of prior work.
- Theoretical support for CSA approximation is heuristic; relies on covariance-shape convergence not established for full CMA-ES.
- Experimental scope omits noisy objectives, constraints, and comparisons to state-of-the-art limited-memory CMA-ES (e.g., LM-CMA) in the same runtime budget.
- Some reporting choices (excluding failures on Rosenbrock) and missing runtime environment details.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Strengthen theory by leveraging known convergence or stationary-shape results (e.g., Beyer 2014) to bound CSA error, or provide finite-time perturbation bounds under mild assumptions.
- Add ablations: quantify CSA error over time (‖E_t^T E_{t-1} − I‖ and its effect on σ), and a variant that recomputes polar/RQ decomposition periodically to bound drift.
- Include noisy and constrained benchmarks; report failure rates instead of excluding runs; test larger d and compare against LM-CMA and diagonal/separable variants at equal evaluation and time budgets.
- Report detailed runtime environment; include memory usage and cache effects analysis.

Minor comments:
- Notation: In Eq. (1), the denominator should be the effective step-size used in the covariance-path update; σ appears where a tilde σ (as in the tutorial) is expected; the same issue appears in footnote 2 on p.3.
- Typo: line 94 “which can by be motivated” → remove the extra “by.”
- Citations need cleanup (years, formatting artifacts like “?\Q2012\E”).

Recommendation: Borderline accept. The work is incremental but well executed, with clear practical benefits and likely impact on CMA-ES implementations. If the committee prioritizes practical improvements with careful analysis over conceptual novelty, this merits acceptance.
