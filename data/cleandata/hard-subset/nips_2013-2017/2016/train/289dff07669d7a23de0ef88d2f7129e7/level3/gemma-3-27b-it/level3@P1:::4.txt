This paper proposes a modification to the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) utilizing the Cholesky factorization instead of the matrix square root, aiming to reduce computational cost, particularly for high-dimensional problems. The core idea is to update and store the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, enabling quadratic-time complexity for the update process, as opposed to the standard CMA-ES which often involves eigenvalue decomposition or repeated square root calculations. The authors provide a theoretical justification for why this modification should not impact the fundamental behavior of the CMA-ES in terms of required objective function evaluations. Empirical results demonstrate that the proposed “Cholesky-CMA-ES” achieves a significant speedup in wall-clock time, particularly as dimensionality increases, while maintaining comparable performance in terms of the number of function evaluations.

The CMA-ES is a well-established and powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm, prominently featured in prior NIPS work (e.g., Gomez et al., 2008; Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001).  Recent NIPS publications continue to explore variations and applications of CMA-ES (e.g., Akimoto et al., 2012, 2014), highlighting its ongoing relevance. Previous attempts to improve computational efficiency through Cholesky decomposition (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) have focused on specific CMA-ES variants. This work distinguishes itself by extending this approach to the standard CMA-ES, addressing the challenge of replacing the matrix square root without compromising performance. The authors correctly position their work within the context of existing research, acknowledging the trade-offs associated with alternative strategies like reducing update frequency or restricting model complexity (Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008).

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear identification of a computational bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES – the covariance matrix update – and its elegant solution through the Cholesky factorization.  The theoretical justification provided for the modified step-size adaptation is a valuable contribution, allaying concerns about potential performance degradation.  The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the speedup achieved by the Cholesky-CMA-ES, particularly in higher dimensions, solidifying its practical benefit. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, making the core ideas accessible. The connection to existing work is solid. Furthermore, the authors provide sufficient detail to allow for reproduction of their results.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the authors state the method is "numerically more stable," the argument is not fully elaborated. A more detailed discussion of the specific numerical issues that arise with the standard CMA-ES (e.g., conditioning, potential for ill-conditioned matrices) and how the Cholesky factorization mitigates these issues would strengthen the justification.  Although the performance in terms of function evaluations is comparable, a more thorough analysis of the impact on convergence speed – beyond just reaching a target function value – would be beneficial. The experimental setup, while comprehensive, could be expanded to include a wider range of problem types, including constrained optimization problems.  Furthermore, the discussion regarding scalability is somewhat limited. While the authors acknowledge the quadratic complexity, the practical limitations at very high dimensions (d=256) due to cache size are noted but not extensively addressed. Exploring combinations with low-dimensional approximation techniques, as suggested in the paper and demonstrated in Loshchilov (2015), would be a promising avenue for future work and could address this limitation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and efficient modification to a widely-used optimization algorithm.
*   Strong theoretical justification for the proposed approach.
*   Convincing empirical results demonstrating significant speedup, particularly at higher dimensions.
*   Addresses a significant computational bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the method and results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The claim of "numerical stability" is not thoroughly substantiated.
*   Limited discussion of scalability to extremely high-dimensional problems.
*   Could benefit from more extensive experimental evaluation on diverse problem types.



Overall, this paper presents a significant and valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a practical and theoretically sound improvement to the standard CMA-ES, with clear benefits for high-dimensional problems. While some minor improvements could be made, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I **recommend acceptance**.
