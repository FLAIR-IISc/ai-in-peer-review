Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C (C = A A^T) instead of storing C or its symmetric square root. This permits O(μ d^2) time and optimal O(d^2) space per iteration (with typical μ = O(log d)), compared to the ω(d^2) cost of repeated eigendecompositions/square roots in standard CMA-ES. The key algorithmic change is in cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA): the original uses C^{-1/2} in the evolution path pσ, whereas the proposed method replaces it by A^{-1} and omits the per-iteration random orthogonal factor Et that relates A to C^{1/2}. The authors justify this approximation via a convergence argument: under mild continuity and convergence assumptions on the normalized covariance sequence, the rotation Et “stabilizes,” and the norm of the accumulated pσ error decays due to exponential forgetting.

The work is positioned against (i) delaying matrix decompositions [Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001; Hansen, 2015], which achieves amortized O(d^2) but uses out-of-date matrices; (ii) structure-restricted variants (diagonal or low-rank) [Ros and Hansen, 2008; Sun et al., 2013; Akimoto et al., 2014; Loshchilov, 2014, 2015], which can degrade search on non-separable, ill-conditioned problems; and (iii) Cholesky updates for special CMA-ES variants or non-triangular factorizations [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015], which either do not align with standard CSA or forgo the uniqueness of triangular factorizations. The proposed rank-one update (Algorithm 2) composes the usual rank-1 and rank-μ covariance updates directly on A, inheriting numerical stability and enabling cheap inversion and eigenvalue access.

Empirically, on standard black-box benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) with random rotations, the authors report identical function-evaluation efficiency to a reference CMA-ES (with default delay that equals 1 for their d) and substantial wall-clock speed-ups that grow with d (e.g., ~20× at d=64), while outperforming delayed-update baselines and a non-triangular Cholesky variant.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The Cholesky rank-one update appears correct and numerically conscientious. The CSA approximation is justified by Lemma 1 and a continuity argument: the Et rotations converge as the normalized covariance converges, and the exponentially weighted evolution path diminishes residual rotation mismatch.
- Scope of guarantees: The convergence-based justification is persuasive but relies on restrictive assumptions (convergence of the normalized covariance shape and smooth changes). There is no formal bound on the bias introduced in σ updates for finite time or non-stationary phases, and affine invariance is no longer exact because the Et rotation is ignored.
- Empirical evaluation: The setup is appropriate (random rotations, multiple d up to 256, 100 trials, both function evaluations and wall-clock). The key claim—same evaluation counts, much faster runtime—is well supported. It is also helpful that Rosenbrock is included to stress slow-changing covariance. Cache effects at d=256 are transparently discussed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and self-contained, with clear algorithms and a careful background on CMA-ES and Cholesky sampling. The RQ/QR relationships and the role of Et are explained well. Suggestions:
- Make explicit the (loss of) affine invariance status with the CSA approximation and clarify whether rotation invariance is empirically preserved to within noise (the rotation-invariance tests are mentioned; summarize results in the main text).
- Provide a brief numerical-stability discussion for Algorithm 2 (e.g., jitter on diagonal, guarding against loss of definiteness).
- Fix minor reference formatting issues (several entries contain placeholders).

Originality
While Cholesky-based updates and limited-memory approximations exist, extending a triangular Cholesky update to the standard multi-offspring CMA-ES with a principled CSA approximation is new. The argument connecting Et stabilization to vanishing CSA error is a useful conceptual contribution. Compared to Suttorp et al. (non-triangular updates) and Krause & Igel (rank-1 case), this paper provides the missing piece to make triangular Cholesky updates practical for standard CMA-ES with rank-μ and CSA.

Significance
The contribution is practically significant when (i) objective evaluations are cheap or moderately expensive relative to O(d^3) matrix ops, and (ii) full covariance adaptation is needed (non-separable, ill-conditioned problems) at moderate-to-large d. This is common in simulation-based RL or tuning tasks with inexpensive surrogates. In many ML applications, evaluation time dominates and d is modest, limiting impact; nevertheless, providing an asymptotically optimal O(μ d^2) and memory-optimal implementation of a widely used optimizer is valuable, and code availability would likely drive adoption.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Track Et explicitly: Run standard CMA-ES and Cholesky-CMA-ES in lockstep on the same seeds and report norms such as ∥EtE_{t-1}^T − I∥ and the angle between pσ with and without the Et correction over time and across spectra.
- Add spectra-controlled quadratics (following Stich & Müller, 2012): vary eigenvalue distributions (e.g., power-law, clustered) to quantify CSA error and convergence speed versus spectral properties.
- Include a real-world or realistic simulated task where evaluation is cheap and speed matters (e.g., direct policy search in robotics), to demonstrate end-to-end impact.
- Report an ablation that occasionally corrects CSA by computing Et via polar decomposition every K iterations to bound any drift; this could empirically validate the theoretical argument about exponential forgetting.
- Clarify baselines: you state the default delay equals one for the d considered, which effectively matches “full” CMA-ES. For completeness, include results with forced eigendecomposition every iteration to rule out implementation subtlety.
- Consider noisy objectives: evaluate robustness of the CSA approximation under noise, where covariance may not stabilize quickly.

Pros
- Clear algorithmic idea with principled justification.
- Optimal asymptotic time and memory for full-covariance CMA-ES.
- Strong empirical speed-ups with unchanged evaluation counts.
- Practical advantages: cheap A^{-1}, eigenvalues “for free,” numerically stable triangular updates.

Cons
- Theoretical guarantees hinge on convergence assumptions; no finite-time error bounds for CSA.
- Temporary loss of affine invariance in CSA; empirical characterization of the impact could be deeper.
- Impact may be limited when evaluation cost dominates, common in many ML settings.
- Minor presentation issues in references; more detail on numerical stability would help.

Recommendation
Leaning toward acceptance. The paper offers a well-executed, practically important improvement to a core optimizer, with a sound argument for the key approximation and convincing experiments. Addressing the suggested empirical analyses (especially Et tracking and a real-world task) would further strengthen the case.
