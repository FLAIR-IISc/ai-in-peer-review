Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, an approximation to CMA-ES that replaces the standard O(d^3) covariance update and square-root computation with an O(μ d^2) scheme by maintaining and updating a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance C instead of C (or its symmetric square root). The key algorithmic change is in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA): the standard CSA uses C^{-1/2} to normalize steps, while the proposed variant uses A^{-1}. This avoids computing or tracking the random orthogonal matrix E_t that relates A to C^{1/2} through an RQ decomposition C^{1/2} = A E. The authors argue that the omitted rotation becomes negligible as E_t → I under restrictive convergence assumptions (convergence of the normalized covariance “shape” C̄_t), making the approximation asymptotically equivalent to the original CMA-ES.

This work builds on prior attempts to update decompositions directly (e.g., Suttorp et al. 2009), and extends the rank-1 Cholesky update of Krause & Igel (2015) from the (1+1)-CMA-ES to the standard rank-μ CMA-ES. It also complements work on reducing complexity via delayed updates, diagonal/low-rank models, or limited-memory CMA-ES (Ros & Hansen 2008; Loshchilov 2014/15; Sun et al. 2013), while preserving full covariance modeling.

Empirical results on standard synthetic benchmarks indicate substantial wall-clock speedups (growing with d), with essentially identical numbers of function evaluations as the reference CMA-ES. Comparisons include a state-of-the-art CMA-ES with delayed decomposition and a non-triangular Cholesky variant.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic contribution: an O(μ d^2)-time, O(d^2)-space CMA-ES with carefully designed CSA to maintain behavior while avoiding cubic operations.
- Solid engineering: triangular updates are numerically stable; A^{-1} can be computed in O(d^2); eigenvalues are available from diag(A) at no extra cost.
- Theoretical insight: under convergence of the covariance “shape,” the random rotations between A and C^{1/2} vanish (E_t → I), supporting the CSA approximation.
- Strong empirical evidence on diverse synthetic benchmarks: similar evaluation counts to CMA-ES, substantial speedups in wall-clock time; sensible scaling with dimension.
- Practical utility for settings where evaluation is cheap and d is moderately large (e.g., controller tuning, synthetic objectives, inner loops of ML pipelines).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theory rests on restrictive assumptions (convergence of normalized covariance and continuity arguments). No finite-time bounds on the CSA error due to omitting E_t are provided; formal guarantees are limited.
- Practical impact may be constrained: in many real-world applications objective evaluations dominate, and typical d is modest, reducing the importance of internal O(d^3) costs.
- Baselines: while two delay strategies are included, a full “no-delay” CMA-ES baseline and explicit wall-clock accounting for decomposition alone vs sampling/selection would improve completeness.
- The paper claims invariance properties are preserved “in practice,” but the CSA modification breaks the precise normalization; more diagnostics on rotation error would be valuable.
- Real-world validation is limited to synthetic tests; no demonstration where the speed-up changes what is feasible in practice.

Quality
The method is technically sound, the Cholesky rank-μ update is well specified, and the CSA modification is motivated by a clear decomposition argument. The asymptotic equivalence claim is plausible under the stated assumptions but not fully compelling without finite-time control. Experiments are thorough on benchmarks, reporting both evaluation counts and wall-clock time, with consistent speedups.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. The link between C^{1/2} = A E, the role of E_t in CSA, and why A^{-1} is a reasonable substitute is clearly explained. Algorithmic details (rank-1 update) are explicit. Minor suggestions: explicitly track and report the norm of p_sigma error attributable to omitting E_t and clarify the exact delay policy in the “reference” CMA-ES baseline.

Originality
Extends known Cholesky-updating ideas to full rank-μ CMA-ES with a principled CSA workaround. The complexity improvement for full-covariance CMA-ES without delaying updates is novel and practically relevant.

Significance
For researchers and practitioners who use CMA-ES in settings where internal linear algebra is the bottleneck (cheap evaluations, medium-to-large d), this is a meaningful advance and likely to be adopted. Broader impact is tempered by the prevalence of expensive objectives and modest d in many applications, where evaluation cost dominates.

Suggested experiments/analyses
- Track E_t explicitly: run full CMA-ES and Cholesky-CMA-ES side-by-side with shared randomness to estimate the evolution of E_t (e.g., ‖E_t − I‖_F, principal angle metrics) and quantify the CSA normalization error over time.
- Report end-to-end wall-clock breakdown: sampling, ranking, covariance update, decomposition; include a full CMA-ES baseline without any decomposition delay for completeness.
- Quadratic tests with continuously varied spectra (cf. Stich & Müller 2012): study solution quality, condition numbers, and E_t error as a function of spectral distributions (e.g., power-law, clustered eigenvalues).
- Real-world case where speed matters: e.g., real-time robotics policy search or inner-loop hyperparameter tuning with cheap surrogates; demonstrate that faster internal updates enable tighter time budgets or higher iteration throughput.
- Robustness checks: noisy objectives, non-stationary targets, and higher dimensions approaching memory/cache limits; report numerical stability and conditioning safeguards.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, well-motivated algorithmic improvement with clear complexity gains and careful implementation details.
- Empirical evidence shows substantial speedup without loss in optimization performance on standard tests.
- Likely to be useful for a non-trivial subset of CMA-ES users; easy drop-in replacement.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical justification is asymptotic and relies on restrictive assumptions; no finite-time guarantees.
- Limited real-world validation and somewhat narrow practical impact where evaluation cost dominates.
- Baseline coverage could be expanded; more diagnostics on the rotation error would strengthen the case.

Overall evaluation
A solid, practical contribution that advances the scalability of full-covariance CMA-ES with an elegant Cholesky-based update and a reasonable CSA approximation. While the theory is not fully satisfying and practical impact depends on use case, the engineering merit and empirical speedups are compelling. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on adding diagnostics of E_t, fuller baseline reporting, and at least one real-world demonstration.
