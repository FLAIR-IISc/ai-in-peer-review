This paper proposes the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a modification of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) designed to reduce computational complexity, particularly in the covariance matrix update step. The core idea is to maintain and update the lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix rather than the matrix itself or its square root, leading to a computational complexity of O(μd²) compared to the standard CMA-ES’s O(d³) or delayed update strategies. The authors motivate this approach by arguing that the omitted estimation of a random orthogonal matrix in each iteration converges to the identity matrix under certain convergence assumptions, rendering the approximation asymptotically equivalent to the original CMA-ES. Empirical results on standard benchmark functions demonstrate significant runtime speed-ups without a loss in optimization performance.

The CMA-ES is a powerful and widely used derivative-free optimization algorithm, notably applied in machine learning for tasks like reinforcement learning policy search and hyperparameter tuning [Gomez et al., 2008; Heidrich-Meisner and Igel, 2009a,b; Igel, 2010]. The computational bottleneck of the standard algorithm lies in the covariance matrix update, making this a crucial area for improvement. Prior work has explored delayed updates [Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001] and restricted model complexity [Poland and Zell, 2001; Ros and Hansen, 2008], each with trade-offs. More recent approaches have focused on updating the Cholesky factor directly [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015], but the authors highlight limitations in applying these methods to the standard CMA-ES. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES aims to address these limitations by providing a theoretically justified and efficient alternative.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method builds upon well-established techniques in numerical linear algebra and optimization. The mathematical justification, while relying on convergence assumptions, provides a reasonable rationale for the approximation. The algorithm is clearly described and appears to be a well-engineered modification of the existing CMA-ES.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The reduction in computational complexity from O(d³) to O(μd²) is a significant improvement, especially for high-dimensional problems where covariance matrix manipulation dominates the runtime. This makes the algorithm more scalable and practical for larger-scale applications.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The results presented on benchmark functions demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves comparable performance to the standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluations, while exhibiting a substantial speed-up in wall-clock time. The comparisons to other CMA-ES variants (delayed updates, Suttorp et al.’s approach) are valuable.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, technical details, and experimental results. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 enhances readability and facilitates implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Restrictive Assumptions:** The theoretical justification relies on the convergence of the orthogonal matrix E_t to the identity matrix. While plausible, this assumption may not hold in all scenarios, potentially affecting the algorithm's performance. The authors acknowledge this limitation but a more detailed analysis of the conditions under which this convergence is guaranteed would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Real-World Evaluation:** While the benchmark results are promising, the lack of a real-world application demonstrating the practical benefits of the speed-up is a notable weakness. The authors suggest robotics policy gradients, which would be a strong addition.
*   **Incomplete Baseline Comparison:** Reporting wall-clock runtimes and performance of the standard CMA-ES *without* delay strategies would provide a more complete baseline comparison and better contextualize the speed-up achieved by the Cholesky-CMA-ES.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The range of tested benchmarks, while standard, may not fully capture the diversity of optimization problems encountered in practice. Experiments on functions with continuously varied spectra, as suggested by Stich and Müller [2012], could provide further insights into the algorithm's behavior.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound modification to a widely used optimization algorithm.
*   The proposed method offers a significant improvement in computational efficiency, addressing a known bottleneck in the standard CMA-ES.
*   The empirical results demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES maintains comparable performance to the original algorithm while achieving substantial speed-ups.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical justification relies on restrictive assumptions that may not always hold in practice.
*   The lack of a compelling real-world application limits the demonstration of practical impact.
*   The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with additional benchmarks and a more complete baseline comparison.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a well-motivated and efficiently implemented modification of the standard CMA-ES, offering significant speed-ups without sacrificing performance on benchmark tests. While the theoretical assumptions and limited real-world evaluation are weaknesses, the overall benefits of the proposed method outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the suggested experiments to track the evolution of E_t and include a real-world application demonstrating the practical speed-up.
