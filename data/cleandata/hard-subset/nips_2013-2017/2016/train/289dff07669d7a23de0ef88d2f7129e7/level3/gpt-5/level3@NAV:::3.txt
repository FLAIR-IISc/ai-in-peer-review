This paper proposes a Cholesky-CMA-ES that maintains the lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance and performs both rank-1 and rank-μ covariance updates by composing a sequence of rank-1 updates. To retain quadratic time and minimal storage, the authors modify CSA by replacing C−1/2 with A−1 and argue, under a covariance-convergence assumption, that the induced random rotations vanish asymptotically, so algorithmic behavior is essentially unchanged. Experiments on standard benchmarks show similar evaluation efficiency to a reference CMA-ES while yielding substantial wall-clock speedups, especially at higher dimensions. The work builds on Suttorp et al. (2009), who updated non-triangular factors, and is closest to Krause & Igel (2015), extending their efficient rank-one Cholesky update to the general rank-μ CMA-ES.

Quality: The Cholesky update derivation is sound and the implementation details are clear. The CSA modification is motivated by a clean RQ/polar-decomposition argument and Lemma 1. However, the key assumption (covariance convergence) is strong; the paper would benefit from formal bounds on the CSA bias or variance inflation induced by replacing C−1/2 with A−1, and clearer conditions under which the rotation error becomes negligible. The experiments are appropriate and support the claims, though broader stress tests (noisy functions, ill-conditioned non-separable functions beyond the current set, and higher d) would strengthen the case.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. The presentation of the rank-μ generalization via sequential rank-1 updates is clear, and the algorithmic complexity benefits are well explained.

Originality: The extension to rank-μ is a natural, arguably straightforward generalization of Krause & Igel (2015). The CSA modification is the more novel aspect, yet remains partially heuristic absent stronger theoretical guarantees. Related work is mostly covered; additional discussion of Beyer (ECJ 2014) and other convergence analyses would contextualize the assumption behind Lemma 1.

Significance: The reported speedups with unchanged evaluation counts are practically valuable for CMA-ES users when full covariance is desired. The contribution is incremental but useful.

Suggestions: Provide theoretical support for covariance convergence and/or explicit error bounds (e.g., leveraging Beyer, ECJ 2014). Add comparisons to LM-CMA and other efficient variants, and ablations isolating the effect of rank-μ versus rank-1 only.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid engineering contribution with strong practical impact (time/memory).
- Clear exposition and reproducible algorithmic details.
- Empirical evidence of substantial speedups without loss in evaluation efficiency.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty relative to Krause & Igel (2015).
- CSA modification lacks rigorous performance guarantees.
- Experimental breadth could be expanded.

Minor issues:
- In Eq. (1) the denominator should be \tilde{σ}; same in footnote 2 (p.3).
- Typo line 94: remove extra “by” (“which can by be motivated ...”).
