

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm that replaces the matrix square root operation with a triangular Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors address a well-known bottleneck in CMA-ES where computing the square root of the covariance matrix dominates runtime and memory requirements, particularly for high-dimensional problems. Previous attempts to address this (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) either applied only to specialized CMA-ES variants or lacked theoretical justification for performance preservation. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation showing that replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor in the cumulative step-size adaptation mechanism (equation 3) does not significantly degrade performance, as the introduced error vanishes asymptotically as the algorithm converges (Lemma 1).

The key strength of this work is its theoretically sound approach to a practical problem. The authors demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same asymptotic time complexity (O(µd²)) as the fastest existing implementations while maintaining the same number of objective function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES. The empirical results (Figure 1-3) convincingly show that Cholesky-CMA-ES significantly reduces wall-clock runtime (up to 20x faster at d=64) without affecting optimization performance, with the speedup increasing with dimensionality. The paper also highlights practical advantages: optimal storage complexity, free eigenvalue computation (useful for monitoring problem conditioning), and numerically stable inversion of the Cholesky factor.

The paper's main contribution is a valuable implementation of Cholesky factorization for CMA-ES with theoretical justification, filling a gap in previous work. The authors' theoretical analysis is particularly strong, addressing the critical concern that replacing the square root might degrade performance, which was a limitation in prior approaches. The empirical evaluation is thorough, testing on multiple benchmark functions across dimensions up to 256 and including rotation invariance tests.

A minor weakness is the insufficient explanation of why the proposed method is "numerically more stable" as claimed. The paper mentions numerical stability but does not provide concrete evidence or analysis to support this assertion. A clearer discussion of numerical properties (e.g., condition number analysis, comparison to standard CMA-ES in floating-point precision) would strengthen this claim.

Another opportunity for improvement is to more explicitly discuss how Cholesky-CMA-ES could complement low-dimensional approximation methods like those by Loshchilov (2015), which the authors cite. While the paper mentions that for extremely high dimensions, low-dimensional approximations may be necessary, it could better articulate how Cholesky-CMA-ES might serve as a component in a hybrid approach that first uses Cholesky-CMA-ES for moderate dimensions and transitions to low-dimensional approximations for very large problems.

The paper is well-written and clear, with appropriate references to the CMA-ES literature (Hansen, 2015; Akimoto et al., 2012) and related work on covariance matrix adaptation. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and implementable, and the experiments are well-designed to demonstrate the claimed benefits.

In terms of significance, this work directly addresses a practical bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm that has applications in reinforcement learning policy search (Gomez et al., 2008) and hyperparameter tuning (Heidrich-Meisner and Igel, 2009a,b). The speedup is particularly valuable for modern machine learning applications where high-dimensional optimization is common, and the approach could enable scaling CMA-ES to larger problems than previously feasible.

The paper satisfies all NIPS quality criteria: it's technically sound with theoretical justification and empirical validation; well-organized with clear exposition; original in providing the theoretical foundation for using Cholesky factors in standard CMA-ES; and significant for its practical impact on a widely used optimization algorithm.

Pro: Theoretical justification for performance preservation, clear empirical validation of runtime improvements, practical implementation with optimal complexity, valuable contribution to a widely used optimization algorithm.

Con: Insufficient explanation of numerical stability claims, limited discussion of integration with low-dimensional approximation methods for extremely high dimensions.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The proposed method should replace the standard CMA-ES implementation whenever covariance matrix update and storage are performance bottlenecks. The paper is well-suited for NIPS 2016 as it addresses a fundamental algorithmic improvement with clear theoretical and empirical validation. I recommend acceptance.
