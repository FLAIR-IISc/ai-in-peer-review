This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the widely used Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that reduces the per-iteration complexity from O(d³) to O(μd²). This work builds on a line of research aimed at improving the scalability of CMA-ES, which is often hampered by the cubic cost of decomposing the covariance matrix. The key idea is to maintain and directly update a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. The main deviation from the standard algorithm lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA), where the authors omit the estimation of an orthogonal matrix `E_t` that arises from the decomposition. They provide a theoretical argument that under certain convergence assumptions, `E_t` approaches the identity matrix, making their approximation asymptotically equivalent to the original CMA-ES.

**Strengths (Quality, Originality, Significance):**
The primary strength of this paper is its solid and practical contribution to accelerating a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization. The proposed method is well-motivated and achieves an asymptotically optimal time complexity for a full covariance matrix update. The empirical evaluation on standard benchmark functions is convincing, demonstrating a substantial reduction in wall-clock runtime with no discernible loss in optimization performance (measured in function evaluations) compared to state-of-the-art baselines. This makes the work a significant advancement for applying CMA-ES in scenarios where function evaluations are computationally cheap relative to the internal updates, particularly as dimensionality `d` grows.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement (Quality):**
The main weakness lies in the theoretical underpinnings and the scope of the empirical validation.
1.  **Theoretical Guarantees:** The claim that the approximation error vanishes relies on restrictive assumptions about the convergence of the covariance matrix shape. This central assumption is not empirically validated in the paper. A crucial experiment would be to run the full CMA-ES and track the evolution of the omitted `E_t` matrix (e.g., via `||E_t - I||_F`) to demonstrate that it indeed converges toward identity on these problems.
2.  **Experimental Scope:** The baselines could be more comprehensive. Reporting wall-clock times for a standard CMA-ES *without* any delay strategies would provide a clearer upper bound on the speed-up. Furthermore, testing on problems with continuously varied spectral distributions (e.g., following Stich & Müller, 2012) would better probe the robustness and solution quality of the approximation under different conditioning.
3.  **Practical Impact:** While technically strong, the paper could better motivate its practical significance. In many real-world applications, the cost of objective function evaluations dominates. Including at least one compelling real-world experiment where this algorithmic speed-up is critical—such as real-time policy search in robotics—would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.
