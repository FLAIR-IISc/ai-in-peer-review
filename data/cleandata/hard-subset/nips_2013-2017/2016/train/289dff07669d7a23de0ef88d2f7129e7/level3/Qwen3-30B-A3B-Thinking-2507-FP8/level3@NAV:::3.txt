

This paper proposes a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called Cholesky-CMA-ES that replaces the matrix square root with a triangular Cholesky factor to reduce computational complexity. The authors extend Krause and Igel's (2015) rank-1 update approach to the standard CMA-ES with rank-μ updates, demonstrating that the modification to cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) does not significantly alter algorithm behavior under covariance convergence assumptions. The paper provides theoretical justification for this claim (Lemma 1) and presents empirical results showing comparable function evaluations with improved runtime complexity.

The paper's strengths include a clear exposition of the algorithmic modifications and thorough experimental validation across multiple benchmark functions. The computational complexity analysis is technically sound, and the demonstration of quadratic-time updates with optimal storage requirements is well-motivated. The authors effectively address the practical concern of covariance matrix update costs in high-dimensional optimization.

However, the contribution's novelty is limited. The extension to rank-μ updates is a straightforward generalization of Krause and Igel (2015), and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work in this area. The theoretical justification for covariance convergence (Lemma 1) would be significantly strengthened by citing Beyer's work (ECJ 2014), which provides a more rigorous foundation for the convergence assumption. Additionally, minor issues require correction: Eq. (1) should use \tilde{σ} instead of σ, and a typo ("which can by be motivated") appears on line 94.

While the computational improvements are theoretically valid, the paper's significance is somewhat constrained by the limited novelty and lack of evidence demonstrating widespread adoption potential. The work represents a useful implementation detail rather than a substantial advancement in the field. The experimental results confirm the claimed runtime benefits but do not provide compelling evidence of broader impact on optimization practice.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with clear presentation, but its contribution falls short of the originality expected for NIPS. The minor theoretical gap and limited novelty make it difficult to recommend acceptance over more innovative work in the field.
