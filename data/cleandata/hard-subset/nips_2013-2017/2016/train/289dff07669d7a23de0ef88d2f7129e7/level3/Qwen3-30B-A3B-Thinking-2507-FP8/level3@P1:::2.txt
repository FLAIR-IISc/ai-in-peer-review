

The paper proposes a variant of the CMA-ES algorithm called Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the matrix square root computation in the standard CMA-ES with a Cholesky factorization to reduce time and space complexity. The authors claim their method achieves the same performance as the standard CMA-ES while reducing the runtime complexity from O(d^3) to O(µd^2) per iteration, where d is the dimensionality and µ is the number of selected points. The approach builds on prior work by Krause and Igel (2015) who proposed a similar Cholesky update for a restricted CMA-ES variant, but extends it to the standard algorithm.

The paper demonstrates theoretical justification for why the Cholesky-based approach does not significantly impact algorithm performance, particularly through Lemma 1 showing that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor diminishes as the algorithm converges. The authors provide empirical results showing comparable performance to the standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluations, while achieving significantly faster wall-clock runtime, especially for higher dimensions.

The paper demonstrates several strengths. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the technical content accessible. The method itself is reasonable and addresses a genuine computational bottleneck in CMA-ES. The empirical results are convincing, showing up to 20x speedup for d=64 with identical function evaluation counts. The approach could indeed become a new standard for CMA-ES implementations where computational efficiency matters.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that prevent it from meeting NIPS standards. First, the originality is limited. The core idea of using Cholesky factorization for covariance updates is nearly identical to Krause and Igel (2015), which the authors cite but fail to adequately distinguish from their work. The authors extend the method to the standard CMA-ES, but this extension appears to be straightforward rather than novel, and the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how this extension differs from prior work in meaningful ways.

Second, the complexity claim is misleading. The paper claims their method achieves "optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity" while being "the same as CMA-ES-Ref," but fails to properly justify this. CMA-ES-Ref uses a delayed update strategy (computing the square root every τ iterations), making it O(d^2) per iteration on average. The authors' algorithm is O(µd^2) per iteration, but since µ is typically O(log d), this is asymptotically equivalent to CMA-ES-Ref. The paper should use Θ-notation to clarify this equivalence and explain why their implementation is actually faster in practice.

Third, the key claim that "the error in the norm will also vanish due to the exponential weighting in the summation" is unvalidated. The authors claim this error decreases over time, but provide no experimental evidence tracking this error throughout the optimization process. Instead, they only compare final performance metrics, which is insufficient to support their claim. Hansen's work on CMA-ES has established that the step-size adaptation is critical to performance, so this unvalidated claim about error propagation is a serious weakness.

Fourth, the paper contains several technical errors and inconsistencies. The notation in Algorithm 2 uses 'wk' instead of 'α_k', the dimensionality in Table 1 is labeled 'n' instead of 'd', and the objective function is inconsistently referred to as 'F' instead of 'f' (line 185). These are not just typos but could cause confusion for readers attempting to implement the method.

The significance of the paper is limited by the lack of novelty. While the computational improvements are real, they stem from a straightforward extension of prior work rather than a novel theoretical insight. For a conference like NIPS, where the bar for significance is high, this incremental contribution does not sufficiently advance the field.

In summary, the paper presents a reasonable computational improvement to an established algorithm with clear empirical benefits. However, the limited originality (appearing to be a trivial extension of prior work), unvalidated theoretical claims, and technical errors prevent it from meeting NIPS standards. The authors should either provide stronger justification for why their extension is novel, validate their error propagation claim with additional experiments, or significantly reframe the paper to focus on a more substantial contribution.

The paper would be suitable for a conference focused on evolutionary computation, but not for NIPS where the bar for novelty and theoretical contribution is higher. I recommend rejection.
