Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C (with C = A A^T) instead of C itself or its matrix square root. The key change is in cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA): the update replaces C^{-1/2} by A^{-1}, yielding an O(µ d^2) per-iteration update with optimal O(d^2) storage while preserving positive definiteness and avoiding cubic-time decompositions. The authors justify the CSA approximation theoretically via a rotation argument: if the normalized covariance converges in shape, the rotation between the square root and triangular factor converges to the identity, and the CSA error vanishes due to exponential weighting. The algorithm achieves the same number of function evaluations as standard CMA-ES on classical benchmarks but substantially reduces wall-clock time, with speedups growing with dimension. The paper builds on and extends previous attempts to update factorizations directly (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), which handled (1+1)-CMA-ES and multi-objective variants or rank-one cases; here, the approach is integrated into the standard CSA-based CMA-ES with a clear theoretical rationale.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation: eigen/sqrt decompositions dominate cost in full CMA-ES for moderate-to-large d; reducing this while preserving evaluation efficiency is valuable.
- Principled algorithmic design: rank-one Cholesky updates yield O(µ d^2) time and optimal storage; triangular form allows fast inversion and simple bookkeeping.
- Theoretical justification for the CSA approximation via convergence of rotations, including why triangularity matters for a bijective, smooth mapping from C to A.
- Empirical results on standard smooth, ill-conditioned, and rotated benchmarks show nearly identical evaluation counts to the reference CMA-ES and large wall-clock speedups (e.g., ~20× at d = 64), with consistent gains increasing in d.
- Practical relevance: implementation-ready, with integration into an open-source library.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Numerical stability claim needs clarification. “Numerically stable” is asserted but not analyzed. Please specify what is meant (e.g., preservation of SPD under finite precision; bounded growth of round-off error; conditioning of A updates), and provide diagnostics (e.g., frequency of re-factorization needed, drift in symmetry/positivity, condition number evolution). Citing standard numerical linear algebra (e.g., Golub & Van Loan) would strengthen this.
- CSA approximation lacks quantitative error bounds. The rotation convergence lemma is useful but relies on shape convergence and does not bound transient errors when C changes rapidly. An empirical ablation comparing A^{-1}-CSA vs exact C^{-1/2}-CSA (using a controlled eigendecomposition baseline) would be helpful, including trajectories of ||p_σ||/E{χ}, and the measured ||E_t E_{t-1}^T − I|| over time (you mention this in the supplement; consider moving key plots to the main text).
- Claim about “eigenvalues for free” from the Cholesky diagonal appears incorrect in general; diag(A) are not the square roots of the eigenvalues of C. If you only need diagonal variances or Gershgorin bounds, please clarify. If you actually need spectral quantities (e.g., enforcing lower bounds along principal components), additional computation is required (e.g., power iterations or occasional eigendecompositions).
- Scope of experiments: consider noisy objectives, constraints, and more rugged multimodal cases, where C may fluctuate and the CSA approximation could be more stressed. Reporting sensitivity to noise and invariance to affine transformations would improve confidence. Also, include absolute memory usage and per-iteration flop counts to substantiate the claimed complexity benefits.
- Practical guidance: discuss when periodic “refresh” (recomputing A from C or vice versa) is advisable to control numerical drift; suggest block- or cache-aware implementations given observed cache effects at d = 256. Provide guidance on the choice of µ and on the delayed-update baseline fairness (e.g., equalizing wall-clock budgets).

Quality:
Overall technically sound. The Cholesky rank-one update is well-motivated and implemented; the CSA approximation is plausible and supported empirically, though a more quantitative analysis of its transient effects and numerical properties would strengthen the work. Experimental evidence is solid for the chosen benchmarks; adding noisy/nonstationary cases would broaden support.

Clarity:
Well-written and organized. The algorithms are clearly stated. Please correct or clarify the statement about “eigenvalues for free” and expand the “numerically stable” claim with concrete definitions and evidence. Some reference formatting needs cleanup.

Originality:
Moderately novel but impactful. The paper extends prior Cholesky-update ideas to the standard CMA-ES with CSA, provides a theoretical explanation for why the approximation should not degrade performance, and demonstrates strong practical benefits. This is a meaningful and nontrivial integration rather than a minor engineering tweak.

Significance:
High practical significance for full-covariance CMA-ES in moderate-to-large d. The speedups without evaluation-count degradation are valuable for ML applications (policy search, hyperparameter tuning). For very high dimensions where O(d^2) remains prohibitive, the method could be complementary to limited-memory/low-rank approaches (e.g., LM-CMA; Loshchilov, 2015). It would be interesting to explore hybrids: use triangular Cholesky updates within a low-rank or limited-memory parameterization to further push scalability.

Pros for acceptance:
- Substantial runtime reductions with unchanged evaluation counts.
- Theoretically motivated modification to CSA; careful argument about rotations and triangularity.
- Asymptotically optimal time (for full covariance) and storage; practical implementation details.
- Clear, useful contribution likely to be adopted by practitioners.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited theoretical quantification of CSA approximation error and numerical stability.
- Misleading statement about eigenvalues from Cholesky; needs correction.
- Experimental scope could be broader (noise, constraints, ruggedness), and more detailed runtime/memory breakdowns would help.

Additional questions to authors:
- Can you report the empirical distribution of ||p_σ||/E{χ} under both CSA variants over time?
- Do you ever need to re-factorize to counteract drift, and how often?
- How does the method behave under noise (additive Gaussian, multiplicative) and constrained variants?
- Could you benchmark combinations with low-rank/limited-memory CMA-ES (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) for neural network settings?
