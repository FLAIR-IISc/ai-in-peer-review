Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper targets the dominant computational bottleneck of CMA-ES: updating and storing the full covariance matrix and recomputing its square root, which in practice costs more than quadratic time per iteration and can dominate runtime in high dimensions. The authors propose Cholesky-CMA-ES, which maintains and updates the triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = AAT directly via a sequence of rank-one updates. This reduces the per-iteration adaptation cost to O(µ d^2) with optimal O(d^2) storage. The main algorithmic change affects cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA): the inverse square root C−1/2 used in the pσ-path update is replaced with A−1. Because A differs from C1/2 by an orthogonal factor, the authors argue that the resulting rotation error in the path norm decreases as the algorithm converges, supported by a continuity argument (Lemma 1) on the RQ decomposition of C1/2. Empirically, the number of function evaluations to reach target precision is essentially unchanged compared to a state-of-the-art CMA-ES baseline, but the wall-clock time is significantly reduced, especially as dimension grows.

The work is well situated relative to CMA-ES variants that (i) delay decompositions [Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001; Hansen, 2015], (ii) restrict the covariance structure (diagonal, low-rank, or limited-memory) [Poland and Zell, 2001; Ros and Hansen, 2008; Sun et al., 2013; Akimoto et al., 2014; Loshchilov, 2014, 2015], and (iii) update non-triangular Cholesky-like factors [Suttorp et al., 2009] or use rank-one Cholesky updates in specific CMA-ES variants [(1+1), MO-CMA-ES; Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015; Bringmann et al., 2013]. Unlike (ii), this method preserves the modeling power of a full covariance, and unlike (iii), it uses a triangular factorization that is bijectively tied to C, yields eigenvalues for free (from A’s diagonal), and allows stable quadratic-time inversion/solves.

Strengths:
- Clear and impactful algorithmic contribution: an O(µ d^2) update with optimal O(d^2) memory while retaining a full covariance model.
- Sound reasoning for the CSA approximation: the rotation factor between C−1/2 and A converges, so the error in the norm of pσ diminishes; this aligns with the intuition that only recent steps matter due to exponential forgetting.
- Practical advantages: fast rank-one Cholesky updates, numerically stable, efficient triangular solves to apply A−1, and free access to eigenvalues for monitoring conditioning.
- Empirical evaluation demonstrates unchanged function-evaluation counts compared to a strong CMA-ES reference and large wall-clock speedups increasing with dimension; includes rotation-invariant tests.
- Relevance: highly pertinent to derivative-free optimization in ML, including direct policy search and hyperparameter tuning.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Theoretical guarantees are limited to the CSA approximation; a full convergence analysis of the modified algorithm (beyond asymptotic rotation alignment) would strengthen the paper. Early-iteration transients, where C changes rapidly, could in principle affect pσ and σ updates.
- Experimental scope: while the set of benchmark functions is standard, it would be useful to report statistical significance on evaluation counts and runtimes (e.g., medians with confidence intervals), especially where differences are small.
- Baselines: given the emphasis on large-scale settings, including LM-CMA [Loshchilov, 2014, 2015] in the runtime comparisons would better position the proposed method in the “fast full-model vs. approximate model” trade-off.
- Fairness and reproducibility of runtime comparisons: please clarify implementation parity (same codebase, compiler flags, BLAS/LAPACK, threading, CPU details), and whether triangular solves are used instead of forming A−1 explicitly in pσ updates (the latter is slower and less stable).
- Figure-specific question: Figure 2 suggests less pronounced speedup differences for Cigar/Discus at d=256. The text attributes a cache effect when the matrix exceeds L2 cache; could you quantify this (e.g., cache sizes and memory footprint) and explain why the effect appears function-dependent? Are access patterns or µ differing across functions?
- Rosenbrock trials with non-convergence are excluded; this may bias evaluation counts. Please report the fraction excluded and, if possible, wall-clock results including all trials with a budget cap.

Quality:
- The algorithmic derivation is technically sound, and the Cholesky rank-one update is carefully implemented. The CSA approximation is justified with a clear lemma and sensible assumptions about smooth changes in C. Experiments are thorough for standard benchmarks and demonstrate compelling speedups without loss in sample efficiency. Some additional statistical reporting and baseline breadth would further strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity:
- The paper is well written and organized. The pseudo-code (Algorithms 1–2) is helpful. Minor suggestions:
  - State explicitly that applying A−1 to a vector should be done via triangular solve (forward substitution) rather than forming A−1.
  - Summarize the numerical stability properties of Algorithm 2 and whether any downdates occur; briefly discuss conditioning safeguards (e.g., eigenvalue floors) and their cost.
  - Provide more system and implementation details for reproducibility of timing results.

Originality:
- While updating Cholesky factors and low-complexity CMA-ES variants have prior art, the combination of a triangular Cholesky update for the standard CMA-ES with a justified CSA modification is, to my knowledge, novel. The argument that the rotational error vanishes and the empirical confirmation of unchanged evaluation counts are new and valuable.

Significance:
- High. The method retains the modeling capacity of full CMA-ES while making it much faster and more memory-efficient, without trading off evaluations. This is likely to become a default choice for medium-to-large d when a full covariance is still desirable.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong practical impact with clear speed/memory gains and unchanged sample efficiency.
- Solid theoretical motivation for the CSA change.
- Careful experimental validation; code integration into a common library.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited theoretical guarantees for the full modified algorithm.
- Runtime comparison details could be clearer; missing some large-scale baselines (LM-CMA).
- Some experimental exclusions and cache-related anomalies merit deeper analysis.

Minor requests for the camera-ready:
- Clarify implementation parity and system details for timing.
- Explain the d=256 behavior on Cigar/Discus and quantify cache/memory effects.
- Report statistics on excluded Rosenbrock runs; include medians with variability.
- Emphasize triangular solves over explicit inversion in pσ.
- Add a brief discussion on early-iteration behavior of the CSA approximation.

Overall recommendation:
A well-motivated, technically sound, and practically significant contribution that materially advances the usability of CMA-ES in higher dimensions. I recommend acceptance after addressing the minor clarifications above.
