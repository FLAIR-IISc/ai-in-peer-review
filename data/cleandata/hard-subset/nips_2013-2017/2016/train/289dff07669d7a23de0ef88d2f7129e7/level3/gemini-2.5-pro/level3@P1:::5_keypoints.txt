- Proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES: maintain/update lower-triangular Cholesky factor to reduce per-iteration matrix-update cost from O(d^3) to O(µd^2).  
- Addresses computational bottleneck in standard CMA-ES for high-dimensional, cheap-evaluation problems.  
- Core challenge: CSA requires multiplication by C^{-1/2}, not directly obtainable from Cholesky factor due to a rotating orthogonal matrix E.  
- Key approximation: omit the orthogonal matrix E in the CSA update and use A^{-1} instead of C^{-1/2}.  
- Theoretical justification (Lemma 1): if normalized covariance C_t converges, then E_t converges and the approximation error vanishes.  
- Limitation of theory: convergence of C_t is a strong, not-guaranteed assumption for stochastic optimization; no formal error bound given.  
- Empirical results: substantial wall-clock speedup on benchmark functions, with same number of function evaluations as baselines.  
- Comparison baselines: evaluated against state-of-the-art delayed-update CMA-ES variants.  
- Experimental setup: uses standard benchmarks and appropriate baselines; plots and presentation are clear and informative.  
- Technical soundness: derivation of Cholesky update is correct and builds on prior work; experiments well executed.  
- Originality: direct Cholesky updates are prior art, but novelty lies in applying this to modern CMA-ES with CSA and providing a theoretical argument.  
- Significance: practically meaningful for derivative-free optimization when function evaluations are cheap and d is moderately large (d > 50).  
- Limited broader impact: negligible speedup when function evaluations are far more expensive than O(d^3) updates (e.g., many ML hyperparameter tuning tasks).  
- Suggested empirical improvement: measure/plot rotational error (e.g., ||E_t E_{t-1}^T - I||) to validate the core assumption.  
- Suggested benchmarks: test on functions with continuously varying spectra to probe approximation limits.  
- Suggested baseline: include full (non-delayed) CMA-ES performing O(d^3) decomposition every iteration for absolute speedup reference.  
- Suggested application: add a real-world case study (e.g., direct policy search in robotics) where the update cost is the dominant bottleneck.  
- Pros summarized: removes known bottleneck, gives empirical wall-clock speedup, practical and memory-efficient, preserves eval-based performance, clearly written.  
- Cons summarized: theoretical justification relies on restrictive assumption and lacks formal bounds; practical relevance limited to specific regimes without real-world case study.  
- Overall recommendation: strong, valuable contribution with convincing empirical evidence; recommended for acceptance despite theoretical caveats.
