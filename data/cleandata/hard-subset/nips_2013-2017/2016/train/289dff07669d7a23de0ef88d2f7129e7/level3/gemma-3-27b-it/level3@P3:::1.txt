This paper presents a compelling modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) by updating the covariance matrix via triangular Cholesky factors, significantly reducing both computational complexity and memory requirements. The core idea – leveraging the Cholesky factorization instead of direct covariance matrix manipulation – builds upon prior work (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) but crucially provides a theoretical justification for maintaining performance and extends the approach to the standard CMA-ES, overcoming limitations of earlier attempts. The authors convincingly demonstrate through empirical results that the proposed “Cholesky-CMA-ES” achieves comparable function evaluation counts to the standard CMA-ES while providing substantial wall-clock speedups, particularly in higher dimensions.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It effectively contextualizes the work within the broader field of derivative-free optimization, highlighting the importance of CMA-ES in areas like reinforcement learning and supervised learning (Gomez et al., 2008; Heidrich-Meisner & Igel, 2009a,b; Igel, 2010). The theoretical justification, centering on Lemma 1 and its explanation of coordinate system rotation, is well-articulated and adds significant value. The experimental setup is thorough, employing a range of benchmark functions and varying dimensionality, and the results are presented clearly in figures and discussed effectively. The connection to information geometry (Akimoto et al., 2012) further strengthens the theoretical grounding.

The originality of this work lies in the successful adaptation of Cholesky factorization to the full CMA-ES framework *with* theoretical backing, overcoming prior limitations. While updating via Cholesky factors has been explored before (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), this paper provides the critical insight into preserving algorithm behavior through careful consideration of the step-size adaptation process and the associated normalization. This represents a non-trivial extension and a significant improvement.  The significance is high; the potential for faster optimization, especially in large-dimensional problems, makes this a valuable contribution to the field. It directly addresses a key bottleneck in CMA-ES – the computational cost of covariance matrix updates – and offers a practical solution. It's likely to become a default choice for medium to large-scale CMA-ES applications.

However, there are a few minor points that could be clarified. First, regarding the CPU/time comparisons, it would be beneficial to explicitly state the programming language and implementation details (e.g., specific LAPACK library versions) used for all algorithms. Ensuring comparability is essential, and this detail would enhance the reproducibility of the results.  While the authors mention using Shark (Igel et al., 2008) and LAPACK, providing more precise information would be helpful.

Second, the paper acknowledges that Figure 2 doesn't show more pronounced differences for the Cigar and Discus functions at dimension 256. An explanation as to why this might be the case would be valuable. Is it due to numerical instability at higher dimensions, a specific characteristic of these functions, or perhaps the scaling of the benchmarks? Further investigation or discussion on this point would strengthen the analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Significant speedups:** Substantial wall-clock time reductions, especially in high dimensions, without sacrificing performance.
*   **Theoretical justification:** A clear and well-supported explanation for why the proposed method doesn't degrade CMA-ES’s convergence properties.
*   **Practical impact:**  Likely to become a default implementation for many CMA-ES users, addressing a key performance bottleneck.
*   **Well-written and thoroughly evaluated:** The paper is clear, concise, and presents a comprehensive experimental analysis.
*   **Optimal Complexity:** Achieves optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Minor clarity requests:**  Specific details regarding implementation and a further explanation of Figure 2 are needed. (These are minor and easily addressable.)



Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. The minor concerns outlined above do not detract significantly from the quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with the request that the authors address the points regarding implementation details and the analysis of Figure 2. This paper has the potential to significantly impact the usability and scalability of CMA-ES.
