This paper proposes a Cholesky-based variant of CMA-ES that replaces explicit eigen- or matrix-square-root updates of the covariance with direct rank-one updates of a triangular Cholesky factor and modifies CSA by substituting C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} (A being the Cholesky factor). The authors argue this yields O(µ d^2) time and optimal memory for full covariance adaptation, while leaving the number of objective evaluations effectively unchanged. A simple asymptotic argument (via continuity of the RQ decomposition) is used to justify that the error in the step-size path decays as the covariance “shape” converges. Empirically, on standard benchmarks (including rotated variants and ill-conditioned cases), the variant matches the evaluation count of the reference CMA-ES and achieves large wall-clock speedups.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on the line of work that replaces full EVD/square-root recomputation by updating decompositions directly: Suttorp et al. (2009) for a non-triangular factor, and Krause & Igel (2015) for an efficient rank-one Cholesky update in the special (1+1)-ES case. The present work extends those ideas to the standard CMA-ES with µ > 1 and integrates them with CSA via an approximation. It contrasts with model-restricted approaches (diagonal or limited-memory CMA variants, e.g., Ros & Hansen 2008; Loshchilov 2014/2015), which trade off performance on non-separable problems. The information-geometry view (Akimoto et al. 2012/2014) is cited to motivate the covariance update.

Strengths
- Practical impact: The approach targets a known bottleneck in CMA-ES and demonstrates large wall-clock gains while preserving evaluation counts on a range of test functions, including rotations and ill-conditioning.
- Implementation-friendly: Maintaining a triangular factor reduces memory and allows efficient triangular solves for A^{-1}.
- Reasonable justification: The asymptotic argument explaining why substituting A^{-1} for C^{-1/2} should not degrade CSA significantly is plausible, and the empirical results are consistent with that claim.
- Open-source integration and fair baselines (reference CMA-ES with delayed decomposition, Suttorp-style factor).

Weaknesses and requested revisions
1) Missing equivalence proof for covariance update. The paper states that Eq. (2) can be decomposed into rank-one updates on A using Algorithm 2, but it does not provide a clear, formal proof (for general µ and the scaling by √(1−c1−cµ)) that the resulting At+1 exactly factorizes the intended Ct+1. Given the centrality of correctness, a detailed derivation or a precise citation covering this general case is needed.

2) CSA approximation: theory vs. evidence. The lemma based on RQ-decomposition continuity is weak for a randomized, non-stationary algorithm and does not quantify the error in pσ nor its impact on σ. To substantiate the “small and decreasing error” claim, please:
- Empirically compare the exact CSA (using C^{-1/2}) vs. the proposed CSA (using A^{-1}) over time, reporting norms of pσ, step-size trajectories, and their deviations across iterations (not only final performance).
- Report statistics on ∥Et Eᵀt−1 − I∥ and the angle between sk and ŝk, to directly test the rotation drift assumption.
- Include results on difficult, slowly rotating cases (e.g., Rosenbrock, rotated Ellipsoid) and with varying λ, µ.

3) Complexity claims need precision. The use of O(µ d^2) should be put in context with the reference algorithm’s amortized cost under delayed decompositions (which combine O(d^3) EVD every τ steps with O(d^2) rank-µ covariance updates). Please:
- Use Θ-notation and provide a per-iteration amortized breakdown for all compared methods, including sampling and sorting.
- Clarify when your approach is strictly asymptotically faster than CMA-ES with delay, and discuss constant factors and the impact of µ ≈ Θ(log d).
- Discuss cache effects (observed beyond d=256) and whether they change the asymptotic comparison or only constants.

4) Incorrect statement about eigenvalues. The paper claims that “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general; the diagonal of L in C = L Lᵀ are Cholesky pivots, not eigenvalues. Consequently, one cannot “get eigenvalues for free” from diag(A). Please correct this claim and revise any conclusions (e.g., conditioning monitoring) that rely on it.

5) Numerical stability conditions. Rank-one Cholesky up-/down-dates are well studied; stability depends on β and SPD preservation. Please specify the conditions under which Algorithm 2 is stable in your setting (given the scaling by √(1−c1−cµ) and positive β for rank-one additions), cite standard cholupdate references, and report diagnostics (e.g., SPD checks, condition numbers) on ill-conditioned problems.

6) Scope and breadth of experiments. While the benchmarks are reasonable, to support a NIPS-level claim of “should replace standard CMA-ES when full covariance is needed,” consider:
- COCO/BBOB evaluation and higher dimensions (d > 256), even if partial, to establish trends.
- Ablations across λ and µ, and noisy functions.
- Fairness details (BLAS/LAPACK versions, threading, same random seeds, identical selection weights, and CPU specs).

7) Clarity and minor issues.
- Notation and typos: Algorithm 2 uses αk; please fix the symbol mismatch noted as wk→αk; “n” in Table 1 should be “d”; “F” should be “f” (line ~185). Double-check the update formula lines in Algorithm 2 for transcription errors.
- The paper would benefit from a concise algorithmic derivation section showing how Eq. (2) maps to the exact sequence of rank-one Cholesky updates and their complexity.

Quality: The empirical results are strong on wall-clock time and suggest no loss in evaluation efficiency, but the paper needs a rigorous equivalence argument for the covariance update, corrected claims about eigenvalues, and deeper validation of the CSA approximation.

Clarity: Generally well written. The algorithmic details for the rank-one updates and the complexity discussion need tightening. Fixing the noted typos and notation will help.

Originality: Incremental. The core technical ingredient (efficient Cholesky rank-one update) is close to Krause & Igel (2015); the main novelty is its integration into standard CMA-ES with µ > 1 and the CSA approximation.

Significance: Potentially high for practitioners if correctness and stability are nailed down; however, the current version overclaims (eigenvalues) and under-substantiates the CSA approximation.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important bottleneck with compelling speedups.
- Simple, widely applicable implementation idea.
- Promising empirical results across several benchmarks.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing rigorous equivalence proof for the covariance update pipeline.
- CSA approximation insufficiently validated; only asymptotic intuition and end-point comparisons.
- Incorrect technical claim about eigenvalues from Cholesky.
- Ambiguous complexity discussion vs. delayed EVD baselines.

Recommendation: Reject in its current form. With a corrected theory section (equivalence proof, eigenvalue claim), stronger CSA validation, and a precise complexity analysis, this could be a solid resubmission.
