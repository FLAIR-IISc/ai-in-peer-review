## Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) aiming to accelerate the covariance matrix update step by replacing the eigenvalue decomposition with a Cholesky factorization-based approach. The core idea – leveraging Cholesky updates for efficiency – aligns with prior work in the field attempting to mitigate the computational bottleneck of covariance matrix maintenance in CMA-ES, particularly as dimensionality increases. The authors correctly identify the quadratic complexity of the standard update as a limitation and aim for a method with complexity O(μd^2). 

The paper is clearly written and the proposed method is presented in a reasonably straightforward manner. The motivation is well-established, outlining the computational cost associated with the standard CMA-ES, and the proposed algorithm (Cholesky-CMA-ES) is logically presented with Algorithm 1 and 2 detailing the update procedure. 

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. The claim of originality is questionable. The approach feels remarkably similar to the work of Krause and Igel (“A More Efficient Rank-one Covariance Matrix Update for Evolution Strategies”), which the authors acknowledge but downplay. The key difference – avoiding the polar decomposition to maintain rotation invariance – introduces error, and the authors fail to provide a rigorous proof of equivalence between their approach and the original CMA-ES formula (2), despite stating it’s a crucial step. While they offer a lemma about convergence of rotation matrices, it doesn't establish functional equivalence.

The complexity claim of O(µd^2) is not adequately substantiated. While correct in stating this matches CMA-ES-Ref, the authors must use Θ-notation and thoroughly explain *why* their method is faster in practice. The experimental results are encouraging but don't delve deeply into the micro-benchmarking needed to demonstrate this speedup. Furthermore, the claim about small and decreasing path error (contrary to Hansen) lacks experimental validation *over time*. Showing only final performance is insufficient.

Finally, several minor errors exist: ‘wk’ should be ‘α_k’ in Algorithm 2, ‘n’ should be ‘d’ in Table 1, and ‘F’ should be ‘f’ on line 185. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Clear writing and presentation of a potentially useful optimization.
* Demonstrated speedups in wall-clock time on benchmark functions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
* Limited originality – heavily resembles existing work.
* Missing crucial equivalence proof.
* Unclear and insufficiently justified complexity claim.
* Unvalidated claim about path error.
* Minor notation/typo errors.



**Overall Recommendation:** Reject. The paper, in its current form, does not represent a significant contribution to NIPS. The lack of a proper equivalence proof and the insufficiently justified performance claims are major concerns. While the idea has merit, the paper requires substantial revision to address these issues before it can be considered for publication.
