This paper presents a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) – the Cholesky-CMA-ES – designed to improve computational efficiency, particularly in high-dimensional optimization problems. The core idea is to update the covariance matrix using its Cholesky factorization instead of directly manipulating the covariance matrix itself, leading to quadratic time complexity and reduced memory requirements. This is a significant contribution, building upon prior work attempting similar optimizations (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) by providing a theoretically justified approach that maintains the algorithm’s performance characteristics.

The CMA-ES is a well-established and powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001), widely used in machine learning for tasks like reinforcement learning (Gomez et al., 2008) and hyperparameter tuning (Heidrich-Meisner & Igel, 2009a,b). The computational bottleneck of the standard CMA-ES lies in the covariance matrix update and decomposition, especially as dimensionality increases. Previous attempts to address this issue involved delaying updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) or restricting model complexity (Poland & Zell, 2001; Ros & Hansen, 2008), often at the cost of increased function evaluations. More recent approaches, like those using Cholesky factorization (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), have shown promise but faced limitations in direct applicability to the standard CMA-ES. 

The authors cleverly address this by modifying the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, replacing the inverse of the square root of the covariance matrix with the inverse of the Cholesky factor. They provide a compelling theoretical argument, based on Lemma 1, showing that this modification introduces a negligible error as the algorithm converges, particularly concerning the norm of the step-size adaptation. The proposed algorithm offers an asymptotically optimal time complexity of O(µd²) and optimal storage complexity.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-defined algorithm with a clear theoretical justification. The proof of Lemma 1 and the explanation regarding the impact of coordinate rotations are particularly strong. The argument that the error introduced by the approximation diminishes with convergence is convincing.
* **Originality:** While building on existing work, the Cholesky-CMA-ES represents a significant advance by adapting the Cholesky factorization to the *standard* CMA-ES in a theoretically sound manner. This overcomes limitations of previous attempts.
* **Significance:** The demonstrated speedups, particularly in higher dimensions, are substantial. This has direct implications for applying CMA-ES to larger-scale problems, including those frequently encountered in modern machine learning. The ability to obtain covariance eigenvalues without additional computation is a valuable side benefit.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The connection to existing CMA-ES literature is well-established. The algorithm is presented in a concise and understandable manner.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are comprehensive, covering a variety of benchmark functions and dimensions. The comparison with other CMA-ES variants (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES) provides a strong validation of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Implementation Details:** While the results are compelling, a more detailed discussion of the implementation would be beneficial. Specifically, clarifying the programming language used, compiler optimizations, and any shared libraries could help ensure the fairness of the CPU/time comparisons. Were all implementations optimized to a similar degree?
* **Figure 2 Interpretation:** The results for the Cigar and Discus functions in Figure 2 do not show as pronounced a difference at d=256 as observed for other functions. A brief discussion of this observation – whether it's due to specific characteristics of these functions or potential limitations of the experimental setup – would strengthen the analysis.
* **Polar Decomposition Consideration:** The paper mentions the computational cost of polar decomposition. While adequately addressed regarding convergence, a brief mention of alternative approaches (if any) to update the orthogonal matrix during optimization may offer further insights.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Significant performance improvement over the standard CMA-ES, particularly in high dimensions.
* Strong theoretical justification for the proposed approximation.
* Clear presentation and thorough experimental validation.
* Addresses a practical bottleneck in the CMA-ES algorithm.
* Likely to become a standard component in CMA-ES implementations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Minor concern regarding the level of detail provided about implementation optimizations.
* A slight lack of explanation of Figure 2 results for specific benchmark functions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a practical and theoretically sound improvement to a widely used algorithm. The demonstrated speedups are substantial, and the potential impact on applications in machine learning and other areas is significant. Despite minor points for clarification, the strengths of this paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses. I strongly recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016.
