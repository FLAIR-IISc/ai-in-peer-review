This paper addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a powerful derivative-free optimization algorithm widely used in machine learning. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a time complexity dominated by the `O(d^3)` cost of decomposing the `d x d` covariance matrix `C` in each iteration, which is necessary to compute its square root inverse `C^{-1/2}` for the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism.

The main contribution of this work is to propose an efficient `O(d^2)` update scheme by maintaining and directly updating a triangular Cholesky factor `A` of the covariance matrix (`C = AA^T`), thus avoiding the expensive decomposition step. The key challenge, which previous works have struggled with, is that the matrix square root `C^{1/2}` required by the CSA update is not equivalent to a Cholesky factor `A`. The authors elegantly circumvent this by replacing `C^{-1/2}` with `A^{-1}` in the CSA update. They provide a theoretical justification for this approximation, arguing via Lemma 1 that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the rotational difference between the coordinate systems defined by `C^{1/2}` and `A` diminishes. Because the CSA update is an exponentially weighted average of past steps, this error is expected to vanish over time. The resulting algorithm, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," is empirically shown to achieve the same convergence rate in terms of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES, but with a significant reduction in wall-clock time, especially for higher dimensions.

This work builds upon previous efforts to reduce the complexity of CMA-ES, such as delaying matrix updates (Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001), restricting the covariance matrix structure (e.g., to diagonal matrices, Ros and Hansen, 2008), or using Cholesky updates for CMA-ES variants (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015). This paper's novelty lies in successfully applying an efficient Cholesky update to the *standard* CMA-ES algorithm and providing a clear theoretical motivation for the necessary modification to the CSA rule.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the Cholesky update is correct, and the theoretical justification for approximating the CSA update, while based on an assumption of convergence, is plausible and well-reasoned. The claims are strongly supported by a well-designed set of experiments on standard benchmark functions. The authors compare their method against appropriate baselines and clearly demonstrate the claimed performance improvements in runtime.

One minor point for improvement is the claim of enhanced numerical stability. The abstract and Section 3 mention that the proposed update is "numerically stable" and avoids instabilities. While it is generally known that working with matrix factors can be more stable than forming and decomposing the full matrix, the paper does not explicitly explain or demonstrate *why* this specific update scheme is more stable than, for example, performing the rank-one updates on `C` and re-computing its Cholesky decomposition. A brief discussion or citation supporting this claim would strengthen the paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the background on CMA-ES is concise but sufficient, and the proposed method is explained logically. The algorithms are presented clearly, and the experimental results are easy to interpret from the figures. The work is presented with enough detail to allow for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The core idea of using rank-one updates on a Cholesky factor is not entirely new (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009). However, the key original contribution is the successful adaptation of this technique to the standard, state-of-the-art CMA-ES algorithm by providing a principled approximation for the CSA mechanism. The theoretical justification in Lemma 1, which connects the convergence of the covariance matrix shape to the validity of the approximation, is a novel and important insight that distinguishes this work from previous attempts.

**Significance:** The contribution is highly significant. CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization with numerous applications in machine learning (e.g., policy search, hyperparameter tuning). The high computational cost of the covariance matrix update has been a major barrier to its application in moderately high-dimensional settings (e.g., `d > 100`). This paper provides a practical, drop-in replacement for the standard implementation that dramatically reduces this cost without impairing optimization performance. This makes the full-covariance CMA-ES feasible for a wider range of problems. Researchers and practitioners who use CMA-ES are very likely to adopt this improved implementation.

While the proposed method significantly improves scaling, it still retains `O(d^2)` complexity, which can be prohibitive for very high-dimensional problems like training large neural networks, as mentioned by the authors. A valuable extension for future work could be to explore how this efficient Cholesky update framework could be combined with limited-memory or low-rank approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) to push the boundaries of scalability even further.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   It solves a long-standing and important computational bottleneck in CMA-ES, a widely used and powerful optimization algorithm.
*   The proposed solution is practical, effective, and backed by strong empirical evidence showing significant wall-clock time speedups.
*   The paper provides a novel theoretical justification for the necessary approximation in the CSA update, which has been a key hurdle for previous Cholesky-based approaches.
*   The work is of high quality, clearly written, and likely to have a significant impact on practitioners and researchers using CMA-ES.

**Arguments for Rejection/Improvement:**
*   The claim of improved numerical stability is asserted but not substantiated with either theoretical analysis or empirical results.
*   The theoretical argument hinges on the convergence of the covariance matrix shape, which is a reasonable but not guaranteed assumption.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It presents a valuable and practical contribution that advances the state-of-the-art for a foundational optimization algorithm.
