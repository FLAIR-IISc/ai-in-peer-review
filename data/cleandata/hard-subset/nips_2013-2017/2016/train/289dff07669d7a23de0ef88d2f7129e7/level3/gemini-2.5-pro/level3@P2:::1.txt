**Summary:**

This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a novel variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) designed to overcome its primary computational bottleneck: the cubic-time complexity of updating the covariance matrix. The authors propose to maintain and directly update a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, rather than the full matrix itself. This approach reduces the per-iteration time complexity to O(µd^2) and the memory complexity to O(d^2), both of which are asymptotically optimal for a full-rank covariance matrix. The key technical contribution is a modification to the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which traditionally requires the inverse of the matrix square root. The authors replace this with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor and provide a theoretical argument that the error introduced by this approximation vanishes as the algorithm converges and the covariance matrix shape stabilizes. The paper's claims are thoroughly supported by empirical results on various benchmark functions, showing that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves significant wall-clock speedups over the standard implementation, particularly in higher dimensions, without compromising the number of function evaluations required for convergence.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper is well-situated within the existing literature on scaling CMA-ES. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous approaches, such as delaying matrix updates (which can impair performance) or restricting the covariance structure to be diagonal (which fails on non-separable problems). The work builds upon prior ideas of using Cholesky factorizations (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), but it makes a crucial and novel contribution by successfully adapting the standard CSA mechanism. This specific adaptation, and the theoretical justification for it, is what has prevented a direct, efficient, and performance-preserving Cholesky update for the standard CMA-ES until now, clearly distinguishing this work from previous attempts.

**Quality:**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithmic modification is sound and well-justified. The theoretical argument in Lemma 1 provides a convincing rationale for why the approximation in the CSA step is principled and should not degrade performance. The experimental methodology is rigorous, comparing the proposed method against standard and state-of-the-art baselines across a range of benchmark functions and dimensionalities. The claims of improved wall-clock time without a loss in convergence speed (in terms of function evaluations) are strongly supported by the results presented. The work is a complete and polished contribution.

**Clarity:**

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The motivation for the work is stated plainly, and the background on CMA-ES is presented concisely. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the core technical challenge—the CSA mechanism's reliance on the matrix square root—and how their proposed modification addresses it. The inclusion of pseudocode (Algorithms 1 and 2) greatly aids in understanding the implementation. The figures are clear and effectively communicate the main experimental findings.

**Originality:**

The originality of this work lies in the novel and effective adaptation of the CSA update rule to work with a triangular Cholesky factorization. While the idea of updating a factorization of the covariance matrix is not new, this paper presents the first practical way to do so for the standard CMA-ES without sacrificing its performance or invariance properties. The theoretical justification for the approximation is also a novel contribution that lends significant weight to the approach.

**Significance:**

The results presented in this paper are highly significant. CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm for derivative-free optimization with numerous applications in machine learning, from policy search in reinforcement learning to hyperparameter tuning. By dramatically reducing the computational overhead of the covariance matrix update, this work makes CMA-ES practical for significantly higher-dimensional problems. The Cholesky-CMA-ES is a strong candidate to replace the standard implementation as the default choice, given its clear advantages in speed and memory with no discernible downside. Researchers and practitioners are highly likely to adopt this improved version.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a significant and practical speedup to a widely used, state-of-the-art optimization algorithm.
*   The proposed method is technically sound, well-motivated, and supported by both theoretical arguments and strong empirical evidence.
*   The work is novel in its specific solution to a long-standing challenge in implementing CMA-ES efficiently.
*   The paper is very clearly written and the results are easy to understand and appreciate.
*   The contribution is likely to have a high impact, as it will probably become the new standard for CMA-ES implementations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   None. The minor points below are suggestions for improvement, not flaws that would warrant rejection.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   To make the wall-clock time comparisons completely watertight, it would be beneficial to explicitly state that all compared methods were implemented in the same language (e.g., C++) and used the same optimized linear algebra libraries (e.g., LAPACK). This would confirm that the observed speedups are due to the algorithmic improvement and not implementation differences.
*   In Figure 2, the performance gain on the Cigar and Discus functions appears to plateau between d=128 and d=256. The authors mention this is likely due to L2 cache effects, which is plausible. A brief sentence or two elaborating on this observation in the results section would be helpful for the reader.
