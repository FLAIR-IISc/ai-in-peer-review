This paper presents a compelling improvement to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) by leveraging the triangular Cholesky factorization instead of the matrix square root in the standard algorithm. The core idea – eliminating the computationally expensive matrix square root computation – is well-motivated, as this step often dominates runtime, especially in high-dimensional optimization problems. The authors convincingly argue that this change doesn’t fundamentally alter the algorithm’s behavior, supported by theoretical justifications and empirical results. This work builds upon previous attempts to utilize Cholesky factors (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) and overcomes limitations of those approaches, specifically addressing applicability to the standard CMA-ES.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear introduction of the CMA-ES and its computational bottlenecks, followed by a detailed explanation of the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES. The background section provides necessary context regarding Cholesky decomposition and its relationship to the matrix square root. The theoretical justification, centered around Lemma 1 and the discussion of coordinate system rotations, is a critical component, providing confidence in the method's convergence properties. The experimental setup is thorough, comparing the Cholesky-CMA-ES against several variants of the standard CMA-ES across a range of benchmark functions and dimensionalities. The results clearly demonstrate significant runtime improvements, especially as dimensionality increases, without any increase in the number of function evaluations.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated. The derivation of the Cholesky update rule and the associated theoretical justification appear rigorous.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts. The use of notation is consistent, and the algorithm is presented in a concise manner.
* **Originality:** While building upon existing work, the authors present a novel combination of techniques – applying a Cholesky factorization update to the *standard* CMA-ES with a theoretical justification for its correctness – that significantly improves performance.
* **Significance:**  The runtime improvements demonstrated in the experiments are significant, particularly in higher dimensions.  The potential for applying this method to large-scale optimization problems, such as those encountered in machine learning (e.g., direct policy search, hyperparameter tuning), makes this work highly relevant and impactful. The optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity is a valuable contribution.
* **Empirical Validation:** The thorough experimental evaluation using a variety of benchmark functions and dimensionality settings strengthens the claims made in the paper.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Numerical Stability:** The abstract claims the proposed method is “numerically stable,” yet this aspect isn’t comprehensively discussed. While the use of Cholesky factorization generally improves numerical stability compared to directly computing the matrix square root, a more detailed explanation of *why* this is the case in the context of the CMA-ES would be beneficial. What specific numerical issues are mitigated by using the Cholesky factor, and are there any potential downsides?
* **Limited Discussion of High Dimensionality:** While the experiments show improvements with increasing dimensionality, the authors acknowledge performance degradation at d=256 due to L2 cache limitations. The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of scaling to very high dimensions and a potential integration with low-dimensional approximation techniques, as suggested (Loshchilov, 2015).
* **Rotation Invariance:** While the theoretical justification for the approximation touches upon rotation invariance, the empirical validation of this aspect could be strengthened.  The experiments mention applying a rotation matrix, but a more detailed analysis of how the performance of Cholesky-CMA-ES compares to the standard CMA-ES under different rotations would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant performance bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm (CMA-ES).
* Provides a theoretically sound and empirically validated improvement.
* Offers a practical implementation with demonstrable runtime benefits, especially in high dimensions.
* Is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to researchers in the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The claim regarding “numerical stability” lacks detailed justification.
* The discussion of scaling to very high dimensions is somewhat limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant and practical improvement to the CMA-ES algorithm. The authors have successfully addressed a key computational bottleneck while maintaining the core properties of the original algorithm. The theoretical justification and empirical results are convincing, and the potential impact on the field of derivative-free optimization is substantial. While the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of numerical stability and scalability, these are relatively minor weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016. The contribution is significant, the work is well-executed, and the paper is of high quality.
