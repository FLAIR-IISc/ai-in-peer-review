

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, an efficient variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that reduces the computational complexity of covariance matrix updates from O(d³) to O(μd²) by maintaining a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix or its square root. The key insight is that replacing the matrix square root in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) with the Cholesky factor introduces an error term involving a rotation matrix E_t, which the authors theoretically justify converges to identity under the assumption that the covariance matrix converges to a stable shape (Lemma 1). Empirically, the method achieves identical optimization performance (function evaluations) to standard CMA-ES while demonstrating significant runtime improvements, especially for higher dimensions (20× faster at d=64). The paper compares against two state-of-the-art baselines: a delayed update strategy (CMA-ES-Ref) and a non-triangular Cholesky approach (Suttorp-CMA-ES).

**Quality**: The technical contribution is sound. The O(μd²) update complexity is asymptotically optimal for full covariance adaptation, and the implementation details (Algorithms 1–2) are clearly presented. The empirical validation on standard benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar, Rosenbrock, etc.) is thorough, with consistent results across 100 trials per dimension (d=4–256). However, the theoretical justification relies on restrictive convergence assumptions (C_t → C̄ with det C̄=1), which may not hold for non-convergent or rapidly changing landscapes common in practice. The authors acknowledge this limitation but provide no formal error bounds for the E_t approximation. The absence of E_t tracking experiments (as suggested in key points) weakens the theoretical claims, as it remains unclear how rapidly the rotation error vanishes in real runs.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a strong focus on the core algorithmic modification. The background on CMA-ES (Section 2) and the Cholesky update derivation (Section 3) are logically presented. The experimental setup (Section 4) adequately describes benchmarks, dimensions, and baselines. However, the discussion of the orthogonal matrix E_t’s role in CSA (Equation 5) could be more intuitive for readers unfamiliar with information geometry. The runtime comparison in Figure 2 uses logarithmic scales, which effectively conveys the dimension-dependent speedup but may obscure absolute time differences for smaller d.

**Originality**: The work extends prior Cholesky-based CMA-ES variants (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) by providing a theoretically grounded approach to the CSA modification. While the core idea of using Cholesky factors is not entirely novel, the authors successfully address a key gap (the E_t rotation) that previous attempts could not resolve, making the method applicable to the standard CMA-ES. This distinguishes it from related work like Ros & Hansen (2008), which restricts to diagonal covariance, and Loshchilov (2014), which uses low-rank approximations. The paper adequately cites these predecessors.

**Significance**: The contribution is significant for high-dimensional optimization scenarios where covariance updates dominate runtime (e.g., d > 64), as demonstrated in the experiments. The method enables efficient use of full covariance adaptation in problems where prior approaches would be infeasible due to O(d³) costs. However, the practical relevance is limited by the context: in most machine learning applications (hyperparameter tuning, policy search), the objective evaluation time far exceeds covariance update time, making the speedup marginal for typical dimensions (d < 50). The authors acknowledge this ("if updating and storing the covariance matrix matters"), but the paper overstates the broader impact by claiming the method "should replace the standard CMA-ES" without qualifying when this bottleneck actually arises. A real-world test (e.g., reinforcement learning on robotics tasks) would have strengthened the significance argument, as suggested in the key points.

**Strengths**:  
- Solves a well-motivated computational bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm.  
- Theoretical justification (Lemma 1) provides a principled basis for the approximation.  
- Empirical results confirm no loss in optimization performance with substantial runtime gains on synthetic benchmarks.  
- Optimal storage complexity (O(d²) vs. O(d²) for standard CMA-ES) and free eigenvalue extraction via Cholesky factor.  

**Weaknesses**:  
- Theoretical guarantees are limited to restrictive convergence assumptions; no formal error analysis for non-convergent cases.  
- Overstated significance for mainstream ML applications where dimensionality rarely necessitates this optimization.  
- Missing key experiments: (1) Tracking E_t evolution to quantify the rotation error, (2) Full CMA-ES runtime without delay strategies for baseline completeness, (3) Real-world application showing tangible speedup.  
- The "optimal" complexity claim ignores the O(μd²) factor (μ = O(log d)), which is asymptotically negligible but matters for moderate d.  

**Recommendation**: The paper presents a technically sound and well-executed improvement to CMA-ES with clear empirical validation. While the theoretical justification is somewhat limited, the practical runtime benefits for high-dimensional problems are compelling. However, the significance for NIPS is moderate due to the narrow scope of applicability (only when covariance update dominates runtime). The work is a solid contribution to optimization theory but does not advance the broader machine learning community as much as a method with wider relevance would. **Accept with minor revisions** (addressing the suggested experiments in the key points, particularly E_t tracking and real-world validation).
