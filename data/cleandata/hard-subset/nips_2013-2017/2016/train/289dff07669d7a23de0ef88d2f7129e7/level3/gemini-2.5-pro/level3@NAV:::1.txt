This paper presents Cholesky-CMA-ES, a computationally efficient variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy, a cornerstone algorithm for derivative-free optimization with numerous applications in machine learning. The authors address the primary bottleneck of standard CMA-ES: the O(d^3) complexity of updating the covariance matrix. The core contribution is a method to maintain and update the covariance matrix via its triangular Cholesky factor, reducing the per-iteration complexity to O(µd^2) with optimal O(d^2) memory usage. This builds upon previous efforts to improve CMA-ES efficiency but provides a novel and stable adaptation of the cumulative step-size mechanism, supported by a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) explaining why the approximation does not degrade optimization performance.

**Strengths:**
The paper's main strength is its clear practical significance. By drastically reducing the wall-clock time of a widely used algorithm without increasing the number of function evaluations, the authors have produced a work that is highly likely to be adopted by practitioners. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating substantial speedups over the reference implementation across a range of benchmark problems, particularly at higher dimensions (d≥64). The proposed method is technically sound, and the authors are honest about the approximation made. Additional benefits, such as obtaining covariance matrix eigenvalues for free, further enhance the utility of the approach. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and positions its contribution effectively within the existing literature. The originality lies in the specific, stable modification that makes a Cholesky-based update feasible for the standard CMA-ES framework.

**Weaknesses:**
The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to details that would strengthen the paper’s clarity and reproducibility.
1.  The authors should clarify the implementation details for the runtime comparisons. To ensure a fair comparison, it is important to confirm that all tested algorithms were implemented in the same language (e.g., C++) and utilized the same underlying numerical libraries (e.g., LAPACK).
2.  A more detailed explanation for the results in Figure 2 would be beneficial. The paper mentions L2 cache effects at d=256, but does not fully explain why the performance differences for the Cigar and Discus functions are less pronounced than for other functions at this dimension.

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a significant and practical advancement to a foundational optimization algorithm. The identified weaknesses are minor and easily addressable. I strongly recommend acceptance.
