Summary and relation to prior work
The paper targets the dominant O(d3) cost in standard CMA-ES stemming from repeated matrix square-root/eigendecomposition when updating and sampling from the full covariance. It proposes maintaining a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance C = AAT and performing all rank-one/rank-µ covariance updates directly on A in O(µ d2) time and O(d2) memory. The second ingredient is a modification of cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA): replace C−1/2 in the CSA path update by A−1, which is cheap to apply. The authors argue that the induced rotational error vanishes as the normalized covariance converges (Lemma 1), and empirically observe that function evaluation counts essentially match standard CMA-ES while wall-clock time decreases substantially with dimension.

This direction relates closely to the Cholesky-update CMA variants by Suttorp et al. (2009), who also avoided explicit covariance formation and updated factors directly (but not constrained to triangular form), and to the efficient rank-one update in Krause and Igel (2015), which provided a quadratic-time update in the (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES settings. It also complements large-scale approximations (e.g., Ros & Hansen 2008; Poland & Zell 2001; Sun et al. 2013; Loshchilov 2014, 2015) that trade model capacity for O(d) cost.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant problem: reducing time and memory of full-covariance CMA-ES without changing evaluation complexity.
- Simple, implementable update: triangular Cholesky factor with a rank-one update routine; A−1 is available in O(d2); eigenvalues are on the diagonal of A and readily monitored.
- Theoretical rationale: Lemma 1 gives intuition for why using A−1 in CSA introduces a diminishing rotational error as the normalized covariance stabilizes.
- Empirically promising: similar evaluation counts to a reference CMA-ES across several benchmarks and substantial wall-clock speedups that grow with d.
- Good discussion of numerical considerations (triangular storage, inversion stability) and complexity.

Weaknesses
- Naming confusion: “Cholesky-CMA-ES” is an established name in the literature (Suttorp et al., 2009). Reusing it for a different variant is misleading.
- Positioning vs prior work is incomplete: the paper does not adequately contrast its triangular update to the non-triangular factor update by Suttorp et al. (2009) nor to the algorithmic advances in Krause & Igel (2015). The novelty appears mainly to be extending the rank-one triangular update to µ > 1 via a loop; this should be better justified and contrasted.
- Empirical comparison is insufficient: a rigorous, head-to-head comparison against Suttorp’s method and the Krause update (where applicable) is needed, both in function evaluations and time, across d, with identical parameter settings and implementation care. Claims of numerical instability in Suttorp (2009) contradict that paper’s own analysis and should be supported by controlled experiments or toned down.
- Overstated “optimality”: the approach is optimal among full-covariance methods for rank-µ updates, but it is not optimal for separable or nearly separable problems where diagonal or limited-memory variants are superior. Also, the improvement over Suttorp’s approach may be modest (often within a small constant factor).
- Scalability: complexity remains quadratic; the method does not address truly large-scale settings where full covariance is infeasible, so the broader impact is limited to regimes where O(d2) is still viable.
- Experimental clarity: differences on the Cigar plateau may be plotting artifacts (e.g., median depiction); clarification is needed (e.g., ECDFs, error bars, and explicit definition of how medians across runs are computed).
- Minor factual point: while the introduction states that computing the square root dominates each iteration, most implementations decompose only periodically; please reconcile statements with actual practice and your chosen reference baseline.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The Cholesky rank-one update routine and the complexity claims are sound. The CSA approximation is plausible and supported by an asymptotic argument, but no quantitative bound is provided; a finite-time error analysis (or at least diagnostic plots of the CSA path length under rotation) would strengthen the case. The claim of superior numerical stability relative to Suttorp’s approach requires evidence.
- Empirical rigor: Stronger baselines and ablations are needed. Suggested additions: (1) Suttorp’s method (well-tuned), (2) Krause & Igel’s update where applicable, (3) diagonal/LM-CMA as lower bounds on time, (4) periodic eigen-decomposition reference with realistic delays, (5) ablation of CSA variant (A−1 vs occasional polar-corrected C−1/2), (6) breakdown of time per component (sampling, selection, updates).

Clarity
- Generally well-written with clear algorithms. Please:
  - Resolve naming to avoid confusion with Suttorp’s “Cholesky CMA-ES.”
  - Explicitly state the incremental nature of the algorithmic change (rank-one update reused µ times) and why this is non-trivial (e.g., numerical stability, triangular constraint, and interactions with CSA).
  - Clarify plotting choices and the apparent plateau differences.
  - Reconcile claims about eigen-decomposition frequency in practice.

Originality
- Moderate. The triangular rank-one update and avoiding explicit covariance are known ideas; the main contribution is integrating them into standard CMA-ES with a CSA modification and providing a justification that the rotational error diminishes. This is useful engineering but not conceptually groundbreaking.

Significance
- Practical for users who must run full-covariance CMA-ES at moderate-to-high d: same evaluation complexity, lower time/memory. Limited impact on truly large-scale optimization where O(d2) is already prohibitive.

Detailed suggestions
- Rename the method to avoid clashing with Suttorp et al. (e.g., TriChol-CMA-ES or tChol-CMA-ES).
- Add thorough comparisons to Suttorp (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015), with matched BLAS/LAPACK usage, cache considerations, and accuracy/stability diagnostics (condition numbers, failure rates, divergence).
- Provide finite-time diagnostics of CSA error (‖pσ‖ trajectories, rotation Et changes, per-iteration variance of path lengths).
- Include ECDFs and error bars for function evaluation counts and wall-clock times; define aggregation (median of medians vs pooled median).
- Quantify constant-factor speedups vs Suttorp and reference CMA-ES across dimensions.
- Temper “optimality” and “numerical stability” claims or support them with evidence.
- Note that eigen-decomposition is typically periodic in reference implementations; ensure baselines reflect that practice.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important practical bottleneck with a simple, implementable change.
- Strong empirical speedups without harming evaluation complexity.
- Useful to the CMA-ES community; code appears straightforward to integrate.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete comparison and positioning vs closely related prior work.
- Limited novelty; main change is a careful integration and CSA approximation.
- Overstated claims (optimality, stability) and unresolved naming conflict.
- Lacks quantitative bounds or robust diagnostics for the CSA approximation.

Overall, this is a solid engineering contribution with practical value, but it needs clearer positioning, stronger baselines, and toned-down claims before being ready for NIPS.
