This paper proposes a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a cornerstone algorithm for derivative-free optimization with many applications in machine learning. The primary contribution is a method to reduce the computational complexity of the covariance matrix update, which is the main bottleneck of CMA-ES in high dimensions. The standard approach requires an O(d^3) eigenvalue decomposition. The authors propose directly maintaining and updating a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. This reduces the update complexity to O(μd^2), which is asymptotically optimal for an update involving μ vectors, and reduces the memory footprint from O(d^2) to O(d^2/2).

A key challenge in this approach is that the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism in standard CMA-ES relies on the matrix square root of the inverse covariance matrix (C^{-1/2}), which is not a triangular matrix and expensive to compute. The authors' main technical contribution is to replace this term with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor (A^{-1}). They provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) that this approximation is sound, arguing that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the error introduced by this substitution vanishes due to the convergence of the associated rotation matrices. This work builds upon previous efforts to accelerate CMA-ES, such as delaying matrix updates or using non-triangular Cholesky updates (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), but provides a more complete and efficient solution for the modern standard CMA-ES algorithm.

**Strengths and Weaknesses (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance)**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithm, "Cholesky-CMA-ES," is well-defined, and the derivation is sound. The theoretical argument for the validity of the approximation in the CSA update is convincing and provides a solid foundation for the method. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing the proposed algorithm against the standard reference implementation and another accelerated variant on a suite of standard benchmark functions across a range of dimensions. The experimental results strongly support the authors' claims: the number of function evaluations required for convergence is statistically indistinguishable from the standard CMA-ES, while the wall-clock time is significantly reduced.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the background on CMA-ES is concise and accurate, and the proposed method is introduced logically. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the subtle but critical technical challenge regarding the CSA update and how their approach resolves it. The inclusion of pseudocode for the main algorithm and the rank-one update routine is helpful for reproducibility. The experimental results are presented clearly in figures that effectively communicate the main findings.

**Originality:** While the general idea of updating Cholesky factors to speed up CMA-ES has been explored before (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), this work presents a novel and significant advancement. The key originality lies in successfully adapting this idea to the full, modern CMA-ES (including the rank-µ update) and, most importantly, providing a principled solution and justification for modifying the CSA mechanism. This modification was a known obstacle that previous work had not fully resolved for the standard algorithm.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. CMA-ES is a powerful and widely used optimization tool in machine learning for problems like direct policy search in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization. Its primary limitation has always been its poor scaling with dimensionality. This paper provides a practical, "drop-in" replacement that alleviates this bottleneck for medium-to-large scale problems (e.g., d up to several hundreds), where maintaining a full covariance matrix is still feasible. The demonstrated speedups (e.g., 20x faster at d=64) are substantial. Given its clear advantages in speed and memory with no apparent downside in optimization performance, this Cholesky-CMA-ES variant is a strong candidate to become the new default implementation for a wide range of applications.

The paper's main weaknesses are very minor and are primarily requests for clarification that would further strengthen the work.

1.  **Implementation Details for Comparison:** The paper mentions that the Cholesky-CMA-ES was implemented in the Shark library using LAPACK. For the wall-clock time comparisons to be fully convincing, could the authors confirm that the baseline "CMA-ES-Ref" was also implemented using a similarly optimized numerical library (e.g., LAPACK) for its O(d^3) eigenvalue decompositions? This would ensure the reported speedups are purely from the algorithmic improvement and not from differences in implementation quality.

2.  **Results in Figure 2:** The performance gains shown in Figure 2 for the Cigar and Discus functions at d=256 appear slightly less pronounced compared to the gains on other functions. The text mentions that L2 cache size was exceeded at d=256, but it would be helpful if the authors could briefly elaborate on why this might affect the Cigar/Discus benchmarks differently, if that is indeed the case.

**Summary of Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
*   Addresses a critical, long-standing scalability bottleneck in a state-of-the-art, widely-used optimization algorithm.
*   Presents a technically sound and elegant solution with a solid theoretical justification.
*   Empirical results are comprehensive and convincingly demonstrate significant wall-clock speedups without degrading optimization performance.
*   The work is of high practical importance and the proposed algorithm is likely to be widely adopted by practitioners and researchers.
*   The paper is very well-written and clear.

**Con:**
*   (Minor) The paper could benefit from minor clarifications regarding experimental implementation and specific results, as noted above.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant and practical contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization, with direct relevance to the machine learning community. It is well-motivated, technically sound, and thoroughly validated. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
