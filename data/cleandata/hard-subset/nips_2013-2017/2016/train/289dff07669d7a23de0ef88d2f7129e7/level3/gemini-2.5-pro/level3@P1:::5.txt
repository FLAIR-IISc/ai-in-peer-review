This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) to reduce its computational complexity. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a time complexity dominated by the O(d^3) cost of decomposing the d x d covariance matrix in each iteration. The authors propose the "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which avoids this cost by directly maintaining and updating a lower-triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix. This reduces the per-iteration complexity for the matrix update to O(µd^2), which is a significant improvement for problems where function evaluations are cheap and dimensionality d is high.

The core challenge in this adaptation lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires multiplying by the inverse of the matrix square root (C^{-1/2}). This cannot be directly replaced by the inverse of a Cholesky factor (A^{-1}) because C^{1/2} = AE, where E is an orthogonal matrix that changes at each iteration. The authors' key contribution is to approximate the CSA update by simply omitting this orthogonal matrix E. They provide a theoretical justification for this approximation in Lemma 1, arguing that under the assumption that the (normalized) covariance matrix C_t converges, the orthogonal matrix E_t will also converge, and the error introduced by this approximation will vanish over time.

The paper empirically validates this approach by comparing the Cholesky-CMA-ES against state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants that use a delayed update strategy. On a suite of standard benchmark functions, the proposed method demonstrates a substantial reduction in wall-clock time, especially for higher dimensions, while requiring the same number of function evaluations to reach the optimum.

**Quality and Technical Soundness**

The paper presents a technically sound and well-executed piece of work. The derivation of the Cholesky update is correct and builds upon prior work in the field. The experimental evaluation is solid, using standard benchmarks and appropriate baselines to demonstrate the method's effectiveness in terms of computational speedup.

However, the theoretical justification for the core approximation has limitations. Lemma 1 relies on the convergence of the normalized covariance matrix C_t, which is a strong assumption for a stochastic optimization algorithm and is not formally guaranteed. While the authors' intuition that the error is small when C_t changes slowly is plausible, the paper lacks a formal analysis of the error bound or the conditions under which this assumption holds. This makes the theoretical contribution more of a well-motivated heuristic than a rigorous guarantee.

**Clarity**

The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly stated, the background on CMA-ES is concise yet sufficient, and the description of the proposed method and its core approximation is lucid. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, with informative plots that effectively convey the main message.

**Originality**

The idea of updating a Cholesky factor directly is not entirely new, and the authors appropriately cite previous work in this area (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015). However, the main novelty and contribution of this work is the successful application of this idea to the standard, modern CMA-ES with its complex CSA mechanism, and the provision of a theoretical argument to justify the necessary approximation. This is a non-trivial extension that makes the efficient update scheme applicable to a much wider and more commonly used variant of the algorithm.

**Significance**

The work is significant for the derivative-free optimization community. CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm, and its O(d^3) complexity is a known bottleneck. This paper provides a practical, easy-to-implement, and empirically validated solution that effectively removes this bottleneck. For applications where function evaluations are computationally cheap (e.g., simple physics simulations for policy search, optimization of analytical functions) and dimensionality is moderately high (d > 50), this contribution could enable the use of CMA-ES on problems that were previously computationally prohibitive.

A potential limitation to the work's broader impact is that in many machine learning applications (e.g., hyperparameter tuning of deep neural networks), the cost of a single function evaluation far exceeds the O(d^3) cost of the CMA-ES update, especially for the modest dimensionalities typically encountered. In such cases, the proposed speedup would be negligible. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including an application or discussion of a real-world problem where this specific computational bottleneck is the primary limiting factor.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Empirical Validation of the Core Assumption:** The central claim is that the error from ignoring the orthogonal matrix E_t is small. This could be strengthened empirically. It would be highly insightful to run the standard CMA-ES and simultaneously compute the Cholesky factor A_t and the corresponding E_t = A_t^{-1}C_t^{1/2}. Plotting a norm of (E_t E_{t-1}^T - I) over the course of an optimization run would provide direct evidence of how quickly the rotational error term decays, lending stronger support to the theoretical argument.

2.  **More Challenging Benchmarks:** The experiments could be extended to better probe the limits of the approximation. For instance, using quadratic functions with continuously varying spectra (as in Stich & Müller, 2012) could reveal if the approximation quality depends on the conditioning or structure of the problem.

3.  **Inclusion of a Non-Delayed Baseline:** For completeness, it would be useful to include results for a standard CMA-ES that performs the full O(d^3) decomposition at *every* iteration. While computationally expensive, this would provide a clearer picture of the absolute speedup achieved compared to the most "correct" version of the algorithm, rather than just against other speedup heuristics like the delayed update.

4.  **Demonstration on a Real-World Problem:** To bolster the argument for practical significance, including a case study on a real-world problem would be very valuable. An example from direct policy search in robotics, where policies might have hundreds of parameters and simulations can be fast, would be a compelling use case.

**Summary**

**Pros:**
*   Addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in a very popular optimization algorithm.
*   The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES provides a significant and empirically verified speedup in wall-clock time.
*   The method is practical, achieves optimal storage complexity, and does not seem to degrade optimization performance (in terms of function evaluations).
*   The paper is clearly written and the contribution is easy to understand.

**Cons:**
*   The theoretical justification relies on a restrictive convergence assumption that is not formally guaranteed.
*   The practical impact is limited to a specific regime (cheap function evaluations, high dimension), and the paper lacks a compelling real-world application to demonstrate this.

Overall, this is a strong and valuable contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. It presents a practical and effective improvement to a widely used algorithm. While the theoretical guarantees are somewhat weak and the practical scope could be better motivated, the empirical results are convincing. I recommend acceptance.
