This paper proposes a modification to the CMA-ES algorithm, replacing the computationally expensive eigen-decomposition required for covariance matrix updates with a Cholesky decomposition. The authors argue this yields significant speedups, particularly in high-dimensional problems, while maintaining the algorithm’s performance. While the core idea is appealing and the writing is generally clear, several critical issues prevent acceptance in its current form.

The paper begins with a good overview of CMA-ES and its challenges, situating the work within the broader context of derivative-free optimization and highlighting applications in machine learning. The motivation for avoiding frequent eigenvalue decompositions is well-established, and the proposal to use Cholesky factorization is a natural extension of previous work attempting to improve computational efficiency (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015). The authors correctly identify the potential for significant runtime reductions, particularly as dimensionality increases.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of originality. The proposed method appears to be a straightforward extension of the work by Krause and Igel (2015), which already demonstrated a quadratic-time Cholesky update for a specific CMA-ES variant. While extending this to the standard CMA-ES is a valuable step, the paper doesn’t adequately emphasize the incremental nature of this contribution. A more thorough discussion comparing and contrasting the approach to Krause and Igel (2015) is crucial. In fact, the similarity to "A More Efficient Rank-one Covariance Matrix Update for Evolution Strategies" is striking and requires careful acknowledgement and differentiation.

A major weakness is the missing equivalence proof. The authors state they present an approach "that comes with a theoretical justification for why it does not deteriorate the algorithm’s performance," but they fail to explicitly demonstrate the equivalence between their algorithm and the original CMA-ES update formula (2). This proof is *essential*, even if the equivalence has been shown previously in related work. Without it, the claim that performance is maintained remains unsubstantiated.

The complexity claim of O(μd^2) is also problematic. This is the same asymptotic complexity as CMA-ES-Ref, and simply stating it’s faster is insufficient. The authors *must* use Θ-notation to accurately represent the complexity and provide a detailed explanation of why their implementation achieves a practical speedup. A clear analysis of the constant factors involved is necessary. The authors should also explain how the µ factor influences the complexity in practice.

The claim regarding a small and decreasing path error, contrasting with Hansen’s work, is not adequately supported. Asserting this requires experimental validation *over time*, not just a comparison of final performance. Tracking the error metric over multiple generations would provide stronger evidence. Observing only the final performance doesn’t rule out the possibility of a transient increase in error during the optimization process.

Minor issues detract from the overall quality. There are notation errors (wk should be α_k in Algorithm 2), and typos (n should be d in Table 1, 'F' should be 'f' on line 185). These suggest a lack of meticulousness in the preparation of the manuscript.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Potential for significant speedups in CMA-ES, especially for high-dimensional problems.
*   Clear writing style and logical presentation of the method.
*   Well-motivated problem statement and identified inefficiency in the original algorithm.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited originality – closely related to prior work (Krause & Igel, 2015) and "A More Efficient Rank-one Covariance Matrix Update for Evolution Strategies".
*   Missing equivalence proof to demonstrate theoretical validity.
*   Unclear and insufficiently justified complexity claim.
*   Unvalidated claim about path error decreasing.
*   Minor notation errors and typos.

**Overall Recommendation:**

Reject. The paper presents a potentially useful improvement to CMA-ES, but the lack of a crucial equivalence proof, the unsubstantiated complexity claim, and the unsupported claim about path error, coupled with the limited originality, render it unsuitable for NIPS in its current form. Addressing these issues through a more rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive experimental validation is essential before resubmission.
