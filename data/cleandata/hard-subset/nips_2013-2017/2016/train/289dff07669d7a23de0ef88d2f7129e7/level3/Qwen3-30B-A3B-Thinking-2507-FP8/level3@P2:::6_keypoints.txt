- Proposes "Cholesky-CMA-ES": replace matrix square root with Cholesky factor in CSA, claiming O(µd²) time/space and similar optimization performance.
- Key insight: Cholesky factor can be updated in quadratic time and the introduced error vanishes as the algorithm converges.
- Theoretical justification for the approach is strong.
- Builds on Hansen & Ostermeier (2001) and relates to Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015) but does not adequately compare to them.
- Correctly identifies matrix square root computation as a computational bottleneck in CMA-ES.
- Empirical results show substantial speedups (up to ~20x at d=64).
- Noted benefit: Cholesky factorization provides eigenvalues as a byproduct.
- Naming is misleading and conflicts with prior Cholesky-based CMA-ES (Suttorp et al. 2009).
- Algorithmic contribution appears incremental (essentially looping rank-one updates for all individuals).
- Experimental evaluation is incomplete—omits direct comparisons to Suttorp’s and Krause’s Cholesky methods.
- Claim of "optimal" complexity is overstated; separable problems admit cheaper diagonal updates than the proposed full Cholesky update.
- Numerical stability claim is questionable and contradicts Suttorp et al.’s stability assertions.
- Misrepresentation of standard CMA-ES: eigenvalue decomposition is typically periodic, not at every update as claimed.
- Paper is technically sound but undermined by insufficient experimental comparison with prior work.
- Writing is generally clear but confusion arises from naming and lack of differentiation from prior methods.
- Originality is limited; main idea is an incremental extension rather than a fundamentally new method.
- Significance: provides practical speedup for non-separable problems but does not resolve scalability for truly large-scale problems.
- Pros: practical speedup, solid theoretical backing, useful eigenvalue byproduct.
- Cons: misleading naming, incomplete prior-work comparison, overstated optimality, questionable stability claims.
- Required revisions: resolve naming conflict, add direct experiments vs Suttorp/Krause, clarify scope of optimality, strengthen numerical stability evidence, correct eigen-decomposition frequency claim.
