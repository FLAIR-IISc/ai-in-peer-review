

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that maintains a lower-triangular Cholesky factor instead of the full covariance matrix, reducing computational complexity from O(d³) to O(μd²) per iteration. The key insight is that by omitting the estimation of a random orthogonal matrix E_t in the cumulative step-size adaptation, the algorithm can use the Cholesky factor directly. The authors claim that under restrictive convergence assumptions (where E_t → I), the Cholesky approximation becomes asymptotically equivalent to the full CMA-ES. Empirical results on standard benchmark functions demonstrate significant speed improvements without loss of optimization performance.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the optimization literature, building on prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015) who explored Cholesky updates in specialized CMA-ES variants. The theoretical justification using Lemma 1 provides a novel argument for why the approximation remains valid under certain convergence conditions.

Quality: The empirical results are well-presented with clear runtime comparisons across dimensions, though the theoretical claims require stronger justification. The paper acknowledges practical limitations (e.g., objective evaluation time dominating in real applications), but the formal guarantees remain limited.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with detailed algorithm descriptions. The connection between Cholesky decomposition and sampling from multivariate Gaussians is particularly well-explained.

Originality: The work extends prior Cholesky-based approaches to standard CMA-ES with theoretical justification, making it a meaningful improvement over existing methods. The focus on optimal storage complexity is novel.

Significance: While the speedup is substantial for high-dimensional problems with cheap function evaluations, the practical impact is limited since most real-world applications involve expensive function evaluations where the covariance update is negligible. The suggested experiments (tracking E_t evolution, testing on real-time robotics problems) would strengthen the significance claim.

Pro: Theoretical justification, clear empirical validation, substantial speedup for high-dimensional problems.

Con: Limited practical impact in real applications, restrictive theoretical assumptions, no real-world application demonstrating speed benefits.

The paper presents a technically sound contribution to optimization algorithms with clear computational advantages for specific scenarios, though its broader significance is somewhat limited. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that it's a valuable technical contribution for a specific niche.
