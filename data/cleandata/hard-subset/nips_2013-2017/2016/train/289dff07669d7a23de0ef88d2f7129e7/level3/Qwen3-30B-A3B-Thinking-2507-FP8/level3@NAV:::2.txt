

This paper proposes a Cholesky-CMA-ES variant that replaces the matrix square root computation in the standard CMA-ES with a Cholesky factorization, claiming O(μd²) complexity instead of O(d³) for covariance updates. The authors argue this approach maintains the algorithm's performance while significantly reducing computational overhead, particularly for high-dimensional problems. The work builds on prior efforts like Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015), but claims to provide theoretical justification for why their modification doesn't degrade performance.

Strengths include clear writing, reasonable methodological approach, and promising empirical speedups (up to 20× faster for d=64). The authors correctly identify the storage complexity advantage and provide a detailed algorithm. The paper also offers useful theoretical insights about the convergence of the rotation matrix error.

However, several critical weaknesses prevent acceptance. First, the originality is severely limited - the approach is nearly identical to "A More Efficient Rank-one Covariance Matrix Update for Evolution Strategies" (Krause & Igel, 2015), which already presented a similar Cholesky-based update for CMA-ES. The paper fails to adequately distinguish itself from this prior work.

Second, the missing equivalence proof is critical. The authors claim the algorithm is equivalent to the standard CMA-ES but provide no proof that their modification to the CSA mechanism (replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹) yields identical behavior. This is essential even if the equivalence was proven elsewhere.

Third, the complexity claim is misleading. Claiming O(μd²) is the same as CMA-ES-Ref is incorrect; the authors should use Θ-notation and clearly explain why their method is faster than the delayed update strategy they compare against.

Fourth, the claim about "small path error that decreases" (contrary to Hansen) lacks proper validation - the paper only shows final performance comparisons, not error evolution over time.

Finally, the paper contains several notation errors (wk→α_k, n→d, 'F'→'f') that undermine credibility.

While the computational benefits are promising, the paper fails to meet NIPS standards due to insufficient originality, missing theoretical justification, and unclear complexity claims. The authors should substantially revise to address these issues before reconsideration.
