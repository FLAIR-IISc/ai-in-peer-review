- Proposes "Cholesky-CMA-ES" that maintains a triangular Cholesky factor to reduce covariance update from O(d³) to O(µd²) and storage to O(d²).
- Replaces C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ in CSA and justifies the approximation via Lemma 1, arguing the error vanishes as covariance shape stabilizes.
- Empirically matches standard CMA-ES in function evaluations while giving substantial wall-clock speedups, especially in higher dimensions.
- Derivation of the Cholesky update is technically sound and experiments clearly demonstrate runtime improvements.
- Claims that non-triangular Cholesky updates are "prone to numerical instabilities" are unsupported and should be removed or evidenced.
- Statements of "asymptotically optimal time/memory complexity" are overstated and need qualification since simpler problems admit linear complexity.
- The work does not overcome the fundamental quadratic scaling limitation of full-covariance CMA-ES; scalability framing should be clearer.
- The name "Cholesky-CMA-ES" conflicts with Suttorp et al. (2009) and creates confusion; renaming is strongly recommended.
- Figure 1 (Cigar function) shows a plateau for the proposed method that needs clarification whether it’s a real degradation or a plotting artifact.
- The paper implies standard CMA-ES does EVD every iteration, but practical implementations use delayed decomposition; this should be clarified early.
- Novelty is incremental: primarily extends Krause & Igel (2015) rank-one triangular update to rank-µ via iteration.
- Major omission: no empirical comparison to Suttorp et al. (2009), the most direct O(d²) competitor, which is essential to validate superiority.
- Should also explicitly compare to and differentiate from Krause & Igel (2015) to clarify precise contribution.
- Practically significant: the runtime reduction makes full-covariance CMA-ES more usable for moderate dimensions when evaluations are cheap.
- Scientifically limited: lack of proper comparisons to prior art weakens the claim of real-world advantage over other quadratic-time variants.
- For acceptance: addresses an important bottleneck, shows strong practical speedup, and provides reasonable theoretical support.
- Against acceptance: contribution seems incremental, omits key baseline comparisons, uses a conflicting name, and contains overstated/unsupported claims.
- Recommended revisions: rename the algorithm, add experiments including Suttorp-CMA-ES (and Krause & Igel), clearly state novelty, and temper/remove unsupported claims.
