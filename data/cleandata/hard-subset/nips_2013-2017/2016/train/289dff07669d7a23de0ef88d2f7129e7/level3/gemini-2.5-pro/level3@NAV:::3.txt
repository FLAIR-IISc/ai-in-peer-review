This paper presents the Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy aimed at reducing its computational complexity from O(d^3) to an optimal O(µd^2) for the covariance update. The core idea, which builds upon previous work like Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015), is to directly maintain and update the triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, rather than the full matrix itself. The authors extend the rank-1 Cholesky update from Krause & Igel to the standard CMA-ES, which includes both rank-1 and rank-μ updates. The main technical challenge is adapting the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA), which requires the matrix square root C^{-1/2}. The paper proposes approximating this term with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor, A^{-1}, and provides a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) that the error introduced by this approximation diminishes as the shape of the covariance matrix converges.

The primary strength of this work lies in its practical significance. The O(d^3) complexity of the standard CMA-ES is a well-known bottleneck, and the proposed method provides a tangible and effective solution. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating a significant wall-clock time speed-up over reference implementations without degrading optimization performance in terms of function evaluations. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and presents a complete algorithm that could be readily adopted by practitioners.

However, the paper has some weaknesses, primarily concerning originality and theoretical rigor. The contribution's novelty is somewhat limited, as the extension of the Cholesky update from the rank-1 case (Krause et al., 2015) to the standard rank-μ case is a relatively straightforward generalization. Furthermore, the theoretical justification for the modified CSA in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption of covariance matrix convergence. This is a strong assumption for a stochastic algorithm, and the paper would be stronger if it acknowledged this limitation more directly. Citing theoretical support for this assumption, such as the convergence of the expectation of C_t (e.g., Beyer, ECJ 2014), would lend more confidence to this central claim.

There are also a few minor issues to address. The σ in the denominator of Eq. (1) and footnote 2 should be \tilde{σ}. There is also a small typo in line 94 ("which can by be motivated...").

In summary, the paper presents a valuable contribution for practitioners. While the core ideas build heavily on prior work, the resulting algorithm is a complete and useful tool that could become the new standard implementation for medium-to-large scale problems. The trade-off is a significant, well-demonstrated practical speed-up for a modest theoretical novelty.
