### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a prominent derivative-free optimization algorithm. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a time complexity dominated by the O(d^3) cost of decomposing the d x d covariance matrix `C` in each iteration to obtain its square root, `C^{1/2}`. To alleviate this, the authors propose the "Cholesky-CMA-ES", a variant that maintains and directly updates the lower triangular Cholesky factor `A` (where `C = AA^T`) instead of the full covariance matrix. This reduces the update complexity to O(μd^2), which is asymptotically optimal.

A key challenge is that the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism in CMA-ES requires `C^{-1/2}` for normalizing the evolution path. The authors propose approximating this term with `A^{-1}`, the inverse of the Cholesky factor. They provide a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the error introduced by this approximation—a random rotation—diminishes. Empirical results on several benchmark functions show that the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves significant wall-clock time speedups over standard implementations without degrading performance in terms of the number of function evaluations required for convergence.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound in its overall motivation and the implementation of the Cholesky update itself seems plausible. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating a clear practical advantage in runtime. However, there are significant weaknesses in the supporting arguments and validation.

*   **Missing Equivalence Proof:** The paper's update mechanism relies on decomposing the rank-1 and rank-µ updates from Equation (2) and applying them sequentially to the Cholesky factor `A` using Algorithm 2. While this is the core of the method, the paper fails to formally prove or cite a proof that this sequence of updates to `A` results in a matrix `A_{t+1}` such that `A_{t+1}A_{t+1}^T` is precisely equal to the `C_{t+1}` defined in Equation (2). This proof is essential for the claim that the algorithm is merely an approximation in the CSA path and not in the covariance matrix update itself.
*   **Unvalidated Central Claim:** The main theoretical contribution is the justification for replacing `C^{-1/2}` with `A^{-1}`. The authors argue that the error introduced by this is a rotation that vanishes as the covariance matrix converges. However, this claim is not experimentally validated. The experiments only compare final performance. To substantiate this claim, the authors should provide an empirical analysis showing how this "path error" (e.g., the angle between `C_t^{-1/2}v` and `A_t^{-1}v`) behaves over the course of an optimization run, especially in early stages or on problems where the covariance matrix does not converge quickly (like the Rosenbrock function). The assertion that this error decreases is crucial and requires direct evidence.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem and proposed solution are presented clearly. However, some aspects could be improved.

*   **Complexity Analysis:** The paper claims an `O(µd^2)` complexity, but the reference CMA-ES implementation (CMA-ES-Ref) with delayed updates also has an amortized complexity of `O(d^2)`. The authors should use more precise notation (e.g., Θ-notation) and provide a more detailed comparison. The key advantage is that the Cholesky method has a consistent Θ(µd^2) cost per iteration, whereas the standard method has a high-cost O(d^3) operation performed intermittently. This distinction should be made more explicit.
*   **Minor Typos:** There are several small errors that detract from the paper's polish:
    *   In Algorithm 2, `wk` should likely be `α_k`.
    *   In Table 1 (presumably in the supplementary material, as it's not in the main text), `n` is used for dimensionality, whereas `d` is used throughout the paper.
    *   In the text (around line 185), `f(x1,t)` is referenced. This appears to be a typo for `f(x_{1,t})`.

**Originality:**

The originality of this work is a major concern. The core idea of performing rank-one updates directly on a Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix is not new.

*   The proposed method appears to be a straightforward extension of the work by Krause and Igel (2015), which introduced an efficient rank-one update for the (1+1)-CMA-ES. The current paper extends this to the (µ, λ)-CMA-ES, but the conceptual leap seems minimal. The contribution primarily lies in applying this known technique to the standard CMA-ES and analyzing the necessary approximation for the CSA path. Given the existence of this prior work, the novelty of the proposed technique is limited.

**Significance:**

Despite the concerns about originality, the practical significance of this work is potentially high. CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm in derivative-free optimization and machine learning applications like policy search. A faster, more memory-efficient implementation that does not sacrifice performance would be a valuable contribution to the community and could easily become the new standard implementation, as the authors suggest. The demonstrated speedups are substantial and would enable the application of CMA-ES to higher-dimensional problems where it was previously computationally infeasible.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for:**
*   The paper tackles a well-defined and important practical problem: the high computational cost of the standard CMA-ES.
*   The empirical results show a significant and impressive speedup in wall-clock time, making the algorithm much more practical for higher-dimensional problems.
*   The writing is clear, and the method is presented in a way that is easy to understand and implement.

**Arguments against:**
*   **Limited Originality:** The core technical idea seems to be a minor extension of previously published work (Krause and Igel, 2015), raising questions about its suitability for a top-tier conference like NIPS.
*   **Insufficient Validation:** The central theoretical justification for the approximation in the CSA path is not backed by direct experimental evidence. This is a critical flaw in the paper's argument.
*   **Missing Technical Details:** The paper lacks a formal proof of equivalence between its Cholesky update procedure and the standard covariance update rule, which is necessary for rigor.

### Conclusion

While the paper presents a method with clear practical benefits and impressive speedups for the widely used CMA-ES algorithm, it suffers from major weaknesses in terms of originality and the validation of its core claims. The contribution appears to be an incremental extension of existing work, and the theoretical argument for the key approximation is not sufficiently supported by empirical evidence. Due to these significant issues, the paper is not acceptable for NIPS in its current form. The work would be much stronger if the authors could provide direct experimental validation for their claims about the CSA path error and more clearly delineate their novel contributions over prior art.
