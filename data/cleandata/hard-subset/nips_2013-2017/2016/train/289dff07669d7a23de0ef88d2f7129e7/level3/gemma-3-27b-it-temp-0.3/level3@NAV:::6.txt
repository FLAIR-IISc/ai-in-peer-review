## Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) focusing on a more efficient covariance matrix update using a Cholesky factorization. The core idea is to avoid explicit computation of the matrix square root, instead directly updating the Cholesky factor, aiming for reduced runtime and memory usage, particularly in high-dimensional spaces. The authors claim this approach maintains the performance of the standard CMA-ES while offering computational benefits.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation behind the proposed method, building upon previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause et al. (2015). The theoretical justification, leveraging Lemma 1 regarding the convergence of the Cholesky factor, is a strength. The experimental setup is reasonably comprehensive, utilizing standard benchmark functions and varying dimensionality. 

However, several critical weaknesses significantly detract from the contribution. First, the naming "Cholesky-CMA-ES" is misleading and directly conflicts with the established nomenclature from Suttorp et al. (2009). This *must* be addressed. Second, the paper lacks a direct empirical comparison with Suttorp (2009) and Krause (2015). While the authors mention these works, demonstrating performance relative to them is crucial. The claim of maintaining performance is insufficient without this comparison. The algorithmic novelty also appears incremental; the core change seems to be simply looping the `rankOneUpdate` for all individuals (as highlighted in Algorithm 1), which feels like a minor implementation detail rather than a substantial algorithmic contribution. 

Furthermore, the experiments, while extensive, could be improved. The claim of "optimality" is overstated; the method isn't optimal for all functions (e.g., Sphere, separable functions). The reported ~2x speedup over Suttorp’s method, while positive, doesn’t represent a revolutionary advance. The assertion that Suttorp’s method is numerically unstable is questionable and contradicts the original paper’s claims. While the quadratic complexity remains unchanged, the paper doesn’t adequately emphasize this limitation regarding scalability for truly large-scale problems. Finally, the plotting of results on the Cigar function warrants further clarification, as the plateau observed might be an artifact of the median plotting method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Clear presentation of the method and motivation.
*   Theoretical justification provided.
*   Demonstrated speedup in runtime.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Misleading naming convention.
*   Missing comparison with key prior work (Suttorp, Krause).
*   Incremental algorithmic contribution.
*   Overstated claims of optimality and numerical stability.
*   Limited discussion of scalability.



**Recommendation:** Reject with major revisions. The authors need to address the naming issue, incorporate a thorough comparison with Suttorp (2009) and Krause (2015), clarify the algorithmic contribution, and temper their claims regarding optimality and numerical stability. Without these revisions, the paper’s contribution to the field is limited.
