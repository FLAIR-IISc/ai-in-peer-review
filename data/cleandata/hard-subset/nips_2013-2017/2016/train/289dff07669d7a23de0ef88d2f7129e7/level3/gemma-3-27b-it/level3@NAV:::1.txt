## Review of "CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity"

This paper presents a compelling modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) focused on improving the computational complexity of covariance matrix updates. The authors propose updating the covariance via triangular Cholesky factors, achieving quadratic time complexity and reduced memory requirements, a significant improvement over the standard CMA-ES’s more expensive eigenvalue decomposition. This builds upon previous work attempting Cholesky-based updates [Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015] by providing a theoretical justification for maintaining performance and adapting it to the full CMA-ES framework.

The core strength of this work lies in its clear mathematical grounding and practical implications. The theoretical justification for the proposed modification, leveraging Lemma 1 and addressing potential coordinate rotation issues, is well-articulated. The empirical results demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES maintains comparable performance (in terms of function evaluation counts) to standard CMA-ES while achieving substantial wall-clock speedups, particularly as dimensionality increases. This is a significant advantage for large-scale problems, relevant to machine learning, reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization as highlighted by the authors. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and appropriately cites relevant prior work, including connections to information geometry [Akimoto et al., 2012].

However, a minor point requires clarification. The paper mentions the implementations are within the Shark library, but doesn’t explicitly state whether all implementations (CMA-ES-Ref, Suttorp-CMA-ES, and Cholesky-CMA-ES) utilize the same programming language and compiler optimizations to ensure a fair CPU/time comparison. Detailing this aspect would bolster the credibility of the speedup claims. Additionally, while Figure 2 demonstrates clear speedups in higher dimensions, the differences for the Cigar and Discus functions at dimension 256 appear less pronounced. Expanding on why these functions don’t exhibit the same magnitude of improvement as others would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Significant reduction in computational complexity with no performance degradation.
*   Strong theoretical foundation and justification for the proposed method.
*   Clear practical advantages demonstrated through comprehensive experiments.
*   Well-written and thoroughly evaluated, relevant to a broad range of NIPS audiences.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Minor ambiguity regarding implementation details affecting timing comparisons.
*   Limited explanation for muted performance differences on certain functions at higher dimensions. 

**Overall:** This paper represents a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization and is a strong candidate to become a default implementation for medium to large-scale CMA-ES applications. The benefits are clear, the work is sound, and the potential impact is substantial. I recommend acceptance.
