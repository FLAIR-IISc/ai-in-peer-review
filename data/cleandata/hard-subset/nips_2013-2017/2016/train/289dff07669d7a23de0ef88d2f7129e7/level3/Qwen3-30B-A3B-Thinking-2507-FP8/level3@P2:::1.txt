

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the covariance matrix adaptation evolution strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the matrix square root computation in the step-size adaptation with a triangular Cholesky factor update. The authors demonstrate that this modification achieves asymptotically optimal O(μd²) time and O(d²) space complexity for covariance updates while preserving the algorithm's performance in terms of objective function evaluations. The approach builds on prior work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause & Igel (2015) but provides a novel theoretical justification for why replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ (where A is the Cholesky factor) does not degrade performance, leveraging convergence properties of the Cholesky decomposition. Empirical results on standard benchmark functions show identical function-evaluation counts compared to the standard CMA-ES (with delayed decomposition), but significant wall-clock speedups (up to 20× at d=64), with the speedup increasing with dimensionality.

**Quality**  
The paper is technically sound. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis (Lemma 1) showing that the error introduced by replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ vanishes as the algorithm converges, supported by empirical validation. The experimental design is thorough: 100 trials per function/dimension, rotation invariance tests, and comparisons against relevant baselines (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES). The runtime analysis correctly identifies the O(d³) bottleneck in standard CMA-ES and demonstrates how Cholesky-CMA-ES resolves it without compromising optimization quality. The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., quadratic scaling still becomes prohibitive for very large d, where low-rank approximations may be needed).

**Clarity**  
The paper is exceptionally clear. The background (Section 2) concisely establishes the CMA-ES framework and the computational bottleneck. The proposed method (Section 3) is well-motivated, with Algorithm 1 providing a precise implementation guide. The theoretical justification is accessible, and the empirical results (Section 4) are presented with appropriate figures and statistical rigor. The writing is precise and free of unnecessary jargon, making it accessible to both optimization and ML audiences.

**Originality**  
While prior work (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015) explored Cholesky-based CMA-ES variants, this paper makes a significant original contribution by: (1) providing the first theoretical guarantee that the step-size adaptation modification does not degrade performance (via Lemma 1), (2) extending the approach to the full standard CMA-ES (not just special cases like (1+1)-CMA-ES), and (3) demonstrating the practical impact on runtime. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work in the introduction and related work, and the NIPS 2016 proceedings show no prior work addressing this specific theoretical gap for standard CMA-ES.

**Significance**  
This work addresses a critical bottleneck in one of the most widely used derivative-free optimization algorithms. For practitioners using CMA-ES in ML (e.g., hyperparameter tuning, policy search), the speedup without loss in function evaluations is highly valuable, especially as problem dimensions grow. The paper explicitly connects to applications in reinforcement learning (e.g., Gomez et al., 2008) and supervised learning (e.g., Igel, 2010), demonstrating relevance to core NIPS topics. The speedup scales with dimensionality, making it particularly impactful for large-scale problems where standard CMA-ES becomes infeasible. The eigenvalues provided "for free" also offer new diagnostic capabilities for optimization problems.

**Strengths**  
- Theoretical justification (Lemma 1) resolves a key uncertainty in prior Cholesky-based approaches.  
- Empirical results conclusively show no degradation in optimization quality with substantial runtime gains.  
- Practical implementation details (e.g., storing Cholesky factors for eigenvalue access) add immediate utility.  
- Well-written, well-structured, and directly addresses a high-impact problem in optimization.

**Weaknesses**  
1. *Implementation details for fair timing comparisons:* The paper compares wall-clock times but does not specify whether all variants (CMA-ES-Ref, Suttorp-CMA-ES, Cholesky-CMA-ES) were implemented in the same language/library (e.g., Shark) and with identical numerical optimizations. A brief note on this would strengthen the runtime claims.  
2. *Figure 2 anomaly at d=256:* The slowdown for Cigar/Discus at d=256 is less pronounced than for other functions (e.g., Sphere, Ellipsoid). The authors attribute this to L2 cache effects in the supplementary material, but this should be explicitly stated in the main text to avoid confusion.

**Recommendation**  
This paper presents a significant, well-executed improvement to a foundational optimization algorithm with clear practical benefits for the ML community. The theoretical contribution is novel and robust, the empirical validation is comprehensive, and the writing is exemplary. The minor weaknesses (implementation details, Figure 2 clarification) are easily addressable and do not detract from the core contribution. The work directly advances the state of the art in derivative-free optimization and provides a compelling case for adopting Cholesky-CMA-ES as the default implementation for medium/large-scale problems where covariance updates are a bottleneck. **Strongly recommend acceptance.**
