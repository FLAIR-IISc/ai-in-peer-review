This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) to improve its computational and storage complexity. The standard CMA-ES has a complexity dominated by the O(d³) cost of decomposing the covariance matrix C in each iteration. The authors propose the "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which avoids storing and decomposing the full covariance matrix by directly maintaining and updating its lower triangular Cholesky factor, A. The update procedure extends the rank-1 update for Cholesky factors from Krause & Igel (2015) to the full rank-µ update of the standard CMA-ES by applying a sequence of rank-1 updates.

A key challenge is that the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism in CMA-ES requires the inverse of the matrix square root (C⁻¹/²), which is not equivalent to the inverse of a Cholesky factor (A⁻¹). The authors' main contribution is to propose replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ and to provide a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) that this approximation is benign. They argue that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges, the rotation matrix relating the square root to the Cholesky factor also converges to the identity, meaning the error introduced by the approximation vanishes over time. The paper presents empirical results on several benchmark functions, showing that the proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES requires a similar number of function evaluations as the standard algorithm but is significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments for Acceptance (Strengths):**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known and critical bottleneck in one of the most powerful derivative-free optimization algorithms. Reducing the update complexity from O(d³) to O(µd²) makes CMA-ES applicable to higher-dimensional problems where the function evaluation is cheap, which is a relevant scenario in many machine learning applications (e.g., policy search with simple simulators).

2.  **Quality & Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithm, Cholesky-CMA-ES, achieves asymptotically optimal time complexity (O(µd²)) for the covariance update and optimal storage complexity (O(d²)). The use of a triangular Cholesky factor is numerically stable and provides additional benefits, such as efficient inversion and access to eigenvalues (via the diagonal elements) for monitoring problem conditioning.

3.  **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a novel theoretical argument in Lemma 1 to justify the crucial approximation made in the CSA update. While based on an assumption of convergence, this analysis provides valuable insight into why replacing C⁻¹/² with A⁻¹ does not seem to harm the algorithm's performance, which is a non-trivial finding.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are strong and clearly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method. The wall-clock time speedup over the reference implementation is substantial and grows with dimensionality, as predicted by the complexity analysis. Crucially, this speedup is achieved without a significant increase in the number of required function evaluations, confirming that the CSA approximation does not degrade optimization performance on the tested benchmarks.

**Arguments for Rejection (Weaknesses):**

1.  **Originality:** The primary weakness of the paper is its limited novelty. The core idea of updating a Cholesky factor to achieve a quadratic-time update has been explored before. In particular, Krause & Igel (2015) showed how to perform a rank-1 update on the Cholesky factor. The rank-µ update in CMA-ES is a weighted sum of rank-1 updates. Therefore, extending the technique from a single rank-1 update to the full rank-µ update is a relatively straightforward generalization. The main novel contribution is thus confined to the analysis of the CSA approximation.

2.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical justification provided in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix C̄t converges. While this is a plausible assumption and supported by empirical evidence and other theoretical work, the paper does not sufficiently justify it. Citing existing literature that analyzes the convergence of the covariance matrix in expectation (e.g., Beyer, ECJ 2014) would significantly strengthen the confidence in this assumption and, by extension, the entire theoretical argument. As it stands, the argument feels more like a plausibility argument than a rigorous proof.

### Detailed Comments

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The proposed algorithm is a valuable contribution to the practical implementation of CMA-ES. The following are minor points for improvement:

*   The confidence in Lemma 1 could be strengthened by citing theoretical work on the convergence properties of the CMA-ES covariance matrix. For instance, the work by Beyer ("Convergence analysis of evolutionary algorithms that are based on the paradigm of information geometry", 2014) discusses the convergence of the expectation of Ct, which is highly relevant to the authors' assumption.
*   There appears to be a small notation error in Equation (1) and the corresponding footnote 1. The step-size σt in the denominator should likely be the one used for sampling the points xi,t, but the text does not make this fully clear, and standard CMA-ES formulations often use an effective step-size here. Please clarify.
*   A minor typo was found on page 3: "which can by be motivated..." should be "which can be motivated...".

In summary, this paper presents a practically significant and well-executed engineering improvement to a widely used algorithm. While the conceptual novelty over prior work is somewhat limited, the justification for the CSA modification and the compelling empirical results make this a solid contribution. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a clear improvement over the standard implementation and is likely to be adopted by practitioners.
