**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the primary computational bottleneck of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a prominent derivative-free optimization algorithm. The standard CMA-ES algorithm's time and space complexity is dominated by the need to update and store a dxd covariance matrix, `C`, and compute its inverse square root, `C^(-1/2)`, which typically involves an O(d^3) eigenvalue decomposition. The authors propose a new variant, the "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which reduces the update time complexity to O(µd^2) and the storage to O(d^2), both of which are asymptotically optimal for a full-rank covariance model.

The core idea is to maintain and directly update the triangular Cholesky factor `A` of the covariance matrix (`C = AA^T`) instead of the full matrix `C`. While efficient O(d^2) rank-one updates for Cholesky factors are known, a key challenge prevented their direct application to the standard CMA-ES: the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism requires the term `C^(-1/2) * step` to normalize search steps. This matrix square root is not equivalent to the Cholesky factor. The authors' main contribution is to propose replacing `C^(-1/2)` with `A^(-1)` and to provide a theoretical justification for this approximation. They argue via Lemma 1 that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the rotation matrix `E` relating the square root to the Cholesky factor (`C^(1/2) = AE`) also converges. Consequently, the rotational error introduced by the approximation diminishes over successive iterations, especially given the exponential weighting in the CSA path.

The authors validate their approach empirically on a suite of standard benchmark functions. The results demonstrate that the Cholesky-CMA-ES requires virtually the same number of function evaluations to converge as the standard CMA-ES, while being significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, with the speedup becoming more pronounced as the problem dimension `d` increases. This work builds upon previous efforts to accelerate CMA-ES, such as infrequent updates or updates on non-triangular factors (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), by providing a solution that is both efficient and robust, without degrading optimization performance.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Practical Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known, critical limitation of one of the most powerful and widely used black-box optimization algorithms. The proposed speedup (e.g., 20x for d=64) is substantial and makes CMA-ES more practical for medium-to-large-scale problems, which are common in machine learning applications like policy search and hyperparameter optimization. The claim that this variant "can and should replace the standard CMA-ES" seems well-founded for applications where computational cost is a factor.
2.  **Technical Quality and Originality:** The central contribution—the justification for replacing `C^(-1/2)` with `A^(-1)` in the CSA update—is both novel and elegant. Previous works have struggled with this exact issue. The argument presented in Lemma 1, which connects the convergence of the covariance matrix shape to the stability of the implicit rotation, provides a solid theoretical underpinning for an otherwise heuristic approximation. This insight is a clear and valuable contribution.
3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. It clearly motivates the problem, explains the technical challenge, presents the proposed solution with its theoretical justification, and provides convincing empirical evidence. The background section is concise but sufficient for understanding the core algorithm. Algorithm 1 and 2 clearly specify the implementation.
4.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The empirical study is well-designed. The authors compare their method against appropriate baselines (the reference implementation and a delayed-update variant) on a set of standard, challenging benchmark functions. The results strongly support the paper's main claims: the number of function evaluations remains unchanged, while the wall-clock time is dramatically reduced. The inclusion of Figure 2, with its log-log plot, effectively visualizes the asymptotic advantage.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / Suggestions for improvement):**

1.  **Strength of Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical argument in Lemma 1 relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix `C̄_t` converges. The authors correctly acknowledge that this is not strictly guaranteed for a stochastic algorithm, although the expectation may converge in simplified models. While the heuristic is powerful and the empirical results are convincing, the argument does not constitute a formal proof of convergence or a bound on the approximation error. This is a minor weakness, as providing such a proof would likely be a significant theoretical undertaking on its own, but it is worth noting the heuristic nature of the justification.
2.  **Clarity on Novelty of Components:** The paper's core novelty is the justification for the CSA modification. The efficient rank-one update for a Cholesky factor (Algorithm 2) is a standard, albeit non-trivial, numerical linear algebra technique. While the paper cites prior work applying this to CMA-ES variants (Krause and Igel, 2015), it could be slightly more explicit that the novelty lies in successfully and justifiably integrating this update into the *standard* CMA-ES algorithm's CSA path, not in the update routine itself.
3.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the benchmark functions are standard in the evolutionary computation literature, the paper would be even more compelling to the broader NIPS audience if it included at least one experiment on a practical machine learning task (e.g., hyperparameter tuning for a small model or direct policy search for a classic reinforcement learning problem). This would directly demonstrate the real-world utility of the achieved speedup.

**Overall Evaluation and Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a simple, clear, and highly practical contribution to the field. It solves an important, long-standing problem in a very effective way. The claims are well-supported by a combination of a neat theoretical argument and strong empirical results. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is a significant improvement over the standard algorithm and is likely to be widely adopted by practitioners.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
