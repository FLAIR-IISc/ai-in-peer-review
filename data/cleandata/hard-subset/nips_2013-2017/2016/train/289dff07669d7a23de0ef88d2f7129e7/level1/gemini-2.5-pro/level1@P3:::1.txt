This paper addresses the primary computational bottleneck of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a highly effective derivative-free optimization algorithm. The standard CMA-ES algorithm has a time complexity of O(d^3) per iteration due to the need to compute the square root of the d-dimensional covariance matrix C, typically via an eigenvalue decomposition. The authors propose a new variant, the "Cholesky-CMA-ES," which reduces this complexity to O(µd^2) by directly updating a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix (C = AA^T). This complexity is asymptotically optimal for maintaining a full covariance matrix.

The main technical challenge in this approach lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which requires the term C^(-1/2) for normalization. Previous attempts to use Cholesky updates, such as Suttorp et al. (2009), used a non-triangular factorization, which is not unique and can introduce arbitrary rotations. The key contribution of this work is a principled approximation for the CSA update: replacing the computationally expensive C^(-1/2) with the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor, A^(-1). The authors provide a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) that this approximation is sound. They argue that as the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, the rotation matrix E in the RQ-decomposition C^(1/2) = AE also converges, meaning the error introduced by omitting this rotation becomes negligible over time. The paper supports these claims with strong empirical evidence on standard benchmark functions, showing that the Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard implementation while being significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, particularly for high dimensions.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a well-known and important limitation of what is arguably the state-of-the-art algorithm for non-convex, derivative-free black-box optimization. The proposed solution is elegant, practical, and provides a substantial speedup. Given the wide use of CMA-ES in machine learning for tasks like hyperparameter tuning and direct policy search in reinforcement learning, this contribution is highly likely to be adopted by the community and become the new standard implementation for problems where a full covariance matrix is feasible.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The core idea of replacing C^(-1/2) with A^(-1) in the CSA update is novel and well-motivated. While direct updates on matrix factorizations have been explored before (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause & Igel, 2015), this work provides the crucial missing piece for efficiently and stably adapting the standard (µ, λ)-CMA-ES. The theoretical argument in Lemma 1, which connects the convergence of the normalized covariance matrix to the stability of the rotation matrices, provides a solid foundation for the approximation, distinguishing it from prior ad-hoc attempts. The choice of a *triangular* Cholesky factor is also technically sound, as it guarantees a unique decomposition, offers optimal O(d^2) storage, and allows for efficient O(d^2) inversion.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The authors effectively explain the problem, situate their work within the existing literature, and present their method and justification logically. The inclusion of pseudo-code in Algorithms 1 and 2 provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the work.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental comparison is thorough and convincing. By benchmarking against the standard reference implementation (CMA-ES-Ref) and a delayed-update version (CMA-ES/d), the authors clearly demonstrate the superiority of their approach. The results in Figures 1 and 2 compellingly show that the Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same convergence profile (in terms of function evaluations) while dramatically reducing wall-clock time, confirming the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**

1.  **Strength of Theoretical Justification:** The argument in Lemma 1 hinges on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix C̄t converges. The authors correctly note that the *expectation* of Ct converges in some settings, but this is not guaranteed for the stochastic matrix C_t itself in a single run, particularly on rugged or multi-modal landscapes where the algorithm might explore different search directions. The argument is more of a strong heuristic justification than a formal proof of equivalence. To strengthen this point, the authors could provide an empirical analysis of the error term, for example by plotting the Frobenius norm of (E_t * E_{t-1}^T - I) over iterations for a few benchmark runs. This would provide direct evidence for their claim that the rotational error diminishes as the search progresses.

2.  **Comparison with Suttorp-CMA-ES:** The paper argues theoretically against the non-triangular update of Suttorp et al. (2009) due to the non-uniqueness of the factorization. However, the empirical results for Suttorp-CMA-ES in the supplement do not show a clear performance degradation in terms of function evaluations on the tested benchmarks. To make the case against this competing method more compelling, it would be beneficial to either discuss its potential failure modes more concretely or demonstrate them on a specific problem where the arbitrary rotations might lead to instability or slower convergence.

3.  **Minor Factual Inaccuracy:** At the end of Section 3, the paper states that "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix". This is incorrect. This property holds only for diagonal matrices. While the condition number of the covariance matrix can still be estimated from its Cholesky factor, this specific claim should be corrected to maintain technical accuracy.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a major computational bottleneck in a foundational optimization algorithm.
*   The proposed solution is practical, efficient (asymptotically optimal complexity), and numerically stable.
*   The key approximation is well-justified by a novel theoretical argument.
*   Empirical results are strong and convincingly demonstrate a significant speedup without sacrificing optimization performance.
*   The work is likely to have a high impact and be widely adopted.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical justification relies on an assumption of convergence that may not hold in all cases.
*   The empirical advantage over the theoretically-weaker Suttorp et al. (2009) method is not clearly demonstrated.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant and practical contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES solves a long-standing efficiency problem in a clean and well-justified manner. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength and importance of the work. This paper is a clear candidate for acceptance and is likely to become a highly cited and influential piece of work.
