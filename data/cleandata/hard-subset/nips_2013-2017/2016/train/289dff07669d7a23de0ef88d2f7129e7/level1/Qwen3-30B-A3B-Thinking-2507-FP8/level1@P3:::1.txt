

# Peer Review

This paper introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the matrix square root computation with a triangular Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors present a theoretically justified approach that maintains the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES while achieving optimal O(d²) time complexity for covariance matrix updates and optimal storage requirements.

The paper builds upon previous work on Cholesky factorization in CMA-ES variants (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015), but makes a significant contribution by providing a theoretical justification for why their approach doesn't degrade performance. Previous attempts to use Cholesky factors directly in the standard CMA-ES were problematic because the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism requires the matrix square root, not the Cholesky factor. The authors resolve this by replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ (where Aₜ is the Cholesky factor) and provide Lemma 1 showing that the introduced error vanishes as the algorithm converges.

The authors' empirical evaluation is comprehensive, testing on multiple benchmark functions across dimensions from d=4 to d=256. They compare against three variants: the standard CMA-ES with delayed matrix square root computation (CMA-ES-Ref), a CMA-ES with delayed updates every d steps (CMA-ES/d), and Suttorp-CMA-ES (the non-triangular Cholesky approach). The results demonstrate that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as CMA-ES-Ref but achieves significant speedups (up to 20x for d=64), with the speedup increasing with dimensionality.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical contribution:** The paper provides a theoretically sound approach to a well-known computational bottleneck in CMA-ES. The authors successfully address a key limitation in previous work by providing a justification for why their modification doesn't degrade performance.

2. **Clarity and organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2), precise mathematical notation, and well-organized presentation of results.

3. **Empirical validation:** The experimental design is thorough, covering multiple benchmark functions, dimensions, and rotation invariance tests. The results consistently show the expected performance benefits.

4. **Practical significance:** For problems where maintaining a full covariance matrix is computationally feasible but expensive (e.g., training neural networks via direct policy search), this approach offers a substantial practical advantage over the standard CMA-ES.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited comparison with alternative complexity-reduction methods:** While the paper mentions low-rank approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015), it doesn't provide a direct comparison between Cholesky-CMA-ES and these methods. For large d where maintaining the full covariance matrix becomes infeasible, it's unclear how Cholesky-CMA-ES compares to these low-rank approaches in terms of both speed and solution quality.

2. **Theoretical justification limitations:** Lemma 1 assumes Ct converges to a certain shape, but CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm that doesn't necessarily converge in the deterministic sense. The connection between the theoretical guarantee and empirical results could be strengthened by analyzing how the error accumulates during optimization.

3. **Memory access considerations:** The paper notes that for d=256, the matrix size exceeded L2 cache, affecting performance. This suggests that beyond a certain dimensionality, cache effects may dominate the computational advantage, which isn't fully addressed in the discussion.

4. **Minor technical inaccuracy:** The claim that "the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues" is not accurate - the Cholesky factorization relates to the eigenvalues through a more complex relationship.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper makes a significant technical contribution to a widely used optimization algorithm
- The theoretical justification is sound and addresses a key limitation in previous work
- The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate clear practical benefits
- The approach is directly applicable to many real-world machine learning problems

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The paper doesn't sufficiently compare with alternative complexity-reduction methods for large-scale CMA-ES
- The theoretical justification has limitations in the context of randomized optimization
- The paper doesn't fully explore the practical limitations of the approach for very large dimensions (beyond cache effects)

This paper represents a valuable contribution to the optimization literature with clear practical implications for machine learning practitioners. The authors have addressed a genuine bottleneck in the CMA-ES algorithm with a theoretically grounded approach that maintains the algorithm's performance characteristics while significantly improving computational efficiency. While the paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with alternative complexity-reduction methods and a deeper exploration of the limitations at very large dimensions, the core contribution is strong and well-supported.

The Cholesky-CMA-ES should be considered for acceptance at NIPS 2016 as it meets all the key criteria for a high-quality scientific contribution: it's technically sound, clearly presented, novel in its specific approach to the problem, and significant for practitioners working with derivative-free optimization in machine learning. The authors have made a clear case for why this approach should replace the standard CMA-ES when computational efficiency matters.
