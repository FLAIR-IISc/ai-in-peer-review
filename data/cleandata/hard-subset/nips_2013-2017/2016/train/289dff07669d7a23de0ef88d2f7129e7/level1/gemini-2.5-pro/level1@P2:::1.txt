**Summary**

This paper addresses a well-known computational bottleneck in the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES), a powerful and widely-used algorithm for derivative-free optimization. The standard CMA-ES requires `O(d^3)` computation per iteration due to the need to compute the square root of the `d x d` covariance matrix `C`. This limits its applicability to high-dimensional problems.

The authors propose a new variant, Cholesky-CMA-ES, that reduces the time complexity of the covariance matrix update to `O(d^2)`, which is asymptotically optimal for a full matrix update. The core idea is to maintain and directly update the triangular Cholesky factor `A` (where `C = AA^T`), instead of the full matrix `C`. While similar ideas have been explored before (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), they were not compatible with the standard CMA-ES due to the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism, which relies on the symmetric matrix square root `C^{-1/2}`. Simply replacing `C^{-1/2}` with the triangular inverse `A^{-1}` introduces an error term corresponding to a rotation matrix.

The key contribution of this paper is to show that this replacement is a valid and effective approximation. The authors provide a theoretical argument (Lemma 1) that as the shape of the covariance matrix converges (a common behavior for CMA-ES), the error introduced by this approximation vanishes. They then present the Cholesky-CMA-ES algorithm, which uses efficient rank-one updates on the Cholesky factor. Extensive experiments on benchmark functions demonstrate that the proposed method achieves virtually identical performance to the standard CMA-ES in terms of function evaluations, while being significantly faster in wall-clock time, with the speed-up becoming more dramatic as dimensionality increases.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithm is clearly described and appears to be sound. The core claims—that the Cholesky-based update is much faster and that the necessary approximation in the CSA step does not harm performance—are well-supported by both theoretical reasoning and strong empirical evidence.

The theoretical justification provided in Lemma 1 is insightful. While it relies on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges, which is a reasonable heuristic but not a formal proof for the full algorithm, it provides a strong intuition for why the approximation works. It correctly identifies that the error is rotational and that this rotation stabilizes as the algorithm converges.

The experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors compare their method against the standard reference implementation and another common variant on a suite of well-established benchmark problems. The results are compelling and unambiguous: the number of function evaluations remains the same, while the runtime is reduced by orders of magnitude in higher dimensions (e.g., 20x faster for d=64). The authors are careful to present the results clearly, using appropriate plots that highlight their claims effectively. This is a complete piece of work, not a preliminary study.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The background section provides just enough detail on CMA-ES for an informed reader to understand the contribution. The core technical section (Section 3) is the highlight, explaining with great clarity the subtle but crucial difference between the matrix square root and a Cholesky factor in the context of CSA, and then lucidly presenting the justification for their approximation. The algorithmic descriptions are precise, and the figures are clean and easy to interpret. The paper provides enough information for an expert to reproduce the results.

**Originality**

The work is highly original. While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor for CMA-ES is not entirely new, this paper is the first to successfully integrate this efficient update scheme into the modern, standard version of CMA-ES. Previous works either applied it to simplified variants of the algorithm or used non-triangular factorizations that lack the uniqueness and efficiency benefits of the authors' approach. The key innovation is the identification of the `C^{-1/2}` vs. `A^{-1}` problem and the novel, theoretically-motivated approximation that resolves it. The paper clearly distinguishes its contribution from prior work by Suttorp et al. and Krause & Igel.

**Significance**

The significance of this work is very high. CMA-ES is a benchmark algorithm in evolutionary computation and is frequently used for challenging optimization problems in machine learning, such as policy search in reinforcement learning and hyperparameter optimization. Its cubic complexity has been a major barrier to its use in problems with more than a few hundred variables. This paper effectively removes that barrier for a significant range of dimensions, pushing the practical limit of where full covariance matrix adaptation is feasible.

The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is not just a marginal improvement; it represents a fundamental enhancement to the implementation of a foundational algorithm. Given the dramatic speed-up and lack of performance degradation, it is highly likely that this method will be adopted as the new standard implementation of CMA-ES. Researchers and practitioners will be able to solve larger-scale problems than before, or solve existing problems much faster. Therefore, the ideas in this paper are very likely to be used and built upon.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Solves a long-standing and important efficiency problem in a widely used optimization algorithm.
*   The proposed solution is elegant, efficient (`O(d^2)`), and asymptotically optimal in both time and memory.
*   The core approximation is justified with a sound theoretical argument.
*   Empirical results are comprehensive and convincingly demonstrate a massive speed-up with no loss of optimization performance.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and clear.
*   The work has high practical significance and is likely to become the new standard for CMA-ES implementations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical argument for the approximation relies on an assumption of convergence which is not formally proven for the full algorithm, making it more of a well-founded heuristic than a rigorous proof. However, this is a very minor weakness given the overwhelming empirical support.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant and practical contribution to the field. It is a model of what a NIPS paper should be: it addresses an important problem, proposes an original and well-motivated solution, and validates it with strong empirical evidence. I strongly recommend acceptance.
