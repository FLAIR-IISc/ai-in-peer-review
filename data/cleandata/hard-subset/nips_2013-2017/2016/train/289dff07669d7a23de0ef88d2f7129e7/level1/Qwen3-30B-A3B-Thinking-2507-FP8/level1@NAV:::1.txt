

This paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factor approach to achieve optimal time and storage complexity. The authors build upon previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), who explored Cholesky factor updates for specialized CMA-ES variants, but provide the first theoretical justification for why this approach preserves algorithmic performance in the standard CMA-ES. The key insight is that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor diminishes as the algorithm converges, as formally shown in Lemma 1.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a clear theoretical foundation and thorough empirical validation across multiple benchmark functions (Sphere, Cigar, Rosenbrock, etc.) with varying dimensions (4-256). The results show that Cholesky-CMA-ES achieves the same number of function evaluations as standard CMA-ES while significantly reducing wall-clock time (up to 20x faster for d=64), with the speedup increasing with dimensionality.

Strengths include the elegant theoretical justification, clear presentation of algorithms, and comprehensive experimental evaluation. The approach provides optimal O(µd²) time complexity for covariance updates (compared to O(d³) for standard CMA-ES) while maintaining the same memory complexity. The paper also highlights practical advantages, such as obtaining eigenvalues of the covariance matrix for free and enabling numerically stable matrix inversion.

Potential weaknesses include limited testing on real-world machine learning problems beyond standard benchmarks, and the paper could better address potential numerical stability concerns with the Cholesky approach compared to the standard matrix square root. Additionally, the claim that "this modification does not change the behavior of the CMA-ES in terms of required objective function evaluations" could be strengthened with more detailed analysis of convergence properties.

The work is highly significant for scaling CMA-ES to larger optimization problems where covariance matrix updates become computationally prohibitive. It addresses a practical bottleneck in a widely used algorithm with clear theoretical and empirical validation.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, clear empirical validation, significant practical impact for scaling CMA-ES.
Con: Limited real-world application testing, could better address numerical stability concerns.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in derivative-free optimization with clear practical benefits, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
