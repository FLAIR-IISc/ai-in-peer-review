- Problem: Standard CMA-ES has O(d^3) per-iteration cost due to eigen-decomposition; paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES to reduce this to O(µ d^2).
- Technical challenge: CSA requires C^(-1/2), and prior non-triangular factorizations introduce non-unique rotations causing potential instability.
- Key contribution: Replace C^(-1/2) with A^(-1) where C = AA^T (triangular Cholesky factor), enabling direct O(d^2) updates.
- Theoretical justification: Lemma 1 argues that as the normalized covariance converges, the rotation matrix in the RQ-decomposition becomes stable, making the A^(-1) approximation sound.
- Practical claim: Asymptotically optimal complexity and O(d^2) storage while maintaining full covariance adaptation.
- Empirical results: On standard benchmarks, Cholesky-CMA-ES matches CMA-ES in function evaluations but greatly reduces wall-clock time, especially in high dimensions.
- Strength — significance/impact: Addresses a major bottleneck in a widely used optimizer; likely to be adopted and become a new standard for full-covariance settings.
- Strength — originality/technical quality: Novel, well-motivated A^(-1) replacement with solid theoretical grounding; triangular factor ensures uniqueness and efficient inversion.
- Strength — clarity/reproducibility: Paper is well-written with clear exposition and pseudo-code enabling reproduction.
- Strength — empirical validation: Thorough comparisons (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d) convincingly demonstrate speedups without loss of optimization performance.
- Weakness — theoretical reliance: Lemma 1 depends on convergence of the normalized covariance matrix, which may not hold in single stochastic runs or rugged/multimodal landscapes.
- Weakness — comparison to prior work: Empirical advantage over Suttorp et al. (2009) is not convincingly demonstrated; failure modes of non-triangular updates are not shown.
- Weakness — factual error: Incorrect claim that diagonal of triangular Cholesky equals square roots of eigenvalues; true only for diagonal matrices and should be corrected.
- Arguments for acceptance: Practical, asymptotically optimal, numerically stable solution to a key bottleneck with strong theoretical and empirical support.
- Arguments against acceptance: Theoretical justification may rest on a questionable convergence assumption and the empirical superiority over Suttorp-style updates is not fully established.
- Conclusion: Overall excellent and impactful work with minor correctable weaknesses; strong candidate for acceptance.
