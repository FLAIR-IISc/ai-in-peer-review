This paper presents Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) that achieves optimal O(µd²) time and O(d²) space complexity for the covariance matrix update. The core idea is to maintain and directly update a triangular Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix, avoiding the standard algorithm's costly O(d³) eigenvalue decomposition. The main technical challenge, adapting the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism which requires the inverse matrix square root, is addressed by using the inverse of the triangular Cholesky factor as a principled approximation. This work builds upon previous efforts to reduce CMA-ES complexity, such as delayed updates (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001) or updating non-triangular factors (Suttorp et al., 2009), but offers a novel, theoretically-justified approach that maintains performance.

The paper's strengths are numerous. **Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and the algorithm is presented with excellent clarity. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and extensive empirical validation. The central theoretical contribution, Lemma 1, provides a solid justification for why the CSA approximation works: as the covariance matrix shape converges, the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor vanishes. **Originality:** While updating Cholesky factors is not a new concept, its successful application to the standard CMA-ES with a formal argument for its validity is a novel and important contribution. **Significance:** The impact of this work is potentially very high. CMA-ES is a cornerstone algorithm for derivative-free optimization in machine learning, and its cubic complexity has been a major barrier to scaling. The empirical results demonstrate a dramatic wall-clock speed-up (e.g., 20x for d=64) with no degradation in the number of function evaluations, making CMA-ES viable for significantly higher-dimensional problems.

A minor weakness is that the theoretical justification relies on the convergence of the normalized covariance matrix, which is a strong assumption for a randomized algorithm. However, the authors correctly argue that slow changes in the matrix—a practical reality in later stages of optimization—are sufficient for the approximation to hold, and the empirical results strongly support this.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical, long-standing computational bottleneck in a widely used and important algorithm.
*   The proposed solution is elegant, efficient, and numerically stable.
*   The work is supported by both a sound theoretical argument and compelling empirical evidence.
*   The contribution is highly likely to be adopted by practitioners and become the standard implementation of CMA-ES.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The theoretical guarantees hinge on a convergence assumption that may not be formally met in all scenarios.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor limitations. It is a clear, well-executed, and significant contribution to the field of optimization.
