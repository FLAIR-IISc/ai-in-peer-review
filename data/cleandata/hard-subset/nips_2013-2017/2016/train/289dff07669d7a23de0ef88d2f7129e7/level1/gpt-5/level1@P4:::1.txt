Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of the standard CMA-ES that maintains and updates only a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance C = A A^T. The main computational bottleneck in CMA-ES is updating and decomposing the covariance matrix, often via an eigendecomposition, which is super-quadratic in dimension. The proposed method rewrites the standard covariance update (rank-1 plus rank-µ) as a sequence of rank-1 updates to the Cholesky factor, yielding O(µ d^2) time per iteration and O(d^2) storage. To retain the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) behavior without explicitly using C^{-1/2}, the authors replace C^{-1/2} by A^{-1}. They justify the resulting rotational discrepancy via a lemma showing that the orthogonal part of the RQ decomposition of C^{1/2} changes smoothly and converges when the (normalized) covariance converges. Empirically, the method matches the original CMA-ES in function evaluations while being substantially faster in wall-clock time, and outperforms a delayed-update baseline and a non-triangular Cholesky update method (Suttorp et al., 2009). The work extends previous Cholesky updates used in (1+1)-CMA-ES and MO-CMA-ES and generalizes the rank-one update of Krause and Igel (2015) to the standard CMA-ES with rank-µ.

Quality
- Theory: The derivation of an O(µ d^2) rank-1 update for the triangular factor is sound and aligns with prior work on fast Cholesky updates. The key approximation replaces C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} in CSA; the analysis via Lemma 1 argues that the induced rotational error vanishes asymptotically when the normalized covariance converges. This is a reasonable justification in the CMA-ES context, although it does not provide non-asymptotic bounds or guarantees under non-convergent regimes. The claim of asymptotically optimal time and storage for full covariance adaptation is well-motivated.
- A notable technical issue: the paper states that “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is incorrect in general. The diagonal of A in the Cholesky factorization C = A A^T does not yield the eigenvalues of C; only in special cases (e.g., diagonal C) would this coincide. What is available “for free” includes the log-determinant (sum of log diagonal entries) and an efficient triangular solve/inverse; eigenvalues still require additional computation.
- Empirics: The benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar/Discus/Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) with random rotations and dimensions up to 256 are appropriate. The results show equal evaluation counts to CMA-ES-Ref and substantial wall-time gains that grow with dimension. However, excluding non-convergent Rosenbrock trials biases the evaluation; success rates should be reported. It would also be useful to include statistical variability (e.g., medians with confidence intervals) and to report hardware/cache details more precisely given the cache effect at d = 256. A comparison to limited-memory CMA-ES (LM-CMA) would contextualize the trade-offs against full-covariance methods at higher dimensions.
- Implementation details (use of Shark and LAPACK) and complexity claims are clearly stated.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful algorithms and references to standard CMA-ES notation. Specific suggestions:
- Correct the statement regarding eigenvalues of C from the Cholesky diagonal.
- State the conditions ensuring uniqueness/continuity in the RQ (or QR) decomposition used in Lemma 1 (e.g., positive diagonal convention).
- Clarify the scope of the invariance claim: CSA is not strictly affine-invariant; discuss whether replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} further weakens invariance, and relate this to the empirical rotation study (summarize key quantitative findings in the main text).
- Summarize numerical stability considerations for repeated rank-1 Cholesky updates (e.g., need for occasional re-factorization or jitter) and whether safeguards were used in code.

Originality
The paper’s main novelty is integrating a fast triangular Cholesky update with the full CMA-ES (rank-µ) and modifying CSA to avoid C^{-1/2}, with a theoretical argument that the induced rotational error is negligible asymptotically. This goes beyond prior uses of Cholesky updates in restricted CMA-ES variants and the rank-one update in Krause and Igel (2015). The combination and its justification are original and relevant.

Significance
Given CMA-ES’s widespread use in ML for derivative-free optimization, reducing per-iteration time and memory while preserving evaluation efficiency is practically important. The presented speedups (particularly at moderate-to-high dimensions where full covariance is still feasible) make this contribution valuable to practitioners and likely to see adoption. The approach retains the benefits of full covariance adaptation on non-separable, ill-conditioned problems.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear algorithmic contribution with sound derivation and asymptotically optimal O(µ d^2) time and O(d^2) storage for full covariance.
- Practical and significant speedups with no observed loss in evaluation efficiency across standard benchmarks.
- Theoretical rationale (Lemma 1) supporting the CSA approximation; empirical confirmation of negligible impact.
- Clean integration into standard CMA-ES and open-source implementation details.

Cons and points to address
- Incorrect claim about obtaining eigenvalues from the Cholesky diagonal; should be corrected.
- Theoretical justification is asymptotic and assumes convergence of normalized covariance; no quantitative non-asymptotic error bounds.
- Excluding failed Rosenbrock runs biases results; report success rates and possibly time-to-target with failures counted.
- Missing comparison to strong baselines for large-scale settings (e.g., LM-CMA), and limited discussion of when full covariance becomes impractical.
- Clarify numerical stability safeguards for repeated rank-1 updates and any periodic reconditioning.

Questions for the authors
- Can you report success rates on Rosenbrock and include non-converged trials in summary statistics?
- Do you occasionally recompute a fresh Cholesky factor to mitigate numerical drift? If so, how often?
- Can you provide quantitative measurements (e.g., norms of E_t E_{t-1}^T − I) in the main text to substantiate the small rotational change, not only in the supplement?
- How does Cholesky-CMA-ES compare to LM-CMA on higher dimensions where full covariance starts to be marginally feasible?

Overall assessment
A technically solid and practically meaningful improvement to CMA-ES with convincing empirical evidence. After correcting the eigenvalue claim and clarifying empirical reporting and stability details, the paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
