Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates a triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T, avoiding explicit eigendecompositions and enabling O(µ d^2) time and O(d^2) memory per iteration. The main algorithmic change is to adapt the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) path pσ using A^{-1} rather than C^{-1/2}. Because the Cholesky factor and the symmetric square root differ by an orthogonal rotation (RQ decomposition), replacing C^{-1/2} with A^{-1} introduces a rotation that the authors argue becomes negligible as the covariance “shape” stabilizes; they formalize this with a simple lemma about the continuity of the RQ decomposition when Ct is normalized by det(Ct) and converges. They extend prior Cholesky-based updates (e.g., Suttorp et al., Krause & Igel) that either targeted special CMA-ES variants ((1+1)-CMA-ES, MO-CMA-ES) or maintained non-triangular factors, by providing a practical, rank-µ update scheme for standard CMA-ES with theoretical justification for the CSA approximation.

Relative to prior work on reducing CMA-ES complexity via delayed eigendecomposition (standard CMA-ES practice), restricted models (diagonal/separable variants, main-vector/LM/limited-memory schemes), and earlier Cholesky updates, this paper aims to preserve full covariance adaptation while achieving asymptotically optimal quadratic complexity in both time and memory. Empirically, on standard BBO functions (including rotated versions) up to d=256, the proposed method matches the reference CMA-ES in number of function evaluations while yielding large wall-clock speedups, especially as d grows.

Strengths:
- Clear, concrete engineering contribution: a practical rank-µ Cholesky update for standard CMA-ES with O(µ d^2) time and O(d^2) memory, avoiding eigendecompositions per iteration.
- Thoughtful treatment of the CSA issue: the argument that the rotation between C^{1/2} and A vanishes as Ct stabilizes is intuitive and supported empirically.
- Strong empirical evidence that evaluation counts are preserved, while runtime is substantially reduced; rotation-invariance behavior is specifically tested.
- Good discussion of numerical aspects: triangular storage is compact; triangular solves are stable; inversion and eigenvalue-related diagnostics are discussed.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- A notable technical error: the paper claims “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” This is false in general. The diagonal of A contains the square roots of the pivots from Cholesky factorization, not the eigenvalues of C; only for diagonal C do these coincide. You can obtain det(C) immediately as the squared product of the diagonals, and triangular solves are efficient, but you do not get eigenvalues “for free.”
- The theoretical justification for the CSA approximation is qualitative: Lemma 1 assumes convergence (after det-normalization) of Ct to a fixed shape; this is not established for standard CMA-ES (the paper notes expectation convergence only in simplified models). No quantitative bound on the deviation between the “true” CSA norm and the approximated one is provided, and no result for nonstationary or noisy regimes is given.
- Baselines and scope: the comparison excludes active CMA-ES (negative update), widely used in practice. Extending the Cholesky update to support negative rank-µ “downdates” (with SPD guarantees) is critical and nontrivial. Similarly, comparisons to LM-CMA and other limited-memory/full-covariance hybrids would contextualize the trade-off between speed and modeling capacity, even if the focus here is on full covariance.
- Experimental reporting: for Rosenbrock, excluding unsuccessful runs biases evaluation counts and timing; success rates and time-to-target including failures should be reported. Also, the default decomposition delay in CMA-ES-Ref seems unusual as written (“max{1, 110d(c1+cµ)}” looks like a typographical error and may unfairly penalize the baseline).
- Implementation detail: computing A^{-1} explicitly is unnecessary and can be numerically less stable; triangular solves (forward/back substitution) suffice for pσ updates and are cheaper in memory.

Quality:
The algorithmic core (rank-µ Cholesky updates) is technically sound and matches well-known cholupdate/downdate principles for SPD matrices; the CSA approximation is plausible but lacks rigorous bounds. Empirical evidence is convincing for noiseless, standard testbeds and demonstrates substantial runtime gains without loss of sample efficiency.

Clarity:
The exposition of CMA-ES and the modification is generally clear, and the algorithm boxes help. However, the incorrect statement about eigenvalues must be fixed. The baseline setting (decomposition delay) should be clarified. Some references contain placeholders/formatting issues that should be cleaned up.

Originality:
The novelty lies in integrating a triangular Cholesky update with rank-µ CMA-ES and adapting CSA via A^{-1} with a theoretical rationale for negligible rotation error. Prior works updated decompositions or used non-triangular factors in special cases; this paper generalizes to standard CMA-ES with a careful argument. While incremental, it is a meaningful advance.

Significance:
Given the widespread use of CMA-ES in ML (policy search, HPO) and in black-box optimization, a drop-in variant that preserves evaluation efficiency while cutting runtime and memory is valuable. The contribution is practical and likely to see adoption, particularly in medium-to-high dimensions where eigendecompositions dominate runtime.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Correct the eigenvalue claim; clarify what information is accessible from A (e.g., determinant, condition proxies) and what is not.
- Provide quantitative analysis or at least empirical ablations isolating the CSA approximation error: track ∥pσ∥ with C^{-1/2} vs A^{-1} on small problems where exact C^{-1/2} is feasible, and report the distributional match (E{χ}) over time.
- Report success rates, not just median runtimes, and include unsuccessful runs in aggregated metrics.
- Clarify and, if needed, correct the CMA-ES-Ref decomposition delay; consider including a baseline tuned to minimize runtime overheads fairly.
- Discuss (or prototype) extension to active CMA-ES (negative weights), addressing numerical stability of downdates and SPD preservation.
- Avoid explicit inversion of A; use triangular solves throughout.
- Consider adding a comparison against LM-CMA to demonstrate the benefit of full covariance when it is affordable versus limited-memory variants.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Practical, widely useful speed/memory improvement to a standard algorithm without hurting evaluation efficiency.
- Clear algorithmic idea, careful implementation, and strong empirical results.
- Novel and well-motivated modification of CSA with reasonable justification.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Theoretical support for the CSA approximation is weak; no bounds or guarantees beyond a convergence assumption.
- Missing important baselines (active CMA-ES; limited-memory methods) and incomplete reporting (Rosenbrock filtering).
- Contains a fundamental technical misstatement about Cholesky diagonals and eigenvalues.

Overall evaluation:
This is a solid, practically important contribution with compelling experiments, but it needs correction of the Cholesky/eigenvalue claim and stronger experimental/theoretical support around the CSA approximation and baselines. I lean toward acceptance after revision.
