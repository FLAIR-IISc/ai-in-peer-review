Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a variant of CMA-ES that maintains and updates the triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance C = AAT, and replaces the inverse matrix square root in cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) by the inverse Cholesky factor A−1. The core idea is to avoid the costly (and potentially dominating) eigen-decomposition-based matrix square root by operating directly on a Cholesky factor via efficient rank-one updates. For CSA, the authors justify approximating C−1/2 by A−1 by analyzing the RQ decomposition C1/2 = A E and arguing that the induced orthogonal rotations E vary smoothly and converge under mild assumptions, so that the norm used in CSA is little affected, especially near convergence.

This builds on earlier work: (i) CMA-ES with delayed covariance updates to amortize decompositions [Hansen 2015], (ii) restricted covariance structures (diagonal, low-rank, and limited-memory approaches) [Ros & Hansen 2008; Loshchilov 2014, 2015; Sun et al. 2013], and (iii) direct updates of factorizations [Suttorp et al. 2009; Krause & Igel 2015]. Compared to Suttorp et al., the key difference is the insistence on a triangular factor (ensuring a bijection C↔A) and a theoretical argument explaining why replacing C−1/2 by A−1 in CSA should not deteriorate performance. Compared to Krause & Igel’s rank-one update for (1+1)-CMA-ES, the paper generalizes to standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and derives an O(µ d^2) scheme with optimal storage.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic contribution: an O(µ d^2) update rule for the full-covariance CMA-ES using a triangular Cholesky factor, with minimal memory and numerically stable rank-one updates.
- Thoughtful justification for the CSA modification via an analysis of rotation convergence in the RQ decomposition and exponential forgetting in CSA.
- Empirical evidence across standard benchmarks (Sphere, Cigar, Discus, Ellipsoid, Rosenbrock, Diffpowers) and dimensions up to 256, showing essentially identical function-evaluation counts to a strong reference, with large wall-clock speedups (e.g., ~20× at d=64).
- Good discussion of invariance properties and when/why the approximation should be benign.
- Practical benefits: A−1 obtainable in O(d^2); triangular storage is minimal; no outdated matrices (versus delay strategies).

Weaknesses
- A technically incorrect statement: “the diagonal elements of a triangular Cholesky factor are the square roots of the eigenvalues of the factorized matrix.” In general, Cholesky diagonals are not eigenvalues (nor their square roots); they are Cholesky pivots. This overstates the ability to “get the eigenvalues for free.”
- The theoretical justification for CSA relies on assumptions (normalized covariance shape convergence) that are plausible but not proven for standard CMA-ES on general functions. The lemma is correct under its assumptions, but the overall claim that behavior is unchanged is empirical rather than theoretically guaranteed.
- Experimental scope: results focus on noiseless, continuous benchmarks. No tests on noisy objectives, constraints, box bounds, or multi-modal rugged landscapes where CSA behavior can be more delicate. The supplementary notes rotation measurements but does not quantify their effect on CSA dynamics in the main text.
- Baselines: the reference CMA-ES computes the decomposition every iteration for the chosen dimensions (per default rules), which may disadvantage it in runtime. Including a carefully tuned delay schedule (beyond the d-step delay) and modern limited-memory full-covariance alternatives (e.g., LM-CMA) would strengthen the case, even if the latter trade off full covariance.
- Reproducibility details are light in the main text (e.g., hardware, BLAS threading, compiler flags). The Shark integration is good, but reporting more system details would help interpret wall-clock comparisons.

Quality
- Technical soundness of the rank-µ Cholesky update is strong and builds on prior rank-one updates. Maintaining a triangular factor ensures a one-to-one smooth map C↔A and avoids degrees of freedom that can cause drifting rotations.
- The CSA approximation is well-motivated but lacks a full end-to-end analysis for CMA-ES. The argument that E_t changes slowly and that exponential averaging damps rotation error is reasonable; experiments corroborate it.
- Complexity claims (time O(µ d^2), optimal storage of O(d^2)) are accurate. The claim about eigenvalues is incorrect and should be corrected.
- Numerical stability is discussed; the use of triangular factors and avoiding dual updates of A and A−1 are sensible.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. Algorithms are stated explicitly. The background on sampling and decompositions is helpful.
- Minor clarity issues: use of “RQ” vs “QR” could be clarified; expand briefly on Algorithm 2’s derivation or cite a standard reference for rank-one Cholesky updates. Fix the eigenvalue claim. Some references have formatting glitches.

Originality
- While using Cholesky factors in ES is not new, the specific combination—extending to standard CMA-ES with rank-µ updates, modifying CSA to use A−1, and providing a convergence-based justification for the approximation—is novel and nontrivial. The work is an incremental but meaningful advance.

Significance
- CMA-ES is widely used in ML (policy search, hyperparameter tuning). A 10–20× (or more) runtime reduction without extra evaluations is practically significant for full-covariance use cases. This work should influence implementations and be adopted in toolkits where full covariance is still feasible.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the statement about eigenvalues: diagonal(A) does not directly provide eigenvalues of C. If eigenvalue information is needed, clarify what can be inferred cheaply (e.g., determinant from product of diagonal entries; conditioning proxies) or provide a method to approximate eigenvalues if required.
- Add experiments on noisy functions and possibly constrained settings to stress CSA. Report sensitivity to µ and to different c1, cµ schedules.
- Include a stronger baseline with tuned decomposition delay and, if appropriate, LM-CMA or low-rank CMA variants to contextualize the trade-offs (even if the focus here is full covariance).
- Provide more system details for runtime comparisons and report single-threaded vs multi-threaded BLAS behavior.
- Discuss transient rotation error early in runs: e.g., plot ‖pσ‖ trajectories vs reference to show matching behavior beyond evaluation counts.

Minor comments
- “adaption” → “adaptation” in several places.
- Clarify big-omega usage and typical cubic cost of eigen/Cholesky decompositions.
- Clean up reference formatting (e.g., missing venues/years in a few entries).

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong practical contribution with clear speedups and minimal loss (none observed) in evaluation efficiency.
- Solid algorithmic engineering with a plausible and well-explained theoretical rationale.
- Relevance to the NIPS community via widely used optimization tooling.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees for CSA equivalence are suggestive rather than formal for general CMA-ES.
- Experimental scope could be broader (noisy, constrained) and baselines more comprehensive.
- Contains a factual error regarding eigenvalues from Cholesky factors, which should be corrected.

Overall, this is a well-executed and impactful engineering improvement to a core algorithm. With the noted correction and modest experimental extensions, it would make a strong NIPS contribution.
