- Introduces Cholesky-CMA-ES: replaces covariance matrix square root with triangular Cholesky factor to reduce computational complexity to O(d²) and storage requirements optimally.

- Provides a theoretical justification showing the modification preserves performance by replacing C⁻¹/²ₜ with A⁻¹ₜ and proving the introduced error vanishes as the algorithm converges (Lemma 1).

- Resolves a key limitation of prior Cholesky-based CMA-ES attempts by addressing the CSA requirement for the matrix square root.

- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive: benchmarks across functions and dimensions d=4..256, testing rotation invariance and multiple baselines.

- Compares to CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, and Suttorp-CMA-ES; shows Cholesky-CMA-ES uses the same number of function evaluations as CMA-ES-Ref.

- Demonstrates substantial runtime speedups (up to ~20× at d=64), with speedup growing with dimensionality until cache/memory effects intervene.

- Strength: strong technical contribution addressing a known computational bottleneck with sound theory and clear algorithms.

- Strength: paper is exceptionally well-written with clear algorithms, notation, and organized presentation.

- Strength: thorough empirical validation showing consistent performance benefits and practical significance for ML problems needing full covariance.

- Weakness: lacks direct comparison to alternative complexity-reduction methods (e.g., low-rank approximations) for very large d.

- Weakness: theoretical guarantee (Lemma 1) assumes convergence shape for Ct, but CMA-ES is randomized and may not converge deterministically; error accumulation during optimization needs further analysis.

- Weakness: memory/cache effects (e.g., L2 cache misses at d=256) may limit practical speedups at very large dimensions and are not fully explored.

- Minor technical inaccuracy: incorrect claim that Cholesky diagonal elements are square roots of eigenvalues.

- Arguments for acceptance: significant, applicable technical advance with sound theory and compelling empirical benefits.

- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient comparison to large-scale alternatives, theoretical limits under randomness, and incomplete exploration of large-d practical constraints.
