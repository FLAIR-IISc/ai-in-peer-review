This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) to reduce the computational complexity of updating the covariance matrix. The standard CMA-ES algorithm's runtime is often dominated by the `O(d^3)` cost of decomposing the `d x d` covariance matrix `C_t` in each iteration. The authors propose an algorithm, Cholesky-CMA-ES, that avoids forming `C_t` explicitly. Instead, it directly maintains and updates its lower triangular Cholesky factor `A_t` (where `C_t = A_t A_t^T`). The update consists of a series of rank-one updates to `A_t`, resulting in an overall complexity of `O(µd^2)` per iteration, which is asymptotically optimal for maintaining a full covariance matrix.

A key challenge in this approach, which has hindered previous attempts, lies in the cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) mechanism of CMA-ES. The CSA update requires multiplication by `C_t^{-1/2}` to normalize the search steps. Replacing the matrix square root inverse `C_t^{-1/2}` with the Cholesky factor inverse `A_t^{-1}` is not straightforward because the relationship `C_t^{1/2} = A_t E_t` involves an orthogonal matrix `E_t` that changes at each iteration. The paper's main theoretical contribution is to justify this replacement. The authors prove (Lemma 1) that if the *shape* of the covariance matrix converges, then the sequence of rotation matrices `E_t` also converges, causing the error introduced by the approximation to vanish over time due to the exponential weighting in the CSA path.

The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is empirically compared against the standard CMA-ES and other variants on a set of benchmark optimization problems. The results show that the proposed method requires a nearly identical number of function evaluations to converge, but is significantly faster in terms of wall-clock time, particularly as the problem dimension `d` increases. This work is situated within a line of research aiming to improve the scalability of CMA-ES, differing from prior work such as delayed updates (Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001), restricted matrix models (Ros and Hansen, 2008), or other Cholesky-based methods (Suttorp et al., 2009) by providing a sound and effective solution for the standard, full-covariance CMA-ES that specifically addresses the crucial CSA mechanism.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed algorithm is based on well-established numerical methods for updating Cholesky factorizations. The central claim—that replacing `C_t^{-1/2}` with `A_t^{-1}` does not harm performance—is supported by both a theoretical argument and strong empirical evidence. Lemma 1 provides a clear and insightful justification for why the approximation is reasonable under the assumption of convergence. The experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed method against relevant baselines on standard benchmarks, and the results convincingly demonstrate the claimed speedup without sacrificing optimization performance. The work is presented as a complete and practical contribution.

A minor weakness in the theoretical argument is its reliance on the assumption that the normalized covariance matrix converges. While this is empirically observed on many unimodal functions and supported by theory for simplified models, it is not a guaranteed property of CMA-ES on all possible objective functions (e.g., highly multi-modal landscapes). A brief discussion of how the approximation might behave in scenarios where the covariance matrix shape does not stabilize would have strengthened the analysis.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The motivation is strong, the background on CMA-ES is concise and sufficient, and the description of the proposed method is easy to follow. The authors clearly identify the key technical hurdle (the CSA update) and present their solution and justification in a logical, step-by-step manner. The inclusion of pseudo-code (Algorithms 1 and 2) further enhances clarity. The experimental section and the presentation of results are also clear and unambiguous.

**Originality:** While the idea of updating a Cholesky factor of the covariance matrix is not entirely new in the context of evolution strategies (e.g., Suttorp et al., 2009), this paper's contribution is novel and significant. The originality lies in successfully adapting this idea to the modern, standard CMA-ES algorithm. Specifically, the key novel insight is the analysis of the error introduced in the CSA path by using a triangular factor instead of the symmetric matrix square root, and the rigorous justification (Lemma 1) for why this error is negligible as the algorithm converges. This solves a critical problem that has prevented a straightforward and efficient Cholesky-based implementation of the full CMA-ES in the past.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is very high. CMA-ES is one of the most powerful and widely used algorithms for derivative-free optimization, with numerous applications in machine learning for tasks like policy search and hyperparameter tuning. Its primary limitation has been its `O(d^3)` computational complexity. This paper presents a practical, "drop-in" replacement that reduces the complexity to the optimal `O(µd^2)` for a full matrix update, with no discernible loss of performance. This effectively pushes the boundary of problem dimensionality for which standard CMA-ES is feasible. The proposed Cholesky-CMA-ES is likely to be adopted as the new standard implementation, making it an important contribution to both the evolutionary computation and machine learning communities.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pro:**
*   Solves a long-standing and practical bottleneck in a cornerstone derivative-free optimization algorithm.
*   The proposed method achieves asymptotically optimal time and space complexity for a full covariance matrix update.
*   The key approximation is well-justified by a clear theoretical argument.
*   Extensive empirical results convincingly demonstrate a significant speedup with no performance degradation in terms of function evaluations.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a technically nuanced contribution accessible.

**Con:**
*   The theoretical justification relies on an assumption of convergence for the covariance matrix shape, which may not hold on all problems.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution that will be of immediate value to researchers and practitioners using CMA-ES.
