

# Review: CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

This paper proposes a modified version of the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) called Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the matrix square root operation with a triangular Cholesky factorization to reduce computational complexity. The authors demonstrate that this modification maintains the algorithm's performance while achieving optimal time and storage complexity for covariance matrix updates.

The paper builds upon previous work by Suttorp et al. (2009) and Krause and Igel (2015), who explored Cholesky factor updates for specific CMA-ES variants but did not provide theoretical justification for the standard CMA-ES. The authors correctly identify that the standard CMA-ES relies on the matrix square root for cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA), which cannot be directly replaced by a Cholesky factor. Their key insight is to show that replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor introduces a small, vanishing error as the algorithm converges, which is theoretically justified through Lemma 1. This is a significant contribution over previous attempts that either lacked theoretical grounding or were limited to specific algorithm variants.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-justified claims. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, particularly Lemma 1 establishing the convergence of the rotation matrices, and the authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses. The experimental section is thorough, comparing Cholesky-CMA-ES with CMA-ES-Ref (with delayed matrix square root computation), CMA-ES/d (delayed update for d steps), and Suttorp-CMA-ES across multiple benchmark functions and dimensions (d = 4 to 256). The results consistently show that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but achieves significant speedups (up to 20x for d=64), with the speedup increasing with dimensionality. The authors also correctly note the limitations of the approach (quadratic scaling with dimensionality).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section clearly explains the CMA-ES algorithm and the role of the matrix square root in CSA. The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 1) is described with sufficient detail, and the pseudocode is clear. The theoretical justification is presented logically, with the lemma and proof following naturally from the problem statement. The experimental setup is well-explained, and the results are presented effectively through figures that clearly demonstrate the performance differences.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by providing the first theoretically justified approach for using Cholesky factors in the standard CMA-ES. While previous work (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) explored Cholesky updates for specific CMA-ES variants, this paper extends the approach to the standard algorithm with a sound theoretical foundation. The authors carefully cite related work and demonstrate how their approach differs from previous contributions, satisfying NIPS' originality criteria.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is substantial. CMA-ES is widely used in machine learning for hyperparameter tuning and reinforcement learning, where computational efficiency is critical. The proposed method reduces the time complexity of covariance matrix updates from O(d³) to O(µd²), which is a major improvement for high-dimensional problems. The paper demonstrates a practical speedup that increases with dimensionality, making it particularly valuable for large-scale optimization problems. The authors correctly note that while the method still scales quadratically with dimensionality, it provides a viable alternative to low-dimensional approximations (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) that can significantly degrade performance on non-separable problems.

**Strengths:**
- Theoretical justification for the Cholesky factor substitution is sound and well-presented.
- Clear demonstration of the algorithm's practical benefits through comprehensive experiments.
- The paper provides a numerically stable implementation with optimal storage complexity.
- The authors correctly identify the limitations and appropriate use cases for the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could better address the computational challenges for extremely high-dimensional problems (d > 1000) where even quadratic scaling becomes problematic.
- The theoretical analysis assumes Ct converges to a certain shape, but CMA-ES is a randomized algorithm where convergence is not guaranteed; more discussion of this limitation would strengthen the paper.
- While the paper compares with several CMA-ES variants, a more detailed comparison with other methods for large-scale CMA-ES (e.g., Loshchilov, 2015) would be beneficial.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The theoretical justification is rigorous, the implementation is practical, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the algorithm's benefits. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level. The Cholesky-CMA-ES should be adopted in practice for CMA-ES applications where covariance matrix updates are computationally expensive, as it provides significant speedups without compromising optimization performance. I recommend acceptance.
