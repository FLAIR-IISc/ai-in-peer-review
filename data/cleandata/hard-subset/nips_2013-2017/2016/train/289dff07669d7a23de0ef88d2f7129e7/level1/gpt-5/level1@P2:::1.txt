Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Cholesky-CMA-ES, a CMA-ES variant that maintains and updates a lower-triangular Cholesky factor A of the covariance matrix C = A A^T rather than updating C and recomputing its square root. This enables an O(µ d^2) update per iteration (with µ the number of weighted elites, typically O(log d)) and optimal O(d^2) storage, while preserving the sampling step x = m + σ A y with y ∼ N(0, I). The main challenge is that standard cumulative step-size adaptation (CSA) uses C^{-1/2} in the evolution path pσ; the authors replace C^{-1/2} by A^{-1}, which introduces a changing orthogonal rotation E_t from the RQ decomposition C^{1/2} = A E. They argue this is harmless because the sequence of rotations stabilizes asymptotically when the normalized shape of C converges. They provide a lemma showing E_{t-1}^T E_t → I if the normalized covariance C̄_t converges, implying that the norm of pσ is asymptotically unaffected by using A^{-1}. They give efficient rank-one updates to A (Algorithm 2), show that A^{-1} is available in O(d^2), and that eigenvalues (diagonal of A) are “for free.”

Relative to prior art, this generalizes Cholesky-based updates known for specialized CMA-ES variants [(1+1)-CMA-ES, MO-CMA-ES] and the rank-one update in Krause & Igel (2015) to the full (µ/µ_w, λ)-CMA-ES with rank-µ updates. It improves over Suttorp et al. (2009), which used a nontriangular factorization lacking a one-to-one mapping to C (hence no control over the induced rotations). It differs from low-complexity approximations (diagonal/separable CMA, LM-CMA) by retaining the full covariance and thus performance on non-separable, ill-conditioned problems.

Strengths:
- Technical contribution: A principled, numerically stable O(µ d^2) Cholesky update for the full covariance CMA-ES with optimal memory; clear computational advantage over periodic eigendecomposition.
- Theoretical justification: Although CSA is modified, the lemma on convergence of the orthogonal factors provides a convincing rationale for why the norm of pσ (and hence step-size adaptation) is asymptotically preserved; the smoothness/bijectivity of the triangular factorization supports small finite-time errors.
- Empirical evidence: On standard black-box benchmarks up to d = 256 with rotation tests, the method matches CMA-ES in number of evaluations while substantially reducing wall-clock time (e.g., >20× at d = 64), with results improving with dimension. Comparisons include a delayed-decomposition baseline (CMA-ES/d) and a nontriangular Cholesky variant.
- Practical benefits: Getting eigenvalues “for free” and an O(d^2) inverse for A are useful for monitoring conditioning and enforcing variance floors; code integrated in Shark and using LAPACK suggests practical readiness.

Weaknesses:
- Theory is asymptotic and conditional: The lemma assumes convergence of the normalized covariance matrix; CMA-ES is stochastic and may not ensure such convergence in finite time, especially far from the optimum or in nonstationary regimes. No finite-time bounds are given for the discrepancy between using C^{-1/2} and A^{-1}.
- Potential invariance issues: Standard CMA-ES is (largely) affine-invariant in its core updates; the modified CSA breaks exact rotation invariance. Experiments suggest negligible impact, but an explicit quantification of invariance loss (e.g., on problems with rapidly rotating Hessians) would strengthen the case.
- Empirical scope: While classic noiseless benchmarks are covered with rotations, there is limited evaluation under noise, constraints, or in real ML tasks (policy search, hyperparameter tuning) where CMA-ES is often applied and where CSA robustness matters.
- Numerical details: Algorithm 2’s stability claims would benefit from explicit comparison to standard cholupdate/downdate routines, discussion of conditioning safeguards (e.g., jitter, variance floor), and error accumulation over many rank-µ updates.

Assessment by NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The algorithmic development is sound, the computational claims are well supported, and experiments are careful with multiple baselines, multiple dimensions, and rotation tests. Theoretical justification is convincing at a high level but lacks nonasymptotic guarantees; nonetheless, empirical results are consistent with the claims.
- Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized, with helpful pseudo-code. Minor issues: the use of ω(d^2) to mean “worse than quadratic” is unusual; clarify the precise constants and defaults for µ, λ; give more intuition for Algorithm 2 and its stability; clean up a few reference-format glitches.
- Originality: Extending triangular Cholesky updates to the full CMA-ES with rank-µ updates and providing an argument for replacing C^{-1/2} by A^{-1} in CSA is novel and nontrivial. The combination of theory and engineering is original relative to prior Cholesky-based updates and low-memory approximations.
- Significance: High for the black-box optimization and ML communities. Many ML settings (e.g., cheap f, moderate-to-high d) are dominated by matrix updates; showing large wall-time reductions without sacrificing evaluations is impactful. The method can become a new default for full-covariance CMA-ES.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong practical speedups with preserved evaluation efficiency and optimal memory.
- Clean integration into the standard algorithm with minimal behavior change.
- Solid theoretical rationale and thorough experiments, including rotation checks.
- Likely to be adopted in practice; useful auxiliary benefits (eigenvalues, A^{-1}).

Cons for acceptance:
- No finite-time theoretical bounds for CSA approximation error.
- Limited evaluation on noisy/constrained problems and real ML tasks.
- Some missing numerical analysis details for the Cholesky rank-one updates.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a bound (even loose) on the norm error in pσ as a function of recent rotation changes and cσ, perhaps leveraging Eq. (6) to quantify finite-time discrepancies.
- Add experiments with noise and on at least one ML application (e.g., policy search) to confirm robustness of the CSA approximation in practice.
- Compare Algorithm 2’s stability and speed to standard cholupdate routines; describe safeguards for positive definiteness and eigenvalue floor enforcement.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a clear, useful, and well-substantiated contribution that improves the efficiency of a widely used algorithm with minimal loss of desirable properties.
