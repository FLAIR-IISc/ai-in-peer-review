This paper proposes a modification to the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) to reduce the computational cost of covariance matrix updates and storage. The key idea is to work directly with the Cholesky factorization of the covariance matrix, avoiding the explicit computation of the matrix square root. The authors argue that this modification doesn’t alter the fundamental behavior of the algorithm, preserving its convergence properties, while significantly reducing runtime, especially in high dimensions. They provide a theoretical justification and empirical validation of their approach, termed Cholesky-CMA-ES.

The paper is well-motivated. CMA-ES is a powerful but computationally expensive algorithm, and the covariance matrix update is a major bottleneck, particularly for large-scale problems. The proposed approach directly addresses this issue. The connection to prior work is also well-established, referencing relevant papers on CMA-ES improvements and related techniques like using Cholesky factors in other CMA variants. The authors clearly differentiate their work from previous attempts, emphasizing their theoretical justification for maintaining performance.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Practical Improvement:** The empirical results demonstrate a substantial speedup compared to standard CMA-ES, especially as dimensionality increases. This is a compelling benefit for practitioners.
* **Theoretical Justification:**  The paper provides a reasoned argument for why replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor doesn’t fundamentally alter the algorithm's convergence properties, addressing a key concern with this type of modification. Lemma 1 regarding the convergence of rotation matrices is a solid contribution to this justification.
* **Optimal Complexity:** The claim of achieving optimal asymptotic storage and runtime complexity for full covariance matrix adaptation is strong and supported by the analysis.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The explanation of the Cholesky-CMA-ES update and its theoretical underpinnings is relatively clear.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments compare the proposed method to several baselines, using a variety of benchmark functions and dimensionality settings.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Justification:** While the theoretical justification is present, it relies on some abstract arguments about coordinate system rotations and convergence of orthogonal matrices. A more intuitive explanation of why the approximation doesn't significantly impact performance might be beneficial for a broader audience.
* **Impact of Rotation Error:** The paper acknowledges the potential for error introduced by using the Cholesky factor instead of the matrix square root, but the impact of this error isn’t thoroughly investigated in the experiments. While the results show comparable performance, it would be valuable to analyze how the rotation matrix *actually* changes during optimization and correlate that with performance differences.
* **Limited Exploration of Parameters:** The experiments primarily use default CMA-ES parameters. Investigating the sensitivity of the Cholesky-CMA-ES to parameter tuning could reveal potential advantages or disadvantages.
* **Cache Effects:** The mention of L2 cache effects at d=256 is a brief acknowledgement of a potential limitation. A deeper discussion of how cache performance and memory access patterns influence the observed speedups would be valuable.
* **Comparison to LM-CMA:** While LM-CMA is included, its performance isn’t discussed in significant detail. A more nuanced comparison of the strengths and weaknesses of Cholesky-CMA-ES and LM-CMA would be informative.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and practical improvement to a widely used optimization algorithm.
* The theoretical justification, while complex, is convincing and addresses a crucial concern.
* The empirical results consistently demonstrate significant speedups without compromising performance.
* The proposed algorithm has the potential to enable the application of CMA-ES to larger-scale problems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical justification could be more accessible to a wider audience.
* The impact of the approximation error isn't fully analyzed.
*  The paper doesn’t extensively explore parameter sensitivity or compare to all relevant related methods.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of derivative-free optimization. The Cholesky-CMA-ES offers a compelling improvement to the standard CMA-ES, addressing a key performance bottleneck while maintaining its effectiveness. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper, particularly its practical impact and theoretical grounding, outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weaknesses section (specifically, a more intuitive explanation of the theoretical justification and a more thorough analysis of the rotation error).
