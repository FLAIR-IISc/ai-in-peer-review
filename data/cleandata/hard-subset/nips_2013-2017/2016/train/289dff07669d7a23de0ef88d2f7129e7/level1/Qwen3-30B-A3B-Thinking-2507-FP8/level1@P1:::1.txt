

# Peer Review: CMA-ES with Optimal Covariance Update and Storage Complexity

## Summary

This paper proposes a variant of the CMA-ES (Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy) algorithm called Cholesky-CMA-ES, which replaces the matrix square root computation with a Cholesky factorization to reduce time and space complexity. The standard CMA-ES has O(d^3) time complexity per iteration due to the need to compute the matrix square root of the covariance matrix, which dominates runtime for large dimensions. The authors show that by using the Cholesky factor instead of the matrix square root, they can achieve O(µd^2) time complexity (with µ typically O(log d)) while maintaining similar performance in terms of objective function evaluations. The paper provides a theoretical justification (Lemma 1) showing that the error introduced by this approximation vanishes as the algorithm converges, and presents empirical results demonstrating significant speedups without affecting optimization performance.

This work builds on previous attempts to improve CMA-ES, including delay strategies (Hansen and Ostermeier, 2001), restricted model complexity (Ros and Hansen, 2008), and Cholesky-based approaches for specific variants (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015). The key novelty is the theoretical justification for applying this approach to the standard CMA-ES, which was previously problematic due to the cumulative step-size adaptation mechanism.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence. The authors provide a rigorous proof (Lemma 1) that the error introduced by replacing the matrix square root with the Cholesky factor vanishes as the algorithm converges. The experimental section is comprehensive, testing on multiple benchmark functions across varying dimensions (4-256), with rotation invariance tests and careful comparison against state-of-the-art CMA-ES variants. The results consistently show that Cholesky-CMA-ES requires the same number of function evaluations as the standard CMA-ES but achieves significant runtime reductions (up to 20x faster for d=64).

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution to the CMA-ES literature by providing the first theoretically justified approach for using Cholesky factorization in the standard CMA-ES. While previous work (Suttorp et al., 2009; Krause and Igel, 2015) explored similar ideas for specific variants, this paper extends the approach to the full CMA-ES with a theoretical guarantee that the performance remains comparable. The paper effectively differentiates itself from related work and is significantly different from recent NIPS papers on CMA-ES.

**Significance:** The work addresses a practical bottleneck in CMA-ES that has limited its applicability to high-dimensional problems. The proposed method enables scaling CMA-ES to larger dimensions without sacrificing optimization performance. The runtime improvements are substantial (20x faster for d=64) and scale with problem dimension, making the algorithm more applicable to real-world machine learning problems like hyperparameter tuning and direct policy search. The authors provide clear motivation for why this improvement matters in practice.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and clearly explains the background, motivation, and technical details. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The theoretical justification is presented clearly, though some readers might need familiarity with matrix decompositions.

## Weaknesses

**Numerical Stability:** The paper does not adequately address whether the Cholesky factorization approach is numerically more stable than the standard matrix square root computation. This is a significant concern for real-world applications where numerical stability is critical, especially for ill-conditioned problems.

**Limited Comparison with Recent Work:** While the paper compares with CMA-ES variants (CMA-ES-Ref, CMA-ES/d, Suttorp-CMA-ES), it does not compare with other recent approaches that also aim to reduce CMA-ES complexity, such as Loshchilov (2015) and Sun et al. (2013). This omission makes it difficult to assess the relative merits of the proposed approach.

**Practical Impact Discussion:** The paper could better articulate the practical impact of the method for specific machine learning applications. While it mentions direct policy search and hyperparameter tuning, it doesn't provide concrete examples of how much larger problems could be solved with the proposed method.

## Pro and Con Acceptance

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Significant theoretical contribution with rigorous justification
- Clear demonstration of substantial runtime improvements without performance loss
- Well-executed experimental evaluation across multiple dimensions and benchmark functions
- Addresses a genuine bottleneck in a widely used optimization algorithm
- Direct relevance to machine learning applications (hyperparameter tuning, reinforcement learning)

**Con Acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of numerical stability concerns
- Incomplete comparison with other recent complexity-reduction approaches for CMA-ES
- Limited discussion of practical impact on specific machine learning applications

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the CMA-ES literature. The theoretical justification and empirical results are compelling, demonstrating that the Cholesky-CMA-ES variant achieves optimal time and space complexity without sacrificing optimization performance. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a meaningful problem in derivative-free optimization. The main weaknesses are the limited discussion of numerical stability and the incomplete comparison with recent complexity-reduction approaches, but these do not undermine the core contribution.

I recommend acceptance to NIPS 2016. This work is highly relevant to the conference's focus on machine learning algorithms and optimization methods, and it provides a practical improvement to a widely used algorithm with clear benefits for machine learning applications. The paper meets all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level, and the minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's overall contribution to the field.
