### **Summary**

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a method for Koopman spectral analysis, to nonlinear dynamical systems using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to apply the principles of DMD not to the original state-space data, but to a feature-space representation of the data induced by a kernel function. The Koopman operator, which describes the evolution of observables, becomes a linear operator in this feature space, making its spectral properties accessible via DMD-like techniques. The authors first derive a "direct" algorithm based on the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization of the Krylov subspace in the RKHS. Acknowledging the potential numerical instability of this direct approach, they then present a more robust algorithm that first projects the feature-space data onto its principal components (via kernel PCA) before performing the eigendecomposition. This is analogous to the use of Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) for robustifying standard DMD. The paper demonstrates the utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features for several applications, including short-term prediction, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection.

The work builds directly upon the literature of DMD [25, 26, 33] and its connection to Koopman operator theory [16]. The use of kernels to handle nonlinearity is a standard technique in machine learning, and its application here is a natural step. The proposed method is closely related to Extended DMD (eDMD) [35], which generalizes DMD to an arbitrary dictionary of observable functions; the kernel-based approach can be seen as using a specific, data-dependent dictionary of functions within the RKHS. It also relates to spectral learning methods for dynamical systems that have appeared at NIPS [15, 17].

### **Strengths**

1.  **Principled Extension:** The paper provides a clear and theoretically well-motivated extension of DMD to nonlinear systems. Lifting the dynamics into an RKHS where the Koopman operator is linear is an elegant way to generalize the linear analysis inherent in standard DMD. The connection to the Arnoldi method and Krylov subspaces is well-maintained in the feature space formulation.

2.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized. It starts with a good overview of the necessary background on Koopman operators and DMD, then logically develops the kernelized version, starting from a direct but potentially unstable formulation (Section 3) and moving to a practical, robust algorithm (Section 4). The theorems provide formal justification for the algorithms.

3.  **Practical Algorithm:** The development of a robust algorithm using kernel PCA is a key strength. This addresses a known practical issue with DMD-like methods (rank-deficiency of the data matrix) and makes the proposed technique practically viable. The entire algorithm can be implemented using Gram matrices, effectively employing the "kernel trick."

4.  **Demonstration of Applications:** The inclusion of Section 7, which explores applications beyond simple spectral analysis, is a significant contribution. It demonstrates that the features extracted by kernel DMD can be used for downstream machine learning tasks like prediction, classification of dynamics, and change-point detection, highlighting the method's potential significance.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Originality and Relation to Extended DMD:** The primary weakness is the significant overlap with the Extended DMD (eDMD) framework [35]. The robust algorithm presented in Section 4 appears to be a specific instance of eDMD where the dictionary of observables is composed of kernel basis functions centered at the data points (i.e., `g(x) = [k(x, x_0), k(x, x_1), ...]`). While the paper mentions eDMD in the related work section, it does not sufficiently clarify the novelty of its contribution in light of this prior work. A more explicit comparison, outlining whether the proposed method offers theoretical, computational, or practical advantages over a direct application of eDMD with a kernel-derived dictionary, is needed to properly assess the originality.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is largely qualitative and serves as a proof-of-concept rather than a rigorous validation.
    *   **Lack of Baselines:** The experiments lack comparisons to relevant baseline methods. For prediction, it would be crucial to compare against standard DMD (to show the benefit of the kernel) and other nonlinear time-series models like Gaussian Process Dynamical Models. For dynamics recognition, a comparison with other feature extraction methods would be informative.
    *   **Quantitative Metrics:** The evaluation mostly relies on visual inspection. For the Hénon map, the assessment that eigenvalues "seem to be approximately estimated" is vague. The locomotion embedding is interesting, but lacks a quantitative task (e.g., classification accuracy) to measure the quality of the embedding. The change-point detection result is also presented without quantitative metrics or comparison to the baseline on more than one example.

3.  **Details for Reproducibility:** Some details in the application section are sparse. The pre-image prediction method is described at a high level with a reference to [18], but more specific details of its implementation in this context would improve clarity and reproducibility. Similarly, the derivation of the change-point detection score could be elaborated upon.

### **Critique Summary**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations appear correct and the proposed algorithms are well-founded. The work is a complete piece, progressing from theory to practical algorithms and applications.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow for a reader with a background in machine learning and dynamical systems.
*   **Originality:** This is the weakest point. The conceptual contribution is a natural combination of two well-known techniques (DMD and kernel methods). However, its practical realization is very similar to prior work on Extended DMD [35], and the paper needs to better articulate its distinct contribution.
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem: data-driven analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. By making DMD more powerful and flexible, the work has the potential to be useful for both researchers and practitioners. The application section successfully highlights this potential. However, the significance is tempered by the limited experimental validation and the question of novelty.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides an elegant, self-contained kernel formulation for Koopman analysis.
*   The method is a principled and powerful way to automate the choice of basis functions for DMD.
*   The paper is well-written, and the theoretical development is clear and sound.
*   The demonstrated applications show the potential utility of the method for various machine learning tasks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Novelty is questionable given the strong similarity to Extended DMD [35].
*   The experimental evaluation is weak, lacking quantitative metrics and comparisons to important baselines.
*   The practical benefits over existing methods are not clearly demonstrated.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting and well-executed idea. However, the concerns regarding originality and the lack of a strong empirical evaluation are significant. The work would be much stronger if the authors could (1) explicitly and thoroughly differentiate their contribution from Extended DMD, and (2) provide a more rigorous experimental comparison against relevant baselines with quantitative performance metrics.
