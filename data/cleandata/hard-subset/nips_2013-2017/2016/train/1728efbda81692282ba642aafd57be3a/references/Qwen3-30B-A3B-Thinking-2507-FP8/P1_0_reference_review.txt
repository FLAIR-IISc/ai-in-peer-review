

# Peer Review: "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors address a key limitation of traditional DMD, which approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials of observables (akin to a one-term Taylor expansion), by instead using kernel functions to create nonlinear basis functions. The core contribution is a modal decomposition algorithm that operates in RKHS, theoretically reducing to orthogonal basis calculation for the Krylov matrix and eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projection. The authors further develop a robustified version using POD bases to address numerical instability issues common in DMD implementations. The paper demonstrates applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data.

This work builds on recent advances in Koopman spectral analysis (e.g., Williams et al., 2015; Korda & Mezić, 2018) and kernel methods (Schölkopf & Smola, 2002), positioning itself as a more expressive approach than traditional DMD by leveraging the representational power of kernel functions. The paper relates well to NIPS work on Koopman operator methods (e.g., "A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator" by Williams et al., 2015) while introducing a novel kernel-based extension.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Soundness**: The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by extending DMD to RKHS, providing rigorous proofs (Theorems 1 and 2) that establish the mathematical foundation of the proposed method. The extension from linear to nonlinear basis functions through kernel methods is conceptually sound and addresses a genuine limitation of traditional DMD.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors recognize and address a key practical issue in DMD implementations—numerical instability—by proposing a robustification technique using POD bases. This is particularly valuable as it makes the method more applicable to real-world scenarios.

3. **Demonstrated Utility**: The paper effectively showcases multiple applications of the extracted features: state prediction (via preimage reconstruction), dynamics recognition (via kernel principal angles), and sequential change-point detection. The empirical examples (Figures 1-4) provide convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness.

4. **Clear Connection to Koopman Theory**: The paper maintains a strong connection to Koopman operator theory throughout, clearly explaining how the proposed method relates to the theoretical framework of Koopman spectral analysis.

5. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is logically organized, with clear sections that build upon each other, from theoretical foundations to algorithmic implementation to practical applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: While the paper references related work (Section 5), it lacks a comprehensive comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods for Koopman spectral analysis. For instance, it doesn't compare directly with the kernel-based Koopman methods proposed in "Nonlinear Koopman Modes and Power System Stability Assessment Without Models" (Suzuki & Mezić, 2018) or the more recent deep learning approaches to Koopman estimation. A thorough comparison would strengthen the paper's positioning in the field.

2. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper does not provide any analysis of computational complexity or scalability of the proposed method. For a method that involves Gram matrix calculations and eigendecompositions, this is a significant omission, especially for a NeurIPS submission where computational efficiency is often a key consideration.

3. **Limited Real-World Application Scope**: While the locomotion data example (Figure 3) is promising, the empirical evaluation is primarily limited to synthetic systems (toy nonlinear system, Hénon map) and simple real-world data. More challenging real-world applications would better demonstrate the method's practical utility.

4. **Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper establishes theoretical foundations but lacks strong theoretical guarantees about convergence or approximation error bounds for the proposed method. This is particularly important for a method that makes a claim about providing a more accurate approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions.

5. **Clarity in Algorithm Description**: Section 4 (Robustifying with POD Bases) could be clearer. The description of the robustified algorithm (steps 1-4) is somewhat dense, and the connection between the POD basis and the final eigenvectors could be explained more explicitly.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel approach to Koopman spectral analysis using kernel methods, addressing a key limitation of traditional DMD
- Strong theoretical foundation with well-constructed proofs
- Practical robustification technique that addresses numerical instability issues
- Demonstrated utility through multiple meaningful applications
- Clear presentation and good connections to existing literature
- Appropriate empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world data

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods in Koopman spectral analysis
- Lack of computational complexity analysis, which is important for NeurIPS
- Limited empirical evaluation on challenging real-world datasets
- Insufficient theoretical guarantees about approximation error or convergence
- Some sections (particularly Section 4) could be clearer

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending DMD to RKHS, providing a more expressive framework for Koopman spectral analysis. The robustification approach is practical and addresses a real limitation of DMD implementations. The empirical results are convincing, and the applications demonstrate the utility of the method.

The main weaknesses are the limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods and the lack of computational complexity analysis. These could be addressed with relatively minor revisions. The paper would benefit from adding a more detailed comparison with recent kernel-based Koopman methods and including a discussion of computational complexity.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add a comparison section**: Include a table or paragraph comparing the proposed method with recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Williams et al., 2015; Suzuki & Mezić, 2018; Korda & Mezić, 2018) in terms of basis functions, computational complexity, and performance metrics.

2. **Include computational complexity analysis**: Add a brief section discussing the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, particularly the cost of Gram matrix construction and eigendecomposition.

3. **Expand real-world applications**: Consider adding one more challenging real-world application to demonstrate the method's utility beyond the locomotion data.

4. **Clarify Section 4**: Provide a more intuitive explanation of how the POD basis relates to the final Koopman eigenfunctions and provide a more detailed description of the robustified algorithm.

5. **Add theoretical guarantees**: If possible, include some discussion of approximation error bounds or convergence properties, even if they are asymptotic.

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of Koopman spectral analysis and would be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS proceedings. With the suggested revisions, it would be significantly strengthened.
