### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a popular method from fluid dynamics for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems. The central idea is to apply Koopman operator theory within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The Koopman operator linearizes nonlinear dynamics by acting on observable functions of the state. While standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear basis of observables, this work leverages the kernel trick to implicitly use a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions, leading to potentially more accurate approximations of the Koopman spectrum for highly nonlinear systems.

The authors propose two algorithms. The first is a direct translation of the DMD principle to RKHS, based on a Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition of the Krylov matrix in the feature space. Acknowledging the potential numerical instability of this approach, they present a second, more robust algorithm. This robust version first finds a low-dimensional subspace using kernel Principal Component Analysis (equivalent to Proper Orthogonal Decomposition, or POD) and then projects the Koopman operator onto this subspace to compute its eigendecomposition. The paper provides theoretical justification (Theorems 1 and 2) connecting the computed eigenvalues and eigenvectors to the true Koopman spectra. Finally, the authors demonstrate the utility of the extracted spectral features in several applications: short-term prediction via a pre-image reconstruction method, embedding and recognition of dynamics on motion capture data, and sequential change-point detection in a chaotic system.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Elegant and Natural Extension:** The application of kernel methods to DMD is a well-motivated and elegant idea. It directly addresses a key limitation of standard DMD—its reliance on a linear combination of observables—by providing a principled way to work with an expressive, infinite-dimensional feature space.
2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a solid, if straightforward, theoretical development. The connection between the proposed algorithm, the Krylov subspace method in RKHS, and the Koopman operator is clearly established in Theorems 1 and 2.
3.  **Practical Robustness:** The authors show good practical judgment by not only presenting the direct kernelization (Section 3) but also developing a more robust version based on kernel PCA (Section 4). This mirrors the development of robust DMD variants in the literature and makes the proposed method more likely to be useful in practice, especially with noisy or rank-deficient data.
4.  **Demonstration of Versatility:** A significant strength is the demonstration of the method's utility across a diverse set of machine learning tasks (prediction, classification, change-point detection). This effectively reframes the output of the Koopman analysis—the modes and eigenvalues—as powerful features for downstream tasks, increasing the paper's significance to the NIPS community.
5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and logically structured. It provides a clear introduction to the necessary background on Koopman operators and DMD before presenting the novel contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Relation to Extended DMD (eDMD):** The paper's primary weakness is its insufficient discussion of the relationship with Williams et al. (2015) [35], who proposed Extended DMD (eDMD). eDMD allows the user to replace the linear observables with a dictionary of arbitrary (nonlinear) basis functions. The proposed kernel DMD can be viewed as a specific instance of eDMD where the basis functions are the kernel feature maps centered at the data points, i.e., `{k(x, x_i)}`. While the proposed method has the advantage of not requiring an explicit choice of basis functions, the conceptual novelty over eDMD is diminished. A more detailed comparison, highlighting the trade-offs and potentially including an empirical comparison, would be necessary to clearly delineate the contribution.
2.  **Details on Applications:** While the breadth of applications is a strength, the depth is sometimes lacking.
    *   **Prediction:** The prediction relies on solving the pre-image problem, which is notoriously difficult for kernel methods. The paper uses one specific method but doesn't discuss its limitations or potential failure modes. The evaluation is purely qualitative (plots without error metrics), making it hard to assess performance.
    *   **Change-Point Detection:** The derivation of the change-point score involves a simplification where a term is dropped because its "variation is much smaller." This heuristic could be better justified or at least more clearly acknowledged as an approximation.
3.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper lacks a discussion on how crucial hyperparameters were chosen. This includes the choice of kernel function (polynomial vs. Gaussian), kernel parameters (width, degree), and the number of principal components `p` to retain in the robust version. These choices are critical for performance, and their omission affects the reproducibility of the work.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The mathematical derivations are sound, and the proposed algorithms are well-founded. The empirical results support the claims, although quantitative evaluation is sometimes missing.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear. The writing is precise, and the flow of ideas from background to the core contribution and applications is easy to follow. The notation is consistent.

**Originality:** The core idea of "kernelizing DMD" is novel and valuable. However, as noted above, its originality is somewhat tempered by its strong similarity to the eDMD framework. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a clearer positioning of its work relative to eDMD. The application of Koopman spectral features to change-point detection and dynamics recognition using kernel principal angles is also a novel contribution.

**Significance:** The work is significant. DMD is gaining traction as a powerful tool for data-driven analysis of dynamical systems, and this paper makes it more powerful and accessible to the machine learning community by integrating it with the familiar kernel methods framework. The demonstration of its use as a feature extraction tool for various ML tasks will likely inspire follow-up work.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pro:**
*   Presents a method that is a natural and powerful combination of two important techniques: DMD and kernel methods.
*   The method is technically sound and is presented with a practical, robust variant.
*   Demonstrates broad utility on several important machine learning problems.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to understand.

**Con:**
*   Insufficient comparison to and differentiation from the highly related Extended DMD (eDMD) method.
*   Lack of quantitative evaluation for the prediction task.
*   Missing details on hyperparameter selection, which impacts reproducibility.

### Recommendation

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution. The weaknesses, particularly the comparison to eDMD, are addressable. I lean towards acceptance, but I would strongly urge the authors to revise the paper to include a detailed discussion and comparison with Extended DMD in Section 5. This would clarify the paper's specific contribution and strengthen its place in the literature. Adding quantitative metrics for the prediction task and a brief note on hyperparameter selection would also improve the manuscript.
