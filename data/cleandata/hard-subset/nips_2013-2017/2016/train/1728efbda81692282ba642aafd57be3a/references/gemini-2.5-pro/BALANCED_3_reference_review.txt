### **Summary**

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), a method the authors term kernel DMD (kDMD). The core idea is to lift the system's state space into an infinite-dimensional feature space via a kernel map, where the nonlinear dynamics are represented by a linear Koopman operator. The paper presents two algorithms to approximate the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions of this operator from data. The first is a direct translation of the DMD algorithm using Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space. Recognizing the potential numerical instability of this approach, the authors propose a second, more robust algorithm based on projecting the data onto its principal components in the feature space (i.e., using kernel PCA). The utility of the extracted Koopman modes as features is demonstrated on several applications, including short-term prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

This work builds directly upon the growing literature on DMD [25, 26, 33] and its connection to Koopman operator theory [16, 20]. It effectively combines this line of work with established techniques from machine learning, namely kernel methods and kernel PCA [28, 29]. The proposed method is closely related to Extended DMD (EDMD) [35], which generalizes DMD to a user-defined dictionary of observable functions. Kernel DMD can be viewed as a specific instance of EDMD where the dictionary is implicitly defined by the kernel function and the data points themselves.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Elegant Formulation:** The paper presents a natural and principled extension of DMD to handle nonlinear dynamics. By leveraging RKHS, it avoids the need for local linearization inherent in standard DMD and provides a powerful, non-parametric way to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions. The formulation is clear and theoretically well-grounded in both Koopman theory and kernel methods.

2.  **Robust Algorithmic Design:** The authors astutely identify the potential for numerical instability in a naive implementation (Section 3) and propose a robust alternative based on kernel PCA (Section 4). This is analogous to the use of truncated SVD in standard DMD and demonstrates a practical understanding of the challenges involved in implementing these spectral methods. Theorem 2 provides a solid theoretical justification for this robust approach.

3.  **Demonstrated Versatility:** A significant strength of the paper is the demonstration of the proposed method's utility across a diverse set of applications (Section 7). Using the learned Koopman modes as features for prediction, embedding/recognition, and change-point detection showcases the potential of kDMD as a general-purpose tool for nonlinear time-series analysis, moving beyond the traditional fluid dynamics context of DMD.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It provides a concise yet sufficient background on Koopman operators and DMD before developing the kernelized extension. The progression from the direct formulation to the robust algorithm is easy to follow.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Originality and Relationship to Extended DMD (EDMD):** The primary weakness of the paper is its significant overlap with the EDMD framework [35]. The robust kDMD algorithm presented in Section 4 is functionally equivalent to EDMD where the dictionary of observables consists of kernel functions centered at the training data points (i.e., a dictionary `D = {k(·, x_i)}`). While the authors briefly acknowledge the similarity, the paper would be much stronger if it included an in-depth discussion contextualizing kDMD within the EDMD framework. The current presentation may overstate the novelty of the approach, as it can be seen as a specific, albeit useful, application of the more general EDMD principle.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** While the applications are well-chosen, the empirical results are mostly illustrative and lack quantitative rigor and comparison to relevant baselines.
    *   **Prediction:** The prediction results in Figure 2 are qualitative. No quantitative metrics (e.g., Mean Squared Error) are provided, and there is no comparison against alternative methods such as standard DMD, EDMD with a different basis (e.g., polynomials), or other time-series forecasting models like Gaussian Process dynamical models.
    *   **Recognition & Change-Point Detection:** Similarly, the dynamics recognition and change-point detection experiments are presented as demonstrations without quantitative performance evaluation (e.g., classification accuracy, detection delay, false alarm rates) or comparison to standard methods in these fields (e.g., Dynamic Time Warping for recognition, other CUSUM-based methods for detection). This makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed method's performance.

3.  **Scalability and Hyperparameter Selection:** The method relies on forming and computing the eigendecomposition of `τ x τ` matrices, where `τ` is the number of data points. The computational complexity, likely `O(τ^3)`, is not discussed but represents a significant limitation for long time series. Furthermore, the performance of the method is highly dependent on the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters (e.g., the width of a Gaussian kernel). The paper does not provide any guidance on how to select these parameters, which is a crucial aspect for practical application.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarify Contribution vs. EDMD:** I strongly recommend rewriting parts of the introduction and related work to more explicitly position kDMD as a kernel-based instantiation of the EDMD framework. The contribution could then be framed as demonstrating the power and elegance of this specific instantiation, which leverages the rich theory of kernel methods to automatically define a powerful, data-driven set of basis functions, thus obviating the need for manual feature engineering.

2.  **Strengthen Experiments:** To meet the standards of a top-tier conference like NeurIPS, the experimental section needs to be substantially improved. Please include:
    *   **Quantitative Metrics:** Report standard metrics for all tasks (e.g., RMSE for prediction, accuracy/F1-score for recognition, etc.).
    *   **Baselines:** Compare kDMD against relevant and strong baselines. For prediction, this should include at least standard DMD and EDMD with a well-chosen dictionary. For recognition, a k-NN classifier with DTW would be a simple but strong baseline.
    *   **Ablation Study:** An analysis of how performance varies with the number of retained principal components (`p` in Section 4) and the choice of kernel/hyperparameters would be very valuable.

3.  **Discuss Limitations:** Please add a discussion on the computational scalability of the method. Mentioning the `O(τ^3)` complexity and suggesting potential avenues for scaling up, such as the Nyström approximation for the Gram matrix, would make the paper more complete. A brief discussion on the challenges of the kernel pre-image problem for the prediction task would also be appropriate.

### **Decision**

This paper presents an interesting and well-formulated extension of DMD using kernel methods. The connection is natural, the theory is sound, and the applications are compelling. However, the contribution's novelty is diminished by its close relationship to the existing EDMD framework, a point which is not sufficiently addressed. More critically, the experimental evaluation is preliminary and lacks the quantitative rigor and comparative analysis necessary to convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages.

While the ideas presented are promising, the paper in its current form falls short of the high standards for originality and empirical validation expected at NeurIPS. Therefore, I recommend rejection at this time. However, I believe that the paper could become a strong contribution if the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by strengthening the experimental validation and clarifying the precise relationship to prior work.
