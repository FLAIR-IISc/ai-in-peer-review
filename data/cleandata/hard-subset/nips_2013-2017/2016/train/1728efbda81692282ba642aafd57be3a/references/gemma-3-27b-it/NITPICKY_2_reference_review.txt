This paper presents an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, aiming to overcome limitations of traditional DMD which relies on linear monomials. While the core idea of employing kernel methods to capture potentially nonlinear eigenfunctions is conceptually sound and aligns with broader efforts in Koopman operator theory, the presentation suffers from numerous, albeit minor, deficiencies that collectively detract from its overall impact and raise concerns about rigor and practical applicability.

The introduction adequately motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature, referencing relevant papers from various fields (physics, machine learning). However, the transition between introducing DMD and the proposed kernel-based extension feels somewhat abrupt. A more thorough discussion of *why* DMD’s reliance on linear monomials is a significant limitation in many real-world scenarios would strengthen the motivation. 

Section 3, detailing the RKHS-based DMD, is mathematically dense but contains a concerning lack of detail regarding practical implementation. The theorem presented is correct, but the discussion lacks sufficient explanation of how to choose the kernel, its parameters, and how to efficiently compute operations within the RKHS.  The assertion that “almost of the theoretical claims…do not necessarily require ϕ to be in RKHS” feels like a hand-waving statement; if RKHS properties aren’t crucial, why emphasize them throughout? Furthermore, the derivation leans heavily on existing results without providing sufficient intuition for readers unfamiliar with functional analysis. 

Section 4, addressing the ill-conditioning issue with POD projection, is a welcome addition. However, the explanation of how the kernel PCA is used to construct the projection basis feels rushed. The connection between the kernel PCA eigenvectors and the Koopman eigenfunctions (Theorem 2) is presented without sufficient justification of the assumption that “κj is in the subspace spanned by the columns of U”.  This is a critical assumption that requires more careful consideration. The notation throughout this section is also inconsistent with previous sections.

The “Related Works” section is comprehensive but lacks critical assessment. Simply listing related techniques is insufficient; the authors should discuss how their approach *specifically* differs in terms of computational cost, accuracy, and applicability compared to existing methods like extended DMD and sparsity-promoting DMD. The inclusion of a link to Matlab code is a positive aspect.

The empirical evaluation (Sections 6 & 7) is the weakest part of the paper. The toy system example is well-behaved and provides little insight into the method’s performance on more complex, high-dimensional data. The Hénon map results are presented graphically without quantitative evaluation.  How accurately are the eigenvalues estimated? What is the sensitivity of the results to the kernel parameters? The application sections (prediction, embedding, and change-point detection) are described at a high level, lacking detailed experimental results or comparisons to baseline methods. The prediction results are only visually shown, with no quantitative performance metrics. The recognition of dynamics example relies on a pre-existing dataset, and the method's advantage over more standard dimensionality reduction techniques is not demonstrated. The change-point detection example seems tangential, relying on a somewhat contrived scenario and offering limited insight into the algorithm's general applicability. 

Numerous minor issues throughout the manuscript further detract from its polish. There are several instances of imprecise language (e.g., “almost of the theoretical claims”), inconsistent notation, and occasional grammatical errors.  The formatting also contains minor inconsistencies (e.g., varying spacing around equations).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The idea of directly applying RKHS to Koopman spectral analysis is a potentially valuable extension of existing methods.
*   Theoretical Soundness: The mathematical derivations, while dense, appear correct.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of Rigor: Insufficient detail regarding practical implementation and critical assumptions.
*   Weak Empirical Evaluation: Limited experimental results and lack of quantitative assessment.
*   Clarity and Polish: Numerous minor issues with writing, notation, and formatting.
*   Significance: The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method offers a significant advantage over existing approaches.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a promising core idea, this paper falls short of NIPS standards due to its lack of rigor, weak empirical evaluation, and numerous minor flaws. While the theoretical foundations seem solid, the practical implications and advantages of the proposed method remain unclear. I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should significantly expand the empirical evaluation, provide more detailed implementation guidance, and address the critical assumptions raised in the review before resubmitting. A more thorough comparison to established methods is also essential. The manuscript requires substantial revision to achieve the clarity and polish expected of a NIPS publication.
