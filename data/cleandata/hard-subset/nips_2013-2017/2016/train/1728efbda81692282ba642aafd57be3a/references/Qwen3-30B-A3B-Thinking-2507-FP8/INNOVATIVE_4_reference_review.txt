

# Review: Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis

This paper introduces a novel approach to Koopman spectral analysis by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to replace the linear monomial basis functions used in traditional DMD with kernel-based nonlinear basis functions, enabling more accurate approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions for nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors develop a modal decomposition algorithm that works in RKHS, with a robustified variant using POD bases to address numerical instability issues.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHS, proving that the Koopman operator can be analyzed using kernel methods, with theorems establishing the connection between the empirical Ritz values and Koopman eigenvalues. Second, it offers a practical implementation that addresses the ill-conditioning issues common in DMD through projection onto POD directions. Third, it demonstrates applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition, showing how the extracted features can be used for practical tasks.

The novelty of this work is substantial. While Extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) has previously used pre-determined basis functions, this paper uses the expressive power of kernel functions to create adaptive, nonlinear basis functions for Koopman spectral analysis. This represents a conceptual leap beyond the linear approximations inherent in traditional DMD and its variants. The paper clearly articulates how their method differs from existing approaches, particularly in its use of kernel methods to capture nonlinear dynamics without requiring explicit basis function selection.

The theoretical development is strong, with well-motivated theorems and proofs that establish the mathematical foundation of the proposed method. The authors demonstrate the algorithm on both synthetic systems (a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map) and real-world locomotion data, showing promising results in eigenvalue estimation and application to prediction and dynamics recognition.

I particularly appreciate the bold conceptual direction this paper takes. While many DMD variants have focused on numerical improvements to the linear framework, this work fundamentally extends the approach by leveraging kernel methods to capture richer dynamics. This is exactly the kind of innovative direction that advances the field rather than making incremental improvements to existing methods.

The paper has some weaknesses that prevent it from being a perfect submission. The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited, relying primarily on synthetic examples and a small locomotion dataset. More comprehensive benchmarking against state-of-the-art methods on standard datasets would strengthen the claims. Additionally, the paper doesn't fully explore why kernel methods are preferable to other nonlinear extensions of DMD (e.g., using neural networks), which would help contextualize the method's advantages.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold conceptual contributions over methodological perfection, I find these weaknesses to be outweighed by the paper's significant conceptual innovation. The core idea of using RKHS for Koopman spectral analysis is sufficiently novel and well-justified to warrant acceptance. The method opens up new possibilities for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems using kernel methods, which could have substantial impact in fluid dynamics, control theory, and other fields where Koopman analysis is applied.

The paper is well-written, well-organized, and makes clear connections to related work. The theoretical development is sound, and the applications section demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed framework. While the empirical validation could be more extensive, the core contribution is sufficiently novel and well-motivated to make this a valuable contribution to the field.

In conclusion, this paper presents a bold and novel extension of DMD to RKHS that fundamentally changes how Koopman spectral analysis can be performed. The theoretical foundation is strong, the conceptual contribution is significant, and the potential applications are compelling. While the empirical evaluation could be expanded, the novelty and conceptual advancement of this work justify acceptance at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Strengths:**
- Novel extension of DMD to RKHS using kernel methods
- Strong theoretical foundation with well-proven theorems
- Conceptually bold approach that moves beyond linear approximations
- Clear applications in prediction, change detection, and dynamics recognition
- Practical robustification using POD bases

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited empirical evaluation on standard datasets
- Could better explain advantages over other nonlinear extensions
- Computational complexity not thoroughly discussed
- Connection to broader machine learning community could be strengthened
