This paper introduces a kernelized extension to Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), aiming to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with richer, nonlinear basis functions using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The core idea – moving beyond linear monomials in standard DMD – is a compelling one, addressing a known limitation of the original method. The authors correctly identify the connection to spectral analysis and the Koopman operator, framing their work within a well-established theoretical context. They also provide a practical refinement by projecting data onto POD directions to mitigate potential ill-conditioning, a crucial consideration for real-world application.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The kernelized DMD approach is a genuinely novel extension. While kernel methods are prevalent in machine learning, applying them directly to the Koopman spectral analysis within the DMD framework is a significant contribution. This moves beyond incremental improvements and proposes a fundamentally different way to approximate Koopman modes.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The paper is mathematically rigorous, clearly establishing the connection between the proposed method and existing Koopman theory and DMD. The theorems presented are well-defined and logically sound.
* **Practical Considerations:** Recognizing the potential for ill-conditioning and incorporating the POD projection demonstrates a strong awareness of practical challenges and provides a valuable robustness enhancement.
* **Empirical Validation:** The initial empirical results, although on simple examples, are encouraging and support the theoretical claims. The inclusion of applications (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) shows the potential for broader impact.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Scope:** While the toy examples and Hénon map are useful for initial validation, the empirical evaluation feels somewhat limited. The demonstration on locomotion data is suggestive but lacks detailed quantitative analysis. A more comprehensive evaluation on diverse datasets, including higher-dimensional and more complex dynamical systems, would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Kernel Selection:** The paper doesn’t provide strong guidance on kernel selection. The choice of polynomial and Gaussian kernels for the toy system and Hénon map, respectively, feels somewhat arbitrary. A discussion of kernel sensitivity and methods for optimal kernel parameter tuning would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:**  The computational cost of the kernelized DMD, particularly the Gram matrix calculations, is not explicitly addressed. A discussion of scalability and potential computational bottlenecks would be valuable.
* **Comparison with Existing Methods:**  The comparison with other kernel-based nonlinear system identification techniques (e.g., kernel state-space models) is relatively brief. A more thorough comparison of performance, computational cost, and interpretability would be useful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is genuinely novel and addresses a significant limitation of standard DMD.
* The mathematical formulation is rigorous and well-motivated.
* The proposed POD projection enhances robustness and addresses a practical concern.
* The paper opens up new avenues for research in Koopman spectral analysis and nonlinear dynamical systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation is limited and lacks comprehensive quantitative analysis.
* The discussion of kernel selection and computational complexity is brief.
* The comparison with existing kernel-based methods is not exhaustive.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some limitations in empirical validation and discussion of practical considerations, this paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to Koopman spectral analysis. The kernelized DMD framework has the potential to significantly expand the applicability of DMD to a wider range of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors have demonstrated a clear understanding of the underlying theory and have addressed potential practical challenges with a thoughtful refinement. I believe the novelty and potential impact of this work outweigh its current limitations, and I **recommend acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I would encourage the authors to expand the empirical evaluation with more diverse datasets, provide more guidance on kernel selection, and discuss the computational complexity of the method in more detail. I would also welcome a more thorough comparison with existing kernel-based nonlinear system identification techniques.
