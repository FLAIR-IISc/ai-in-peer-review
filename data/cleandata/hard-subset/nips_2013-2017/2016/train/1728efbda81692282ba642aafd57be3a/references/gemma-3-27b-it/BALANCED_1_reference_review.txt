This paper proposes a Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) method extended to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to move beyond linear monomials of observables as basis functions, leveraging the expressiveness of kernel functions to better approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, particularly in scenarios where linear approximations are insufficient. The authors provide a theoretical framework and algorithm, and demonstrate its application to both synthetic and real-world data, showcasing its potential for tasks like state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamic pattern recognition.

The paper is well-motivated, placing the work within the context of both dynamical systems theory and machine learning approaches to system identification. It clearly articulates the limitations of traditional DMD and positions the kernel-based extension as a potential solution. The mathematical derivations are generally rigorous, and the connection to the existing DMD framework is well-explained. The inclusion of empirical examples, though relatively simple, provides initial evidence of the method’s efficacy. The detailed description of related work is comprehensive and demonstrates an awareness of the relevant literature.  The suggestion to robustify the method using POD directions is a practical and valuable addition, addressing a potential implementation challenge.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. First, while the theoretical framework is sound, the practical advantages of this approach over existing kernel-based DMD variants (e.g., extended DMD [35]) aren’t fully elucidated. The authors mention extended DMD, but don't provide a detailed comparison highlighting the specific benefits of their approach. It is not clear *when* their method would demonstrably outperform alternatives. The theoretical justification for the use of the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization, while correct, is followed by an immediate acknowledgement that it can be ill-conditioned. The proposed fix with POD bases feels somewhat ad-hoc; a more thorough investigation of regularization techniques or alternative orthogonalization methods would be beneficial. 

Second, the empirical evaluation is limited. The toy system, while helpful for initial validation, is too simplistic to showcase the advantages of the kernel method. The Hénon map example, though more complex, lacks a comprehensive comparison with other state-of-the-art methods for chaotic system analysis. The applications section presents interesting possibilities, but the results are largely qualitative and lack rigorous quantitative evaluation. For instance, the change-point detection example simply shows a plot of the change score without defining performance metrics or comparing to existing change detection algorithms. The locomotion data example lacks detail on the experimental setup and evaluation protocol. 

Third, the paper’s clarity suffers from occasional dense mathematical notation and a slightly disorganized structure. While the core ideas are well-defined, navigating the various equations and theorems can be challenging. Section 5, "Related Works," is quite long and could be streamlined to focus on the most relevant contributions. 

Finally, while the authors provide a link to Matlab code, reproducibility would be enhanced by providing more detailed implementation specifics (e.g., kernel parameter selection, POD truncation criteria) within the paper itself.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of DMD to RKHS, offering a potentially more expressive framework for Koopman spectral analysis.
*   Sound theoretical foundation, building upon existing work in dynamical systems and kernel methods.
*   Practical inclusion of POD-based robustification, addressing a potential implementation issue.
*   Comprehensive related work section demonstrating awareness of the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical evaluation and lack of quantitative results.
*   Unclear advantages over existing kernel-based DMD variants.
*   Somewhat dense and disorganized presentation.
*   Lack of detailed implementation information hindering reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

I assess this paper as **weak reject**. While the idea is interesting and the technical foundation is solid, the lack of compelling empirical evidence and clear differentiation from existing methods prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The authors have identified a promising research direction, but further work is needed to demonstrate the practical benefits and broader impact of their approach. 

**Recommendations for improvement:**

*   **Expand the empirical evaluation:**  Include more challenging datasets and compare performance against state-of-the-art methods. Provide quantitative results with appropriate performance metrics.
*   **Clarify the advantages:**  Specifically address how the proposed method differs from and improves upon existing kernel-based DMD techniques.
*   **Improve clarity and organization:**  Streamline the mathematical notation and reorganize the paper for better readability.
*   **Provide implementation details:**  Include more detailed information about the implementation, including kernel parameter selection and POD truncation criteria.
*   **Investigate alternative robustification techniques:** Explore regularization strategies or other orthogonalization methods beyond POD-based projection.
*   **Thoroughly evaluate applications:** Provide robust evaluation of the proposed applications (prediction, change-point detection, dynamic recognition) with clearly defined metrics and comparisons.
