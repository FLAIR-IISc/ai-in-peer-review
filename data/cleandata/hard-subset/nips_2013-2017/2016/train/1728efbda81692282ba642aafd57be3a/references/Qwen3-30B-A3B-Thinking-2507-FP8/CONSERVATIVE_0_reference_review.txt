

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors claim their method, called KDMD, provides a more accurate approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions by using nonlinear basis functions derived from kernel methods, as opposed to the linear monomials used in traditional DMD. The paper presents a mathematical framework for performing modal decomposition in RKHS, with a robustified variant using POD bases for improved numerical stability. The authors demonstrate their approach on synthetic data, the Hénon map, and a locomotion dataset, with applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a mathematically sound contribution to the theoretical foundations of Koopman spectral analysis. The extension of DMD to RKHS is clearly articulated, with rigorous proofs of the key theorems (Theorems 1 and 2). The robustification via POD bases is well-motivated by the known numerical issues with standard DMD. The mathematical framework is well-organized and builds logically on existing work in Koopman theory and DMD.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the mathematical soundness, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in empirical validation and comparative evaluation that undermine its claim of novelty and practical value.

1. **Lack of comprehensive empirical comparison:** The paper fails to compare KDMD against established baselines in the field. The only comparison shown is with "Equilibrium DMD" in Figure 1, which is not a standard baseline. The paper cites Williams et al.'s "extended DMD" as a related method but does not provide a direct comparison between KDMD and extended DMD, which appears to have similar goals. Without such comparison, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed kernel-based approach actually provides meaningful improvements over existing methods.

2. **Limited empirical validation:** The empirical examples are limited to simple synthetic data and the Hénon map. The locomotion dataset application (Figure 3) is presented without quantitative comparison to standard approaches for motion recognition. The prediction results (Figure 2) show visual comparisons but lack quantitative metrics to assess the quality of predictions. The change-point detection application (Figure 4) shows a qualitative comparison with a kernel change detection method but doesn't provide statistical significance or performance metrics.

3. **No demonstration of practical advantages:** The paper claims KDMD provides "a more accurate approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions" but doesn't provide evidence that this leads to better performance in practical applications. For instance, the prediction results don't compare the prediction error of KDMD against standard DMD or other time series forecasting methods. The paper doesn't address computational complexity or scalability, which are critical for practical adoption.

4. **Questionable novelty:** The core idea of using kernel methods for Koopman analysis appears to be a natural extension of existing work, particularly Williams et al.'s extended DMD. The authors acknowledge this connection but don't clearly articulate how KDMD differs in a meaningful way from these existing approaches. The mathematical formulation is novel, but the practical contribution appears incremental.

5. **Lack of discussion on limitations:** The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the proposed method. For instance, it doesn't discuss how the choice of kernel affects performance, the computational burden of kernel operations, or when this approach would be preferable to simpler alternatives.

**Overall assessment:**

While the paper presents a mathematically sound extension of DMD to RKHS, it falls short of demonstrating the practical significance of this extension. The field of Koopman spectral analysis is already populated with several variants of DMD (including Williams et al.'s extended DMD), and this paper doesn't convincingly show that KDMD provides meaningful improvements over these established methods. The lack of comprehensive empirical validation against relevant baselines is particularly problematic for a paper making claims about improved approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. A thorough comparison against established baselines, particularly Williams et al.'s extended DMD
2. Quantitative metrics for all empirical results
3. A clear demonstration of when KDMD would be preferred over simpler alternatives
4. Discussion of computational complexity and scalability
5. More detailed analysis of the impact of kernel choice on performance

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting extension of DMD to RKHS, but the lack of rigorous empirical validation against established baselines and failure to demonstrate clear practical advantages prevent it from making a compelling contribution to the field. The method appears to be a natural mathematical extension rather than a substantial improvement with demonstrable benefits for practitioners. Without evidence of meaningful improvements over existing approaches, the paper does not meet the high standards expected for publication at NIPS.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**
- Pro: Mathematically sound extension of DMD to RKHS with clear theoretical framework
- Pro: Well-organized presentation of mathematical derivations
- Con: Lacks comprehensive empirical comparison with established baselines
- Con: No quantitative evidence of practical advantages over existing methods
- Con: Fails to demonstrate when this approach would be preferable to simpler alternatives
- Con: Limited empirical validation on synthetic and simple real-world examples

Given the current state of the manuscript, I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence of practical significance and lack of meaningful comparison to established methods. The paper would be suitable for publication in a more specialized journal focused on dynamical systems, but not for NIPS where we expect strong empirical validation of novel methods.
