Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to perform Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). Two algorithms are presented: (i) a direct approach that constructs a Krylov subspace in the feature space, performs a Gram–Schmidt QR factorization, and computes the eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator; and (ii) a “robustified” variant that projects data onto kernel principal components (kernel PCA) before eigendecomposition, analogous to projecting onto POD/SVD modes in standard DMD to improve conditioning.

The paper states two main theoretical results. Theorem 1 essentially lifts the standard Arnoldi/DMD expansion to the RKHS setting, giving a finite-sum expansion of feature vectors in terms of empirical Ritz values/vectors in RKHS. Theorem 2 shows that, under the assumption that Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the span of the kernel principal directions, the eigenvectors of the reduced operator coincide with Koopman eigenfunctions, and the dynamics admit a linear expansion in these modes.

Beyond the core algorithm, the paper sketches applications: short-term prediction via preimage recovery (using an MDS-inspired reconstruction), embedding/recognition of dynamic patterns via kernel principal angles between subspaces spanned by estimated Koopman eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-style statistic derived from an RKHS exponential-family model. Small demonstrations on a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, motion-capture sequences, and a Lorenz-map change-point scenario are shown.

This work sits in a well-established line of research connecting DMD and Koopman operator theory. In particular, Extended DMD (EDMD) and kernel DMD (KDMD) provide closely related formulations in which one uses a dictionary of observables or an implicit dictionary via kernels to approximate the Koopman operator. The submitted manuscript cites EDMD but does not clearly differentiate from KDMD, which uses the kernel trick to avoid explicit construction of large dictionaries and computes a low-rank approximation of the Koopman operator from Gram matrices. The robustification via kernel PCA is analogous to the truncated-SVD/POD projection used widely in exact/optimized DMD.

Strengths:
- Clear high-level objective and connection to Koopman spectral analysis. The RKHS formulation is natural and well motivated.
- Algorithmic steps for both the direct and robustified variants are explicit and implementable via Gram matrices, leveraging the kernel trick.
- Theorem 2 articulates conditions under which the reduced operator’s eigenvectors correspond to Koopman eigenfunctions, helping to clarify when kernel PCA projection suffices.
- Practical considerations (ill-conditioning and truncation) are addressed by projecting onto dominant kernel principal components.
- The applications section illustrates potential uses beyond pure spectral analysis.

Weaknesses:
- Originality: The core contribution largely overlaps with EDMD/KDMD. The direct RKHS-Arnoldi/QR viewpoint is a reformulation of KDMD; the robustified variant via kernel PCA mirrors the standard low-rank projections used in DMD variants. The paper does not convincingly articulate a theoretical or computational advantage over KDMD, nor does it provide empirical comparisons.
- Quality/technical details: 
  - Theorem 1 is essentially a restatement of standard DMD in RKHS; its novelty is limited.
  - Theorem 2 assumes Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the span of kernel PCs; this is strong and problem-dependent. The proof sketch uses Ḡ−1, which is undefined without truncation/pseudoinverse; the algorithm itself avoids this via S̄−1/2. Clarifying assumptions, matrix invertibility, and consistency would improve rigor.
  - The statement “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is not generally correct; this holds for measure-preserving systems, while dissipative systems can have eigenvalues inside the unit disk.
  - The preimage reconstruction method is only briefly described and appears ad hoc; stability, parameter choices (e.g., neighbor count), and sensitivity are not analyzed.
  - The change-point detection derivation is heuristic; assumptions on the stochastic extension of the Koopman operator and the approximation that drops terms in the likelihood ratio need justification and empirical validation.
- Clarity: There are numerous typos and grammatical errors, ambiguous notation (e.g., ϕc definition, mixing ϕ/φ), and incomplete/inconsistent references. The exposition would benefit from tighter organization and careful editing.
- Significance/empirics: Experiments are small-scale, largely qualitative, and lack quantitative metrics, statistical analysis, or comparisons to baselines (EDMD, KDMD, exact/optimized DMD, GPDM/GPSSM for prediction, established change detection methods). No ablations of kernel choice or truncation level are provided; scalability and computational complexity are not discussed.
- Double-blind policy: A code link revealing author identity violates anonymity.

Questions for the authors:
- What is the precise theoretical and algorithmic difference from KDMD? Can you prove any advantage (e.g., consistency, bias/variance, conditioning) or demonstrate superior empirical performance?
- Under what conditions do the empirical eigenvalues/eigenfunctions converge to the true Koopman spectrum in your RKHS formulation?
- How are kernel and bandwidth selected? How sensitive are spectra and downstream tasks to these choices and to truncation rank?
- Can you formalize the preimage procedure (objective, optimization, guarantees) and quantify prediction error against EDMD/KDMD and GP-based baselines?
- What is the computational complexity and memory footprint as a function of trajectory length and kernel rank? Is there an incremental/streaming version?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clearly position the work relative to EDMD and KDMD; add direct empirical comparisons and highlight any distinctive contributions.
- Provide quantitative evaluations (eigenvalue recovery error, prediction accuracy, classification accuracy for dynamics recognition, detection delay/false alarm rates for change detection) with baselines and sensitivity analyses.
- Tighten the theory: replace informal inverses with pseudoinverses, state assumptions explicitly, and correct the attractor/eigenvalue claim.
- Improve clarity/notation and fix references (complete bibliographic details). Remove any de-anonymizing information for compliance.
- Discuss scalability and provide guidance on kernel and truncation choices.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, intuitive RKHS formulation of DMD/Koopman analysis with practical implementation details.
- Potentially useful consolidation of ideas (kernelization, POD truncation) for practitioners interested in Koopman-based feature extraction and applications.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Overlap with established EDMD/KDMD with limited novelty.
- Insufficient empirical validation and lack of comparisons to strong baselines.
- Theoretical exposition contains strong assumptions and some inaccuracies.
- Clarity, reference quality, and anonymization issues.

Recommendation:
While the topic is relevant to NIPS and the high-level approach is sound, the contribution appears incremental relative to KDMD/EDMD, and the experimental and theoretical support is currently insufficient. I recommend rejection in its current form, with the suggestion to strengthen novelty, rigor, comparisons, and presentation.
