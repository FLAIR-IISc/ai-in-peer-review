

# Peer Review: Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a novel extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) that incorporates reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to provide a more expressive approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions. The authors address a key limitation of traditional DMD, which uses linear monomials of observables as basis functions (analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion), by instead using kernel functions to create nonlinear basis functions. The core idea is to perform spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in RKHS, where the Koopman operator is defined on the feature map ϕ(x) rather than the original state space. The authors present a modal decomposition algorithm based on the equivalent principle of DMD, which involves calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. They also provide a robust version of the algorithm that uses POD (Principal Orthogonal Directions) to avoid ill-conditioned implementations.

This work relates to several important lines of research in the field. It builds upon the Koopman operator framework for nonlinear dynamics analysis (Mezić, 2005; Rowley et al., 2009), extends the DMD algorithm (Schmid, 2010; Tu et al., 2014), and connects with kernel methods (Schölkopf et al., 1998; Shawe-Taylor & Cristianini, 2004). The paper also relates to the growing body of work on kernel-based methods for dynamical systems analysis (e.g., Williams et al., 2015 on extended DMD, Kawahara et al., 2009 on kernel subspace methods).

## Quality Assessment

**Strengths:**
1. The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHS, with clear mathematical derivations and proofs (Theorems 1 and 2) that establish the connection between the proposed method and Koopman spectral analysis.
2. The authors demonstrate a practical implementation that addresses a known issue with traditional DMD (ill-conditioned algorithms) through the use of POD bases, which is a well-known technique in the DMD literature.
3. The empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real-world data is comprehensive, including a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, and applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.
4. The paper provides a clear connection between the theoretical framework and practical applications, showing how the extracted features can be used in meaningful ways.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper lacks a thorough comparison with related kernel-based methods for dynamical systems analysis. While the authors cite Williams et al. (2015) on extended DMD, they don't explicitly compare their method with other kernel-based approaches like Kawahara et al. (2009) or the kernel-based spectral clustering methods for dynamics.
2. The computational complexity analysis is missing. The proposed method involves kernel computations and eigendecomposition of matrices with size τ×τ (where τ is the number of data points), which can become computationally expensive for large datasets. The paper doesn't discuss scalability or provide runtime comparisons with traditional DMD or other methods.
3. The robustness of the method to noise in the data is not thoroughly evaluated. The empirical examples use clean data, but real-world applications often involve noisy measurements, and the paper doesn't discuss how the method handles this.
4. The paper claims "the Koopman operator defined in Eq. (7) is identical to the transition operator on an observable" but doesn't provide sufficient justification for this statement in the context of the kernel feature map.

## Clarity Assessment

**Strengths:**
1. The paper is generally well-organized, with clear section headings that guide the reader through the theoretical development, algorithm description, and applications.
2. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout the paper.
3. The empirical examples are well-presented with appropriate visualizations (Figures 1-4).

**Weaknesses:**
1. Some sections, particularly Section 3, are dense with mathematical notation without sufficient intuitive explanation for readers unfamiliar with RKHS or Koopman operator theory. For example, the connection between the kernel feature map and the Koopman operator could be made clearer.
2. The algorithm description in Section 4 (robustification with POD bases) is somewhat technical and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why POD bases help with the ill-conditioning problem.
3. The paper could better explain the practical considerations for choosing kernel parameters (e.g., polynomial degree for the toy system, Gaussian kernel width for Hénon map) and how these choices affect the results.

## Originality Assessment

The paper makes a clear contribution by extending DMD to RKHS, which allows for more expressive nonlinear basis functions. This is a meaningful extension beyond traditional DMD, which is limited to linear monomials. The authors correctly identify this as a limitation of existing DMD approaches, stating "this is certainly not applicable to all systems (in particular, beyond the region of validity for local linearization)."

However, the originality is somewhat limited by the fact that:
1. The idea of using kernel methods for dynamical systems analysis has been explored before (e.g., Kawahara et al., 2009; Williams et al., 2015).
2. The connection between DMD and kernel methods is not entirely novel - Williams et al. (2015) on extended DMD already works with pre-determined basis functions, which could include kernel-based features.

That said, the specific integration of RKHS with the Koopman operator framework and the proposed robustification method using POD bases represent a novel contribution. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from previous work, particularly in Section 5 (Related Works), where it discusses the relationship with extended DMD and other methods.

## Significance Assessment

**Strengths:**
1. The paper addresses an important limitation of existing DMD methods, which is the restriction to linear basis functions. This limitation is well-known in the DMD community, and the proposed method could potentially provide more accurate spectral analysis for a wider range of nonlinear systems.
2. The applications demonstrated (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) are practical and relevant to real-world problems in engineering and science.
3. The paper provides a theoretical foundation for using kernel methods in Koopman spectral analysis, which could inspire further research in this direction.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The significance of the improvement over existing methods is not clearly demonstrated. While the paper shows that the method works well on the examples provided, it doesn't show quantitatively how much better the kernel DMD is compared to traditional DMD or other kernel-based methods on standard benchmarks.
2. The paper doesn't clearly articulate why the RKHS approach is necessary for the applications presented. For example, in the dynamics recognition task (Figure 3), it's not clear that the kernel approach provides a significant advantage over simpler methods like PCA or traditional DMD.
3. The paper doesn't discuss potential limitations or when the kernel DMD might not be appropriate, which would help practitioners understand the scope of the method.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD that addresses a known limitation of existing methods.
- The mathematical derivations are correct and well-supported.
- The empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world data.
- The applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition are relevant and well-executed.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with related kernel-based methods for dynamical systems analysis.
- The computational complexity and scalability of the method are not discussed.
- The significance of the method over existing approaches is not clearly demonstrated with quantitative comparisons.
- The paper doesn't adequately address how the method handles noisy data, which is a practical concern for real-world applications.

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a theoretically interesting extension of DMD using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces. While the core idea is not entirely novel, the specific integration with Koopman spectral analysis and the robustification using POD bases represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and shows promise for practical applications.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses in terms of comparing with related work, demonstrating the method's significance over existing approaches, and addressing practical considerations like computational complexity and robustness to noise. These weaknesses prevent the paper from being a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS, despite its theoretical soundness.

## Specific Recommendations for Authors

1. Include a thorough comparison with related kernel-based methods for dynamical systems analysis (e.g., Kawahara et al., 2009; Williams et al., 2015) in both the theoretical and empirical sections.

2. Add a section discussing computational complexity and scalability, including runtime comparisons with traditional DMD and other methods.

3. Provide more detailed evaluation of the method's performance on noisy data, which is crucial for real-world applications.

4. Clarify the connection between the kernel feature map and the Koopman operator in Section 3, particularly the claim that "the Koopman operator defined in Eq. (7) is identical to the transition operator on an observable."

5. In the applications section, provide quantitative comparisons showing how much better the kernel DMD performs compared to traditional DMD or other methods on the same tasks.

6. Consider adding a discussion of when the kernel DMD might not be appropriate or when simpler methods would suffice, to help practitioners understand the scope of the method.

Overall, with these revisions, the paper would be a strong candidate for NIPS. As it stands, it has a solid theoretical foundation but needs stronger empirical justification for its significance and practical utility.
