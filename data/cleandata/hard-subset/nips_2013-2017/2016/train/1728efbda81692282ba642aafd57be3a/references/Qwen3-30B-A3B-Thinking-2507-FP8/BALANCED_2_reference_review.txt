

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary
This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to better approximate the Koopman operator for nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors extend traditional DMD, which uses linear monomials as basis functions, to employ kernel functions for a more expressive nonlinear approximation. The theoretical framework builds on Koopman operator theory and DMD, with a robust implementation using POD (Proper Orthogonal Decomposition) bases to address numerical instability. The paper demonstrates applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical development, including well-structured theorems and proofs establishing the connection between the proposed method and Koopman spectral analysis. The extension of DMD to RKHS is mathematically sound and addresses a clear limitation of traditional DMD.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors recognize and address the numerical instability issue common in DMD implementations by introducing a POD-based robustification step. This practical consideration significantly enhances the method's applicability.

3. **Relevant Applications**: The paper demonstrates meaningful applications of the method in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The locomotion data example (Fig. 3) is particularly compelling, showing how the extracted features can be used for meaningful pattern recognition.

4. **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with a logical flow from theoretical development to empirical demonstration. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, making the theoretical contributions accessible to readers familiar with the field.

## Weaknesses
1. **Novelty Clarification**: The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution from existing kernel-based Koopman methods. The connection to Williams et al.'s extended DMD (2015) is acknowledged, but the paper fails to clearly articulate how this work differs from or improves upon prior kernel-based Koopman approaches (e.g., Suzuki & Mezić, 2016; Kawahara et al., 2013).

2. **Lack of Comparative Analysis**: The empirical evaluation lacks comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods. While the paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on specific examples, it does not provide quantitative comparisons with alternative approaches (e.g., extended DMD, kernel-based Koopman methods) across multiple datasets or metrics.

3. **Computational Complexity**: The paper does not address the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to existing approaches. Given the use of kernel methods, the computational cost could be substantial for large datasets, which is a critical consideration for practical applications.

4. **Theoretical Limitations**: The theoretical development assumes data is sufficiently rich and the Krylov subspace is sufficiently large. The paper does not discuss how these assumptions might fail in practice or provide guidelines for determining when the method will be applicable.

## Recommendations for Improvement
1. **Clarify Novelty**: The authors should explicitly compare their approach with recent kernel-based Koopman methods (e.g., Suzuki & Mezić, 2016; Kawahara et al., 2013) and clearly articulate the theoretical and practical advantages of their specific approach.

2. **Add Comparative Experiments**: Include quantitative comparisons with relevant baseline methods on multiple datasets. Metrics such as prediction error, computational time, and robustness to noise would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

3. **Address Computational Considerations**: Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method and provide insights into how it scales with dataset size. Consider discussing potential approximations or optimizations for large-scale applications.

4. **Discuss Limitations**: Acknowledge the assumptions made in the theoretical development (e.g., sufficient data richness) and discuss scenarios where the method might fail or require modification.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHS with practical implementation considerations. The method addresses a meaningful limitation of traditional DMD by using nonlinear basis functions. However, the paper would benefit from stronger differentiation from prior work, more comprehensive empirical validation against existing methods, and clearer discussion of computational considerations.

While the theoretical contribution is solid and the applications are relevant, the lack of comparative analysis and insufficient novelty articulation limit the paper's significance. The method has potential for impact in fields studying dynamical systems, but this potential is not fully demonstrated in the current form.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the Koopman operator literature and offers a practical extension of DMD. However, the insufficient differentiation from prior work and lack of comparative empirical analysis prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. With the suggested improvements (particularly adding comparative experiments and clarifying novelty), this would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
