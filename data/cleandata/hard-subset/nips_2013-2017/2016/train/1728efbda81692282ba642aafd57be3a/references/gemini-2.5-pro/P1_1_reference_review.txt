### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a method for spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to approximate the eigenfunctions and eigenvalues of the Koopman operator, a linear operator that governs the evolution of observables of a nonlinear system. While standard DMD approximates these eigenfunctions using linear combinations of the observed states, this work leverages the kernel trick to implicitly work with a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions defined by the RKHS.

The authors first formulate a direct "kernel DMD" algorithm based on performing a Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition of the Krylov matrix in the feature space. Recognizing that this approach can be numerically unstable (a known issue in standard DMD), they then propose a more robust algorithm. This robust version projects the Koopman operator onto a lower-dimensional subspace spanned by the principal components in the feature space, which is equivalent to performing kernel PCA on the time-series data. The paper establishes the theoretical connection between the eigenvalues/eigenvectors of this projected operator and the true Koopman spectrum. Finally, the authors demonstrate the utility of the extracted Koopman modes as features for several downstream tasks: short-term prediction (via a pre-image reconstruction), embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection.

This work builds upon the growing literature on DMD and Koopman operator theory for data-driven analysis of dynamical systems. It is closely related to "Extended DMD" (Williams et al., 2015), which generalizes DMD to user-defined dictionaries of observable functions. The key contribution here is the use of kernels to provide a non-parametric and powerful way of defining this dictionary, avoiding the need for manual feature engineering.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Extension:** The combination of Koopman theory, DMD, and kernel methods is a natural and powerful one. The paper provides a principled way to move beyond the linear-observable limitation of basic DMD, enabling the analysis of a much broader class of nonlinear systems without explicit linearization or manual selection of basis functions.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical development is clear and appears correct. The paper properly motivates the need for the robust version of the algorithm (Sec 4) by acknowledging the potential ill-conditioning of the direct approach (Sec 3). The connection between the computed modes and the true Koopman eigenfunctions is established in Theorem 2, under reasonable assumptions about the richness of the data.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. It provides a concise background on Koopman operators and DMD, clearly develops its own contribution, and then illustrates the method's potential through a series of diverse applications. The flow from theory to practice is logical.

4.  **Demonstrated Versatility:** The applications section (Sec 7) is a significant strength. It shows that the learned Koopman eigenfunctions are not just for analysis but can serve as versatile features for prediction, classification, and change-point detection. This breadth significantly increases the potential impact of the work. The embedding of motion capture data is particularly compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper mentions Extended DMD (EDMD) but could benefit from a more detailed comparison. The proposed method can be viewed as an instance of EDMD where the dictionary of observables is the feature space of an RKHS. A discussion of the practical trade-offs would be valuable. For instance, how does the computational cost of forming and decomposing a Gram matrix compare to working with an explicit, finite dictionary in EDMD? What are the advantages of an implicit, infinite-dimensional basis beyond convenience?

2.  **Experimental Rigor:** While the experiments are illustrative and compelling, they lack quantitative comparisons against strong baselines.
    *   **Prediction:** The prediction task is only evaluated visually. How does it compare to standard nonlinear time-series models like Gaussian Process Dynamical Models or LSTMs?
    *   **Change-point Detection:** The comparison is made against a single, vaguely referenced "kernel change detection method" ([7]), and the evaluation is again visual on a single dataset. A more rigorous comparison on benchmark datasets would strengthen this claim.
    *   **Dynamics Recognition:** The MDS embedding is visually convincing, but a quantitative classification accuracy metric (e.g., leave-one-out classification error) would make the result more concrete.

3.  **Practical Considerations:** The paper does not discuss some important practical aspects of the method.
    *   **Scalability:** The method requires building and computing the eigendecomposition of Gram matrices of size τ × τ, where τ is the number of data points. This has a computational complexity of roughly O(τ³), which can be prohibitive for long time series. A discussion of this limitation and potential approximation methods (e.g., Nyström) would be welcome.
    *   **Hyperparameter Selection:** As with all kernel methods, the choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., the width of a Gaussian kernel) is crucial. The paper provides little guidance on this, stating only the kernels used for each experiment. A discussion of sensitivity to these choices or a cross-validation strategy would improve the paper's practical utility. The choice of the truncation dimension `p` in the robust method also warrants discussion.

### Overall Evaluation and Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that presents a solid, well-motivated, and elegant contribution to the field of learning dynamical systems. The fusion of DMD with kernel methods is a valuable idea that is both theoretically sound and practically useful. The clarity of the presentation and the breadth of applications are commendable.

The main weaknesses lie in the experimental evaluation, which could be more rigorous and quantitative, and in the lack of discussion on practical issues like scalability and hyperparameter selection. The connection to Extended DMD could also be elaborated upon more clearly.

Despite these points, the strengths of the paper—its novelty, technical quality, and demonstrated potential—are significant. The proposed "kernel DMD" is a valuable tool that researchers and practitioners are likely to use and build upon. The work clearly advances the state of the art in data-driven analysis of nonlinear dynamics.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and principled combination of Koopman theory, DMD, and kernel methods.
*   Technically sound development with a robust algorithm variant.
*   Clearly written and well-organized manuscript.
*   Convincing demonstrations of the method's versatility across several important applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Experimental comparisons to strong, alternative baselines are missing.
*   Practical limitations regarding scalability and hyperparameter tuning are not discussed.
*   The relationship and novelty with respect to Extended DMD could be better articulated.
