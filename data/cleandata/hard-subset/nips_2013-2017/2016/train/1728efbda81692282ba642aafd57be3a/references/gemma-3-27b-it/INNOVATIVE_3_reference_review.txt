This paper presents a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), aiming to improve the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions beyond the limitations of linear monomial basis functions used in standard DMD. The core idea – leveraging the expressiveness of RKHS to capture more complex dynamics – is compelling and potentially significant. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of spectral methods for dynamical systems, relating it to POD, DMD, and Koopman theory.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of reproducing kernels to DMD is a genuinely novel contribution. While kernel methods have been used in related areas like kernel PCA, extending them directly to the DMD framework, and framing it as approximating Koopman eigenfunctions in RKHS, represents a meaningful advance.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide a solid mathematical foundation for their approach, deriving theorems that connect the kernelized DMD to the standard DMD framework and the Koopman operator. The connection to the Krylov subspace and Ritz values is clearly articulated.
* **Practical Considerations:** Recognizing the potential for ill-conditioning, the authors introduce a robustification technique using POD, drawing a parallel to existing approaches in DMD. This demonstrates awareness of practical challenges.
* **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of experiments on both a toy system and the Henon map provides initial validation of the proposed method. While limited, these results are encouraging.
* **Potential Impact:** Successful kernelized DMD could unlock the ability to analyze and predict dynamics in a wider range of nonlinear systems where linear approximations are insufficient. The applications section (prediction, change detection, dynamics recognition) hints at the broader applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited. The toy system is simple, and the Henon map, while chaotic, is still a relatively well-studied benchmark. More complex, real-world datasets would be necessary to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the kernelized approach.
* **Kernel Selection:** The choice of kernel (polynomial, Gaussian) appears somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic investigation of different kernel parameters and their impact on performance would strengthen the results. There's no discussion of kernel selection strategies or sensitivity analysis.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't thoroughly address the computational complexity of the kernelized DMD, particularly concerning the Gram matrix calculations.  This could be a significant bottleneck for large datasets.
* **POD Robustification:** The reason *why* POD projections improve stability is hinted at, but not fully explained. A more detailed discussion of the interplay between the RKHS representation and the POD projection would be beneficial.
* **Applications Section:** The applications section feels somewhat cursory. The descriptions are brief, and the empirical results aren’t deeply analyzed. A more thorough exploration of these applications would bolster the paper's impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is genuinely novel and represents a potentially important step forward in DMD and Koopman spectral analysis.
* The theoretical foundation is sound and the derivation is clear.
* The authors address a practical issue (ill-conditioning) with a reasonable solution.
* The initial empirical results are encouraging, suggesting the approach is viable.
* The paper's potential impact on the analysis and prediction of nonlinear systems is significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical validation is limited, and the lack of more complex datasets raises concerns about generalizability.
* The computational cost of the method is not thoroughly addressed.
* The application section could be significantly strengthened with more in-depth analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, combined with the solid theoretical foundation, outweighs the limitations in empirical validation and computational analysis. While a more thorough investigation of these areas would be desirable, the paper introduces a significant new concept that warrants acceptance. I would rate this paper as **Accept with Minor Revisions**, primarily requesting a more extensive empirical evaluation with more complex datasets, and a discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method. A more detailed analysis of the application section, with clear performance metrics, is also recommended. The authors should also address the kernel selection process.
