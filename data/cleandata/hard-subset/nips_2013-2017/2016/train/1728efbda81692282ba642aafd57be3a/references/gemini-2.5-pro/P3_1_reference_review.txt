**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems, called Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels (k-DMD). The core idea is to generalize the well-known Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) algorithm by lifting the system's state space into a high-dimensional feature space (an RKHS) using the kernel trick. Standard DMD approximates the eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator using linear observables. In contrast, k-DMD approximates them using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions implicitly defined by the kernel, allowing it to capture more complex dynamics. The authors first derive a principled algorithm based on a Gram-Schmidt process in the RKHS. Recognizing its potential numerical instability, they then propose a more robust variant based on projecting the dynamics onto kernel principal components (i.e., performing DMD in the feature space of kernel PCA). The paper demonstrates the method on toy systems and showcases its utility as a feature extraction tool for applications like short-term prediction, dynamic pattern recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a highly elegant and significant synthesis of three powerful concepts: Koopman operator theory for linearizing nonlinear dynamics, the data-driven DMD framework, and kernel methods from machine learning. This combination is a natural and potent extension of DMD. It moves beyond the limitations of linear observables (or pre-specified dictionaries in Extended DMD) by implicitly using the infinite-dimensional feature map of a kernel as the observable dictionary. This makes a powerful physics-based analysis tool much more accessible and applicable to the machine learning community, where kernel methods are a standard tool. The work clearly advances the state of the art in data-driven analysis of dynamical systems.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical development is solid and well-founded. The paper correctly formulates the Koopman operator in the RKHS and establishes the connection to DMD via the Krylov subspace in Theorem 1. The robustification procedure in Section 4 is crucial for any practical application and is well-justified by Theorem 2, which connects the eigenvectors of the projected operator to the Koopman eigenfunctions. The formulation of the projected Koopman operator `F̂` in Equation (11) entirely in terms of Gram matrices is the key step that makes the kernel trick applicable and is correctly derived.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with a clear introduction to the Koopman operator and DMD, making the work accessible to readers not deeply familiar with the fluid dynamics literature. It then naturally builds up to the proposed k-DMD method, first presenting the direct (but unstable) formulation and then the practical, robustified version. The connections to prior work are clearly articulated.

4.  **Potential for Impact:** The proposed k-DMD method provides a principled way to extract "dynamic features" that characterize the temporal evolution of a system. The demonstrated applications are compelling. In particular, the use of the learned Koopman eigenfunctions for embedding and recognition of dynamics (Fig. 3) and for change-point detection (Fig. 4) are innovative and highlight the method's potential beyond simple system identification or prediction. The change-point detection application is especially interesting, as it is designed to be sensitive to changes in dynamics rather than just the state's distribution.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Relationship to Extended DMD (e-DMD):** The paper correctly cites Williams et al. [35] on e-DMD, which generalizes DMD to a user-defined dictionary of observables. The authors note the similarity but could strengthen the paper by discussing this connection in more detail. In essence, k-DMD can be viewed as a specific instance of e-DMD where the dictionary is the (potentially infinite) set of basis functions spanning the RKHS. This is a powerful feature, as it automates the difficult process of dictionary selection, replacing it with the more familiar problem of kernel selection and hyperparameter tuning. A more explicit discussion of this trade-off would better position the work.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments are illustrative, they lack quantitative rigor and comparisons to strong baselines.
    *   For the Hénon map, the eigenvalues of the equilibrium points are known. The paper states they "seem to be approximately estimated," but a direct quantitative comparison between the true and estimated eigenvalues would be much more convincing.
    *   The application sections lack baselines. For prediction, how does k-DMD compare against other nonlinear time-series models like Gaussian Process Dynamical Models? For the recognition task, how does the embedding compare to one based on, for example, time-delay embedding followed by PCA? For change-point detection, the comparison with a kernel-based two-sample test is good, but comparisons against other modern change-point methods would strengthen the claim of superiority.

3.  **The Preimage Problem:** The prediction application relies on finding a pre-image of a point in the RKHS. This is a notoriously ill-posed and difficult problem. The paper mentions a method based on MDS but provides few details. While a full treatise on the topic is not expected, the authors should be more forthright about the challenges and limitations of this step, as it can be a significant practical hurdle. The quality of the prediction is highly dependent on the quality of the pre-image reconstruction.

4.  **Justification of Approximations:** The derivation for the change-point detection score includes a key simplification where a term is ignored because its "variation is much smaller." While plausible, this assumption would be stronger if supported by either a brief theoretical argument or an empirical validation showing that the simplified score correlates well with the full log-likelihood ratio.

**Final Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear, novel, and significant contribution. It elegantly bridges the gap between the physics-based DMD community and the machine learning community. The proposed method, k-DMD, is a powerful and natural extension of an important algorithm. The theoretical foundations are sound, and the paper is well-written. While the experimental evaluation could be more thorough, the provided examples and applications convincingly demonstrate the method's potential. This work is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up research on kernel-based methods for dynamical systems analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and significant synthesis of Koopman theory, DMD, and kernel methods.
*   Technically sound with solid theoretical justification.
*   Addresses a key limitation of standard DMD (reliance on linear observables).
*   Demonstrates compelling applications beyond simple prediction, especially in dynamics recognition and change-point detection.
*   Well-written, clear, and likely to have high impact.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Experimental validation lacks quantitative rigor and competitive baselines.
*   The connection and comparison to the closely related Extended DMD could be more detailed.
*   Practical difficulties (e.g., the preimage problem) are somewhat downplayed.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This is a strong paper suitable for publication at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept**
