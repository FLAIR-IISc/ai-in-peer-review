

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors argue that standard DMD, which uses linear monomials as basis functions, can be improved by using kernel functions to provide richer nonlinear basis functions. The paper develops a theoretical framework for this approach, including a modal decomposition algorithm based on the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in RKHS, and provides a robustified version using POD (Principal Orthogonal Decomposition) bases. The authors demonstrate the method on synthetic data (a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map) and apply it to three applications: state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with solid theoretical foundations. The mathematical derivations are rigorous, and the connection between the proposed method and standard DMD is well-explained. The robustification approach using POD bases is reasonable and aligns with established practices for stabilizing DMD implementations.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's primary weakness is the lack of thorough empirical validation against established baselines. While the authors present results on toy systems, they fail to demonstrate that their method outperforms standard DMD or Extended DMD (which the authors cite as related work) in any meaningful way. Extended DMD [35] already works with pre-determined basis functions, including kernel-based ones, and the paper does not clearly distinguish its approach from this existing method. The authors claim that their kernel-based approach provides "richer basis functions" than linear monomials, but provide no evidence that this leads to better performance in practice.

The empirical examples are limited to two simple synthetic systems, with no comparison to established methods on real-world datasets where the method might actually be useful. The applications section (prediction, change detection, dynamics recognition) is superficial and fails to demonstrate any clear advantage of the kernel-based approach over simpler methods. For instance, the prediction example uses the same toy system as in the empirical section, with no comparison to standard DMD or other prediction methods.

The paper also neglects to address critical practical considerations for kernel methods: computational complexity (kernel methods scale poorly with dataset size), the challenge of choosing appropriate kernels for specific dynamical systems, and the risk of overfitting. The authors claim that "the proposed algorithm provides a modal decomposition of the dynamics into a finite number of modes," but they don't demonstrate how this decomposition leads to better interpretability or performance compared to existing approaches.

The significance claim is overstated. The authors suggest their method could be useful for "state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition," but provide no evidence that it outperforms simpler, established methods in these applications. The paper lacks the thorough empirical validation required to demonstrate that the proposed method offers a meaningful improvement over existing techniques.

**Comparison to Related Work:** The paper mentions Extended DMD [35] as a related method, but fails to properly contrast their approach with it. Extended DMD works with pre-determined basis functions, which could include kernel-based functions, making the distinction between the two methods unclear. The authors also cite "kernel change detection" as a comparative method in their applications section, but don't provide a rigorous comparison of performance.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the theoretical framework is sound, the paper lacks the extensive empirical validation required to support its claims of improvement over established methods. The proposed method appears to be a straightforward extension of existing techniques without clear evidence of significant advantages in practice. The paper would need substantial additional empirical work, including comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods on diverse datasets, to justify its novelty and significance for NIPS. As it stands, the paper presents an interesting theoretical idea but fails to provide sufficient evidence that it offers practical benefits over existing approaches. The lack of thorough empirical validation against established baselines is particularly concerning, given the paper's claim of "improved" performance.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by: (1) comprehensive empirical comparisons with standard DMD, Extended DMD, and other relevant methods on diverse datasets; (2) a clear demonstration of the practical advantages of kernel-based approaches over linear ones in real-world scenarios; (3) discussion of computational complexity and scalability; and (4) more detailed analysis of when kernel-based approaches would be preferable to linear ones. Without these, the paper fails to meet NIPS' standards for empirical validation of new methods.
