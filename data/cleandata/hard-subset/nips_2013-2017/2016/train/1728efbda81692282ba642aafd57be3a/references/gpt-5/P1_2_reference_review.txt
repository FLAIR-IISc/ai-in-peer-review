Summary
The paper proposes a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) framework to approximate Koopman spectral objects for nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to lift observations into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), form a Krylov subspace using feature snapshots, and estimate empirical Ritz values/vectors via the eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. The authors first give a Gram–Schmidt/Arnoldi-style derivation in RKHS (Theorem 1), then “robustify” it by projecting onto kernel-PCA (POD) directions to mitigate ill-conditioning (Theorem 2). They argue the method recovers Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions if the feature-span is rich and the subspace is large. They illustrate on a toy quadratic system and the Hénon map, and sketch applications to short-term prediction via preimage reconstruction, dynamic pattern recognition via kernel principal angles between Koopman subspaces, and sequential change-point detection via a CUSUM criterion in a kernel exponential-family model.

Relation to prior work
The paper is closely related to extended DMD (EDMD) and its kernelized variants (KDMD), which approximate Koopman operators using a dictionary (or kernel) of observables and compute a finite-dimensional Galerkin projection. The robustified variant here—forming kernel-PCA bases and projecting the Koopman action—appears mathematically equivalent in spirit to EDMD/KDMD with kernel trick and SVD-based truncation (as in Williams et al., J. Nonlinear Sci. 2015). The manuscript acknowledges EDMD [35] but positions the proposed method as a DMD-in-RKHS derivation via Krylov/QR, leading to similar computational formulas through Gram matrices. Connections to exact/optimized DMD and subspace/Hankel DMD are mentioned but not empirically compared.

Strengths
- Clear operator-theoretic framing: The RKHS-based Krylov viewpoint and the derivations of projected operators and Ritz values are well-motivated and connect standard DMD/Arnoldi to kernels.
- Practical stabilization via kernel-PCA: The move to POD directions mirrors best practices in DMD and is likely necessary in practice; the matrix expressions and how to compute them with Gram matrices are useful.
- Broad applicability: The use-cases (prediction via preimage, dynamic pattern recognition via subspace angles, and change-point detection) are interesting and can make Koopman features actionable.
- Computationally accessible: The algorithm only requires Gram matrices and eigendecompositions; implementation details are reasonably clear.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to EDMD/KDMD: The robustified algorithm largely reproduces KDMD/EDMD with kernel dictionaries and SVD truncation; the contribution seems to be an alternative derivation and packaging rather than a fundamentally new algorithm.
- Theory relies on strong assumptions: Theorems assume distinct eigenvalues, that Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the feature span and even in the span of a finite set of kernel-PCA directions; no convergence or consistency guarantees are provided beyond these assumptions.
- Empirical evaluation is minimal: Only two small examples are shown, with qualitative plots and no quantitative error metrics (e.g., eigenvalue/eigenfunction error, prediction accuracy). The application sections are interesting but lack rigorous evaluation, baselines (beyond a single change-detection method), ablations, or sensitivity analyses.
- Some statements are inaccurate or confusing: For example, “on an attractor, Koopman eigenvalues always lie on the unit circle” is not generally true for dissipative systems; stable fixed points yield eigenvalues inside the unit disk.
- Scalability and regularization: The approach scales as O(τ^3) in the number of snapshots with O(τ^2) memory; there is no discussion of Nyström approximations, streaming updates, or Tikhonov regularization to handle noise/ill-conditioning.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Mathematically sound at the level of finite-dimensional projections; the Gram-matrix-based implementation is correct. However, the theoretical guarantees are limited, and the empirical section does not convincingly demonstrate advantages over EDMD/KDMD or exact/optimized DMD. The preimage method is heuristic with no error analysis.
- Clarity: Generally understandable, with derivations that an expert can follow. Some notation is dense, a few typos/formatting issues exist, and some figures/references appear incomplete. Implementation details for kernel choice, regularization, and hyperparameters are sparse.
- Originality: The RKHS-Krylov derivation is a nice perspective, but the resulting algorithm is very close to KDMD/EDMD. The applications are plausible but not methodologically novel.
- Significance: Potentially useful as a tutorial-style synthesis for practitioners wanting a kernel DMD recipe. As a research contribution, significance is limited without stronger theory or empirical evidence demonstrating superiority or new capabilities.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Clearly position the contribution vs. EDMD/KDMD: What is new beyond the RKHS-QR derivation? Can you show cases where your approach yields better numerical properties than KDMD (e.g., fewer spurious modes, improved robustness to noise)?
- Provide quantitative experiments: Report eigenvalue errors, mode reconstruction error, short-term prediction RMSE, and robustness to noise. Include baselines: DMD, exact/optimized DMD, EDMD with polynomial dictionaries, and KDMD.
- Regularization and scalability: Discuss and test ridge regularization for F̂, rank-truncation criteria, and Nyström or random feature approximations for large τ. Analyze computational complexity.
- Correct and refine claims: Fix the statement about eigenvalues on attractors; clarify assumptions in Theorem 2 and discuss when they hold (e.g., universal kernels on compact sets). Provide guidance on kernel/hyperparameter selection.
- Preimage prediction: Compare against standard kernel preimage methods (e.g., fixed-point for RBF) and provide quantitative prediction results.
- Related work: Consider comparing with subspace/Hankel-DMD and stochastic Koopman operator approximations; discuss recent convergence results for EDMD-like methods.
- Reproducibility: Ensure figures and code are complete and that experiments can be replicated.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, unified presentation of kernelized DMD with practical stabilization.
- Potentially useful for NeurIPS audience interested in operator-theoretic learning.
- Interesting downstream applications that could be developed further.

Cons for acceptance
- Overlap with established EDMD/KDMD; limited novelty.
- Weak empirical validation and lack of quantitative comparisons.
- Some technical and clarity issues that need revision.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper’s ideas are sound and could be valuable as a consolidated treatment of kernel DMD, but the overlap with existing EDMD/KDMD work and the limited, qualitative experiments make it fall short for NeurIPS in its current form. Strengthening the empirical evaluation, sharpening the positioning vs. prior art, and adding either stronger theory or demonstrable performance gains would substantially improve the submission.
