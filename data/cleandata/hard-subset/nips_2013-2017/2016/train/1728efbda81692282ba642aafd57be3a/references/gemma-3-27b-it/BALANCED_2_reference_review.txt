This paper introduces a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) method extended with reproducing kernels to perform Koopman spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to leverage the expressiveness of kernel functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, moving beyond the linear monomials used in traditional DMD. The authors present an algorithm based on Krylov subspace calculations and eigendecomposition, and propose a robustification strategy using POD to address potential ill-conditioning. Applications to state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition are demonstrated with synthetic and real-world data.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of existing work in spectral methods for dynamical systems, including Koopman operator theory, DMD, and POD. The connection to previous work is well-established through numerous citations. The authors appropriately situate their work within this context, highlighting the limitations of traditional DMD and motivating the need for a more flexible approach. The extension to RKHS is a natural and promising direction, allowing for the representation of more complex dynamics. The inclusion of a POD-based robustification step is also a valuable contribution, addressing a practical concern with the proposed algorithm.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of reproducing kernels to DMD and Koopman spectral analysis is a significant contribution. It addresses a key limitation of standard DMD by allowing for the representation of nonlinear eigenfunctions.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical framework appears well-developed, with clear definitions of the Koopman operator in RKHS and a theorem establishing the connection between the proposed algorithm and the Koopman eigenvalues/eigenvectors.
* **Practical Considerations:** The inclusion of a POD-based robustification strategy demonstrates an awareness of potential numerical challenges and a commitment to practical implementation.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a variety of empirical results, including synthetic data, a chaotic map (Henon), and applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas. The explanations of the theoretical concepts are reasonably clear, though some sections could benefit from further simplification.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the authors demonstrate the performance of their method on several tasks, there is a limited comparison to other state-of-the-art techniques. For example, in the change-point detection example, the comparison to a kernel change detection method is provided, but a more thorough comparison against alternative approaches (e.g., those based on recurrent neural networks or other DMD variants) would strengthen the claims.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of kernel methods is often sensitive to the choice of kernel parameters (e.g., kernel type, bandwidth). The paper provides some details about the kernel parameters used, but a more systematic exploration of parameter sensitivity and its impact on the results would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:** The computational cost of the proposed algorithm is not explicitly discussed. Given the use of kernel methods and the need for eigendecomposition, it is likely to be significant, especially for large datasets. A discussion of the computational complexity and potential scalability issues would be valuable.
* **Clarity in Application Sections:**  The descriptions of the applications (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) are somewhat concise. Expanding these sections with more details about the experimental setup, evaluation metrics, and specific results would enhance the clarity and impact.
* **Theorem 2 Assumption:** The assumption that the Koopman eigenfunctions lie within the span of POD directions (Theorem 2) is a strong one. While it may hold in some cases, it is not always guaranteed. A discussion of the implications of this assumption and potential alternatives would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of DMD with reproducing kernels, addressing a key limitation of the original method.
* The inclusion of a robustification strategy improves the practical applicability of the algorithm.
* The comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrates the potential of the proposed method for various applications.
* The paper is well-written and situated within the relevant literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of extensive comparative analysis limits the ability to assess the true advantages of the proposed method.
* The potential parameter sensitivity and computational complexity raise concerns about its scalability and usability.
* Some sections of the paper could benefit from further clarification and detail.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of Koopman spectral analysis and DMD. The extension to reproducing kernels is a significant advancement, and the inclusion of a robustification strategy enhances its practical relevance. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding the comparative analysis and discussion of computational complexity, these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above by providing a more thorough comparative analysis, discussing parameter sensitivity and computational complexity, and expanding the application sections with more details. Specifically, adding more baseline comparisons to the empirical sections would greatly increase confidence in the proposed method.
