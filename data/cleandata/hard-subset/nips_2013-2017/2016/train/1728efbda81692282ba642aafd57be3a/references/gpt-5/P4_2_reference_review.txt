Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels (kernel DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. Given a sequence x0, …, xτ from xt+1 = f(xt), the authors lift the states into an RKHS via a feature map ϕ and consider the Koopman operator KHϕ(x) = ϕ(f(x)). They (i) compute Ritz values/vectors by forming the RKHS Krylov matrix Mτ = [ϕ(x0)…ϕ(xτ−1)], orthogonalizing it (via Gram–Schmidt using kernel inner products), projecting KH onto span{ϕ(x0),…,ϕ(xτ−1)}, and eigendecomposing the resulting finite operator; and (ii) provide a numerically robust variant that projects onto a truncated kernel-PCA (POD) subspace before computing the finite Koopman approximation. Theorems connect the empirical eigenpairs to Koopman eigenpairs under assumptions (e.g., distinct Ritz values; Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the RKHS closure of data features and in the retained kernel-PCA subspace). Applications considered include short-term prediction via preimage estimation, dynamics recognition using kernel principal angles between subspaces of estimated eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection framed through a kernel exponential family/CUSUM surrogate. Empirical demonstrations on a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, mocap locomotion, and a Lorenz-map change-point scenario illustrate feasibility.

The work builds on DMD [Schmid 2010], its numerically improved variants (exact/optimized/sparsity-promoting DMD), and EDMD [Williams et al., J. Nonlin. Sci. 2015], which approximates Koopman operators using prescribed dictionaries of observables. The proposed approach is also closely related to kernelized EDMD/KDMD that use RKHS dictionaries and Gram matrices to avoid explicit feature maps, and to kernel transfer operator literature (e.g., kernel Koopman/ Perron–Frobenius operators) that approximate spectral objects via RKHS embeddings.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The high-level formulation—projecting KH onto a data-driven finite subspace in RKHS and eigendecomposing—is standard and mathematically sound in spirit. Using Gram matrices to execute orthogonalization/projections is appropriate.
- The robust procedure (projection onto kernel-PCA subspace and eigendecomposition of F̂ = U∗KHU) is a standard stabilization tactic (analogous to POD-projected DMD/EDMD).
- However, several derivations are informal or contain gaps:
  - In Theorem 1/proof, identities involving F, Pτ, R, and Mτ (e.g., “F = Mτ−1KHMτ” and “MτF = KHMτ”) implicitly assume invertibility or left/right inverses of rectangular operators without stating conditions. Dimensions and existence of inverses/pseudoinverses should be clarified.
  - The assumption of distinct Ritz values and Vandermonde diagonalization is restrictive; numerical stability of Vandermonde-based constructions is poor. Robust computations typically avoid explicit Vandermonde matrices.
  - Theorem 2 requires that Koopman eigenfunctions lie in Φ and that their representers κj fall exactly in span(U). This is a strong realizability assumption; approximation statements or error bounds are not provided.
  - The preimage-based prediction section sketches a heuristic MDS-like reconstruction but does not fully specify the optimization or regularization, nor assess ambiguity/non-uniqueness of preimages.
  - The change-point detection derivation via a kernel exponential family and CUSUM drops terms “since their variation is much smaller,” without justification or empirical calibration of thresholds.
- No statistical consistency or approximation error analysis is provided (e.g., convergence of eigenvalues/eigenfunctions as τ,p grow), unlike some operator-approximation works.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and structured, but there are notational overloads (ϕ for features, φ for Koopman eigenfunctions, φ̃/φ̄ for empirical vectors), occasional typos, and overloaded symbols (H for Hilbert space and centering matrix). Some steps (e.g., Gram–Schmidt in RKHS, centering, handling of means) could benefit from explicit, dimensionally consistent formulas or pseudocode.
- Several claims (e.g., “fully recovered if ψ=0”) should be qualified as “on the sampled data subspace.”
- The applications sections are terse and rely on qualitative plots; experimental protocols are underspecified (data sizes, kernel parameters, truncation ranks, numerical tolerances).

Originality
- The core idea—RKHS-based finite-dimensional approximation of the Koopman operator with Gram-matrix computations—is closely aligned with EDMD and its kernelized instantiations (often termed KDMD/Kernel EDMD), and with RKHS transfer operator methods. The robust projection via kernel PCA/POD is widely used in DMD/EDMD practice.
- The paper’s main novelty appears to be a specific derivation route (via RKHS Krylov/QR and Vandermonde arguments) and a unifying presentation, plus application sketches. Differentiation from prior kernel EDMD/KDMD work is not made explicit, and several relevant references (e.g., kernel transfer operators, KDMD variants) are missing.

Significance
- Potentially useful as a tutorial-style consolidation and as a practical recipe for kernelized Koopman spectral analysis with POD stabilization.
- However, the empirical validation is limited (small toy systems, qualitative figures, no quantitative benchmarks), and there is no systematic comparison to DMD, EDMD, or kernel EDMD. Without stronger theory or experiments, the incremental contribution is modest.

Pros
- Clear motivation for moving beyond linear observables; RKHS provides flexible nonlinear features.
- Practical and implementable pipeline; kernel trick avoids explicit high-dimensional features.
- Addresses numerical ill-conditioning via POD/kPCA projection.
- Broad application potential; code link provided.

Cons
- Overlaps substantially with existing EDMD/kernel transfer operator/KDMD literature; differentiation is insufficient.
- Derivations contain gaps/assumptions; no error or consistency analysis.
- Experimental evaluation is minimal and lacks quantitative comparisons and ablations (kernel choice, truncation rank, sensitivity).
- Preimage reconstruction and change-point detection sections are heuristic and under-validated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explicitly position the method relative to EDMD and kernel EDMD/KDMD, and cite kernel transfer operator works; clarify what is new.
- Replace Vandermonde-based arguments with numerically standard procedures; clarify linear algebra (dimensions, use of pseudoinverses).
- Provide approximation guarantees or at least finite-sample error characterizations under clear assumptions.
- Add thorough experimental comparisons against DMD, EDMD, and kernel EDMD on standard benchmarks (eigenvalue accuracy, prediction error, classification accuracy, change-point detection metrics), with sensitivity to kernel width and truncation.
- Detail the preimage optimization and evaluate reconstruction accuracy.
- Improve notation, provide pseudocode, and fix typos.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical kernelized Koopman analysis pipeline with stabilization, potentially useful to practitioners.
- Unifying view connecting DMD, RKHS, and POD.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to established kernel EDMD/KDMD literature.
- Insufficient theoretical rigor and empirical validation; baselines and quantitative results are missing.
- Some derivations are not fully sound as written.

Overall, this is in scope for NeurIPS, but as written, the contribution is incremental and under-evaluated.
