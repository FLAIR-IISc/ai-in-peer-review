**Summary**
This paper introduces a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending the popular Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) framework to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to apply the principles of Koopman operator theory not on the raw, low-dimensional state space, but in the high-dimensional feature space induced by a kernel function. This allows the method, termed Kernel DMD (kDMD), to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions, moving beyond the linear observables used in standard DMD. The authors present a direct algorithm based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space and a more practical, robust variant that projects the data onto kernel principal components before estimating the dynamics. The utility of the extracted Koopman modes as features is demonstrated through applications in prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The primary strength of this paper is its novelty and conceptual elegance. The synthesis of Koopman operator theory, DMD, and kernel methods is a significant and original contribution. Standard DMD has proven to be a powerful tool, but its reliance on a linear combination of observables limits its ability to capture globally nonlinear dynamics. By lifting the problem into an RKHS, the proposed method provides a principled, non-parametric approach to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions in a much richer function space. This is not an incremental improvement but a fundamental and bold extension of DMD that opens up a compelling new direction for research. The theoretical development, particularly Theorems 1 and 2, is sound and clearly establishes the connection between the proposed algorithm and the underlying Koopman operator theory. The paper is also well-written and clearly motivated.

The significance of this work is potentially very high. It bridges the gap between the physics- and engineering-oriented DMD community and the machine learning community's work on kernel methods and spectral learning. The idea of using the learned Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features for downstream machine learning tasks like classification or change-point detection is particularly appealing and highly relevant to the NIPS audience.

The main weakness of the paper lies in its empirical evaluation, which feels more illustrative than conclusive. While the applications presented in Section 7 are interesting proofs-of-concept, they lack the rigorous, quantitative comparison needed to firmly establish the method's advantages.
1.  **Lack of Comparative Baselines:** The paper does not compare its performance against standard DMD or, more critically, against Extended DMD (eDMD) [35]. eDMD also uses a dictionary of nonlinear observables to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions. A key question is how kDMD compares to eDMD with a well-chosen dictionary (e.g., polynomials or radial basis functions). The authors should demonstrate cases where the implicit, infinite-dimensional feature space of a kernel provides a tangible advantage over a finite, explicit basis.
2.  **Quantitative Metrics are Missing:** The prediction results are shown visually but without any quantitative error metrics (e.g., RMSE). The dynamics recognition example uses MDS for visualization, which is insightful, but a more compelling validation would involve a quantitative classification task with accuracy scores. The change-point detection experiment is similarly qualitative.
3.  **Scalability:** The method relies on the construction and eigendecomposition of Gram matrices of size τ x τ, where τ is the number of data points. The authors do not discuss the computational limitations this imposes for long time-series data.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core idea of kernelizing Dynamic Mode Decomposition is highly novel, elegant, and conceptually significant.
*   It proposes a bold new direction for data-driven analysis of nonlinear systems, combining techniques from different fields in a creative way.
*   The theoretical foundation is solid and well-presented.
*   The potential impact is high, both for improving the analysis of dynamical systems and for providing a new feature extraction technique for machine learning tasks.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental validation is preliminary and lacks rigor.
*   The paper fails to demonstrate a clear empirical advantage over closely related methods, particularly Extended DMD.
*   The work feels somewhat incomplete due to the absence of quantitative benchmarking.

**Recommendation**

I recommend **acceptance**. This paper introduces a genuinely new and exciting idea. The fusion of Koopman theory with kernel methods to generalize DMD is a significant conceptual leap that is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up work. While the empirical validation is not as thorough as one might hope, the strength, novelty, and potential significance of the core contribution are outstanding. This is precisely the type of bold, idea-driven paper that NIPS should champion. The weaknesses in the experimental section are forgivable in light of the strength of the idea and can be addressed in future work by the authors and the community they will inspire. The paper provides a strong foundation and a clear new direction, prioritizing a novel concept over methodological perfectionism, which I value highly.
