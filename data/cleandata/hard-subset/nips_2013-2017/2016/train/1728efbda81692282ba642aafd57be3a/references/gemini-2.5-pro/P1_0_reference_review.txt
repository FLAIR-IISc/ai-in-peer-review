### Paper Review

**Summary of the Main Ideas**

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems called "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels" (Kernel DMD). The core idea is to extend the popular Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) framework by leveraging the kernel trick. Standard DMD approximates the eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator using a linear combination of the observed state variables, which can be insufficient for highly nonlinear systems. This work proposes lifting the state data into a high-dimensional Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) and performing the Koopman analysis there. This allows for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions using a much richer, nonlinear set of basis functions implicitly defined by the chosen kernel.

The authors first present a direct formulation of Kernel DMD based on a Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization of the Krylov subspace in the feature space. Recognizing that this approach can be numerically unstable (an issue also present in basic DMD), they then propose a more robust algorithm. This robust version is analogous to "exact DMD" and involves projecting the data onto its principal components within the RKHS (i.e., performing Kernel PCA) before computing the projected Koopman operator. The paper provides theoretical justification (Theorems 1 and 2) connecting the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the derived finite-dimensional matrices to the true Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions. Finally, the utility of the method is demonstrated through several applications: short-term prediction, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection.

This work builds upon a rich literature of spectral methods for dynamical systems. It directly extends DMD [25, 26] and its variants [8, 33]. The concept of using a richer set of basis functions is central to Extended DMD (eDMD) [35], to which the proposed method is very closely related, effectively providing a kernel-based framework for selecting these basis functions. It also connects to a broader class of kernel-based methods for learning dynamical systems in machine learning [15].

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled and Elegant Formulation:** The extension of DMD to RKHS is a natural and powerful idea. It provides a principled way to move beyond the linear dictionary of observables used in standard DMD, allowing the method to capture more complex nonlinear dynamics. The use of the kernel trick to make an infinite-dimensional problem computationally tractable is elegant.
2.  **Robust Practical Algorithm:** The paper rightly identifies the potential numerical instability of a naive implementation and provides a robust alternative based on projection onto kernel principal components (Section 4). This shows a thoughtful consideration of practical implementation and significantly increases the method's usability.
3.  **Versatile Applications:** The paper demonstrates the usefulness of the extracted Koopman features in three distinct and non-trivial applications. The prediction and recognition tasks are natural extensions, but the formulation of a sequential change-point detection algorithm based on the learned Koopman structure is particularly novel and interesting. These applications convincingly argue for the significance of the proposed feature extraction method.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It effectively introduces the necessary background on the Koopman operator and DMD before developing the kernelized version and its robust variant. The theoretical results are stated clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficient Discussion of Originality relative to Extended DMD (eDMD):** The most significant weakness is the limited discussion of the relationship with Williams et al. [35], "A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator: Extending dynamic mode decomposition" (eDMD). The eDMD framework allows for the use of an arbitrary dictionary of observable functions. The proposed Kernel DMD can be viewed as a specific instance of eDMD where the dictionary is the set of functions in the RKHS, spanned by the kernel functions centered on the data points. While the kernel perspective is valuable, the paper does not sufficiently articulate what novel theoretical insights or practical advantages its formulation offers over the more general eDMD framework. A more detailed comparison and discussion are needed to clearly position the paper's contribution.
2.  **Lack of Quantitative and Comparative Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is illustrative but lacks rigor.
    *   In Section 6, the estimated eigenvalues are compared to the true values, but not against any baseline methods (e.g., standard DMD, eDMD with polynomial basis). It is unclear how much of an improvement Kernel DMD provides on these nonlinear examples.
    *   The applications in Section 7 are demonstrated but not quantitatively evaluated against competing methods. For prediction, a comparison to other time-series models (e.g., Gaussian Process models) would be valuable. For dynamics recognition, comparison against methods like Dynamic Time Warping would strengthen the results. For change-point detection, a quantitative analysis of detection delay and false alarm rates is needed.
3.  **Scalability and Parameter Selection:** The method requires the construction and eigendecomposition of Gram matrices of size `τ x τ`, where `τ` is the number of data points. This leads to a computational complexity of at least `O(τ^2)` for construction and `O(τ^3)` for decomposition, which scales poorly for long time series. The paper does not address this limitation or discuss potential approximation techniques (e.g., Nyström method). Furthermore, the crucial choice of the kernel function and its hyperparameters is not discussed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a well-motivated and elegant kernelization of the popular DMD algorithm.
*   It provides a theoretically grounded and practically robust algorithm for Koopman analysis of nonlinear systems.
*   It demonstrates the utility of the approach with a diverse and interesting set of applications, including a novel change-point detection scheme.
*   The work bridges ideas from physics-based dynamical systems analysis (DMD) and machine learning (kernel methods), which is valuable for the NIPS community.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The originality of the core method is questionable in light of Extended DMD (eDMD) [35], and the paper fails to adequately differentiate its contribution.
*   The experimental evaluation is purely demonstrative and lacks quantitative comparisons to relevant baselines, making it difficult to assess the method's performance.
*   Practical issues such as computational scalability and hyperparameter selection are not addressed.

**Qualitative Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations appear correct, and the proposed robust algorithm addresses a key practical issue. The work is a complete piece, presenting theory, an algorithm, and applications.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The progression of ideas is logical and easy for an informed reader to follow.
*   **Originality:** The originality is moderate. While the direct combination of kernels and DMD is a novel presentation, the underlying idea is very similar to eDMD. The main originality lies in the specific RKHS formulation and, more significantly, in the application to sequential change-point detection.
*   **Significance:** The work has the potential for high significance. DMD is a widely used tool in many scientific domains, and providing a powerful, nonlinear extension grounded in well-understood kernel methods could have a broad impact. The demonstrated applications suggest that the extracted features are meaningful and useful for downstream machine learning tasks.

**Recommendation**

This is a well-written paper on an interesting topic that connects two important fields. The main weakness is the insufficient comparison to and differentiation from Extended DMD. If the authors can clarify this relationship and strengthen their experiments with quantitative baselines, it would be a strong contribution. As it stands, it is a borderline paper. I lean towards acceptance, with a strong recommendation for the authors to address the originality and experimental comparison issues in the final version.
