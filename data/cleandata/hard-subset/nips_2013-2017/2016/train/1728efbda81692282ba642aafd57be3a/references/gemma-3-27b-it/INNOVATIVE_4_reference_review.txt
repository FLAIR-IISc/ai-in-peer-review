This paper presents a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), aiming to improve the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions beyond the limitations of linear monomials used in traditional DMD. The core idea – leveraging the expressiveness of RKHS to capture more complex dynamics – is a strong one, and the connection to DMD is clearly articulated. The paper is well-structured, with a comprehensive overview of related work and a clear explanation of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of reproducing kernels to DMD is a genuinely novel contribution. While kernel methods are commonplace in machine learning, their integration with DMD for Koopman spectral analysis is a valuable extension, opening up possibilities for analyzing highly nonlinear systems. This isn't merely an incremental improvement, but a potentially significant shift in how DMD can be applied.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical derivation, particularly Theorem 1, is rigorous and provides a solid foundation for the proposed algorithm. The connection to Krylov subspaces and Ritz value/vector approximations is well-established.
* **Potential Impact:** Successfully implementing this approach could broaden the applicability of DMD to systems where linear approximations are inadequate. The application examples (prediction, change-point detection, dynamic recognition) are reasonable and demonstrate the versatility of the extracted features.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a good background on DMD and the Koopman operator, making the work accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation, while present, feels somewhat superficial. The toy system demonstrates the method's ability to recover known eigenvalues, but the Hénon map results are less convincing (“seem to be approximately estimated”). More rigorous validation with a wider range of systems (higher dimensionality, more complex dynamics) is needed.
* **Computational Cost:** Kernel methods are notoriously computationally expensive, especially for large datasets. The paper doesn't adequately address the scalability of the proposed algorithm. The POD projection step is a good attempt to mitigate this, but the computational implications should be more thoroughly discussed.
* **Kernel Selection:** The choice of kernel (polynomial, Gaussian) and its parameters (degree, width) are not justified beyond mentioning common practice. A sensitivity analysis regarding kernel choice would strengthen the paper. How does the performance vary with different kernels and parameter settings?
* **Practical Implementation Details:** The description of the pre-image calculation for prediction is high-level. More details on how the local coordinate is constructed and the challenges associated with it would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is novel and has the potential to significantly advance the field of DMD and Koopman spectral analysis.
* The theoretical foundation is sound and well-presented.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.
* The potential applications are relevant and demonstrate the versatility of the approach.
* Minor methodological flaws are acceptable given the novelty and potential impact of the work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The empirical validation is limited and doesn’t convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the kernelized DMD across diverse systems.
* The computational cost and scalability issues are not adequately addressed.
* The sensitivity to kernel selection is not explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **acceptance** with minor revisions. The novelty and theoretical soundness of this work are compelling. While the empirical validation is limited, I believe the potential impact justifies overlooking this weakness, *provided* the authors address the scalability concerns and provide a more thorough analysis of kernel sensitivity in a revised version. The paper presents a significant new direction in DMD research, and I am excited to see how this work develops. I recommend the authors prioritize expanding the empirical evaluation and elaborating on the computational aspects.
