Summary
The paper proposes a kernelized variant of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to approximate Koopman spectra from data. The authors work in an RKHS, define a Koopman operator acting on feature maps, and show that an Arnoldi/QR-based procedure on the kernel Krylov subspace yields empirical Ritz values and vectors (Theorem 1). Because naïve Gram–Schmidt in feature space can be ill-conditioned, they present a “robust” version that projects onto truncated kernel PCA directions before eigendecomposition (Theorem 2). They position the method as a feature extractor for nonlinear dynamical systems, and sketch applications to short-term prediction via kernel pre-image, dynamics recognition via subspace angles, and sequential change-point detection. Experiments are limited to a toy quadratic system, the Hénon map, motion capture sequences (qualitative embeddings), and an example of change detection on the Lorenz map.

Relation to prior work
There is extensive prior work connecting DMD to Koopman spectral analysis, and on improved numerical procedures (exact/optimized/sparse DMD). The closest machine learning–oriented antecedent is Extended DMD (EDMD; Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, J. Nonlinear Sci., 2015), which constructs a finite dictionary of nonlinear observables and learns a linear map; a kernelized version (KDMD) has also been explored. The proposed “robustified” variant using kPCA directions closely mirrors EDMD with a kernel dictionary and truncation. The paper cites EDMD but does not adequately distinguish its contributions from EDMD/KDMD beyond a different derivation via Gram–Schmidt/Arnoldi in RKHS.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the Koopman/DMD connection and an RKHS formulation that allows nonlinear dictionaries via kernels.
- The two-stage procedure (kernel Arnoldi and a numerically better kPCA-based truncation) is reasonable and aligns with practical needs in DMD.
- Theorems 1 and 2 formalize the eigen-decomposition relationships in the finite-data, finite-rank setting, and the derivations are concise.
- The paper suggests several application directions and provides code.

Weaknesses
Quality and soundness:
- The theory largely restates standard Arnoldi/DMD facts in a kernel setting. Theorem 2 depends on strong assumptions (true Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the RKHS and in the span of the retained kPCA subspace), with no discussion of when these are satisfied. There are no consistency or approximation error bounds, nor guidance for truncation.
- The claim “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is not generally true; unit-modulus eigenvalues arise for unitary/measure-preserving dynamics. Dissipative systems have eigenvalues inside the unit circle, and chaotic systems often exhibit continuous spectra on attractors. This undermines part of the interpretive discussion.
- The “prediction via preimage” is ad hoc and relies on an MDS-like local reconstruction; no guarantees are provided, and the preimage problem is ill-posed for common kernels (e.g., RBF).
- The stochastic extension and the CUSUM-based change detection are not well-founded. Modeling p(xt) through an exponential family with sufficient statistic ψ(xt)=ϕ(xt) and natural parameter ϕ∘f(xt−1) is not derived from a principled probabilistic dynamical model; the resulting detector drops terms heuristically and is not evaluated with standard detection metrics.

Empirical evaluation:
- Experiments are small-scale and mostly qualitative. There is no rigorous comparison to established baselines such as EDMD/KDMD (with and without regularization), exact/optimized/sparse DMD, subspace identification, SINDy, or GPDM.
- No quantitative metrics are reported: e.g., eigenvalue/eigenfunction approximation errors, predictive RMSE vs horizon with confidence intervals, classification accuracy for dynamics recognition, or standard change-detection metrics (false alarm rate, detection delay, ROC/AUC).
- The Hénon example shows a few eigenvalues “approximately” near equilibria, but Koopman spectra in chaotic systems are subtle; the paper does not clarify whether it targets local linearization near equilibria (then a local EDMD should be used) or global spectra (often continuous).
- Hyperparameter selection (kernel type/width, truncation level), regularization, sensitivity to noise, and scalability (time/memory O(τ^2–τ^3)) are not addressed.

Originality:
- The core idea—learning a finite-dimensional approximation of the Koopman operator via a kernel dictionary and truncation—is essentially EDMD/KDMD. The Arnoldi/QR derivation in RKHS is a different presentation but yields the same computational objects (Gram matrices and an eigenproblem). The paper does not convincingly articulate novel technical advances beyond this rederivation.

Clarity:
- The manuscript is generally readable but contains grammatical errors, notation overload (e.g., φ for eigenfunctions and φ̃/φ̄ for vectors), and some ambiguous statements. Several references are incomplete. Algorithmic details (regularization, conditioning, how to compute kernelized QR robustly) are under-specified.

Significance
Without stronger theoretical guarantees and substantial empirical evidence against established baselines, the contribution is incremental. Kernelized DMD/EDMD is already in the literature; this paper’s added value is not demonstrated convincingly, and practical utility is not quantified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Position the work precisely relative to EDMD/KDMD; clarify what is truly new (e.g., specific numerical schemes, theoretical properties) and provide proofs or analyses beyond restating known results.
- Correct the statement about eigenvalues on the unit circle and discuss continuous spectra and dissipative vs measure-preserving settings.
- Add regularization (e.g., Tikhonov) and analyze conditioning; report computational complexity and memory.
- Provide thorough experiments with quantitative metrics and strong baselines on standard benchmarks (e.g., Duffing, Van der Pol, Burgers/Navier–Stokes snapshots, noisy settings), including sensitivity to kernel/truncation/noise.
- For prediction, report RMSE vs horizon and compare to EDMD/KDMD, GPDM, and local linear models; for recognition, provide classification accuracy; for change detection, provide ROC/AUC and detection delay/false alarm tradeoffs.

Pros
- Clear RKHS-based presentation of Koopman/DMD concepts
- Sensible truncation via kPCA
- Code provided and interesting application ideas

Cons
- Limited novelty over EDMD/KDMD
- Weak theory (strong assumptions, one incorrect claim)
- Insufficient, largely qualitative experiments and no strong baselines
- Missing regularization, scalability, and robustness analyses

Recommendation: Reject. The topic is relevant, but the novelty and empirical validation are insufficient for NeurIPS at this stage.
