Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) framework for Koopman spectral analysis. Two variants are presented: (i) a “kernel Arnoldi”/Gram–Schmidt approach that constructs an orthonormal basis of the Krylov subspace in an RKHS and computes Ritz values/vectors of the projected Koopman operator; and (ii) a “robustified” version that projects onto a truncated kernel PCA (KPCA) subspace, forms a finite-dimensional operator F̂ via Gram matrices, and performs eigendecomposition. Theorems relate the resulting Ritz values/vectors to Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions under span assumptions. The authors illustrate the method on a toy system and the Henon map, and outline applications (short-term prediction via preimage, dynamics recognition via subspace angles, and change-point detection via a CUSUM-like score).

This direction is closely related to Extended DMD (EDMD) [Williams et al., 2015], KDMD (kernel EDMD), and kernel transfer operator methods (e.g., Klus, Nüske, Schütte and coauthors, 2016–2018), as well as Koopman analysis in RKHS (Giannakis/Das et al.). The robust/KPCA-based construction and Galerkin projection onto data-driven features are near-identical in form to kernel EDMD/transfer operator estimators, where the choice of dictionary is implicitly induced by a kernel. The Arnoldi/DMD interpretation in feature space is conceptually natural, but it leads to the same projected operator as EDMD when implemented with Gram matrices.

Strengths
- Clear operator-theoretic framing: recasts DMD as spectral analysis of a projected Koopman operator and extends it to RKHSs.
- Practical pipeline: the KPCA-based truncation is numerically stabilizing and aligns with best practices in DMD/EDMD (truncated SVD/regularization).
- Theorems provide transparent algebraic relations between projected operators and eigen-expansions; the Ritz expansion in feature space is intuitive.
- Applications are diverse and point to potential utility (recognition, change detection).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Originality/novelty: The robust algorithm is essentially kernel EDMD/KDMD (Galerkin projection in an RKHS using Gram matrices) and kernel transfer operator approaches. The paper cites EDMD but undersells the overlap and does not cite key kernel-based Koopman/transfer operator works (e.g., Klus et al., 2016–2018; Williams et al., KDMD; Giannakis et al.). The novelty beyond a kernelized Arnoldi viewpoint is limited.
- Theory: Theorems 1–2 are largely algebraic restatements of Ritz theory and Galerkin projection; there is no convergence or consistency analysis (e.g., conditions under which eigenvalues/eigenfunctions of the finite estimator converge to true Koopman spectra). Assumptions like “ψ=0” or “κj ∈ span(U)” are very strong and rarely verifiable. Functional-analytic conditions on KH (boundedness, domain, ergodic measure) are not specified. The claim that “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is not generally correct; for dissipative systems with attracting fixed points/limit cycles, eigenvalues are typically inside the unit circle except for continuous spectra and symmetries.
- Algorithmic clarity: The Gram–Schmidt Arnoldi in RKHS (Algorithm in Sec. 3) is ill-conditioned; the paper acknowledges this but then the stable algorithm in Sec. 4 is essentially the final method and coincides with kernel EDMD. The use of centering (H) in building F̂ mixes KPCA with Koopman estimation; centering alters observables and is uncommon in EDMD unless carefully justified.
- Experiments: Very limited and largely qualitative. No quantitative errors (e.g., eigenvalue error, modal reconstruction error, prediction RMSE), no comparisons to DMD/EDMD/KDMD/optimized DMD, no sensitivity to kernel width, truncation level, or noise. The preimage-based forecasting is ad hoc (MDS-style preimage) with no evaluation against simpler baselines (e.g., predicting in observable space, kernel ridge regression preimage).
- Change-point detection: The probabilistic derivation via a kernel exponential family with θ(·)=ϕ∘f(x_{t−1}) is heuristic; the log-partition g is ignored, and the simplification that drops the second term in the log-likelihood ratio is not justified. A more defensible route would be to use prediction residuals/innovations or likelihood under a learned one-step predictor with proper calibration.
- Scalability: The method scales cubically in the number of snapshots due to τ×τ Gram eigendecompositions; no discussion of streaming/blocked approximations or Nyström/random features.

Quality
Technically competent in linear-algebraic parts, but key theoretical claims are either well-known (EDMD/KDMD equivalence) or rely on strong assumptions without error analysis. Empirical support is insufficient for NeurIPS.

Clarity
Generally readable, but there are notational collisions (ϕ feature map vs φ eigenfunctions), occasional typos, and some confusing steps (e.g., why centering is used for Koopman estimation). Proofs gloss over infinite-dimensional subtleties and invertibility conditions.

Originality
Limited relative to EDMD/KDMD and kernel transfer operator literature; the contribution is primarily a rephrasing of known estimators with a kernel Arnoldi view.

Significance
Potentially useful to practitioners unfamiliar with EDMD/KDMD, but as written, the incremental contribution and weak empirical evidence limit impact.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify how your robust algorithm differs, in estimator form and guarantees, from kernel EDMD/KDMD and kernel transfer operator methods (provide explicit operator equivalences).
- Under what assumptions do your estimated eigenvalues/eigenfunctions converge to the true Koopman spectra? Any finite-sample error bounds?
- Why is centering appropriate for Koopman estimation here? What is the effect on learned eigenfunctions?
- Can you provide quantitative comparisons to EDMD/KDMD/optimized DMD on standard benchmarks (eigenvalue error, reconstruction/prediction error)?
- How do you choose kernel and truncation; how sensitive are results?
- Can you replace the preimage step with a learned inverse map (e.g., kernel ridge) and report accuracy?
- Please correct the statement about eigenvalues on the unit circle.

Pros
- Clear operator-theoretic framing and kernelization of DMD
- Practical stabilized estimator via KPCA
- Broad application ideas

Cons
- Overlaps substantially with existing EDMD/KDMD/kernel transfer operator work
- No convergence analysis; strong unverified assumptions
- Sparse, largely qualitative experiments; no baselines
- Heuristic change-point detection and preimage steps
- Scalability and hyperparameter selection not addressed

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper would be much strengthened by clarifying novelty vs. KDMD/transfer operators, adding rigorous approximation/convergence results, and providing thorough quantitative comparisons and ablations.
