- Proposal: Extend DMD to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to better approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using non-linear bases.
- Method: Project the Koopman operator onto an RKHS Krylov subspace and compute Ritz values via eigendecomposition, with a POD step to mitigate ill-conditioning.
- Applications: Demonstrated on synthetic (toy system, Hénon map) and real-world locomotion data for prediction, change-point detection, and pattern recognition.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on DMD, Koopman theory, POD, spectral methods, and various DMD variants; notes connection to subspace identification needing further study.
- Theoretical strength: Mathematically well-motivated and rigorous with clear links to Krylov subspace methods and Ritz value derivation.
- Addresses limitation: Uses kernel methods to overcome linear-basis limitations of traditional DMD, allowing non-linear Koopman eigenfunction approximation.
- Robustification: Incorporates POD projection to improve numerical stability, mirroring successful strategies from standard DMD.
- Empirical support: Toy-system results convincing; includes other experiments though details are sparse.
- Weak empirical detail: Locomotion data description, dataset size/dimensionality, and quantitative metrics for tasks are missing or insufficient.
- Computational concerns: Paper omits discussion of computational cost and scalability of RKHS-DMD for high-dimensional or large datasets.
- POD justification lacking: POD projection feels ad-hoc without theoretical linkage to relevant Koopman modes.
- Notation issues: Dense and sometimes unclear notation (e.g., distinctions between different eigenvalues and vectors).
- Missing baselines: Change-point detection and other applications lack comparisons to established methods.
- Qualitative scores: Quality 7/10, Clarity 6/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 6/10.
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, mathematically sound extension of DMD that addresses known limitations and shows application potential.
- Arguments against acceptance: Insufficient empirical validation, unaddressed computational/scalability issues, missing baselines, and clarity problems.
- Recommendation: Borderline reject; encourage resubmission after addressing empirical detail, quantitative metrics, baseline comparisons, and computational cost analysis.
