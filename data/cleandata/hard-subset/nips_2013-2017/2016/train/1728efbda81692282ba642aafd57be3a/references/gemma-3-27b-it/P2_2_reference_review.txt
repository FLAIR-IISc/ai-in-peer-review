## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to utilize reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs), aiming to improve the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions beyond the limitations of linear monomials used in standard DMD. The authors present an algorithm based on projecting the Koopman operator onto a Krylov subspace constructed in the RKHS, and address potential ill-conditioning issues with a POD-based projection. They demonstrate the method on a toy system and the Henon map, and explore applications in prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Extension of DMD:** The core idea of incorporating RKHSs into DMD to capture non-linear Koopman eigenfunctions is a worthwhile extension. This addresses a known limitation of standard DMD, which struggles with highly nonlinear dynamics.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a relatively thorough theoretical justification for the proposed method, drawing connections to the Koopman operator, Krylov subspace methods, and kernel methods. The theorems (1 & 2) establish the mathematical basis for the proposed algorithm.
* **Robustness Considerations:** Recognizing the potential for ill-conditioning, the authors proactively address this through a POD-based projection step, demonstrating awareness of practical implementation challenges.
* **Demonstration of Applications:**  The exploration of prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection showcases the potential utility of the extracted Koopman modes beyond simply spectral analysis.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The paper is generally well-organized and provides a good background on DMD and the Koopman operator.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation is relatively limited. While the toy system and Henon map provide initial validation, the results aren't particularly compelling.  The visualizations (Fig. 1) aren't very informative.  A more thorough comparison with existing DMD variants (especially kernel DMD or extended DMD) on more complex datasets would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Lack of Quantitative Results in Applications:** The applications section (prediction, recognition, change-point detection) lacks quantitative evaluation. It’s difficult to assess the performance of the proposed method without concrete metrics. Showing prediction error, classification accuracy, or detection rates would be crucial.
* **Clarity in Application Details:**  The description of the application details is somewhat vague. For example, the choice of kernel parameters and the specific implementation of the pre-image calculation in prediction are not fully explained.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** While the Related Work section mentions some relevant papers, the connection to *extended DMD* [Williams et al., 2015] seems particularly underdeveloped. Extended DMD also utilizes pre-determined basis functions, and a more detailed comparison would be beneficial. The paper could also better contextualize its contribution within the broader landscape of spectral methods for dynamical systems.
* **Overly Dense Notation:** The mathematical notation is sometimes dense and could be simplified for better readability.



**Review Criteria Assessment:**

* **Quality:** Technically sound, but the validation is weak. The theoretical foundation is good, but the empirical support doesn't fully justify the claims.  It feels incomplete – more comprehensive experiments are needed.
* **Clarity:** Generally clear, but the mathematical notation and application details could be more accessible. 
* **Originality:** The extension of DMD to RKHS is novel, but the degree of originality is somewhat diminished by the existence of related work like extended DMD. The paper needs to more clearly articulate its distinct contribution.
* **Significance:** The potential significance is moderate.  If the method can demonstrably outperform existing techniques on challenging dynamical systems, it could be valuable. However, the current lack of strong empirical evidence makes it difficult to assess the significance.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Presents a novel extension to DMD with a sound theoretical basis.
* Addresses potential practical issues (ill-conditioning) with a POD projection.
* Explores a range of potential applications.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited empirical validation with unconvincing results.
* Lack of quantitative evaluation in applications.
* Underdeveloped comparison to closely related work (e.g., extended DMD).
* Some clarity issues in application details and notation.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Reject with Encouragement to Revise.**

I believe the paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution, but it requires significant revisions. The authors need to strengthen the empirical evaluation with more complex datasets, provide quantitative results for the applications, and clearly delineate their work from related approaches.  Specifically, a head-to-head comparison with extended DMD would be highly valuable.  Improving the clarity of the application details and simplifying the notation would also enhance the paper’s accessibility.  With substantial revisions addressing these concerns, the paper could be reconsidered.
