**Summary**

This paper proposes a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to lift the system's state into the high-dimensional feature space of an RKHS, where the nonlinear dynamics are more likely to be represented by a linear Koopman operator. The authors present this as "kernel DMD" (kDMD). They first derive a principled algorithm based on an Arnoldi-like method and QR decomposition in the feature space. Recognizing the numerical instability of this direct approach—a known issue in standard DMD—they then propose a more robust variant that first projects the data onto its kernel principal components before performing the decomposition. All computations are efficiently performed using the kernel trick, avoiding explicit manipulation of the high-dimensional feature vectors. The utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features is demonstrated through several applications: short-term prediction (via pre-image estimation), recognition of dynamic patterns in motion capture data, and sequential change-point detection.

The work builds upon the growing literature on DMD from the physics and engineering communities and connects it to established machine learning techniques, namely kernel methods. It is most closely related to Extended DMD (eDMD), which generalizes DMD to an arbitrary dictionary of observable functions. The authors' contribution is to specifically frame this dictionary within the RKHS framework, thereby gaining access to the powerful and well-understood machinery of kernel methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Elegant Formulation:** The paper provides a very clear and theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHS. The core idea of replacing linear observables with a rich, nonlinear feature map via the kernel trick is a natural and powerful way to enhance the capabilities of Koopman analysis. The connection between the DMD algorithm and the Arnoldi method on a Krylov subspace is well-explained and elegantly lifted to the feature space.

2.  **Practical Robustness:** A significant strength is the authors' awareness of the practical numerical issues with naive DMD implementations. The introduction of the robust version in Section 4, which uses kernel PCA for dimensionality reduction and denoising prior to spectral analysis, is crucial. This makes the proposed method far more likely to be useful in practice on real-world, noisy data. Theorem 2 provides a solid justification for this approach, connecting the eigenvectors of the reduced system to the true Koopman eigenfunctions under reasonable assumptions.

3.  **Novel and Compelling Applications:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting a new algorithm and demonstrates its value as a feature extraction tool for downstream machine learning tasks. The applications for dynamics recognition (using kernel principal angles between subspaces of eigenfunctions) and sequential change-point detection are particularly novel and showcase the potential of using Koopman spectra to characterize and compare complex dynamics. These applications are highly relevant to the NIPS community.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with a sufficient background on the Koopman operator and DMD, making it accessible to readers unfamiliar with the topic. It then systematically develops the kernelized version and its robust variant, followed by clear empirical validation and application examples.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Comparison with Extended DMD (eDMD):** The paper mentions eDMD [35] as related work, but the discussion could be more detailed. The robust algorithm presented in Section 4 is procedurally very similar to eDMD where the dictionary of observables is chosen to be a set of kernel basis functions (e.g., `k(x, x_i)`). The authors should more explicitly delineate their contribution in this context. The main advantage of the kDMD formulation is its direct integration with the entire suite of kernel machinery (like kernel PCA), which is a strong point, but this should be emphasized more clearly as the primary novelty over the more general eDMD framework.

2.  **Discussion of Assumptions and Limitations:**
    *   The assumptions of Theorem 2—that the true Koopman eigenfunctions lie within the subspace spanned by the top kernel principal components of the data—are quite strong. This warrants more discussion. It implies that the dynamics of interest must be captured by the directions of highest variance in the feature space, which may not always be true.
    *   The method's computational complexity is O(τ³), where τ is the number of data points, due to the construction and eigendecomposition of the τ×τ Gram matrix. This makes it unsuitable for very long time series. A discussion of this limitation and potential remedies (e.g., Nyström approximation, random features) would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Details on Applications:** The application sections are compelling but somewhat brief.
    *   For prediction, the pre-image problem is a well-known, non-trivial challenge for kernel methods. The paper references an MDS-based approach but provides few details. Given that prediction is a key application, a more thorough description or analysis of the pre-image step's reliability and computational cost would be beneficial.
    *   The change-point detection method introduces a non-parametric exponential family model. The motivation for this specific formulation and the subsequent simplifications in the decision rule could be explained more thoroughly.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution at the intersection of machine learning and nonlinear dynamics. It presents a method that is technically sound, practically relevant, and well-motivated. By systematically kernelizing DMD and its robust variant, the authors have developed a powerful tool for data-driven spectral analysis of complex systems. The strengths, particularly the elegant formulation, the focus on numerical robustness, and the demonstration of novel applications, significantly outweigh the weaknesses. While a more detailed comparison to eDMD and a discussion of scalability would improve the paper, the work stands as a solid and significant contribution that is likely to be influential and built upon by others in the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
