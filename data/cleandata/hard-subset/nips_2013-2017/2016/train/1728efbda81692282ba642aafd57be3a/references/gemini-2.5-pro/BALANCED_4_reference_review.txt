### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems within the framework of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to extend Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a popular data-driven technique from fluid dynamics, by applying the kernel trick. The Koopman operator, which governs the evolution of observable functions of a system's state, is linear even for nonlinear dynamics. Standard DMD approximates this operator by assuming the observables are linear functions of the state. This paper lifts the state into a high-dimensional feature space via a kernel map, where a much richer set of nonlinear observables can be represented.

The authors first derive a "kernel DMD" (kDMD) algorithm that is equivalent to performing DMD in the feature space. This involves a Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization of the Krylov subspace formed by the time-evolved data points in the RKHS. Recognizing that this direct approach can be numerically unstable, they propose a second, more robust algorithm. This robust version is analogous to variants of DMD that use Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) for dimensionality reduction and denoising. In the proposed method, this is achieved by first projecting the data onto its principal components in the feature space (i.e., using Kernel PCA) and then approximating the Koopman operator in this reduced-rank subspace. The paper demonstrates the utility of the extracted Koopman eigenvalues and modes as features for downstream tasks, including time-series prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

This work builds upon the extensive literature on DMD [25, 26, 33] and Koopman operator theory [16, 20]. It connects these ideas to the well-established field of kernel methods in machine learning [28, 29]. The proposed method is closely related to Extended DMD (eDMD) [35], which also generalizes DMD to use arbitrary dictionaries of basis functions; this paper's contribution can be seen as using the kernel trick to work with an implicitly defined, infinite-dimensional dictionary.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The paper presents a natural and elegant synthesis of Koopman operator theory and kernel methods. Kernelizing DMD is a well-motivated step to move beyond the linearity assumptions of the original method, allowing it to capture complex nonlinear dynamics without requiring the user to manually specify a suitable set of nonlinear observables.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical development is clear and appears to be correct. Theorem 1 provides a solid foundation for the direct kDMD algorithm by extending the DMD principle to the feature space. The derivation of the robustified algorithm via Kernel PCA projection (Theorem 2) is well-justified, drawing a clear and useful parallel to the use of POD in standard DMD to improve numerical stability and handle noise.

3.  **Practicality and Significance:** The proposed method offers a practical, data-driven tool for analyzing nonlinear systems. The ability to extract dynamic modes (frequencies, growth/decay rates) from complex time-series data is significant for many fields. The paper further enhances its significance by demonstrating that these modes are not just for analysis but can serve as powerful features for machine learning tasks like prediction, classification of dynamics, and change-point detection. This bridges the gap between analysis methods from physics and applied machine learning.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. It provides a concise but sufficient background on the Koopman operator and DMD, making the work accessible. The progression from the direct, theoretically pure algorithm to the more practical, robustified version is easy to follow.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Baselines:** The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of a thorough experimental comparison with relevant state-of-the-art methods.
    *   **Extended DMD (eDMD) [35]:** This is the most direct and important baseline. eDMD also generalizes DMD using a dictionary of observables. A key selling point of kDMD should be that it obviates the need to manually select this dictionary. A quantitative comparison is needed to demonstrate this. For instance, showing that kDMD with a standard Gaussian kernel can outperform eDMD with a reasonably chosen set of polynomial or radial basis functions (or requires less tuning) would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.
    *   **Application Baselines:** The application experiments are presented in isolation. For prediction (Section 7), the results should be compared against standard time-series forecasting models like LSTMs or Gaussian Process dynamical models. For dynamics recognition, a comparison to methods like Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) with a k-NN classifier would provide a useful performance benchmark.

2.  **Lack of Experimental Detail and Depth:** The empirical evaluation feels more illustrative than rigorous.
    *   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of kDMD will depend on the choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., the width of the Gaussian kernel), as well as the truncation rank `p` in the robust algorithm. The paper provides no discussion of how these were chosen or how sensitive the results are to these choices. A sensitivity analysis would greatly improve the practical value of the work.
    *   **Clarity in Figures:** In Figure 1, the baseline "Equilibrium DMD" is not defined or explained in the text, leaving the reader to guess its meaning. The change-point detection plot in Figure 4 is qualitative; quantitative metrics (e.g., detection delay, false alarm rate) would be more informative.

3.  **Briefness of Application Sections:** The descriptions of the applications in Section 7 are quite condensed, which may hinder reproducibility.
    *   The prediction method relies on a pre-image estimation technique that is only briefly sketched. More algorithmic details or pseudo-code would be beneficial.
    *   The change-point detection derivation involves several approximations (e.g., extension to stochastic systems, simplification of the log-likelihood ratio). While these steps may be reasonable, they deserve more justification and discussion of their potential impact.

### Detailed Comments

*   The remark at the end of Section 4—that the method can work with a collection of paired data points `(x_t, x_{t+1})` rather than a single continuous trajectory—is a significant practical advantage. This broadens its applicability and could be highlighted more prominently.
*   The connection to subspace identification methods is mentioned as an open problem. While a full exploration is beyond the scope of this paper, a slightly more detailed discussion of the potential connections would be insightful for readers familiar with that literature.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a valuable and well-motivated contribution by developing a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition. The proposed kDMD method is theoretically sound and provides a powerful, general-purpose tool for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The main weaknesses lie in the experimental evaluation, which lacks comparisons to key baselines and sufficient depth to fully convince the reader of the method's practical superiority.

Despite these weaknesses, the core idea is strong, novel, and likely to be of significant interest to the NeurIPS community. I believe the paper is a solid contribution. I would recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the weaknesses in the experimental section, particularly by adding a comparison to Extended DMD [35], in the final version of the paper.
