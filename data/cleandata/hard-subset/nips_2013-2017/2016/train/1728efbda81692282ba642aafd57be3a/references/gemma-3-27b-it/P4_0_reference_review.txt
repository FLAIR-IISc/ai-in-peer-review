This paper introduces a kernelized Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) method for Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to extend standard DMD by operating in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), allowing for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions, unlike the linear monomials used in traditional DMD. The authors present an algorithm based on Krylov subspace calculations and provide a robustification strategy using POD. The method is demonstrated on synthetic data (toy system and Henon map) and applied to prediction, dynamic pattern recognition, and change-point detection.

The paper builds upon established work in spectral analysis of dynamical systems, including the Koopman operator theory and DMD (references [16, 25, 26]). It appropriately positions itself within the context of existing decomposition techniques like POD ([12]) and discusses related works such as sparsity-promoting DMD ([13]) and extended DMD ([35]). The introduction clearly articulates the motivation for extending DMD with kernel methods, addressing the limitations of linear approximations for strongly nonlinear systems. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of RKHS and kernel methods to DMD is a valid extension of existing techniques. The use of kernel functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions offers potential benefits for analyzing highly nonlinear systems.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical development, including the derivation of the algorithm and theorems 1 and 2, appears mathematically correct and well-motivated. The connection to the standard DMD framework is clearly explained. The inclusion of a POD-based robustification step is a practical consideration.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is standard, and the key concepts are explained adequately. The flow from the introduction of background material to the proposed method and applications is logical.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The inclusion of empirical examples, although limited in scope, demonstrates the feasibility of the proposed method and provides initial validation. The application examples (prediction, recognition, change-point detection) are relevant and showcase potential use cases.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical section relies on a toy system and the Henon map. The results, while promising, lack a rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods on more complex and realistic datasets. The parameter selection for the kernel functions (degree and width) is not discussed in detail and may significantly influence the results.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While the paper mentions the possibility of ill-conditioning, a deeper discussion of the computational cost and scalability of the proposed method, particularly for high-dimensional data, is missing. The use of kernel methods can be computationally expensive, and the authors do not adequately address this issue.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification for POD:** The use of POD for robustification is motivated by analogy to standard DMD, but a more thorough theoretical justification for its effectiveness in the kernelized setting would strengthen the paper.
*   **Connections to Existing Kernel Methods:** The paper doesn't fully explore the relationship between the proposed method and other established kernel-based approaches for dynamical systems modeling, such as kernel state-space methods or Gaussian process dynamical models ([17, 37]). A more comprehensive discussion of these connections would contextualize the contribution.
*   **Discussion of Kernel Choice:**  The choice of kernel function can be critical, and the paper does not offer guidance on selecting the appropriate kernel for different types of dynamical systems.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods, addressing a limitation of the standard technique for highly nonlinear systems.
*   The mathematical development is rigorous and well-explained, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.
*   The inclusion of empirical examples, though limited, demonstrates the feasibility of the approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing techniques on challenging datasets.
*   The discussion of computational complexity and scalability is lacking.
*   The connection to related work in kernel-based dynamical systems modeling could be strengthened.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of dynamical systems modeling. However, the limited empirical evaluation and lack of detailed discussion regarding computational cost and connections to existing kernel methods raise concerns.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should prioritize expanding the empirical evaluation with more complex datasets and a comparison to established methods, providing a more in-depth discussion of computational complexity, and elaborating on the relationship between the proposed method and other kernel-based techniques.
