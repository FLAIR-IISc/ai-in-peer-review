This paper presents a method for spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), termed kernel DMD. The central idea is to apply the principles of Koopman operator theory, which provides a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics in an infinite-dimensional function space, within an RKHS. Standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear combinations of observables. By lifting the state space to an RKHS via a kernel function, the proposed method can approximate these eigenfunctions using a much richer, nonlinear set of basis functions implicitly defined by the kernel. The paper first derives a direct algorithm based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization of the Krylov subspace in the feature space. Recognizing potential numerical instability, a common issue in DMD methods, the authors then propose a more robust algorithm. This robust version first projects the data onto its principal components in the feature space (via Kernel PCA) before computing the spectral decomposition. The work is related to the broader fields of Koopman operator analysis [16], DMD [25, 26], and kernel methods [28]. It is particularly close in spirit to Extended DMD [35], which uses an explicit dictionary of basis functions, whereas the proposed method uses an implicit dictionary defined by the kernel. The authors demonstrate the utility of the extracted Koopman modes as features for downstream tasks including state prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

### Strengths

1.  **Technical Quality and Soundness:** The paper is technically sound. The extension of the DMD framework to RKHS is well-founded and the derivations leading to Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 are clear and appear correct. The connection between the Arnoldi method interpretation of DMD and the kernelized version is well-established. A significant strength is the authors' acknowledgment of the potential numerical instability of a direct kernelization (Section 3) and the subsequent development of a robust algorithm based on projection onto kernel principal components (Section 4). This mirrors the development of robust methods in the standard DMD literature (e.g., [33]) and demonstrates a thorough understanding of the problem's practical aspects.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It provides a concise but sufficient background on Koopman operator theory and DMD, making it accessible to readers who may not be experts in fluid dynamics where DMD originated. The progression from the theoretical foundation to the direct algorithm and finally to the robustified, practical algorithm is logical and easy to follow.

3.  **Originality and Significance:** The combination of DMD with kernel methods is an elegant and powerful idea. While the concept of using richer observables is not entirely new (e.g., Extended DMD [35]), this paper presents a principled, non-parametric framework for doing so via the kernel trick, avoiding the need to manually specify a dictionary of basis functions. The significance of the work is amplified by the applications presented in Section 7. Moving beyond simple spectral analysis, the paper demonstrates how the learned Koopman spectral features can be effectively used for complex machine learning tasks like prediction, embedding/recognition, and change-point detection. This bridges the gap between a method popular in the physical sciences and core problems in the machine learning community, suggesting a high potential for impact and future work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Relation to and Comparison with Extended DMD (eDMD):** The paper mentions eDMD [35] as related work but does not sufficiently explore the relationship. Kernel DMD can be interpreted as a specific instance of eDMD where the dictionary of observables consists of the feature map functions {k(x, ·)}. A more detailed discussion of the connections, advantages, and disadvantages would significantly strengthen the paper's positioning. For example, kernel DMD provides a systematic way to introduce a rich dictionary, but at the cost of the pre-image problem and potentially higher computational complexity for kernel matrix calculations. An empirical comparison against eDMD with a well-chosen dictionary on one of the tasks would be highly valuable.

2.  **The Pre-image Problem:** The prediction application (Section 7) relies on solving the pre-image problem to map the predicted state in the feature space back to the input space. This is a well-known and challenging problem for kernel methods, often ill-posed and lacking a unique solution. The paper proposes a method based on [18] but the description is brief. The quality and reliability of the prediction are critically dependent on this step. A more in-depth discussion of the limitations and potential failure modes of the pre-image method is warranted. For instance, how does prediction accuracy degrade as the prediction horizon increases?

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the applications are compelling, the empirical validation could be more rigorous.
    *   **Lack of Baselines:** The experiments primarily serve as demonstrations. To establish the method's effectiveness, comparisons against relevant baselines are necessary. For prediction, comparisons to standard time-series models like Gaussian Process Dynamical Models [34] or even standard DMD would be informative. For change-point detection, the comparison to "kernel change detection method [7]" is useful, but the description of this baseline and the experimental setup is minimal.
    *   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of kernel methods is highly sensitive to the choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., Gaussian width). The paper states which kernels were used but provides little justification for the choice or analysis of how sensitive the results are to these hyperparameters. A discussion on the methodology for kernel selection would improve the paper's practical utility.
    *   **Quantitative Detail:** The analysis of the Hénon map results is qualitative ("seem to be approximately estimated"). Providing quantitative error metrics on the estimated eigenvalues would make the validation stronger.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a technically sound, novel, and elegant fusion of Koopman operator theory (via DMD) and kernel methods.
*   The work is well-motivated, addressing the limitation of standard DMD's linear observables. The development of a robust algorithm shows practical foresight.
*   It demonstrates significant potential by applying the extracted spectral features to a diverse set of important machine learning problems, thereby broadening the applicability of DMD.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-structured.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The novelty is somewhat tempered by its close relationship to Extended DMD [35], a connection that is not discussed in sufficient depth or evaluated empirically.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks rigorous comparisons to baseline methods, making it difficult to assess the practical state-of-the-art advancement.
*   Key practical aspects, such as the challenges of the pre-image problem for prediction and the methodology for kernel selection, are not fully addressed.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper with a strong core contribution. The weaknesses identified, primarily concerning the experimental depth and positioning relative to prior work, are addressable. The strengths in technical soundness, originality, and potential significance outweigh these concerns. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the NIPS community by connecting a powerful physical modeling technique with mainstream machine learning tools and problems.
