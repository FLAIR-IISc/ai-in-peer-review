Summary
The paper proposes a kernelized variant of dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis. The main idea is to perform DMD in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), treating the sequence of feature-mapped snapshots ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ) as a Krylov sequence and computing Ritz values/vectors of the Koopman operator projected onto the span of these features. A “robust” version projects onto kernel PCA directions (akin to POD truncation in standard DMD) to mitigate ill-conditioning and rank deficiency. Two theorems establish relationships between the computed eigenpairs and Koopman eigenpairs under assumptions. The paper also sketches three applications: short-term prediction via a preimage step, recognition via subspace angles between sets of Koopman eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection using a kernelized exponential-family view. Experiments include a toy polynomial system, the Henon map, an MDS visualization on motion-capture data, and a qualitative changepoint example on a Lorenz-like system.

Relation to prior work
This line of work is closely related to Extended DMD (EDMD) and kernel EDMD (Williams et al., J. Nonlinear Science 2015), which explicitly introduce user-chosen feature maps and a kernelized implementation to approximate the Koopman operator. The robustification via projection onto (kernel) POD directions also mirrors common DMD practice (exact DMD, SVD/POD truncation) and later kernel DMD implementations. There is also close overlap with subsequent EDMD variants (e.g., Hankel/Time-Delay DMD, sEDMD/variational EDMD, and connections to subspace identification). The paper cites EDMD [35] but underplays the overlap; the presented algorithm is, in essence, EDMD with an implicit kernel map and a POD-style truncation.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: enriching the DMD basis using RKHS to capture nonlinear observables better than linear monomials.
- Conceptually simple kernel-only implementation: all computations expressed via Gram matrices; no need to explicitly enumerate basis functions.
- The “robust” variant via kernel PCA mirrors best practice in DMD (truncation), and the algebra connecting reduced operators to Gram matrices is useful in practice.
- Theorems attempt to connect the computed eigenpairs to Koopman eigenpairs and clarify when recovery is possible.
- The paper hints at interesting downstream uses (prediction, subspace-based recognition, change detection).

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Novelty: The core contribution overlaps substantially with EDMD and kernel EDMD (Williams et al., 2015), which already introduced dictionary-based Koopman approximations and a kernel trick; several later works formalize convergence/consistency. The present paper does not clearly articulate what is fundamentally new beyond known kernel EDMD and standard DMD robustification.
- Theoretical correctness and rigor:
  - Theorem 1’s proof contains inaccuracies. In particular, the step “F = R−1PτR = M−1τ KHMτ ⇒ MτF = KHMτ” is not correct: Mτ is rectangular (no inverse), and DMD approximates KHMτ via a projection QτQτ* M+, not equality. The argument should explicitly use projected dynamics MτF = QτQτ* M+ and standard Arnoldi/DMD relations.
  - Claims like “the eigenfunctions are fully recovered if ϕ(xτ) ∈ span{ϕ(x0),…,ϕ(xτ−1)}” are too strong. Recovery requires that the invariant subspace of interest is contained in the feature subspace and that the data excite those modes; simple linear dependence of snapshots is insufficient.
  - The robust method centers data (kernel PCA). Centering changes the feature subspace and is not standard for Koopman approximation; the effect on the operator and consistency should be justified. The expressions use Ḡ−1 without addressing rank deficiency (pseudoinverse) and required conditions.
  - Assumptions in Theorem 2 (“φj ∈ Φ and κj ∈ span(U)”) are strong and essentially presuppose that the kernel PCA subspace already contains Koopman eigenfunctions; the practical relevance and identifiability should be discussed.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and largely qualitative:
  - Only a toy system and a Henon example with a scatter of eigenvalues; no comparisons to EDMD/kernel EDMD, exact DMD, Hankel-DMD, or other baselines on standard benchmarks.
  - For chaotic systems, Koopman spectra are typically continuous; interpreting “equilibrium eigenvalues” from global data is problematic without localization.
  - The recognition experiment uses an MDS plot; no classification metrics. The change-point section uses a heuristic derivation; no quantitative detection delay/false alarm evaluation or baselines beyond a single kernel method.
  - No study of sensitivity to kernel choice/width, truncation level p, or trajectory length; no complexity analysis.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Notation is overloaded (ϕ feature map vs. φ eigenfunction) and inconsistent; Hermitian transpose and RKHS inner product are conflated in places.
  - Several derivations are compressed or rely on informal identities; conditions (distinct eigenvalues, rank conditions, pseudoinverses) are not carefully stated.
  - References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted; some key related works are missing or undercited (e.g., kernel EDMD variants, variational EDMD, Hankel-DMD/time-delay embedding connections).

Quality
Technically plausible but with errors/overstatements in proofs and insufficient rigor on assumptions and centering. The empirical support is too weak and lacks quantitative, comparative evaluation.

Clarity
Readable but needs substantial polishing in notation, proofs, and precise statements of assumptions. The writing has grammatical issues and ambiguous uses of symbols. The bibliography needs to be fixed and expanded.

Originality
Limited. The core idea matches known EDMD with a kernel implementation and POD truncation. The paper does not clearly differentiate itself from kernel EDMD or provide new theoretical guarantees.

Significance
Potentially useful in practice, but given existing kernel EDMD literature, the incremental contribution and lack of thorough experiments reduce impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical kernelized DMD recipe expressed purely via Gram matrices.
- Attempts to connect reduced operators to Koopman spectra and to offer downstream applications.
- Topic of broad interest at NeurIPS (operator-theoretic learning of dynamics).

Cons for acceptance
- Overlaps significantly with prior EDMD/kernel EDMD; novelty unclear.
- Theoretical inaccuracies and strong/unjustified claims.
- Weak, mostly qualitative experiments; no strong baselines or quantitative metrics.
- Presentation and references need considerable improvement.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clearly position the method with respect to EDMD and kernel EDMD; cite and discuss differences. Show equivalence/advantages (e.g., conditioning, complexity).
- Correct Theorem 1 and related derivations to explicitly use projected dynamics; state all assumptions and use pseudoinverses where appropriate.
- Justify centering in the Koopman context or provide an uncentered variant; analyze its effect.
- Provide convergence/error analysis under ergodicity assumptions, or at least discuss identifiability and consistency.
- Add comprehensive experiments with quantitative metrics and baselines (EDMD/kernel EDMD, exact DMD, Hankel-DMD) on standard benchmarks (Duffing, FitzHugh–Nagumo, cylinder wake). Include sensitivity to kernel/truncation and ablation of the robust step.
- For applications, report classification accuracy (recognition) and standard CPD metrics (ARL, detection delay) against strong baselines.

Recommendation: Reject (weak). The topic is important, but the novelty over existing kernel EDMD is limited, theory has issues, and the empirical evaluation is insufficient for NeurIPS. With a stronger positioning relative to prior work, corrected analysis, and rigorous experiments, the contribution could become publishable.
