Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The main idea is to apply a Krylov/Arnoldi-style decomposition to feature-mapped observables ϕ(x), obtaining Ritz values/vectors that approximate Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions in H. Theoretical statements (Theorem 1) show that, under distinct eigenvalues and standard Arnoldi assumptions, the feature snapshots admit a finite expansion with empirical Ritz values and vectors; Theorem 2 connects the eigenstructure of a projected operator F̂ (obtained via kernel PCA) to Koopman eigenpairs under assumptions that Koopman eigenfunctions are representable in the span of the retained kernel principal components. The authors also provide a “robust” variant that projects onto truncated kernel-POD directions to mitigate ill-conditioning, and outline three applications: short-term prediction via pre-image reconstruction, dynamics recognition using subspace distances (kernel principal angles), and sequential change-point detection based on a CUSUM-like score derived from the kernelized Koopman prediction model. Experiments include a toy system, the Hénon map, and small illustrative examples for the applications.

This line of work is closely related to dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) and its variants. In particular, Extended DMD (EDMD; Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, 2015) approximates the Koopman operator on a user-chosen dictionary; EDMD includes kernelization to implicitly handle very large dictionaries via Gram matrices (commonly referred to as kernel DMD/KDMD). Exact and optimized DMD variants address numerical robustness; sparsity-promoting DMD provides model parsimony. The present paper’s method is largely an RKHS/Gram-matrix reformulation of EDMD/DMD with an Arnoldi perspective, plus a practical stabilization via kernel PCA.

Strengths:
- Clear and principled operator-theoretic framing: mapping to RKHS and projecting the Koopman operator onto a data-driven subspace is well motivated.
- The Arnoldi/Krylov interpretation and derivations in feature space connect nicely to classical DMD and provide intuition for Ritz values/vectors in RKHS.
- The robustified variant via kernel PCA is practical and aligns with best practices (truncation/regularization).
- The method works with pairs of consecutive samples, not necessarily a single contiguous trajectory, which broadens applicability.
- Applications explored (prediction, recognition, change detection) hint at the utility of Koopman features beyond pure spectral analysis.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty relative to EDMD/KDMD is limited. EDMD already formulates Koopman regression on a feature/dictionary space and shows how to kernelize it with Gram matrices. The current paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contribution or fully credit overlapping techniques.
- Some theoretical statements are too strong or inaccurate. For instance, “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is generally false except under specific measure-preserving/isometric settings; dissipative systems exhibit eigenvalues inside the unit disk. Claims about “fully recovering” eigenfunctions when ψ=0 only guarantee interpolation on the data subspace, not true recovery.
- Theorem 2 rests on strong assumptions (eigenfunctions in Φ and in span(U)); discussion on when these hold (e.g., universality of kernels, sampling richness, and truncation effects) is minimal.
- Empirical evaluation is very limited: mostly qualitative eigenvalue plots for toy/Hénon, and illustrative application figures. There are no quantitative metrics (e.g., spectral error vs. ground truth, prediction error vs. horizon), no baselines (DMD, EDMD, optimized DMD), and no ablations (effect of kernel choice, truncation rank, regularization).
- Important practical aspects are underdeveloped: computational complexity and scalability (O(τ^3) eigensolves on τ×τ Gram matrices), memory, hyperparameter selection (kernel width/degree), regularization (ridge/Tikhonov), and out-of-sample extensions.
- Clarity issues: notation overload between ϕ (feature map), φ (eigenfunctions), and φ̄ (empirical modes); the Gram–Schmidt in RKHS is mentioned but not fully specified for numerical implementation; references contain numerous incomplete entries.

Quality:
Technically plausible and consistent with the DMD/EDMD literature; the RKHS derivations are standard once one adopts a feature-space view. However, the paper contains inaccuracies (unit-circle claim), overly strong statements about recovery, and insufficient detail on numerical stabilization and regularization. Experimental support is weak.

Clarity:
Generally readable but could be improved. Suggestions: unify notation for feature maps vs. eigenfunctions; provide explicit pseudocode with all matrix shapes; detail how to compute orthonormal bases in RKHS via Gram matrix (e.g., Cholesky/SVD-based orthonormalization), and include computational complexity and memory costs. Fix typos and incomplete citations.

Originality:
Incremental. The core idea is closely aligned with EDMD and its kernelization; the “robustification via kernel PCA” is a natural analogue of truncated SVD/POD in DMD and has prior art. The Arnoldi perspective in RKHS is interesting but not a fundamental departure.

Significance:
Moderate at best. Kernelized Koopman analysis is practically useful, but the paper does not yet provide compelling empirical evidence or a clear theoretical advance that would move the state of the art for NeurIPS.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid operator-theoretic framing in RKHS with clear connections to DMD/Arnoldi.
- Practical robustification via kernel PCA is reasonable and useful.
- Potentially useful applications of Koopman features.

Cons for acceptance:
- Overlaps substantially with EDMD/KDMD; novelty and attribution are insufficient.
- Weak experimental validation and lack of baselines/quantitative metrics.
- Some inaccurate statements and strong assumptions without analysis.
- Limited discussion of scalability, hyperparameters, and regularization.

Recommendation: Reject in current form.

Constructive suggestions:
- Positioning and attribution: Clearly articulate how this differs from EDMD and its kernelized form (KDMD). If the contribution is primarily a unifying Arnoldi perspective and a particular robust pipeline, say so and compare directly (theoretically and empirically).
- Correct and nuance theoretical claims (e.g., eigenvalue location) and add error analyses or at least discussion of approximation properties (e.g., impact of finite data, kernel universality, truncation, and noise).
- Add regularization to the Gram-based solves (ridge/Tikhonov) and discuss its effect; report sensitivity to kernel choice and hyperparameters with cross-validation or heuristics.
- Substantially strengthen experiments: include quantitative spectral accuracy and prediction error on standard benchmarks (e.g., Duffing, Lorenz, cylinder wake/video), compare against DMD, EDMD, optimized/sparse DMD, and ablate the role of kernel type and truncation rank. Provide runtime/memory scaling with τ.
- Pre-image and applications: provide explicit algorithms (and alternatives like fixed-point pre-image or gradient-based reconstruction), and evaluate accuracy vs. baselines. For change-point detection, compare to standard kernel CUSUM/MMD detectors with ROC/AUC on multiple datasets.
- Improve clarity, notation, and citation completeness; include precise pseudocode and implementation details (centering, handling non-consecutive data, handling noise).

With these improvements, the work could be a solid contribution documenting a practical kernel-based Koopman pipeline and its applications.
