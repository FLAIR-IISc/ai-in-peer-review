## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to utilize reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors argue that standard DMD relies on linear monomials which limits its ability to capture complex nonlinear dynamics. By operating in RKHS, they propose a method capable of approximating Koopman eigenfunctions with richer, nonlinear basis functions. They present an algorithm based on Krylov subspace construction and eigendecomposition, and propose a practical robustification technique using POD. The paper demonstrates the method on synthetic data (toy system and Henon map) and includes preliminary applications like prediction, dynamic pattern recognition, and change-point detection.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Limitation of DMD:** The paper correctly identifies a key limitation of standard DMD – its reliance on linear approximations – and proposes a theoretically sound extension to overcome it. Using RKHS allows for a richer representation of the Koopman eigenfunctions, potentially improving accuracy for strongly nonlinear systems.
* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The mathematical development is rigorous, building on the established theory of Koopman operators, DMD, and RKHS. The theorem proving the extension of DMD with reproducing kernels is well-presented.
* **Practical Robustification:** Recognizing the potential for ill-conditioning, the inclusion of a POD-based robustification step is a valuable contribution, mirroring successful strategies in standard DMD.
* **Demonstration of Applications:**  The exploration of potential applications – prediction, dynamic recognition, and change-point detection – showcases the potential utility of the proposed method beyond theoretical analysis.  The empirical results, while preliminary, are encouraging.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors do a good job of connecting the proposed method to existing literature on DMD, Koopman operators, and kernel methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:**  The empirical evaluation is a significant weakness. The experiments are limited to two relatively simple systems (a toy system and the Henon map).  The performance isn't compared to other state-of-the-art nonlinear system identification or DMD variants (e.g., extended DMD, sparse DMD) in a rigorous, quantitative way. The prediction results are qualitatively shown but lack a clear metric for evaluation.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the practical challenges, the computational cost of working in RKHS (specifically, calculating and manipulating kernel matrices) is not adequately discussed.  This could be a significant barrier to applying the method to high-dimensional or large datasets.  A discussion of scalability would be beneficial.
* **Kernel Selection:** The choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., kernel width) can significantly impact performance. The paper doesn't provide clear guidelines for kernel selection or a sensitivity analysis of the results to kernel parameters.
* **Applications are Exploratory:** The applications section feels somewhat exploratory rather than a demonstration of significant performance gains. They serve more as proof-of-concept than concrete results.
* **Related Work Could be Expanded:** While the authors cite relevant papers, a more thorough discussion of related work in nonlinear system identification and machine learning techniques for dynamical systems would be valuable.



**Quality:** 7/10 – The paper presents a technically sound and theoretically justified extension of DMD. However, the limited empirical validation weakens the overall quality.

**Clarity:** 8/10 – The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the underlying concepts.

**Originality:** 7/10 – The idea of combining DMD with RKHS is novel and represents a worthwhile extension of existing techniques.

**Significance:** 6/10 – The significance is currently limited by the lack of extensive empirical validation and discussion of computational costs. If the method can demonstrate superior performance on more complex datasets, its significance would be substantially higher.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a known limitation of DMD and proposes a principled solution.
* The theoretical foundation is strong and the proposed algorithm is well-defined.
* The inclusion of a POD-based robustification strategy enhances the practical applicability of the method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method over existing techniques.
* The computational cost and kernel selection issues are not adequately addressed.
* The applications section is exploratory and lacks quantitative results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field of dynamical systems analysis. However, in its current form, the limited empirical validation and lack of detailed discussion of practical considerations hold it back.  

**Recommendation:**

**Weak Reject.** I recommend rejecting the paper in its current form, but strongly encourage the authors to address the weaknesses identified above, particularly by conducting a more comprehensive empirical evaluation on a wider range of datasets and comparing their method to relevant baselines. A thorough discussion of computational cost and kernel selection would also be beneficial.  If these improvements are made, the paper could be a strong candidate for acceptance in a future submission.
