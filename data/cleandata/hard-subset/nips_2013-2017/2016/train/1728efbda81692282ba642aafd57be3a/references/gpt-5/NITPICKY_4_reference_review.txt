Summary
The paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in an RKHS by (i) constructing a Krylov subspace from feature-mapped observations, (ii) projecting the Koopman operator onto this subspace, and (iii) extracting empirical Ritz values/vectors via eigendecomposition to obtain “kernel DMD” modes. Because direct Gram–Schmidt in feature space is numerically fragile, a second, “robustified” variant projects onto kernel PCA (POD) directions before estimating the finite-dimensional operator. The paper sketches connections to the Koopman eigenfunctions and provides two theorems indicating that, under strong assumptions, the procedure recovers eigenvalues/eigenfunctions. Applications are illustrated on toy data, the Hénon map, motion-capture embedding/recognition via kernel principal angles, and a CUSUM-style change-point detector using a kernel exponential family model.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on DMD and Koopman operator theory and is related to Arnoldi/Prony methods, exact/optimized DMD, EDMD (Williams et al.), and kernelized variants (often called KDMD). The idea of representing observables in a lifted feature space and estimating a finite operator via Gram matrices is very close to EDMD with a kernel dictionary. The POD/SVD-based robustification mirrors standard DMD practice. The paper cites EDMD [35] and DMD variants but does not clearly position itself against kernel EDMD/KDMD literature; several relevant works (e.g., kernelized EDMD and other Koopman-with-kernel papers by Klus et al., Wynn et al., etc.) are not discussed. The novelty beyond existing kernelized EDMD/KDMD is unclear.

Strengths
- Timely topic; Koopman/operator-theoretic approaches with kernels are of interest in ML and applied sciences.
- A unified, kernel-based derivation that parallels standard DMD and includes a POD/SVD-type stabilization step.
- Clear motivation for using richer (nonlinear) function spaces to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions.
- Theorem 2 offers a useful formal link between kernel PCA subspaces and Koopman eigenfunction representation, under explicit assumptions.
- Potentially broad applications; the paper outlines prediction via preimage, dynamics recognition, and change detection.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Novelty: The main algorithm is extremely close to kernel EDMD/KDMD. The paper does not convincingly differentiate itself conceptually or algorithmically from existing kernelized Koopman approaches. The “robustified” variant is analogous to exact/optimized DMD’s SVD truncation.
- Theorem 1/proof issues: The claim that F:=R^{-1}Q* M+ is a companion matrix and thus Vandermonde-diagonalizable is stated without justification. In standard Arnoldi, one obtains an upper Hessenberg matrix H=Q* K Q; a companion matrix appears only for a very particular monomial basis/shift operator. As presented, F need not be companion. The diagonalization of a general projected operator by a Vandermonde matrix is unjustified and likely incorrect. This undermines Eq. (9) and the theoretical narrative.
- Kernelization details: The Gram–Schmidt QR in RKHS is asserted to be reducible to Gram matrix operations, but the paper does not provide the concrete, numerically stable procedure (e.g., kernel Gram–Schmidt or Cholesky with pivoting, regularization). How are Q and R represented and computed without explicit features? How is R−1 computed when G is ill-conditioned?
- Assumptions: Theorem 2 assumes Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the RKHS span, and that their representers κj lie in the finite-dimensional kernel PCA subspace (U). These are very strong and typically unmet; no approximation or error bounds are provided. Statements like “eigenfunctions are fully recovered if ψ=0” are tautological and not informative about consistency.
- Empirical evaluation is very limited: only small examples, almost entirely qualitative figures, no quantitative error metrics (e.g., eigenvalue errors, prediction accuracy), no statistical robustness, no sensitivity to kernel hyperparameters or truncation rank, and no runtime/scalability analysis. Critically, there are no comparisons to EDMD/KDMD, exact/optimized DMD, or other baselines.
- Applications are underspecified: 
  - Preimage: the MDS-inspired preimage recovery is heuristic; details are insufficient for reproducibility; no comparison to standard kernel preimage methods; no quantitative results.
  - Recognition via subspace angles: evaluation is anecdotal; no accuracy, confusion matrices, or baselines.
  - Change-point detection: the proposed kernel exponential family model is unconventional (the “natural parameter” depends on f(x_{t-1})); derivation of the log-likelihood ratio and α-coefficients is unclear; the approximation that drops the second term is unjustified.
- Notation/clarity:
  - Overloaded symbols (H as RKHS, centering matrix, and adjoint; KH vs. H for centering).
  - Inconsistent or imprecise indexing (e.g., elements of M* M+), and frequent typographical errors.
  - Several steps jump over crucial derivations (e.g., how F̂ is kernelized and computed stably).
- Formatting/references:
  - Numerous incomplete references (missing years/pages), typos in author names, and inconsistent accents.
  - The code link appears de-anonymizing, which is inappropriate for a double-blind review.
- Scope/limitations: No discussion of noise, regularization, model selection, or theoretical convergence; no guidance for kernel/width selection; scalability is limited by O(τ^2) memory and O(τ^3) time for eigenproblems.

Questions for the authors
- How does your method differ substantively from kernel EDMD/KDMD? Please provide a clear theoretical and algorithmic distinction and empirical comparisons.
- Can you correct/clarify Theorem 1 and the “companion matrix” claim? If not, how do you justify Eq. (9)?
- Provide a complete kernelized and numerically stable orthogonalization procedure with regularization details.
- Can you provide quantitative experiments (with noise) and ablations (kernel choice, rank truncation), along with baselines?
- Please clarify the change-point detection derivation and define the α coefficients precisely.

Pros
- Topic is important and relevant.
- Conceptual unification of DMD/Koopman with kernels; intuitive motivation.
- The stabilized (kernel PCA) projection is sensible in practice.

Cons
- Overlaps heavily with existing KDMD/EDMD; novelty unclear.
- Theoretical gaps/errors (companion/Vandermonde claim).
- Weak, largely qualitative experiments; no baselines.
- Numerous clarity, notation, and formatting issues; possible anonymity breach.

Recommendation
Reject. Despite addressing an interesting problem, the manuscript has substantial issues in originality, theoretical correctness/rigor, clarity, and empirical validation. I encourage the authors to (i) correct the theory, (ii) present a fully kernelized, stable algorithm with complexity analysis, (iii) position clearly against kernel EDMD/KDMD with thorough empirical comparisons, and (iv) significantly improve experimental depth and presentation quality.
