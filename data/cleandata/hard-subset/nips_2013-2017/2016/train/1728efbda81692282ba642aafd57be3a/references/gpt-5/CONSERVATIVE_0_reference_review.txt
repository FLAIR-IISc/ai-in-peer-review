Summary
The paper proposes a kernelized variant of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors define the Koopman operator on a feature map in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), construct a Krylov subspace from feature-mapped snapshots, and compute empirical Ritz values/vectors via a Gram–Schmidt QR in feature space using kernel matrices. To improve numerical robustness, they project onto kernel-PCA (POD) directions and then perform an eigendecomposition of a projected operator. Two theorems relate the resulting Ritz values/vectors to Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions under strong assumptions. Small empirical demos (toy nonlinear system and Hénon map) and three proof-of-concept applications (short-term prediction via preimage, dynamics recognition via subspace angles, and sequential change-point detection) are presented.

Relation to prior work
DMD and its variants (exact DMD, optimized DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD) are well-established. Williams et al. (2015, J. Nonlinear Science) introduced extended DMD (EDMD) and a kernelized implementation (KDMD), which approximates Koopman spectra via a Galerkin/eigenproblem in a (possibly implicit) feature space. The proposed approach is very close in spirit to KDMD/EDMD: both use kernels to define rich basis functions and reduce to Gram-matrix computations, and both advocate truncation/regularization via (kernel) PCA/SVD for robustness. The “robustified” algorithm here is essentially EDMD with a kernel basis and truncation.

Strengths
- Clear, unified RKHS perspective on Koopman spectral estimation; the kernel trick is used appropriately to avoid explicit feature maps.
- Theorems 1 and 2 formally connect the finite-dimensional projected operator in feature space to Ritz values/vectors; the linear-algebraic development is sound as far as it goes.
- The paper touches on several applications and provides an accessible pipeline from data to modes to downstream tasks.
- Implementation reduces to standard linear algebra on Gram matrices, which many readers can reproduce.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited. Kernel DMD (KDMD) and EDMD already provide a kernel-based Koopman framework; the robustification via POD/kernel PCA is standard practice in DMD/EDMD. The paper acknowledges [35] but does not convincingly differentiate its contribution.
- Theory relies on strong assumptions (e.g., Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the RKHS and within the span of retained kernel-PCA directions; Krylov subspace is “sufficiently large” so ψ=0). These are rarely verifiable for nonlinear/chaotic systems with continuous spectra. No convergence/error analysis or conditions ensuring boundedness/compactness of the Koopman operator in the chosen RKHS are provided.
- A questionable claim: “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle.” This is not generally true for dissipative systems (decaying modes with |λ|<1 are typical). Please correct/qualify with precise conditions (e.g., measure-preserving dynamics).
- Numerical aspects are underdeveloped. Gram–Schmidt in RKHS is known to be unstable; the suggested kernel-PCA projection helps but no regularization (e.g., Tikhonov, ridge EDMD) or conditioning analysis is provided. Scalability is not addressed (O(τ^3) in τ snapshots; memory O(τ^2)), nor are approximations (Nyström, randomized SVD).
- Empirical validation is very weak: only two small systems, no quantitative metrics (e.g., spectral accuracy, prediction error curves, classification accuracy, change-detection delay/false alarms), no comparisons to EDMD/KDMD, exact/optimized DMD, or other baselines. Figures are qualitative; hyperparameter selection (kernel types/widths, truncation ranks) is ad hoc. The preimage-based prediction is fragile and ill-posed, yet no robustness study is provided.
- Clarity and presentation: numerous typos/grammar issues, some imprecise notation, incomplete references (many missing bibliographic fields), and an ephemeral code link. Reproducibility details (preprocessing, kernel bandwidth selection, truncation criteria, stopping tolerances) are missing.

Quality
Technically, the linear-algebraic mapping of DMD to RKHS is correct, but the main claims are not supported by rigorous analysis (e.g., consistency, approximation error, sample complexity) nor by thorough experiments. Some statements need correction (eigenvalues on unit circle). The application sections are inventive but methodologically shallow and lack quantitative evaluation.

Clarity
Moderately clear at a high level, but the manuscript needs careful editing, complete references, and more explicit algorithms/pseudocode, complexity analysis, and parameter-selection guidelines to be reproducible.

Originality
Low to moderate. The core idea largely overlaps with EDMD/KDMD; the “robustification” mirrors standard truncation practices. The paper does not articulate a crisp contribution beyond existing kernel DMD frameworks.

Significance
Currently limited. Without strong empirical comparisons and clearer theoretical guarantees, it is hard to see this displacing or advancing beyond EDMD/KDMD in practice.

Pros
- Conceptually clean RKHS formulation of Koopman/DMD with kernel trick.
- Bridges DMD with kernel PCA for robustness.
- Potentially useful unifying exposition for readers coming from physics/engineering.

Cons
- Overlaps substantially with prior EDMD/KDMD; limited novelty.
- Weak, largely qualitative experiments with no baselines or metrics.
- Strong, unverified assumptions; some incorrect or overbroad claims.
- Lacks scalability/regularization strategy and reproducibility details.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clearly delineate how this differs from and improves upon EDMD/KDMD (theory, algorithms, or performance).
- Provide quantitative evaluations on standard benchmarks (e.g., Duffing, Van der Pol, fluid datasets), comparing to exact/optimized DMD, EDMD/KDMD, with metrics (eigenvalue error against ground truth when available, reconstruction/prediction MSE vs horizon, classification accuracy, change-detection delay/false alarm).
- Add regularization (ridge EDMD), kernel/bandwidth/Rank selection (cross-validation), and report sensitivity analyses.
- Discuss operator-theoretic conditions (boundedness/compactness; impact of continuous spectrum), and fix the unit-circle statement.
- Address scalability (Nyström, randomized methods) and provide computational complexity.
- Improve writing, complete references, and provide stable, documented code.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper’s main ideas are too close to established EDMD/KDMD, and the empirical/theoretical support is insufficient for NeurIPS. With a clearer differentiation from prior work, rigorous analysis, and strong comparative experiments, a future version could be competitive.
