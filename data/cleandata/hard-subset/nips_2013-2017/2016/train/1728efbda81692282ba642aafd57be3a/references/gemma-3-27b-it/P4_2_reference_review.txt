This paper introduces a Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) method leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to extend traditional DMD, which often relies on linear monomials as basis functions, to utilize the richer representational power of kernel functions, allowing for a more accurate approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions. The authors present an algorithm based on Krylov subspace calculations and eigendecomposition, and further propose a robustification technique using POD projections to address potential ill-conditioning issues. The method is demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world data, with applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly establishing the limitations of traditional DMD and the potential benefits of a kernel-based approach. The mathematical formulation is generally sound, building on established concepts from Koopman operator theory, DMD, and RKHS. The inclusion of a POD-based robustification step is a practical consideration, acknowledging and addressing a common challenge in implementing DMD. The empirical evaluation, while limited in scope, provides initial evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed method. The connections drawn to related work, including POD, spectral methods, and other DMD variants, are reasonable and demonstrate an understanding of the existing literature. 

However, the paper has several weaknesses. Firstly, the novelty of the approach is somewhat incremental. While applying kernel methods to DMD is a natural extension, the paper doesn't fully articulate *how* this differs fundamentally from existing extended DMD methods that also operate with pre-determined basis functions. The relationship to Williams et al. (2015) [35] specifically needs further clarification, as their extended DMD shares conceptual similarities. Secondly, the theoretical analysis, while correct, lacks depth. The assumptions underlying Theorem 1 and 2, particularly the requirement for eigenfunctions to reside within the span of the POD basis (Theorem 2), are not thoroughly discussed, and their practical implications are not clearly addressed.  The practical considerations for choosing the kernel and its parameters are also glossed over; no justification for the choices made in the experiments is provided. Thirdly, the empirical evaluation is relatively limited. The toy system provides a good proof-of-concept, but the Hénon map example is not particularly compelling. More complex and higher-dimensional datasets would be needed to demonstrate the true scalability and effectiveness of the proposed method. The application sections (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) are presented briefly and lack a rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods. Fourthly, the writing quality could be improved. The paper suffers from occasional redundancies and lacks a cohesive narrative flow. The organization could be streamlined by integrating the related work more seamlessly into the discussion. Finally, the availability of the Matlab code is a positive aspect, but its documentation and ease of use should be considered. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a logical extension of DMD using kernel methods.
*   Provides a mathematically sound algorithm based on well-established principles.
*   Addresses a practical issue (ill-conditioning) with a POD-based robustification.
*   Demonstrates initial feasibility through empirical examples.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty; insufficient differentiation from existing extended DMD methods.
*   Limited theoretical depth and discussion of assumptions.
*   Limited empirical evaluation; lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Writing quality and organization could be improved.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a reasonable, albeit incremental, contribution to the field of DMD and Koopman spectral analysis. While the method is technically sound and potentially useful, the limited novelty, theoretical depth, and empirical evaluation prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. I believe the paper could benefit significantly from a more thorough comparison to related work, a more rigorous analysis of the underlying assumptions, and a more comprehensive empirical validation.  A revision addressing these points would substantially strengthen the manuscript. I recommend **weak reject** at this time.
