- Paper proposes kernel-based DMD in RKHS for Koopman spectral analysis, with a POD robustification step and applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

- Theoretical foundation is strong: Theorems 1–2 link the kernel approach to Koopman spectral analysis with sound derivations.

- Authors address numerical ill-conditioning by using POD, following established DMD practices.

- Demonstrated applications (prediction, change-point detection, locomotion recognition) are relevant and illustrate feature utility.

- Paper is well-organized and clearly written.

- Fails to clearly distinguish the method from Williams et al.'s Extended DMD and lacks a detailed comparison showing practical advantages.

- Experimental evaluation is limited (toy system, Hénon map, single locomotion dataset) and omits comparisons with Extended DMD and other methods.

- No systematic study of kernel choice, hyperparameters, or computational complexity.

- Theoretical gaps: no convergence guarantees, no error bounds for Koopman eigenvalue/eigenfunction estimation, and no conditions identifying when kernel methods outperform linear bases.

- Practical implementation details are insufficient (missing hyperparameter settings, limited reproducibility, unclear effect of POD on Koopman modes).

- Applications are underdeveloped, lacking comparisons to simpler or state-of-the-art alternatives (e.g., standard time-series prediction, explicit change-point methods).

- Claim that kernel-induced nonlinear bases capture dynamics beyond linear bases is not empirically supported; toy examples could be solvable by linear bases.

- Hénon map results are presented without quantitative performance metrics (reconstruction or prediction error) against baselines.

- Change-point detection comparison is unclear because the competing "kernel change detection method" is unspecified.

- Related work section inadequately contrasts the proposed approach with Extended DMD in basis selection, theory, and performance.

- Overall assessment: theoretically sound but insufficiently novel or empirically validated; not convincing that it outperforms existing methods.

- Recommendation: reject in current form due to limited novelty demonstration and weak experimental/theoretical support.

- Suggested improvements: thorough comparisons with Extended DMD, add convergence/error analyses, broaden experiments and datasets, report hyperparameters and complexity, clarify methodological differences, and include quantitative evaluation metrics.
