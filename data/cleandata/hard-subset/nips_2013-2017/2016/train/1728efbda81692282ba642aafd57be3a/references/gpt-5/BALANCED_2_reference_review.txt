Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a kernelized variant of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors define the Koopman operator on a feature map into an RKHS and derive an Arnoldi/DMD-like procedure in feature space: (i) form the RKHS Krylov matrix from consecutive snapshots, (ii) perform Gram–Schmidt orthogonalization in H (via Gram matrices), (iii) eigendecompose the projection of the Koopman operator, and (iv) recover Ritz values/vectors that approximate Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions. Because Gram–Schmidt in H can be ill-conditioned, they “robustify” via kernel PCA/POD truncation, leading to a low-rank projected operator whose eigendecomposition yields modes. They then illustrate small examples (toy polynomial system and Hénon map), and sketch three applications using the extracted Koopman features: short-term prediction via a preimage mapping, dynamic pattern recognition using kernel principal angles between subspaces, and sequential change-point detection with a CUSUM-like statistic in a kernel exponential family.

This work is closely related to the extended DMD (EDMD) framework of Williams, Rowley, and Kevrekidis (J. Nonlinear Sci., 2015), which approximates the Koopman operator using a finite dictionary; the kernelized version of EDMD (often called KDMD) uses the kernel trick to avoid explicit dictionaries and computes Koopman spectra from Gram matrices. The proposed “robustified” variant using a POD/kPCA basis is also standard in the DMD literature (e.g., exact/optimized DMD and SVD truncation). The applications (preimage reconstruction, subspace angles, kernel exponential family) build on known tools in kernel methods and spectral learning. The paper cites EDMD [35] but underplays the overlap; in practice the proposed algorithm reduces to KDMD.

Strengths
- Clear high-level goal: approximate Koopman spectra using nonlinear feature maps in RKHS to go beyond linear observables used by classical DMD.
- Algorithmic derivation mirrors Arnoldi/DMD in a Hilbert space and is implementable via Gram matrices; the connection to Ritz values/vectors is well-motivated.
- Robustification using kPCA/POD is practical and aligns with best practices in DMD.
- The applications section is ambitious and shows the breadth of potential uses (prediction, recognition, change detection).
- Code availability is mentioned.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Theoretical development largely repackages known KDMD/EDMD formulations without providing new guarantees (e.g., consistency, convergence rates, bias due to finite samples, or conditions for recovery). Theorem 1/2 mostly restate standard Arnoldi and projected-operator arguments, with some notational imprecision (e.g., MτF = KHMτ is claimed while Mτ is not square/invertible; care with operators between H and C^τ is needed).
- The claim “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is not generally correct; moduli ≤ 1 can occur, with unit modulus only for measure-preserving dynamics.
- Empirical validation is minimal, qualitative, and lacks baselines. Plots of eigenvalues on two small systems, an MDS embedding for motion data, and a change-score trace do not quantify accuracy or robustness, nor compare against DMD/EDMD/KDMD.
- Preimage reconstruction via MDS-like distances is heuristic and may be unstable/non-unique; no error analysis is provided.

Clarity
- Exposition is generally understandable but contains typos and notational ambiguities (e.g., missing 1/τ in the centered feature mean; 1τ notation; switching between bold and scalar φ).
- Computational complexity, memory usage, and numerical conditioning (e.g., Gram matrix conditioning, choice of truncation rank) are not discussed.
- Kernel choice/parameter selection and their effect on spectra are not analyzed.

Originality
- The main algorithmic idea (kernelizing DMD/EDMD via Gram matrices and SVD/POD truncation) has been published (EDMD and KDMD). The paper does not clearly differentiate its contribution beyond re-deriving KDMD via Gram–Schmidt in H and aggregating applications.

Significance
- Without new theory or strong empirical evidence, the contribution over EDMD/KDMD is incremental. The applications are interesting but not convincingly demonstrated. It is hard to see practitioners preferring this over established KDMD unless stronger benefits are shown.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Solid, pedagogical framing of Koopman analysis in RKHS; potentially useful tutorial for a physics/engineering audience.
- Practical algorithm implementable with kernels; code available.
- Broad application vision.

Cons:
- Limited novelty relative to EDMD/KDMD and truncated DMD variants.
- Weak empirical validation; no quantitative benchmarks or comparisons.
- Some technical inaccuracies/ambiguities and missing details on scalability and parameter selection.

Suggestions for improvement
- Positioning and citations: Clearly relate and compare to EDMD/KDMD (Williams et al., 2015; KDMD follow-ups), exact/optimized DMD (Tu et al., Chen et al.), and convergence analyses (e.g., Korda & Mezić, 2018; Klus et al., 2018). Explain precisely what is new.
- Theory: Provide conditions under which the kernel-Krylov subspace captures Koopman invariant subspaces; discuss convergence as τ→∞ and p→∞ (dictionary size); clarify assumptions in Theorems 1–2; fix the attractor/eigenvalue statement.
- Algorithms and numerics: Detail complexity O(τ^3), memory O(τ^2); discuss regularization (Tikhonov, spectral truncation), handling repeated/near-repeated eigenvalues, and kernel parameter selection (e.g., median heuristic, cross-validation).
- Experiments: Add quantitative benchmarks on standard systems (Lorenz-63/96, FitzHugh–Nagumo, fluid datasets) comparing DMD, EDMD, KDMD, and your variants, with metrics: eigenvalue/eigenfunction accuracy, short-horizon prediction RMSE, robustness to noise, sample complexity. Include ablations on truncation rank and kernel choices.
- Preimage: Compare several preimage strategies (fixed-point iteration for RBF kernels, kernel ridge regression decoders, Nyström methods), and report reconstruction/prediction errors.
- Applications: For recognition, report classification accuracy vs. baselines (e.g., kernel PCA subspace angles, temporal CNN/RNN) with statistical significance. For change detection, evaluate detection delay/false alarm vs. kernel change-point methods on synthetic and real data.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper addresses an important topic and is technically competent, but the core method overlaps substantially with established KDMD/EDMD, the theoretical contributions are limited, and the experimental evidence is insufficient and largely qualitative. With clearer positioning, stronger theory or novel algorithmic elements, and rigorous empirical comparisons, this work could become a solid contribution in a future submission.
