**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis by extending the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) framework to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The central idea is that standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear combination of observables, which can be limiting for highly nonlinear systems. By leveraging the kernel trick, the proposed "kernel DMD" (kDMD) implicitly works with a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions in an RKHS, allowing for potentially more accurate approximations of the Koopman operator's spectrum and eigenfunctions. The authors present two algorithmic variants: a direct formulation based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space and a more numerically robust version that utilizes a projection onto principal components found via Kernel PCA, analogous to how standard DMD is often robustified using POD. The paper situates this work within the context of decomposition techniques for dynamical systems (e.g., POD, DMD) and spectral methods from machine learning and control (e.g., subspace identification). The proposed approach can be seen as a natural synthesis of DMD, a popular tool in fluid dynamics, and kernel methods, a cornerstone of machine learning. The authors correctly note the similarity of their robust version to Extended DMD (eDMD) [35], which also uses a pre-determined dictionary of basis functions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Soundness:** The paper's core theoretical contribution is well-founded. The extension of the DMD principle to an RKHS is a logical and elegant step. The derivation, particularly Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, clearly establishes the connection between the proposed algorithm and the underlying Koopman operator theory. The analogy drawn between the POD-based robustification of standard DMD and the Kernel PCA-based robustification of kDMD is clear and compelling.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It provides a concise but sufficient background on the Koopman operator and DMD before introducing the kernelized extension. The mathematical formalism is presented clearly, and the algorithms are laid out in a step-by-step manner.

3.  **Potential Significance:** The application of powerful nonlinear function approximation techniques from machine learning to the problem of Koopman spectral analysis is a promising research direction. The paper demonstrates the potential utility of the extracted Koopman modes as features for various downstream tasks, including prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation and Baselines:** This is the most significant weakness of the paper. While the proposed method is interesting, the empirical evidence supporting its superiority over existing techniques is critically lacking.
    *   **Core Task Evaluation:** The experiments in Section 6 are purely illustrative. For the toy system, the method finds the true eigenvalues, but there is no comparison to what standard DMD or eDMD would produce. For the Hénon map, the result is qualitative ("seem to be approximately estimated"), which is insufficient for a rigorous evaluation. A robust evaluation would require quantitative comparisons against established baselines like standard DMD, optimized DMD [8], and especially Extended DMD [35] on a suite of benchmark nonlinear systems.
    *   **Application Evaluation:** The applications in Section 7 are presented as proofs-of-concept without any comparative analysis. For prediction, the method should be benchmarked against standard time-series models (e.g., Gaussian Processes, LSTMs). For change-point detection, the proposed score is compared only to another kernel-based method [7], without comparison to more conventional CUSUM variants or other standard algorithms. For dynamics recognition, performance metrics are needed to assess the quality of the embedding. The lack of comparison to strong, established baselines makes it impossible to judge the practical utility of the proposed method.

2.  **Limited Novelty over Extended DMD:** The authors acknowledge the similarity to Extended DMD [35], which generalizes DMD to an arbitrary dictionary of observable functions. The proposed kDMD can be viewed as a specific instance of eDMD where the dictionary is implicitly defined by the kernel function. While the use of kernels is a valid and useful contribution, the paper does not sufficiently articulate the conceptual or practical advantages of this implicit approach over an explicit, user-defined dictionary in eDMD. Is the primary benefit convenience, or is there a fundamental performance advantage? This question is not addressed, weakening the claim of originality.

3.  **Lack of Ablation and Parameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The performance of kDMD is heavily dependent on the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters (e.g., kernel width for an RBF kernel, degree for a polynomial kernel), as well as the number of principal components `p` retained in the robust version. The paper provides no analysis or discussion of how these choices affect the results. A thorough empirical study would investigate the sensitivity to these parameters and provide guidance on their selection.

4.  **Scalability Concerns:** The proposed method requires the computation and eigendecomposition of Gram matrices of size τ×τ, where τ is the number of data points. This O(τ³) complexity makes the method computationally prohibitive for long time series, a common scenario in dynamical systems analysis. While this is a known limitation of many kernel methods, it is a practical concern that should be acknowledged and discussed.

**Recommendation**

While the paper presents a methodologically sound and elegant combination of DMD and kernel methods, it falls short of the standards required for publication at NIPS due to a profound lack of rigorous empirical validation. The core claims about the method's capabilities are not substantiated through thorough comparisons with established and relevant baselines. The experiments are anecdotal rather than quantitative, and key aspects such as parameter sensitivity and scalability are not addressed. Furthermore, the novelty relative to closely related work like Extended DMD is not strongly argued.

The paper introduces a promising idea, but in its current form, it reads more like a position paper or a workshop contribution. It requires a substantial expansion of the experimental section with rigorous, quantitative comparisons against strong baselines to demonstrate a clear advantage over the state of the art.

**Decision: Reject**
