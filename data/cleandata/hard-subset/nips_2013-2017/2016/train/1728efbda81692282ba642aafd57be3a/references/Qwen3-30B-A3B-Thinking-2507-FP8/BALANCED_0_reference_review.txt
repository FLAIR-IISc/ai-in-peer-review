

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors extend traditional DMD, which approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials, to use richer nonlinear basis functions through kernel methods. The core contribution is a modal decomposition algorithm based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in RKHS, followed by a robustification step using POD (Principal Orthogonal Decomposition) to handle ill-conditioning. The authors demonstrate applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition on synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides clear mathematical development with well-structured theorems (Theorems 1 and 2) that establish the connection between the kernel-based DMD and Koopman spectral analysis. The derivation of how the kernel method extends traditional DMD is particularly well-executed.

2. **Practical implementation considerations:** The authors address a critical practical limitation of DMD through the POD-based robustification step (Section 4), which is well-motivated and effectively addresses the ill-conditioning problem that plagues many DMD implementations.

3. **Relevant applications:** The paper demonstrates the utility of the method through meaningful applications beyond basic spectral analysis, including state prediction via preimage estimation, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection. The empirical results (Figures 2-4) are well-presented and support the claims.

4. **Clear exposition:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good connections between the theoretical development and practical implementation. The relationship between traditional DMD and the kernel extension is well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental novelty:** While the kernel-based approach is theoretically sound, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from existing kernel-based DMD extensions. Williams et al.'s extended DMD (2015) already uses pre-determined basis functions, and the connection to this work is mentioned but not thoroughly compared. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of how this approach differs from and improves upon existing kernel-based methods.

2. **Limited theoretical analysis:** The paper lacks theoretical analysis of convergence rates or error bounds for the kernel-based method. It would be valuable to have a discussion of how the choice of kernel affects the quality of approximation and the conditions under which the method is guaranteed to perform well.

3. **Inadequate comparison with state-of-the-art:** The empirical evaluation primarily focuses on demonstrating the method's functionality rather than comparing it against relevant state-of-the-art methods. A more thorough comparison with existing DMD variants and kernel-based approaches would strengthen the paper's significance claim.

4. **Scalability concerns:** The paper does not address the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly for large datasets. The Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in RKHS can become computationally intensive, and the paper would benefit from discussing practical limitations and potential scalability solutions.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. Expand the related work section to more thoroughly compare with Williams et al.'s extended DMD (2015) and other recent kernel-based DMD approaches. Explicitly state how the proposed method differs and why these differences matter.

2. Add a theoretical section analyzing the error bounds or convergence properties of the kernel-based DMD algorithm. This would strengthen the quality assessment of the paper.

3. Include a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods in the empirical evaluation. For example, compare the proposed method with extended DMD, exact DMD, and optimized DMD on standard benchmarks.

4. Discuss the computational complexity and scalability of the method, including potential approximations that could make it applicable to larger datasets. Consider adding a brief analysis of the trade-offs between kernel choice and computational cost.

5. Clarify the specific limitations of the method in the discussion. For example, under what conditions might the kernel choice become critical, and how does the method handle noisy data?

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods for Koopman spectral analysis. The theoretical development is strong, and the practical applications are relevant. However, the novelty is somewhat incremental compared to existing work, and the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods and stronger theoretical analysis. The practical robustification step is a valuable contribution, but the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how the kernel-based approach offers significant advantages over existing methods.

The paper meets the technical quality standards of NIPS but falls short on demonstrating sufficient significance and novelty. With the suggested improvements, particularly a more thorough comparison with existing methods and additional theoretical analysis, this could become a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper makes a useful incremental contribution to the literature on Koopman spectral analysis, but the significance claim is not fully supported by the current empirical and theoretical evidence.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. The paper has merit but requires significant revisions to better establish its novelty and significance relative to existing work.
