Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes doing Koopman spectral analysis in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The core idea is to carry out dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) on the feature-mapped observables ϕ(x), which yields a finite-dimensional projection of the Koopman operator KH onto the Krylov subspace span{ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)}. The authors present (i) a kernelized DMD algorithm via Gram–Schmidt in feature space and an eigen-decomposition of the projected operator, and (ii) a “robustified” variant that projects onto kernel PCA directions (analogous to truncated POD/Exact DMD). They provide two theorems linking the computed Ritz values/vectors to Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions under assumptions, propose applications (short-term prediction via pre-image, dynamics recognition via kernel principal angles between subspaces, and sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-like score), and show small empirical illustrations on a toy system, the Hénon map, MoCap sequences, and a Lorenz change detection example.

This work is closely related to Extended DMD (EDMD) and especially kernel EDMD/DMD (using implicit feature maps) introduced by Williams et al. (2015) and follow-ups, as well as Exact/Optimized DMD and sparsity-promoting DMD. The robustification via truncated kernel PCA mirrors the common practice of projecting onto leading POD/SVD directions in DMD. The applications build on existing ideas: kernel pre-image estimation, subspace angles in RKHS, and kernel change-point detection.

Quality:
- Theory: The high-level operator-theoretic framing is sound: KH is linear on H, and projecting onto a data-driven subspace gives Ritz approximations. Theorem 1 (feature-space Arnoldi/companion matrix derivation) and Theorem 2 (conditions under which kernel PCA subspace recovers Koopman eigenpairs) are plausible, but some steps are imprecise. In particular, the proof of Theorem 1 asserts Mτ F = KH Mτ via F = R−1Q*KHQR and Mτ = QR; in general Q Q* is a projection, so KH Mτ = Mτ F + residual, and the residual treatment should be explicit (as in standard Arnoldi relations). Theorem 2 relies on strong assumptions (κj ∈ span(U)), which are reasonable as an approximation statement but limit identifiability claims. These caveats can be fixed by tightening the derivations and consistently accounting for residuals.
- Algorithms: The “kernel DMD” procedure and the “robustified” version via kernel PCA are reasonable and computationally implementable with Gram matrices. However, there is no complexity or numerical stability analysis (memory O(τ^2), time O(τ^3) are typical for kernel methods), nor guidance on kernel/regularization choices, truncation criteria, or conditioning.
- Experiments: The empirical section is weak. The toy/Hénon examples show eigenvalue plots but no quantitative error versus ground truth or baselines. The recognition example shows an MDS embedding qualitatively; there is no classification accuracy or comparison against EDMD/KEDMD or other representation methods. The change-point detection compares to a “kernel change detection method” but details, metrics, and statistical operating points are not reported. Without quantitative evaluation and solid baselines, it is hard to assess the practical benefit.
- Claims vs. evidence: The paper argues that kernelization affords richer eigenfunction approximation; this is believable, but the paper does not empirically demonstrate improvements over EDMD/KEDMD or Exact/Optimized DMD.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally understandable but has numerous grammatical issues and typos (e.g., “a way to robustly it”), inconsistent notation (e.g., use of M−1τ), and occasional steps that skip residual terms or dimensional checks. Some symbols are overloaded and the roles of centered vs. uncentered features are easy to confuse. The applications section is terse and lacks sufficient detail to reproduce results (e.g., hyperparameters, pre-image solver specifics).
- References are often incomplete and some crucial related work is under-cited or not clearly differentiated (kernel EDMD).

Originality:
- The central idea—performing DMD/Koopman spectral estimation with kernels—is not new; EDMD with large dictionaries and its kernelized variant have appeared in the literature and are even briefly cited. The robustification via kernel PCA is analogous to standard truncated SVD practices. The particular derivation via Gram–Schmidt in H and the two theorems are a reasonable re-framing but seem incremental. The applications (pre-image prediction, subspace-angle recognition, CUSUM score) are creative combinations but not deeply novel methodologically.

Significance:
- Conceptually, RKHS-based Koopman analysis is important and has influenced practice. For NeurIPS, the present contribution would be more compelling with: (i) clear theoretical advances over EDMD/KEDMD (e.g., generalization guarantees, consistency with finite data, stability bounds), and/or (ii) strong empirical evidence on challenging nonlinear systems with quantitative metrics and comparisons demonstrating clear gains. As written, the impact is limited by incremental novelty and underpowered evaluation.

Pros:
- Clear operator-theoretic motivation; connects Koopman analysis with RKHS tools.
- Practical algorithmic recipe with kernel matrices; robust version aligns with best practices.
- Theorems give intuition about when kernel PCA subspaces recover Koopman structure.
- Interesting application ideas that could be useful in practice.

Cons:
- Novelty over EDMD/kernel EDMD is limited and not crisply articulated.
- Theoretical derivations need tightening (explicit residuals; remove questionable equalities).
- Experiments are qualitative and small-scale; lack baselines and quantitative metrics.
- No discussion of computational complexity, regularization, or hyperparameter selection.
- Writing/notation issues and incomplete references.

Suggestions/questions for the authors:
- Clarify the relation and differences to EDMD and kernel EDMD: are your algorithms equivalent under certain choices? When do they differ and why is that beneficial?
- Fix the derivations to account for residuals (Arnoldi relation) and avoid undefined inverses; provide conditions under which equalities hold.
- Add quantitative evaluations: eigenvalue/eigenfunction errors, prediction RMSE vs. time horizon, classification accuracy for dynamics recognition, ROC/CUSUM performance for change detection; compare against EDMD/kernel EDMD, Exact/Optimized DMD, and simple baselines.
- Discuss complexity, numerical conditioning, truncation/regularization, and kernel selection strategies.
- Improve clarity and completeness of the references and text.

Overall assessment:
The paper addresses a timely topic and presents a coherent RKHS framing of Koopman spectral analysis, but its novelty relative to existing EDMD/kernel EDMD is limited, and the current theory and experiments are not strong enough for NeurIPS. Strengthening the derivations and substantially expanding the empirical comparisons could change this assessment.
