### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The central idea is to lift the system's state space into an infinite-dimensional feature space where the nonlinear dynamics are represented by a linear Koopman operator. Standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear combination of observables, which can be insufficient for highly nonlinear systems. The proposed method, termed kernel DMD (kDMD), approximates these eigenfunctions using the richer set of basis functions implicitly defined by a kernel function.

The authors formulate the problem as finding the Ritz values and vectors of the Koopman operator projected onto a Krylov subspace in the RKHS. They present two algorithms. The first is a direct translation of the DMD principle using Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space, which is shown to be mathematically correct but potentially numerically unstable. The second, more practical algorithm robustifies the method by projecting the Koopman operator onto the subspace spanned by the top principal components in the feature space, which is equivalent to performing Kernel Principal Component Analysis (KPCA) on the time-series data. The paper concludes by demonstrating the utility of the extracted Koopman modes as features for several downstream tasks, including state prediction (via a pre-image method), dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound. The derivation of kDMD as a natural extension of the DMD framework to RKHS is well-founded. Theorem 1 correctly establishes the decomposition in the feature space based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization. The acknowledgment of the potential numerical instability of this approach and the subsequent development of a robustified algorithm based on KPCA (Theorem 2) is a significant strength, paralleling similar developments in the standard DMD literature (e.g., projecting onto POD modes). The connection between the eigenvalues of the projected operator and the true Koopman eigenvalues is clearly established under reasonable assumptions.

However, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened. While the examples illustrate the method's capabilities, they lack quantitative comparisons against relevant baselines. For instance, in the eigenvalue estimation and prediction tasks, it would be beneficial to compare kDMD against standard DMD to demonstrate in which regimes the kernel-based approach provides a significant advantage. The prediction results are presented visually but would be more compelling with standard error metrics (e.g., RMSE) computed on a held-out test set. Furthermore, the paper does not discuss the computational complexity or scalability of the proposed method. Since it relies on the construction and eigendecomposition of `τ x τ` Gram matrices, it scales as `O(τ^3)` with the number of data points, which could be a significant limitation for long time series. A discussion of this limitation and potential remedies (e.g., Nyström approximation) would make the work more complete.

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear motivation for the work, and the background section on Koopman operators and DMD is concise and informative for readers unfamiliar with the topic. The progression from the direct, but ill-conditioned, algorithm in Section 3 to the robustified version in Section 4 is logical. The algorithms are presented in clear, step-by-step formats.

One area for improvement is the discussion of the pre-image problem in the prediction application. The paper mentions using an MDS-based approach from a cited work [18] but provides minimal detail. Given that finding a pre-image is a non-trivial and often ill-posed problem for kernel methods, a more detailed explanation of the specific procedure and its potential challenges would improve the reproducibility and clarity of this application.

**Originality:**

The paper presents a novel and well-motivated combination of Koopman operator theory, DMD, and kernel methods. While using kernels to handle nonlinearity is a standard technique in machine learning, its application to provide a data-driven, non-parametric dictionary for DMD is a valuable contribution. It offers a principled alternative to manually selecting basis functions, as is done in Extended DMD (eDMD).

However, the relationship to eDMD [35] is understated. The authors mention that "the resulting procedure is similar to the robust-version of our algorithm," but the connection appears to be much stronger. The robust kDMD algorithm can be viewed as a specific instance of eDMD where the dictionary of observable functions is chosen to be the set of kernel functions centered at the data points, i.e., `{k(·, x_i)}`. This connection should be discussed more explicitly to properly contextualize the paper's contribution relative to existing work. While kDMD offers a powerful and systematic way to choose the dictionary for eDMD, clarifying this relationship is crucial for assessing its novelty.

**Significance:**

The proposed method has the potential for significant impact. It broadens the applicability of DMD, a tool rapidly gaining popularity in the physical sciences, to a wider range of nonlinear systems common in machine learning contexts. By framing the output of kDMD as a feature extraction procedure, the paper effectively bridges the gap between dynamics decomposition methods from physics and representation learning in machine learning. The demonstrated applications in prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection are compelling and likely to inspire further research and use by both researchers and practitioners. The work advances the state of the art by providing a powerful, data-driven tool for analyzing and representing complex nonlinear dynamics without requiring domain-specific knowledge to engineer basis functions.

### Arguments for acceptance:

*   Presents a novel and elegant kernel-based extension of the popular DMD method for Koopman spectral analysis.
*   The technical development is sound, and the proposed robust algorithm is well-justified.
*   The work is significant as it makes Koopman analysis more accessible for general nonlinear systems and connects it to machine learning tasks like representation learning and prediction.
*   The paper is clearly written and the core ideas are communicated effectively.

### Arguments for rejection:

*   The relationship to Extended DMD (eDMD) is not sufficiently discussed, potentially overstating the novelty of the proposed algorithm.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks quantitative comparisons to baseline methods, making it difficult to assess the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
*   The paper does not address the computational scalability of the method, which is a key practical consideration for a kernel-based algorithm.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a solid and valuable contribution. The weaknesses, primarily related to experimental comparison and positioning with prior art, are addressable. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly encourage the authors to revise the manuscript to include a more thorough discussion of the relationship with eDMD and to strengthen the experimental section with quantitative baseline comparisons.
