

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to better capture nonlinear dynamics through the Koopman operator framework. The authors build upon traditional DMD, which approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials, by introducing kernel-based nonlinear basis functions. The paper develops a theoretical framework for kernel DMD, provides a robustified version using POD bases, and demonstrates applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

## Strengths

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the Koopman spectral analysis literature. The theoretical development (Theorems 1 and 2) is rigorous and well-motivated, providing a principled extension of DMD to kernel-based methods. The robustification approach using POD bases effectively addresses numerical stability issues common in DMD implementations, demonstrating practical awareness of implementation challenges. The empirical results on synthetic systems (toy nonlinear system and Hénon map) validate the theoretical claims, showing accurate eigenvalue estimation. The applications section (prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition) demonstrates the potential utility of the method in practical domains, with the locomotion data example showing promising results in dynamics recognition.

## Weaknesses

The paper's main weakness lies in its limited differentiation from existing work. The authors cite Williams et al.'s extended DMD as a related method but fail to clearly articulate how their kernel-based approach fundamentally differs or improves upon it. Extended DMD already uses pre-determined basis functions rather than linear monomials, making the kernel approach seem like a natural extension rather than a significant advancement. The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods (including extended DMD and other kernel-based approaches), making it difficult to assess the relative performance advantages.

The empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic data and simple examples. While the toy system and Hénon map results are promising, the paper lacks demonstration of the method's advantages on more challenging real-world problems where nonlinear dynamics are particularly difficult to model. The applications section describes potential uses but doesn't provide sufficient quantitative evidence that the kernel DMD approach outperforms existing methods in these applications.

The paper also omits important discussion of computational complexity and scalability. For practical applications, understanding the computational cost of the kernel-based approach compared to traditional DMD or extended DMD would be crucial. The paper doesn't address how the method scales with data size or dimensionality, which is a significant limitation for real-world applications.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Clarify the novelty**: Explicitly compare the proposed method with Williams et al.'s extended DMD and other kernel-based DMD approaches. Demonstrate how the kernel-based approach provides a distinct advantage over these existing methods, particularly in capturing nonlinear dynamics.

2. **Expand empirical evaluation**: Include comparisons with state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets. Add experiments on more complex real-world systems where traditional DMD would fail, demonstrating clear performance advantages of the kernel-based approach.

3. **Address computational aspects**: Analyze the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm and compare it with existing methods. Discuss practical considerations for implementing the method on large-scale datasets.

4. **Strengthen applications section**: Provide quantitative results showing how the kernel DMD approach improves upon existing methods in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition tasks. Include error metrics and statistical significance tests.

5. **Improve relation to prior work**: More thoroughly discuss the connection between this work and recent advances in Koopman operator learning, particularly methods that use neural networks or other nonlinear function approximators.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods with potential value for nonlinear dynamical systems analysis. However, the contribution is incremental rather than transformative, with insufficient differentiation from existing work and limited empirical validation. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly through a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods and more substantial empirical validation.

I recommend rejection, but with the hope that the authors can address these concerns in a future revision. The method has potential, but as presented, it doesn't sufficiently advance the state of the art to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS. The authors should focus on clearly articulating the novel aspects of their approach and demonstrating its advantages through rigorous comparisons and more challenging empirical evaluations.
