This paper introduces a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a method for approximating the spectral properties of the Koopman operator for nonlinear dynamical systems. The central idea is to lift the system's state space into a high-dimensional Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) and perform the DMD analysis there. This allows the method to approximate the Koopman eigenfunctions using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions implicitly defined by the kernel, overcoming the key limitation of standard DMD, which is restricted to linear observables. The authors propose two algorithmic variants: a direct approach based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the RKHS, and a more numerically robust version that first projects the data onto its kernel principal components. The utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues is demonstrated through applications in state prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

This work sits at a compelling intersection of operator-theoretic approaches to dynamical systems, popular in physics and fluid dynamics, and kernel methods, a cornerstone of machine learning. It builds directly upon the DMD literature (e.g., Schmid, 2010; Rowley et al., 2009) and connects it with well-established concepts like kernel PCA (Schölkopf et al., 1998). The proposed method is closely related to Extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), which uses a user-defined dictionary of basis functions. This paper's contribution can be seen as providing a systematic and powerful framework for defining this dictionary implicitly through the choice of a kernel, a significant and elegant conceptual step.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance):**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The primary strength of this paper is its core idea. The synthesis of Koopman operator theory with kernel methods is a bold and powerful approach. It provides a principled and non-trivial extension of DMD to handle highly nonlinear dynamics, moving beyond local linear approximations. This is precisely the kind of work that can open up new research directions by introducing a powerful tool from another domain (fluid dynamics) to the machine learning community in a much more general and applicable form.

2.  **Potential for High Impact:** The ability to extract a set of linear modes (the Koopman modes) that globally describe the evolution of a nonlinear system is incredibly valuable. The extracted eigenvalues and eigenfunctions provide an interpretable, low-dimensional representation of the dynamics, encoding frequencies and growth/decay rates. The paper effectively showcases this potential in its applications section. While the experiments are preliminary, they convincingly argue that this method can serve as a potent feature extraction technique for a wide range of time-series analysis tasks, from prediction to classification.

3.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is well-written and theoretically sound. The authors provide a clear background on the Koopman operator and DMD before carefully deriving their kernelized extension. The two theorems provide a solid theoretical justification for the proposed algorithms, connecting the computed Ritz values and vectors in the RKHS to the true Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions under reasonable assumptions. The motivation for the robustified, kPCA-based version is clear and practically important.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Closely Related Work:** The paper mentions Extended DMD (eDMD) but could benefit from a more in-depth discussion. Kernel DMD can be interpreted as an instance of eDMD where the basis functions are kernel functions centered at the data points. The key advantage of the kernel approach is that one does not need to manually specify a dictionary of observables, but instead chooses a kernel. A more explicit discussion of this trade-off (choosing kernels/parameters vs. choosing dictionaries) would strengthen the paper's positioning.

2.  **Experimental Validation is Preliminary:** While the applications are well-chosen to illustrate the method's versatility, the empirical validation lacks rigor and comparison to relevant baselines.
    *   For the prediction task, no alternative methods (e.g., standard DMD, Gaussian Process models, RNNs) are compared against, making it difficult to assess performance. The pre-image method, a non-trivial problem, is also described very briefly.
    *   For dynamics recognition, the visualization is qualitative. Quantitative results on a classification benchmark would be more compelling.
    *   The change-point detection relies on a heuristic simplification of the likelihood ratio, and its performance is only compared to one other kernel-based method.
    While I do not consider this a fatal flaw given the paper's conceptual novelty, the work would be substantially stronger with more thorough benchmarking.

3.  **Practical Considerations Overlooked:** The paper does not discuss the computational complexity or scalability of the proposed method. The algorithms rely on the construction and eigendecomposition of Gram matrices, which scale as O(τ^3) where τ is the number of time steps. This limits the method's applicability to very long time series, and a discussion of this limitation and potential remedies (e.g., subset-of-data approximations) would be welcome.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper presents a highly novel and significant contribution by developing a kernelized version of Dynamic Mode Decomposition. It introduces a powerful, interpretable, and broadly applicable tool for the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The fusion of Koopman theory and kernel methods is elegant and opens up exciting possibilities for time-series feature extraction and modeling.

While the experimental evaluation is more illustrative than exhaustive and the comparison to closely related work like eDMD could be sharpened, these are forgivable weaknesses in light of the strength of the core idea. This is not an incremental paper; it introduces a genuinely new approach. It is the kind of bold, conceptually rich work that NIPS should encourage, as it is likely to inspire follow-up research. The potential impact of providing the machine learning community with a practical tool for Koopman spectral analysis is substantial. Therefore, I strongly recommend acceptance.
