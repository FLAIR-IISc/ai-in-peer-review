Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to (i) build a Krylov matrix from feature maps ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1), (ii) obtain an orthonormal basis via Gram–Schmidt in feature space using kernel evaluations, (iii) project the (feature-space) Koopman operator KH onto this subspace and eigendecompose the finite-dimensional projection to obtain empirical Ritz values/vectors, and (iv) reconstruct observables and modes. Theorem 1 mirrors Arnoldi/DMD: it yields exact reconstruction within the snapshot window and a residual for the next snapshot. Because Gram–Schmidt can be ill-conditioned, the paper “robustifies” the method by projecting onto kernel PCA (POD in feature space): the projected operator F̂ is formed from centered Gram matrices and its eigenspectrum is used; Theorem 2 connects these eigenpairs to Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions under representer assumptions. The authors illustrate on a toy system and the Hénon map, and sketch three applications: short-term prediction via preimage reconstruction, embedding/recognition of dynamics via kernel principal angles between subspaces spanned by Koopman eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-like criterion expressed with kernel inner products.

This line of work is closely related to DMD (Schmid 2010; Tu et al. 2014 exact DMD; Chen et al. 2012 optimized variants) and especially to extended DMD (EDMD; Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley 2015), which approximates Koopman operators in a chosen dictionary. The kernelized variant of EDMD (“kernel DMD/KDMD”) has appeared in the literature and uses the kernel trick to work implicitly in high-dimensional feature spaces. The robustified procedure here (POD/SVD truncation of snapshots/Gram) is also standard in the DMD/EDMD family. The paper cites EDMD and notes similarities, but the methodological overlap is substantial.

Quality:
- Theory: The RKHS-Arnoldi equivalence is sound in spirit, and Theorem 1 captures the standard Arnoldi/DMD reconstruction property. However, some steps are compressed and conflate the projected operator with the true operator; MτF = KHMτ holds only up to projection onto span(Mτ). Theorem 2’s assumptions (eigenfunctions in the RKHS and their representers lying in the retained KPCA span) are strong, but typical for EDMD-style results. A factual error appears: “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is not generally true; for dissipative systems, magnitudes are ≤ 1 and are often strictly inside the unit circle.
- Algorithms: Practical implementation details (numerical stability, centering constants, consistent notation) need refinement; the centering vector ϕc should be a mean, not a sum. Complexity and memory scaling (O(τ^3), O(τ^2)) and kernel hyperparameter selection are not discussed.
- Experiments: Very light. The eigenvalue plots for a toy system/Hénon map are qualitative; applications (prediction, recognition embedding, change detection) are illustrative without quantitative metrics, baselines beyond a single kernel change-detection comparison, ablations, or noise sensitivity studies. There is no direct comparison to EDMD/KDMD, exact/optimized DMD, or recent Koopman learning methods.

Clarity:
- Generally readable and organized, but there are grammatical errors, notation overload (φ for eigenfunctions and different Ritz vectors), and occasional imprecision (e.g., centering, projections). Algorithm steps would benefit from clear pseudocode, consistent symbols, and explicit statements about projection vs true operator action.
- Some claims could be more carefully qualified (e.g., eigenvalues on unit circle) and the role of the projection highlighted to avoid confusion.

Originality:
- The main methodological contribution—kernelizing DMD/Koopman spectral analysis and using POD/SVD truncation—is not new; it closely matches EDMD with kernels (KDMD). The paper’s perspective via Arnoldi in RKHS and the specific derivations are useful pedagogically, but the novelty beyond existing KDMD/EDMD derivations is limited. Related work on kernel-based Koopman/transfer operators could be cited more comprehensively.

Significance:
- Koopman analysis with kernels is impactful in practice, but the paper, as presented, offers limited advancement over known KDMD/EDMD methods and lacks strong empirical evidence demonstrating clear superiority or unique capabilities. The code availability is a plus and could facilitate adoption.

Pros:
- Clear, operator-theoretic framing of DMD in RKHS with an Arnoldi viewpoint.
- Practical robustification via kernel PCA/POD is sensible and standard.
- Connections to applications (prediction, subspace-based recognition, change detection) are interesting.
- Code provided.

Cons:
- Limited novelty relative to EDMD/KDMD; overlap is substantial.
- Weak empirical evaluation: small-scale, largely qualitative, minimal baselines.
- Some technical inaccuracies and notational/clarity issues.
- No analysis of computational complexity, hyperparameter sensitivity, or noise robustness.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clearly distinguish this method from EDMD/KDMD, both conceptually and algorithmically; if equivalent, say so and focus on what is new (e.g., theoretical guarantees, conditioning analysis, convergence).
- Fix technical inaccuracies (e.g., eigenvalues on unit circle statement), clean up notation, and provide precise algorithm boxes with complexity.
- Provide quantitative experiments: eigenfunction approximation error, prediction RMSE vs EDMD/KDMD/exact DMD on standard benchmarks (e.g., FitzHugh–Nagumo, Duffing, fluid datasets), noise sensitivity, and ablations on truncation levels and kernel choices.
- Discuss kernel selection and cross-validation strategies; study scalability to large τ via randomized/Nyström approximations.
- For applications, report classification accuracy (recognition) and detection delay/false alarm trade-offs (change-point), with appropriate baselines.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper offers a clear exposition of Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS and a reasonable algorithmic pipeline, but the methodological novelty relative to EDMD/KDMD appears limited, and the empirical validation is too thin for NeurIPS standards. Strengthening the comparative evaluation, clarifying the relationship to existing kernel Koopman methods, and tightening the theory/notation would substantially improve the paper.
