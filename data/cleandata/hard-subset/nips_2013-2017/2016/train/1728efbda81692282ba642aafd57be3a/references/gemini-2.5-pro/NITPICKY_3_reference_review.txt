### **Summary**

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to generalize Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) by operating on feature maps induced by a kernel function, thereby allowing for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions using a richer, nonlinear set of basis functions. The authors first present an algorithm based on a Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in the feature space, which is analogous to the Arnoldi method interpretation of DMD. Acknowledging that this approach is likely ill-conditioned, they then propose a more robust variant that projects the Koopman operator onto a subspace spanned by the principal components from kernel PCA (equivalent to POD in the feature space). The utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features is demonstrated through applications in short-term prediction, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Conceptual Elegance:** The paper presents a well-motivated and theoretically appealing synthesis of three powerful concepts: Koopman operator theory for linearizing nonlinear dynamics, DMD as a practical data-driven tool for Koopman analysis, and kernel methods for introducing potent nonlinearity and working with infinite-dimensional feature spaces. The extension of DMD to an RKHS is a natural and elegant idea.
2.  **Connection to Existing Methods:** The authors correctly situate their work within the broader context of decomposition techniques like POD and DMD, and also acknowledge connections to subspace identification methods in machine learning and control.
3.  **Potential for Application:** The proposed method is framed as a feature extraction technique for dynamical systems, and the paper explores several interesting applications. While the experiments are preliminary, they suggest the potential of this approach for tasks like dynamics classification and anomaly detection.

### **Weaknesses**

While the paper's core idea is interesting, the manuscript in its current form is beset by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from minor presentational issues to significant concerns about originality and experimental rigor.

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The most significant weakness is the insufficient differentiation from Extended DMD (eDMD) [35]. The robust algorithm presented in Section 4 appears to be a special case of eDMD, where the user-defined dictionary of observables is implicitly chosen to be the feature map of a kernel function. The paper mentions eDMD is "similar," but this understates the overlap. The central contribution of eDMD is the projection of the Koopman operator onto a subspace spanned by a general set of basis functions. This paper's contribution seems to be the specific choice of a kernel-induced basis. This needs to be explicitly stated and the novelty of this specific choice, beyond being a standard "kernel trick," must be rigorously justified. As written, the work appears to be largely a re-derivation of an existing idea in the language of kernel methods.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from a lack of polish and contains numerous inconsistencies.
    *   The organization is confusing. Section 3 is dedicated to developing a "mathematically correct" algorithm based on Gram-Schmidt, which is then immediately dismissed in Section 4 as "ill-conditioned" and impractical. This section feels extraneous and could be condensed or removed in favor of a more direct presentation of the robust method.
    *   The reference list is poorly formatted, with inconsistent styles for conferences vs. journals, missing information (e.g., publisher for books), and inconsistent author formatting (e.g., [12]). This level of carelessness is concerning.
    *   The description of the applications is often superficial. The preimage algorithm for prediction relies heavily on [18], providing little intuition or detail for the reader. The derivation of the change-point detection statistic in Section 7 is particularly weak, hand-wavingly dropping a term with the justification that its variation is "much smaller" based on a citation. This is not sufficiently rigorous.
    *   There appear to be typos. The y-axis label "kDMD-SVM" in Figure 4 is unexplained and seems incorrect in the context of the CUSUM-like statistic described in the text.
    *   The inclusion of a non-anonymized link to the author's code in a footnote is a breach of the standard double-blind review process for NIPS.

3.  **Technical Soundness:**
    *   The theoretical claims rest on strong assumptions whose implications are not discussed. Theorem 2 assumes that the true Koopman eigenfunctions lie within the subspace spanned by the kernel principal components of a finite dataset. This is a very strong condition, and the paper should discuss the nature and impact of the approximation error when this assumption is violated.
    *   A critical aspect of any kernel-based method—the choice of the kernel function and its hyperparameters—is completely ignored. The parameters used in the experiments (e.g., polynomial degree 3, Gaussian width 1) are stated without any justification. The sensitivity of the estimated spectra and the performance of the downstream applications to these choices is a crucial question that has been left unaddressed.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is anecdotal and lacks the rigor expected for a NIPS publication.
    *   The experiments are performed on simple, low-dimensional systems. For the Hénon map, the result is only qualitatively described as "approximately estimated," which is not a convincing validation. No quantitative error metrics for the estimated eigenvalues are provided.
    *   Crucially, there are **no comparisons to any baseline methods**. For prediction, the method should be compared against standard DMD, eDMD with polynomial bases, or Gaussian Process models. For dynamics recognition, a comparison with other time-series feature extraction methods is necessary. For change-point detection, the proposed method should be benchmarked against the method it is compared to in Figure 4 [7] using quantitative metrics like detection delay and false alarm rate, not just a single visual example.
    *   The results are presented purely qualitatively. The MDS plot in Figure 3 is illustrative, but gives no sense of actual classification performance. The prediction plots in Figure 2 lack a stated prediction horizon or error bars. This level of evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate that the proposed method provides any tangible benefit.

### **Recommendation**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is built upon an interesting premise, the execution is flawed. The questionable novelty over existing work like eDMD, combined with a lack of experimental rigor, vague technical descriptions, and numerous presentational issues, prevents this manuscript from meeting the high standards of NIPS. The authors should significantly strengthen their contribution by (1) clearly delineating their work's novelty with respect to eDMD, (2) performing a rigorous and comparative experimental evaluation against relevant baselines with quantitative metrics, (3) providing a thorough discussion on practical considerations like kernel selection, and (4) carefully revising the manuscript to improve clarity and correct the many formatting and stylistic errors.
