This paper introduces a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) algorithm extended to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), aiming to improve upon standard DMD's reliance on linear monomials by utilizing the expressiveness of kernel functions. The core idea is to perform a modal decomposition by projecting the Koopman operator onto a subspace defined by orthogonal bases computed in the RKHS, analogous to the standard DMD process but with richer basis functions. The authors present theoretical results demonstrating equivalence to DMD and propose a robustification technique using POD. Applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition are demonstrated empirically.

The paper is well-motivated, situating the work within the context of spectral methods for dynamical systems and the limitations of traditional DMD. The connection to the Koopman operator is clearly explained, and the mathematical derivation of the RKHS-based DMD is presented rigorously. The inclusion of a POD-based robustification step addresses a known practical issue with DMD variants and is a valuable addition. The empirical evaluation, while limited in scope, demonstrates the potential of the method on both synthetic and real-world data, covering prediction, embedding, and change-point detection. The related work section provides a comprehensive overview of relevant literature, positioning the contribution effectively.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical framework is sound, the practical advantages of the proposed kernel DMD over existing methods, particularly extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) which also uses pre-determined basis functions, are not clearly articulated.  A more detailed comparative analysis, particularly quantifying the performance gains (or lack thereof) with different kernels and datasets, would strengthen the contribution. The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited. The toy system and Hénon map are useful for initial validation, but more complex and high-dimensional datasets would be needed to convincingly demonstrate the method's scalability and effectiveness. The application sections (prediction, embedding, change detection) feel somewhat cursory; a deeper exploration of the parameter sensitivity and performance limitations would be beneficial.  The notation, while mostly clear, could be improved for readability – for example, consistently using subscripts for indices (e.g., xt instead of x_t).  The link between the theoretical analysis and the practical implementation details is not always explicit, potentially hindering reproducibility.  Specifically, the choice of kernel parameters (e.g., bandwidth in the Gaussian kernel) is not fully justified and could significantly impact the results.  Finally, while the code is available, the documentation needs expansion to facilitate usage by other researchers.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivation is rigorous and logically presented, building upon established concepts in Koopman operator theory and RKHS.
*   **Novelty:** Extending DMD to RKHS offers a potentially more flexible approach for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions, leveraging the power of kernel methods.
*   **Potential Significance:**  If demonstrated to outperform existing methods on relevant datasets, this work could provide a valuable tool for analyzing and predicting complex dynamical systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method.
*   **Lack of Comparative Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison with related techniques, such as extended DMD, making it difficult to assess its unique contributions.
*   **Implementation Details:** The details concerning kernel parameter selection and practical implementation could be more clearly articulated to ensure reproducibility and facilitate adoption by other researchers.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and potentially significant extension of DMD. However, the limited empirical validation and lack of comprehensive comparative analysis raise concerns. I recommend **conditional acceptance** contingent upon the authors: 1) performing a more thorough empirical evaluation on a wider range of datasets, including comparisons with extended DMD; 2) providing a more detailed discussion of the parameter sensitivity and performance limitations of the proposed method; and 3) improving the documentation for the released code. Without these revisions, the paper’s contribution remains somewhat incremental.
