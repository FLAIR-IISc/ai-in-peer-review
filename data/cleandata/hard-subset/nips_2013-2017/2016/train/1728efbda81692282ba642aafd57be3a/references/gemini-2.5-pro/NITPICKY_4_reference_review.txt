**Summary:**
This paper proposes a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to approximate the Koopman operator and its eigenfunctions within an RKHS, allowing for richer, nonlinear basis functions compared to standard DMD. The authors first present a direct formulation based on a QR decomposition in the feature space, which is analogous to the Arnoldi method interpretation of DMD. Acknowledging the potential numerical instability of this approach, they then propose a more robust variant that projects the data onto principal directions found via kernel PCA (analogous to using POD modes in standard DMD). The paper demonstrates the utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features for several applications, including short-term prediction, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection.

**Strengths:**
1.  The extension of DMD to RKHS is a natural and elegant idea, providing a principled way to move from linear observables to a rich, nonlinear function class for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions.
2.  The paper makes a clear connection between the proposed algorithm and the foundational principles of DMD, including the Krylov subspace interpretation and the use of POD modes for robustification.
3.  The exploration of multiple applications (prediction, recognition, change-point detection) demonstrates the potential of the extracted spectral features and broadens the paper's scope.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, while containing promising ideas, is undermined by a significant number of flaws ranging from conceptual clarity and methodological rigor to experimental validation and presentation quality.

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The main weakness is the understated relationship with Extended DMD (EDMD) [35]. The proposed robust algorithm (Section 4) appears to be a specific instance of EDMD, where the dictionary of observables is implicitly formed by the kernel basis functions evaluated at the data points, and the projection is onto the kernel principal components. The authors mention EDMD only in passing, stating their procedure is "similar." This is an insufficient comparison. A thorough discussion is required to precisely delineate the contribution beyond applying the kernel trick to the EDMD framework, which itself is a fairly standard technique in machine learning. Without this, the novelty of the core algorithm is questionable.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:**
    *   The paper's organization is somewhat awkward. The algorithm in Section 3 is presented as the primary method but is immediately noted to be "ill-conditioned" and "incapable of extracting multiple modes." The robust version in Section 4 is the one used in practice. The paper would be clearer if the robust method were presented as the main contribution, with the direct method serving as theoretical motivation.
    *   The notation is inconsistent and confusing. `H` is used to denote both the RKHS in Section 3 and the centering matrix `I - 1τ` in Section 4. This is a critical ambiguity that can easily mislead the reader.
    *   Several terms like "equivalent principle of DMD" are used without a clear definition.
    *   The description of the applications lacks crucial detail. The prediction method via pre-image (Section 7) is described at a very high level, making it difficult to understand or reproduce. The derivation of the change-point detection score is particularly problematic; it relies on an ad-hoc assumption about a nonparametric exponential family and then simplifies the decision rule by arguing a term can be ignored because its "variation is much smaller," a claim made without any theoretical or empirical support.

3.  **Technical Soundness and Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The experimental results are preliminary and mostly illustrative. For the Hénon map (Fig. 1), the authors state that only "part of the eigenvalues of the equilibrium points seem to be approximately estimated." This is a weak result that raises more questions than it answers: Why are some eigenvalues missed? How does the performance depend on the kernel or data size?
    *   The applications are not rigorously evaluated. The prediction section references a "Fig. 2," which is not present in the paper—a major oversight. The dynamics recognition example (Fig. 3) provides a qualitative visualization but lacks any quantitative comparison to baseline methods for time-series classification or clustering. The change-point experiment (Fig. 4) is a single, cherry-picked example. For a NIPS submission, one would expect quantitative comparisons on benchmark datasets to substantiate the claims of utility.
    *   The choice of kernels and their hyperparameters is not justified. The performance of kernel methods is critically dependent on this choice, and a sensitivity analysis or a more principled selection method is needed.

4.  **Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   The paper suffers from numerous typographical and grammatical errors (e.g., "almost of the theoretical claims").
    *   The reference list is very poorly formatted, with missing information, incorrect punctuation, and apparent copy-paste errors (e.g., "[1] ... Quarterly of Applied Mathematics,, , ,"). This lack of attention to detail is concerning.
    *   The inclusion of a URL as a footnote for code is non-standard for an anonymous submission.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a coherent and intuitive kernelization of the popular DMD method.
*   It showcases a diverse set of potential applications for the resulting features.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The novelty is questionable in light of existing work, particularly EDMD [35], and this relationship is not adequately discussed.
*   The experimental validation is weak, qualitative, and lacks comparisons to relevant baselines.
*   Key sections describing applications (prediction, change-point detection) are not rigorously derived and lack sufficient detail for reproducibility.
*   The paper contains significant presentation flaws, including a missing figure, ambiguous notation, and a poorly formatted bibliography, suggesting a lack of careful preparation.

**Decision:**
I recommend **rejection**. While the core idea is appealing, the paper in its current form does not meet the high standards of NIPS. The limited novelty, insufficient experimental validation, and numerous clarity and presentation issues prevent me from recommending acceptance. The work has potential, but it requires a substantial revision to address the weaknesses in its methodology, evaluation, and positioning relative to prior art before it is ready for publication.
