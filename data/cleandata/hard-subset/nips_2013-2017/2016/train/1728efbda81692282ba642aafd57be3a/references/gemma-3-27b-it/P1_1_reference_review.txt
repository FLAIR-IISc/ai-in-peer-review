## Peer Review: Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel extension to Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) by incorporating reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors argue that traditional DMD relies on linear approximations and propose using kernel methods to represent the Koopman eigenfunctions with richer, non-linear basis functions. The core contribution is an algorithm for performing this kernel-based DMD, along with a practical robustification technique using POD projections to address potential ill-conditioning issues. The authors demonstrate the method on both a toy system and the Henon map, and explore potential applications in prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper situates itself within the growing field of spectral methods for dynamical systems, drawing connections to Koopman operator theory, DMD, Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD), and related techniques like subspace identification. The authors correctly acknowledge the limitations of POD and the original DMD, and clearly highlight how their approach differs by offering a non-linear approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions. They cite relevant work from the DMD literature (e.g., optimized DMD, sparse DMD) and also link to broader areas like kernel methods and Gaussian processes. The authors correctly point out the similarity of extended DMD to their robust version.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of extending DMD with RKHS is a valuable contribution, potentially overcoming limitations of linear basis functions and enabling the analysis of more complex dynamics.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the kernel-based DMD appears solid, with a clear connection to the Koopman operator and DMD principles. The theorem relating to Ritz values and vectors is well-presented and provides a theoretical justification for the proposed algorithm.
*   **Practical Considerations:** Recognizing and addressing the potential ill-conditioning issues with a POD-based robustification is a strong point. This makes the method more practical for real-world applications.
*   **Exploration of Applications:** The exploration of applications (prediction, dynamics recognition, change-point detection) broadens the potential impact of the work and demonstrates its versatility.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is relatively clear, and the explanations are accessible.
* **Availability of Code:** Providing a link to the Matlab code is a major plus for reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Empirical Validation:** While the examples demonstrate the method's functionality on simple systems, the empirical evaluation feels limited. The results on the Henon map are suggestive, but more detailed analysis and comparison with existing methods would be beneficial. A more comprehensive set of benchmark problems (e.g., from the Dynamical Systems Toolbox) would strengthen the claims.
*   **Kernel Selection:** The paper lacks discussion on kernel selection. The choice of polynomial and Gaussian kernels seems arbitrary, and the sensitivity of the results to kernel parameters is not explored. A more systematic investigation of different kernels and parameter tuning methods is needed.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The computational cost of kernel-based DMD, especially with large datasets, is not discussed.  While POD helps, the use of kernel functions can still be expensive.  A discussion of scalability and potential optimizations would be valuable.
* **Application Depth:** The application sections are somewhat brief and lack detailed quantitative evaluation. For example, the change-point detection results are presented without a clear performance metric.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:** While related work is mentioned, the paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how this approach compares to other kernel-based methods for dynamical systems, like kernel state-space models.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*   The method is theoretically sound and introduces a novel extension to DMD.
*   The authors address practical concerns related to ill-conditioning.
*   The code is publicly available.
*   The potential applications are promising.

**Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical validation is limited.
*   Kernel selection and computational complexity are not adequately addressed.
*   The application sections lack quantitative evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. The mathematical foundation is strong, and the authors address a relevant challenge in the field of dynamical systems modeling. However, the limited empirical evaluation and the lack of discussion regarding kernel selection and computational complexity prevent me from giving it a strong recommendation for acceptance. 

**Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept.**  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should significantly expand the empirical evaluation, provide a more thorough discussion of kernel selection and computational considerations, and strengthen the quantitative evaluation of the applications. Addressing these weaknesses would substantially improve the paper and increase its potential impact. I would suggest the authors revisit and refine the empirical evaluation as a priority, perhaps focusing on one or two applications in detail with comprehensive performance metrics.
