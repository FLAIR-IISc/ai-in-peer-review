

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary
This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors build upon the Koopman operator framework, which represents nonlinear dynamics as an infinite-dimensional linear operator, and extend traditional DMD (which uses linear monomials as basis functions) to use kernel functions for richer nonlinear representations. The paper provides a theoretical foundation for this extension, including theorems about the relationship between the empirical Ritz values and the Koopman eigenvalues, and proposes a robustification method by projecting data onto POD directions. The authors demonstrate applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data.

The paper relates to previous work on Koopman spectral analysis, DMD variants, and kernel methods. It references key works in the field (e.g., Williams et al.'s extended DMD, Jovanović et al.'s sparsity-promoting DMD), but could have provided more detailed comparisons with these methods.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with a solid mathematical foundation. The authors provide clear theorems and proofs for their proposed method, particularly Theorem 1 which extends DMD to RKHS, and Theorem 2 which establishes the connection between the proposed method and Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions. The robustification approach using POD bases (Section 4) addresses a known issue in DMD implementations.

The empirical examples (Section 6) are reasonable, showing the method works on a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map. The applications in Section 7 (prediction, dynamics recognition, change-point detection) are well-motivated and provide some evidence of the method's utility. However, the paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods. For instance, it doesn't compare the proposed method's performance against extended DMD, optimized DMD, or other kernel-based approaches on the same datasets. The computational complexity of the proposed method is not discussed, which is important for practical applications.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting that the method requires sufficiently rich data and that the eigenfunctions are only fully recovered if the Krylov subspace is sufficiently large.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction provides a good overview of the Koopman operator and DMD, and the subsequent sections build logically on this foundation. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the algorithms are described step-by-step with sufficient detail.

The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of the RKHS concepts for readers without a strong background in functional analysis. Some of the mathematical derivations (e.g., in Theorem 2) might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with kernel methods and spectral analysis.

The figures (particularly Figure 1 showing estimated eigenvalues) are clear and effectively illustrate the key findings. The empirical examples in Figure 2, 3, and 4 are well-presented and help demonstrate the applications.

## Originality
The core idea of extending DMD to RKHS is novel in the context of Koopman spectral analysis. While kernel methods have been used in other contexts, this specific application to Koopman operator analysis represents a meaningful extension. The authors correctly position their work relative to existing methods, noting that extended DMD [35] works with pre-determined basis functions rather than the kernel-based approach proposed here.

The paper's connection between Koopman spectral analysis and kernel methods is not entirely new (as kernel methods have been applied to dynamical systems before), but the specific integration with DMD is novel. The robustification approach using POD bases is similar to existing DMD variants [8, 33], but the integration with RKHS is a novel contribution.

The paper references related work thoroughly in Section 5, but could have provided a more detailed comparison of how this work differs from Williams et al.'s extended DMD [35] and other kernel-based approaches to dynamical systems.

## Significance
The Koopman operator framework has gained significant attention in fluid dynamics and systems theory, and this paper bridges the gap between machine learning and these fields by providing a kernel-based extension to DMD. The applications to state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition are practical and relevant to real-world problems.

However, the significance would be stronger if the paper demonstrated clear advantages over existing methods in terms of accuracy, computational efficiency, or applicability to a wider range of systems. The empirical examples are limited to synthetic data and a simple chaotic map, with no comparison to state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets. The paper's claim that the method "provides a modal decomposition of the dynamics into a finite number of modes" is a valuable contribution, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how this decomposition offers advantages over existing approaches for practical applications.

The paper's potential impact is limited by the lack of comprehensive experimental validation. For a conference like NIPS, where empirical validation is critical, the paper would benefit from more extensive comparisons with existing methods on standard datasets.

## Overall Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Solid theoretical foundation with clear theorems and proofs
- Novel extension of DMD to RKHS for nonlinear basis functions
- Practical robustification approach using POD bases
- Well-motivated applications to prediction, change detection, and dynamics recognition
- Good organization and clear presentation of the mathematical concepts

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited empirical validation with insufficient comparisons to state-of-the-art methods
- Lack of detailed discussion on computational complexity and scalability
- Limited real-world applications with only a few synthetic examples
- Insufficient discussion of how this method differs from extended DMD [35] and other kernel-based approaches
- No clear demonstration of the method's superiority in practical scenarios

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The work is technically sound and represents a meaningful extension of DMD to RKHS, which is a novel contribution to the field. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the applications are well-motivated.

However, the paper's significance is limited by its lack of comprehensive empirical validation. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Add detailed comparisons with existing methods (especially extended DMD, optimized DMD, and other kernel-based approaches) on standard datasets
2. Discuss computational complexity and scalability more thoroughly
3. Provide more detailed analysis of how the method performs on real-world datasets beyond the locomotion data example
4. Clarify the distinction between their method and Williams et al.'s extended DMD [35]

With these improvements, the paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS. As it stands, it's a solid technical contribution that could be significantly strengthened with more comprehensive empirical validation.
