- Overall assessment: solid theoretical RKHS extension of DMD but insufficient empirical evidence to justify added complexity — recommendation: weak reject.  
- Motivation: sound — RKHS can overcome DMD’s linear basis limitation for nonlinear systems.  
- Theoretical contribution: largely correct and well-connected to Koopman operator theory; Theorems 1 and 2 are clear.  
- Relation to prior work: appropriately situates work among POD, balanced truncation, kernel PCA, and DMD variants.  
- Robustification: inclusion of POD to address ill-conditioning is practical and appropriate.  
- Applications scope: broad (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) but superficially treated.  
- Major weakness — empirical validation: experiments are too limited and toy examples don’t demonstrate clear advantage.  
- Lack of comparative analysis: no thorough comparisons to existing DMD variants (e.g., extended, sparsity-promoting, optimized DMD).  
- Quantitative evaluation missing: prediction and change-point examples lack error metrics and statistical analysis.  
- Hénon map results: suggestive but not definitive or quantitatively compared.  
- Parameter sensitivity not addressed: no study or guidance on kernel selection and bandwidth.  
- Computational cost omitted: no complexity analysis or runtime comparisons to justify added cost.  
- Implementation details sparse: Matlab code exists but paper lacks kernel/parameter tuning and reproducibility details.  
- Notation clarity: generally clear but occasionally dense, especially in Section 3.  
- Originality/significance: kernelizing DMD is not entirely novel, but the RKHS formulation and theory could be valuable if empirical benefits are shown.  
- Pros for acceptance: solid theory, addresses DMD limitation, potential for improved accuracy in nonlinear systems.  
- Cons against acceptance: insufficient empirical validation, poor comparative analysis, superficial applications, and limited implementation discussion.  
- Suggested revisions: expand empirical benchmarks (chaotic, real-world, fluid simulations), perform rigorous comparisons, report quantitative metrics, analyze parameter sensitivity, and add computational cost analysis.
