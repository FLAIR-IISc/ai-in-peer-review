### **Summary**

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), which they term "kernel DMD". The central idea is to extend Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a popular data-driven method for analyzing dynamical systems, to a nonlinear setting by applying the "kernel trick". The Koopman operator, which linearly evolves observable functions of a nonlinear system, is approximated in an RKHS. The authors first propose a direct algorithm based on a Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition of the Krylov matrix in the feature space. Recognizing the potential numerical instability of this approach, they then present a more robust version that first projects the data onto its principal components in the feature space (via Kernel PCA) before computing the projected Koopman operator. The extracted Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions are then used as features for several downstream tasks, including short-term prediction (requiring a pre-image reconstruction), recognition of dynamic patterns, and sequential change-point detection.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Elegant Conceptual Contribution:** The paper presents a natural and elegant synthesis of two powerful concepts: Koopman operator theory (via its approximation with DMD) and kernel methods. Lifting the state space to an RKHS to linearize the dynamics is a well-motivated approach, and this paper provides a concrete and principled algorithm for doing so in the DMD framework. This connects a popular technique from the physics and fluid dynamics communities with mainstream machine learning, which is a valuable contribution for the NIPS audience.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The theoretical development is clear and sound. The derivation in Section 3, while acknowledged as potentially unstable, correctly establishes the theoretical foundation by extending the Krylov subspace interpretation of DMD to the feature space. The robustified algorithm in Section 4 is a practical and well-justified improvement, analogous to the use of POD/SVD truncation in standard DMD. Theorem 2 provides a clear link between the eigenvectors of the computed finite-dimensional matrix `F̂` and the true Koopman eigenfunctions, under reasonable assumptions about the richness of the data.

3.  **Novel Applications:** The paper demonstrates the utility of the learned spectral features on a diverse and interesting set of applications beyond simple reconstruction. The use of kernel principal angles to measure distances between subspaces of Koopman eigenfunctions for dynamics recognition (Fig. 3) is a sophisticated and appropriate application. The formulation of a sequential change-point detection algorithm based on the learned model is also novel and interesting, showing how the method can be used for system monitoring.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Relationship to and Distinction from Prior Work:** The connection to Extended DMD (eDMD) by Williams et al. [35] could be made much clearer. The proposed robust algorithm in Section 4 is conceptually very similar to eDMD. In eDMD, one provides a dictionary of basis functions and computes a finite-dimensional approximation of the Koopman operator. The method in this paper effectively uses a dictionary of basis functions implicitly defined by the kernel and the data points, with Kernel PCA serving as a data-driven method to select a good, low-dimensional basis from this dictionary. While the authors cite Williams et al., they should more explicitly frame their contribution as a specific, non-parametric, and data-driven instantiation of the eDMD framework, where the dictionary selection is automated via Kernel PCA. This would better situate the paper's originality.

2.  **Superficial Experimental Evaluation:** While the applications are interesting, the empirical evaluation is mostly qualitative and lacks rigor.
    *   **Quantitative Metrics:** For the Hénon map (Fig. 1), the paper states eigenvalues "seem to be approximately estimated" without providing any quantitative error. For the prediction task (Fig. 2), no standard metrics like Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) are reported, nor is the prediction horizon discussed.
    *   **Baselines:** The experiments lack comparisons against relevant baselines. The prediction task should be compared against standard time-series models like Gaussian Process dynamical models or LSTMs. The change-point detection method is compared to a "kernel change detection method" which is not fully specified, and the evaluation is limited to a single illustrative example.
    *   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of kernel methods is critically dependent on the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters (e.g., kernel width). The paper provides little to no discussion on how these were selected or how sensitive the results are to these choices. The same applies to the truncation rank `p` in the robust algorithm.

3.  **Practical Concerns Not Addressed:**
    *   **Scalability:** The method relies on constructing and decomposing Gram matrices of size τ x τ, where τ is the number of time points. This scales as O(τ³), which can be prohibitive for long time series. This limitation should be acknowledged and discussed.
    *   **Pre-image Problem:** Prediction in the original state space requires solving the pre-image problem, which is known to be difficult and ill-posed for many kernels. The paper employs a reasonable heuristic based on MDS, but does not discuss its computational cost, accuracy, or potential failure modes. This is a significant practical hurdle for using the method for prediction.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written. However, in the proof of Theorem 1, `MτF = KHMτ` seems to be used. It would be clearer to state that `KHMτ` is approximated by `MτF` on the Krylov subspace, as `KH` acts on the entire space `H`.
*   **Significance of Section 3:** The algorithm in Section 3 is presented as the primary theoretical contribution but is immediately dismissed as impractical. It might be better to frame Section 3 as a direct but naive extension, motivating the necessity of the robust projection-based approach in Section 4 from the outset.
*   **To strengthen the experimental section, I would recommend:**
    1.  Including quantitative error metrics for eigenvalue estimation on the chaotic systems.
    2.  Adding at least one standard baseline method for the prediction task and reporting RMSE over multiple time steps and trials.
    3.  For the dynamics recognition task, reporting classification accuracy in a confusion matrix to show which motions are difficult to distinguish.
    4.  Providing more details on the baseline for change-point detection and evaluating it on more examples, possibly including false positive/negative rates.
    5.  Adding a paragraph in the appendix discussing the choice of kernel and hyperparameter settings.

### **Conclusion**

This paper presents a valuable and well-motivated extension of DMD to nonlinear systems using kernel methods. The theoretical formulation is sound, and the demonstrated applications are compelling. However, the work is weakened by a lack of rigorous empirical evaluation and a somewhat understated connection to the closely related eDMD framework. Despite these weaknesses, the core idea is strong and likely to be of significant interest to the machine learning community working on dynamical systems. I lean towards acceptance but strongly encourage the authors to address the weaknesses in the experimental validation and clarify the paper's novelty with respect to prior art.
