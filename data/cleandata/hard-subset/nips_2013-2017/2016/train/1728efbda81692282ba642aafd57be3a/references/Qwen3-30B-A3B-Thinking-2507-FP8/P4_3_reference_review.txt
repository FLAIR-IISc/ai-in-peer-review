

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary
This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors address a limitation of standard DMD, which uses linear monomials of observables as basis functions (equivalent to a one-term Taylor expansion), by introducing kernel-based nonlinear basis functions. The paper develops a modal decomposition algorithm that operates in RKHS, providing a more expressive approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions. The approach is theoretically grounded with proofs of key theorems, and the authors present a robust implementation via projection onto POD directions. The paper demonstrates applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data.

The work builds upon established Koopman operator theory and DMD variants [25, 26, 33, 35], extending the "extended DMD" framework [35] by working directly in the feature space rather than with pre-determined basis functions. The paper correctly cites relevant prior work, including Williams et al.'s extended DMD and the connections to kernel methods [28, 36].

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a mathematically rigorous extension of DMD using RKHS. Theorems 1 and 2 provide solid theoretical foundations, and the algorithmic steps are clearly defined. The robustification via POD projection (Section 4) addresses a known practical limitation of DMD, making the method more applicable to real-world problems.

2. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are logically presented, and the connections between standard DMD and the proposed kernel-based approach are well-explained. The empirical examples (Figures 1-4) effectively demonstrate the method's capabilities.

3. **Novelty**: The use of RKHS for Koopman spectral analysis represents a meaningful extension beyond existing DMD variants. While extended DMD [35] works with pre-determined basis functions, this paper uses kernel methods to generate richer, adaptive basis functions directly from the data, which is a significant technical contribution.

4. **Significance**: The applications demonstrated (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) showcase the practical utility of the method. The paper successfully bridges theoretical Koopman analysis with practical applications in dynamical systems, potentially benefiting fields like fluid dynamics, control theory, and time-series analysis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Existing Methods**: While the paper cites relevant work, it does not provide a thorough comparative analysis with existing methods, particularly extended DMD [35]. A more detailed comparison of computational complexity, accuracy, and applicability would strengthen the claims of novelty and significance.

2. **Lack of Limitation Analysis**: The paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed approach. For instance, the computational complexity of the Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in RKHS for large datasets is not discussed, nor are the conditions under which the kernel-based approach would be superior to standard DMD.

3. **Insufficient Justification for Kernel Parameters**: The paper uses specific kernel parameters (e.g., polynomial degree 3 for the toy system, Gaussian width 1 for Hénon map) without justification. A discussion of how to select these parameters for different systems would improve practical utility.

4. **Limited Real-World Validation**: While the paper demonstrates applications on the Hénon map and locomotion data, the empirical examples are somewhat limited. More diverse, large-scale real-world applications would better establish the method's significance.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Pro-acceptance arguments**:
- The paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD with clear theoretical justification
- The RKHS-based approach offers a meaningful advancement over existing DMD variants
- The empirical results demonstrate practical utility across multiple applications
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized

**Con-acceptance arguments**:
- The comparison with existing methods (particularly extended DMD) is insufficient
- The paper does not adequately address computational limitations
- The kernel parameter selection lacks justification
- The empirical validation is limited to a small number of examples

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a technically sound contribution to Koopman spectral analysis by extending DMD with kernel methods. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the approach offers a meaningful improvement over standard DMD for capturing complex nonlinear dynamics. While the empirical validation is promising, the paper would be strengthened by more thorough comparisons with existing methods and a clearer discussion of computational limitations. The work aligns well with NIPS's focus on theoretical and practical advances in machine learning, particularly in the intersection of dynamical systems and kernel methods.

The paper meets the quality, clarity, and significance criteria for NIPS. While it has some limitations in the comparative analysis and empirical validation, these do not undermine the technical contribution. The novelty of using RKHS for Koopman spectral analysis is sufficiently distinct from previous work, as evidenced by the clear theoretical differences from extended DMD [35].

I recommend acceptance, as the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems.
