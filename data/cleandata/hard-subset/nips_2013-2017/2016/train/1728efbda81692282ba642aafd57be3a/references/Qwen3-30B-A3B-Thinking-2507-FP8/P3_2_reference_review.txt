

# Peer Review: "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors extend the traditional DMD framework, which approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials of observables, to use kernel-based nonlinear basis functions. The core idea is to perform spectral analysis directly in the feature space induced by a kernel function, thereby capturing more complex dynamics that linear basis functions cannot represent. The paper develops a theoretical framework for this approach, provides an algorithm for implementation, and demonstrates applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The authors further introduce a robustification step using Principal Orthogonal Directions (POD) to address numerical instability issues common in DMD implementations.

The work connects to several established lines of research: Koopman operator theory, DMD, kernel methods, and spectral analysis of dynamical systems. It builds on Williams et al.'s Extended DMD, which also uses pre-determined basis functions, but extends this to a kernel-based framework that automatically learns nonlinear basis functions rather than relying on predefined ones.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for extending DMD to RKHS. Theorems 1 and 2 clearly establish the connection between the kernel-based approach and the Koopman spectral analysis framework. The mathematical derivations are sound and well-justified.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors recognize a key practical limitation of the naive kernel-based DMD (ill-conditioning) and provide a robustification approach using POD, which is well-motivated and follows established practices in DMD literature.

3. **Relevant Applications**: The demonstrated applications (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) are compelling and directly leverage the spectral features extracted by the method. The locomotion data example is particularly convincing for demonstrating the utility of the feature extraction approach.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from theoretical background to algorithm development to applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Clear Distinction from Extended DMD**: The paper mentions Williams et al.'s Extended DMD (2015) as related work but fails to clearly articulate how their kernel-based approach differs from it. Extended DMD also uses pre-determined basis functions, which is conceptually similar to the kernel-based approach. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a detailed comparison showing why the kernel-based approach is superior in specific scenarios where Extended DMD might fail.

2. **Limited Experimental Evaluation**: The experiments are limited to a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, and a single locomotion dataset. The paper lacks:
   - Comparison with Extended DMD and other kernel-based Koopman analysis methods
   - A systematic study of how kernel choice and hyperparameters affect performance
   - Experiments on datasets where linear basis functions would clearly fail but kernel methods would succeed
   - Analysis of computational complexity compared to existing methods

3. **Theoretical Gaps**: While the paper provides a theoretical framework, it lacks:
   - Convergence guarantees for the kernel-based approximation
   - Error bounds for the Koopman eigenvalue/eigenfunction estimation
   - Conditions under which the kernel-based approach would provide significant advantages over linear basis functions

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper mentions a MATLAB code repository but doesn't provide sufficient details about the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments (e.g., kernel width for Gaussian kernel, degree for polynomial kernel), making reproducibility difficult. The robustification step via POD is described, but the paper doesn't analyze how this affects the quality of the Koopman modes.

5. **Underdeveloped Applications**: The applications sections (prediction, change detection, recognition) are promising but lack sufficient justification for why the kernel-based approach is better than simpler alternatives. For example, the prediction via preimage method is not compared with standard time-series prediction methods.

## Specific Concerns

1. **Section 3 - Theoretical Development**: The paper states "the proposed algorithm gives an approximation with a set of nonlinear basis functions due to the expressiveness of kernel functions." However, it doesn't provide any evidence that these nonlinear basis functions actually capture dynamics that linear basis functions cannot. The toy example with the two eigenvalues (0.5 and 0.9) could have been solved with linear basis functions, making the need for kernel methods unclear.

2. **Section 6 - Empirical Example**: The Hénon map example shows the estimated eigenvalues, but it's not clear how much better the kernel-based method performs compared to standard DMD. The paper should include quantitative metrics (e.g., reconstruction error, prediction accuracy) to demonstrate the advantage of the kernel-based approach.

3. **Section 7 - Applications**: The change-point detection example (Figure 4) shows a comparison with "kernel change detection method" but doesn't specify what this method is or how it relates to the proposed approach. A clearer comparison with state-of-the-art change-point detection methods would strengthen this contribution.

4. **Related Work (Section 5)**: The related work section mentions Williams et al.'s Extended DMD but fails to provide a thorough comparison of the two methods. The paper should explicitly state how their kernel-based approach differs from Extended DMD in terms of basis function selection, theoretical guarantees, and practical performance.

## Overall Assessment

The paper addresses an important problem in dynamical systems analysis and makes a theoretically sound contribution to extending DMD with kernel methods. However, the lack of clear distinction from Extended DMD, limited experimental evaluation, and insufficient theoretical guarantees prevent the work from making a sufficiently significant contribution to the field.

## Recommendation

I recommend **rejection** of this paper in its current form. While the theoretical foundation is strong, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate the novelty and advantages of the proposed kernel-based approach over existing methods. The experiments are limited in scope and don't provide sufficient evidence that the method offers meaningful improvements over standard DMD or Extended DMD.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Conduct a thorough comparison with Extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) on a range of benchmark datasets, including cases where linear basis functions would fail.

2. Add a section on theoretical guarantees (e.g., convergence rates, error bounds) for the kernel-based Koopman spectral analysis.

3. Expand the experimental section to include more diverse datasets, including real-world time series where nonlinear dynamics are known to be present.

4. Provide more detailed implementation information, including hyperparameter choices and computational complexity analysis.

5. Clarify the relationship between the proposed method and Extended DMD, explicitly stating the advantages of the kernel-based approach.

6. Include quantitative metrics (e.g., prediction error, reconstruction error) to demonstrate the practical benefits of the kernel-based approach.

## Final Thoughts

The paper addresses an important problem in nonlinear dynamics analysis, but the current version doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that the kernel-based approach provides meaningful advantages over existing methods. With more thorough experimental validation, clearer theoretical distinctions from related work, and a stronger justification for the kernel-based approach, this could be a valuable contribution to the field. As it stands, however, the paper's novelty and significance are not sufficiently demonstrated.
