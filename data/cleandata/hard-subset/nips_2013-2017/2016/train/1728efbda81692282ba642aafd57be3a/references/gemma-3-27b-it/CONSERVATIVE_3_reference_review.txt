This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to improve the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions, moving beyond linear monomials. The core idea is to project the dynamics into a feature space defined by a kernel, perform a DMD-like decomposition there, and then recover modes. The authors present a theorem establishing the equivalence to standard DMD and address potential ill-conditioning by projecting onto POD directions. Applications to prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection are demonstrated on synthetic and real data.

While the concept of extending DMD with kernel methods is a natural progression, and the mathematical derivations appear sound, I find the presentation lacking in substantial novelty and rigorous validation. The core contribution – applying kernel methods to DMD – feels incremental, and the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over established DMD variants, particularly those addressing the same ill-conditioning issues.  The presented empirical evaluation, while demonstrating functionality, is insufficient to justify acceptance to NIPS.

**Strengths:**

*   **Mathematical Correctness:** The theoretical framework appears logically sound, extending DMD in a mathematically principled way. The theorem establishing the connection to standard DMD is a positive aspect.
*   **Addressing Ill-Conditioning:** Recognizing and attempting to address the potential ill-conditioning via POD projection is a practical consideration.
*   **Clear Presentation of Background:** The introduction provides a reasonable overview of DMD, Koopman operators, and related techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The application of kernel methods to DMD feels largely expected. The core idea doesn’t represent a significant paradigm shift and doesn’t present a fundamentally new approach to Koopman spectral analysis. Many kernel-based methods already exist for system identification and nonlinear dimensionality reduction; the specific contribution to this broader landscape isn't clearly articulated.
*   **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is weak.  The toy system is overly simplistic and doesn’t demonstrate the benefits of the kernel approach over standard DMD. The Hénon map example provides some validation, but the results are not compelling, and the comparison to existing methods on the Hénon map is absent.  The application sections (prediction, recognition, change-point detection) lack detailed comparisons against state-of-the-art methods.  Simply *showing* results doesn’t demonstrate superiority.  What is the performance gain? What are the limitations?
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  The paper doesn't include ablation studies to demonstrate the impact of different kernel choices, kernel parameters, or the POD projection. How sensitive is the performance to these choices?
*   **Missing Discussion of Computational Complexity:** Introducing kernel methods generally increases computational cost. The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to standard DMD, particularly in high-dimensional spaces. This is a critical practical consideration.
*   **Vague Claims of Superiority:** The paper repeatedly asserts that the kernel approach allows for approximation of eigenfunctions with "richer basis functions," but doesn't rigorously demonstrate this advantage in practice. What types of dynamics *require* the expressiveness of the kernel method, and how does this method outperform alternatives in those cases?
* **Related Work Coverage:** While the related work section is present, it doesn’t fully engage with the broader literature on kernel methods for system identification and dynamical systems. The connection to spectral methods and subspace identification is mentioned but not fully explored.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Mathematically sound extension of DMD.
*   Addresses a potential practical issue (ill-conditioning).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty.
*   Insufficient empirical validation and comparison to existing methods.
*   Lack of analysis of computational complexity.
*   Vague claims of improvement without rigorous demonstration.
*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the work is technically correct and addresses a reasonable extension of DMD, it lacks the novelty, rigorous validation, and compelling results required for acceptance at NIPS. The authors need to significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation, compare their method to state-of-the-art alternatives, and provide a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity and practical limitations. The current paper reads more like a preliminary investigation than a complete and impactful contribution. The authors should consider submitting to a more specialized conference or journal after addressing these concerns.
