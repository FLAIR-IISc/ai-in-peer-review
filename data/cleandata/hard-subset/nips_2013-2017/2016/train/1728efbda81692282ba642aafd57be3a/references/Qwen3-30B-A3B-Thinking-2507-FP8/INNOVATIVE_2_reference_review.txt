

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes a novel extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) by incorporating reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors extend traditional DMD, which uses linear monomials as basis functions for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions, to employ kernel functions that provide nonlinear basis functions. This approach allows for richer approximations of the Koopman eigenfunctions, particularly in regions where linear approximations fail. The paper presents a theoretical framework for this extension, including two algorithms (one direct, one robustified via POD bases), and demonstrates applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper makes a strong contribution by connecting kernel methods with Koopman spectral analysis, a relatively underexplored area. While Extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) has previously used pre-determined basis functions, this work is novel in using RKHS to automatically generate nonlinear basis functions through kernel methods. This approach is theoretically grounded and provides a principled way to capture nonlinear dynamics beyond the local linearization that standard DMD relies on. The authors also provide a robustified version using POD bases, addressing a known practical limitation of DMD implementations.

The paper is well-written, with clear mathematical derivations and a logical flow from theory to applications. The connection between the proposed method and existing DMD variants is well-explained, and the related work section appropriately cites key papers in the field. The empirical examples on synthetic systems (toy nonlinear system, Hénon map) demonstrate the method's ability to estimate Koopman eigenvalues accurately. The applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition show reasonable practical utility, though the empirical validation is somewhat limited to synthetic data and a single real-world dataset (locomotion data).

The main strengths of this paper are:
- **Novel approach**: Using RKHS for DMD represents a significant conceptual advance over existing methods
- **Theoretical soundness**: The mathematical derivations are correct and well-motivated
- **Practical relevance**: The robustified implementation addresses a known practical challenge
- **Clear presentation**: The paper is well-organized and accessible to readers familiar with DMD and kernel methods

The main weaknesses are:
- **Limited empirical validation**: The method is demonstrated on only two synthetic systems and one real-world dataset, with no comparison to state-of-the-art methods on standard benchmarks
- **Implementation details**: The paper doesn't sufficiently address computational complexity or scalability to high-dimensional systems
- **Application novelty**: While the applications are relevant, they don't clearly demonstrate superior performance over existing methods

The paper's core idea—using RKHS to generate nonlinear basis functions for Koopman spectral analysis—is sufficiently novel and conceptually bold to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors have made a significant contribution by bridging kernel methods with Koopman analysis, a field that has seen growing interest but lacks this specific integration. While the empirical validation could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons to existing methods, the theoretical novelty and conceptual contribution outweigh this limitation.

This work aligns well with NIPS' interest in novel approaches to dynamical systems modeling, which has been a recurring theme at the conference (e.g., recent papers on Koopman operators, neural dynamical systems, and kernel-based methods for time series analysis). The paper makes a clear contribution to this emerging area, offering a fresh perspective on how to better approximate the Koopman operator for nonlinear dynamics.

**Recommendation**: Accept

**Pro for acceptance**:
- Novel integration of RKHS with DMD for Koopman spectral analysis
- Theoretical contribution that significantly extends existing DMD approaches
- Clear mathematical development with sound theoretical foundation
- Potential to influence future work in dynamical systems modeling

**Con for acceptance**:
- Limited empirical validation with only synthetic data and one real-world application
- Lacks comparison to state-of-the-art methods on standard benchmarks
- Implementation details could be more thoroughly explained

The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to Koopman spectral analysis that addresses a fundamental limitation of standard DMD. While the empirical validation could be expanded, the core contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors have provided a compelling theoretical foundation that opens up new possibilities for modeling nonlinear dynamical systems using kernel methods. This work has the potential to inspire future research at the intersection of kernel methods, dynamical systems, and machine learning.
