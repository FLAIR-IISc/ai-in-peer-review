

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an innovative extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) by incorporating reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors address a fundamental limitation of traditional DMD, which approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials (analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion), by instead using kernel functions to create expressive nonlinear basis functions. This represents a significant conceptual advance in the Koopman operator framework, as it enables the approximation of eigenfunctions beyond the local linearization regime that limits standard DMD.

The paper makes a strong case for the novelty of this approach. Traditional DMD variants (including extended DMD by Williams et al.) work with pre-determined basis functions or linear monomials, while this paper leverages the full expressive power of kernel methods to create a richer functional approximation. The authors provide a theoretically sound derivation showing how the Koopman spectral analysis can be performed via Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in RKHS, followed by a robustification step using kernel PCA to address numerical instability issues. The paper demonstrates the approach on synthetic and real-world datasets, including a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map, with promising results on eigenvalue estimation. The applications section shows the utility of the method for state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition, with empirical validation on locomotion data and the Lorenz map.

The core contribution is highly novel - extending DMD to RKHS for Koopman spectral analysis is not a simple incremental improvement but rather a new conceptual direction. This aligns well with NIPS's emphasis on bold, novel approaches. The paper successfully bridges the gap between kernel methods (common in machine learning) and Koopman operator theory (common in physics and engineering), creating a valuable connection between these communities.

The theoretical foundation is solid, with clear theorems establishing the connection between the proposed algorithm and Koopman spectral analysis. The robustification approach using POD bases is well-motivated and addresses a practical concern that plagues many DMD variants. The empirical results are convincing for the toy examples and the Hénon map, though the real-world application examples could benefit from more extensive comparisons to existing methods.

In terms of significance, this work opens up new possibilities for analyzing complex nonlinear dynamical systems using kernel methods. The applications demonstrated (prediction, change detection, dynamics recognition) show clear potential for practical impact in various domains. The paper makes a compelling case for how this approach could be more expressive than existing DMD variants, particularly for systems where local linearization is inadequate.

Minor weaknesses include limited scalability analysis, a somewhat narrow set of empirical examples (though the paper states the method is applicable to general dynamical systems), and insufficient comparison to kernel-based methods for Koopman analysis (if any exist). However, these are secondary to the fundamental novelty of the approach.

The paper is well-written, well-organized, and properly contextualizes its contribution within related work. The connection to previous DMD variants (including extended DMD) is clearly articulated, and the paper distinguishes its contribution from these prior works.

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance that could inspire new directions in both machine learning and dynamical systems analysis. While the empirical validation is promising but somewhat limited, the core idea is bold, novel, and well-motivated. The paper successfully demonstrates how kernel methods can enrich Koopman spectral analysis beyond the linear approximations of traditional DMD.

I believe this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field and would be a valuable addition to NIPS. The novelty of the approach outweighs the minor weaknesses in the empirical validation and scalability discussion. The authors have presented a clear new direction for Koopman spectral analysis that could lead to important developments in both theory and applications.

**Recommendation: Accept**
