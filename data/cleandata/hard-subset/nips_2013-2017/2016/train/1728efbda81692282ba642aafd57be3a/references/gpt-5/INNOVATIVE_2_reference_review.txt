Summary
The paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The core idea is to view DMD as an Arnoldi/Krylov procedure and then lift it to an RKHS via a feature map ϕ, so the Koopman operator KH: ϕ(x) ↦ ϕ(f(x)) remains linear. The authors derive an RKHS DMD using Gram–Schmidt on the Krylov matrix Mτ = [ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)], define empirical Ritz values/vectors, and show a reconstruction analogous to standard DMD (Theorem 1). Because Gram–Schmidt in RKHS can be ill-conditioned, they “robustify” using kernel PCA (POD) to project onto leading directions and compute a reduced projected operator F̂ (Theorem 2). Finally, they present applications: short-term prediction via pre-image, dynamics recognition using kernel principal angles between subspaces of Koopman eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection via a CUSUM-style statistic, with small synthetic and motion-capture demonstrations.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: casting DMD explicitly as a Krylov/Arnoldi method in RKHS is a clean perspective that unifies DMD and kernel methods.
- Practical kernelization: the algorithm is written in terms of Gram matrices, enabling use with implicit feature maps and standard kernels.
- Robust variant: the kernel-PCA/POD truncation is sensible, helps conditioning, and ties to common practice in DMD.
- Applications: the paper goes beyond operator estimation to show how Koopman features can support prediction, recognition, and change detection, which is appealing to practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior art: Kernel DMD/EDMD (Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, 2015) already kernelizes DMD/Koopman Galerkin approximation, including a Gram-matrix formulation with projection/truncation. The presented procedure appears largely equivalent in spirit and mechanics; Theorem 1 is essentially Arnoldi in feature space; Theorem 2’s connection to Koopman eigenfunctions under subspace-invariance assumptions mirrors EDMD’s dictionary-invariance claims. The paper needs a sharper differentiation.
- Theory is mostly a re-packaging of standard linear algebra: proofs rely on QR/companion/diagonalization arguments; conditions for recovery (e.g., “fully recovered if ϕ(xτ) lies in the span”) are strong and not operationally verifiable. Statements like “eigenvalues on the unit circle on an attractor” are not generally correct in this breadth.
- Experimental evaluation is weak: qualitative figures on toy/Hénon, one change-point baseline, and a small embedding demo. No quantitative comparisons against EDMD/kernel DMD/optimized DMD, no metrics (eigenvalue error, prediction RMSE, classification accuracy), no sensitivity to kernel choice, no scalability benchmarks.
- Clarity/notation issues and minor inaccuracies: heavy overloading of ϕ/φ, some algebraic steps glossed (e.g., inversion/conditioning), typos, and an anonymization breach via a code URL.

Quality
Technically the methods are sound as far as standard kernelized linear algebra goes, but several claims are imprecise or over-stated, and important practicalities are underdeveloped:
- Numerical stability and regularization: the robust variant hints at truncation, but there is no discussion of regularization (e.g., Tikhonov) or how to choose ranks/thresholds.
- Pre-image prediction: the MDS-based pre-image step is heuristic and not analyzed; competing pre-image solvers or dictionary-based regression baselines are not compared.
- Complexity/scalability: no discussion of computational costs (O(τ3) eigendecompositions; Gram matrices scale as τ2) or remedies (Nyström, random features).

Clarity
The paper is mostly readable but should be edited for precision:
- Tighten notation (use distinct symbols for feature map, Koopman eigenfunctions, and Ritz vectors; avoid overloading φ).
- Be explicit about dimensions and conditioning; avoid claims that require non-generic invariance assumptions.
- Fix typos and grammar (“robustly it”, “we proceed with this assumption in the following parts”, etc.).
- Remove non-anonymized code link.

Originality
Moderate. The RKHS-Krylov view is elegant but close to kernel DMD/EDMD. The robustification via kernel PCA aligns with existing DMD practices (projected/exact/optimized DMD). The application ideas are natural extensions rather than fundamentally new methods.

Significance
Potentially useful as a pedagogical unification, but current empirical evidence is insufficient to claim an advance over existing kernel DMD/EDMD. Stronger comparisons and demonstrations on challenging, higher-dimensional datasets (e.g., video, fluid flows) would raise impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Positioning: Clearly articulate how this differs from and improves upon EDMD/kernel DMD. For example, is the Krylov/Arnoldi perspective yielding better estimators, sample efficiency, or numerical stability? Provide theory or experiments.
- Theory: Replace blanket statements (e.g., on unit-circle spectra) with precise conditions; discuss invariant subspaces and approximation error (Galerkin/projection error bounds).
- Numerics: Add regularization strategies, rank-selection criteria, and complexity analysis; consider Nyström/random features for scalability.
- Experiments: Quantitatively compare against EDMD/kernel DMD/exact/optimized DMD on: (i) eigenvalue/mode recovery on systems with known spectra; (ii) multi-step prediction RMSE and pre-image quality; (iii) classification accuracy for dynamics recognition; (iv) change-point detection AUROC/ARL metrics. Include sensitivity to kernel choice and hyperparameters.
- Anonymity and clarity: remove identifying links; clean up notation and proofs.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean unification of Koopman/DMD with RKHS and a practical algorithm.
- Breadth of potential applications and an appealing conceptual contribution.

Cons for acceptance
- Overlaps substantially with prior kernel DMD/EDMD; limited novelty.
- Weak empirical validation and lack of quantitative, comparative results.
- Some theoretical/informational gaps and clarity issues.

Recommendation: Reject
While the direction is appealing and I value bold, unifying ideas, the current submission does not sufficiently differentiate itself from EDMD/kernel DMD nor provide strong empirical evidence. With clearer positioning, stronger experiments, and improved rigor, a revised version could be impactful.
