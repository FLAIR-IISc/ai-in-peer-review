- Summary: Extends DMD into RKHS to better approximate nonlinear Koopman eigenfunctions via a Krylov-subspace projection with a POD-based fix for ill-conditioning; tested on a toy system and the Henon map and applied to prediction, recognition, and change-point detection.

- Novelty: Introduces a worthwhile extension of DMD to RKHS to capture nonlinear Koopman eigenfunctions beyond linear monomials.

- Theoretical foundation: Provides solid theoretical justification with connections to Koopman operators, Krylov methods, kernel methods, and supporting theorems.

- Robustness: Recognizes and addresses ill-conditioning using a POD-based projection.

- Applications demonstrated: Shows potential uses in prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

- Presentation: Generally well-organized with good background on DMD and Koopman theory.

- Empirical validation: Evaluation is limited to simple examples (toy system, Henon map) and results are not compelling.

- Visualizations: Figures (e.g., Fig. 1) are not very informative.

- Comparative evaluation: Lacks thorough comparison to existing DMD variants, especially kernel DMD and extended DMD.

- Quantitative evaluation: Applications lack quantitative metrics (prediction error, classification accuracy, detection rates), making performance hard to assess.

- Application details: Important implementation choices (kernel parameters, pre-image computation) are under-specified and vague.

- Related work connection: Insufficiently connects to and distinguishes from extended DMD and broader spectral-method literature.

- Notation: Mathematical notation is sometimes overly dense and could be simplified for readability.

- Quality assessment: Technically sound theory but empirical support is weak and incomplete.

- Clarity assessment: Generally clear but application details and notation need improvement.

- Originality assessment: Novel extension but originality tempered by related prior work like extended DMD.

- Significance assessment: Potentially moderate significance if empirical performance can be demonstrated on challenging systems.

- Arguments for acceptance: Novel method with sound theory, addresses ill-conditioning, and explores practical applications.

- Arguments against acceptance: Weak empirical validation, lack of quantitative results, inadequate comparison to related work, and some clarity issues.

- Recommendation: Reject with encouragement to revise — strengthen experiments on complex datasets, add quantitative application metrics, perform head-to-head comparison with extended DMD, and improve clarity and notation.
