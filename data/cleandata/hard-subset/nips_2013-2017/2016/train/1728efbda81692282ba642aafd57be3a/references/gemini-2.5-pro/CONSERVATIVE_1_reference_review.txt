This paper presents an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for the spectral analysis of the Koopman operator by leveraging Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The central idea is to lift the state of a nonlinear dynamical system into an RKHS via a kernel function, and then perform DMD in this high-dimensional feature space. The authors propose that this approach, termed kernel DMD (kDMD), allows for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions using a richer, nonlinear set of basis functions compared to the linear observables used in standard DMD. The paper first derives a method based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space and then presents a more numerically robust version that projects the data onto kernel principal components (POD bases in the feature space). The utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features is demonstrated through applications in state prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Theoretical Framework:** The paper provides a methodologically sound and elegant synthesis of three well-established fields: Koopman operator theory, dynamic mode decomposition, and kernel methods. The extension of DMD principles to an RKHS is a natural and logical step. The derivation, particularly in Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, clearly connects the proposed computational procedures to the underlying theory of Koopman spectral analysis.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It begins with a sufficient background on the Koopman operator and DMD, making the work accessible. The progression from the direct, but potentially unstable, Gram-Schmidt formulation to the more practical, robustified version using kernel PCA is logical and demonstrates an awareness of potential implementation issues.

3.  **Practical Robustification:** The authors rightly identify the potential for numerical instability in their initial formulation (Section 3) and propose a robust version based on kernel PCA (Section 4). This approach is analogous to the widely used practice of projecting data onto POD modes before applying standard DMD, and it shows a good understanding of the practical challenges involved in these decomposition methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation and Baselines:** The primary weakness of this work lies in its empirical evaluation. While the presented applications are interesting, they lack the rigor and comparative analysis necessary to establish the significance of the proposed method.
    *   **Lack of Baselines:** The experiments fail to compare kDMD against relevant and state-of-the-art baselines. For the prediction task (Fig. 2), how does the proposed method compare to standard nonlinear time-series models like Gaussian Process dynamical models [34] or recurrent neural networks? For dynamics recognition (Fig. 3), no quantitative classification results are provided, nor is there a comparison to other dynamics-aware kernel methods or sequence classification models. In the change-point detection example (Fig. 4), the comparison is limited to a single method, and critical performance metrics like detection delay or false positive rates are not evaluated. Crucially, there is no comparison against standard DMD or, more importantly, Extended DMD [35].
    *   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The core quantitative results are demonstrated on a simple 2D toy system and the Hénon map. While useful for illustration, these low-dimensional examples do not provide compelling evidence that the method is effective or necessary for more complex, high-dimensional problems where the power of kernel methods is typically most pronounced.

2.  **Understated Relationship to Prior Work:** The proposed robust algorithm (Section 4) is functionally very similar to the Extended DMD (eDMD) framework proposed by Williams et al. [35]. In eDMD, the Koopman operator is approximated in a subspace spanned by a user-defined dictionary of observable functions. The proposed kDMD can be viewed as an instance of eDMD where the dictionary is implicitly defined by the kernel feature map. While the RKHS perspective is elegant, the paper does not adequately discuss this strong connection or demonstrate a clear advantage over a more general eDMD approach with a well-chosen dictionary of basis functions (e.g., polynomials, radial basis functions). This significantly tempers the claim of originality.

3.  **Lack of Hyperparameter Analysis:** The performance of any kernel-based method is highly dependent on the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters (e.g., the width of a Gaussian kernel or the degree of a polynomial kernel). The paper provides no analysis of this sensitivity. A thorough evaluation would include a discussion of how these parameters were selected and how their variation affects the quality of the estimated Koopman spectrum and the performance in downstream tasks. Without this, the reproducibility and practical utility of the method are questionable.

4.  **Scalability Concerns:** The proposed algorithm requires the computation and manipulation of Gram matrices of size τ × τ, where τ is the length of the data sequence. The computational complexity appears to be at least O(τ²), which would be prohibitive for the long time-series data often encountered in dynamical systems analysis. The paper does not address or discuss these scalability limitations.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   The paper presents a theoretically sound and elegant kernelization of the popular DMD algorithm.
    *   The presentation is clear and methodologically rigorous.
    *   The authors propose a practical, robustified algorithm that mirrors best practices in the field.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The empirical evaluation is weak and lacks comparison with established, relevant baselines, making it impossible to assess the practical significance of the contribution.
    *   The novelty is limited given the strong similarity to the existing Extended DMD framework, a connection that is not sufficiently explored.
    *   Critical practical aspects, such as hyperparameter sensitivity and computational scalability, are completely ignored.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper presents a theoretically appealing combination of established ideas, it fails to provide the necessary evidence to demonstrate its value as a practical tool for data analysis. The lack of a rigorous empirical comparison against strong baselines is a significant flaw. An algorithm, no matter how elegant, must prove its worth through thorough validation. As it stands, this work feels more like a preliminary study than a complete contribution. I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The authors should be encouraged to conduct a comprehensive experimental study, including detailed comparisons to methods like Extended DMD and standard machine learning models for dynamical systems, and to analyze the practical aspects of their method before resubmission.
