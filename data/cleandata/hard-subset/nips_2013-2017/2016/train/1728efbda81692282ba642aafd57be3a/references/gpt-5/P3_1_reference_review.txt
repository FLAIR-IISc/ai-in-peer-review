Summary
The paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) by (i) constructing a Krylov subspace from feature-mapped snapshots, (ii) projecting the Koopman operator onto this subspace via a Gram–Schmidt QR in feature space, and (iii) extracting eigenvalues/vectors (dynamic modes) from the projected operator. To improve numerical conditioning, the paper then introduces a “robustified” variant that projects onto truncated kernel PCA directions (akin to POD-truncated DMD). The authors illustrate the approach on a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map, and sketch applications: short-term prediction via a preimage method, dynamics recognition via subspace angles, and sequential change-point detection.

Relation to prior work
- The approach is closely related to Extended DMD (EDMD) and kernel DMD (KDMD) [Williams et al., JNS 2015], which formulate a Galerkin approximation of the Koopman operator on a finite dictionary, with kernelization providing an implicit high-dimensional feature map. The proposed “robustified” variant—building a kernel PCA basis from snapshot features and estimating the projected evolution operator using snapshot pairs—is essentially equivalent to KDMD (with centering/truncation and regularization choices).
- There is substantial related literature on operator approximation in RKHS, e.g., kernel transfer operators (Song et al., ICML 2013; Klus et al., 2018), VAMP/TICA (Noé et al., 2017), and spectral methods for dynamical systems. The paper cites EDMD but does not clearly delineate how the proposed method differs from, or improves upon, KDMD/EDMD and kernel transfer-operator approaches.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: DMD with linear observables can be too limited; kernelizing admits richer nonlinear features.
- The derivation emphasizes a Krylov/Arnoldi perspective in feature space, which may help connect DMD and subspace-ID viewpoints.
- The kernel-PCA-based truncation addresses numerical conditioning in a way consistent with best practices in DMD.
- The applications section suggests interesting downstream uses (prediction, recognition, change detection).

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Novelty: The core algorithmic contribution appears to reproduce KDMD/EDMD’s operator approximation in RKHS. The “robustified” variant via kernel PCA truncation is standard in practice. The differences from EDMD/KDMD are not made precise, limiting originality.
- Theorem 1 proof and linear algebra: The claim that F is a companion matrix after QR and the use of Mτ−1 with tall Mτ are not rigorous. In general Arnoldi yields an upper-Hessenberg projected operator; Vandermonde diagonalization via a companion form is only valid under restrictive conditions (e.g., Krylov basis, exact shift-invariance). As written, the equalities MτF = KHMτ and “companion” structure are asserted without the invariance conditions and with ambiguous dimensions. This undermines the main theoretical justification.
- Assumptions in Theorem 2 are very strong (true Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the RKHS feature span and furthermore within the subspace spanned by top kernel PCs). The result then restates a Galerkin projection property; no convergence/error bounds are provided (contrast with EDMD convergence analyses).
- Conceptual inaccuracies: “For a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is false in general; this holds for measure-preserving dynamics. Dissipative systems typically have eigenvalues strictly inside the unit disk.
- Centering: The method centers the Gram matrix (kernel PCA) while projecting dynamics. Centering changes the observable space and can break invariance; the effect on Koopman spectra is not discussed.
- Experiments are limited, mostly qualitative, and lack baselines. The Hénon map has a continuous spectrum on the chaotic attractor; focusing on equilibria eigenvalues is a very special case. No noise robustness, sensitivity to kernel choice/bandwidth, or scalability analysis is provided. The applications (preimage-based prediction, recognition via principal angles, change-point detection) are interesting but largely heuristic and lack quantitative comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., EDMD/KDMD, GPDM, TICA/VAMP, BOCPD/kernel CUSUM, DMD-based classifiers).
- Clarity/notation: The paper intermixes scalar Koopman eigenfunctions with vector-valued feature maps, calls φj “eigenfunctions” in H where KH acts on vector-valued features, and reuses notation φ̃j/φ̄j for vectors in H. Several equations omit conditions (e.g., when r=0) and manipulate non-square matrices as if invertible.

Quality
- The high-level idea is sound and consistent with operator-theoretic data-driven modeling. However, key derivations are not rigorous as written, and the empirical evaluation is insufficient to substantiate claims.

Clarity
- The paper is readable but suffers from imprecise notation, missing technical conditions, and some misleading statements. Proofs should be revised to use standard Arnoldi/DMD relations in Hilbert spaces, avoiding incorrect companion-matrix claims.

Originality
- Limited. The method largely recapitulates KDMD/EDMD with a kernel-PCA truncation and does not deliver clear novel theory or algorithms beyond existing formulations.

Significance
- Potentially useful in practice, but without stronger theoretical guarantees or thorough empirical comparisons, the contribution is incremental.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely position the method relative to EDMD/KDMD and kernel transfer operators; either show a genuine algorithmic/theoretical improvement (e.g., improved conditioning, provable error bounds, convergence results), or frame the work as a unifying perspective with careful proofs.
- Correct Theorem 1, avoiding companion-matrix claims; present the standard projected operator Hτ from Arnoldi in Hilbert space and prove properties under explicit subspace invariance assumptions.
- Discuss the impact of kernel centering on the Koopman spectrum; justify or avoid centering in the operator estimation step.
- Provide quantitative experiments with baselines (EDMD/KDMD, TICA/VAMP, exact DMD), include noise, kernel choice sensitivity, scalability, and out-of-sample prediction accuracy. For change-point detection and recognition tasks, compare against established methods.
- Clarify the preimage procedure and report its accuracy and computational cost.
- Fix technical inaccuracies (e.g., eigenvalues on unit circle), tighten notation, and complete/correct references.

Pros
- Motivated kernelization of Koopman spectral analysis
- Clear link to subspace projections; practical truncation
- Interesting application ideas

Cons
- Overlaps substantially with KDMD/EDMD; unclear novelty
- Theoretical derivations contain errors/overclaims
- Sparse, mostly qualitative experiments without baselines
- Some conceptual inaccuracies and notation issues

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The topic is important and the direction is promising, but the current version lacks rigor and novelty relative to established KDMD/EDMD, and the empirical validation is insufficient. With corrected theory and stronger experiments that demonstrate advantages over KDMD/EDMD, this could become a solid contribution.
