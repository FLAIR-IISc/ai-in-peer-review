- Proposes extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to better approximate Koopman eigenfunctions.  
- Introduces a Krylov-subspace-based algorithm and eigendecomposition of a projected Koopman operator in RKHS.  
- Adds a POD-based projection step to mitigate ill-conditioning and improve robustness.  
- Empirically evaluates the method on synthetic and real data for prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.  
- Provides a solid mathematical foundation with clear theorems and derivations (Theorems 1 & 2).  
- Positions the work within existing spectral methods, building on DMD literature and drawing inspiration from kernel PCA.  
- Acknowledges connections to subspace identification but notes further investigation is needed.  
- Writing and organization are clear, making theory and experiments easy to follow.  
- Lacks a strong comparative analysis quantifying improvements over standard DMD and other kernel-DMD variants.  
- Does not discuss how kernels and hyperparameters were chosen or analyze sensitivity to kernel parameters.  
- Fails to address computational complexity or scalability concerns for kernel methods on large datasets.  
- Omits some recent related work on kernel-based DMD, Koopman learning with control, and deep-learning-based Koopman methods.  
- No theoretical error bounds or convergence analysis; lacks discussion of potential error sources and their impact.  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically sound extension with practical robustness via POD and versatile empirical demonstrations.  
- Against acceptance: insufficient comparative benchmarks, missing discussion on computational cost and kernel selection, and limited error analysis.  
- Overall assessment: promising contribution but weakened by practical and comparative gaps — recommendation: Weak Accept.  
- Required revisions: add comparative analyses, detail kernel selection and sensitivity, discuss computational complexity and scalability, update related work, and include an error analysis discussion.
