This paper introduces a kernelized Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) method for Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to extend standard DMD by performing the decomposition in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), allowing for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions, unlike traditional DMD which relies on linear monomials. The authors present an algorithm based on Krylov subspace calculations and discuss a robustification technique utilizing POD. They demonstrate the method on synthetic and real-world data, and explore applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper is well-motivated, situating the work within the broader context of spectral methods for dynamical systems and highlighting the limitations of existing techniques like POD and standard DMD. The mathematical formulation is generally clear, and the extension to RKHS is logically presented. The inclusion of a robustification step using POD directions is a practical consideration and addresses a potential issue with ill-conditioning. The empirical evaluation includes both a toy system and a more complex chaotic map (Henon map), showcasing the method's ability to estimate eigenvalues. The exploration of various applications (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) strengthens the paper’s potential impact. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, citing relevant literature in DMD, Koopman operator theory, and kernel methods.

However, the paper suffers from a few weaknesses. While the mathematical derivations are presented, the connection between the theoretical framework and practical implementation is not always fully elucidated. For instance, the choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., kernel width) seems somewhat arbitrary and lacks a detailed justification. The empirical results, while demonstrating feasibility, are not extensive enough to provide strong evidence of superiority over existing methods. The prediction results, for example, lack a quantitative comparison to other prediction techniques. The description of the application sections is concise, leaving details of implementation ambiguous. The reference to code availability is helpful but a more thorough documentation would increase reproducibility. Moreover, the robustness improvement via POD could be expanded upon – a clearer discussion of when POD projection is necessary and how to select the appropriate number of POD modes would be beneficial. While Theorem 2 is stated, the implications of the assumption that κj is in the subspace spanned by U are not sufficiently discussed; what happens if this assumption isn’t met? The paper could benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of the proposed method, such as the computational cost associated with kernel operations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of DMD to RKHS, enabling the use of nonlinear basis functions for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions.
*   Mathematically sound formulation and algorithm.
*   Practical robustness improvement via POD projection.
*   Demonstration of feasibility on synthetic and real-world data.
*   Exploration of relevant applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical evaluation and lack of quantitative comparison to existing methods.
*   Insufficient discussion of parameter selection (kernel width etc.) and implementation details.
*   Limited discussion of limitations and computational cost.
*   The conditions of theorem 2’s applicability are not fully discussed.
*   Presentation could be improved with more details and analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a potentially valuable extension to DMD, addressing a known limitation of the method by incorporating kernel techniques. However, the limited empirical validation and lack of detailed discussion regarding implementation and limitations raise concerns. I recommend **weak accept** with a strong encouragement for the authors to address the aforementioned weaknesses in a revised version, particularly by providing a more thorough empirical evaluation, justifying parameter choices, and elaborating on the limitations of the approach. Specifically, including quantitative comparisons to other relevant methods would significantly strengthen the contribution.
