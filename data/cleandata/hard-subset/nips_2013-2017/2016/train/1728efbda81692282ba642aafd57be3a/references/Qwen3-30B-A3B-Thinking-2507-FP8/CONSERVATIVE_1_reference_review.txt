

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions, rather than the linear monomials used in standard DMD. The authors present theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) showing how to perform spectral analysis in RKHS, with a robustified variant using POD bases to address numerical stability issues. The paper demonstrates applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**
The paper provides a theoretically sound extension of DMD within the Koopman operator framework. The mathematical derivations are correct and well-organized, with clear presentation of the theoretical framework. The robustification approach using POD bases (Section 4) addresses a known issue in DMD implementations, which is a valuable contribution. The paper correctly situates its work within the broader literature on Koopman analysis and DMD variants, including references to Extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) and exact DMD.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper suffers from significant weaknesses in empirical validation and justification of novelty. Despite claiming to provide "a modal decomposition of the dynamics into a finite number of modes" with "richer basis functions," the authors fail to demonstrate substantial improvements over established methods. The empirical evaluation is limited to two synthetic examples (a simple nonlinear system and the Hénon map) with minimal comparison to state-of-the-art approaches.

The most concerning weakness is the lack of rigorous comparison with existing methods. The paper mentions Extended DMD but doesn't compare against it or other relevant approaches like sparsity-promoting DMD (Jovanović et al., 2014) or kernel-based methods for dynamical systems. For instance, in Section 6, the authors claim their method "precisely estimates" eigenvalues for the toy system, but provide no error metrics or comparison to standard DMD or Extended DMD. The Hénon map results (Fig. 1) show approximate eigenvalues for equilibrium points, but without quantifying the error or comparing to existing methods, it's impossible to assess whether this is an improvement.

The applications section (Section 7) lacks meaningful comparison to existing approaches. For the locomotion recognition task (Fig. 3), the authors don't show how their method compares to standard approaches like LSTM networks or traditional DMD. The change-point detection example (Fig. 4) claims superiority over "kernel change detection," but doesn't provide quantitative metrics (e.g., F1 score, false positive rate) to support this claim.

The paper also fails to address the computational complexity of the proposed method. While kernel methods are known to be computationally intensive, the authors don't discuss how their approach scales with data size or dimensionality, which is a critical consideration for real-world applications.

**Novelty and Significance:**
The core idea of using kernel methods with DMD is not sufficiently novel. The paper's key contribution appears to be a theoretical extension of DMD to RKHS, but the authors don't convincingly demonstrate why this is necessary or superior to existing approaches like Extended DMD, which already uses pre-determined basis functions. The paper claims their method "gives an approximation with a set of nonlinear basis functions due to the expressiveness of kernel functions," but doesn't provide evidence that these nonlinear basis functions yield better results than carefully chosen linear or nonlinear basis functions in Extended DMD.

The significance of the work is limited by the lack of empirical evidence showing substantial improvements over existing methods. For a paper to be significant at NIPS, it should demonstrate clear advantages in performance, scalability, or applicability compared to established baselines. This paper falls short on all these dimensions.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD, it fails to provide sufficient empirical validation against established baselines to support its claims. The lack of rigorous comparison to relevant existing methods (particularly Extended DMD) and the minimal empirical evaluation make it difficult to assess whether the proposed approach offers meaningful advantages over the state of the art.

The paper would benefit from:
1. Comprehensive comparisons with Extended DMD and other relevant DMD variants on standard benchmark datasets
2. Quantitative metrics showing performance improvements over existing methods
3. Discussion of computational complexity and scalability
4. Clearer justification of why kernel-based nonlinear basis functions are necessary or superior to other approaches

As a reviewer who prioritizes methodological rigor and thorough empirical validation, I find this paper's claims unsupported by sufficient evidence. The field of Koopman spectral analysis has seen many incremental improvements, but this work doesn't provide convincing evidence that it represents a significant advance.
