Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). It formulates a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) by constructing a Krylov subspace in feature space from a sequence of mapped states ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1), projecting the Koopman operator KH onto this subspace, and computing empirical Ritz values/vectors via a Gram–Schmidt QR factorization expressed through Gram matrices. A second, “robustified” variant projects onto kernel PCA (KPCA) directions (with truncation) before eigendecomposition, which improves conditioning and allows rank truncation. The paper provides two short theorems connecting the resulting eigenvalues/eigenvectors in feature space to Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions under assumptions, and presents three applications: short-term prediction via a pre-image step, dynamics recognition via subspace (principal angle) distances between sets of Koopman eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection via a CUSUM-style statistic derived from kernelized likelihood ratios.

This work is closely related to extended DMD (EDMD) and kernel DMD (KDMD), e.g., Williams, Kevrekidis, and Rowley (J. Nonlinear Sci., 2015), which approximate Koopman operators from data using a dictionary of observables and employ the kernel trick for implicit high-dimensional dictionaries. The present formulation emphasizes an Arnoldi/Krylov view in RKHS, derives Ritz expansions via QR in feature space, and uses KPCA truncation to stabilize the procedure, which resembles KDMD’s use of Gram matrices and truncated eigenspaces. The paper also connects to prior DMD variants (exact/optimized/sparse DMD) and to spectral learning for dynamical systems.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The feature-space Arnoldi/QR derivation and the link to Ritz values/vectors are standard in spirit and appear internally consistent. The use of Gram matrices to realize QR in RKHS is well-motivated.
- The robustified variant using KPCA and the projected operator F̂ is mathematically plausible and aligns with known KDMD constructions. Theorem 2 formalizes when the projected eigenvectors recover Koopman eigenfunctions under strong assumptions (φj ∈ Φ and κj in span(U)).
- The assumptions required for exact recovery are restrictive and not analyzed in realistic settings (finite data, noise, model misspecification). No finite-sample or asymptotic error bounds, stability analyses, or consistency results are given.
- The applications are illustrative but largely heuristic. The pre-image step references MDS-based ideas, yet details, complexity, and failure modes are not rigorously covered. The change-point detection approximation drops terms without control of false-alarm/detection rates.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and the main pipeline is clear. The use of ϕ for feature maps and φ for Koopman eigenfunctions is consistent but dense; some notational overload (e.g., Φ) could be clarified.
- Several typos and grammatical issues are present. Algorithmic steps would benefit from clearer pseudo-code and explicit complexity statements. Some derivations (e.g., relationships among Mτ, Qτ, R, and companion matrices) are sketched; additional detail or an appendix would help.
- The scope of assumptions (distinct eigenvalues; ψ = 0; eigenfunctions in the span of the selected subspace; ergodicity/noise) is not clearly stated near the theorems.

Originality
- Kernelizing DMD/Koopman approximations and using Gram matrices to operate in feature space have been introduced in EDMD/KDMD. The paper’s main algorithmic ideas (RKHS formulation, Gram-matrix-based projected operator, truncation via KPCA) substantially overlap with KDMD.
- The explicit Arnoldi/QR perspective in RKHS and the particular derivational route are a different presentation, but the resulting estimators and practice are very close to existing methods. The applications (prediction via pre-images, subspace-angle-based recognition, change detection) are interesting but are not developed into new algorithms with formal guarantees, and the recognition/change-detection ideas have analogs in kernel subspace and kernel change-detection literatures.

Significance
- Koopman-based learning is impactful and kernelization can capture nonlinear observables effectively. A clear, kernelized DMD pipeline with stabilization is practically useful.
- However, the empirical evaluation is limited: simple toy systems and a qualitative Henon example for eigenvalues; mocap embedding without quantitative recognition accuracy; a single change-detection example with minimal statistical evaluation; and no comparisons to EDMD/KDMD, exact/optimized/sparse DMD, or model-based baselines. Sensitivity to kernel type/width and truncation, robustness to noise, and scalability are not assessed. Without stronger empirical or theoretical contributions beyond KDMD, the significance is limited.

Strengths
- Clear operator-theoretic framing linking Arnoldi/Ritz concepts to Koopman analysis in RKHS.
- Practical stabilization via KPCA truncation; implementation through Gram matrices.
- Broad set of potential applications, demonstrating generality of the representation.
- Theorems provide a clean bridge between projected operators and Koopman spectra under assumptions.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to EDMD/KDMD; overlap not fully acknowledged or empirically distinguished.
- No error or consistency analysis; strong assumptions for exact recovery left unexamined.
- Empirical validation is qualitative, small-scale, and lacks comparisons and metrics.
- Pre-image and change-detection components are heuristic with insufficient evaluation.
- No complexity/scalability discussion; kernel methods can be O(τ^2–τ^3) in time/memory.
- Presentation issues: minor typos, some derivational gaps.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide direct comparisons to EDMD/KDMD and exact/optimized/sparse DMD on standard benchmarks (including noise, partial observability), with quantitative metrics (eigenvalue accuracy, prediction error, classification accuracy, detection delay/false alarms).
- Analyze computational complexity and memory, and demonstrate scalability (e.g., Nystrom/randNLA approximations).
- Add sensitivity studies for kernel choice/bandwidth and truncation thresholds.
- Provide theoretical guarantees (approximation error bounds or consistency under mixing/ergodicity) or discuss conditions under which assumptions are reasonable.
- Detail the pre-image algorithm and compare against baseline pre-image methods; quantify reconstruction/prediction error.
- For change detection, derive thresholds and error control (e.g., ARL) or compare to established kernel change-point methods under controlled regimes.

Arguments for acceptance
- Coherent, operator-theoretic RKHS formulation of DMD with a stabilizing KPCA projection.
- Potentially useful practical recipe with accessible Gram-matrix implementation.
- Applicable across multiple tasks; may interest communities working on Koopman analysis.

Arguments against acceptance
- Overlaps substantially with prior KDMD; incremental novelty.
- Weak experimental evaluation without quantitative comparisons.
- Lacks theoretical error/convergence analysis and scalability discussion.
- Some components (pre-image, change detection) are heuristic and under-evaluated.

Scope
- The topic is in scope for NeurIPS (kernel methods, spectral methods, learning dynamical systems).
