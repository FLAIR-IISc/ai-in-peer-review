Summary
This paper proposes a kernelized variant of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors formulate DMD in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), derive an algorithm based on Gram–Schmidt orthogonalization of the Krylov matrix in feature space, and obtain Ritz values/vectors by projecting the Koopman operator onto the span of these bases. Because direct Gram–Schmidt can be ill-conditioned, they propose a “robust” variant based on kernel PCA (POD) truncation and show how the projected operator can be eigendecomposed in the reduced subspace. Two theorems connect the finite-dimensional eigendecomposition to Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions under strong assumptions. They illustrate the approach on a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map, and sketch applications to short-term prediction via preimage estimation, dynamics recognition via subspace angles, and change-point detection via a CUSUM-like score in a kernel exponential family.

Relation to prior work
DMD and Koopman operator methods are well established. Extended DMD (EDMD) approximates Koopman operators with user-chosen dictionaries; kernel variants (often called KDMD or kernel EDMD) use kernels to implicitly represent large dictionaries and have been discussed in the Koopman literature. The manuscript cites EDMD [35] but does not convincingly differentiate its contribution from kernelized EDMD/KDMD approaches that project Koopman operators in feature space and use truncation for numerical stability. The robustification via (kernel) POD is also standard in DMD practice. Overall, the paper’s core ideas largely mirror EDMD with kernels, framed via Gram–Schmidt/Arnoldi in RKHS.

Strengths
- Topical: Koopman/DMD with kernels is relevant to ML and applied dynamics.
- Conceptually clear derivation: the RKHS formulation and projection steps are understandable.
- Practical step: kernel PCA truncation addresses numerical instability in a familiar way.
- Breadth: discusses several potential applications of the extracted modes/features.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Theoretical framing and algorithms closely align with existing EDMD/KDMD; differences to prior kernel Koopman methods are not clearly articulated.
- Sparse and small-scale evaluation: Only a toy system and Hénon map are considered; no quantitative comparisons against established baselines (exact DMD, EDMD, optimized DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD, subspace ID methods), no noise, robustness, or scalability studies.
- Strong assumptions without error analysis: Theorems assume eigenfunctions lie in the RKHS span and in the subspace spanned by kernel PCs; no approximation/error bounds, consistency, or sample complexity results are provided.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: No guidance or analysis for kernel choice, bandwidth, truncation level p, or regularization; no ablations.
- Preimage and application sections are underdeveloped: The preimage method is sketched but not rigorously specified or evaluated; recognition and change-point detection results are qualitative without metrics.
- Reproducibility: Many reference entries are incomplete; implementation details (complexity, memory, numerical conditioning, regularization) are missing; link durability unknown.

Quality
Technically, the RKHS projection of the Koopman operator and the use of Ritz values/vectors are standard consequences of Arnoldi/DMD generalized to a Hilbert space. Theorems largely restate known properties under restrictive conditions; there is no analysis under noise or model mismatch. Empirically, evidence is insufficient: tiny benchmarks, no baselines, no quantitative metrics. Claims such as “fully recovered” eigenfunctions when ψ=0 are mathematically trivial and do not translate into practical guarantees.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but contains grammatical errors and occasional imprecise statements (e.g., “almost of the theoretical claims…”). Some proofs are terse and rely on distinct eigenvalues; corner cases are not discussed. Important implementation choices (regularization, truncation selection, kernel selection, complexity) are not specified. References are incomplete and make it difficult to verify prior art.

Originality
As presented, the contribution appears incremental relative to EDMD and existing kernelized Koopman approaches. The robustification via kernel PCA/POD and Gram–Schmidt/Arnoldi perspective are known. The paper does not convincingly delineate what is truly new vs known.

Significance
Potentially useful if it offered a stronger theoretical or empirical advance, but in its current form the impact is limited: no rigorous analysis beyond restating known results in RKHS, and no compelling empirical demonstration against baselines on standard benchmarks or real, noisy data.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clearly position the contribution relative to EDMD and kernel EDMD/KDMD; cite and contrast with closely related kernel Koopman works and subspace ID baselines.
- Provide quantitative comparisons on standard nonlinear systems (e.g., Duffing, Van der Pol, Lorenz, cylinder wake snapshots) with noise, and report metrics (eigenvalue/mode error, prediction error, classification accuracy, change-point detection ROC).
- Analyze sensitivity to kernel choice/bandwidth, truncation p, and regularization; provide principled selection methods (e.g., cross-validation).
- Include error bounds or at least asymptotic consistency results under mild assumptions (e.g., ergodic sampling, universal kernels).
- Detail computational complexity and memory costs; consider scalable approximations (Nyström/random features) and regularized solves.
- Flesh out the preimage method (objective, optimizer, initialization) and compare to kernel ridge regression inversion or fixed-point methods.
- Improve clarity and completeness of references; fix typos and notation.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant problem and connects Koopman/DMD with kernel methods.
- Conceptual framework is reasonably clear and potentially practical.
- The POD/truncation step is pragmatic and widely used.

Cons
- Limited novelty versus existing kernel Koopman methods.
- Insufficient empirical validation and lack of baselines.
- No theoretical error/convergence analysis under realistic settings.
- Underdeveloped applications; qualitative results only.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is plausible but appears incremental and currently lacks rigorous validation and positioning relative to prior kernelized Koopman methods. With substantial theoretical and experimental strengthening, this could become a solid contribution.

Confidence: High.
