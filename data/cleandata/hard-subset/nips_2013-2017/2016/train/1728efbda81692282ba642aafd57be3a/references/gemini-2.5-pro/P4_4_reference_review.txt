**Summary of the Main Ideas**

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending the popular Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) algorithm to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The central idea is to use the kernel trick to implicitly map the system's state space into a high-dimensional feature space where the nonlinear dynamics can be better approximated by a linear operator—the Koopman operator. Standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear combination of observables. In contrast, the proposed kernel DMD (kDMD) leverages the representational power of kernels to construct approximations from a much richer, nonlinear set of basis functions without explicitly defining them.

The authors first present a direct formulation of DMD in an RKHS, showing that the Koopman modes and eigenvalues can be found via a Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition of the Krylov matrix in the feature space (Theorem 1). Recognizing the potential numerical instability of this approach, they then propose a more robust algorithm. This practical method is analogous to the common practice of projecting data onto Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) modes before applying DMD. Here, the projection is performed onto the principal components in the feature space, identified via kernel PCA. The eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projected onto this subspace yields the desired spectral information (Theorem 2). The paper demonstrates the utility of the extracted features (Koopman eigenvalues and modes) in three applications: short-term prediction via a pre-image technique, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection.

This work builds upon a significant body of literature on DMD [25, 26, 33] and Koopman operator theory [16, 20]. It is closely related to Extended DMD (EDMD) [35], which also uses a dictionary of basis functions to enrich the approximation. The key difference is that kDMD uses the kernel trick to implicitly work with a potentially infinite-dimensional basis, thereby circumventing the need for the user to explicitly select a finite set of basis functions. The work also fits into the broader context of spectral methods for learning dynamical systems in the machine learning literature [15, 31].

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development, culminating in Theorems 1 and 2, provides a solid mathematical foundation for the proposed algorithms. The connection between the robust version of the algorithm and kernel PCA is well-established and principled. The derivation of the projected Koopman operator in Equation (11) is clear and correct. The work is presented as a complete contribution, including theoretical justification, algorithms, and applications. The authors are honest about the potential numerical instability of the direct Gram-Schmidt approach, motivating the more practical POD-based version.

However, the empirical evaluation, while illustrative, could be strengthened. The prediction experiments (Figure 2) lack quantitative metrics and comparison to other standard time-series forecasting methods (e.g., Gaussian Process Dynamical Models [34] or RNNs), which makes it difficult to assess the state-of-the-art performance. Similarly, the change-point detection section relies on an approximation of the log-likelihood ratio whose impact is not empirically validated. The comparison in Figure 1 to "Equilibrium DMD" is not explained, leaving the reader to guess its definition.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. It provides a concise but sufficient background on Koopman operators and DMD, making the work accessible to readers familiar with either dynamical systems or kernel methods. The progression from the mathematically elegant but impractical algorithm in Section 3 to the robust, practical algorithm in Section 4 is a logical and effective way to present the material. The algorithms themselves are summarized in clear, step-by-step procedures.

Some sections would benefit from more detail to improve reproducibility. The description of prediction via pre-image is brief; more details on the implementation of the MDS-like approach from [18] would be helpful. The derivation of the change-point detection score could be elaborated upon, particularly the justification for simplifying the log-likelihood ratio.

**Originality:** The fusion of DMD with kernel methods is a novel and elegant contribution. While EDMD [35] allows for user-defined basis functions, the key originality here lies in using the kernel trick to avoid this explicit, and often difficult, choice. By implicitly using the RKHS basis, kDMD provides a powerful, non-parametric extension to the standard DMD framework. The application of the extracted Koopman spectral features to tasks like dynamics recognition using subspace angles and sequential change-point detection are also novel uses of this type of analysis. The paper adequately references related work in both the physics (DMD) and machine learning (spectral learning) communities. A more detailed comparison discussing the precise relationship between kDMD and an EDMD that uses a dictionary of kernel functions centered at the data points would further clarify the contribution.

**Significance:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of modeling and understanding nonlinear dynamics from data. DMD is a powerful tool that has gained significant traction in the physical sciences, but its effectiveness is often limited by the linearity assumption. By kernelizing DMD, this work significantly extends its applicability to a wider range of strongly nonlinear systems. The proposed method is not just a theoretical curiosity; the robust algorithm is practical and the source code is provided. The applications demonstrate that the extracted features are not only useful for system analysis but also for downstream machine learning tasks, making this work of high interest to both practitioners analyzing complex systems and researchers in machine learning. This approach has the potential to be widely used and built upon.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel, principled, and powerful extension of DMD for nonlinear systems using kernel methods.
*   The method is technically sound, with clear theoretical justification.
*   It addresses a significant problem at the intersection of machine learning and physical sciences.
*   The presented applications are compelling and demonstrate the practical utility of the extracted features.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, with quantitative comparisons to relevant baseline methods.
*   Some implementation details, particularly for the prediction and change-point detection applications, are sparse, potentially hindering full reproducibility.
*   The discussion on hyperparameter selection (e.g., kernel choice and parameters), a critical aspect of any kernel method, is minimal.
