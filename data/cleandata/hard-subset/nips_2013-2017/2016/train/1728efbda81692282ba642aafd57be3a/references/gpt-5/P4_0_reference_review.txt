Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a kernelized formulation of Koopman spectral analysis for discrete-time nonlinear dynamical systems x_{t+1} = f(x_t). It defines the Koopman operator acting on a feature map ϕ into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), constructs a Krylov subspace spanned by ϕ(x_0), …, ϕ(x_{τ−1}), and computes empirical Ritz values/vectors by projecting the Koopman operator onto this subspace. The main algorithm is a kernelized analogue of DMD: perform Gram–Schmidt QR on the feature-space Krylov matrix (via Gram matrices), compute the companion-like matrix F = R^{-1}Q* M_+, and eigendecompose F to obtain approximate Koopman eigenvalues/vectors. Because Gram–Schmidt can be ill-conditioned, the paper proposes a robust variant that projects onto kernel PCA directions (analogous to the POD-projected DMD) and shows how eigenpairs of a reduced operator F̂ relate to Koopman eigenfunctions under strong assumptions. The paper then sketches three applications using the recovered modes/features: short-term prediction via preimage reconstruction, dynamics recognition using kernel principal angles between subspaces of estimated eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection via a kernel exponential-family likelihood ratio. Empirical examples include a toy system, the Hénon map, an embedding of motion capture sequences, and a change-point detection example on a Lorenz map.

This work is closely related to DMD [25, 26], exact/optimized/sparsity-promoting DMD [33, 8, 13], and especially to extended DMD (EDMD) with pre-specified dictionaries [35]. The kernel-based formulation presented here is essentially kernel DMD (KDMD), which applies EDMD with an implicit, potentially infinite dictionary via the kernel trick; KDMD was developed within the EDMD framework in [35]. The use of POD/kernel PCA for numerical robustness is standard practice in DMD variants [8, 33]. The applications (preimage reconstruction, subspace angles, kernel exponential families) combine existing tools with the proposed spectra.

Quality
- Technical correctness: The high-level constructions (feature-space Krylov subspace, projection, eigendecomposition via kernel matrices) follow standard EDMD/KDMD derivations. However, there are technical gaps and inaccuracies:
  - In Theorem 1’s proof, the identity F = M_τ^{-1} K_H M_τ and M_τ F = K_H M_τ are stated; M_τ is rectangular and not invertible, and equalities should be stated for projected operators (e.g., Q Q* K_H M_τ), otherwise a residual appears. This is partially acknowledged by introducing ψ ⟂ span{ϕ(x_0), …}, but the algebra conflates exact and projected relations.
  - The statement “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle [20]” is not generally true for dissipative systems; typically, magnitudes ≤ 1 with unit-modulus corresponding to conservative/periodic components.
  - Theorem 2 relies on strong assumptions (eigenfunctions in the span of data features, and κ_j ∈ span(U)) without discussion of when they hold or how approximation errors behave. No consistency/error bounds are provided.
- Algorithmic details are mostly present, and the kernelization is implementable via Gram matrices. However, choices crucial for stability (regularization, truncation strategy, handling near-rank-deficiency) are only sketched. Computational complexity and memory costs (O(τ^3) eigensolves and O(τ^2) storage) are not discussed.
- Experiments are limited and largely qualitative. There is no rigorous quantitative comparison to DMD/EDMD/KDMD baselines, no sensitivity analysis to kernel/regularization choices, and no systematic evaluation on standard benchmarks.

Clarity
- The paper is readable, but there are numerous typos and grammatical issues that impede clarity. Notation overlaps/conflicts (e.g., ϕ used for feature map and φ for eigenfunctions; mixing of φ, φ̃, φ̄) can be confusing. The proofs conflate projected and exact relations; clarifying when equalities hold up to projection would improve rigor.
- Several references are incomplete (missing years/titles), and KDMD is not clearly identified as such; [35] should be explicitly connected to KDMD.
- The applications section is high-level; the preimage method relies on an MDS-like scheme with little detail on optimization or convergence, and the change-point detection criterion drops terms ad hoc.

Originality
- The core idea—performing EDMD in an RKHS via the kernel trick—is known as KDMD and was introduced within [35]. The use of a POD/kernel-PCA basis for robustness is standard in DMD literature [8, 33], and KDMD implementations typically include truncation. The paper’s derivation via Gram–Schmidt in RKHS and the explicit Theorems 1–2 may offer a slightly different perspective, but the underlying method and operator estimates match KDMD.
- The application sketches (preimage prediction, subspace-angle recognition, change detection via kernel exponential families) are sensible but incremental combinations of existing techniques; they are not developed to the level of new methodology with guarantees.

Significance
- Koopman-based spectral methods are important, and kernelizing DMD broadens applicability. However, given the overlap with KDMD, the contribution is incremental.
- The empirical validation is too limited to demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods or to establish practical impact. Without quantitative benchmarks or ablations, it is difficult to assess significance.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear motivation to enrich DMD with nonlinear feature spaces; kernelization is natural and potentially powerful.
- Concrete, implementable algorithms expressed via Gram matrices; code link is provided.
- The theoretical statements help connect reduced operators to Koopman spectra (albeit under strong assumptions).
- Applications illustrate the flexibility of Koopman features beyond classical physics use cases.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Limited novelty relative to EDMD/KDMD [35]; robustification via (kernel) POD is standard [8, 33].
- Technical inaccuracies/gaps (projection vs. exact equalities; incorrect statement about eigenvalues on attractors).
- Sparse, mostly qualitative experiments; no quantitative comparisons to DMD/EDMD/KDMD or other baselines.
- Clarity issues (notation, typos, incomplete references) and insufficient discussion of computational/regularization choices.
- Application sections are underdeveloped; preimage and change-detection methods lack rigorous treatment and evaluation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explicitly position the work with respect to KDMD and EDMD, cite accurately, and delineate what is genuinely new.
- Correct the technical inaccuracies and rewrite the proofs to use projected operators; discuss approximation error and provide at least finite-sample perturbation bounds or consistency statements.
- Add quantitative experiments comparing to DMD/EDMD/KDMD across synthetic and real datasets; include sensitivity analyses to kernel choice and truncation.
- Clarify algorithmic details (regularization, truncation criteria, complexity) and improve notation and writing.
- Either deepen one application (with baselines and metrics) or focus the paper on the core spectral method.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper’s main method overlaps substantially with known KDMD/EDMD, contains technical inaccuracies, and lacks sufficient empirical evidence to establish significance. With a clearer positioning relative to KDMD, corrected theory, and robust empirical evaluations, the contribution could be reconsidered.
