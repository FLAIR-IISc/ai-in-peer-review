### **Summary**

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis by extending the popular Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) framework to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The central idea is to lift the system's state into a high-dimensional feature space defined by a kernel function, where the nonlinear dynamics can be approximated by a linear Koopman operator. The authors propose two algorithms: a direct method based on a Gram-Schmidt procedure in the feature space, and a more numerically robust version that projects the operator onto the principal components derived from Kernel PCA (analogous to the use of POD in standard DMD). The eigenvalues and eigenfunctions of this projected operator serve as data-driven "features" of the dynamics. The paper demonstrates the utility of these features in several applications, including short-term prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection, using both synthetic and real-world data.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Contribution:** The paper presents a well-motivated and elegant synthesis of two powerful frameworks: Koopman operator theory for dynamical systems and kernel methods for nonlinear function approximation. Moving beyond the linear observables of standard DMD to the rich, implicitly defined basis functions of an RKHS is a natural and significant step. This "Kernel DMD" (kDMD) provides a principled, data-driven method for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions for a much broader class of nonlinear systems.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical development is clear and appears correct. Theorem 1 establishes the foundation for the method by showing how the DMD expansion holds in the feature space. Recognizing the potential numerical instability of this direct approach, the authors provide a robust alternative based on Kernel PCA (Section 4). Theorem 2 provides a solid theoretical justification for this robust algorithm, connecting the eigenvectors of the reduced-order model to the true Koopman eigenfunctions under reasonable assumptions. The derivations are presented logically and are easy to follow.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and structured. It starts with a concise review of Koopman theory and DMD, making the paper accessible. It then logically builds up the kernelized extension, addresses practical implementation issues, and situates the work within the relevant literature. The algorithms are clearly presented.

4.  **Demonstrated Utility:** A major strength is the exploration of diverse applications beyond simple spectral analysis. Showing how the learned Koopman modes can be used for prediction, embedding/recognition, and change-point detection substantially broadens the paper's impact. These applications highlight that the method is not just a theoretical curiosity but a practical tool for feature extraction from time-series data.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Relationship to Extended DMD (eDMD):** The paper briefly mentions Williams et al. [35] (eDMD), stating that its procedure is "similar to the robust-version of our algorithm." This understates the connection. The proposed robust algorithm is effectively a specific, albeit powerful, instance of eDMD where the dictionary of basis functions is chosen to be the kernel principal components. While the RKHS framing is elegant and provides a specific recipe for choosing a good data-driven basis, the novelty relative to the general eDMD framework should be clarified and discussed more thoroughly. An explicit comparison would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Practical Considerations (Hyperparameters and Scalability):** The performance of any kernel method is critically dependent on the choice of the kernel function and its hyperparameters (e.g., the width of a Gaussian kernel). The paper provides no discussion on how these were selected for the experiments, nor does it analyze their sensitivity. This is a significant omission for a practical method. Furthermore, the method requires constructing and diagonalizing matrices of size τ×τ (where τ is the number of data points), leading to a computational complexity of at least O(τ³). The authors should discuss this limitation and perhaps mention potential remedies for large datasets, such as Nyström or Random Fourier Feature approximations.

3.  **Details on Applications:** While the breadth of applications is a strength, the descriptions and justifications are sometimes brief.
    *   **Prediction:** The prediction relies on solving the pre-image problem, which is notoriously difficult and ill-posed. The paper relies on an external method [18] but does not discuss the potential challenges or limitations of this crucial step. For instance, how accurate is the pre-image reconstruction, and how does this affect multi-step prediction accuracy?
    *   **Change-Point Detection:** The derivation of the change-point score involves a key approximation (ignoring the second term in the log-likelihood ratio) with limited justification. While the empirical result is interesting, the theoretical underpinnings of this specific application could be made more rigorous or at least be presented with more caveats.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are illustrative but lack some depth. For the Hénon map, it would be more informative to plot the true eigenvalues of the fixed points alongside the estimated ones for a direct comparison. In the applications, comparisons are either absent (prediction, embedding) or limited to a single alternative method (change-point detection). A more comprehensive quantitative evaluation against relevant baselines (e.g., standard DMD, eDMD with other bases, Gaussian Process dynamical models) would provide a stronger case for the proposed method's superiority.

### **Conclusion**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a valuable and well-executed idea. The combination of Koopman theory and kernel methods is powerful, and the proposed kDMD algorithm is a solid contribution to the field of data-driven analysis of dynamical systems. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and demonstrates the method's potential across a range of interesting applications. The main weaknesses lie in the insufficient discussion of the strong connection to eDMD, the lack of detail on practical issues like hyperparameter selection and scalability, and the need for a more thorough empirical evaluation. Despite these points, the strengths of the paper—its novelty, technical quality, and demonstrated potential—are substantial. This work is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up research. I recommend acceptance.
