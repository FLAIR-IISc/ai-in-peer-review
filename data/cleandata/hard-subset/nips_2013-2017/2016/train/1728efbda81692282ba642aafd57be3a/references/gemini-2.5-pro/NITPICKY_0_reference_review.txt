### Summary

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) for the purpose of Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The central idea is to replace the linear observables used in standard DMD with a nonlinear feature map `ϕ(x)` into an RKHS. This allows for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions using a richer set of basis functions defined implicitly by a kernel function. The authors first present a direct formulation based on a Gram-Schmidt process in the feature space, which they acknowledge is prone to numerical instability. They then propose a more robust algorithm that first projects the data onto its principal components in the feature space (via kernel PCA) before performing the eigendecomposition. The utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues is demonstrated through applications in short-term prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection. The proposed method, Kernel DMD (kDMD), connects the popular DMD framework from physics with the powerful toolkit of kernel methods from machine learning.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The paper presents an elegant and intuitive synthesis of two powerful frameworks: Koopman operator theory (via DMD) and kernel methods. Applying the kernel trick to DMD to handle nonlinearity is a natural and well-motivated idea.
2.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The theoretical motivation is well-established. The paper provides a clear, albeit brief, review of the Koopman operator and DMD in Section 2, and the extension to RKHS in Section 3 is mathematically sound.
3.  **Broad Potential Applications:** The authors demonstrate that the learned spectral features can be used for more than just system analysis. The applications to prediction, embedding/recognition, and change-point detection (Section 7) showcase the potential versatility of the method as a general-purpose feature extraction tool for dynamical systems.

### Weaknesses

Despite the appealing core idea, the paper suffers from a significant number of flaws in its presentation, technical depth, and experimental evaluation.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper lacks the polish expected of a top-tier publication.
    *   **Figures:** Figure 1 (right) is difficult to interpret. The term "Equilibrium DMD" is used in the legend but is never defined in the text. The axis labels ("Image" instead of "Imaginary") and their font size are suboptimal. Figure 2 lacks axis labels and any indication of the prediction horizon, which is critical for evaluating performance on chaotic systems.
    *   **Formatting and Typos:** The bibliography is poorly formatted, with inconsistent capitalization and parsing errors (e.g., reference [12], "Quarterly of applied mathematics"). A footnote with a code link is awkwardly placed in the middle of Section 4. The phrase "almost of the theoretical claims" in Section 3 is grammatically incorrect and vague.
    *   **Vague Statements:** The paper relies on loose analogies, such as DMD being "analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion," without formal justification. The derivation of the change-point detection score in Section 7 makes a critical simplification by dropping a term, justifying it with a weak reference ("cf. [7]") and the claim that its variation is "much smaller," which requires more rigorous support.

2.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The paper's primary weakness is its insufficient differentiation from "Extended DMD" (eDMD) by Williams et al. [35]. The authors acknowledge the similarity, stating "the resulting procedure is similar to the robust-version of our algorithm." However, this understates the issue. eDMD provides a general framework for using a dictionary of basis functions to approximate the Koopman operator. The proposed method can be viewed as a specific instance of eDMD where the basis functions are implicitly defined by a kernel and data points. The paper fails to provide a detailed theoretical or empirical comparison to eDMD, making the incremental contribution unclear. This lack of engagement with the most relevant prior work significantly diminishes the paper's perceived originality.

3.  **Technical Depth and Assumptions:**
    *   The theoretical results, particularly Theorem 2, hinge on strong assumptions—that the true Koopman eigenfunctions lie within the span of the data in the feature space and that their representations `κj` lie within the subspace of the top principal components. The paper informally translates this to the data being "sufficiently rich," but this condition is never formalized or discussed in terms of practical verifiability.
    *   The handling of the pre-image problem for prediction is cursory. This is a notoriously difficult, often ill-posed problem, yet it is presented as a straightforward application with little discussion of its stability, accuracy, or limitations.
    *   The choice of kernel and its parameters is a critical methodological detail that is only addressed via heuristics ("polynomial kernel of degree three", "width was set as the median of the distances"). The sensitivity of the estimated Koopman spectrum to these choices is a crucial question that is completely ignored.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical results are superficial and serve more as illustrations than as a rigorous validation of the method.
    *   **Lack of Quantitative Metrics:** For the Hénon map, eigenvalues are described as "approximately estimated," but no quantitative error is provided. The prediction results in Figure 2 are purely qualitative.
    *   **Insufficient Baselines:** The change-point detection experiment includes one comparison method, but the prediction and dynamics recognition applications are presented without any competing baselines, making it impossible to judge their performance. A comparison with eDMD using a comparable set of basis functions would have been essential.
    *   **Limited Scope:** The experiments are conducted on low-dimensional, well-known systems. While useful for illustration, they do not provide evidence that the method scales to or provides significant advantages on the "large-scale neural recordings" or "turbulent cavity flows" mentioned in the related work.

### Decision

I recommend **rejection**.

While the paper is founded on an interesting and promising idea, the execution is flawed. The numerous issues in presentation, the insufficient engagement with closely related prior work (Extended DMD), the lack of technical depth in key derivations and assumptions, and the superficial experimental validation collectively prevent this paper from meeting the high standards of NIPS. The work feels preliminary and would require a major revision—including a thorough comparison to baselines, a more rigorous theoretical and empirical analysis, and a significant improvement in presentation—to be considered for publication.
