

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors address a limitation of traditional DMD, which approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials of observables (effectively a one-term Taylor expansion). Their approach instead uses kernel functions to create nonlinear basis functions, enabling richer approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions. The work builds upon the Koopman operator theory (Koopman, 1931; Mezić, 2005) and DMD (Schmid, 2010; Rowley et al., 2009), while relating to Williams et al.'s extended DMD (2015) which uses pre-determined basis functions. The paper distinguishes itself by operating directly in functional space using kernel methods rather than relying on monomials or pre-determined functional maps.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness (Quality):** The paper presents a mathematically rigorous theoretical foundation with well-proven theorems (Theorems 1 and 2) establishing the equivalence of the proposed method to DMD in RKHS. The derivation is logically sound, and the robustification step using POD bases appropriately addresses numerical stability concerns. The empirical examples on both synthetic (toy system, Hénon map) and real-world (locomotion data) datasets demonstrate the method's functionality.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the methods. The connection between the theoretical development and the practical implementation is well-explained, with clear algorithmic descriptions (Sections 3 and 4).

**Originality:** The core contribution of using RKHS to provide nonlinear basis functions for Koopman spectral analysis represents a meaningful extension of DMD. While Williams et al. (2015) also use pre-determined basis functions, the kernel-based approach here provides a more expressive and flexible framework for basis selection. The paper clearly articulates how this differs from previous approaches, particularly noting that traditional DMD is conceptually a one-term Taylor approximation while the kernel method can capture richer nonlinear relationships.

**Significance:** The proposed method has clear applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The empirical results on both synthetic and real-world data demonstrate the practical utility of the approach. The ability to extract meaningful features from nonlinear dynamical systems could benefit researchers in fluid dynamics, control theory, and other fields where dynamical systems analysis is important.

## Weaknesses

**Quantitative Comparison:** While the paper demonstrates the method's functionality, it lacks a thorough quantitative comparison with state-of-the-art methods (including standard DMD and extended DMD) in terms of accuracy, computational efficiency, or robustness. The empirical examples show the method works but don't clearly establish how much better it performs compared to existing approaches.

**Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't adequately address the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to standard DMD. The kernel-based approach, particularly with the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in RKHS, likely has higher computational costs than linear methods, but this trade-off isn't discussed.

**Application Justification:** While the applications section demonstrates promising results, the paper could better articulate why the kernel-based approach is particularly advantageous for these specific applications compared to other feature extraction methods. The connection between the nonlinear basis functions and the specific application benefits isn't always clear.

**Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant literature, it could more explicitly compare with recent kernel-based approaches for dynamical systems analysis (e.g., Kawahara et al., 2009; Wang et al., 2006), which might provide a stronger foundation for the novelty claim.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- The theoretical foundation is solid and well-justified.
- The approach represents a meaningful extension of DMD with clear mathematical novelty.
- The empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world data demonstrates practical utility.
- The robustification approach addresses a practical limitation that would otherwise make the method unusable.

**Con:**
- Lacks quantitative comparison with state-of-the-art methods to demonstrate clear advantages.
- Doesn't adequately address computational complexity trade-offs.
- Application justification could be strengthened by more explicitly connecting the kernel-based features to specific advantages for the demonstrated tasks.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound and novel extension of DMD using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces for Koopman spectral analysis. The theoretical development is rigorous, and the empirical examples demonstrate the method's functionality across multiple applications. While the paper has some weaknesses in the quantitative comparison and computational complexity analysis, these are relatively minor given the solid theoretical contribution and clear demonstration of the method's practical utility. The work makes a meaningful contribution to the field of dynamical systems analysis and would be of interest to researchers working on Koopman operator theory, spectral analysis, and kernel methods. The paper meets the quality, clarity, and significance criteria for NIPS, with a strong novelty claim that distinguishes it from previous work in the area.
