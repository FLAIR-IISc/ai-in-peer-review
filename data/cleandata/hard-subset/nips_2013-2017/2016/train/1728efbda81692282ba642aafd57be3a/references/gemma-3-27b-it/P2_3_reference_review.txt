## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to operate within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs), aiming to improve upon the limitations of traditional DMD which relies on linear monomials for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions. The authors propose an algorithm based on projecting the Koopman operator onto Krylov subspaces constructed using kernel methods, providing a more flexible and potentially accurate representation of nonlinear dynamics. They further address potential ill-conditioning issues with a POD-based projection step. The paper demonstrates the method on both synthetic and real data, showcasing its ability to estimate Koopman eigenvalues and apply the extracted features to tasks like prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a clear limitation of DMD:** The paper directly tackles the well-known issue of DMD’s reliance on linear approximations, which can be limiting for strongly nonlinear systems.  Using kernel methods provides a pathway to capturing more complex dynamics.
* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The extension of DMD to RKHS is presented with a good level of mathematical rigor, including a theorem establishing the connection between the proposed approach and the traditional DMD framework.
* **Practical Considerations:** Recognizing the potential for ill-conditioning, the authors incorporate a POD-based projection step, demonstrating awareness of practical implementation challenges.
* **Demonstration of Applications:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting the algorithm and showcases its potential in several applications (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) further solidifying the usefulness of the method.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Literature:** The introduction and related work sections effectively position the paper within the broader context of spectral methods, DMD, and kernel methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the examples provided are illustrative, the empirical evaluation feels somewhat limited. The Hénon map example is a good starting point, but a more comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art nonlinear system identification techniques (e.g., neural ODEs, sparse identification of nonlinear dynamics (SINDy)) would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Kernel Choice Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't fully address the sensitivity of the results to the choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., bandwidth in the Gaussian kernel). A sensitivity analysis would be helpful to understand how robust the method is to these choices.
* **Computational Complexity:** The computational cost of the kernel-based approach isn’t explicitly discussed. RKHS methods can be computationally expensive, especially for large datasets. A discussion of scalability and potential optimization strategies would be valuable.
* **Theorem 2 Assumption:** Theorem 2’s assumption that the Koopman eigenfunctions lie within the span of the POD basis is restrictive. While justified, it's important to acknowledge this limitation and discuss scenarios where it might not hold.
* **Lack of Ablation Study:** There's no clear ablation study to demonstrate the improvement gained by using the kernel-based approach versus the POD projection alone. How much does each contribute to the performance?

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the theoretical derivations appear correct. The claim of extending DMD with RKHS is well-supported.  However, the lack of a strong comparative evaluation weakens the overall assessment of the method’s quality.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are reasonably accessible.
* **Originality:** Extending DMD to the RKHS framework is a novel contribution. While kernel methods have been used in related areas, applying them in this specific way to improve DMD is a worthwhile extension.
* **Significance:** The potential impact is moderate. If the method proves robust and scalable, it could be valuable for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems where traditional DMD struggles.  However, the significance depends on demonstrating a clear advantage over existing techniques.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel extension of a popular technique (DMD) to address a known limitation.
* Solid theoretical foundation.
* Demonstrates potential for practical applications.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Limited empirical evaluation and lack of comparison with strong baselines.
* Insufficient discussion of computational complexity and kernel choice sensitivity.
* Restrictive assumption in Theorem 2.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is **borderline accept**.  It presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of dynamical systems analysis. However, the limited empirical evaluation and the lack of thorough discussion regarding computational complexity and parameter sensitivity prevent me from recommending a strong acceptance. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing these weaknesses through more extensive experiments and a more comprehensive analysis of the method’s practical limitations. Specifically, a comparison to SINDy or neural ODEs, a kernel parameter sensitivity analysis, and a discussion of scalability would greatly improve the paper.
