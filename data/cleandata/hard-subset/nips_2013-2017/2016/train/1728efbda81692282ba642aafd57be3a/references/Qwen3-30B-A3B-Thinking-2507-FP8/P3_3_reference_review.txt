

# Peer Review: "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary
This paper proposes a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors argue that standard DMD uses linear monomials as basis functions (akin to a one-term Taylor expansion), while their method employs nonlinear kernel functions to better approximate Koopman eigenfunctions. The approach involves computing orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. The paper also introduces a robust variant using POD bases to address numerical instability.

## Strengths
The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHS, with well-structured mathematical derivations (Theorems 1 and 2). The connection between DMD and Koopman spectral analysis is clearly articulated, and the robustification via POD bases addresses a practical numerical challenge that has been acknowledged in the DMD literature. The applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition demonstrate the potential utility of the method.

## Weaknesses and Critiques

### Originality and Related Work
The core contribution appears to be a minor extension of existing work rather than a significant advancement. The proposed method bears strong resemblance to Williams et al.'s Extended DMD (2015), which also uses pre-determined basis functions rather than linear monomials. The authors acknowledge this connection but fail to clearly articulate how their kernel-based approach differs in meaningful ways from Extended DMD. More critically, the paper omits key prior work by Korda and Mezić (2016) on kernel-based Koopman analysis, which directly addresses the same problem using kernel methods. This omission significantly undermines the novelty claim of the paper.

### Experimental Evaluation
The empirical evaluation is limited and lacks rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods. The toy system and Hénon map examples are expected to work well with any Koopman-based method, but the paper fails to demonstrate clear advantages over standard DMD or Extended DMD. The applications (prediction, change detection, recognition) are described but lack proper baselines for comparison. For instance, the prediction example in Figure 2 shows reasonable results but doesn't compare against standard DMD or other nonlinear prediction methods. The change-point detection example (Figure 4) compares only against a "kernel change detection method" without explaining how it differs from standard approaches.

### Technical Depth
The paper lacks a thorough discussion of computational complexity and scalability. While the robustification via POD is mentioned, the paper doesn't address how the kernel method scales with data size compared to standard DMD. The choice of kernels (polynomial for toy system, Gaussian for Hénon) is not justified, and the paper doesn't explore how kernel selection affects performance.

### Theoretical Contributions
The main theoretical contribution appears to be the extension of DMD to RKHS, but this is largely a straightforward application of existing DMD principles to a different functional space. The connection to Koopman theory is well-established, and the RKHS framework doesn't provide fundamentally new insights into the Koopman operator itself. Theorems 1 and 2 are valuable but don't represent a major theoretical breakthrough.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution to the field of Koopman spectral analysis. While the kernel-based approach is conceptually reasonable, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate its superiority over existing methods. The lack of proper comparison with Extended DMD and omission of key prior work significantly weaken the novelty claim. The empirical evaluation is insufficient to support the significance of the method.

The paper would benefit from:
1. Properly distinguishing the method from Extended DMD and citing Korda & Mezić (2016)
2. Adding rigorous comparisons with state-of-the-art methods
3. Providing a thorough analysis of computational complexity and scalability
4. Justifying kernel selection and exploring parameter sensitivity

## Recommendation

**Weak Reject**

While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it fails to make a sufficiently novel contribution to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The core idea is too similar to existing work (particularly Extended DMD), and the empirical evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages. The omission of critical prior work further undermines the paper's novelty. The authors should substantially revise the paper to better position their work within the existing literature and provide stronger empirical evidence of their method's superiority.

The paper would be more appropriate for a specialized journal or conference in fluid dynamics or dynamical systems, but it doesn't advance the state-of-the-art in a way that would be significant for the broader machine learning community represented at NIPS.
