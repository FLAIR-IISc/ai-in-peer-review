## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) by incorporating reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The motivation – to overcome the limitations of DMD's reliance on linear monomials and improve its ability to capture complex nonlinear dynamics – is sound. The theoretical development is generally well-presented and builds logically upon existing literature. However, the empirical validation, while demonstrating the method works, is limited and doesn't convincingly showcase significant advantages over existing DMD variants, especially given the increased complexity. I believe the paper is a solid contribution, but leaning towards a weak accept. Significant improvements in empirical demonstration, particularly comparison to state-of-the-art DMD variants, would strengthen it considerably.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of learning nonlinear dynamical systems using data-driven methods, specifically focusing on approximating the Koopman operator’s eigenfunctions. Existing DMD approaches are framed as approximating these eigenfunctions using linear monomials, limiting their ability to capture highly nonlinear dynamics. This work extends DMD by leveraging the expressiveness of RKHS, allowing for the use of nonlinear basis functions via kernel methods.  The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of spectral methods, POD, and DMD, acknowledging the strengths and weaknesses of these approaches. The connection to kernel PCA is well established and provides a practical implementation route. The authors also accurately place their work in relation to recent advances in DMD like exact DMD and sparsity-promoting DMD.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The mathematical development is rigorous and clearly presented.  The derivation of the algorithm, especially Theorem 1, is well-structured and builds upon existing DMD theory in a logical way. The extension to robustify the method with POD (Theorem 2) is a valuable addition, addressing a common practical issue with DMD.
* **Novelty:** The extension of DMD to RKHS is a novel contribution. Utilizing kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions provides a potentially powerful way to capture nonlinear dynamics.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the work, and the subsequent sections build upon each other logically.
* **Practical Implementation Considerations:** The inclusion of the POD-based robustification and the discussion of practical implementations (reducing calculations via Gram matrices) show a good understanding of the practical challenges of implementing this method.
* **Applications Discussed:** The application sections (prediction, embedding/recognition, change-point detection) show foresight and suggest potential use cases beyond simply extracting modes.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:**  The empirical results are somewhat underwhelming. The toy system demonstrates correct eigenvalue estimation, but it’s a relatively simple example. The Henon map example shows approximate estimation, but lacks a quantitative comparison to other methods (e.g., standard DMD, exact DMD, or sparsity-promoting DMD).
* **Lack of Comparative Analysis:** The most significant weakness is the absence of a comprehensive comparative analysis. The paper doesn’t demonstrate how the proposed method outperforms existing DMD variants on challenging datasets.  Without such a comparison, it’s difficult to assess the true benefits of the RKHS extension.
* **Kernel Choice & Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the results to the choice of kernel and kernel parameters (e.g., bandwidth for the Gaussian kernel). This is a critical aspect of kernel methods and should be addressed, ideally with a sensitivity analysis.
* **Computational Cost:** While acknowledging the reduction to Gram matrix operations, the computational cost associated with kernel methods (especially for large datasets) isn’t fully discussed.  Is the computational overhead justified by the improved accuracy or performance?
* **Applications – Superficial:** The application sections feel somewhat superficial. While the ideas are promising, the empirical demonstrations are limited.  More thorough evaluations of these applications with more realistic datasets would be beneficial.

**4. Significance & Originality:**

The originality is good - extending DMD to RKHS is a clear step forward. The significance is currently moderate.  If the method can demonstrably outperform existing DMD approaches on challenging real-world problems, its significance would increase substantially. The potential for improved accuracy in capturing nonlinear dynamics is valuable, but needs to be proven empirically.

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel extension of DMD with a solid theoretical foundation.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Addresses a relevant problem in the field of nonlinear dynamical systems.
* Inclusion of practical considerations (POD-based robustification).

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited empirical validation and lack of comparative analysis.
* Insufficient discussion of kernel choice and parameter sensitivity.
* Superficial treatment of application sections.
* Concerns about computational cost aren't fully addressed.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept.** The paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution and a potentially promising extension of DMD. However, the lack of robust empirical validation and comparative analysis weakens its impact. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors significantly strengthening the empirical section, providing a thorough comparison to state-of-the-art DMD variants on benchmark datasets, and addressing the concerns regarding kernel selection and computational cost.  If these issues are not addressed, a rejection would be more appropriate.
