**Summary**

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by leveraging the representational power of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to generalize Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a popular technique for extracting dynamically relevant modes and frequencies from data. Standard DMD approximates the Koopman operator using a linear dictionary of observables. In contrast, this work lifts the system's state into an RKHS, implicitly using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions defined by a kernel. The authors present two algorithms: a direct formulation based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space, and a more robust variant that regularizes the problem by first projecting the data onto its principal components in the RKHS (via kernel PCA), which is analogous to the common practice of using Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) to stabilize standard DMD. The paper demonstrates the utility of the extracted Koopman modes as features for several downstream tasks, including state prediction, recognition of dynamic patterns, and sequential change-point detection.

This work builds upon a well-established line of research on DMD and its connection to Koopman operator theory (e.g., Schmid, 2010; Rowley et al., 2009). The key novelty lies in the integration of kernel methods (Schölkopf et al., 1998) into the DMD framework. The proposed method is closely related to Extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), which allows for arbitrary user-defined dictionaries of observables; the proposed kernel DMD can be seen as an instance of Extended DMD where the dictionary is implicitly and systematically constructed using kernel functions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant Conceptual Framework:** The paper provides a clear and principled extension of DMD to nonlinear systems. The formulation in an RKHS is a natural way to move beyond the limitations of linear observables used in standard DMD. The parallel drawn between the robustified algorithm (using kernel PCA) and the established practice of POD-based DMD is particularly insightful.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical development appears to be correct. The derivations for both the direct and robustified algorithms are well-explained. Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 provide a solid theoretical foundation, formally connecting the computed eigenvalues and eigenvectors to the true Koopman spectra under reasonable assumptions (e.g., that the Koopman eigenfunctions lie within the span of the principal components in the feature space).

3.  **Broad Applicability:** The authors demonstrate the versatility of their method by applying it to a diverse set of tasks beyond simple spectral analysis. Showing how the extracted Koopman modes can serve as powerful features for prediction, recognition, and change-point detection significantly broadens the potential impact of the work and highlights its utility as a general-purpose feature extraction technique for dynamical systems.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It starts with a good review of Koopman theory and DMD, clearly motivating the need for a kernel-based approach. The progression from the direct (but potentially unstable) method to the robustified one is logical and demonstrates practical awareness.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Originality in a Crowded Field:** The primary concern is the significant overlap with Extended DMD (eDMD) by Williams et al. (2015). The robust version of the proposed algorithm is effectively an application of eDMD where the dictionary of observables consists of the principal components from kernel PCA. While the authors briefly acknowledge the similarity, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution. The novelty seems to lie in the specific choice of a kernel-induced dictionary, but the advantages of this specific choice over other possible dictionaries for eDMD are not empirically or theoretically established. This makes the incremental contribution over existing work unclear.

2.  **Lack of Comparative Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is illustrative but lacks the rigor expected for a top-tier conference. The experiments demonstrate that the method works on selected problems, but they do not show that it works *better* than existing methods. There are no quantitative comparisons to relevant baselines. For instance:
    *   How does kernel DMD compare to standard DMD and, crucially, to eDMD with a well-chosen polynomial or Fourier basis?
    *   In the prediction task, how does it fare against standard nonlinear time-series models like LSTMs or Gaussian Process Dynamical Models?
    *   For dynamics recognition, a comparison to methods like Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) would be necessary to contextualize the performance.
    Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed method.

3.  **Details on Applications and Practicalities:**
    *   The discussion on kernel selection and hyperparameter tuning (e.g., kernel width, number of principal components `p`) is absent. These choices are critical for any kernel method, and their impact on the quality of the learned Koopman spectra should be discussed.
    *   The descriptions of the applications in Section 7 are brief. The derivation for the change-point detection score involves several approximations that are not rigorously justified. Similarly, the prediction task relies on a non-trivial pre-image algorithm, but its details and potential failure modes are not discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents an elegant and technically sound fusion of Koopman theory and kernel methods.
*   Provides a principled, data-driven way to select a rich set of basis functions for Koopman analysis, potentially simplifying the use of eDMD-type methods.
*   Demonstrates the utility of the approach on a diverse and interesting set of applications.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Significant conceptual overlap with Extended DMD, with insufficient discussion of the incremental contribution.
*   The experimental validation is weak, lacking quantitative comparisons against strong, relevant baselines.
*   Important practical aspects, such as hyperparameter selection and sensitivity, are not addressed.

**Decision**

While the paper presents a well-formulated and interesting idea, the concerns regarding its originality relative to Extended DMD and the lack of a rigorous, comparative experimental evaluation prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The work has significant potential, but in its current form, the contribution to the state of the art is not clearly demonstrated.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Explicitly Position Relative to eDMD:** I strongly encourage the authors to add a section that directly and thoroughly compares their method to eDMD. They should clearly articulate what their contribution is. Is it the automatic, data-driven dictionary construction via kernels? If so, they should demonstrate why this is advantageous over manually selecting a dictionary of, for instance, polynomials or radial basis functions. A direct experimental comparison on a challenging problem would be very compelling.

2.  **Strengthen the Experimental Section:** To make a stronger case for the method's utility, please include quantitative comparisons to relevant baselines in all applications. For example:
    *   Add standard DMD and eDMD (with a reasonable basis) to the eigenvalue estimation experiments.
    *   For prediction, compare RMSE against an LSTM and a GP-based model.
    *   For recognition, report classification accuracy and compare it against a strong baseline like kernelized DTW.

3.  **Add a Discussion on Practical Considerations:** Please include an analysis or discussion on the sensitivity of the results to the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters. A brief study showing how the estimated eigenvalues or prediction accuracy changes with, for example, the Gaussian kernel width would greatly improve the paper's practical value.

4.  **Elaborate on Applications:** The descriptions of the applications could be fleshed out to improve clarity and reproducibility. For the change-point detection method, providing more intuition or theoretical justification for the approximations in the likelihood ratio would be helpful. For the pre-image calculation, including a brief algorithmic summary would be beneficial for the reader.

I believe that addressing these points, particularly the first two, would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a very strong candidate for a future submission.
