

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to replace the linear monomial basis functions used in traditional DMD with nonlinear kernel-based functions, enabling a richer approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions. The authors present a theoretical framework for this extension, including a modal decomposition algorithm based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in RKHS, and a robustified variant using POD bases to handle ill-conditioned problems. The paper demonstrates applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper's main strength lies in its novel conceptual approach, which significantly extends the theoretical foundation of DMD. While traditional DMD uses linear monomials as basis functions (akin to a first-order Taylor expansion), this work leverages the expressiveness of kernel functions to provide a nonlinear basis for Koopman spectral analysis. This represents a bold conceptual advancement that bridges kernel methods with Koopman operator theory, a connection that hasn't been fully explored in the literature. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from existing methods like extended DMD, which uses pre-determined basis functions rather than learning them through kernel methods.

The theoretical development is sound and well-structured, with clear theorems establishing the connection between the proposed kernel DMD and Koopman spectral analysis. The robustification step via POD bases is well-motivated and addresses a practical limitation of the pure RKHS approach. The applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition demonstrate the practical utility of the method, though the empirical validation is somewhat limited to synthetic examples and a single real-world dataset (locomotion data).

I believe the paper's primary contribution is its novel conceptual framework, which opens up new possibilities for Koopman spectral analysis. The connection between kernel methods and Koopman operator theory is particularly insightful and represents a significant conceptual advance over existing DMD variants. While the empirical validation could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons to related methods (particularly extended DMD), the novelty of the approach outweighs this limitation in my assessment.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with good mathematical exposition and helpful illustrations (Fig. 1-4). The authors properly contextualize their work within the existing literature, though they could strengthen the comparison with Williams et al.'s extended DMD by explicitly addressing how their kernel approach differs from using pre-determined basis functions.

**Strengths:**
- Novel conceptual contribution: Extending DMD to RKHS for Koopman spectral analysis represents a significant theoretical advance
- Clear theoretical development with well-justified theorems
- Good connection between kernel methods and Koopman operator theory
- Demonstrated applications to prediction, change detection, and dynamics recognition
- Well-written and organized with clear mathematical exposition

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited empirical validation (only two synthetic examples and one real-world dataset)
- Insufficient comparison with extended DMD and other kernel-based approaches
- Robustification via POD is a standard technique in DMD literature, not particularly novel
- No discussion of computational complexity or scalability to large datasets

**Recommendation:** Accept

This paper introduces a genuinely novel approach that significantly extends the theoretical foundation of DMD by connecting it with kernel methods and Koopman operator theory. While the empirical validation is somewhat limited, the novelty of the conceptual contribution is substantial enough to outweigh this limitation. The paper represents a bold step forward in Koopman spectral analysis that could inspire new research directions in dynamical systems analysis. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field and should be accepted for presentation at NIPS.
