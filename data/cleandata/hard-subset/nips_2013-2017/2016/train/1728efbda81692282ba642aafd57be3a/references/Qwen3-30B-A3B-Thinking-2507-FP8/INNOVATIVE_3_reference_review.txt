

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper presents a novel extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors propose a modal decomposition algorithm that replaces the linear monomial basis functions used in traditional DMD with nonlinear basis functions derived from kernel methods. The key innovation is the theoretical framework for performing Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS, where the Koopman operator is defined on the feature map and the analysis reduces to computing orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS followed by eigendecomposition of the projected operator. The paper also provides a robust variant using POD bases to address numerical instability.

The paper builds upon the established Koopman operator framework and DMD literature, distinguishing itself from prior work such as Williams et al.'s extended DMD (which uses pre-determined basis functions) by creating adaptive nonlinear basis functions through kernel methods. This represents a conceptual leap beyond incremental improvements to DMD variants, aligning with NIPS' emphasis on novel approaches. The theoretical development is sound, with two key theorems establishing the connection between the kernel-based DMD and Koopman spectral analysis.

The main strength of this paper is its bold conceptual contribution: extending DMD to use expressive kernel-based nonlinear basis functions rather than the limited linear monomials used in standard DMD. This is a significant step beyond previous work, as it fundamentally changes how the basis functions are constructed for Koopman spectral analysis. The paper clearly articulates how this approach differs from traditional DMD (which is "analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point") and extended DMD (which uses pre-determined basis functions).

The paper demonstrates the approach on synthetic data (toy nonlinear system, Hénon map) and real-world applications including prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using locomotion data. The empirical results (Fig. 1-4) show that the method can successfully estimate Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions, and the applications are well-motivated and relevant.

While the paper's theoretical foundation is strong, there are some weaknesses in the empirical evaluation. The comparison to existing methods is limited to qualitative observations rather than quantitative performance metrics. The paper claims that their kernel-based approach "gives an approximation with a set of nonlinear basis functions due to the expressiveness of kernel functions," but doesn't clearly demonstrate how this translates to superior performance in practical scenarios compared to existing methods like extended DMD with carefully chosen basis functions. The computational complexity of the proposed method isn't discussed, which is important for practical adoption.

The paper also doesn't fully articulate the specific advantages of the kernel approach over other nonlinear methods for Koopman spectral analysis. While the theoretical framework is elegant, the paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of why kernel methods are particularly well-suited for this problem compared to other nonlinear approaches.

Despite these limitations, the core contribution is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant acceptance. The paper introduces a genuinely new direction in Koopman spectral analysis that moves beyond incremental improvements to DMD variants. The theoretical framework is sound, the applications are relevant, and the approach has the potential to influence future work in dynamical systems analysis.

As a reviewer prioritizing novelty and bold concepts over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. The kernel-based extension of DMD represents a significant conceptual advance that could open new research directions. While the empirical validation could be strengthened, the novelty of the core idea outweighs these concerns.

The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The related work section adequately references relevant literature, including recent DMD variants.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound extension of DMD using reproducing kernels for Koopman spectral analysis, which represents a significant conceptual advance beyond existing work. The core idea is bold and promising, with clear potential for future research and applications. While the empirical validation could be more comprehensive, the novelty and theoretical contribution are strong enough to recommend acceptance.
