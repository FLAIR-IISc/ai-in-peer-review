### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The central idea is to leverage the kernel trick to implicitly map the system's state data into a high-dimensional feature space, where the action of the Koopman operator can be approximated linearly. The authors argue that this approach allows for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions with a richer set of nonlinear basis functions compared to standard DMD, which typically uses linear observables. The paper presents a direct formulation based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the RKHS and a more practical, robust version that uses a projection onto kernel principal components (analogous to POD-based robustification in standard DMD). The utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features is demonstrated through applications in state prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Clarity:** The paper provides a clear and logical extension of the well-known DMD framework to the RKHS setting. The mathematical derivations are straightforward and build upon established principles from both the DMD literature (e.g., the connection to Krylov subspaces and the use of POD for robustification) and kernel methods (e.g., the kernel trick, kernel PCA). This makes the proposed method, "kernel DMD" (kDMD), easy to understand.

2.  **Conceptual Elegance:** The formulation is elegant. It offers a principled way to incorporate a rich class of nonlinear observables into the DMD framework without requiring the user to explicitly define a dictionary of basis functions, as is needed in related methods like Extended DMD. The choice of kernel implicitly defines the function space.

3.  **Potentially Useful Applications:** The paper correctly identifies that the spectral decomposition provided by kDMD can be viewed as a feature extraction technique. The proposed applications—prediction, embedding/recognition, and change-point detection—are relevant and demonstrate the potential breadth of the method's utility.

### Weaknesses

The paper suffers from significant weaknesses, primarily concerning its originality and the rigor of its empirical evaluation.

1.  **Limited Originality:** The core idea of applying the kernel trick to a linear algorithm is a standard recipe in machine learning. The proposed method is conceptually very similar to Extended DMD (eDMD) [35], which generalizes DMD to arbitrary dictionaries of observable functions. Kernel DMD can be seen as a specific instance of eDMD where the dictionary is implicitly defined by the RKHS feature map. While the paper acknowledges this similarity, it does not adequately articulate a significant conceptual advance or demonstrate a clear advantage over a well-chosen explicit basis in eDMD. The novelty appears to be more in the implementation rather than a fundamental new approach to Koopman analysis.

2.  **Insufficient and Inadequate Empirical Validation:** This is the most critical flaw of the paper. The experiments serve as simple "proof-of-concept" illustrations rather than a rigorous scientific validation of the proposed method's capabilities.
    *   **Absence of Baselines:** The paper makes claims about the benefits of its approach but fails to substantiate them through comparison with any relevant baseline methods. For prediction on the Hénon map, how does kDMD compare to standard DMD, eDMD with polynomial bases, or established nonlinear time-series models like Gaussian Process Dynamical Models [34]? For dynamics recognition, how does a classifier using kDMD features compare to one using features from standard time-series analysis or other manifold learning techniques? The lack of comparative analysis makes it impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers any real-world advantages.
    *   **Lack of Quantitative Metrics:** The evaluation is almost entirely qualitative, relying on visual inspection of plots. For prediction, no quantitative error metrics (e.g., Mean Squared Error) are reported. For recognition, the MDS plot is illustrative, but there is no report of classification accuracy or comparison of the embedding quality. For change-point detection, the plot of the score is presented without metrics like detection delay or false alarm rate. High-quality research requires quantitative evidence to support its claims.
    *   **Superficial Treatment of Applications:** The application sections are underdeveloped. The prediction method relies on solving the kernel pre-image problem, which is notoriously ill-posed and challenging. The paper simply references a method without analyzing its performance, stability, or computational cost, all of which are critical for a practical prediction algorithm. Similarly, the change-point detection algorithm is based on a series of approximations whose validity is not discussed or tested.

3.  **Lack of Methodological Detail and Analysis:** Key details for reproducibility and practical use are missing. The selection of kernels and their hyperparameters (e.g., polynomial degree, Gaussian width) is presented without justification or a sensitivity analysis. The robustified method requires truncating the kernel PCA spectrum at a rank `p`, but no guidance is offered on how to select this critical hyperparameter. A rigorous paper would investigate the impact of these choices on the final results.

### Conclusion and Recommendation

While the paper presents a methodologically sound kernelization of DMD, its contribution is ultimately incremental and, more importantly, is not supported by sufficient evidence. The lack of rigorous empirical validation against established baselines is a fatal flaw. The experiments are preliminary and fail to demonstrate that the proposed method provides any significant improvement over existing techniques. The work, in its current form, reads more like a workshop paper or a technical report introducing an idea, rather than a complete research paper suitable for NeurIPS. To be acceptable, the paper would need a complete overhaul of its experimental section, including comprehensive quantitative comparisons against a suite of relevant and strong baselines for each of the claimed applications. Without this evidence, the significance of the contribution remains unproven. I therefore recommend rejecting this paper.
