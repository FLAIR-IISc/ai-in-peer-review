This paper introduces a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) method extended with reproducing kernels (RKHS-DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using nonlinear basis functions afforded by kernel methods, rather than linear monomials as in standard DMD, potentially improving accuracy when linear approximations are insufficient. The authors provide a detailed mathematical formulation, a robust implementation incorporating POD for ill-conditioning, and empirical demonstrations on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showcasing applications like state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon a strong foundation in DMD and Koopman operator theory. The extension to RKHS is a natural and potentially valuable direction, addressing a known limitation of traditional DMD. The mathematical derivations are thorough and largely clear, and the inclusion of a POD-based regularization step is a practical consideration for real-world applications. The empirical results, while somewhat limited in scope, demonstrate the method's feasibility and potential benefits. The connections to related work, including spectral methods, subspace identification, and kernel methods, are well-established.  The availability of code is a significant positive.

However, several weaknesses detract from the overall impact of the work. Firstly, the novelty of the approach is somewhat incremental. While the RKHS extension is valid, the concept of using nonlinear basis functions in DMD is not entirely new (e.g., extended DMD, kernel DMD variants) and the authors don’t fully differentiate their approach from these existing methods. A more comprehensive comparison with related work, detailing the specific advantages and disadvantages of RKHS-DMD versus alternatives, is needed. 

Secondly, the empirical evaluation lacks depth. The toy system example is a good starting point, but the Hénon map results are only qualitatively assessed. A quantitative comparison against other DMD variants (e.g., optimized DMD, sparse DMD) on the Hénon map, using metrics like prediction error or spectral accuracy, would significantly strengthen the claims.  The application sections (prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition) are presented with limited detail and lack rigorous evaluation. For instance, the change-point detection example only shows a single time series, and the performance isn't quantified (e.g., detection accuracy, false alarm rate). The locomotion data example uses MDS, but the justification for this choice and the interpretation of the resulting embedding aren't fully explained. The limited size of the datasets used also makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of the method.

Thirdly, the discussion on computational complexity is absent. Kernel methods can be computationally expensive, especially for large datasets. A discussion of the computational cost of RKHS-DMD, and how it scales with data size, would be crucial for assessing its practical applicability.

Finally, while the theorem on the robustification with POD bases is presented, its practical implications and parameter selection guidelines (e.g., how to choose the number of POD modes) are not sufficiently discussed.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Sound theoretical foundation and clear mathematical formulation.
*   Addresses a known limitation of standard DMD by incorporating nonlinear basis functions.
*   Practical implementation with a POD-based regularization step.
*   Availability of code promotes reproducibility and further research.
*   Well-connected to existing literature on DMD, Koopman operator theory, and kernel methods.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Incremental novelty; limited differentiation from existing kernel-based DMD variants.
*   Insufficiently rigorous empirical evaluation with limited datasets.
*   Lack of quantitative comparison against other DMD methods.
*   Missing discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
*   Insufficient details regarding POD parameter selection and its impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the work is technically sound and presents a potentially valuable extension to DMD, the incremental novelty and the lack of a thorough empirical evaluation limit its impact. The paper needs significant revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, a more comprehensive comparison with related work, a more rigorous and quantitative evaluation on diverse datasets, a discussion of computational complexity, and clearer guidelines for parameter selection are essential for potential acceptance in a future iteration. The authors should also consider clarifying the specific contribution of their method compared to existing kernel-based DMD techniques.
