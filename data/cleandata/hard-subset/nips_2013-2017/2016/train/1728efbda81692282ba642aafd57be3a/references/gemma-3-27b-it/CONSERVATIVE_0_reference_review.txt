This paper proposes a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to perform DMD in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) to leverage the expressiveness of kernel functions for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions, moving beyond the linear monomials used in traditional DMD. The authors present an algorithm and demonstrate its application on a toy system and the Hénon map, including applications to prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

While the mathematical formulation appears sound, and the extension of DMD to RKHS is a logical step, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. Firstly, the empirical validation is limited. The “toy system” is overly simplistic, and the Hénon map, while chaotic, is a well-studied system. The results on the Hénon map, while showing *some* correspondence with known eigenvalues, are not presented with sufficient rigor. A more comprehensive comparison with established techniques—specifically, standard DMD, extended DMD, or kernel PCA directly applied to the state space—is crucial. The authors mention related work on DMD variants, but lack a direct quantitative comparison. Without this baseline, it is difficult to assess the benefits of the proposed kernelized approach.

Secondly, the practical implementation details are glossed over. The choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., width for the Gaussian kernel, degree for polynomial kernels) significantly impacts the results. The justification for the chosen parameters feels arbitrary and a sensitivity analysis is absent. The POD-based robustification step, while acknowledged as potentially important, isn’t clearly motivated in terms of the kernelized DMD framework – it feels added as an afterthought to address numerical instability rather than being an integral part of the method. The connection to existing kernel-based methods, such as kernel PCA, isn't fully explored; the benefits of performing the decomposition within the Koopman framework versus applying kernel PCA directly to the data are not clearly articulated. 

Thirdly, the applications section feels underdeveloped. The description of prediction via pre-image is conceptually interesting but lacks detail on the practical challenges of recovering pre-images in high-dimensional feature spaces. The embedding for dynamics recognition relies on kernel principal angles, which are standard, and the novelty lies primarily in the features themselves, not the recognition methodology. The change-point detection example uses a simplified CUSUM test; more sophisticated methods tailored to nonlinear dynamics would be more compelling.

The paper’s strengths lie in its theoretically sound extension of DMD. The proposed algorithm is a natural generalization and offers potential for capturing more complex dynamics than standard DMD. The connection to Koopman theory provides a potentially powerful framework for analysis and control. However, these strengths are overshadowed by the limited empirical evaluation, insufficient justification for implementation choices, and underdeveloped application demonstrations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Mathematically sound extension of DMD.
*   Potential for improved accuracy in approximating Koopman eigenfunctions.
*   Connections to established Koopman operator theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical validation with inadequate baselines.
*   Lack of sensitivity analysis for kernel parameters.
*   Underdeveloped applications section.
*   Insufficient justification for the POD-based robustification.
*   Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. The paper presents an interesting theoretical idea, but it falls short of demonstrating its practical value due to a lack of rigorous validation and a limited scope of empirical evaluation. Substantially more experimental work, including a thorough comparison with existing methods and a detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity, is required before this work can be considered a significant contribution to the field. The authors should also provide more detail on the practical aspects of implementation and explore more challenging real-world datasets.
