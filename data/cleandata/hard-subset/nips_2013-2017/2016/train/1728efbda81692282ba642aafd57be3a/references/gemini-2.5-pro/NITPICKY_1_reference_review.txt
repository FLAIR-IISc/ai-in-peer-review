**Summary:**

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), which they term kernel Dynamic Mode Decomposition (kDMD). The central idea is to lift the state-space dynamics into an infinite-dimensional feature space via a kernel map, where the nonlinear Koopman operator becomes a linear operator. The paper presents two algorithms to approximate the eigenvalues (Koopman eigenvalues) and corresponding functions (Koopman eigenfunctions) of this operator. The first is a direct approach based on a Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition of the Krylov matrix in the feature space. The second, more practical algorithm projects the operator onto a subspace spanned by the principal components from kernel PCA, providing a robust way to handle rank-deficiency and noise. The utility of the extracted Koopman spectra is demonstrated through applications in state prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Elegant Formulation:** The paper presents a theoretically appealing extension of the popular DMD framework to RKHS. Marrying the spectral analysis capabilities of DMD with the representational power of kernel methods is a natural and powerful idea for handling complex nonlinear dynamics.
2.  **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide theoretical grounding for their methods. Theorem 1 establishes the connection between the proposed algorithm and the Koopman expansion for the direct QR-based approach. Theorem 2 provides a similar justification for the more practical, robust algorithm based on kernel PCA, linking the computed eigenvectors to the true Koopman eigenfunctions under reasonable assumptions.
3.  **Demonstrated Versatility:** The paper showcases the potential of the method across several interesting applications, including prediction via pre-imaging, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and change-point detection. This highlights that the extracted Koopman modes can serve as a useful feature set for various downstream machine learning tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising direction, the paper suffers from a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, experimental validation, and positioning relative to prior work.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's clarity is hampered by numerous minor but distracting issues.
    *   **Formatting:** The reference style is inconsistent and does not conform to standard NIPS formatting (e.g., using numerical citations `[11, 34]` instead of author-year). Many reference entries are incomplete (e.g., missing page numbers or conference details). Figure 1 contains two plots, but the caption is singular ("Figure 1: Estimated eigenvalues..."). The axis labels ("Real", "Image") in the right plot of Fig. 1 are misaligned with the axes.
    *   **Inconsistencies:** There is a critical inconsistency in the change-point detection experiment. The text describes a CUSUM-like method, but the legend in Figure 4 refers to "kDMD-SVM". The role of an SVM is never mentioned or explained in Section 7, making the result difficult to interpret. Similarly, the term "Equilibrium DMD" is used in Figure 1 without any definition.
    *   **Vagueness:** The proofs of Theorems 1 and 2 are presented as very brief sketches. Key implementation details, such as the use of the kernel trick to compute `F̂` in Eq. (11), are stated without clear derivation. The "Remark" at the end of Section 4 mentions that the method can be applied to a collection of pairs of data points, but the necessary re-definition of the data matrices `Mτ` and `M+` is described vaguely as requiring "appropriate definitions".

2.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The paper's primary weakness is its failure to adequately differentiate itself from Extended DMD (eDMD) by Williams et al. [35]. eDMD provides a general framework for Koopman analysis using a dictionary of arbitrary observable functions. The proposed kDMD can be viewed as a specific instantiation of eDMD where the dictionary functions are the kernel functions centered at the training data points (i.e., `ψ_i(x) = k(x_i, x)`). The paper acknowledges a similarity ("the resulting procedure is similar to the robust-version of our algorithm") but does not engage in a thorough discussion of this connection. This understatement significantly obscures the paper's novel contribution. Is the contribution the specific choice of a kernel-based dictionary, or is there a more fundamental difference? This must be clarified.

3.  **Methodological Rigor:**
    *   **Hyperparameter Selection:** As with any kernel method, the choice of the kernel function and its hyperparameters (e.g., Gaussian width, polynomial degree) is crucial. The paper simply states the parameters used in the experiments with no discussion of how they were selected, nor any analysis of the method's sensitivity to these choices. This is a significant omission.
    *   **Approximations:** The derivation of the change-point detection score in Section 7 relies on a hand-wavy argument that one term in the log-likelihood ratio can be ignored because its variation "is much smaller than the first one." This claim requires theoretical or empirical justification, which is not provided.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is weak and lacks the rigor expected for a top-tier conference.
    *   **Lack of Baselines:** The experiments are largely presented in a vacuum. The prediction results in Figure 2 are shown without comparison to any alternative methods (e.g., standard DMD, Gaussian Process models, or other nonlinear time-series models). The change-point detection experiment only compares to one other method.
    *   **Qualitative Results:** The dynamics recognition application (Figure 3) is presented only as an MDS visualization. While illustrative, it provides no quantitative measure of performance. A simple classification experiment using the learned embeddings would have made the evaluation much stronger.
    *   **Vague Interpretation:** The analysis of the Hénon map results is weak, stating that "the part of the eigenvalues of the equilibrium points seem to be approximately estimated". The plot shows many estimated eigenvalues, and it is unclear which correspond to the true ones and how accurate the approximation is.

**Decision:**

I recommend **rejection**.

While the core idea of combining DMD with kernel methods is sound and interesting, the paper is undermined by a lack of clarity, insufficient experimental rigor, and, most importantly, an inadequate discussion of its novelty with respect to existing work like Extended DMD. The numerous minor flaws in formatting, notation, and explanation accumulate to significantly detract from the quality of the manuscript. A substantial revision is needed to address these issues before the work can be considered a solid contribution to the field.
