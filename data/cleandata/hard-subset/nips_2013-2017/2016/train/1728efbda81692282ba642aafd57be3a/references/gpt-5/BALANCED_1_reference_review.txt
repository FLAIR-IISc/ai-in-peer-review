Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), yielding a “kernel DMD”-style algorithm. Section 3 derives a DMD-like procedure directly in the feature space: build the Krylov matrix from feature vectors ϕ(x0),…,ϕ(xτ−1); compute an orthogonal basis via Gram–Schmidt; project the Koopman operator onto this subspace; and extract eigenvalues/eigenvectors (Ritz values/vectors). Section 4 introduces a numerically robust variant by projecting onto kernel PCA directions, leading to an algorithm that depends only on Gram matrices and admits truncation of small singular values. The paper provides two toy demonstrations (a simple 2D nonlinear system and the Hénon map), and sketches three applications enabled by the extracted Koopman features: short-term prediction via pre-image recovery, dynamics recognition via subspace distances (kernel principal angles), and sequential change-point detection via a kernel exponential family/CUSUM heuristic.

The core idea—approximating the Koopman operator in a (potentially infinite-dimensional) feature space via kernel methods—is closely related to extended DMD (EDMD) and its kernelized instantiations. Williams, Kevrekidis, and Rowley (J. Nonlinear Science, 2015) develop EDMD and show how to implement it efficiently with kernels; their “kernel EDMD/KDMD” formulation is mathematically equivalent to projecting onto a data-driven feature subspace via Gram matrices and computing finite-dimensional operator eigenpairs—very similar to Sec. 4 here. There is also related work on kernel transfer operators and spectral analysis in RKHS (e.g., Klus et al.), subspace DMD/exact DMD/optimized DMD variants for robustness, and sparsity-promoting DMD. While the manuscript cites EDMD [35] and a variety of DMD variants, the contribution beyond kernel EDMD/KDMD is not clearly delineated.

Strengths
- Sound motivation for moving beyond linear observables to richer (nonlinear) RKHS features for Koopman analysis.
- Algorithmic exposition connects Krylov subspaces, Ritz approximations, and kernelization; the robust variant based on kernel PCA is practical and aligns with best practices (truncation).
- Theoretical statements (Theorems 1–2) help relate the finite-dimensional projected eigenpairs to Koopman eigenfunctions under span assumptions.
- Applications section illustrates potential utility beyond modal interpretation (prediction, recognition, change detection), and code availability is a plus.

Weaknesses
- Originality: The robust kernel method in Sec. 4 appears to recover kernel EDMD/KDMD as in Williams et al. (2015), but this is not clearly acknowledged nor differentiated. The Gram–Schmidt formulation in Sec. 3 provides an alternative derivation, but it is numerically inferior and largely pedagogical; its novelty and practical benefit are unclear.
- Empirical evaluation is minimal and largely qualitative. There are no quantitative benchmarks (e.g., multi-step prediction error, spectral accuracy against ground truth, classification accuracy, detection delay/false alarm rates), no comparisons to DMD/EDMD/KDMD baselines, and no study of sensitivity to kernel choice/width, rank truncation, or noise.
- Some theoretical and conceptual issues:
  - The statement “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is incorrect in general; dissipative systems typically yield eigenvalues inside the unit disk (decay). Unit-circle eigenvalues arise for measure-preserving/quasi-periodic dynamics.
  - Theorem assumptions (distinct Ritz values; κj within span(U)) are strong and their practical implications are not discussed; convergence/consistency guarantees are not addressed.
  - The Gram–Schmidt/Vandermonde construction is ill-conditioned; although acknowledged, the paper does not justify retaining it as a main algorithm.
- Clarity issues: notation overload (φ/ϕ/φ̄), dimensions and adjoints in RKHS not always explicit, some typos and minor ambiguities (e.g., centering, use of H and Ḡ−1). Figures lack quantitative details (axes, error bars, metrics).
- The change-point detection derivation mixes a kernel exponential family with Koopman-based predictions and then drops terms heuristically; this requires justification and quantitative validation.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: The core algorithm in Sec. 4 is technically sound and aligns with established kernel EDMD/KDMD practice. However, proofs are sketch-level, some claims are inaccurate, and experiments do not substantiate performance or robustness claims.
- Clarity: Generally understandable but needs tighter notation, clearer assumptions, better figure descriptions, and a more structured algorithms section (pseudocode, complexity).
- Originality: Limited. The robust method closely matches existing KDMD; the paper does not make a compelling case for novelty beyond an alternative derivation and a collection of application sketches.
- Significance: Potentially high if framed as a unifying perspective with strong experiments; as written, the contribution is incremental with insufficient evidence of practical advantage.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clearly position the work relative to EDMD and kernel EDMD/KDMD (Williams et al., 2015) and related kernel transfer operator literature. Either demonstrate a substantive theoretical difference (e.g., new guarantees, consistency under RKHS assumptions) or a practical advantage (e.g., better numerical stability, accuracy, or scalability).
- Provide quantitative experiments:
  - Spectral accuracy on systems with known Koopman spectra; perturbation/noise robustness; sensitivity to kernel width and rank truncation.
  - Multi-step prediction errors versus DMD/EDMD/KDMD, GPDM, and other baselines.
  - Dynamics recognition: report accuracy, confusion matrices, and ablations on the feature construction.
  - Change-point detection: detection delay, false alarm rate, ROC curves versus strong baselines (e.g., kernel change detection, subspace CUSUM).
- Correct the eigenvalue-on-unit-circle claim; discuss the spectral structure across attractors and mixing dynamics.
- Replace the Gram–Schmidt/Vandermonde path with a numerically stable presentation (SVD-based low-rank projection), and discuss regularization (e.g., Tikhonov) for noisy data.
- Add algorithm pseudocode, computational complexity, and memory costs; discuss scalability and mini-batch/streaming variants.
- Clarify the pre-image method, its computational burden, and robustness; compare against standard pre-image techniques and/or avoid pre-images by operating directly in RKHS for prediction of observables.

Pros vs. cons for acceptance
- Pros: Solid motivation; coherent kernel formulation; helpful connections to Koopman analysis; interesting applications; code provided.
- Cons: Overlaps substantially with established kernel EDMD/KDMD; limited novelty; insufficient experimental rigor; some inaccuracies and clarity issues.

Recommendation
Reject in its current form. With a clearer positioning relative to kernel EDMD, corrected claims, and a thorough, quantitative empirical evaluation demonstrating advantages over existing methods, this work could become a strong contribution.
