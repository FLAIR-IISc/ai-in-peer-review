## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to operate within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), aiming to improve the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions beyond the limitations of linear monomials used in traditional DMD.  The authors present an algorithm based on Krylov subspace calculations and eigendecomposition, and address potential ill-conditioning issues by incorporating a projection onto POD directions. They demonstrate the method on toy examples and locomotion data, showcasing applications in prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The extension of DMD to RKHS is well-motivated and mathematically grounded. The authors clearly articulate the connection to the Koopman operator and provide a theorem establishing the validity of their approach.
* **Addresses a Limitation of DMD:** The paper directly tackles a known weakness of standard DMD—its reliance on linear monomials which restrict its ability to capture highly nonlinear dynamics. Using kernel methods offers a more expressive function space for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge and address the potential for ill-conditioning in the algorithm, proposing a robustification step using POD, a common practice in DMD.
* **Demonstrated Applications:** The paper presents several potential applications of the extracted features, moving beyond purely theoretical analysis. The empirical results, while relatively simple, demonstrate the viability of the approach.
* **Clear Connections to Related Work:** The introduction and related work section provide a good overview of relevant techniques like POD, DMD, and spectral methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the examples are illustrative, they are relatively simple and lack a rigorous comparison to other state-of-the-art nonlinear system identification methods.  The choice of kernel parameters (degree 3 polynomial, Gaussian width 1) appears somewhat arbitrary without justification.
* **Computational Complexity:**  The paper doesn't explicitly discuss the computational cost associated with working in RKHS, which can be significant, particularly for large datasets. The complexity of the Gram matrix calculations is a potential bottleneck.
* **Lack of Scalability Discussion:** The paper does not discuss how well this approach scales to high-dimensional data or complex systems.  This is a crucial aspect for practical applicability.
* **Incremental Improvement:** While the idea of using kernel methods for DMD is natural and the technical contribution is sound, it feels like an incremental improvement rather than a breakthrough. The novelty compared to existing extended DMD variants is not entirely clear. The relationship to extended DMD, mentioned briefly, warrants deeper exploration.
* **Overly Dense Writing:** While the authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the material, the writing style is quite dense and can be challenging to follow at times. More concise explanations and better visual aids could improve clarity.



**Review Criteria Assessment:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the proposed algorithm is mathematically well-defined. The claims are generally supported, but more thorough experimental validation is needed. (Score: 7/10)
* **Clarity:** The paper is reasonably clear, but the density of the mathematical notation and the writing style make it difficult to read quickly.  Improved organization and more intuitive explanations would enhance clarity. (Score: 6/10)
* **Originality:** The application of RKHS to DMD is a reasonable extension, but the degree of novelty is moderate. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how it differs from related work, especially extended DMD variants. (Score: 6/10)
* **Significance:** The potential applications are interesting, but the limited empirical evaluation makes it difficult to assess the significance of the work.  Demonstrating performance on more challenging datasets would significantly strengthen the paper. (Score: 6/10)

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD with potential to improve performance on nonlinear systems.
* The authors address a known limitation of traditional DMD.
* The work is well-connected to related research in dynamical systems and machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation is limited and lacks rigorous comparisons to other methods.
* The computational complexity and scalability are not adequately discussed.
* The novelty of the approach is moderate, and the connection to extended DMD needs to be clarified.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak acceptance** with major revisions. 

The paper presents a solid technical contribution, but requires significant improvements in the empirical evaluation and discussion of practical considerations before it can be considered a strong NIPS paper. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Expand the empirical evaluation:**  Include more complex datasets and compare the performance of their method to other state-of-the-art nonlinear system identification techniques.
2.  **Discuss computational complexity:** Analyze the computational cost of the algorithm and consider strategies for improving scalability.
3.  **Clarify novelty:**  Provide a more detailed comparison to extended DMD and other related approaches, highlighting the unique contributions of their work.
4. **Improve Clarity**: Simplify the writing style, and use more visual aids to explain the key concepts.
