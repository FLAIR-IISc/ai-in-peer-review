Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a kernelized variant of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to approximate Koopman spectral objects for nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to (i) work in an RKHS via a feature map ϕ(x), (ii) construct a Krylov subspace from {ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)}, (iii) compute a projected Koopman operator and its eigen-decomposition to obtain empirical Ritz values/vectors (Theorem 1), and (iv) “robustify” the procedure by projecting onto kernel-PCA directions (akin to truncated SVD in exact/optimized DMD), yielding a practical algorithm (Theorem 2 under strong assumptions). The paper then sketches three applications: short-term prediction via preimage reconstruction, dynamics recognition via subspace distances of estimated Koopman eigenfunctions, and change-point detection via a kernelized CUSUM-style score.

This sits squarely in the stream of work on Koopman spectral analysis and DMD variants. The connection to Arnoldi and Krylov subspaces is standard in DMD; the extension to richer basis functions is essentially the premise of Extended DMD (EDMD) and kernel DMD: Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley (J. Nonlinear Science, 2015) introduced EDMD and discussed kernelization; Tu et al. (JCD 2014) and exact/optimized DMD address numerical robustness; subspace/Hankel DMD and related spectral methods address noise and consistency. The paper cites EDMD and optimized/exact DMD, but does not position its contribution clearly against these, nor provide empirical comparisons.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean derivation: the RKHS formulation, Gram–Schmidt/QR perspective (Theorem 1), and the kernel-PCA truncation (Theorem 2) are coherent and align with how DMD variants are stabilized.
- The algorithmic steps are expressed in terms of Gram matrices, making the approach implementable without explicit feature maps.
- The applications section hints at broader utility (prediction, recognition, change detection), which could be interesting if substantiated.
- The connection to kernel principal angles for comparing subspaces is a reasonable tool for dynamics recognition.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty relative to EDMD/kernel DMD is unclear. EDMD already uses flexible (including kernel) dictionaries and projects the Koopman operator onto the span of lifted observables; the proposed method appears to be a rephrasing via Gram–Schmidt/QR and kernel-PCA truncation. The paper does not articulate a technical distinction or advantage, nor compare empirically.
- Theoretical contributions are limited. Theorem 1 is a straightforward restatement of Arnoldi/DMD in feature space. Theorem 2 hinges on very strong assumptions (Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the RKHS feature span and, moreover, in the subspace spanned by the retained kernel principal components). Under these assumptions, the conclusion is unsurprising. No perturbation/error bounds, consistency, or sample complexity analyses are provided.
- Empirical evaluation is sparse and largely qualitative. The toy system and Henon map plots show eigenvalues but lack quantitative error metrics, sensitivity to noise, or comparisons to baselines (EDMD, exact/optimized DMD, subspace/Hankel DMD). The applications (prediction via preimage, recognition via principal angles, change detection) are demonstrated with minimal detail and no rigorous metrics (e.g., prediction RMSE vs horizon, classification accuracy/confusion matrix, detection delay/false alarm rates).
- The preimage-based prediction is especially fragile: the preimage problem is ill-posed and the paper uses an MDS-style heuristic without quantifying accuracy, robustness, or comparing to established predictors (e.g., EDMD time propagation + regression preimage, GPDM, SSMs).
- Claims are sometimes oversimplified. For example, “Koopman eigenvalues always lie on the unit circle for systems evolving on an attractor” requires conditions (e.g., measure-preserving dynamics on the attractor); dissipative systems can have non-unit spectral components depending on the observable space.
- Practical guidance is lacking: kernel choice/width selection, regularization (ridge/Tikhonov) in operator estimation, truncation criteria, computational complexity and memory scaling, and handling of noise are not adequately discussed.
- Presentation issues: grammar/typos, incomplete references, and a non-anonymous code link are problematic for a NeurIPS submission.

Quality
Technically mostly sound but modest: the derivations mirror established DMD/EDMD kernels with standard linear algebra. However, claims are not backed by rigorous error analyses, and empirical support is insufficient and lacks baselines and metrics.

Clarity
Readable at a high level, but several notational and editorial issues, and key algorithmic choices are underspecified. Reproducibility is limited due to missing implementation details and hyperparameter protocols.

Originality
Limited. Kernelizing DMD/EDMD and stabilizing via truncated eigen/singular value decompositions are well-known. The paper does not convincingly differentiate itself from EDMD/kernel DMD or exact/optimized/subspace DMD.

Significance
Given the unclear novelty and weak experimental validation, the likely impact is limited. Stronger theory (e.g., finite-sample error bounds) or compelling large-scale, quantitative experiments against established baselines would be needed.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Precisely differentiate your method from EDMD/kernel DMD. What is new beyond a change of linear-algebraic viewpoint?
- Provide quantitative comparisons on benchmark systems (e.g., Duffing, FitzHugh–Nagumo, Lorenz, Henon) under noise: eigenvalue recovery error, Koopman mode reconstruction error, prediction RMSE vs horizon, classification accuracy, and change detection metrics. Compare to EDMD (with polynomial/RBF dictionaries), exact/optimized DMD, subspace/Hankel DMD.
- Add regularization to operator estimation and study sensitivity to kernel width and truncation level. Provide computational complexity and memory scaling analyses.
- Either strengthen the preimage approach (with guarantees or strong empirical results) or de-emphasize it.
- Clarify the conditions under which eigenvalues lie on the unit circle and scope of your claims.
- Fix anonymity and references; improve writing and provide implementation details.

Pro/Con for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear, coherent presentation of an RKHS/DMD formulation.
- Potentially useful perspective linking kernel PCA truncation and Koopman projections.
- Broad application ideas.

Cons:
- Overlaps substantially with EDMD/kernel DMD; novelty unclear.
- Weak, largely qualitative experiments without baselines or metrics.
- Strong assumptions and limited theory; no error bounds.
- Practical aspects (regularization, hyperparameters, scalability) underdeveloped.
- Presentation/anonymity issues.

Recommendation: Reject
