This paper presents a method for spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to apply the principles of DMD not to the state-space observations directly, but to their feature-space mappings via a kernel function. This allows for the approximation of the Koopman operator's eigenfunctions using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions implicitly defined by the kernel. The authors present a formulation based on a QR decomposition in the feature space and propose a more numerically robust variant based on a projection onto kernel principal components. The paper demonstrates the method on toy examples and suggests applications in prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

The work is well-written and methodologically sound. It bridges the gap between the DMD literature, which is popular in fluid dynamics, and the well-established framework of kernel methods in machine learning. The motivation to move beyond the linear observables of standard DMD to better approximate nonlinear Koopman eigenfunctions is clear and compelling. The proposed algorithm is a natural and elegant synthesis of these two areas.

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses, primarily concerning originality and the rigor of its empirical validation. While the proposed combination of techniques is sound, the work feels more like a straightforward application of a standard machine learning technique (the kernel trick) to an existing algorithm (DMD) rather than a fundamental contribution. The authors rightly note the connection to Extended DMD (EDMD) [35], which allows for an arbitrary dictionary of basis functions. The proposed kernel DMD can be viewed as a specific instance of EDMD where the basis functions are implicitly defined by the kernel. The paper fails to sufficiently argue for the superiority of this implicit approach over a carefully chosen explicit basis within the ED-MD framework, thus limiting its perceived novelty.

The most critical shortcoming of this paper is the lack of a thorough and quantitative empirical evaluation against established baselines. The experiments presented are more akin to proofs of concept than rigorous validation of a new method.

1.  **Prediction Task:** The paper shows qualitative plots of predicted versus true values for two low-dimensional systems. This is insufficient for a top-tier machine learning conference. There is no quantitative comparison of prediction error (e.g., MSE) against any alternative methods. At a minimum, the proposed kernel DMD should have been benchmarked against standard DMD, EDMD with polynomial or Fourier bases, and standard time-series models from the machine learning literature, such as Gaussian Process Dynamical Models [34] or even a simple LSTM-based recurrent neural network. Without these comparisons, the claim that the method provides a "precise predictive model" is unsupported.

2.  **Dynamics Recognition:** The application to locomotion data is presented as an MDS visualization based on a distance metric between subspaces. While visually appealing, this does not constitute a proper evaluation. A quantitative analysis, such as a classification task where different motions must be identified, is necessary. The accuracy of a classifier using the proposed Koopman eigenfunction features should be compared against classifiers using features from other standard techniques, such as (kernel) PCA on the time-series data.

3.  **Change-Point Detection:** The evaluation is again purely qualitative, showing a single time series with the method's "change score" plotted alongside one other kernel-based method. A rigorous study would involve experiments on multiple datasets with known change points, reporting standard metrics like detection delay and false alarm rates, and comparing against a suite of established change-point detection algorithms.

In its current state, the paper introduces a plausible idea but does not provide the necessary evidence to demonstrate its significance or advantage over existing approaches. The applications are presented as brief vignettes rather than fully developed and validated contributions. For this work to be compelling, it needs to demonstrate, on challenging and relevant problems, that its approach offers a tangible improvement over the state of the art in learning dynamical systems.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper is clearly written and presents a methodologically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods.
*   It provides a potentially useful bridge between the dynamical systems community and machine learning researchers working with kernel methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The novelty is limited, as the method can be viewed as an implicit implementation of the existing Extended DMD framework.
*   The empirical validation is critically weak and lacks quantitative comparisons to any relevant baselines, making it impossible to assess the method's practical utility or advantages.
*   The significance of the work is unproven due to the superficial nature of the experiments and applications.

In summary, while the theoretical derivation is correct and the idea is sensible, the paper does not meet the high standards of empirical rigor expected at NIPS. The lack of comparison to established methods is a fatal flaw. The work is promising but seems premature for publication in its current form. It requires a comprehensive experimental section with quantitative benchmarks against strong baselines to substantiate its claims and demonstrate a clear contribution to the field.
