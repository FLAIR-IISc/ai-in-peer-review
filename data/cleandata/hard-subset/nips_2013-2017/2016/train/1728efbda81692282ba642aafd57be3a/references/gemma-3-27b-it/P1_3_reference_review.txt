## Peer Review for "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) by incorporating reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors aim to overcome the limitations of traditional DMD, which relies on linear monomials and can struggle with highly nonlinear dynamics. The theoretical development is sound, and the empirical results, while limited, demonstrate the potential of the proposed method. However, the paper lacks a deeper comparison with existing kernel-based DMD approaches, and the applications section feels somewhat superficial. I lean towards **weak accept**, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns detailed below.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of modeling nonlinear dynamical systems by extending DMD to operate within RKHS.  Traditional DMD approximates the Koopman operator's eigenvalues and eigenvectors using linear combinations of observables, effectively performing a local linearization. This work proposes to use kernel methods to represent the eigenfunctions more accurately, allowing for the capture of more complex nonlinear dynamics.  The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of spectral methods and decomposition techniques like POD, highlighting the advantages of DMD in providing dynamic information (growth rates and frequencies). 

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, covering POD, DMD variants (exact DMD, optimized DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD, extended DMD), and kernel methods. However, it lacks a specific and detailed comparison with *existing* kernel-based DMD methods (e.g., those leveraging kernel PCA within a DMD framework). This is a significant gap, as the authors claim novelty based on richer basis functions, but don't explicitly demonstrate how their approach differs from prior kernelized versions. The mention of subspace identification methods is relevant, but the connection could be explored further.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The mathematical derivation of the kernelized DMD algorithm is rigorous and builds logically upon the existing DMD framework. The theorems provided are clearly stated and supported.
* **Addresses a Known Limitation:**  The paper directly addresses a significant limitation of standard DMD: its difficulty in capturing highly nonlinear dynamics due to the reliance on linear monomials.
* **Robustification via POD:**  The inclusion of a POD-based projection step to address potential ill-conditioning is a practical and important contribution, mirroring similar approaches in standard DMD.
* **Potential for Feature Extraction:** The presented method offers a way to extract potentially valuable features from nonlinear dynamical systems, opening doors to applications in prediction, change detection, and pattern recognition.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is limited to a simple toy system and the Henon map. While these examples demonstrate the algorithm's functionality, they lack the complexity to truly showcase its advantages over existing methods.  More diverse datasets, including real-world applications, are needed.
* **Lack of Comparative Analysis:** A critical weakness is the absence of a direct comparison with other kernel-based DMD methods.  The authors need to demonstrate quantitatively how their approach outperforms existing techniques in terms of accuracy, computational cost, or robustness.  Specifically, how does their RKHS-based method perform compared to approaches that directly incorporate kernel PCA into the DMD pipeline?
* **Superficial Applications:** The applications section (prediction, embedding, change-point detection) feels somewhat superficial. The methods are presented briefly, and the results lack detailed analysis or comparison with baseline methods. For example, the prediction section doesn't evaluate prediction error or compare to other prediction techniques.
* **Clarity Issues:** While the paper is generally well-written, some sections (particularly regarding the robustification with POD) could benefit from clearer explanations and more intuitive motivations.
* **Code Availability:** The code is available, which is a positive aspect, but the documentation needs to be improved to facilitate reproducibility.

**4. Significance & Originality:**

The originality lies in the explicit formulation of DMD within the RKHS framework and the detailed theoretical treatment. However, the *incremental* nature of the originality is a concern. While a valuable extension, it’s not a paradigm shift. The significance depends on the demonstrated improvement over existing methods. If the authors can convincingly show that their approach offers substantial benefits in real-world applications, then the significance will be higher.

**5. Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Sound theoretical foundation and clear presentation of the mathematical framework.
* Addresses a known limitation of standard DMD.
* The robustification via POD is a valuable practical contribution.
* Potential for impactful applications in dynamical systems analysis.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited empirical evaluation and lack of comparison with existing kernel-based DMD methods.
* Superficial applications section.
* The incremental nature of the originality raises concerns about the overall impact.



**6. Recommendations:**

* **Expand Empirical Evaluation:**  Include experiments on more complex and realistic datasets.
* **Comparative Analysis:** Conduct a thorough comparison with existing kernel-based DMD methods, quantifying performance differences.
* **Deepen Applications Section:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the applications, including quantitative results and comparisons with baseline methods.
* **Clarify POD Robustification:**  Provide a more intuitive explanation of the POD-based robustification step.
* **Improve Code Documentation:** Add more detailed documentation to the released code.




**Confidence:** 7/10 (I am reasonably confident in my assessment, but the lack of comparative experiments makes it difficult to be definitive.)
