

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions. The authors aim to overcome the limitation of standard DMD, which uses linear monomials as basis functions, by leveraging the expressiveness of kernel functions. They present a theoretical framework and algorithms for Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS, with applications to state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a meaningful gap in Koopman spectral analysis by extending DMD to nonlinear basis functions through kernel methods.
- The theoretical foundation is generally sound, with well-structured theorems and proofs.
- The empirical examples on synthetic and real-world data provide some validation of the approach.
- The applications to state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition are relevant to the machine learning and dynamical systems communities.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Errors and Inconsistent Notation:** The paper contains multiple serious technical errors and inconsistent mathematical notation. For example:
   - Equation (5) uses "λ l j" instead of the standard "λ_j^l"
   - Equation (6a) uses "λ̃ t j" which should be "λ̃_j^t" (the paper's notation is inconsistent with standard mathematical practice)
   - Equation (9a) similarly uses "λ̃ t j" instead of proper notation
   - The paper uses both "τ" and "l" to represent time indices, causing confusion

2. **Theoretical Inaccuracies:** The claim that "for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle" (Section 2) is misleading. While eigenvalues corresponding to the attractor's dynamics may lie on the unit circle, Koopman eigenvalues for the entire system can include values outside the unit circle.

3. **Missing Critical Details:** The paper lacks essential implementation details:
   - The choice of kernel parameters (e.g., Gaussian kernel width = 1 for Hénon map) is arbitrary without justification
   - The method for selecting the number of modes (p in Section 4) is not explained
   - The paper fails to clarify how the Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in RKHS reduces to operations on the Gram matrix, despite claiming this is possible due to the reproducing property

4. **Reference and Formatting Issues:** 
   - Several references are poorly formatted with missing information (e.g., "Journal of Fluid Mechanics,, , ,")
   - The URL for the Matlab code ("http://en.44nobu.net/codes/kdmd.zip") appears to contain a typo
   - The paper cites [29] for the Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition but does not provide this reference in the bibliography

5. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims "the proposed algorithm provides a modal decomposition of the dynamics into a finite number of modes" without explaining how to determine this finite number. The robustification step in Section 4 suggests truncation based on singular values, but the paper doesn't provide guidance on how to select the appropriate truncation level.

6. **Lack of Comparison:** The paper doesn't compare its approach to other kernel-based methods for Koopman analysis (e.g., [35]), which weakens the claim of novelty.

**Specific Technical Concerns:**
- Theorem 1's proof contains a critical error: "Mτ = QτR (R ∈ Cτ×τ)" should specify R ∈ C^{τ×τ} or R ∈ C^{τ×τ} (with proper formatting).
- In Section 4, the authors define U as "[ν1, ..., νp]" (a matrix), but then refer to it as "the kernel principal directions" (plural), creating confusion between the matrix and its columns.
- The paper states "the kernel principal directions U" (Section 4), but U is defined as a matrix containing the directions, not a direction itself.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper addresses an interesting problem and presents theoretically sound extensions of DMD, the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, missing implementation details, and theoretical inaccuracies significantly undermine the paper's credibility. These issues are not merely minor formatting problems but substantive technical flaws that would make it difficult for other researchers to understand, reproduce, or build upon the work. The paper's claims about the advantages of kernel-based DMD are not adequately supported by the presented theoretical framework or empirical results.

The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from existing work, particularly extended DMD [35], which operates on pre-determined basis functions rather than kernel functions. The authors fail to adequately justify why their approach is superior to existing methods.

Given the stringent standards of NeurIPS for technical correctness and clarity, and the substantial number of technical issues identified, I recommend rejection. The authors would need to thoroughly revise the paper, addressing all technical errors, providing complete implementation details, justifying parameter choices, and properly differentiating their work from existing methods before it could be considered for publication.
