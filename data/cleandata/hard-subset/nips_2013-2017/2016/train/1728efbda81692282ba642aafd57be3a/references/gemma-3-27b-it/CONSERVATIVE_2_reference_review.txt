This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions. While the core idea of leveraging kernel methods for DMD is not entirely novel – related work in kernelized DMD and spectral methods is acknowledged – the authors aim to provide a more mathematically grounded formulation and address potential ill-conditioning issues with a POD-based projection.

The paper is generally well-written and the mathematical derivations appear correct. The connection to the standard DMD framework is clearly articulated, and the authors attempt to position their work within the broader context of spectral methods and Koopman operator theory. However, the presentation lacks sufficient detail regarding the practical implementation aspects, especially concerning kernel selection and parameter tuning. The empirical evaluation, while demonstrating proof-of-concept on a toy system and the Henon map, feels limited. The results on the Henon map are described as "approximately estimated," which is concerning; more rigorous validation and comparison against existing DMD variants are crucial.

**Strengths:**

*   **Mathematical Soundness:** The theoretical formulation appears solid, building upon established concepts from functional analysis and spectral methods. The extension to RKHS is presented in a clear and logical manner.
*   **Addressing Ill-Conditioning:** The recognition of potential ill-conditioning issues and the proposed POD-based projection offer a practical improvement over naive implementations.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors appropriately situate their work within the existing literature on DMD, Koopman operator theory, and kernel methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of combining DMD with kernel methods isn’t groundbreaking, and the paper doesn't fully articulate how this approach significantly advances the state-of-the-art beyond existing kernelized DMD techniques. The authors emphasize the mathematical correctness, but fail to demonstrate tangible advantages in performance or applicability.
*   **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation is weak. The results on the Henon map are not convincingly demonstrated, and there’s a lack of comparison with standard DMD or other relevant baselines (e.g., extended DMD). The application section feels superficial and lacks rigorous analysis. The choice of kernels seems somewhat arbitrary without justification.
*   **Lack of Practical Guidance:** The paper provides limited guidance on practical implementation issues, such as kernel selection, parameter tuning (kernel width, POD truncation), and computational complexity.
*   **Overly Theoretical Focus:** The paper heavily emphasizes theoretical details while neglecting practical considerations. A more balanced approach, with a stronger focus on experimental results and practical implications, would be more compelling.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a mathematically rigorous formulation of kernelized DMD.
*   Addresses the potential ill-conditioning issues associated with the method.
*   Offers a potentially useful extension to the DMD toolkit, although its advantages remain to be fully demonstrated.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Limited novelty compared to existing kernelized DMD techniques.
*   Insufficient empirical validation to support the claimed benefits.
*   Lack of practical guidance for implementation and application.
*   The applications section feels underdeveloped and lacks insightful results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the theoretical work is sound, the limited novelty, weak empirical validation, and lack of practical guidance make it unlikely to have a significant impact on the field. The authors need to provide more compelling evidence that their approach offers substantial advantages over existing methods and demonstrate its effectiveness on more challenging and realistic datasets. Further, a more detailed discussion of computational complexity and practical implementation issues is essential. The paper, as it stands, feels more like a preliminary investigation than a complete and impactful contribution to NIPS. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit a revised version with more robust experimental results and a stronger focus on practical implications.
