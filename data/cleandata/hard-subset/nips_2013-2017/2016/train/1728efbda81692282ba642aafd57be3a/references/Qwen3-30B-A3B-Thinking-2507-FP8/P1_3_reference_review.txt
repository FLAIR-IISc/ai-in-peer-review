

# Peer Review: "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary
This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors extend traditional DMD, which uses linear monomials as basis functions for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions, by employing kernel functions to obtain richer, nonlinear basis functions. The method involves calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projection. The paper also presents a robustified version using POD bases to handle ill-conditioned problems and demonstrates applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

This work builds upon the foundational DMD literature (Rowley et al., 2009) and extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), which uses pre-determined basis functions rather than kernel-based ones. The kernel-based approach represents a novel extension to the Koopman spectral analysis framework, addressing the limitation of linear approximations inherent in standard DMD.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a mathematically rigorous extension of DMD using RKHS. Theorems 1 and 2 clearly establish the connection between the proposed kernel-based DMD and the Koopman operator theory. The derivation of the robustified version using POD bases is well-motivated and addresses a practical issue with the naive implementation.

2. **Novel Application of Kernel Methods**: The paper successfully applies kernel methods to Koopman spectral analysis in a novel way. While kernel methods have been used in dynamical systems (e.g., Gaussian process dynamical models), this is the first to directly apply them to Koopman operator spectral analysis with DMD.

3. **Practical Applications**: The paper demonstrates several meaningful applications (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) that show the practical utility of the method beyond just theoretical novelty. The empirical results on both synthetic and real-world data (locomotion data) are convincing.

4. **Clear Connection to Prior Work**: The paper properly situates its contribution within the DMD literature and acknowledges related work (including Williams et al.'s extended DMD), making it clear how this work differs.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Empirical Comparison**: The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on two synthetic examples and one real-world dataset. However, it lacks comprehensive comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods for Koopman spectral analysis, particularly extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015). A quantitative comparison on standard benchmark datasets would strengthen the empirical claims.

2. **Lack of Computational Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method or how it scales with dataset size. Given the use of kernel methods, which typically have O(n^3) complexity, this is a significant omission for a machine learning conference like NIPS.

3. **Arbitrary Kernel Selection**: The choice of kernel parameters (e.g., polynomial degree 3 for the toy system, Gaussian with width 1 for Hénon map) appears arbitrary without justification or sensitivity analysis. The paper should discuss how to select appropriate kernels and parameters for different dynamical systems.

4. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: While the paper acknowledges that the method requires sufficiently rich data, it doesn't thoroughly discuss other limitations, such as sensitivity to noise, scalability to high-dimensional systems, or the theoretical guarantees on the quality of approximation.

## Specific Comments

1. The theoretical development is sound, but the paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of the practical implementation steps, particularly regarding how to handle the kernel matrix calculations efficiently.

2. In Section 6, the empirical results show promising results on the toy system and Hénon map, but the paper would be stronger with additional benchmarking on standard dynamical systems (e.g., Lorenz system) commonly used in the literature.

3. The connection to other kernel-based methods for dynamical systems (e.g., Gaussian process dynamical models) could be more thoroughly explored, particularly in terms of how this approach differs from or complements those methods.

4. The application to locomotion data (Section 7.2) is compelling, but the paper should provide more details about how the method compares to standard approaches for dynamics recognition in this domain.

## Recommendation

This is a solid theoretical contribution that extends the DMD framework with kernel methods in a mathematically rigorous way. The paper demonstrates a clear application of kernel methods to Koopman spectral analysis, which is novel in the context of dynamical systems. The method has potential to be useful in various applications where nonlinear dynamical systems analysis is needed.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More comprehensive empirical comparisons with related methods
2. A detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability
3. Justification for kernel parameter selection
4. More thorough exploration of limitations

Given the quality of the theoretical contribution and the practical applications demonstrated, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address these empirical and methodological gaps.

## Pro/Con for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel combination of kernel methods with Koopman spectral analysis
- Solid theoretical foundation with clear theorems
- Demonstrated practical applications with empirical results
- Properly situates the work within the DMD literature

**Con:**
- Limited empirical comparison with state-of-the-art methods
- Inadequate discussion of computational complexity
- Arbitrary kernel parameter selection without justification
- Insufficient exploration of limitations

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical applications that advances the state of the art in Koopman spectral analysis. With the suggested revisions, it would make a valuable contribution to NIPS.
