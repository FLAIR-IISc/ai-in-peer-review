- Paper proposes kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using RKHS to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with richer nonlinear bases.  
- Theoretical development is sound with clear proofs (Theorems 1 and 2) and a well-described algorithm separating theory from implementation.  
- Practical contribution: robust implementation using POD bases to mitigate DMD ill-conditioning.  
- Empirical validation on synthetic (toy system, Hénon map) and real locomotion data provides preliminary support.  
- Lacks thorough comparison with extended DMD and other kernel-based DMD methods in accuracy and computational efficiency.  
- No theoretical analysis of approximation error or computational complexity relative to standard or kernel DMD variants.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited (only two synthetic examples and one real dataset); more diverse experiments needed.  
- Claim that method "fully recovers" Koopman eigenfunctions lacks guarantees on required Krylov subspace size.  
- Paper is well-organized, notation is consistent, and figures (e.g., eigenvalue plots) effectively illustrate results.  
- Connection between theory and practical implementation is under-specified (e.g., details on Gram-Schmidt QR in RKHS reduced to Gram matrix operations).  
- Sections 3–4 use dense math that may be hard to follow for readers unfamiliar with RKHS or Koopman theory; more intuition would help.  
- Empirical results would be stronger if contextualized with comparisons to standard DMD and other kernel methods.  
- The RKHS approach is a natural, expressive extension of existing DMD methods and addresses a practical DMD limitation.  
- Novelty is limited since the work builds on extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) and similar kernel-based ideas; distinction from prior kernel-DMD work is not clearly articulated.  
- Applications (prediction, change-point detection, recognition) are relevant but not shown to clearly outperform existing methods.  
- Recommendation: weak acceptance.  
- Pro-acceptance: sound theory, practical mitigation of ill-conditioning, clear exposition, reasonable empirical results.  
- Con-acceptance: limited novelty, insufficient empirical comparison, missing theoretical analysis of approximation quality and complexity.  
- Suggested improvement 1: add thorough comparisons with extended DMD and other kernel-DMD methods (accuracy and runtime).  
- Suggested improvement 2: expand empirical validation across more and diverse datasets.  
- Suggested improvement 3: include theoretical analysis of approximation error and computational complexity.  
- Suggested improvement 4: clarify distinction from Williams et al. (extended DMD) in introduction and related work.  
- Suggested improvement 5: discuss practical limitations (kernel choice effects, scalability to large datasets).  
- Final assessment: technically sound and well-executed but limited novelty and empirical evidence; reasonable contribution that would benefit from clearer comparisons and deeper analysis.
