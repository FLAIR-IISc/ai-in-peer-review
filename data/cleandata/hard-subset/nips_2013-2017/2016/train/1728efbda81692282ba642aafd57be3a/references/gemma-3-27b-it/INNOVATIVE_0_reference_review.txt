This paper presents a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), aiming to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with richer, nonlinear basis functions. The core idea – leveraging the expressiveness of RKHS to improve upon the linear monomial basis of standard DMD – is compelling and conceptually sound. It builds upon a solid foundation of existing work in spectral methods, DMD, and kernel methods, and the connection to the Arnoldi method is clearly articulated. The authors provide a mathematically rigorous formulation, extending DMD principles to the kernel setting and addressing potential ill-conditioning with a POD-based projection. The empirical evaluation, while limited, demonstrates the method’s ability to estimate eigenvalues accurately on a toy system and approximate results for the Henon map. Applications to prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection are briefly explored, adding breadth to the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of reproducing kernels to DMD, allowing for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions, is a genuinely novel contribution. Standard DMD relies on linear approximations, and this work offers a pathway to address that limitation.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical derivation and the extension of DMD theorems to the RKHS setting are well-presented. The inclusion of the POD-based robustness enhancement is a practical and sensible addition.
*   **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The paper clearly positions itself within the broader landscape of spectral methods, DMD, and Koopman theory. The relationship to Arnoldi decomposition is well explained.
*   **Potential Impact:** If successful, this approach could significantly improve the accuracy and applicability of DMD for a wider range of nonlinear dynamical systems. The potential applications outlined suggest real-world relevance.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation is relatively limited. While the toy system provides a good sanity check, the Henon map example, while illustrative, is a well-studied chaotic system. More diverse and complex datasets, preferably from real-world applications, are needed to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method. The impact of kernel choice and its hyperparameters is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Computational Cost:** The computational complexity of kernel DMD, especially with the POD projection, is not fully discussed. The authors acknowledge potential ill-conditioning but don’t provide detailed scalability analysis or practical guidelines for handling large datasets.
*   **Application Details:** The applications section feels somewhat cursory. The implementation details for prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition are not sufficiently described to fully assess their effectiveness.
*   **Comparison to Existing Kernel DMD Variants:** While the authors cite related works, there's a lack of direct comparison to other existing kernel-based DMD approaches (e.g., extended DMD with kernel methods) which could better highlight the specific advantages of their approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel idea with strong theoretical foundations.
*   The extension of DMD to the kernel setting has the potential to broaden the applicability of the method to a wider range of nonlinear systems.
*   The POD-based robustness enhancement is a practical and valuable addition.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contribution within the relevant literature.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The empirical validation is limited and does not definitively demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches.
*   The computational cost and scalability of the method are not adequately addressed.
*   The applications section lacks sufficient detail and practical demonstration.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising and novel contribution to the field of spectral methods for dynamical systems. While the empirical validation is currently limited, the theoretical soundness and potential impact of the work are significant. I lean towards **acceptance** due to the novelty and boldness of the approach. The potential benefits of kernelizing DMD outweigh the current limitations, and I believe that further research building on this foundation will be valuable. However, the authors should be encouraged to address the weaknesses identified above – particularly expanding the empirical evaluation and providing a more detailed analysis of computational cost – in future work. A minor revision request focusing on these aspects would be appropriate.
