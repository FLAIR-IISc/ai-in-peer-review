Summary
The paper proposes a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) framework for Koopman spectral analysis. The main idea is to perform Arnoldi/DMD-like computations in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), by building a Krylov subspace from feature-mapped snapshots ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1), orthogonalizing via Gram–Schmidt, and computing Ritz values/vectors by projecting the Koopman operator onto this subspace. A “robustified” variant projects onto kernel-PCA directions (analogous to DMD with POD truncation) and estimates eigenvalues via an operator F̂ = U* K_H U that is expressed entirely in terms of Gram matrices. The paper claims that this yields richer (nonlinear) basis functions than linear DMD and can approximate Koopman eigenfunctions in RKHS. Applications to short-term prediction via preimage, dynamics recognition via subspace distances, and change-point detection are illustrated on toy examples (a simple nonlinear 2D system, the Hénon map, locomotion data, and a Lorenz-map change detection scenario).

Relation to prior work
The approach is closely related to Extended DMD (EDMD) and its kernelized variant (Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, J. Nonlinear Science 2015), which approximates the Koopman operator on a finite dictionary and shows how to implement EDMD with kernels to avoid explicit feature maps. The “robustification” via kernel PCA and the use of Gram matrices is essentially the kernel-EDMD setting, where the projected operator is assembled from GXX and GYX. The paper also relates to exact/optimized DMD and method-of-snapshots variants for numerical stability. The novelty appears to be primarily in recasting the derivation through RKHS Krylov subspaces and an Arnoldi/QR perspective and stating two theorems linking the kernel-PCA projection to Koopman eigenfunctions.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: DMD with linear monomials can be too restrictive; kernels provide expressive nonlinear features.
- Conceptual unification: The RKHS/Arnoldi view helps connect DMD and EDMD and makes the projection/eigendecomposition pipeline explicit in feature space.
- Practical recipe: The robust variant formulates all steps with Gram matrices and admits rank truncation (akin to POD), which is numerically important.
- Applications: The paper sketches how to use the extracted modes for prediction (with preimage), dynamics recognition (via principal angles), and change-point detection, indicating potential utility beyond basic spectral analysis.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Novelty relative to kernel EDMD: The robust algorithm in Sec. 4 (operator F̂ built from centered Gram matrices and its eigendecomposition) is essentially kernel EDMD/method-of-snapshots. The paper cites EDMD [35] but underplays the overlap; it does not clearly differentiate its contributions beyond an alternative derivation and minor algorithmic variations. A direct comparison (empirical and conceptual) to kernel EDMD is missing.
- Mathematical rigor and consistency:
  - Theorem 1’s proof conflates multiple projected/companion operators. In standard DMD, with X = Q R, one uses F = R^{-1} Q* Y (Y are shifted snapshots), and X F ≈ Y. Here, the text alternates between F = R^{-1} Q* M+ and F = R^{-1} Pτ R and even Mτ^{-1} K_H Mτ, the latter being ill-defined since Mτ maps C^τ to H and is not invertible in general. This should be corrected to the standard projected-shift relation and the conditions under which the Vandermonde argument applies in infinite-dimensional H.
  - The assumptions required for ψ = 0 (invariance of the span under K_H) and for diagonalizability (distinct eigenvalues) are strong; the paper does not discuss how violations (noise, continuous spectrum, near-defective cases) impact the estimates.
  - Theorem 2 assumes φ_j ∈ Φ and representer φ_j(x) = ⟨ϕ(x), κ_j⟩ with κ_j in span(U). This is a restrictive assumption that amounts to (i) Koopman eigenfunctions lying in the RKHS closure of feature spans and (ii) being captured by the chosen kernel-PCA subspace. No conditions on the kernel or the dynamics (boundedness of K_H, invariance of Φ, ergodicity, measure) are given to justify when this holds.
  - The statement “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is, in general, false unless one imposes measure-preserving/unitary dynamics; dissipative systems typically produce eigenvalues inside the unit disk.
- Experimental evaluation is limited:
  - Only small qualitative demos without rigorous metrics (e.g., eigenvalue/mode error vs. ground truth, sensitivity to noise, sample size, kernel parameters) or comparisons to exact DMD/EDMD/kernel EDMD. The Hénon example shows partial recovery but no quantitative analysis.
  - The preimage method is heuristic (MDS-based reconstruction) and not benchmarked against alternatives (e.g., kernel ridge preimage, gradient-based inversion). No assessment of preimage error’s effect on prediction is provided.
  - The recognition and change-point experiments lack enough detail: dataset sizes, splits, kernel choices/tuning, runtime, baselines; the change-point example compares to a kernel method without clear calibration or error rates.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Notation overload and inconsistencies (ϕ vs φ, H for both RKHS and centering operator, 1τ definition, adjoints in H) make some parts hard to follow.
  - Several references are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages), and some claims are not properly supported by citations.
  - Important algorithmic details (complexity, memory, numerical stabilization, regularization) are not discussed; kernel methods scale as O(τ^3) in general—practical limits and remedies should be addressed.

Significance
Kernelizing Koopman/DMD methods has proven useful, but this has been established in prior work (kernel EDMD). Without theoretical advances (e.g., convergence guarantees under noise, approximation error bounds in RKHS) or substantial empirical evidence that this formulation materially improves performance or stability, the incremental significance is limited. The applications sketched are interesting but underdeveloped.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the operator algebra in Sec. 3: distinguish Pτ = Q* K_H Q from the shift-based F = R^{-1} Q* M+, and eliminate the incorrect Mτ^{-1} expression. State precise conditions for Theorem 1 (invariance, distinctness, noiselessness) and how they are relaxed in practice.
- Strengthen theory: provide approximation/convergence results for the kernel-PCA-projected operator, or at least discuss conditions on kernels/dynamics (e.g., universality, compactness, boundedness of K_H) under which φ_j ∈ Φ is plausible.
- Position relative to EDMD/kernel EDMD: explain exactly how the proposed method differs, when it is equivalent, and whether the Arnoldi perspective offers computational or statistical benefits. Include head-to-head comparisons on benchmark systems with quantitative metrics and ablations (effect of kernel, truncation rank, noise).
- Expand experiments: report eigenvalue/mode errors, prediction error vs. horizon, robustness to noise and kernel bandwidth, scalability, and comparisons to baselines (DMD, exact DMD, EDMD, kernel EDMD). For preimage, compare reconstruction schemes and quantify errors.
- Improve clarity: fix notation, complete references, and revise claims about eigenvalue locations.

Pros
- Sound motivation; natural extension of DMD into RKHS.
- Practical, implementable recipe using Gram matrices with truncation.
- Connections between kernel PCA subspaces and Koopman spectra are insightful.
- Potentially useful application directions.

Cons
- Overlaps substantially with kernel EDMD; novelty not convincingly established.
- Some mathematical arguments are inconsistent or insufficiently justified.
- Experimental validation is weak and mostly qualitative.
- Clarity and referencing need significant polish.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is worthwhile but needs clearer positioning relative to kernel EDMD, corrected and strengthened theory, and substantially more rigorous empirical evaluation. I encourage the authors to revise accordingly.
