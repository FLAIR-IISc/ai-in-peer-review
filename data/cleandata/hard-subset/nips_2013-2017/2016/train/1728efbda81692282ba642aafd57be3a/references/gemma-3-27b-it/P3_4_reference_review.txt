## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) by incorporating reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The core idea of using kernels to enrich the basis functions for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions is well-motivated, addressing a key limitation of standard DMD. However, the paper suffers from a lack of substantial novelty in its core algorithmic contribution and a somewhat superficial empirical evaluation. While technically sound, the significance of the proposed method over existing kernel-based DMD variants is not convincingly demonstrated. I recommend **weak accept** with a request for more compelling empirical results and a clearer articulation of the method's unique contributions.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The paper builds upon the established framework of Koopman operator theory and DMD. It correctly identifies the limitations of traditional DMD – its reliance on linear monomials and inability to effectively capture highly nonlinear dynamics. The authors propose extending DMD by operating within an RKHS, allowing for the use of nonlinear basis functions via kernel methods. This approach aims to improve the accuracy of Koopman eigenfunction approximation. 

The paper provides a reasonable, though not exhaustive, review of related work. It correctly positions the contribution within the broader landscape of spectral methods, POD, DMD, and kernel methods for dynamical systems analysis.  The authors mention several important variants of DMD (e.g., exact DMD, optimized DMD) but could benefit from a more detailed comparison of their approach to extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), which also utilizes pre-determined basis functions and shares conceptual similarities.  The connection to subspace identification methods is also acknowledged but remains underdeveloped.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The mathematical derivation of the kernel-based DMD algorithm appears correct and well-presented. The theorem establishing the relationship between empirical Ritz values/vectors and Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions is a valuable contribution.
* **Addressing a Limitation of DMD:** The motivation for using RKHS to capture nonlinear dynamics is strong and directly addresses a known weakness of traditional DMD.
* **Robustification Technique:** The inclusion of POD-based projection to address potential ill-conditioning is a practical consideration and enhances the algorithm's robustness.
* **Clear Presentation of Mathematical Details:** The paper’s mathematical notation is clear and well-defined, making the core concepts accessible to readers familiar with the underlying theory.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of combining DMD with kernel methods isn’t entirely novel. Kernel DMD variants already exist (though perhaps not with the same theoretical grounding), and the presented method doesn’t offer a dramatically different approach. The claim of providing a richer basis function set doesn't fully stand out given existing kernel-based approaches. 
* **Superficial Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical examples (toy system and Henon map) are rather basic. While they demonstrate the algorithm’s ability to approximate eigenvalues, they lack the complexity and realism to convincingly demonstrate its advantages over existing methods.  The choice of kernels appears somewhat arbitrary without a strong justification.
* **Applications Lack Depth:** The applications section (prediction, embedding/recognition, change-point detection) feels underdeveloped. The presented results are qualitative and lack quantitative comparison to baseline methods. The description of the prediction scheme is particularly brief and doesn’t fully explain how the pre-image is recovered in practice.
* **Lack of Computational Complexity Analysis:**  The paper does not provide any discussion of the computational cost of the proposed method, especially compared to standard DMD or other kernel-based variants.  This is a crucial consideration for practical applicability.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some sections (e.g., the proof of Theorem 1) could benefit from further streamlining and improved readability.



**4. Specific Comments/Questions:**

* **Comparison to Extended DMD:** How does this approach differ from Extended DMD in terms of basis function selection, computational complexity, and theoretical guarantees? A direct comparison would be valuable.
* **Kernel Selection:**  The choice of kernel (polynomial for the toy system, Gaussian for the Henon map) seems ad-hoc.  What criteria were used to select these kernels?  Did the authors explore other kernel options and compare their performance?
* **POD Truncation:** How is the number of POD modes selected? What is the impact of this choice on the accuracy of the results?
* **Pre-image Calculation:** Could the authors provide more details on the pre-image recovery scheme used for prediction? What specific MDS algorithm was employed, and how was the distance between the feature and input spaces calculated?
* **Scalability:** How does this algorithm scale with the size of the dataset and the dimensionality of the state space? Is it applicable to high-dimensional dynamical systems?




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a mathematically sound extension of DMD with a clear motivation.
* The inclusion of POD for robustification is a practical improvement.
* The theoretical results (e.g., Theorem 1) are valuable contributions to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the approach is limited, as kernel-based DMD variants already exist.
* The empirical evaluation is superficial and lacks compelling evidence of improved performance.
* The applications section is underdeveloped and lacks quantitative results.
*  Lack of computational complexity analysis hinders assessing the practical value.




**Overall, while the paper offers a technically sound contribution, its limited novelty and superficial evaluation prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing more rigorous empirical validation and a clearer articulation of the method’s unique advantages, would significantly strengthen the paper.**
