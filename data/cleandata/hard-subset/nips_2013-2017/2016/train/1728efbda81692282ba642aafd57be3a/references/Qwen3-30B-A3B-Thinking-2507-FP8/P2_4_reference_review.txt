

# Review: "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary
This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproduce kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. While standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials of observables (effectively a local linear approximation), the authors introduce a kernel-based method that uses nonlinear basis functions. The approach involves performing Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in RKHS followed by eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. The authors also present a robustified version using POD (Principal Orthogonal Decomposition) to address numerical instability. Applications include state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition, demonstrated on synthetic systems (toy nonlinear system, Hénon map) and real-world locomotion data.

## Quality
The paper presents technically sound theoretical extensions of DMD to RKHS. Theorems 1 and 2 provide rigorous mathematical foundations for the proposed algorithm, and the connections to Koopman spectral theory are well-motivated. The robustification approach via POD is a practical and well-justified solution to the ill-conditioning problem known in DMD implementations. The empirical evaluation demonstrates the method's capabilities across multiple domains, with appropriate use of synthetic and real-world data. 

However, the paper lacks comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods. While it references "Extended DMD" [35], it doesn't provide quantitative comparisons on standard benchmarks or demonstrate clear performance advantages over existing approaches. The implementation details (e.g., kernel parameter selection, computational complexity) are somewhat superficial, which limits reproducibility. The theoretical analysis doesn't address fundamental questions about the relationship between kernel choice and the quality of Koopman eigenfunction approximation.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from theoretical background to proposed method to applications. Mathematical notation is consistent, and the key theorems are well-explained. The figures effectively illustrate the empirical results (e.g., Fig. 1 showing accurate eigenvalue estimation for the toy system). The presentation of the algorithm (Section 4) is clear, though the explanation of the pre-image problem for prediction (Section 7.1) could be more detailed. The writing is accessible to researchers familiar with DMD and RKHS, though some readers might need to consult references for background on Koopman operator theory.

## Originality
The core idea of extending DMD to RKHS is novel in the context of Koopman spectral analysis. While Extended DMD [35] uses pre-determined basis functions, this paper uses kernel functions to define a more flexible, data-adaptive basis. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from prior work: "While existing DMD algorithms can conceptually be thought of as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables... our algorithm gives an approximation with a set of nonlinear basis functions due to the expressiveness of kernel functions."

This is a meaningful extension rather than a trivial combination of known techniques. The use of kernel methods to address the limitations of local linearization in standard DMD is conceptually fresh. The paper's contribution fits well within the recent NIPS trend of applying kernel methods to dynamical systems analysis, though it's not entirely unprecedented (Williams et al. 2015 on Koopman operators is cited).

## Significance
The proposed method addresses a genuine limitation of traditional DMD: its reliance on local linear approximations that fail for complex nonlinear dynamics. By using kernel functions, the method can capture more complex dynamics without requiring a priori knowledge of the system's structure. The applications demonstrated—state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition—are relevant to real-world problems in engineering and science.

However, the significance would be stronger with more compelling evidence of superior performance over existing methods. The paper shows the method works, but doesn't convincingly demonstrate it outperforms state-of-the-art approaches on standard benchmarks. The locomotion data example (Fig. 3) is promising, but the comparison with other methods is missing. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of when and why one should choose this kernel-based DMD over other Koopman-based methods.

## Overall Assessment
This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the growing literature on Koopman operator methods for dynamical systems. The extension to RKHS is well-motivated and theoretically rigorous, with a practical robustification approach. The applications demonstrate the method's utility in real-world contexts.

The main weaknesses are the lack of comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods and insufficient detail on implementation choices. The significance would be enhanced by showing clear advantages over existing techniques.

## Recommendation
**Weak Accept**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Koopman spectral analysis by extending DMD to RKHS. It's technically sound, well-written, and addresses a genuine limitation of existing methods. While the empirical evaluation could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons, the theoretical foundation and practical applications are sufficiently compelling for acceptance. The work aligns well with recent NIPS trends in applying kernel methods to dynamical systems analysis, and it advances the state of the art in Koopman-based methods.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the following:
1. Include quantitative comparisons with Extended DMD [35] and other relevant methods on standard benchmarks
2. Add a discussion of computational complexity and scalability
3. Provide more detail on kernel parameter selection and sensitivity analysis
4. Clarify the connection between kernel choice and the quality of Koopman eigenfunction approximation

These revisions would strengthen the paper without requiring major rework, making it an excellent contribution to the field.
