Summary
The paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors reinterpret DMD as an Arnoldi/Ritz approximation of the Koopman operator and then “kernelize” this by working with Krylov subspaces in feature space, reducing computations to Gram matrices. They provide: (i) a direct kernelized DMD procedure via Gram–Schmidt in RKHS with an associated Ritz-value/vector result (Theorem 1), and (ii) a numerically robust variant that projects onto kernel-PCA (POD) directions before eigendecomposition (Theorem 2), enabling truncation and improved conditioning. They demonstrate applications—short-term prediction via a pre-image heuristic, dynamics recognition via kernel principal angles between eigenspaces, and sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-style statistic—with small-scale experiments on a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, CMU mocap sequences, and a Lorenz-map change-point task.

Relation to prior work
DMD approximates Koopman spectra; extended DMD (EDMD) lifts data with user-chosen dictionaries and has kernelized versions (kEDMD). The robust variant here is close in spirit to EDMD/kEDMD (the authors acknowledge this), differing mainly in the Arnoldi/Krylov perspective and a particular construction via kernel PCA bases. There is also related work on kernel transfer operators (e.g., Klus et al.) and spectral methods for nonlinear systems.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a clean RKHS-based Arnoldi/Ritz view of Koopman spectral approximation. The mapping of Krylov-subspace operations to kernel Gram-matrix computations is elegant.
- Practical twist: the kernel-PCA/POD projection for robustness and truncation is sensible and aligns with best practices in DMD.
- Broad applicability: the paper outlines several downstream tasks (prediction, recognition, change detection) built on Koopman features.
- Accessibility: algorithmic steps are provided; code is released.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to EDMD/kEDMD is limited. The robust method is very similar to EDMD with kernels; the direct Gram–Schmidt variant is a modest algorithmic variation on an already-established operator-approximation paradigm. The paper does not adequately differentiate itself empirically or theoretically from EDMD/kEDMD and kernel transfer-operator approaches.
- Empirical validation is weak: mostly qualitative figures without quantitative error metrics, ablations, or comparisons to EDMD/kEDMD, exact/optimized DMD, or kernel transfer-operator baselines. The Hénon example lacks systematic accuracy evaluation; the mocap recognition uses MDS visualization without classification accuracy; change detection lacks rigorous metrics and comparisons beyond one kernel method.
- Theory relies on strong assumptions. Theorem 2 essentially assumes the target eigenfunctions lie in the span of the kernel-PCA subspace; guarantees are thus conditional and not surprising. No discussion of approximation error or sampling/statistical rates.
- Clarity and rigor issues: several notational inconsistencies/typos (e.g., centering matrix definitions), incomplete references, and some statements that are not generally correct (e.g., “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle”—true for measure-preserving/unitary settings, not generally for dissipative systems). Figures and algorithm descriptions could be tighter, with complexity and conditioning discussed more carefully. The pre-image prediction method is ad hoc and not compared to standard pre-image techniques.

Quality
Technically plausible with correct high-level operator manipulations, but proofs hinge on restrictive assumptions; numerical issues acknowledged but not deeply analyzed; experiments insufficient to substantiate robustness or superiority.

Clarity
Readable overall but needs polishing: tighten notation, fix centering and Gram-matrix definitions, provide explicit pseudocode, and make figures/tables quantitative. Many references are incomplete.

Originality
Moderate. The main ideas are close to EDMD/kEDMD and kernel transfer operator literature; the Arnoldi-in-RKHS framing is a reasonable perspective but not a step-change.

Significance
Potentially useful as a unifying exposition and for practitioners who prefer Krylov/DMD views; however, without strong empirical gains or new guarantees over EDMD/kEDMD, the impact is limited.

Reasons to accept
- Clean RKHS/DMD synthesis with an intuitive robustification.
- Could help broaden use of Koopman methods in ML by connecting Krylov/DMD and kernel toolkits.
- Code available; may be a useful tutorial-style contribution.

Reasons to reject
- Limited novelty over EDMD/kEDMD and kernel transfer-operator methods; the paper itself notes similarities.
- Lacks quantitative, comparative experiments demonstrating advantages.
- Theoretical results are conditional; no new approximation/error analysis.
- Presentation and references need significant polishing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide rigorous empirical comparisons to EDMD, kEDMD, exact/optimized DMD, and kernel transfer operators on standard benchmarks (including runtime/conditioning analyses).
- Quantify prediction error, eigenvalue/eigenfunction approximation accuracy, classification accuracy for dynamics recognition, and change-point detection performance (AUROC/average run length).
- Clarify novelty relative to EDMD/kEDMD; articulate when Arnoldi-in-RKHS is preferable and why.
- Correct centering/notation issues; complete citations; add complexity and memory analyses; include pseudocode.
- Revisit the statement about eigenvalues on the unit circle; specify assumptions (e.g., unitary Koopman operator).

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is appealing and could be impactful if clearly differentiated from EDMD/kEDMD and validated more rigorously. With stronger empirical evidence and a crisper positioning relative to prior kernel Koopman methods, this work could become competitive.
