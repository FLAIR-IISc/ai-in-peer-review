### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a method for spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems via the Koopman operator. The core idea is to lift the system's state space into a high-dimensional reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) and perform Koopman analysis there. Standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear basis of observables, which can be limiting. By using the kernel trick, the proposed method, which could be called kernel DMD (kDMD), can approximate these eigenfunctions using a much richer, nonlinear set of basis functions derived from the kernel.

The authors present two algorithmic formulations. The first is a direct translation of the DMD principle into the RKHS, based on a Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition of the Krylov matrix in the feature space. Recognizing that this approach can be numerically unstable (an issue also present in classic DMD), they propose a second, more robust algorithm. This robust version projects the dynamics onto the principal components in the feature space (i.e., performing Kernel PCA) before computing the projected Koopman operator and its eigendecomposition. The paper provides theoretical justification (Theorems 1 and 2) connecting the computed eigenvalues and modes to the true Koopman spectra. Finally, the authors demonstrate the utility of kDMD as a feature extraction technique in several applications: short-term prediction via pre-imaging, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Natural Extension:** The paper presents a well-motivated and elegant combination of two powerful frameworks: Koopman operator theory (via DMD) from dynamical systems and kernel methods from machine learning. Kernelizing DMD is a natural step to enhance its ability to handle complex nonlinear dynamics, making the technique more accessible and powerful for a machine learning audience.

2.  **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide a clear theoretical framework for their method. The connection between the Arnoldi method, Krylov subspaces, and Koopman theory is well-explained. Theorem 2, in particular, formally connects the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the finite-dimensional operator `F̂` (computed from data) to the true Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions, under reasonable assumptions.

3.  **Practical and Robust Algorithm:** The paper correctly identifies the potential for numerical instability in a naive implementation and proposes a robust version based on Kernel PCA (Section 4). This shows a thoughtful approach to developing a practical algorithm and aligns with best practices in both DMD (using POD truncation) and kernel methods (using a low-rank approximation).

4.  **Diverse and Interesting Applications:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting a new algorithm and demonstrates its utility on a compelling set of tasks. The applications to motion capture data for dynamics recognition and to the Lorenz system for change-point detection are particularly strong, showcasing the potential of kDMD as a general-purpose feature extractor for time-series data.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Originality and Relation to Extended DMD (eDMD):** The primary weakness of the paper is its relationship to prior work, specifically Extended DMD (eDMD) by Williams et al. [35]. The robust algorithm presented in Section 4 is functionally very similar, if not equivalent, to eDMD where the dictionary of basis functions is chosen to be a set of kernel functions centered at the data points. The paper mentions eDMD but states that "the resulting procedure is similar," which understates the deep connection. A more detailed discussion and comparison, both theoretical and empirical, against eDMD is necessary to clearly establish the paper's novel contribution. Without this, the work appears to be an incremental, though well-presented, application of existing ideas.

2.  **Experimental Rigor:** While the applications are interesting, the experimental evaluation could be more rigorous and quantitative.
    *   **Prediction:** The prediction results (Fig. 2) are only shown qualitatively. Standard quantitative metrics like Mean Squared Error (MSE) are missing, and there are no comparisons to baseline methods (e.g., standard DMD, eDMD, or even a Gaussian Process dynamical model).
    *   **Change-Point Detection:** The comparison is made against a "kernel change detection method" [7], but this baseline is not clearly defined. The performance is again shown visually without quantitative metrics like detection delay or false alarm rate, which are standard in this domain.
    *   **Hyperparameter Selection:** The choice of kernels and their parameters (e.g., kernel width) is crucial for performance but is only briefly mentioned. A discussion of the sensitivity to these hyperparameters or a description of the model selection process would strengthen the paper's practical value.

3.  **Clarity of Pre-image Method:** The prediction task relies on solving the pre-image problem, which is notoriously difficult for kernel methods. The paper outlines an MDS-based approach from [18], but the description is brief. Given the importance of this step for the prediction application, a more detailed explanation and a discussion of its potential limitations would be beneficial.

### Detailed Comments

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations appear correct, and the progression from the direct but unstable method to the robust one is logical. However, the lack of rigorous quantitative comparisons in the experiments weakens the support for the claims of the method's effectiveness.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction and background sections provide excellent context. The core ideas are communicated clearly. The remark at the end of Section 4 about using data pairs `{x_1^(i), x_2^(i)}` instead of a single time series is an important practical point that could have been highlighted earlier.
*   **Originality:** As discussed in the "Weaknesses" section, the originality is questionable in light of eDMD [35]. While the specific formulation and derivation from the perspective of kernelizing the Krylov subspace might be new, the resulting practical algorithm is very close to existing work. The authors need to make a stronger case for what sets their method apart.
*   **Significance:** The work has high potential significance. It provides a bridge between the fluid dynamics/physics communities where DMD is popular and the machine learning community. If its advantages over related methods like eDMD can be clearly demonstrated, it could become a widely used tool for nonlinear time-series analysis. The demonstrated applications are compelling and suggest broad applicability.

### Recommendation

This is a well-written paper on an interesting and timely topic. The proposed kDMD method is elegant, and the applications are compelling. However, the significant overlap with Extended DMD [35] is a major concern that is not adequately addressed. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation lacks the quantitative rigor and baseline comparisons expected for a top-tier conference like NIPS.

Given these points, I am on the borderline. The strengths (clarity, potential significance, interesting applications) are substantial, but the weakness in originality and experimental validation is also significant. I lean towards rejection but would be open to changing my mind if the authors can convincingly argue their contribution's novelty over eDMD and strengthen their experimental claims during the author response period.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Clear, well-motivated combination of kernel methods and DMD.
*   Theoretically sound and presents a practical, robust algorithm.
*   Showcases a variety of interesting and non-trivial applications.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Novelty is questionable due to strong similarity with Extended DMD [35].
*   Experimental validation lacks quantitative metrics and strong baselines.
*   Contribution over existing methods is not clearly established.
