

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors aim to overcome the limitation of standard DMD, which uses linear monomials as basis functions, by employing nonlinear kernel functions to better approximate Koopman eigenfunctions. The theoretical framework is developed, with a robust implementation via POD projection, and applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition are demonstrated.

**Quality Assessment**

The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHS, with proofs for key theorems (Theorems 1 and 2). However, several critical technical inconsistencies undermine the quality:

1. The Koopman operator definition is problematic. The authors define "KHϕ(x) = ϕ ◦ f(x)" (Equation 7), but the Koopman operator should act on functions, not feature maps. The correct definition would be (Kφ)(x) = φ(f(x)) for φ ∈ H, not on the feature map ϕ.

2. Notation is inconsistent throughout. The Koopman operator is denoted as both K and KH without clear distinction, and the use of Mτ as a matrix with columns in infinite-dimensional H is confusing.

3. The claim "for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle" (Section 2) is factually incorrect. For stable systems, eigenvalues should have magnitude ≤1, not necessarily on the unit circle.

4. Section 4's kernel PCA application is ambiguous - it's unclear whether PCA is applied to the original data or to the feature vectors ϕ(xi).

5. The robustification method using POD bases is described but not convincingly differentiated from standard DMD robustification techniques.

**Clarity and Presentation**

The paper is generally well-organized, but suffers from several clarity issues:

1. The connection between the theoretical framework and the algorithmic implementation is not sufficiently explained. For instance, the Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in RKHS is claimed to reduce to operations on a Gram matrix, but the reduction is not shown.

2. Key terms like "empirical Ritz values" and "Krylov matrix" are used without sufficient explanation for readers unfamiliar with DMD.

3. Figure 1's caption states "kernel DMD Equilibrium DMD" for the right plot, which is confusing and should be clarified.

4. The references to "kernel DMD" in the figures lack clear citations to previous work on kernel-based DMD.

**Originality and Significance**

The core idea of using kernel functions to extend DMD is not entirely novel. The paper acknowledges Williams et al.'s extended DMD [35], which works on pre-determined basis functions, but doesn't clearly differentiate the proposed kernel-based approach from this prior work. The paper states "the resulting procedure is similar to the robust-version of our algorithm," but fails to specify the key differences.

The empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic examples (toy system, Hénon map) without comparison to standard DMD or extended DMD. The applications (prediction, change-point detection) are standard for DMD but don't clearly demonstrate advantages of the kernel approach. The lack of real-world applications where kernel DMD would provide clear benefits weakens the significance claim.

**Other Issues**

1. The paper cites "kernel DMD" in the figures but doesn't provide a clear reference to previous work on kernel-based DMD methods.

2. The choice of kernel parameters (polynomial degree 3, Gaussian width 1) is arbitrary and not justified or optimized.

3. The claim "the Koopman operator defined in Eq. (7) is identical to the transition operator on an observable" is misleading - the Koopman operator acts on functions, not on feature maps.

4. The reference to Arnoldi [1] is incomplete (missing volume, page numbers).

**Conclusion**

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting extension of DMD to RKHS, it is undermined by pervasive technical inconsistencies, unclear notation, and insufficient empirical validation. The theoretical framework is sound but not sufficiently differentiated from existing work. The paper would benefit from clearer notation, more rigorous mathematical definitions, and a more comprehensive comparison with prior kernel-based DMD methods.

The minor but numerous technical flaws and presentation issues significantly impact the paper's overall quality. While the core idea has potential, the current presentation does not convincingly demonstrate the novelty or advantages of the proposed approach over existing methods. The paper would require substantial revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Reject**
