- Paper proposes kernel-based Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using RKHS to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions.
- Core contribution is an algorithm for kernel-DMD plus a POD-based robustification to mitigate ill-conditioning.
- Demonstrations include a toy system and the Henon map, with applications suggested for prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.
- Places work within Koopman operator theory, DMD, POD, and subspace identification, and notes similarity to extended DMD.
- Novelty: extending DMD with RKHS is a valuable and potentially impactful idea for complex dynamics.
- Theoretical soundness: formulation and a theorem on Ritz values/vectors provide solid justification.
- Practicality: addressing ill-conditioning using POD makes the method more applicable to real problems.
- Presentation: paper is well-written, mathematically clear, and includes accessible explanations.
- Reproducibility: Matlab code is provided, which is a strong plus.
- Empirical validation is limited; examples are simple and comparisons to existing methods are insufficient.
- Kernel selection is under-discussed; choice of kernels and sensitivity to parameters are not explored.
- Computational complexity and scalability for large datasets are not addressed, despite potential kernel expense.
- Applications lack depth and quantitative evaluation—change-point detection presented without clear performance metrics.
- Connection to related kernel-based dynamical methods could be more nuanced and thorough.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically solid, addresses ill-conditioning, and provides code with promising applications.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical evaluation, missing kernel selection and complexity discussions, and weak quantitative application results.
- Overall assessment: solid, novel contribution with strong math but insufficient experiments and discussion on kernels/complexity.
- Recommendation: Weak Accept, with minor revisions to expand empirical evaluation, discuss kernel choice and computational considerations, and strengthen quantitative application metrics.
