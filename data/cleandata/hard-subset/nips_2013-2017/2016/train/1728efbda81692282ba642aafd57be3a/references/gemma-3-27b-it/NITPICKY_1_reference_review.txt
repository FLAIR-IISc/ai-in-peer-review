This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, aiming to overcome limitations of traditional DMD which relies on linear monomials. The core idea is to perform the analysis within the RKHS feature space, enabling the use of nonlinear basis functions. The authors present a theoretical framework and algorithms for this “kernel DMD”, including a robustification strategy based on POD projections. The paper attempts to demonstrate the method on both synthetic and real-world data, showcasing applications in prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

While the concept of extending DMD with kernel methods is a natural and potentially valuable direction, this paper falls short of convincing me of its substantial contribution to the field, and I recommend rejection due to a multitude of flaws, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to significant issues in the presentation, clarity, and practical justification of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

*   The core idea of applying RKHS to DMD is conceptually sound and addresses a known limitation of standard DMD.
*   The attempt to provide a theoretical grounding for the kernel DMD approach is commendable.
*   The inclusion of application examples (prediction, recognition, change-point detection) provides a glimpse into the potential utility of the method.

**Weaknesses (and they are numerous):**

*   **Lack of Novelty:** The paper doesn’t sufficiently differentiate itself from existing kernel-based approaches for Koopman operator learning (e.g., extended DMD). The connection to extended DMD, while mentioned, is glossed over and the distinct advantages of this proposed method aren’t clearly articulated.  The algorithm largely mirrors concepts from extended DMD but frames it in a different (and less clear) notation.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The notation is often inconsistent (e.g., using both *ϕ* and *κ* to represent kernel feature maps). The flow of the derivation is difficult to follow, and some equations appear without sufficient explanation. Section 3, in particular, is densely packed and lacks intuitive explanation.
*   **Technical Gaps and Oversimplifications:** The discussion of the Krylov subspace construction lacks depth. The claim of reducing the calculation to a Gram matrix operation is correct but requires more detailed justification and referencing of established kernel methods literature. The connection between the theoretical framework (Theorem 1 and 2) and the practical implementation is not fully established.  The algorithm description in Section 4 feels fragmented and lacks sufficient detail for replication.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is surprisingly weak. The "toy system" example is trivial and provides minimal insight. The Hénon map example is slightly more convincing but lacks a comparative analysis against existing DMD variants or other kernel-based methods. The parameter choices for the kernels are not justified.  The applications section is superficial; the results are presented without sufficient detail or rigorous evaluation. Figures 3 and 4 are poorly explained and lack appropriate baselines for comparison.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The related work section is surprisingly limited. Crucially, the paper fails to adequately acknowledge and contrast itself with kernel-based spectral methods for system identification developed in the control theory community (e.g., kernel subspace identification).
*   **Minor Issues:** Numerous minor issues detract from the overall quality of the paper. These include grammatical errors, inconsistent formatting, and a somewhat verbose writing style. The link to the code repository is helpful but doesn't mitigate the aforementioned issues.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The concept is promising.
*   Theoretical framework is attempted.

**Con:**

*   Lack of substantial novelty.
*   Poor clarity and organization.
*   Weak empirical evaluation.
*   Inadequate comparison to related work.
*   Numerous technical and presentation flaws.



In conclusion, while the authors identify a potentially interesting research direction, the paper is significantly hampered by its lack of clarity, weak empirical validation, and limited novelty. The theoretical framework, while present, is not sufficiently well-explained or connected to practical implementation. The empirical results are insufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to existing alternatives.  I believe substantial revisions, addressing the points raised above, would be necessary before this paper could be considered for acceptance. Currently, I recommend rejection.
