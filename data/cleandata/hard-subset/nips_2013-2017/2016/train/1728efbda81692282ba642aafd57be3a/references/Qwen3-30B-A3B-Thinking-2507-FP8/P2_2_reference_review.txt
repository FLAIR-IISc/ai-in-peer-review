

# Review: "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary
This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The Koopman operator is an infinite-dimensional linear operator that can represent nonlinear dynamics, and DMD is a popular method for approximating its eigenvalues and eigenfunctions. The authors extend DMD by replacing the linear monomials used as basis functions in traditional DMD with kernel functions, allowing for a more expressive representation of the Koopman eigenfunctions. The paper provides a modal decomposition algorithm that operates in RKHS, along with a robust variant that projects data onto POD directions to address numerical instability. The method is validated on synthetic data and applied to state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper relates to recent NeurIPS work on Koopman operator learning, such as Williams et al.'s "A Data-Driven Approximation of the Koopman Operator" (2015) and Jovanović et al.'s "Sparsity-promoting Dynamic Mode Decomposition" (2014), which also extend DMD to improve the representation of Koopman eigenfunctions. The paper also connects to kernel methods in machine learning, particularly kernel PCA and kernel-based approaches to dynamical systems.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a mathematically sound extension of DMD using RKHS. The theoretical foundation is well-developed, with two key theorems (Theorem 1 and Theorem 2) that clearly establish the connection between the proposed algorithm and the Koopman operator theory.
- The algorithmic description is clear and complete, with step-by-step procedures for both the basic and robustified versions of the method.
- The empirical validation is reasonable, with examples on a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map, showing that the method can correctly estimate Koopman eigenvalues.
- The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., potential numerical instability in the basic algorithm) and addresses them with the POD-based robustification, demonstrating awareness of practical implementation challenges.

**Weaknesses:**
- The comparison with existing methods is insufficient. The paper references "Extended DMD" [35] but doesn't clearly articulate how the kernel-based approach differs in meaningful ways or provides demonstrable advantages. A thorough comparison with Extended DMD and other kernel-based Koopman methods would strengthen the quality assessment.
- The empirical results are limited to synthetic examples. While the toy system and Hénon map provide good theoretical validation, the paper lacks substantial real-world applications that would demonstrate the method's practical utility for large-scale or complex dynamical systems relevant to machine learning.
- The computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method aren't adequately discussed. For instance, the Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in RKHS could become computationally prohibitive for large datasets, but this isn't addressed.
- The paper doesn't provide a rigorous analysis of the approximation error when using finite-dimensional RKHS approximations, which is crucial for understanding the theoretical guarantees of the method.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from introduction to related work, methodology, and applications.
- The mathematical notation is consistent, and the theorems are well-explained with appropriate proofs.
- The figures (Fig. 1-4) are helpful in illustrating the results and making the theoretical concepts more concrete.

**Weaknesses:**
- Some sections, particularly Section 3 and Section 4, might be challenging for readers without a strong background in functional analysis or kernel methods. While the paper does a reasonable job of explaining the concepts, more intuitive explanations or examples would improve accessibility.
- The connection between the theoretical framework and practical implementation could be clearer. For instance, the paper mentions that the Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition can be reduced to operations on a Gram matrix, but doesn't provide enough detail on how this is implemented in practice.
- The description of the preimage calculation for prediction (Section 7.1) is somewhat brief and could benefit from more detailed explanation or pseudocode.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The core idea of extending DMD using RKHS to allow for nonlinear basis functions is novel in the context of Koopman spectral analysis. While Extended DMD [35] uses pre-determined basis functions, the RKHS approach provides a more flexible and data-driven way to construct these basis functions.
- The specific application of POD projection to robustify the kernel DMD algorithm is a meaningful contribution that addresses a practical issue known in DMD literature.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from related work. For example, the paper mentions "kernel principal angles" in Section 7.2, which was previously used in "Learning over Sets using Kernel Principal Angles" [Wolf & Shashua, 2005], but doesn't clearly articulate how this specific application to Koopman eigenfunctions is novel.
- The theoretical novelty is somewhat incremental. While the RKHS extension is a good idea, the paper doesn't provide a strong theoretical argument for why this specific approach is fundamentally better than existing methods like Extended DMD.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the proposed method relates to recent NeurIPS work on Koopman operator learning, such as the "Kernel Methods for Pattern Analysis" [Shawe-Taylor & Cristianini, 2004] or "Gaussian Process Dynamical Models" [Wang et al., 2005], which could have provided a stronger theoretical foundation.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The Koopman operator framework has significant potential for applications in machine learning, particularly for modeling nonlinear dynamical systems without linearization.
- The demonstrated applications (prediction, change detection, dynamics recognition) are relevant to machine learning and data analysis, and the paper shows promising results on these tasks.
- The method could potentially be useful for systems where traditional DMD fails due to the limitations of linear basis functions.

**Weaknesses:**
- The significance of the contribution is somewhat diminished by the lack of a clear demonstration of superiority over existing methods. Without a strong empirical comparison showing that the kernel DMD method significantly outperforms Extended DMD or other state-of-the-art methods, it's hard to assess the practical significance.
- The applications shown are relatively simple (toy systems, Hénon map) and don't demonstrate the method's utility on larger, more complex real-world problems that would be of interest to the NeurIPS community.
- The paper doesn't convincingly argue why the RKHS-based approach is necessary for the applications presented. For instance, it's not clear that the kernel DMD method provides better results than Extended DMD for the specific applications shown.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces for Koopman spectral analysis. While the core idea has merit and the mathematical development is solid, the paper falls short in several key areas that would be expected at NeurIPS.

The main weaknesses are the insufficient comparison with existing methods (particularly Extended DMD), limited empirical validation on real-world problems, and insufficient demonstration of the method's practical advantages. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with related work, additional empirical results on larger-scale or more complex datasets, and a clearer articulation of the advantages of the kernel-based approach over existing methods.

The paper is technically sound but lacks the level of empirical validation and clear demonstration of advantages that would be expected for a NeurIPS publication. The contributions are incremental rather than transformative, and the significance is not convincingly demonstrated.

## Recommendation

**Decision:** Reject

**Reasoning:**
While the paper presents a mathematically sound extension of DMD using RKHS, the lack of strong empirical validation, insufficient comparison with existing methods, and limited demonstration of practical advantages prevent it from meeting NeurIPS quality standards. The contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to convince the NeurIPS community that this approach offers significant improvements over existing methods for Koopman spectral analysis.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. A thorough comparison with Extended DMD and other state-of-the-art methods
2. More substantial empirical results on real-world datasets
3. A clearer articulation of how the proposed method differs from and improves upon existing approaches
4. A more detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability

Without these improvements, the paper doesn't provide a sufficiently compelling contribution to the field of machine learning for NeurIPS publication.
