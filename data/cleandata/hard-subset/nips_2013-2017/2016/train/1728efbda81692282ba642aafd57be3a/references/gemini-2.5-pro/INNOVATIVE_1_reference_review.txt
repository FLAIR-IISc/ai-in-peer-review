### Summary

This paper introduces a kernelized version of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems via the Koopman operator. The core idea is to lift the system's state space into a high-dimensional Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) and perform DMD in this feature space. This allows the method, termed Kernel DMD (kDMD), to approximate the Koopman eigenfunctions using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions implicitly defined by the kernel, overcoming the limitations of standard DMD which typically relies on linear observables. The authors first derive a formulation based on a Gram-Schmidt process in the RKHS and then present a more practical and robust algorithm that projects the Koopman operator onto the principal components found by Kernel PCA. The utility of the learned Koopman modes and eigenvalues is demonstrated through several applications: short-term prediction, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and elegant synthesis of three powerful concepts: Koopman operator theory for dynamical systems, Dynamic Mode Decomposition, and the kernel trick. Extending DMD to RKHS is a natural but highly non-trivial step that fundamentally enhances the method's expressive power. It moves DMD from a tool that is often effective for systems with quasi-linear dynamics to one that can, in principle, handle complex, global nonlinearities without requiring the user to hand-engineer a dictionary of basis functions. This is a bold and significant conceptual leap.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear and sound theoretical development. The connection between the Arnoldi method interpretation of DMD and the proposed kernelized algorithm is well-established. Theorem 1 provides the foundation for the direct kernel extension, while Theorem 2 provides the justification for the more practical, robust algorithm based on Kernel PCA, connecting the eigenvectors of the projected operator back to the true Koopman eigenfunctions. This demonstrates a solid grasp of the underlying mathematics.

3.  **Potential for High Impact:** This work opens up a powerful new tool for the machine learning and scientific communities. By framing Koopman analysis as a kernel method, it makes it accessible and applicable to a wide range of problems where nonlinear dynamics are crucial. The idea of using the learned Koopman eigenfunctions as features for downstream tasks (classification, change detection) is compelling and aligns well with the goals of representation learning. This method has the potential to become a standard non-parametric technique for data-driven analysis of dynamical systems.

4.  **Demonstration of Versatility:** The authors go beyond simply presenting the algorithm and illustrate its potential through a diverse set of applications on both synthetic and real-world data (locomotion capture). This shows the breadth of problems the proposed framework can address and strengthens the paper's contribution by grounding the theoretical work in practical use-cases.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Relationship to Extended DMD (eDMD):** The paper briefly mentions Williams et al. [35] (eDMD), which also extends DMD to a dictionary of user-defined basis functions. The connection is very strong; kDMD can be seen as a specific instance of eDMD with an implicit, infinite-dimensional dictionary provided by the kernel. While the proposed method is more general and elegant by avoiding the need to explicitly choose basis functions, the paper could have benefited from a more detailed comparison and discussion of the relationship between the two methods to better situate its own novelty.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation is Illustrative, Not Exhaustive:** While the experiments successfully demonstrate the method's capabilities, they lack quantitative rigor and strong baselines.
    *   For prediction on the Hénon map, no quantitative error metrics (e.g., RMSE) are provided, nor is there a comparison against other standard time-series prediction models (e.g., Gaussian Process Dynamical Models).
    *   The change-point detection experiment is compared to a single kernel-based method, but not against a wider suite of established change-point algorithms.
    *   The embedding for locomotion data is visually compelling, but a quantitative classification experiment would have been more convincing.
    This lack of rigorous benchmarking makes it difficult to assess the practical performance of kDMD relative to the state of the art in these specific application domains.

3.  **Practical Limitations are Not Discussed:** The paper does not address some of the well-known practical challenges of kernel methods. The construction of the Gram matrix scales quadratically with the number of time points (`τ`), which could be prohibitive for long time series. Furthermore, the prediction task relies on solving the pre-image problem, which is notoriously difficult and ill-posed for many kernels. While the authors employ a reasonable MDS-based approach, a discussion of the limitations, computational complexity, and potential pitfalls of this step is missing.

### Recommendation: Accept

This paper introduces a highly novel, conceptually powerful, and elegant idea: the fusion of Koopman spectral analysis with kernel methods. This is precisely the kind of bold, forward-thinking research that NIPS should encourage. The method provides a principled way to extend DMD to handle complex nonlinear systems without requiring manual feature engineering, opening up a significant new direction for data-driven modeling.

While the experimental validation is more of a proof-of-concept than a rigorous benchmark, and the discussion of practical limitations could be more thorough, these are minor flaws in the context of the paper's main contribution. The core idea is significant enough to stand on its own and will likely inspire a great deal of follow-up work to explore its theoretical properties and practical performance more deeply. The paper prioritizes a groundbreaking concept over methodological perfection, and in this case, the trade-off is well worth it. I am confident that this work will be of great interest to the community and will have a lasting impact.
