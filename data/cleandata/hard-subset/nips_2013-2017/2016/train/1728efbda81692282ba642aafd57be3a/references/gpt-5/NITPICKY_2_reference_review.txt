Summary
The paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in an RKHS by (i) building a Krylov subspace from kernel feature maps of snapshots, (ii) projecting the (feature-space) Koopman operator onto this subspace, and (iii) extracting empirical eigenvalues/vectors via eigendecomposition. A “robustified” variant projects onto kernel PCA (POD) directions to mitigate ill-conditioning. The method is used for three applications: short-term prediction via pre-image reconstruction, recognition of dynamical patterns via kernel principal angles between subspaces spanned by estimated eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-style criterion derived from a kernel exponential family model. Experiments on a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, CMU MoCap sequences, and a Lorenz-map change-point scenario are provided.

Relation to prior work
Conceptually, this is very close to the Extended DMD (EDMD) framework and its kernelized variant (Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, J. Nonlinear Science 2015), which already formulates Koopman operator approximation via Galerkin projections on user-chosen bases and shows how to do this with kernels. The robustification via POD is also standard in the DMD literature (exact DMD, SVD truncation). The submission cites [35] but does not adequately delineate the novelty relative to EDMD/kernel-EDMD or other kernelized DMD variants. Several statements and algorithmic choices overlap known results without a clear differentiation.

Strengths
- Timely topic: Koopman/operator-theoretic analysis and DMD variants remain active areas, and kernelization is practically useful.
- The RKHS projection viewpoint and Gram-matrix-only implementation are appealing for high-dimensional observables.
- The robustification via kernel PCA is sensible in practice and aligns with best practices in DMD.
- The applications (prediction/pre-image, subspace-based recognition, and change detection) demonstrate potential breadth.

Weaknesses (technical correctness and rigor)
- Theorem 1 and its proof are problematic. The matrix F = R^{-1}PτR (with Pτ = Q*KHQ) is not, in general, a companion matrix, and there is no justification that T built as a Vandermonde of the eigenvalues diagonalizes F. The Vandermonde/companion argument only holds for specific Krylov/monomial bases and special recurrences; for a generic Arnoldi/Hessenberg projection in a Hilbert space, the eigenvector matrix is not Vandermonde. Consequently, the step “φ̃j are columns of V = Mτ T^{-1}” is not justified. This undermines Eq. (9) and the ensuing algorithmic step (3)–(4).
- The statement “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle [20]” is inaccurate in general; for dissipative systems one expects eigenvalues inside the unit disk (decay), while unit-circle spectra arise under measure-preserving dynamics. This conceptual inaccuracy recurs in the narrative.
- Theorem 2 relies on centered Gram matrices and expressions with Ḡ^{-1} without assumptions on invertibility or regularization; the use of H Ḡ^{-1} H is unconventional and needs careful derivation (typically a pseudo-inverse and regularization are required). The algebra leading to U* M+ H Ḡ^{-1} H M* U = F̂ is not clearly justified.
- The pre-image approach is ad hoc. The MDS-style pre-image does not have guarantees, and distance relationships in RKHS generally require careful regularization. No analysis of when the pre-image exists or is unique is provided.
- The change-point detection formulation via a kernel exponential family and the simplification of the log-likelihood ratio by “ignoring the second term” are heuristic, with no bounds on approximation error or detection performance guarantees. The link to stochastic Koopman operators is only mentioned but not developed rigorously.

Weaknesses (empirics and evaluation)
- Experiments are minimal and largely qualitative. There is no quantitative comparison to EDMD or kernel EDMD, despite their high relevance. No error metrics for eigenvalues/eigenfunctions, reconstructions, or predictions are reported, and no sensitivity to kernel parameters, truncation ranks, or regularization is studied.
- Figures are incomplete/inconsistent: Fig. 2 is referenced but missing; there are garbled plot axes/labels (e.g., “2 3 4 5 6 7 0 .2 .4 .6 .8”), and captions lack detail. It is difficult to assess results.
- The MoCap recognition experiment lacks details: number/length of sequences, preprocessing, classification protocol (train/test split, cross-validation), and quantitative accuracy are not given. The MDS plot is not a substitute for a classification evaluation.
- The change-point experiment has insufficient baselines and no quantitative metrics (detection delay, false alarm rate). The compared “kernel change detection” method is not precisely specified and no parameters are reported.

Clarity and presentation
- The manuscript has numerous grammatical errors and unclear sentences (e.g., “a way to robustly it”), inconsistent notation (ϕ vs φ; KH vs K; vectors vs functions), and definitions missing normalizing constants (e.g., ϕc should include 1/τ). Many references are incomplete (missing years, venues, pages), have typos in names, and do not follow a consistent style.
- Several mathematical steps are asserted without proof or with insufficient conditions (distinct eigenvalues, finite-dimensional spans, invertibility), and the role of centering in kernel PCA vs operator approximation is not clearly separated.
- Algorithmic complexity, memory footprint, and numerical stability/regularization (e.g., Tikhonov on Gram matrices) are not discussed.

Originality
- The core idea—approximating the Koopman operator in RKHS using Gram matrices and a POD-truncated subspace—is very close to kernel EDMD. The paper does not make a clearly novel theoretical contribution beyond prior art, and some of the claimed derivations are incorrect or insufficiently justified.

Significance
- While the topic is important, the current contribution does not convincingly advance the state of the art due to theoretical issues and weak empirical validation.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem with a potentially practical kernel-based formulation.
- Attempts to connect Koopman analysis with subspace methods (kernel PCA) and applications.

Cons
- Flawed/unsupported theoretical claims (notably Theorem 1 and unit-circle statement).
- Overlap with established EDMD/kernel-EDMD without clear novelty.
- Sparse and qualitative empirical evaluation, missing key baselines and metrics.
- Numerous presentation, notation, and referencing issues.

Questions for authors
- Precisely state how your method differs from kernel EDMD (formulation, guarantees, computational advantages). Can you benchmark directly against it?
- Can you correct the Theorem 1 derivation or remove the Vandermonde/companion assumption and present a valid result?
- What regularization do you use for Gram matrices and inverses/pseudo-inverses? How sensitive are results to kernel width and truncation rank?
- Provide quantitative evaluations (eigenvalue error on systems with known spectra, prediction RMSE, classification accuracy, change-point detection metrics).

Recommendation
Reject. Despite an interesting and relevant direction, the paper suffers from significant theoretical inaccuracies, unclear novelty relative to kernel EDMD, weak and incomplete experiments, and substantial clarity/formatting issues.

Confidence
High.
