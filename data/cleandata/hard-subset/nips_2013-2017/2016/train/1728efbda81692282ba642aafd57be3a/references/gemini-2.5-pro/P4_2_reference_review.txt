**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), a method termed kernel DMD (kDMD). The core idea is to lift the system's state space into a high-dimensional feature space defined by a kernel function, where the action of the Koopman operator can be approximated. Standard DMD is limited as it approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear combination of observables. By using kernels, the proposed method can implicitly work with a much richer, nonlinear set of basis functions, potentially leading to more accurate approximations of the Koopman spectrum and eigenfunctions for highly nonlinear systems.

The authors present two algorithmic variants. The first is a direct translation of the DMD principle to RKHS using Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space. Recognizing that this approach can be numerically unstable, they propose a second, more robust algorithm. This robust version is based on projecting the Koopman operator onto a subspace spanned by the principal components found via kernel Principal Component Analysis (kernel PCA). This is analogous to the common practice of stabilizing DMD by projecting onto Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) modes. The paper provides theoretical justification connecting the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the resulting finite-dimensional matrix approximation to the true Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions. The utility of the learned spectral features is demonstrated through applications in state prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

This work builds upon the well-established literature of DMD [25, 26] and Koopman operator theory [16]. It connects these ideas from physics and dynamical systems with the powerful framework of kernel methods [28] from machine learning. The work is also closely related to Extended DMD (eDMD) [35], which uses a user-defined dictionary of basis functions; kDMD can be viewed as a method that implicitly defines this dictionary through the kernel and the data points.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled Extension:** The paper presents a well-motivated and natural extension of DMD. Moving from a linear space of observables to an RKHS is a logical step to capture complex nonlinearities, and the authors provide a clear mathematical formulation for this extension.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical development is sound. The derivation of the robust algorithm using kernel PCA is clear and well-justified. Theorems 1 and 2 formally connect the computed Ritz values/vectors from the algorithms to the underlying Koopman operator's spectral properties, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed methods.

3.  **Practicality:** The authors correctly identify the potential numerical instability of a direct Gram-Schmidt approach in RKHS and propose a robust alternative based on kernel PCA. This demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of practical implementation and makes the method more likely to be applicable in real-world scenarios.

4.  **Demonstrated Utility:** The paper showcases the versatility of the learned representation through three distinct and relevant applications: prediction (requiring a pre-image step), embedding/recognition of dynamics, and change-point detection. These examples effectively illustrate that the extracted Koopman modes can serve as powerful features for downstream machine learning tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Comparison with Related Work:** The relationship to Extended DMD (eDMD) [35] is mentioned, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed comparison. Kernel DMD can be interpreted as a specific instance of eDMD where the dictionary consists of kernel functions centered at the data points, i.e., `{k(x_i, ·)}`. This connection should be made explicit, and the relative advantages and disadvantages should be discussed. For instance, kDMD automates the choice of basis functions (given a kernel), whereas eDMD requires domain knowledge to specify a dictionary. Conversely, kDMD's dictionary size scales with the data size, which has implications for computational cost that are not fully addressed. An empirical comparison against eDMD with a well-chosen dictionary on one of the tasks would be highly valuable.

2.  **Scalability:** The proposed robust algorithm relies on the construction and eigendecomposition of Gram matrices of size `τ x τ`, where `τ` is the number of data points. The computational complexity is at least O(τ²), which will be prohibitive for long time series or large datasets. This is a common limitation of kernel methods, and a discussion of this issue, along with potential approximation techniques (e.g., Nyström method, random features), would make the paper more complete.

3.  **Experimental Rigor:** The experiments, while illustrative, lack some quantitative depth and comparison to baselines.
    *   For the prediction task (Section 7, Figure 2), the paper only shows qualitative plots. Providing quantitative metrics like Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) over increasing prediction horizons would allow for a more objective assessment of performance. A comparison against relevant baselines (e.g., standard DMD, an RNN-based model, or a Gaussian Process Dynamical Model) would be necessary to demonstrate the method's advantages.
    *   The choice of hyperparameters (kernel type, kernel parameters, truncation rank `p`) is critical for any kernel-based method. The paper does not discuss how these were selected or the method's sensitivity to them. This information is crucial for reproducibility and for understanding the practical deployment of the method.
    *   In the change-point detection application, the simplification of the log-likelihood ratio score is justified with a brief statement. More theoretical or empirical validation for this approximation would be beneficial.

4.  **Clarity:** While generally well-written, the description of the prediction via pre-image is quite brief. More detail on the MDS-based pre-image algorithm [18] and its implementation would improve the clarity and reproducibility of this application.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and elegant synthesis of DMD and kernel methods.
*   The method is theoretically well-founded and addresses a key limitation of standard DMD.
*   The proposed robust algorithm is practical and applicable.
*   The paper demonstrates the potential of the method across several interesting applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation is largely qualitative and lacks comparisons to key baselines, particularly eDMD.
*   Important practical aspects, such as scalability and hyperparameter selection, are not discussed.
*   The novelty is somewhat incremental when viewed in the context of eDMD.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and well-reasoned contribution to the literature on learning dynamical systems. The fusion of Koopman theory with kernel methods is a powerful concept. The primary weaknesses lie in the lack of a thorough empirical evaluation against state-of-the-art methods and a detailed discussion of the method's practical limitations. Despite these shortcomings, the novelty and technical quality of the core idea make it a strong candidate for the conference. The paper would be significantly improved if the authors addressed the points above in a final version.
