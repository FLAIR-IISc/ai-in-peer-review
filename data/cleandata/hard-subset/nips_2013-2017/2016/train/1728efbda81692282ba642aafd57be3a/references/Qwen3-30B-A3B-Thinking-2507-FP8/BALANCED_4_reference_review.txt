

# Review: Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors aim to overcome limitations of traditional DMD, which uses linear monomials as basis functions (analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion), by employing kernel functions to provide richer, nonlinear basis representations. The paper presents theoretical extensions of DMD to RKHS, a robust implementation via POD projection, and demonstrates applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work
The paper builds upon the Koopman operator framework, which provides a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics. While DMD has been widely used to approximate Koopman eigenvalues and modes, it relies on linear basis functions. The authors position their work as a significant advancement by using kernel methods to obtain nonlinear basis functions, which they argue can better capture complex dynamics. This relates to Williams et al.'s Extended DMD (2015), which also uses pre-determined basis functions, but the authors claim their kernel-based approach provides a more expressive representation. The paper also connects to kernel PCA and other spectral methods for dynamical systems.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous mathematical derivations, including two key theorems establishing the connection between the proposed method and Koopman spectral analysis. The extension to RKHS is well-motivated and theoretically grounded.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors address a known issue with DMD (ill-conditioned implementations) by proposing a robustification method using POD bases, which is a practical contribution that makes the method more applicable in real scenarios.

3. **Clear Applications**: The paper demonstrates the utility of the proposed method through three meaningful applications: short-term prediction via preimage estimation, dynamics recognition using kernel principal angles, and sequential change-point detection. These applications are well-motivated and relevant to the target audience.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The toy system and Hénon map examples provide reasonable validation of the method's ability to recover Koopman eigenvalues. The locomotion data example demonstrates practical utility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Clear Novelty**: While the paper claims to provide "nonlinear basis functions" beyond traditional DMD, the relationship to Extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) is not sufficiently clarified. The authors state that "the resulting procedure is similar to the robust-version of our algorithm," but don't clearly articulate how their approach differs from or improves upon Extended DMD. A more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods is needed.

2. **Insufficient Experimental Comparison**: The paper lacks comprehensive experimental results comparing the proposed method with existing DMD variants (including Extended DMD). The empirical examples are limited to toy systems and do not demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods in terms of accuracy, computational efficiency, or scalability. The Hénon map results show some approximation of eigenvalues but don't quantify the improvement over traditional DMD.

3. **Practical Limitations Not Addressed**: The paper doesn't adequately discuss practical challenges of using kernel methods, such as kernel selection, computational complexity, or scalability to large datasets. The use of polynomial and Gaussian kernels is not justified, and no ablation studies explore the impact of kernel choice.

4. **Overstated Significance**: The paper claims the method "could be considered as a feature extraction procedure for a nonlinear dynamical system," but doesn't convincingly demonstrate how these features lead to better performance in applications compared to standard approaches. The locomotion recognition example uses kernel principal angles, which is a standard technique, and doesn't clearly show that the Koopman-based features are superior to other feature representations.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD using RKHS, with a practical robustification method. However, the novelty is not sufficiently established relative to existing work, and the empirical validation is limited. The paper would benefit significantly from:

1. A detailed comparison with Extended DMD and other kernel-based DMD methods, including quantitative metrics showing clear advantages.
2. A more thorough discussion of practical considerations, including computational complexity, kernel selection strategies, and scalability.
3. Additional experiments on larger or more complex datasets to demonstrate the method's practical utility.
4. A clearer articulation of how the kernel-based approach differs from and improves upon existing methods.

The paper makes a contribution to the Koopman spectral analysis literature, but its significance would be greatly enhanced with more comprehensive experimental validation and clearer differentiation from related work. The current version does not sufficiently demonstrate that the proposed method provides meaningful improvements over existing approaches.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro (for acceptance):**
- Strong theoretical foundation with well-established mathematical derivations
- Practical robustification approach addressing a known issue in DMD
- Demonstrated applications to prediction, change detection, and dynamics recognition
- Well-organized and clearly written

**Con (for rejection):**
- Insufficient experimental comparison with state-of-the-art methods
- Unclear novelty relative to Extended DMD and other kernel-based approaches
- Limited empirical validation that doesn't convincingly demonstrate advantages
- Overstated significance without sufficient evidence

The paper has merit but requires substantial revision to address the gaps in experimental validation and novelty demonstration before it would be suitable for publication at NIPS. The authors should focus on providing a more rigorous comparison with existing methods and better articulating the unique contributions of their approach.
