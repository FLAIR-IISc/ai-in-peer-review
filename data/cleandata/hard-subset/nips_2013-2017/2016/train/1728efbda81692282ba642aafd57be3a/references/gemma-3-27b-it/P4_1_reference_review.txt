This paper introduces a Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) method extended with reproducing kernels (RKHS-DMD) to perform Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using nonlinear basis functions derived from kernel methods, offering a potential advantage over traditional DMD which relies on linear monomials. The authors provide a theoretical foundation for the method, a robust implementation addressing potential ill-conditioning through POD projection, and empirical validation on synthetic and real-world data, including applications to state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the motivation, theoretical background, algorithm details, and experimental results. It builds upon existing work in DMD and Koopman operator theory, properly referencing relevant publications (e.g., [25, 26, 16, 33, 8]). The extension to RKHS is a logical progression, aiming to address the limitations of linear basis functions in capturing complex nonlinear dynamics. The inclusion of POD projection to improve robustness mirrors established practices in DMD. The variety of applications demonstrated provides a good indication of the method’s potential utility.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the RKHS-DMD algorithm is presented with reasonable mathematical rigor, establishing a clear connection to the Koopman operator and DMD framework. Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 provide a solid foundation for understanding the method’s behavior.
*   **Addressing Practical Challenges:** Recognizing the potential for ill-conditioning, the authors proactively incorporate POD projection to stabilize the algorithm. This is a practical consideration that enhances the usability of the method.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation covers both synthetic data (allowing for ground truth comparison) and real-world data (demonstrating applicability). The multiple application examples showcase the versatility of the extracted features.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a logical flow of ideas and clear explanations of the key concepts.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:**  The authors adequately situate their work within the context of related research, referencing important papers in DMD, Koopman theory, and kernel methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** While the application of RKHS to DMD is a natural extension, the core idea isn't radically new. Kernel methods are commonly used in dimensionality reduction and function approximation, and applying them to the Koopman framework feels incremental. The paper does not sufficiently highlight what specifically differentiates this approach from related techniques, like kernel PCA combined with DMD.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of kernel methods is highly sensitive to kernel parameter selection (e.g., kernel width). The paper does not provide a detailed discussion of how these parameters are chosen or the impact of different parameter settings on the results.
*   **Computational Cost:**  Kernel methods often have higher computational complexity than linear methods, especially for large datasets. The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the RKHS-DMD algorithm and how it scales with the size of the data or the dimensionality of the feature space. A comparison to standard DMD in terms of runtime would be valuable.
*   **Empirical Comparison:** The comparison to other methods (e.g., standard DMD, kernel change detection) is somewhat limited.  More extensive comparisons, including statistical significance tests, would strengthen the empirical validation.
*   **Application Depth:** The applications presented, while diverse, are relatively shallow.  A more in-depth analysis of the performance and limitations of RKHS-DMD within each application domain would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a logically sound and well-motivated extension of DMD using kernel methods.
*   The inclusion of POD projection addresses a practical issue in DMD and enhances the algorithm’s robustness.
*   The empirical evaluation provides evidence of the method’s effectiveness on a variety of datasets and applications.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the approach is limited, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from existing work.
*   The parameter sensitivity and computational cost of kernel methods are not adequately addressed.
*   The empirical comparison to other methods is not comprehensive enough.
*   The applications are presented at a relatively high level and lack in-depth analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of dynamical systems modeling. However, the incremental novelty and lack of extensive comparative analysis raise concerns.  While the technical soundness and clarity are commendable, the paper falls short of demonstrating a significant advance over existing techniques. A revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly a more thorough comparative analysis and discussion of computational cost and parameter sensitivity, would substantially improve the paper’s impact.

**Recommendation:** Borderline Reject. I recommend rejection in its current form, but would encourage resubmission after addressing the identified weaknesses.
