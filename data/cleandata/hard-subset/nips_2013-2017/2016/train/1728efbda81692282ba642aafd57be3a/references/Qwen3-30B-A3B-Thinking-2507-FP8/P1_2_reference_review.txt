

# Peer Review: "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors present a theoretical framework that replaces the linear monomial basis functions used in standard DMD with nonlinear kernel-based basis functions, allowing for more expressive approximations of Koopman eigenfunctions. The paper develops a modal decomposition algorithm that works in the feature space of a kernel, with a robustified version using POD bases to address numerical stability issues. The authors demonstrate their method on synthetic data (a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map) and show applications in state prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

The work builds on the well-established Koopman operator theory and DMD framework, extending previous work such as Williams et al.'s Extended DMD, which also uses pre-determined basis functions. The paper makes a clear connection between the kernel-based approach and the Koopman spectral analysis, providing theoretical guarantees through theorems and demonstrating empirical results on simple systems.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for extending DMD to RKHS, with well-structured theorems (Theorems 1 and 2) that establish the connection between the kernel-based approach and Koopman spectral analysis. The mathematical derivations are sound and carefully presented.

2. **Practical Robustification**: The authors address a known issue with DMD (numerical instability) by proposing a robust version using POD bases, which is a practical and well-motivated modification that improves the algorithm's applicability.

3. **Clear Applications**: The paper demonstrates several meaningful applications of the extracted features (prediction via preimage, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection), showing the utility of the proposed method beyond just theoretical analysis.

4. **Well-Organized Structure**: The paper is well-structured, with a logical flow from theoretical background to algorithm development, practical considerations, and empirical validation.

5. **Good Related Work Coverage**: The paper adequately references relevant work, including DMD, Koopman operator theory, and kernel methods, providing context for the proposed approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Empirical Validation**: The empirical evaluation is limited to simple synthetic datasets (a toy system and the Hénon map). There is no comparison with state-of-the-art methods on more complex or real-world datasets. The paper would benefit from showing how the method performs on larger, more challenging systems where linear approximations fail.

2. **Lack of Comparative Analysis**: The paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison with existing approaches like Extended DMD [35] or other kernel-based methods for dynamical systems. It's unclear how the proposed method compares in terms of accuracy, computational efficiency, or scalability.

3. **Insufficient Discussion of Computational Complexity**: The computational cost of the kernel-based approach is not adequately addressed. For large datasets, kernel methods can become computationally prohibitive (O(n^3) complexity for Gram matrix inversion), which is a significant drawback that the authors should discuss.

4. **Underdeveloped Applications**: While the applications section shows promise, the empirical results don't convincingly demonstrate that the kernel-based approach provides substantial advantages over standard DMD for these tasks. For instance, the prediction results in Figure 2 show reasonable performance but don't compare against baseline methods.

5. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper uses specific kernels (polynomial for the toy system, Gaussian for the Hénon map) but doesn't explore how the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters affects the results. This is particularly important for kernel-based methods.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound, with correct mathematical derivations and clear theorems. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the robustification approach using POD bases is well-motivated. However, the empirical validation is limited to synthetic examples, which doesn't fully support the claim that the method is useful for real-world applications. The paper doesn't adequately address the computational challenges of kernel methods for larger datasets, which is a significant limitation for practical use.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The theoretical concepts are presented logically, and the connection between standard DMD and the kernel-based extension is well-explained. The empirical examples are clearly presented with appropriate figures. However, some sections (particularly the robustification part in Section 4) could benefit from more detailed explanations of the practical implementation steps.

## Originality

The extension of DMD to RKHS is a novel idea, though it builds on existing work in kernel methods and Koopman analysis. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from Extended DMD [35] by working directly in the functional space rather than using pre-determined basis functions. However, the paper could better articulate how this approach differs from other kernel-based methods for dynamical systems (e.g., [15, 17, 19]). While the core idea is novel, the novelty is somewhat incremental given the existing literature on kernel-based dynamical system analysis.

## Significance

The method has potential significance for nonlinear dynamics analysis, particularly for systems where linear approximations (like standard DMD) are insufficient. The applications section demonstrates promising directions for using the extracted features. However, the paper doesn't convincingly show that the method provides significant improvements over existing approaches for real-world problems. The significance would be much stronger with more comprehensive empirical validation on larger, more complex datasets.

## Specific Comments

1. **Figure 1**: The eigenvalue estimation results are promising for the toy system, but it's unclear how well the method performs on the Hénon map (which is chaotic) compared to standard DMD. A quantitative comparison would strengthen this result.

2. **Section 6**: The empirical example section is limited to two simple examples. I recommend including results on a more complex real-world dataset (e.g., from the CMU motion capture database mentioned in Section 7) to demonstrate the method's applicability to practical problems.

3. **Section 7**: The applications section shows some promising results, but the paper doesn't compare the performance of the proposed method against standard baselines for each application (e.g., standard DMD for prediction, standard kernel methods for change-point detection). A comparative analysis would strengthen the claims about the method's utility.

4. **Computational Complexity**: The paper should include a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method, especially how it scales with the size of the dataset. The O(n^3) complexity of kernel methods for large n could be a significant limitation for practical applications.

5. **Kernel Selection**: The paper uses specific kernels for each example but doesn't discuss how to select appropriate kernels for new applications. A brief discussion on kernel selection strategies would be helpful for practitioners.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHS, which is a meaningful contribution to Koopman spectral analysis.
- The robustification approach using POD bases addresses a practical issue with DMD, making the method more applicable.
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
- The applications section demonstrates potential utility beyond basic spectral analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The empirical validation is limited to simple synthetic datasets without comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper doesn't adequately address the computational challenges of kernel methods for larger datasets.
- The significance of the method over existing approaches isn't convincingly demonstrated.
- The applications section lacks comparative performance metrics against baseline methods.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to Koopman spectral analysis, but the empirical validation is insufficient to demonstrate the method's practical utility. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including more comprehensive experiments on larger, more complex datasets with comparisons to relevant baselines. Without this, it's difficult to assess the true value of the proposed method.

The authors should address the weaknesses I've identified, particularly by providing more thorough empirical validation and a better discussion of computational complexity and kernel selection. If these issues can be addressed, the paper would be a strong contribution to the field.

## Additional Suggestions

1. Include a comparison with Extended DMD [35] on the toy system and Hénon map to demonstrate the advantages of the kernel-based approach.

2. Add a section discussing computational complexity and scalability, including runtime measurements on datasets of varying sizes.

3. Provide more details on how to select appropriate kernels for different dynamical systems.

4. Include results on a real-world dataset (e.g., from the CMU motion capture database mentioned in Section 7) to demonstrate practical utility.

5. Add a discussion of the limitations of the method, particularly in terms of computational cost for large datasets.
