Summary
The paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The central idea is to transport the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) machinery to feature space via kernels: (i) construct a Krylov subspace spanned by ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1), (ii) compute a Gram-Schmidt QR factorization of the feature-space data matrix using kernel evaluations, (iii) form and diagonalize the projected Koopman operator to obtain empirical Ritz values/vectors. The authors also provide a “robustified” variant that projects onto kernel principal components (kernel POD), akin to the truncated SVD step in practical DMD, and show that the eigenpairs of the projected operator recover Koopman eigenpairs under strong assumptions. They illustrate the method on a toy system and the Hénon map, and sketch applications to short-term prediction via preimage estimation, dynamics recognition via subspace distances (kernel principal angles), and sequential change-point detection using a kernel exponential family.

Relation to prior work
DMD approximates Koopman spectra from data; exact/optimized DMD address numerical issues. Extended DMD (EDMD) generalizes DMD to user-chosen dictionaries; using kernels yields a compact representation of large dictionaries. The proposed method is essentially EDMD in RKHS with an Arnoldi/QR perspective, and the robustified variant corresponds to projecting onto kernel PCA directions, paralleling practical DMD’s SVD truncation. The paper cites EDMD [Williams et al.] and related DMD variants but does not clearly articulate how its algorithm and theorems differ materially from (kernel) EDMD/KDMD other than the Arnoldi viewpoint and some implementation details.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: A clean derivation of kernelized DMD via Krylov subspaces and Gram-Schmidt in feature space, and a projection-based variant with kernel PCA. Theorems 1–2 formalize the approximation and connection to Koopman eigenpairs.
- Practicality: The robust variant allows rank truncation and uses only Gram matrices, enabling application to high-dimensional observations.
- Breadth of applications: The paper outlines how learned Koopman modes/eigenfunctions can support prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition in a unified framework.
- Accessibility: The algorithmic steps are explicit, and a code link is provided.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to EDMD/KDMD: The core contribution overlaps significantly with EDMD (projection of the Koopman operator on a finite span of observables) and its kernelized formulations. The Arnoldi/QR derivation is elegant but seems largely equivalent in outcome to kernel EDMD. The paper should make differences precise (e.g., stability, sample complexity, numerical properties) and experimentally demonstrate advantages.
- Theoretical assumptions: The guarantees require strong conditions (e.g., eigenfunctions lie in Φ, distinct Ritz values, data sufficiently rich so that κj ∈ span(U), ψ=0). No discussion of how these conditions relate to realistic sampling, noise, and model misspecification; no convergence or consistency results under ergodicity/mixing.
- Limited empirical evaluation: Experiments are restricted to a toy system and the Hénon map with qualitative plots. There are no quantitative metrics (e.g., eigenvalue/eigenfunction estimation error, reconstruction/prediction error), no comparisons to DMD, exact/optimized DMD, EDMD, or KDMD, and no analysis of robustness to noise, trajectory length, or kernel choice.
- Applications are underdeveloped: The preimage step is acknowledged to be difficult and is handled via a heuristic MDS-style procedure without evaluation versus known preimage approaches or baselines; the change-point method is heuristic (dropping terms), with no detection performance metrics; the recognition task lacks classification accuracy, ablations, or baselines.
- Some inaccurate/general statements: “For a system evolving on an attractor, Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is not generally true; this holds for measure-preserving/unitary settings, while dissipative systems have eigenvalues within the unit disk.
- Clarity and polish: Notation is dense in places; some proofs are sketchy; references are incomplete/irregular; figures are small and not fully interpretable; grammar issues.

Quality
Technically sound in its derivations under stated assumptions, but incomplete in supporting claims with thorough experiments. The robustified algorithm is reasonable and parallels best practices in DMD. However, the empirical section does not substantiate advantages or practical guidance (e.g., kernel/regularization selection, truncation criteria, computational complexity).

Clarity
Readable with effort; the main pipeline is understandable. The paper would benefit from tightening notation, clearly contrasting with EDMD/KDMD, and adding algorithmic complexity and numerical stability discussions. Improve figure readability and captions. Fix the statement about eigenvalues on attractors.

Originality
Incremental. The core idea aligns closely with EDMD in RKHS; the Arnoldi viewpoint is a different lens rather than a distinct algorithmic advance. The application sketches are not sufficiently developed to constitute novel contributions.

Significance
Potentially useful as a self-contained exposition and implementation of kernelized Koopman spectral analysis with a DMD/Arnoldi flavor. As written, the lack of rigorous comparisons and quantitative results limits impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely position the method relative to EDMD/KDMD and exact/optimized DMD; explain differences and expected benefits. Include complexity and memory comparisons.
- Provide quantitative benchmarks: eigenvalue/mode accuracy on systems with known spectra (e.g., linear systems, Duffing/Van der Pol), reconstruction/prediction error curves versus τ and noise; ablations on kernel, bandwidth, and truncation level; comparisons to DMD, EDMD, KDMD.
- Add regularization (e.g., Tikhonov) and discuss numerical conditioning; report sensitivity to kernel parameters and propose selection strategies (e.g., cross-validation).
- Either provide a stronger preimage method with evaluation or focus the paper on spectral estimation and downstream tasks that do not require preimage.
- For change detection and recognition, include baselines, metrics, and datasets; clarify the theoretical basis of the simplified CUSUM criterion.
- Fix inaccuracies and polish references; add missing details to enable reproduction.

Pros
- Clear kernelized reinterpretation of DMD with derivations and implementable steps.
- Practical robust variant via kernel PCA/POD.
- Unifying view for several downstream tasks.

Cons
- Overlaps significantly with EDMD/KDMD; novelty unclear.
- Sparse and largely qualitative experiments; no baselines.
- Strong assumptions with limited discussion of practicality.
- Some technical inaccuracies and presentation issues.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper has a solid idea and could be a useful resource, but to meet NeurIPS standards it needs a clearer differentiation from existing kernel EDMD/KDMD, stronger theoretical framing, and substantially more rigorous and comparative empirical validation.
