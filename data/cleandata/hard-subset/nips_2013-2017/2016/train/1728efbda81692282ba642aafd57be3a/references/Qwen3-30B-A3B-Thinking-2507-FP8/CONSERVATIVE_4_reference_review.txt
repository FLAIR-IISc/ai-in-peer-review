

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors argue that traditional DMD, which uses linear monomials as basis functions, provides only a local approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions, and that their kernel-based approach can provide richer, more expressive basis functions. The method involves computing the Krylov subspace in RKHS, followed by an eigendecomposition of the projection of the Koopman operator onto this subspace.

The paper is well-organized and technically sound in its mathematical formulation. The authors provide clear theorems (Theorems 1 and 2) that establish connections between their kernel-based approach and the Koopman operator. The paper also includes empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets, including a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, and locomotion data.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical foundation is solid, with proper mathematical formulation of the kernel-based extension of DMD.
- The paper is well-written with clear explanations of the mathematical concepts.
- The proposed method is theoretically motivated and appears to be a natural extension of existing DMD approaches.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of rigorous empirical validation:** The paper presents limited experimental comparison against established baselines. The authors claim their method "provides a modal decomposition of the dynamics into a finite number of modes" and "could be considered as a feature extraction procedure," but they fail to demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods. The empirical results in Figure 1 show reasonable performance on synthetic data, but there is no comparison to standard DMD, extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), or other kernel-based methods. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the kernel approach provides meaningful advantages.

2. **Questionable novelty:** The paper references Williams et al.'s "extended DMD" which works with pre-determined basis functions, and the proposed method appears conceptually similar. The authors claim their approach is "analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion," but the kernel-based method is simply using a different class of basis functions. The novelty is overstated, as the core technique is a straightforward extension of existing methods rather than a fundamentally new approach.

3. **Insufficient discussion of limitations:** The paper mentions that the Gram-Schmidt approach can be "ill-conditioned" and proposes a robustification using POD, but fails to discuss how often this occurs in practice or how the robustified method compares to standard DMD variants like exact DMD or optimized DMD. The paper also doesn't address the computational cost of the kernel approach, which is typically higher than linear methods.

4. **Weak empirical evidence for applications:** The applications section (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) shows promising results but doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the kernel approach provides significant advantages over simpler methods. For example, the prediction results in Figure 2 could potentially be achieved with standard DMD or even linear methods. The change-point detection results in Figure 4 show similar performance to the kernel change detection method, without clear evidence of improvement.

5. **Lack of theoretical guarantees:** While the paper presents a theoretically sound algorithm, it doesn't provide theoretical guarantees about the approximation quality of the kernel-based approach compared to linear DMD, nor does it discuss the conditions under which the kernel method would be expected to outperform linear methods.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods, but the novelty is limited and the empirical validation is insufficient to demonstrate clear advantages over established approaches. The paper fails to provide rigorous comparisons against relevant baselines, which is particularly concerning given the authors' claim that their method "provides a richer approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions." Without compelling evidence that this approach outperforms existing methods in meaningful ways, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.

The paper would benefit from:
1. More comprehensive empirical comparisons against established DMD variants and kernel-based methods
2. A clearer discussion of when the kernel approach would be expected to outperform linear methods
3. Analysis of computational complexity and scalability
4. Discussion of limitations and failure cases

While the mathematical formulation is correct, the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that this method provides meaningful improvements over existing techniques that would justify its adoption in practice. The lack of thorough empirical validation against established baselines is particularly concerning for a paper claiming to advance the state of the art in Koopman spectral analysis.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper presents a theoretically plausible extension of existing methods but fails to provide sufficient evidence of significant practical advantages over established approaches. The contribution appears incremental rather than transformative, and the empirical validation is insufficient to support the authors' claims.
