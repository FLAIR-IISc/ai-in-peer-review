

# Peer Review: "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors argue that traditional DMD, which approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials of observables, is limited to local approximations, and propose a method that uses kernel functions to provide richer, nonlinear basis functions. The paper develops a modal decomposition algorithm based on the Krylov subspace in RKHS and introduces a robustification technique using POD (Principal Orthogonal Decomposition) to address numerical instability. The authors demonstrate applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition on synthetic and real-world data.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, correctly situating its work within Koopman operator theory and DMD, and citing key related work including Williams et al.'s Extended DMD [35], which also uses pre-determined basis functions. The paper acknowledges the connection between their approach and Extended DMD, noting that "the resulting procedure is similar to the robust-version of our algorithm."

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation with well-constructed proofs (Theorems 1 and 2) establishing the connection between the kernel-based approach and Koopman spectral analysis. The extension of DMD to RKHS is mathematically sound.

2. **Practical Robustness**: The authors address a critical limitation of DMD (ill-conditioned algorithms) by incorporating POD-based robustification, which is a practical and well-known technique in the DMD literature. This makes the proposed method more applicable to real-world data.

3. **Clear Applications**: The paper demonstrates several meaningful applications of the method (state prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition), showing the practical utility of the approach.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The empirical results on both synthetic (toy system, Hénon map) and real-world data (locomotion data, Lorenz map) provide reasonable validation of the proposed method.

5. **Good Organization**: The paper is well-structured, with clear sections that flow logically from theoretical background to proposed method, robustification, applications, and empirical results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees**: While the paper provides a theoretical foundation, it lacks rigorous guarantees about the approximation quality of the method. For instance, there's no analysis of how the choice of kernel affects the approximation error or bounds on the error as a function of the number of data points. This is a significant gap in a paper claiming to provide a new spectral analysis method.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper mentions Extended DMD [35] as related work but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of how the proposed method differs or improves upon it. The claim that "the resulting procedure is similar to the robust-version of our algorithm" is too vague. The authors should directly compare with Extended DMD, kernel-based methods for dynamical systems [15, 35], and other recent Koopman approximation techniques to establish the novelty and advantages of their approach.

3. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method. Kernel methods typically have O(n^3) complexity for matrix inversion (where n is the number of data points), which could be a major drawback for large-scale applications. This is a significant omission, especially since the paper claims to be applicable to real-world data.

4. **Kernel Selection Guidance**: The paper uses specific kernels (polynomial for the toy system, Gaussian for Hénon map) without providing guidance on how to choose an appropriate kernel for a given problem. This is a critical issue for kernel methods, as the choice of kernel can dramatically affect performance. The paper doesn't discuss kernel selection strategies or sensitivity to kernel parameters.

5. **Limited Empirical Comparison**: The empirical results (Figures 1-4) lack comparisons with strong baselines. For example, Figure 1 compares with "Equilibrium DMD" but not with Extended DMD or other state-of-the-art methods. The prediction results in Figure 2 don't compare with standard time-series prediction methods (e.g., ARIMA, LSTM), making it difficult to assess the relative performance of the proposed method.

6. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper assumes that Koopman eigenfunctions can be represented in the RKHS, but doesn't discuss the conditions under which this assumption holds. Not all Koopman eigenfunctions may lie in the RKHS, which is a fundamental limitation of the approach.

7. **Missing Ablation Studies**: The paper lacks ablation studies to demonstrate the impact of different components of the method (e.g., the effect of the kernel choice, the robustification step). This makes it difficult to determine which aspects of the method are most important.

## Specific Technical Comments

1. **Section 3 (Kernel DMD)**: The extension of DMD to RKHS is conceptually sound, but the connection to Extended DMD [35] is not sufficiently detailed. Extended DMD already uses a set of pre-determined basis functions (not just linear monomials), so the main difference appears to be the use of kernel functions to define these basis functions. The paper should clarify how their approach differs from and improves upon Extended DMD.

2. **Section 4 (Robustifying with POD Bases)**: The robustification technique is well-motivated, but the paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why this approach works. The mathematical derivation is correct but dense.

3. **Section 6 (Empirical Example)**: The results on the toy system are convincing, but the results on the Hénon map (Figure 1) are ambiguous. The right graph shows "kernel DMD" and "Equilibrium DMD" eigenvalues, but it's unclear how these compare to the true eigenvalues. The paper should provide more quantitative metrics (e.g., error in eigenvalue estimation) rather than just visual comparisons.

4. **Section 7 (Applications)**: The applications are well-chosen, but the change-point detection results (Figure 4) lack a clear demonstration of the advantage of the proposed method over existing change-point detection methods. The comparison with "the kernel change detection method" (referenced in the caption) should be more explicit.

5. **References**: The paper cites relevant work, but could include more recent papers on kernel-based Koopman methods. For example, the work by [15] on kernel-based spectral analysis of dynamical systems is not cited.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHS with practical robustification, and demonstrates promising applications. However, the lack of rigorous theoretical guarantees, insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and omission of computational complexity analysis are significant weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong contribution.

The paper would be significantly improved by:

1. Providing a detailed comparison with Extended DMD [35] and other state-of-the-art Koopman approximation methods
2. Including a thorough analysis of computational complexity and scalability
3. Adding ablation studies to demonstrate the importance of different components of the method
4. Providing more detailed guidance on kernel selection
5. Including quantitative performance metrics rather than just visual comparisons

Without these improvements, the paper's contribution appears to be incremental rather than transformative, and it's difficult to assess the practical advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Theoretical foundation is sound with correct proofs
- Addresses a known limitation of DMD (ill-conditioning) through robustification
- Demonstrates practical applications with empirical validation
- Well-organized and clearly written
- Makes a meaningful contribution to the extension of DMD to nonlinear basis functions

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Lacks rigorous theoretical guarantees about approximation quality
- Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods (particularly Extended DMD)
- No computational complexity analysis, which is critical for kernel methods
- No guidance on kernel selection, a critical aspect of kernel methods
- Limited empirical comparison with strong baselines
- Theoretical limitations (Koopman eigenfunctions may not lie in RKHS) are not adequately addressed

In conclusion, while the paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD, it falls short of making a compelling case for the novelty and advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches. With the suggested improvements, this could be a strong contribution to the field, but as it stands, it is a marginal improvement over existing methods.
