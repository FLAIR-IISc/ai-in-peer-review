This paper presents a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), aiming to improve the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions beyond linear monomials. The core idea – leveraging the expressiveness of RKHS to capture more complex dynamics – is compelling and conceptually sound. The connection to existing DMD methods is well-articulated, and the proposed algorithm is mathematically grounded. The empirical validation, while limited, demonstrates promising results on both a toy system and the Henon map.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core contribution – applying RKHS to DMD for richer Koopman eigenfunction approximation – is genuinely novel. While kernel methods are pervasive in machine learning, their direct application to extending DMD in this manner hasn’t been widely explored. This moves beyond incremental improvements.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear mathematical derivation of the proposed method, linking it back to the established theory of DMD and the Koopman operator. The theorems establish the correctness of the approach.
* **Potential Significance:** If successfully scaled, this method could unlock the ability to analyze and model more complex nonlinear dynamical systems where linear approximations break down. The potential applications – prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition – are relevant and impactful.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors do a good job of explaining the core concepts and the algorithm's steps.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental section feels somewhat cursory. While the toy system and Henon map provide initial validation, they are relatively simple examples. The results on the Henon map are described as "approximately estimated", which raises concerns. A more comprehensive evaluation on higher-dimensional, real-world datasets is crucial.
* **Computational Cost:** Kernel methods are notoriously computationally expensive, especially for large datasets. The paper doesn't thoroughly address the scalability of the proposed algorithm. The POD projection is a good step, but a more in-depth discussion of computational complexity and potential optimizations is needed.
* **Kernel Selection:** The choice of kernel (polynomial and Gaussian) appears somewhat arbitrary. A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different kernel parameters and types would strengthen the results. A discussion about how to choose an appropriate kernel for a given dynamical system is missing.
* **Application Depth:** The application section demonstrates potential, but lacks depth. The prediction results, in particular, aren't presented with sufficient detail to assess their effectiveness. The sequential change-point detection example is also brief.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to extending DMD with a strong theoretical foundation.
* The core idea has the potential to significantly impact the field of nonlinear dynamical systems analysis.
* The preliminary results are promising and warrant further investigation.
* The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods and position their work as a meaningful contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical validation is limited and doesn’t fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on complex, real-world datasets.
* The computational cost and scalability concerns are not adequately addressed.
* The application section feels underdeveloped and lacks sufficient detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a bold and potentially groundbreaking idea. While the empirical validation is currently limited, the novelty and theoretical soundness of the approach are compelling. I believe the potential benefits of this work outweigh its current weaknesses. I lean towards acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the scalability concerns and providing a more comprehensive empirical evaluation in a revised version. Specifically, I would like to see results on larger, more complex datasets and a more detailed analysis of the computational performance of the algorithm. The authors should also expand on the application section, providing more concrete examples and quantitative results.



**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.
