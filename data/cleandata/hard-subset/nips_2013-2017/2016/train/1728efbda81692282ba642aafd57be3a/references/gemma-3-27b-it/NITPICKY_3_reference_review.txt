This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions, aiming to overcome limitations of traditional DMD relying on linear monomials. While the core idea of employing RKHS for Koopman spectral analysis is conceptually sound and builds upon established techniques, the presented manuscript suffers from a multitude of shortcomings, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to substantial concerns regarding practical implementation and demonstrable benefit.

The introduction adequately contextualizes the problem, referencing relevant literature on dynamical systems modeling and spectral methods. However, the motivation for transitioning to RKHS isn’t sufficiently compelling. The argument that DMD's linear basis functions are insufficient "beyond the region of validity for local linearization" lacks specific examples illustrating where this limitation significantly hinders performance. The paper would benefit from a more focused discussion on scenarios where RKHS-based DMD demonstrably outperforms existing methods.

Section 2 provides a reasonable review of the Koopman operator and DMD. Section 3, detailing the RKHS extension, contains several areas of concern. The mathematical derivations, while generally correct, are presented without sufficient justification for certain steps. The discussion surrounding the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization and its reduction to kernel operations feels cursory, and could benefit from a more detailed explanation for readers unfamiliar with kernel methods. The proof of Theorem 1, while technically accurate, relies on assumptions (distinct eigenvalues) that are not always guaranteed and lack discussion of robustness to near-degeneracy. Furthermore, the statement that the eigenfunctions are “fully recovered” with a large enough Krylov subspace is overly optimistic and ignores practical limitations related to computational cost and numerical conditioning.

Section 4 attempts to address the ill-conditioning problem via projection onto POD directions. While this is a common practice in DMD, the connection between the theoretical framework of RKHS-DMD and the practical implementation using POD isn't fully elucidated. The use of kernel PCA feels somewhat ad-hoc; a clearer justification for this specific choice of projection technique is needed. Theorem 2, while mathematically sound, feels almost trivially derived given the preceding sections and lacks significant insight.

The related work section is surprisingly brief, particularly given the extensive literature on both DMD and kernel methods. Important references, such as kernelized DMD variants and recent advances in Koopman operator learning, are missing. The claim that the proposed method is analogous to extended DMD [35] requires more detailed comparison and justification. The lack of detailed comparison with existing kernel-based DMD approaches is a significant weakness.

Section 6 presents empirical results on a toy system and the Henon map. The results are encouraging but insufficient to demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. The choice of kernel parameters (degree 3 polynomial, Gaussian width 1) appears arbitrary and lacks sensitivity analysis. The comparison with standard DMD is missing, making it difficult to assess the performance gain (if any) achieved by using RKHS. Moreover, the claim that the algorithm "precisely estimated" the eigenvalues for the toy system is somewhat misleading, as the accuracy isn't quantified. The empirical section should include comparisons with other relevant approaches (e.g., sparse DMD, extended DMD).

Section 7’s applications are described in broad strokes, lacking sufficient detail and quantitative evaluation. The discussion of prediction via preimage reconstruction feels incomplete, and the connection to MDS isn't fully explained. The change-point detection example requires more detailed explanation of the experimental setup and a comparison with established change-point detection methods. The figures are small and difficult to interpret. The inclusion of error bars and statistical significance tests is essential.

Minor issues include inconsistent notation (e.g., the use of both ϕ and φ to represent eigenfunctions), occasional grammatical errors, and a lack of formatting consistency. The link to the Matlab code is appreciated, but the code itself should be more thoroughly documented and tested.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Lack of compelling demonstration of superiority over existing DMD methods.
*   Insufficiently detailed mathematical derivations and justifications.
*   Limited and poorly analyzed empirical results.
*   Brief and incomplete related work section.
*   Numerous minor technical and presentational flaws.
*   The connection between the theory and practical implementation (POD projection) feels weak.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The core idea of extending DMD with RKHS is conceptually valid.
*   The mathematical framework is generally correct.
*   The paper introduces a novel combination of techniques.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the proposed approach, the paper’s numerous weaknesses outweigh its strengths. The lack of convincing empirical evidence, combined with the technical shortcomings and incomplete presentation, make it difficult to justify acceptance to NIPS. A substantial revision addressing the aforementioned concerns would be necessary for reconsideration. I recommend **rejection**.
