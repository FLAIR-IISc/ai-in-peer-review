### Paper Summary

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), a method the authors term kernel DMD (kDMD). The core idea is that standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear combination of observables, which can be insufficient for highly nonlinear systems. By lifting the system's state into an RKHS via a kernel function, the authors formulate a linear Koopman operator in the high-dimensional feature space. This allows for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions with a much richer set of nonlinear basis functions.

The paper presents two algorithmic variants. The first is a direct formulation based on a Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in the feature space, which is shown to be theoretically sound (Theorem 1). Recognizing that this direct approach can be numerically unstable (an issue also present in standard DMD), the authors propose a second, more robust algorithm. This practical variant uses kernel Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to project the data onto a truncated set of principal directions before performing the decomposition, effectively regularizing the problem. Theorem 2 provides a theoretical link between the eigenfunctions found by this robust procedure and the true Koopman eigenfunctions. The utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features is demonstrated through several applications: short-term prediction via a pre-image reconstruction, recognition of dynamic patterns in motion capture data, and sequential change-point detection.

### Review

This is a well-written and technically solid paper that presents a natural and powerful extension of DMD using kernel methods. It successfully bridges concepts from the physics/fluid dynamics community with mainstream machine learning techniques, resulting in a flexible and useful tool for nonlinear systems analysis.

**Quality: High**
The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development in Sections 3 and 4 is clear and the provided theorems rigorously establish the connection between the proposed algorithms and the Koopman operator framework. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the practical pitfalls of spectral methods by explicitly addressing the numerical instability of the naive formulation and proposing a robust variant based on kernel PCA. This is analogous to the best practices in the standard DMD literature (e.g., using SVD truncation) and shows a mature approach to the problem.

The experimental validation is convincing. The toy examples in Section 6 serve as an effective sanity check, showing that the method can recover the true eigenvalues of a known nonlinear system. The applications in Section 7 are well-chosen to demonstrate the versatility and utility of the extracted Koopman features. The prediction, embedding/recognition, and change-point detection tasks showcase how kDMD can be used as a powerful feature extraction front-end for various downstream machine learning problems.

A minor weakness in the evaluation is the limited comparison to competing methods. While the change-point detection experiment includes a baseline, the prediction and recognition tasks do not compare against other state-of-the-art time-series models (e.g., Gaussian Process Dynamical Models, LSTMs, or even standard DMD/Extended DMD). Including such comparisons would further strengthen the paper's claims.

**Clarity: High**
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The authors provide a concise but sufficient background on Koopman operators and DMD, making the paper accessible to readers not deeply familiar with the topic. The logical flow from the core theoretical idea (Section 3) to the practical, robust implementation (Section 4) is easy to follow. The mathematical notation is consistent and the algorithms are laid out clearly. The figures are illustrative and support the main claims of the paper effectively.

One area for potential improvement would be a more detailed discussion of the relationship with Extended DMD (EDMD) by Williams et al. (2015). The robust version of kDMD, which uses kernel principal components as a basis, is conceptually very similar to EDMD, which uses a pre-defined dictionary of basis functions. The key advantage of kDMD is that the basis is learned from the data in a principled way via the kernel. Explicitly framing kDMD as a data-driven, non-parametric approach to basis selection for EDMD-like methods would help to better position the work and highlight its novelty.

**Originality: High**
The core contribution—the formulation of DMD within an RKHS—is a novel and elegant idea. While "kernelizing" algorithms is a familiar theme in machine learning, its application to DMD for Koopman analysis is a significant and non-trivial contribution. It provides a principled way to move beyond the linear observable space of standard DMD without having to manually engineer a dictionary of nonlinear functions, which is the main limitation of methods like EDMD.

The applications, particularly the use of kernel principal angles between subspaces of Koopman eigenfunctions for dynamics recognition and the formulation of a change-point score, are also original and demonstrate the creative application of the proposed framework. The work clearly distinguishes itself from previous contributions by providing a more powerful, flexible, and data-driven tool for Koopman analysis.

**Significance: High**
The paper presents a significant contribution with the potential for broad impact. It provides a practical and powerful tool for the analysis of nonlinear time-series data, which is ubiquitous across many fields (e.g., robotics, control theory, neuroscience, finance, and climate science). Researchers and practitioners are likely to find this method useful for extracting meaningful, dynamically-relevant features from complex datasets.

By connecting DMD to the well-established framework of kernel methods, the authors open the door for future work exploring different kernel families, multiple kernel learning for dynamics, and online/streaming versions of kDMD. The method advances the state of the art in data-driven discovery of dynamical systems by providing a more expressive and automated alternative to existing techniques.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods.
2.  The method is practically useful, with the paper providing a robust algorithm that addresses potential numerical instabilities.
3.  The work is very clearly written and well-motivated.
4.  The applications are diverse and compelling, demonstrating the significance and versatility of the extracted features for real-world tasks.
5.  The paper effectively bridges concepts from physics and machine learning, making it a great fit for the NIPS audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The experimental comparison with other state-of-the-art methods for time-series analysis could be more comprehensive.

**Conclusion:**
Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a valuable and original method for nonlinear systems analysis. The technical quality, clarity, and significance are all high. The minor weakness in experimental comparison does not detract from the importance of the core contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
