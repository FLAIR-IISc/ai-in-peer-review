### **Summary**

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to lift the system's state into a high-dimensional feature space using a kernel function, where the nonlinear dynamics can be approximated by a linear Koopman operator. The authors propose an algorithm, kernel DMD (kDMD), to compute the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions (Koopman modes) of this operator. They first present a direct formulation based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the RKHS. Recognizing its potential numerical instability, they then propose a more robust version that projects the dynamics onto the principal components in the feature space, which is equivalent to performing Kernel PCA before the DMD-like analysis. The utility of the extracted Koopman spectra as features is demonstrated through several applications: short-term prediction, recognition of dynamic patterns (locomotion), and sequential change-point detection.

### **Review**

This is a well-written and technically solid paper that presents a natural and powerful extension of a popular data analysis technique. The combination of Koopman theory, DMD, and kernel methods is elegant, and the proposed applications effectively demonstrate the method's value for machine learning tasks. While the core algorithmic idea has strong parallels with prior work, the paper provides a clear, self-contained formulation and showcases its utility in novel ways.

#### **Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Soundness:** The theoretical development is sound. The extension of the DMD principle to an RKHS is well-founded, and Theorem 1 provides a clear analogue to the standard DMD formulation in the feature space. The derivation of the robustified algorithm in Section 4 is particularly strong; recognizing the need for regularization via projection (in this case, onto kernel principal components) shows a mature understanding of the numerical challenges inherent in these methods. The connection between the eigenvectors of the projected operator and the true Koopman eigenfunctions, established in Theorem 2, provides a solid theoretical justification for the approach.
*   **Completeness:** The work is a complete contribution. It presents the theory, develops a practical algorithm, and validates it on both synthetic and real-world data across multiple applications. The inclusion of the robust version (Section 4) is crucial, as the direct Gram-Schmidt method would likely be too fragile for many real-world problems.
*   **Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is good. The toy examples clearly illustrate that the method can recover the correct eigenvalues. The applications are well-chosen and demonstrate the practical utility of the extracted features. The authors are reasonably careful in their presentation, though a more in-depth discussion of hyperparameters and limitations would be beneficial (see weaknesses).

#### **Clarity**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

*   The background sections (Sections 2 & 5) effectively situate the work within the broader context of dynamical systems analysis, DMD, and related spectral methods.
*   The progression from the basic principle (Section 3) to the robust, practical algorithm (Section 4) is logical and easy to follow.
*   The mathematical notation is consistent and the derivations are presented with sufficient detail for an expert to understand. The algorithm steps are clearly laid out.

#### **Originality**

This is the paper's main weakness. The proposed method, particularly the robust version in Section 4, is very closely related to "Extended DMD" (eDMD) by Williams et al. [35]. In eDMD, the state is augmented with a dictionary of basis functions; if one chooses the dictionary to be the kernel feature maps of the data points, eDMD becomes functionally equivalent to the kDMD algorithm presented here.

The authors acknowledge eDMD as "similar" in the related work section, but this understates the degree of overlap. The novelty of the core algorithm is therefore limited. However, the paper's contributions can be seen from a different perspective:

1.  **Formulation:** It provides a clear, principled derivation of this algorithm from the specific viewpoint of "kernelizing" DMD, which is an insightful and valuable contribution in its own right.
2.  **Applications:** The paper's strength lies in framing the method as a feature extractor and demonstrating its power on downstream machine learning tasks. The application to sequential change-point detection, with its derivation based on a nonparametric exponential family, appears to be a novel and significant use of Koopman spectra. The dynamics recognition via kernel principal angles is also a compelling application.

Thus, while the core algorithm may not be entirely novel, its formulation and the demonstrated applications are original and significant for the NIPS community.

#### **Significance**

The paper's significance is high.

*   DMD is an increasingly popular technique in physics and engineering, but its application in mainstream machine learning has been limited, partly due to its linear-observable formulation. This paper provides an accessible and powerful kernel-based extension that makes the technique much more applicable to the general nonlinear problems common in ML.
*   The idea of using Koopman eigenfunctions as features for dynamical systems is very powerful. This paper provides a practical algorithm to extract these features and demonstrates their utility for tasks like classification (recognition) and anomaly detection (change-points). This could inspire further work on feature engineering for time-series and dynamical data.
*   The results, especially on the locomotion and Lorenz map data, are compelling and suggest that kDMD is a useful tool for real-world data analysis. Researchers working with complex time-series data are likely to build upon these ideas.

#### **Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Extension:** The paper presents a very natural kernelization of DMD, providing a solid theoretical bridge between Koopman theory and kernel methods.
2.  **Practical Robustness:** The inclusion of the Kernel PCA-based projection makes the algorithm practical and numerically stable, a crucial step for real-world applicability.
3.  **Strong Application-Oriented Focus:** The paper excels in demonstrating how to *use* the output of the spectral analysis. The applications in recognition and change-point detection are particularly relevant and novel for the NIPS audience.
4.  **High Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

#### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarification of Novelty:** The authors absolutely must include a more detailed discussion of the relationship between their method and eDMD [35]. They should explicitly state whether their robust algorithm is a special case of eDMD (with a specific kernel-based dictionary) and clarify what their contribution is in that context. This is crucial for proper academic attribution.
2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a discussion on the choice of crucial hyperparameters, such as the kernel type, kernel parameters (e.g., Gaussian width), and the truncation rank `p` for the POD/Kernel PCA projection. An analysis of the method's sensitivity to these choices would significantly strengthen the paper's practical value.
3.  **Scalability:** The method relies on constructing and decomposing Gram matrices of size τ x τ, where τ is the number of data points. The computational complexity should be explicitly stated (O(τ³)), and the authors should briefly comment on the method's scalability to very long time series.

### **Final Recommendation**

Despite the significant overlap with prior work on eDMD, I lean towards acceptance. The paper's clear formulation, practical robustification, and compelling demonstration of the extracted Koopman modes as features for downstream machine learning tasks make it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. The work serves as an excellent bridge between the DMD literature in physics and the kernel methods literature in machine learning.

My recommendation is contingent on the authors addressing the main weakness by adding a thorough and honest comparison with Extended DMD in the final version.
