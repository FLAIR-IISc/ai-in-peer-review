### **Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"**

**Summary**

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The central idea is to address a key limitation of standard DMD, which approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear combination of observables. By leveraging the kernel trick, the proposed method implicitly works with a much richer, nonlinear set of basis functions, aiming for a more accurate approximation of the Koopman operator's spectrum and eigenfunctions for nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors present two algorithmic variants: a direct formulation based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space, and a more robust version that projects the data onto kernel principal components before analysis. The utility of the extracted Koopman modes as features is demonstrated through applications in state prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

**Strengths**

1.  **Conceptual Soundness:** The motivation for this work is strong and well-founded. Extending DMD to RKHS is a natural and elegant way to generalize the method from linear observables to a rich class of nonlinear functions. The theoretical connection between the standard DMD framework (based on Krylov subspaces) and its kernelized counterpart is clearly established.

2.  **Methodological Consideration:** The authors correctly identify the potential for numerical instability in a naive implementation of kernel DMD (Section 3) and propose a robustification strategy based on kernel PCA (Section 4). This mirrors the development of robust DMD variants in the literature (e.g., projecting onto POD modes) and demonstrates a practical understanding of the underlying numerical challenges.

3.  **Potential for Application:** The paper explores several interesting applications beyond simple spectral analysis, treating the learned Koopman modes as features for downstream tasks. This highlights the potential significance of the method as a tool for feature extraction in time-series and dynamical systems analysis.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** My primary concern with this paper is the lack of rigorous empirical comparison to existing, state-of-the-art methods. The paper critiques standard DMD for its reliance on linear observables but never presents an experiment where standard DMD fails and the proposed method succeeds. More importantly, the relationship to Extended DMD (eDMD) [35] is significantly understated. The robust version of the proposed algorithm (Section 4), which uses kernel PCA for projection, appears to be a special case of eDMD where the dictionary of observables is implicitly defined by the kernel feature map. A thorough comparison against eDMD, using a well-chosen dictionary of basis functions (e.g., polynomials, radial basis functions, or Fourier features), is essential to demonstrate a clear advantage. Without this, the novelty and practical benefit of the proposed kernel-based formulation remain unproven.

2.  **Unconvincing Experimental Validation:** The empirical results provided are not strong enough to fully support the paper's claims.
    *   **Hénon Map (Fig 1):** The results for the chaotic Hénon map are presented as an approximate estimation, but the plot shows a wide scatter of eigenvalues with no clear correspondence to the known eigenvalues of the system's equilibrium points. This result is inconclusive at best and does not build confidence in the method's ability to handle complex chaotic dynamics.
    *   **Prediction (Fig 2):** While prediction on the toy system is good, the performance on the Hénon map is mediocre, especially for the `x2` component. While long-term prediction in chaotic systems is difficult, the short-term results are not compelling enough to be a major selling point without stronger baselines.
    *   **Change-Point Detection (Fig 4):** This experiment is particularly weak. It is based on a single trial run. The proposed method's change score exhibits a large spike around t=600, where no change occurred, which appears to be a significant false positive. The authors argue that the baseline method incorrectly responds to changes in data range, but their own method's performance on this single example is questionable. This application requires a much more systematic evaluation (e.g., multiple runs, quantitative metrics like detection delay and false alarm rate) to be credible.

3.  **Clarity of Assumptions:** Theorem 2, which grounds the robust algorithm, relies on the assumption that the Koopman eigenfunctions (represented by `κj` in the feature space) lie within the subspace spanned by the top kernel principal components. This is a strong assumption that essentially requires the data to be "sufficiently rich" and the kernel to be well-chosen. The paper does not adequately discuss the implications of this assumption, how sensitive the method is to its violation, or provide guidance on how to choose the kernel and the truncation dimension `p` to ensure it holds.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a conceptually elegant and well-motivated extension of DMD to kernel spaces.
*   The theoretical derivation is clear and connects the proposed method back to the principles of DMD and Koopman theory.
*   The work explores a range of interesting applications for the learned spectral features.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty is questionable in light of Extended DMD (eDMD), to which the proposed method is closely related but not adequately compared.
*   The experimental validation is insufficient and unconvincing. The results on standard benchmarks (Hénon map) are weak, and the application experiments lack rigor and comparison to relevant baselines.
*   The claims of the paper are not fully substantiated by the provided evidence. A method proposed as an improvement over existing techniques must demonstrate this improvement empirically.

**Conclusion**

While the core idea of this paper is appealing, the execution falls short of the standards required for publication at NIPS. The work's primary weakness is the lack of a rigorous and convincing empirical evaluation, particularly the absence of comparisons to established and highly relevant baselines like Extended DMD. The experiments that are presented are anecdotal and do not provide strong evidence for the method's superiority or robustness. Therefore, I recommend rejection in its current form. The paper could be significantly strengthened by conducting a thorough experimental study comparing Kernel DMD against standard DMD and eDMD on a variety of dynamical systems, and by providing a more robust evaluation of the proposed applications.
