### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a popular data-driven method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to lift the system's state space into a high-dimensional Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) and perform DMD in this feature space. This allows the method to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using a rich, nonlinear basis implicitly defined by the kernel, in contrast to standard DMD which typically uses linear observables.

The authors propose two algorithms. The first is a direct formulation based on a Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in the RKHS, which is shown to be theoretically sound (Theorem 1). Recognizing the potential numerical instability of this approach, they propose a second, more robust algorithm. This version first projects the data onto its principal components in the feature space (via kernel PCA) and then approximates the Koopman operator on this reduced-rank subspace (Theorem 2). This is analogous to the "exact DMD" or POD-projected DMD variants in the standard literature. The paper concludes by demonstrating the method's utility on synthetic and real-world data across several applications: short-term prediction, recognition of dynamic patterns, and sequential change-point detection.

This work fits well within the growing literature on data-driven methods for Koopman analysis. It directly extends the line of work on DMD [Schmid, JFM 2010; Tu et al., JCD 2014]. Its closest relative is arguably Extended DMD (eDMD) [Williams et al., JNS 2015], which generalizes DMD to a user-defined dictionary of observable functions. The proposed kernel DMD can be seen as a non-parametric version of eDMD, where the kernel function implicitly defines an infinite-dimensional dictionary of observables, a standard and powerful technique in machine learning. The robust version also shares strong conceptual similarities with subspace identification methods and spectral learning algorithms [Kawahara et al., NIPS 2011].

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Generalization:** The fusion of Koopman operator theory, DMD, and kernel methods is a natural and powerful combination. It provides a principled way to move beyond the limitations of linear observables in standard DMD without requiring the user to manually engineer a suitable dictionary of nonlinear functions, as is necessary for eDMD.

2.  **Solid Theoretical Grounding:** The paper develops its methodology clearly, starting from the definition of the Koopman operator in an RKHS. Theorems 1 and 2 provide a formal justification for the proposed algorithms, establishing a clear link between the computed eigenvalues/vectors and the true Koopman spectra, under reasonable assumptions. This provides a solid foundation for the method.

3.  **Practicality and Robustness:** The authors astutely identify the numerical instability of a naive QR-based implementation in the feature space—an issue that plagues many DMD variants. The proposed robustification via kernel PCA projection is a well-motivated and practical solution that makes the algorithm viable for real-world data, which is often noisy or rank-deficient.

4.  **Demonstration of Versatility:** The paper goes beyond a simple proof-of-concept on eigenvalue recovery. The application sections on prediction, dynamics embedding/recognition, and change-point detection are compelling. They effectively showcase how the learned Koopman modes can serve as a powerful feature representation for a variety of downstream tasks, highlighting the potential significance of the work.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with eDMD:** The most critical missing piece is a direct comparison to Extended DMD. Since eDMD can also employ nonlinear basis functions (e.g., polynomials, radial basis functions), it stands as the most relevant state-of-the-art competitor. A discussion and, ideally, an empirical comparison are needed to understand the trade-offs. For instance, when is the non-parametric kernel approach superior to a carefully chosen finite dictionary in eDMD, and what are the relative computational costs?

2.  **Scalability Concerns Not Addressed:** A major drawback of kernel methods is their computational complexity, which typically scales as O(τ³) in time and O(τ²) in memory, where τ is the number of data points. This could make the proposed method prohibitive for the long time-series data often encountered in fluid dynamics or neuroscience, where DMD is popular. The paper does not discuss these limitations or potential approximations (e.g., Nyström method) to mitigate them.

3.  **Evaluation in Applications is Preliminary:** While the applications are interesting, their evaluation is more illustrative than rigorous.
    *   For **prediction**, the non-trivial pre-image problem is handled by citing a method, but its accuracy and potential pitfalls in this context are not discussed. Furthermore, the predictions are not compared against standard time-series forecasting models (e.g., GPs, LSTMs).
    *   For **change-point detection**, the derivation of the decision rule relies on an assumption ("the variation of the second term is much smaller") that needs better justification. The empirical result in Figure 4 is difficult to interpret without more statistical analysis or comparison across multiple trials.
    *   For **dynamics recognition**, the MDS embedding is visually appealing, but a quantitative evaluation (e.g., classification accuracy) would provide a stronger demonstration of the features' utility.

4.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** The paper is generally well-written, but a few points could be clarified. For example, a more explicit formula for evaluating a learned Koopman eigenfunction `φ_j(x)` on a new, unseen point `x` would be helpful for practitioners wanting to use the method. While derivable from the equations, stating it clearly would improve the paper's accessibility.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Quality:** The core theoretical work is sound. The connection between the QR-based and PCA-based versions is clear and well-motivated. The weakness lies in the empirical validation, which feels more like a proof-of-concept than a thorough scientific evaluation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are easy to follow. Section 7 on applications could benefit from more detail on the experimental setups to aid reproducibility.
*   **Originality:** The idea of kernelizing DMD is novel and significant. While it builds on a rich history of both DMD and kernel methods, the synthesis is original and contributes a new, powerful tool to the field of data-driven dynamical systems analysis.
*   **Significance:** The work has high potential significance. It provides a more automated and potentially more powerful alternative to existing methods like eDMD for analyzing complex nonlinear dynamics. Researchers in physics, engineering, and machine learning are likely to find this method useful and build upon it. The demonstrated applications are relevant to many NIPS attendees.

### Final Recommendation

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel and well-founded method. The combination of DMD and kernel methods is elegant and offers a significant step forward in the data-driven analysis of nonlinear systems. The primary weaknesses relate to the lack of comparison with the most relevant baseline (eDMD) and the preliminary nature of the application studies. However, the strengths—the novelty of the core idea, the solid theory, and the demonstrated potential—outweigh these weaknesses. This paper would be a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the comparison to eDMD and discuss the method's scalability in the final version.
