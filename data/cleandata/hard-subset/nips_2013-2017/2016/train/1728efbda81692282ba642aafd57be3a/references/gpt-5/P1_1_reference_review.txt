Summary
The paper proposes a kernelized formulation of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors define the Koopman operator in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), construct a Krylov subspace from feature-mapped snapshots, and compute Ritz eigenvalues/vectors via a Gram–Schmidt QR factorization and eigendecomposition of a projected operator. To address numerical ill-conditioning, they propose a robust variant that projects onto kernel-PCA (POD) directions, yielding a computable finite-dimensional operator F̂ in terms of Gram matrices. They establish two theorems linking the computed Ritz pairs to Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions under assumptions. They then demonstrate applications: short-term prediction via preimage in input space, recognition via subspace angles between estimated Koopman eigenspaces, and sequential change detection via a CUSUM-like statistic in a kernel exponential-family model. Empirical illustrations include a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, motion-capture locomotion embeddings, and a Lorenz change-detection example. Code is provided.

Relation to prior work
- DMD and its variants (exact/optimized/sparsity-promoting) are well covered ([25,26,33,8,13]).
- Extended DMD (EDMD) [35] is the closest related method: it projects the Koopman operator onto a span of chosen dictionary functions and solves a finite-dimensional eigenproblem using Gram matrices. The proposed robust kernel-POD variant is essentially an RKHS version of EDMD with a data-adapted basis (kernel PCA), and the operator F̂ has the same form as EDMD’s A vs. G generalized eigenproblem. The paper mentions EDMD, but the equivalence is not made precise and no comparisons are given.
- The kernel trick for Koopman/EDMD has been explored in the Koopman literature after DMD; convergence and consistency of EDMD have also been analyzed (e.g., Korda & Mezić; Klus et al.). These are not cited.
- The preimage problem and kernel subspace angles are appropriately referenced ([17,36]).

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension: casts DMD in RKHS and derives a fully kernelizable algorithm that only requires Gram matrices.
- The robust kernel-POD projection is practical and aligns with stabilized DMD practice; the operator F̂ is computable and scalable to moderate τ.
- Theorems 1 and 2 provide a clear link between computed Ritz pairs and Koopman spectra (under assumptions), and the algebra for F̂ is correct and implementable.
- Multiple downstream applications suggest broader utility; code availability aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to EDMD is limited. The robust kernelized algorithm and its operator algebra are very close to EDMD with kernels and POD truncation. The paper does not formally relate its derivations to EDMD’s generalized eigenproblem nor provide empirical comparisons against EDMD/exact DMD.
- Mathematical rigor is uneven:
  - In Theorem 1 and its proof, statements like F = Mτ^{-1}KHMτ are not meaningful since Mτ is rectangular; the Arnoldi/companion connection needs a careful residual treatment (as in standard DMD). Assumptions about distinct eigenvalues and the Vandermonde diagonalization should be discussed for repeated/continuous spectrum.
  - The claim “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is incorrect in general (e.g., dissipative systems have eigenvalues inside the unit disk).
  - Theorem 2 assumptions (eigenfunctions belong to RKHS and lie in span(U)) are strong; no discussion of when this holds or of convergence as τ→∞.
- Experiments are illustrative but not rigorous:
  - Toy/Hénon: only plots of eigenvalues; no quantitative error vs ground truth; no sensitivity to noise/kernel hyperparameters.
  - Prediction: preimage recovery is ad hoc (MDS-like) and lacks quantitative forecasting metrics or comparison to baselines (EDMD, exact DMD, GPR/GPDM, AR models).
  - Recognition: only an MDS visualization; no classification accuracy or ablations.
  - Change-point detection: single example, no ROC/delay vs false-alarm tradeoffs or controlled benchmarks.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Notational overload (ϕ, φ for different objects), some typos and missing details (dimensions, centering, residuals).
  - Bibliography is incomplete/inconsistent (many entries lack years/venues).
  - Computational complexity and memory requirements are not discussed; no guidance on kernel choice or truncation strategy.

Quality
Technically plausible and implementable, but some derivations are informal and core claims (e.g., “fully recover” eigenfunctions when ψ=0) are overstated. Empirical evaluation is too limited to substantiate advantages over established EDMD/KDMD baselines.

Clarity
Readable overall, but could be improved with careful notation, explicit dimensions, a more rigorous Arnoldi/DMD derivation in RKHS, and tighter writing. The applications section needs clearer algorithms and evaluation protocols.

Originality
Moderate. The RKHS formulation and robustification are natural and closely related to EDMD with kernels and POD truncation. The main conceptual step (kernelizing DMD/EDMD) appears incremental relative to existing literature.

Significance
Potentially useful to practitioners of Koopman analysis seeking kernelized, numerically stable pipelines. However, lacking comparisons and quantitative validation, it is hard to assess improvement over EDMD/DMD.

Suggestions for improvement
- Make the equivalence to EDMD explicit: derive the generalized eigenproblem A v = λ G v from your F̂ and show when they coincide. Compare empirically against EDMD/exact DMD on the same datasets.
- Tighten theory: correct the unit-circle statement; formalize the Arnoldi relation with residuals; discuss conditions ensuring φj ∈ RKHS and convergence with τ, links to EDMD convergence results; address repeated/continuous spectra.
- Strengthen experiments: quantitative eigenvalue/mode accuracy on systems with known spectra; short-term prediction RMSE vs baselines; robustness to noise; sensitivity to kernel width and truncation p; classification accuracy for dynamics recognition; change-detection metrics (ARL, detection delay) vs baselines.
- Discuss scalability (time/memory O(τ^3)/O(τ^2)), possible approximations (Nyström, random features), and kernel/hyperparameter selection.
- Preimage: compare to standard kernel preimage methods (e.g., Kwok & Tsang), report quantitative results.
- Improve clarity and bibliography formatting.

Pros
- Clear, kernelizable derivation; practical robustification.
- Theoretical link (Ritz pairs ↔ Koopman spectra) in RKHS.
- Multiple application avenues and released code.

Cons
- Limited novelty vs EDMD; no head-to-head comparisons.
- Some mathematical inaccuracies/overstatements.
- Weak empirical validation; mostly illustrative.
- Clarity and references need work.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and relevant, but the contribution over EDMD needs to be clarified and validated, and both theory and experiments should be strengthened to meet NeurIPS standards.
