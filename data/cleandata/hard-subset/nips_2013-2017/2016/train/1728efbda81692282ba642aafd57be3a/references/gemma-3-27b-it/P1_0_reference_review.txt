## Peer Review for "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

**Summary:**

This paper extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to utilize reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), aiming to improve the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions beyond the limitations of linear monomials used in standard DMD. The core idea is to perform the decomposition in the feature space induced by a kernel, allowing for the capture of more complex, nonlinear dynamics. The authors present a theoretical framework, an algorithm based on Krylov subspace calculations, and a robustification technique leveraging POD. They demonstrate the method on a toy system and the Henon map, showcasing its ability to estimate eigenvalues and also explore applications in prediction, dynamic recognition, and change-point detection.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the growing body of work surrounding DMD and Koopman operator theory. It correctly positions itself within the landscape of spectral methods for dynamical systems, referencing key works like those of Koopman, Mezić, and Schmid. The authors acknowledge connections to POD, exact DMD, and kernel methods, highlighting how their approach differs in its use of RKHS for function approximation.  The relation to subspace identification methods is noted, but suggested as an area for future work. The work clearly distinguishes itself from extended DMD by performing operations directly in the functional space, potentially recovering the full Koopman eigenfunctions.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a mathematically grounded extension of DMD. The derivation of the algorithm, based on the Krylov subspace and eigendecomposition in RKHS, appears rigorous. Theorem 1 and 2 provide clear justification for the method and connect it to established theory.
* **Novelty:**  Applying RKHS and kernel methods to DMD is a logical and potentially significant extension. It addresses a key limitation of standard DMD – the reliance on linear approximations – and offers a path towards capturing more complex nonlinear dynamics.
* **Robustification with POD:** Recognizing the potential for ill-conditioning, the inclusion of a POD-based robustification step is a practical and valuable addition. This addresses a common issue in DMD implementations.
* **Applications Showcase:** The exploration of prediction, dynamic recognition, and change-point detection demonstrates the potential utility of the extracted Koopman modes, moving beyond purely analytical results.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the results on the toy system and Henon map are encouraging, the empirical evaluation feels limited. The choice of kernels (degree 3 polynomial, Gaussian with width 1) appears somewhat arbitrary, with limited discussion of kernel selection or sensitivity analysis. More complex and real-world datasets would strengthen the claims.  Specifically, demonstrating performance gains over standard DMD or other nonlinear system identification methods is crucial but missing.
* **Computational Cost:** The computational complexity of performing DMD in a high-dimensional RKHS is not adequately addressed.  The authors mention ill-conditioning, but do not provide a detailed discussion of the scalability of the method to large datasets or high-dimensional state spaces. The impact of the POD truncation on performance also isn’t explored.
* **Application Depth:** The application sections are somewhat superficial. While demonstrating feasibility, they lack detailed comparative analysis or benchmark results. For example, the change-point detection example doesn’t compare the performance against established change detection algorithms.
* **Missing Discussion of Kernel Choice:** The choice of kernel significantly impacts the performance. There is little discussion on how to select the optimal kernel for a given problem or how the kernel parameters affect the results.
* **Connection to Existing Kernel Methods:** The work doesn't fully leverage or discuss how it connects to other established kernel-based methods for dynamical systems modeling, such as kernel state space models.



**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of DMD with the potential to improve the modeling of nonlinear dynamical systems.
* The inclusion of a POD-based robustification step is a practical and important addition.
* The applications showcase the potential utility of the method.

**Con Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation is limited and lacks convincing comparative results.
* The computational cost and scalability of the method are not adequately addressed.
* The application sections are superficial and lack detailed analysis.
* Insufficient discussion regarding kernel choice and its impact on performance.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising extension of DMD with the potential to address its limitations in capturing nonlinear dynamics. The theoretical framework is solid, and the robustification technique is valuable. However, the limited empirical evaluation and lack of detailed analysis raise concerns about the practical impact and scalability of the method. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with major revisions.  The authors should significantly expand the empirical evaluation with more complex datasets, perform a thorough sensitivity analysis of kernel parameters, provide a detailed discussion of the computational cost, and deepen the analysis of the applications with comparative benchmarking.  Addressing these weaknesses would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its contribution to the field.  A more detailed discussion of the relationship to existing kernel-based methods would also be beneficial.
