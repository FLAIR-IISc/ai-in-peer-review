Summary
The paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and derives two algorithms: (i) a kernelized Arnoldi/DMD procedure that builds an orthonormal basis of the feature-space Krylov subspace from a trajectory and computes Ritz values/vectors (Theorem 1), and (ii) a “robustified” variant projecting onto kernel PCA directions prior to estimating the finite-dimensional Koopman approximation (Theorem 2), analogous to truncated-SVD DMD. The authors argue that using kernels yields richer (nonlinear) basis functions than standard DMD, improving approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions. They discuss applications: short-term prediction via pre-image estimation, dynamics recognition via kernel principal angles, and change-point detection via a CUSUM-style test. Toy examples (a simple 2D system and the Hénon map) and brief application demos are provided.

Relation to prior work
This line of work is closely related to Extended DMD (EDMD) and, in particular, kernel DMD (KDMD) as introduced by Williams, Kevrekidis, and Rowley (2015, Journal of Nonlinear Science), as well as subsequent kernel-EDMD developments (e.g., Klus et al., 2016). The “robustified” variant here—projection onto kernel PCA and eigen-decomposition of the projected Koopman operator formed from Gram matrices—is essentially the KDMD recipe. The Arnoldi/Krylov interpretation is standard in DMD; the paper extends that narrative to RKHS.

Quality
- Theory: The central constructions are reasonable and align with known operator-theoretic/DMD derivations. Theorem 1’s statement that feature snapshots admit a finite Ritz expansion within the feature-space Krylov subspace mirrors the usual Arnoldi/DMD result. Theorem 2 connects Koopman eigenfunctions representable in the kPCA span to eigenvectors of the projected operator; this matches the EDMD/KDMD perspective. However, some steps are hand-wavy or rest on strong assumptions that are not carefully stated:
  - Invertibility and well-definedness in infinite-dimensional settings (e.g., Mτ “inverse,” companion/shift structure) require more care.
  - Assumptions such as distinct Ritz values, ψ = 0, or φj ∈ Φ with κj in span(U) are strong; guidance on when they hold is missing.
  - The claim that “for a system evolving on an attractor, Koopman eigenvalues always lie on the unit circle” is not generally correct; it holds for measure-preserving systems, not for generic dissipative systems with contraction.
- Algorithms: The kernel QR/Arnoldi route is likely ill-conditioned; the authors acknowledge this and switch to the kPCA-projected approach, which is standard. Practical details (regularization, truncation criteria, noise handling, kernel/bandwidth selection, computational complexity) are insufficiently discussed.
- Applications: The pre-image step is crucial for prediction but is treated heuristically (MDS-like inversion). No error analysis or robustness study is provided, and alternative regressors (e.g., kernel ridge regression mapping Koopman coordinates back to state) are not compared. The change-point detection formulation via a nonparametric exponential family is speculative and only briefly validated.
- Experiments: Evaluations are minimal, largely qualitative plots for eigenvalues and small demos. There are no quantitative metrics (spectral recovery error, prediction error vs. baselines), no noise/robustness studies, and no comparisons to EDMD/KDMD or exact/optimized DMD.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and organized, but there are several grammatical issues and notational shortcuts that obscure key steps. The derivations could benefit from clearer finite-vs-infinite dimensional arguments and explicit assumptions. The references are incomplete/poorly formatted and miss some key related work (e.g., KDMD by Williams et al.; kernel EDMD by Klus et al.). Figures lack detailed experimental settings and quantitative measures. The code link is not sufficient substitute for methodological clarity.

Originality
The core contribution overlaps strongly with KDMD/EDMD. The robustified RKHS algorithm via kPCA and Gram matrices is essentially the established KDMD approach. The Arnoldi/Krylov framing in RKHS is a nice perspective but not substantially different in outcome from existing methods. The applications (pre-image-based prediction, subspace recognition via kernel principal angles, CUSUM detection) are incremental and not evaluated deeply enough to claim novelty.

Significance
Koopman spectral methods with kernels are important and widely used. However, given the limited novelty relative to KDMD/EDMD and the weak empirical validation, the current submission is unlikely to significantly advance the state of the art. A stronger unifying theory, new computational advantages (e.g., scalable/incremental algorithms), or compelling empirical wins would be needed to have high impact.

Pros
- Clear operator-theoretic motivation; connects Arnoldi/DMD to RKHS.
- Correctly identifies numerical conditioning issues and uses a practical projected variant.
- Theorem 2 provides an interpretable link between Koopman eigenfunctions and kPCA subspaces.
- Shows potential applications beyond pure spectrum estimation.

Cons
- Overlaps substantially with existing KDMD/EDMD; novelty unclear and not positioned precisely.
- Theoretical exposition requires clearer assumptions and corrections (e.g., eigenvalues on unit circle).
- Minimal, largely qualitative experiments; no strong quantitative evidence or baselines.
- Pre-image and change-detection components are heuristic and under-evaluated.
- References are incomplete; key related work is missing or not properly discussed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely position the work relative to EDMD/KDMD and articulate what is new (theoretical insight, algorithmic differences, or practical gains).
- Strengthen theory with clear assumptions, finite-dimensional approximations, and corrected statements about spectral properties.
- Provide thorough empirical comparisons to DMD, EDMD, KDMD on standard benchmarks (Duffing, Lorenz, cylinder wake), including prediction error, spectral recovery, and robustness to noise; ablate kernel choices and truncation.
- Evaluate pre-image strategies quantitatively and compare alternatives; discuss computational complexity and scalability.
- Fix and expand the bibliography to include KDMD and kernel-EDMD literature; improve writing and notation.

Recommendation: Reject (mainly due to limited novelty and weak empirical validation), confidence: high.
