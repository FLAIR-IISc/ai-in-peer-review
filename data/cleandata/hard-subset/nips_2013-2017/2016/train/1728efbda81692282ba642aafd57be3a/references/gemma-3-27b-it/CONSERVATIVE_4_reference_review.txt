This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to better approximate Koopman eigenfunctions. The core idea is to move beyond linear monomials traditionally used in DMD, leveraging the expressiveness of kernel functions to capture more complex dynamics. While the mathematical formulation appears sound, and builds on established concepts like the Koopman operator and DMD, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence that the proposed method offers significant advantages over existing techniques, particularly given its increased complexity.

The authors correctly identify the limitations of standard DMD – its reliance on local linearizations – and attempt to address this with kernel methods. The derivation of the algorithm, extending DMD to the RKHS feature space, is mathematically presented adequately, and the connection to existing work (POD, DMD variants) is acknowledged. The inclusion of a robustification step via projection onto POD directions is a sensible addition, recognizing potential ill-conditioning issues. However, the empirical evaluation is insufficient to justify the added complexity. 

The experiments are limited to a simple toy system and the Hénon map. While the toy system results are as expected, demonstrating the algorithm *can* work, the Hénon map results are only “approximately” estimated, as the authors themselves admit. This is a significant weakness. Showing a clear advantage on a chaotic system, with demonstrably improved accuracy compared to standard DMD or other relevant baselines (e.g., extended DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD), is crucial. The applications section – prediction, embedding/recognition, change-point detection – feels underdeveloped and lacks thorough comparison to existing methods in each domain. The presentation of prediction results, for instance, lacks quantitative metrics. The embedding/recognition application relies on locomotion data and MDS, but doesn’t demonstrate superior performance against established dimensionality reduction and classification techniques. Similarly, the change-point detection example feels illustrative rather than rigorously evaluated.

**Strengths:**

*   Mathematically sound formulation extending DMD to RKHS.
*   Addresses a known limitation of standard DMD.
*   Inclusion of POD projection for robustness.
*   Clear connection to existing literature on Koopman spectral analysis and DMD.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited empirical validation with only two test cases.
*   Lack of quantitative comparison against baseline methods (standard DMD, extended DMD, etc.).
*   Applications section is underdeveloped and lacks rigorous evaluation.
*   The claim of approximating eigenfunctions with “richer basis functions” isn't convincingly demonstrated in the experiments.
*   The choice of kernels (degree 3 polynomial, Gaussian with fixed width) appears somewhat arbitrary and not justified. A kernel selection or tuning strategy would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel, theoretically grounded extension of DMD.
*   The RKHS formulation *could* be valuable for systems where linear approximations are insufficient.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficient empirical evidence to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The complexity of the method doesn't appear justified by the limited improvements shown.
*   The applications section lacks depth and quantitative evaluation.
*   The paper feels incomplete, more a proof-of-concept than a fully developed contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the theoretical contribution is non-trivial, the lack of compelling empirical evidence and the underdeveloped applications section raise significant concerns. The paper presents a mathematically interesting idea but fails to demonstrate its practical value or significance. The authors need to substantially strengthen the experimental evaluation, compare their method against strong baselines, and provide more detailed analysis of the applications to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The current form feels more suitable for a workshop or a more theoretical venue.
