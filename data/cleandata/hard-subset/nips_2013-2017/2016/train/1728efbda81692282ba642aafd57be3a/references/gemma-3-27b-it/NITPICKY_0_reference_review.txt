This paper proposes an extension to Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions. The core idea is to perform the analysis within the feature space induced by a kernel, allowing for potentially richer representations compared to DMD’s reliance on linear monomials. The authors present a theoretical framework and algorithms for this kernel-based DMD, including a robustification technique utilizing POD. Empirical evaluation is performed on a toy system and the Henon map, with additional applications discussed.

While the extension to RKHS is conceptually sound and represents a natural progression of DMD, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet collectively substantial, flaws in its presentation, mathematical rigor, and experimental validation. The core weakness lies in the lack of substantial novelty relative to existing kernel-based approaches and a somewhat superficial treatment of practical considerations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Conceptually Reasonable:** The idea of leveraging RKHS for DMD is a logical step towards addressing the limitations of linear basis functions.
*   **Theoretical Framework:** The authors attempt a rigorous mathematical formulation, linking the proposed method to DMD and the Koopman operator.
*   **Robustification:** Recognizing the potential for ill-conditioning and proposing a POD-based solution is a positive step towards practicality.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Clear Novelty:** The connection to kernel PCA (KPCA) is barely acknowledged. The proposed method is, in many aspects, a reformulation of KPCA applied to the Koopman operator's eigenproblem. The authors don’t adequately position their work relative to existing literature on kernel methods for dynamical systems, specifically those already utilizing kernel-based approximations of Koopman operators. Reference [28] is cited, but its implications aren't fully explored.
*   **Mathematical Imprecision:** The notation is occasionally inconsistent (e.g., using both `ϕ` and `phi` for the feature map). Certain claims lack sufficient justification. The treatment of stochastic systems in Section 7, while mentioned, feels tacked on and lacks sufficient detail. The jump from assuming `φj ∈ Φ` to its consequence in Theorem 2 feels overly hasty and requires more justification.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The empirical results are limited to two relatively simple systems. The choice of kernels (degree 3 polynomial and Gaussian) appears arbitrary and lacks justification based on the system dynamics. The figures are low-resolution and lack informative error bars or statistical significance testing. The prediction results (Fig. 2) are not quantitatively assessed.
*   **Overstated Claims:** Assertions like "the eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator are fully recovered" are too strong without clear quantification of the approximation error. The statement that "the algorithm can perform the truncation of eigenvectors" is trivial; any eigenvalue decomposition allows truncation.
*   **Formatting and Writing Issues:** Numerous minor grammatical errors and awkward phrasing detract from readability. The sentence structure is often convoluted. The paper's organization could be improved for better flow. The use of numbered lists within paragraphs feels disorganized.
*   **Missing Details:** The implementation details are scarce. What software was used? How were the kernel parameters chosen? This lack of reproducibility hinders assessment.
*   **Application Section is Superficial:** The application sections feel more like suggestive future work rather than concrete demonstrations of the method's utility. The discussion of change-point detection is particularly weak, lacking a clear comparison to established methods.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The idea of extending DMD to RKHS is conceptually valuable.
*   The authors attempt a mathematically rigorous formulation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of significant novelty compared to existing kernel-based techniques.
*   Insufficient experimental validation and quantitative results.
*   Numerous minor flaws in presentation and mathematical rigor.
*   Overstated claims and superficial application demonstrations.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **Reject**. While the idea presented is not without merit, the numerous flaws in its execution and presentation outweigh its limited novelty. The paper reads more like an initial exploration of an idea than a polished and well-supported contribution to the field. The authors need to significantly expand the experimental validation, address the mathematical imprecisions, and clearly differentiate their work from existing kernel-based methods before it is suitable for NIPS publication. Substantial revisions are required. A more focused contribution that deeply analyzes a specific aspect of this kernel-DMD extension—perhaps focusing on kernel selection or theoretical convergence properties—would be more impactful.
