Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in an RKHS by (i) constructing the Krylov subspace from feature-mapped snapshots ϕ(x0),…,ϕ(xτ−1), (ii) projecting the Koopman operator KH onto this subspace via a Gram–Schmidt QR in feature space (using kernel evaluations), and (iii) extracting empirical Ritz values/vectors as approximations to Koopman eigenvalues/modes. Recognizing numerical instability, the paper then “robustifies” the approach by projecting onto kernel-PCA (POD-in-RKHS) directions and eigendecomposing the reduced operator F̂. Two theorems connect Ritz values/vectors in feature space to Koopman spectral objects under assumptions (distinct eigenvalues; eigenfunctions lying in the span of the data feature space and within the kernel-PCA subspace). The paper explores three downstream uses: short-term prediction via a preimage procedure, sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-like score built from kernelized predictions, and recognition of dynamic patterns via subspace distances (kernel principal angles).

This work sits squarely in the growing literature connecting DMD to Koopman spectral analysis and to RKHS/dictionary methods. The closest prior is Extended DMD (EDMD) [Williams et al., J. Nonlinear Sci. 2015], which replaces monomials by a chosen dictionary and explicitly discusses kernelization to access rich feature spaces; “Kernel DMD” (KDMD) has since become a standard term for this variant. The present manuscript’s first algorithm is essentially Arnoldi/DMD in feature space; the second is a kernel-PCA-based projection akin to the SVD/POD-based robust DMD pipeline [Tu et al., Rowley et al.], but carried out with kernels. The applications (preimage-based prediction, dynamic subspace recognition, kernelized CUSUM) are interesting but lightweight extensions.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual story: do DMD in an RKHS to obtain nonlinear “modes” and richer Koopman approximations; stabilize via kernel-PCA truncation.
- Derivations are mostly correct at the level of linear-algebraic identities, and the kernelization is clean.
- The connection to Ritz values/vectors in feature space is useful pedagogically; Theorem 2 clarifies when reduced operators inherit Koopman eigen-structure.
- The paper articulates several downstream tasks that leverage the learned modes (prediction via preimage, recognition via subspace distances, change detection).
- Code availability (if anonymization issues are handled) is a plus.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation
Originality: The core idea—approximating the Koopman operator via DMD/EDMD in a high-dimensional feature space and using kernel tricks—is not new; it closely overlaps with EDMD and kernelized variants already proposed. The proposed “robustified” algorithm mirrors standard DMD practice (projecting onto dominant POD/SVD directions) and kernel PCA is the direct analog. The novelty therefore lies mainly in presentation and the specific linear-algebraic route (QR in feature space) plus a few application sketches, which feels incremental relative to EDMD/KDMD.

Quality: 
- Theory: Theorems rely on strong assumptions (distinct eigenvalues; eigenfunctions in Φ and in span(U)). No convergence or consistency results, no approximation error bounds, and no conditions on dictionary richness or kernel choice. The claim “eigenvalues on the unit circle for a system evolving on an attractor” is not generally correct; dissipative attractors typically yield eigenvalues inside the unit disk unless the operator is unitary (e.g., measure-preserving systems).
- Numerics: Ill-conditioning is acknowledged; the kernel-PCA fix is sensible but standard. There is little discussion of computational complexity (Gram matrices O(τ2) storage, O(τ3) eigen-ops), memory constraints, or regularization strategies (e.g., Tikhonov, ridge in Gram inverses).
- Experiments: Limited and mostly qualitative. The toy example and Hénon map plots show rough agreement, but there is no quantitative error analysis, ablations (kernel choice, truncation level), or comparisons to EDMD, exact/optimized DMD, or KDMD baselines. The preimage-based prediction and change-point detection are intriguing but ad hoc; again, no comparisons to established predictors or change detectors, and no statistical evaluation (ROC, delay vs false-alarm).

Clarity:
- The exposition is generally readable but dense. Some notation is overloaded (φ for both Koopman eigenfunction and empirical vectors), and several typos/grammar issues persist. The preimage section is under-specified (e.g., how neighbors are chosen, how distances are regularized in high dimensions, how non-uniqueness is handled).
- Figures are referenced but not deeply analyzed; parameter choices (kernel width, truncation rank) need justification or cross-validation guidance.

Significance:
- Given substantial overlap with EDMD/KDMD, the incremental contribution and limited empirical validation reduce impact. The downstream use cases suggest utility, but without thorough comparisons, it is hard to judge practical gains.

Constructive suggestions and questions for authors
- Precisely position the work relative to EDMD and kernel EDMD. What, concretely, is novel beyond prior kernelized EDMD formulations? Can you show cases where your QR-in-RKHS view or the kernel-PCA variant outperforms KDMD/EDMD numerically?
- Provide quantitative comparisons on standard benchmarks (e.g., noisy linear systems, Duffing, FitzHugh–Nagumo, Hénon, Lorenz) versus EDMD, exact/optimized DMD, and KDMD, with metrics on eigenvalue/mode recovery and short-horizon prediction.
- Correct the statement about eigenvalues on the unit circle and discuss when your operator is unitary/contractive in H.
- Discuss kernel and truncation selection (median heuristic, cross-validation, spectral gap heuristics), regularization (ridge on Gram matrices), and computational complexity.
- Clarify and evaluate the preimage method: include reconstruction error and stability to noise; compare to alternative preimage solvers.
- For change-point detection, include baselines (kernel CUSUM, MMD-based detectors) and report detection delay/false-alarm tradeoffs.
- Improve clarity: unify notation (differentiate Koopman eigenfunctions vs empirical vectors), add pseudocode, and proof details (especially Theorem 2).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, pedagogical derivation of KDMD-like methods with useful theorems tying feature-space Ritz objects to Koopman spectra.
- Practical stabilization via kernel-PCA aligns with DMD best practices.
- Interesting application sketches that may inspire further work.

Cons for acceptance
- Overlaps substantially with EDMD/kernel EDMD; novelty is limited.
- Weak empirical evaluation with no rigorous comparisons.
- Some technical overstatements and missing guidance on key practical choices.

Recommendation
Reject. While the paper is well-motivated and the overall idea is sound, the contribution appears incremental relative to EDMD/KDMD, and the empirical evidence is not strong enough to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS. Strengthening the novelty claims, adding rigorous comparisons, and tightening the theory and clarity could make this a valuable contribution in a future submission.

Confidence: High.
