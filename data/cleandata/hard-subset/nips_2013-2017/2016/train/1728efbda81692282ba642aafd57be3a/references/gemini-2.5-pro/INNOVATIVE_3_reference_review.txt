### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The central idea is to apply the principles of Koopman operator theory not on the original state space or a manually chosen set of observables, but within the high-dimensional feature space induced by a kernel function. The authors correctly identify that standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear combination of observables, which can be insufficient for highly nonlinear systems. By leveraging the kernel trick, their method, which they term kernel DMD (kDMD), can implicitly work with a much richer, nonlinear set of basis functions to find better approximations of the Koopman eigenfunctions and eigenvalues.

The paper first presents a direct extension of the Arnoldi method interpretation of DMD to an RKHS, deriving an algorithm based on a Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in the feature space. Recognizing the potential numerical instability of this approach, the authors then propose a more robust and practical algorithm. This second method projects the dynamics onto the principal components in the feature space (via kernel PCA), which is analogous to the common practice of stabilizing standard DMD by projecting onto Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) modes. The paper concludes by demonstrating the utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features for several downstream tasks, including short-term prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The primary strength of this paper is its core idea. The synthesis of Koopman operator theory, DMD, and kernel methods is both natural and highly innovative. It provides an elegant, non-parametric way to enrich the space of observables for Koopman analysis, moving beyond the limitations of linear DMD without requiring the user to explicitly define a dictionary of basis functions, as in Extended DMD. This represents a significant conceptual advance for data-driven analysis of dynamical systems.

2.  **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear and mathematically sound derivation for its proposed methods. The connection between the Arnoldi method and DMD is well-explained, and its generalization to the RKHS setting in Section 3 (Theorem 1) is compelling. The theoretical justification for the robust, kPCA-based variant in Section 4 (Theorem 2), which connects the eigenvectors of the projected operator back to the true Koopman eigenfunctions, is well-articulated and provides confidence in the method.

3.  **Practicality and Foresight:** The authors demonstrate good practical sense by not stopping at the mathematically pure, but potentially unstable, QR-based algorithm. The development of the robustified version using kernel PCA is crucial, as it makes the method far more likely to be useful in practice, especially with noisy or high-dimensional data where the Gram matrix may be ill-conditioned. This foresight strengthens the contribution significantly.

4.  **Demonstrated Potential for Impact:** The paper showcases the potential of kDMD not just as an analysis tool, but as a powerful feature extraction method for dynamics. The applications to prediction, embedding/recognition of motion capture data, and change-point detection are compelling proofs of concept. They effectively frame the method as a general-purpose tool that could be readily adopted by researchers in various fields, increasing its potential significance and impact.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Empirical Comparison:** While the experiments serve as effective illustrations, they lack rigorous, quantitative comparisons to relevant state-of-the-art baselines. For instance, the prediction task could be compared against Gaussian Process Dynamical Models; the dynamics recognition against other time-series classification methods (e.g., those based on LSTMs or temporal kernels); and the change-point detection against other modern techniques. The current results demonstrate feasibility but do not establish superiority or clearly delineate the regimes where kDMD excels.

2.  **Insufficient Discussion of Extended DMD (eDMD):** The paper briefly mentions eDMD [35], which is arguably the most closely related work. Both methods aim to enrich the basis functions used for Koopman approximation. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs would be valuable. The key advantage of kDMD is its ability to use an implicit, infinite-dimensional feature space, avoiding the need to manually specify a basis. However, eDMD might be more computationally efficient or interpretable in some cases. An empirical comparison on a problem where a good explicit basis is known would have substantially strengthened the paper's positioning.

3.  **Practical Considerations Not Addressed:** The performance of kDMD will be highly dependent on the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters. The paper provides little to no discussion on how to select these, or how sensitive the results are to this choice. Furthermore, the method's computational complexity scales with the number of data points due to the construction of the Gram matrix, which could be a limitation for very long time series. A brief discussion of these practical limitations would be welcome.

### **Decision and Justification**

This paper presents a bold, novel, and conceptually powerful idea. The fusion of Koopman analysis with kernel methods is an important contribution that opens up new avenues for the data-driven modeling of complex nonlinear systems. While the empirical validation is somewhat preliminary and could be strengthened with more extensive comparisons, the core theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed.

Following my reviewing philosophy, I weigh the novelty and potential impact of the core idea more heavily than the completeness of the experimental section. This paper introduces a fundamental new tool, and its weaknesses are primarily ones of omission (e.g., more comparisons, discussion of hyperparameter tuning) rather than fundamental flaws in the method itself. These are issues that can be readily addressed in future work, which this paper will undoubtedly inspire. The work is clear, technically sound, and presents a significant new direction.

**Recommendation: Accept**
