This paper introduces a Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) method leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to extend standard DMD by approximating Koopman eigenfunctions using nonlinear basis functions derived from kernel methods, thereby potentially overcoming limitations of DMD's reliance on linear monomials. The authors provide a theoretical foundation, an algorithm, and demonstrate the method on both synthetic and real-world data, highlighting applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamic pattern recognition.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation in spectral methods for dynamical systems, properly citing relevant work in Koopman operator theory (Koopman, 1963), DMD (Schmid, 2010; Rowley et al., 2009), and related techniques like Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) (Sirovich, 1990). The connections to kernel methods (Schölkopf et al., 1998) and their application in dimensionality reduction (kernel PCA) are also well established.  The authors correctly position their work within the broader landscape of system identification and model reduction techniques. The inclusion of related work is generally good, although some potentially relevant contributions in sparse DMD (Jovanović et al., 2016) and extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) are mentioned briefly but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of their differences and synergies.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of DMD to RKHS is a valuable contribution. By moving beyond linear monomials, the method has the potential to capture more complex dynamics that are inaccessible to standard DMD.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a clear and mathematically rigorous derivation of the algorithm.  Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 effectively establish the theoretical foundations and connect the proposed method to existing DMD theory.
* **Robustification with POD:** Recognizing the potential for ill-conditioning, the authors’ inclusion of a POD-based robustification strategy is a significant practical improvement.
* **Demonstrated Applications:**  The exploration of applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamic recognition demonstrates the potential utility of the method beyond purely analytical insights.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the examples are illustrative, the empirical validation is somewhat limited. The toy system and Hénon map are useful for initial testing, but more complex and higher-dimensional real-world datasets would strengthen the claims. The locomotion data example is promising, but lacks detailed quantitative results.
* **Kernel Parameter Selection:** The paper does not adequately address the crucial issue of kernel parameter selection (e.g., bandwidth for Gaussian kernels, degree for polynomial kernels).  The performance of the method is highly sensitive to these parameters, and a systematic exploration of their impact is needed.
* **Computational Cost:**  The computational complexity of kernel methods can be significant, especially for large datasets. A discussion of the computational cost and scalability of the proposed algorithm would be beneficial.
* **Comparison to Existing Methods:** The comparison to other nonlinear system identification methods (e.g., kernel state-space models, Gaussian process dynamical models) is limited.  A more thorough comparative analysis would help establish the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.
* **Connection to Existing Koopman Literature:** While acknowledging the Koopman operator, the paper could delve deeper into the connections between the proposed method and recent advances in Koopman-based machine learning, such as the use of deep neural networks to approximate Koopman operators.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of DMD that has the potential to improve the accuracy and applicability of Koopman spectral analysis.
* The inclusion of a robustification strategy addresses a potential practical limitation of the method.
* The exploration of various applications demonstrates the potential utility of the approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The empirical validation is limited, and the lack of a thorough kernel parameter study weakens the conclusions.
* The computational cost and scalability of the method are not adequately addressed.
* The comparison to existing nonlinear system identification methods is insufficient.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a worthwhile contribution to the field of nonlinear dynamical systems analysis. The extension of DMD to RKHS is a novel and promising idea, and the authors have provided a solid theoretical foundation for their work. However, the limited empirical validation and the lack of a comprehensive discussion of computational cost and parameter sensitivity prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form.

**Recommendation:** Reject with Major Revisions.

**Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Expand Empirical Validation:**  Include results on more complex and higher-dimensional real-world datasets.
* **Kernel Parameter Study:**  Conduct a systematic study of the impact of kernel parameters on the performance of the method.
* **Computational Analysis:**  Analyze the computational cost and scalability of the algorithm.
* **Comparative Analysis:**  Compare the proposed method to other relevant nonlinear system identification techniques.
* **Discussion of Deep Koopman Methods:** Discuss the connections to recent advances in Koopman-based machine learning utilizing deep neural networks.
* **Clarify Assumptions:** Further clarify the assumptions underpinning the application of the Koopman operator to stochastic systems (Section 7).
