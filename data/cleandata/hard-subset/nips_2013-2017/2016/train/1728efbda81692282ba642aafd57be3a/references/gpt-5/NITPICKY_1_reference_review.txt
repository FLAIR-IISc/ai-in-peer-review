Summary
The paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors recast Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) as a Ritz/Arnoldi-type method in a feature space, constructing a Krylov subspace from ϕ(x0),…,ϕ(xτ−1), computing a QR factorization in H to obtain a finite-dimensional projection of the Koopman operator KH, and extracting “Ritz” eigenvalues/vectors that play the role of Koopman eigenvalues/modes. Because Gram-Schmidt in H is ill-conditioned, a “robust” variant is proposed using kernel PCA (POD) truncation, leading to a finite-dimensional operator F̂ expressed purely via Gram matrices and its eigendecomposition. Applications are sketched: prediction via preimage reconstruction, dynamics recognition via subspace angles of estimated Koopman eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-like score. Small-scale experiments on a toy system, the Hénon map, motion capture sequences, and a Lorenz-map change-point task are shown.

Relation to prior work
The framing of DMD as a Galerkin/Ritz approximation to the Koopman operator is standard. The proposed kernelization is very close to Extended DMD (EDMD) and its kernelized variants (dictionary methods with implicit feature maps), e.g., Williams et al. (JNS 2015) and subsequent kernel EDMD literature. Connections to convergence analyses (e.g., Korda & Mezić, 2018) and RKHS-based Koopman approaches (e.g., Giannakis and collaborators) are not discussed. The “robust” variant using kernel PCA truncation resembles standard regularized/low-rank DMD practices.

Strengths
- Clear motivation to go beyond linear monomials by using kernels; operator expressions are given in terms of Gram matrices, enabling practical implementation.
- The kernel PCA–based truncation is a sensible stabilization strategy and is compatible with DMD best practices.
- The connection between the projected operator F̂ in the kernel PCA subspace and Koopman spectral objects is articulated (Theorem 2), and the shift structure M+ = KHMτ is exploited cleanly.
- The applications point to potentially useful downstream uses (prediction, recognition, change detection).

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and assumptions:
- Theorem 1 relies on a QR factorization and a companion-matrix argument invoking a Vandermonde T built from distinct Ritz values. This is numerically fragile and theoretically assumes R invertible and distinct eigenvalues; no discussion of conditioning/Jordan blocks or what happens with repeated/clustered eigenvalues.
- The use of Gram-Schmidt in an RKHS is glossed over. A kernel-QR or kernel-Arnoldi algorithm is not described in sufficient detail to be reproducible or numerically stable; the paper later abandons it for kernel PCA truncation.
- Theorem 2 assumes φj(x) = <ϕ(x), κj> with κj ∈ H, i.e., that Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the representer span induced by ϕ and that κj lies in span(U). This is a very strong assumption rarely satisfied except in idealized cases. The implications and limitations are not discussed.
- Claim “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle [20]” is inaccurate without qualification. Dissipative systems typically have point spectrum inside the unit disk and continuous spectrum; unit-circle behavior pertains to measure-preserving dynamics.
- Centering (H) and Koopman action: kernel PCA uses centered features. KH generally does not commute with centering and the shift relation M+ = KHMτ changes under H. The paper uses H on both sides in Eq. (11) but does not justify the effect of centering on operator estimation.
- The “preimage via MDS-style” reconstruction is heuristic; no stability, uniqueness, or error guarantees. For non-invertible kernels or non-injective ϕ, preimages may not exist.
- The change-point detection section posits a nonparametric exponential-family model p(xt) = exp(<ϕ∘f(xt−1), ϕ(xt)> − g(…)). This construction is ad hoc; the normalizer depends on xt−1, and the approximation dropping the second term is unjustified. No statistical guarantees are provided.

Originality and relation to prior work:
- The core idea—projecting the Koopman operator onto the span of lifted features using kernels—is essentially kernel EDMD. The paper cites EDMD [35] but does not clearly distinguish its contribution beyond re-expressing steps via kernel PCA/QR. Prior kernelized Koopman works (kernel EDMD; RKHS-based Koopman spectral analysis) are not adequately cited or contrasted, nor is convergence theory discussed.

Empirical evaluation:
- Experiments are limited and mostly qualitative. No quantitative error metrics, no comparisons against EDMD/exact/optimized DMD, no sensitivity analyses (kernel width, truncation rank), no ablations (kernel PCA vs Gram-Schmidt).
- The Hénon results are weakly supported (“seem to be approximately estimated”) without ground-truth matching or error bars.
- Change-point detection lacks standard performance metrics (detection delay, false alarm rate) and baselines beyond one kernel method; the modeling is tenuous.
- The prediction section refers to “Fig. 2” which is missing in the provided paper; reproducibility details are sparse.

Clarity and presentation:
- Numerous grammatical issues and typos (e.g., “to robustly it”; “almost of the theoretical claims”; inconsistent accents and notation). Mixing ϕ (feature map), φj (eigenfunctions), and φ̃j (Ritz vectors) is confusing—some are elements of H, others scalar functions; the text sometimes conflates them.
- Several references are incomplete, malformed, or missing years and venues. Some author names/entries look corrupted (“L.S”).
- The MATLAB code footnote is oddly placed; a stray superscript “1” appears later.
- Figures are not fully described; axes/units are unclear, and one referenced figure is missing.

Pros
- Timely topic; kernelization of Koopman spectral methods is valuable.
- Derivations to express all computations in terms of Gram matrices are useful.
- The stabilization via kernel PCA aligns with best practices in DMD.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to kernel EDMD; insufficient positioning and missing key citations.
- Strong, unexamined assumptions in the theorems; some incorrect or misleading statements.
- Weak, largely qualitative experiments with missing figure and no rigorous comparisons.
- Presentation, notation, and references require substantial revision.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely delineate how your method differs from (kernel) EDMD. Is your algorithm mathematically equivalent under a choice of dictionary = span{ϕ(xi)}? What advantages do you claim?
- How do centering operations interact with KH? Can you justify Eq. (11) under centering, or propose an uncentered alternative?
- Provide quantitative comparisons to EDMD/exact/optimized DMD on standard benchmarks (e.g., Duffing, Lorenz-63/96, cylinder wake), including spectral error and prediction error.
- Clarify conditions under which φj(x) = <ϕ(x), κj> holds. What happens when it doesn’t?
- Include a numerically stable, reproducible kernel-QR/Arnoldi procedure or drop that path and focus on the kernel PCA variant with regularization.

Scores
- Quality: 4/10
- Clarity: 4/10
- Originality: 3/10
- Significance: 4/10

Recommendation
Reject. While the topic is relevant and the high-level ideas are sensible, the paper’s novelty over existing kernel/extended DMD is unclear, theoretical claims rely on strong or incorrect assumptions, the empirical evaluation is insufficient and partially incomplete, and the presentation (notation, grammar, references) needs significant polishing. Strengthening the positioning versus kernel EDMD, correcting theoretical statements, providing robust experiments with quantitative benchmarks and baselines, and tightening the exposition could make a future version competitive.
