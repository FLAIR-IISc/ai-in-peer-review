Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a kernelized variant of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to (i) lift data into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), (ii) form the Krylov matrix Mτ = [ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)] in feature space, (iii) compute a projected Koopman operator via a Gram–Schmidt QR factorization and Ritz analysis to obtain eigenvalues/eigenvectors, and (iv) “robustify” the procedure by projecting onto kernel-PCA (POD-in-RKHS) directions before eigendecomposition. Theoretical statements (Theorem 1, 2) formalize the finite-dimensional projection and show that if Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the span of selected kernel principal directions, then the computed eigenpairs coincide with the true ones. The paper also sketches applications: short-term prediction via preimage recovery, dynamics recognition using subspace distances between estimated Koopman eigenfunction spans, and change-point detection via a CUSUM-like criterion.

Conceptually, this paper sits squarely within the EDMD/KDMD literature. Williams, Kevrekidis, and Rowley (J. Nonlinear Science, 2015) introduced EDMD and its kernelized counterpart (KDMD), which constructs a finite-dimensional approximation to the Koopman operator on a user-chosen dictionary or implicitly via kernels, and computes eigenvalues/modes from the resulting operator. The “robustified” approach here—kernel PCA for truncation followed by eigendecomposition of the projected time-advance operator expressed entirely in terms of Gram matrices—is very close in spirit and practice to KDMD. The connection to the Arnoldi/Ritz perspective is a useful lens, but the resulting algorithms and assumptions largely overlap with existing work. The paper cites [35] but underplays that KDMD is already a kernelized EDMD formulation that addresses the same goals and practicalities (dictionary richness, truncation, numerical stability).

Strengths
- Clear, operator-theoretic framing: The Krylov/Ritz view in RKHS is coherently presented and ties Koopman spectral analysis to classical numerical linear algebra.
- Kernelization makes the approach flexible and expressive without explicit feature design; all computations are kernelized via Gram matrices.
- The robustification via kernel PCA is practically motivated and aligns with common practice in DMD/EDMD to mitigate ill-conditioning.
- Applications sketched show potential breadth (prediction, recognition, change detection).

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality/technical soundness:
- Novelty relative to KDMD is limited. The “robustified” algorithm (Eq. 11) and its truncation strategy mirror KDMD/EDMD with kernel dictionaries and SVD/POD truncation. Theorems 1–2 formalize well-known projection properties; conditions such as “φj ∈ Φ and κj ∈ span(U)” are strong and standard in EDMD analyses.
- Some claims are imprecise or overstated. For example, “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is false in general; dissipative systems with stable fixed points have eigenvalues inside the unit disk. The statement “eigenfunctions are fully recovered if ψ = 0” is tautologically true but vacuous as a practical guarantee.
- The change-point detection derivation uses a nonparametric exponential family p(xt) = exp(⟨ϕ∘f(xt−1), ϕ(xt)⟩ − g(·)), which lacks justification (why this pairing corresponds to a valid conditional density model; how g is computed; what assumptions ensure normalization). The resulting CUSUM surrogate seems heuristic.
- No analysis of consistency, approximation error, or sample complexity; no discussion of noise robustness, regularization (e.g., Tikhonov on nearly singular Gram matrices), or conditioning beyond heuristic truncation.

Clarity:
- The notation φ is used for Koopman eigenfunctions and also for feature-space vectors/eigenvectors, which is confusing; KH acting “on the feature map ϕ” is unusual phrasing—typically Koopman acts on scalar observables; here ϕ is vector-valued into H, and this deserves more careful exposition.
- The proofs rely on companion-matrix/QR manipulations; they are fine as algebraic identities but would benefit from a clearer statement of assumptions (distinct eigenvalues, finite Krylov rank, noiseless setting).
- Several sections compress important implementation details: choice of kernel and bandwidth, truncation criterion p, stabilization/regularization, computational complexity (O(τ^3) eigensolves; O(τ^2) memory for Gram matrices).

Originality:
- The core algorithmic contribution overlaps substantially with KDMD. The Arnoldi/Krylov viewpoint is a different narrative, but the practical estimator and its kernelized implementation do not appear materially novel. The paper should more directly position itself with respect to KDMD, including explicit derivations showing equivalence/differences of F̂ to the standard KDMD operator.

Significance/empirical evaluation:
- Experiments are minimal and largely qualitative (toy system, Hénon map eigenvalues approximate equilibria; motion capture embedding; a change-point example). There is no systematic benchmarking against EDMD/KDMD/exact DMD, no quantitative metrics (eigenvalue error, mode reconstruction error, prediction error), no noise/stability studies, and no ablations on kernel/dictionary/truncation choices. This makes it difficult to assess practical advantage or robustness.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a precise comparison to KDMD: derive equivalence (or differences) between your F̂ and the KDMD estimator, and clarify what is genuinely new (e.g., a provable benefit of the Krylov/Ritz construction, improved conditioning, or different approximation properties).
- Correct/qualify the statement about eigenvalues on the unit circle; clarify assumptions on the dynamics (measure-preserving vs dissipative).
- Expand experiments with quantitative metrics and baselines (EDMD, KDMD, exact DMD, optimized DMD), including noisy data and parameter sensitivity; report truncation selection and kernel hyperparameters.
- Justify the change-point model or remove it; alternatively, base detection on forecast residuals with principled thresholds.
- Discuss regularization for near-singular Gram matrices and provide complexity/scale considerations.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear operator-theoretic framing and kernelization of DMD with an Arnoldi/Ritz perspective.
- Potentially useful exposition and unification that could benefit practitioners.
- Broad set of applications hinted.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to established KDMD/EDMD.
- Weak and largely qualitative empirical evaluation; no rigorous baselines.
- Some technical inaccuracies and unclear modeling choices (notably in change detection).
- Missing analysis of approximation/consistency and practical regularization details.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper’s core ideas are valuable but largely overlap with existing KDMD/EDMD work; to be competitive, it needs a crisper positioning relative to prior art, stronger theory or practical advantages, and substantially more rigorous empirical validation.
