Summary
The paper proposes a kernelized variant of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis. The main idea is to perform DMD in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), thereby approximating Koopman eigenfunctions using nonlinear feature maps rather than linear monomials of observables. The authors (i) derive a kernel DMD (kDMD) procedure via Gram–Schmidt QR in feature space and the eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator; (ii) propose a more numerically robust variant that projects onto kernel-PCA (POD) directions, allowing truncation; and (iii) illustrate applications: short-term prediction via a pre-image construction, dynamics recognition using kernel principal angles between Koopman subspaces, and sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-style statistic built from learned coefficients. Theoretical statements (Theorems 1–2) show equivalences between the proposed finite-dimensional approximations and Koopman spectral expansions under standard assumptions about richness of the data and subspace inclusion.

Relation to prior work
DMD and Koopman operator-based learning are well-established; exact DMD, optimized DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD, and especially extended DMD (EDMD) are directly relevant. EDMD frames Koopman approximation as a Galerkin least-squares projection on a set of basis functions—and crucially, Williams et al. show a kernel formulation enabling large, implicit dictionaries (often referred to as kernel EDMD or kDMD). The robustification via POD/SVD is also standard practice in DMD. The paper cites EDMD [35] but treats the proposed method as “similar to the robust-version of our algorithm,” whereas conceptually and algorithmically the overlap with EDMD+kernels appears substantial.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: using RKHS to move beyond local linear monomial approximations in DMD and better approximate Koopman eigenfunctions.
- Linear-algebraic development is clean and intuitive; working entirely with Gram matrices makes the approach practical.
- The robust variant via kernel PCA is sensible and aligns with best practice in DMD to address ill-conditioning and truncation.
- Flexibility: the remark that sequential data are not strictly necessary (pairs suffice) is practical.
- The application sketches (pre-image prediction, Koopman-subspace recognition via principal angles, and change-point detection) suggest utility beyond pure spectral analysis.
- Public code (though see anonymity concern).

Weaknesses
- Novelty appears limited relative to EDMD with kernels, which already provides a kernelized finite-dimensional approximation to the Koopman operator using only Gram matrices and includes the same projection/truncation themes. The paper does not convincingly distinguish its contribution from kEDMD/kDMD beyond a different derivation (Arnoldi/QR vs least-squares/Galerkin) and notation.
- The theoretical results largely restate standard Krylov/Arnoldi properties in feature space and require strong assumptions (eigenfunctions in the RKHS span; inclusion within the kernel-PCA subspace). No new error bounds or consistency results are provided.
- Empirical evaluation is very limited and mostly qualitative (toy system and Henon map eigenvalues; a small MoCap embedding; a single change-point example). There are no quantitative comparisons against baselines (DMD, exact DMD, EDMD, kernel EDMD), no ablation on truncation ranks, no sensitivity to kernel choice/width/regularization, and no scalability/memory/runtime analysis.
- The pre-image step is heuristic and may be brittle; its accuracy/sensitivity is not evaluated.
- The change-point detection derivation is hand-wavy (drops terms “since variation is smaller,” lacks calibration and false-alarm control) and is not benchmarked against established methods.
- Several presentation issues: typos, missing details in references (years/venues), and claims like “fully recovered if ψ=0” that, while true in a finite-dimensional algebraic sense, are not informative about when this happens in practice.
- Anonymity breach: the paper includes a code link that appears to reveal author identity; this violates double-blind policy.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify concretely how your algorithm differs from EDMD with kernels (Williams et al., J. Nonlinear Science 2015), both theoretically and algorithmically. What does the Arnoldi/QR perspective buy beyond the EDMD least-squares/Galerkin approach? Are the outputs equivalent under mild conditions?
- How do you regularize ill-conditioned Gram matrices (e.g., Tikhonov, truncated eigenspectrum), and how sensitive are results to such regularization and kernel width?
- What is the computational and memory complexity in τ and n (state dimension), and how does it scale compared to EDMD/kEDMD?
- How do you select truncation rank p? Do you use an energy criterion, cross-validation, or stability diagnostics?
- Can you provide quantitative benchmarks against EDMD/kEDMD on prediction error, eigenvalue accuracy, and mode reconstruction across standard systems (e.g., Duffing, Lorenz, cylinder wake snapshots)?

Suggestions for improvement
- Position the work explicitly relative to EDMD/kEDMD, possibly unifying the formulations and proving equivalence under assumptions; if there is a genuine difference (e.g., numerical advantages or stability), demonstrate it empirically.
- Provide robust experiments with quantitative metrics and baselines, including sensitivity analyses (kernel, rank, noise).
- Discuss and implement regularization systematically.
- Either formalize the change-point detection method with statistical guarantees or present it as a heuristic with thorough empirical validation.
- Clean up references and improve clarity of proofs (state assumptions precisely, define all symbols, and provide reproducible settings).
- Remove identifying links to maintain anonymity.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline)
While the paper is well-motivated and presents a coherent kernelized DMD pipeline with interesting application sketches, its core technical ideas overlap substantially with existing EDMD/kEDMD. Given NeurIPS standards and my emphasis on novelty, I do not see sufficient differentiation or empirical evidence to justify acceptance in the current form.
