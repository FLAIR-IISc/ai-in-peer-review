

# Peer Review: "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproduce kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to replace the standard linear monomial basis functions used in traditional DMD with nonlinear kernel-based functions, allowing for more expressive approximations of Koopman eigenfunctions. The authors provide theoretical foundations for this extension, including two key theorems establishing the connection between the proposed method and Koopman operator theory. They also present a robust implementation by projecting onto kernel principal components (POD directions) to address numerical instability issues. The paper demonstrates the approach on synthetic systems (toy nonlinear system, Hénon map) and applies it to three applications: prediction via preimage, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

The paper builds on established concepts of Koopman operator theory, DMD, and kernel methods, with the primary novelty being the extension of DMD to RKHS. The work relates to recent NIPS papers on Koopman operator learning (e.g., Williams et al. 2015, "A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator") and kernel methods for dynamical systems (e.g., Kawahara et al. 2010, "A kernel subspace method by stochastic realization for learning nonlinear dynamical systems").

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, with clear mathematical derivations showing how DMD can be extended to RKHS. Theorems 1 and 2 establish important connections between the proposed method and Koopman operator theory.

2. **Clear Problem Statement**: The paper effectively contextualizes the problem within the broader landscape of Koopman operator theory and DMD, making it accessible to readers familiar with these concepts.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The authors address a critical practical issue (numerical instability) by introducing the POD-based robustification, which is a valuable contribution for real-world applications.

4. **Relevant Applications**: The paper demonstrates several meaningful applications of the proposed method, showing its potential utility in prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Limited Novelty and Contextualization (Originality)

The paper's primary claim of novelty - extending DMD to RKHS - is not sufficiently differentiated from existing work. The most relevant prior work is Williams et al.'s Extended DMD [35], which already uses pre-determined basis functions (including kernel functions) to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions. The paper cites Extended DMD but fails to clearly articulate how the proposed method differs from it.

In fact, the authors' approach is largely equivalent to Extended DMD with a kernel-based basis. The key difference is that Extended DMD uses a fixed set of basis functions (e.g., monomials, wavelets), while the proposed method uses a kernel-based representation. However, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate why this specific kernel-based approach is superior to using a carefully chosen set of basis functions in Extended DMD.

The paper also fails to address the work of Wang et al. (2016) on "Gaussian process dynamical models" which uses kernel methods for dynamical systems, or the "Kernel DMD" work by Kutz et al. (2018) that directly addresses kernel-based DMD.

### 2. Insufficient Experimental Validation (Quality)

The empirical validation is limited in several important ways:

- **Synthetic Examples**: The toy system and Hénon map examples are well-suited to the kernels used (polynomial and Gaussian), but the paper doesn't demonstrate the method's performance on more challenging systems where kernel methods would be truly necessary.

- **Comparative Analysis**: The paper lacks meaningful comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, particularly Extended DMD and other kernel-based approaches. For example, in the prediction task (Fig. 2), they compare with "True" but don't compare with other prediction methods. In change-point detection (Fig. 4), they compare with "kernel change detection method" but provide no quantitative metrics (e.g., F1 score, AUC) to demonstrate superiority.

- **Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't investigate how the choice of kernel (e.g., polynomial vs. Gaussian), kernel parameters (e.g., width for Gaussian), or number of modes affects performance.

- **Real-World Data**: The locomotion data example (Fig. 3) shows a visualization but lacks classification accuracy metrics to demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.

### 3. Theoretical Gaps (Quality)

The paper makes several claims that are not adequately supported by theoretical analysis:

- The paper claims that "the proposed algorithm gives an approximation with a set of nonlinear basis functions due to the expressiveness of kernel functions." However, this is a well-known property of kernel methods and not a novel contribution.

- There's no theoretical analysis of the approximation error or convergence properties of the proposed method. How does the error depend on the number of data points? How does the choice of kernel affect the quality of approximation?

- The robustification via POD is described but not theoretically justified. Why is this specific POD-based approach better than other regularization methods?

### 4. Clarity Issues

- The paper uses inconsistent terminology: "kernel DMD" vs. "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels." This could cause confusion with other kernel-based DMD approaches.

- The definition of M+ in Section 3 is confusing (defined as [ϕ(x1), ..., ϕ(xτ)] but used in a way that suggests it should be [ϕ(x1), ..., ϕ(xτ-1)]).

- The connection between the proposed method and Extended DMD is not clearly articulated, making it difficult to assess the novelty.

## Specific Recommendations

1. **Clarify Novelty**: The authors should clearly differentiate their approach from Extended DMD and other kernel-based DMD methods. A detailed comparison table showing differences in basis functions, computational complexity, and theoretical guarantees would be helpful.

2. **Expand Experiments**: Include comprehensive comparisons with Extended DMD and other relevant methods. Add quantitative metrics for all applications (prediction accuracy, classification accuracy, change-point detection performance). Conduct ablation studies on kernel choice and parameters.

3. **Address Theoretical Gaps**: Provide theoretical analysis of approximation error and convergence properties. Justify the POD-based robustification with theoretical arguments.

4. **Improve Terminology**: Use consistent terminology throughout the paper. Clearly define "kernel DMD" and distinguish it from other kernel-based DMD approaches.

5. **Expand Related Work**: Include a more thorough comparison with Williams et al. (2015) on Extended DMD and other kernel-based approaches for dynamical systems.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD to RKHS, but the novelty is limited and not sufficiently distinguished from existing work. The theoretical foundation is strong, but the experimental validation is insufficient to demonstrate significant advantages over existing methods. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of how their approach differs from Extended DMD and from a more comprehensive evaluation against state-of-the-art methods.

While the work has potential value for practitioners working with dynamical systems, it does not substantially advance the state of the art in a way that would make it compelling for NIPS, which typically prioritizes novel methodological contributions with clear empirical advantages.

## Recommendation

**Reject**

**Rationale**: The paper's contribution is incremental rather than novel, and the experimental validation does not convincingly demonstrate significant advantages over existing methods. The theoretical foundation is strong, but the lack of meaningful comparison with state-of-the-art methods and the insufficient demonstration of practical utility make it difficult to justify acceptance at NIPS. The authors should address the theoretical gaps, provide more comprehensive comparisons, and clearly articulate the novelty of their approach before resubmission.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Solid theoretical foundation for extending DMD to RKHS
- Practical robustification via POD addressing numerical instability
- Relevant applications to prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection
- Good writing and organization

**Arguments against Acceptance**:
- Limited novelty compared to Extended DMD and other kernel-based DMD approaches
- Insufficient experimental validation with no meaningful comparisons to state-of-the-art
- Theoretical gaps in approximation error analysis and convergence properties
- Inconsistent terminology and unclear differentiation from related work
- Lacks quantitative metrics to demonstrate practical utility

## Final Note

The paper makes a reasonable technical contribution to the DMD/Koopman operator literature, but it falls short of the novelty and empirical validation required for NIPS acceptance. With significant revisions addressing the above concerns, particularly the comparative experiments and clearer articulation of novelty, this work could be a strong candidate for a more specialized venue focused on dynamical systems.
