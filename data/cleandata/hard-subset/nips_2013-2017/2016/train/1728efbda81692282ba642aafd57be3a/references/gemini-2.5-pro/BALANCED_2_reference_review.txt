### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to lift the system's state space into a high-dimensional feature space via a kernel map, where the nonlinear dynamics can be better approximated by a linear Koopman operator. The authors present two algorithms for this "Kernel DMD" (kDMD). The first is a direct extension of the Arnoldi method interpretation of DMD, based on a Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in the feature space. Recognizing that this approach can be numerically unstable, they propose a second, more robust algorithm. This robust version first projects the data onto a truncated set of principal components in the feature space (via Kernel PCA, analogous to Proper Orthogonal Decomposition or POD) before estimating the Koopman operator's projection. The paper demonstrates the utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features for several downstream tasks, including short-term state prediction, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection. This work bridges the gap between the DMD literature, which has been popular in physics and fluid dynamics, and the kernel methods literature prevalent in machine learning. It relates to prior work on spectral methods for learning dynamical systems and, most notably, to Extended DMD (eDMD), which uses a user-defined dictionary of basis functions.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel, elegant, and principled extension of DMD using kernel methods. This allows for a data-driven, non-parametric approach to approximating Koopman eigenfunctions from a much richer function class than the linear observables used in standard DMD. This contribution is significant as it provides a powerful new tool for nonlinear system analysis that is accessible to the machine learning community and could have a substantial impact on time-series analysis, representation learning for dynamical systems, and applications in various scientific domains.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is sound. The paper provides clear derivations for both the direct and robust versions of kDMD. Theorem 1 establishes the basis for the direct algorithm by extending the DMD decomposition principle to RKHS. Theorem 2 provides a crucial justification for the robust algorithm, connecting the eigenvectors of the projected operator to the true Koopman eigenfunctions under reasonable assumptions. The inclusion of the robust algorithm (Section 4) is a key strength, as it addresses a well-known numerical instability issue in DMD-like methods and makes the proposed technique practical.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. It provides a concise yet sufficient background on the Koopman operator and DMD, making the work accessible to readers not deeply familiar with the topic. The mathematical derivations are easy to follow for an audience with a background in kernel methods. The provision of code is a commendable step towards reproducibility.

4.  **Broad Applicability:** The authors demonstrate the versatility of their method by applying it to a diverse set of tasks: prediction, classification/embedding of dynamics, and change-point detection. This effectively showcases that kDMD is not just a tool for system analysis but also a powerful feature extraction method for various downstream machine learning problems involving dynamical data.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Positioning Relative to Prior Work:** While the paper mentions Extended DMD (eDMD) [35], the relationship could be discussed in greater depth. Kernel DMD can be viewed as a specific instance of the eDMD framework where the dictionary of observables is implicitly and non-parametrically defined by the kernel's feature map. A more detailed comparison discussing the trade-offs would strengthen the paper. For instance, what are the advantages of kDMD's implicit dictionary over explicitly choosing a dictionary for eDMD (e.g., polynomials, radial basis functions)? The primary advantage seems to be convenience, but a more thorough discussion is warranted.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical results are promising and illustrative, but they lack quantitative rigor and comparison against relevant baselines.
    *   **Prediction:** The prediction results are shown qualitatively. A quantitative comparison against other nonlinear time-series forecasting models (e.g., Gaussian Process Dynamical Models, LSTMs) would be necessary to establish the state of the art. Furthermore, the pre-image problem is non-trivial, and a deeper discussion of the limitations and computational cost of the chosen MDS-based approach would be beneficial.
    *   **Change-Point Detection:** The derivation of the change score is highly heuristic, relying on an informal argument to discard a term in the log-likelihood ratio. While the results are visually appealing, this section would be much stronger with a more rigorous justification or an ablation study. Quantitative comparison on a benchmark dataset against other change-point detection methods (including the cited kernel method [7]) is also missing.
    *   **Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper does not discuss how crucial hyperparameters were selected, such as the kernel type, kernel parameters (e.g., Gaussian width), and the truncation rank `p` for the robust method. The performance of kernel methods is often sensitive to these choices, and a discussion of this aspect is essential for the method's practical usability.

3.  **Scalability:** The proposed algorithms rely on constructing and decomposing Gram matrices of size `τ x τ`, where `τ` is the number of data points. The computational complexity is therefore at least O(τ²), which may limit its applicability to very long time series. A brief discussion of computational complexity and potential avenues for scaling (e.g., using random Fourier features or Nyström approximation) would be a valuable addition.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   The paper introduces a well-motivated and theoretically sound method that elegantly combines Koopman theory with kernel methods.
*   The contribution is original and significant, providing a powerful, non-parametric tool for nonlinear dynamical systems analysis.
*   The robust version of the algorithm is practical and addresses a key limitation of naive DMD extensions.
*   The work is likely to be of high interest to researchers in machine learning, control theory, and scientific domains employing data-driven modeling.

### Arguments for Rejection:

*   The experimental validation is preliminary and lacks quantitative comparisons to established baseline methods, making it difficult to assess the practical performance of the proposed approach.
*   The relationship and comparison to the very similar Extended DMD framework are not sufficiently detailed.
*   Key practical details, such as hyperparameter selection and computational complexity, are not adequately addressed.

### Suggestions for Improvement:

Even with a decision to accept, I strongly encourage the authors to incorporate the following in their final version:

1.  **Expand on Related Work:** Dedicate a few sentences to explicitly frame kDMD as a non-parametric instance of the eDMD framework and discuss the pros and cons (e.g., avoiding manual feature engineering vs. potential for less tailored basis functions).
2.  **Strengthen Experiments:**
    *   For prediction, add tables with standard error metrics (e.g., MSE, MAE) and compare against at least one or two standard time-series baselines.
    *   For change-point detection, either provide a more solid justification for the simplified score or explicitly acknowledge it as a heuristic. If possible, add a quantitative comparison on a synthetic benchmark where the ground truth is known.
    *   Add a paragraph (or an appendix section) detailing the hyperparameter selection process for the experiments (e.g., "The Gaussian kernel width was set using the median heuristic," "The truncation rank p was chosen to retain 99% of the variance.").
3.  **Add Practical Considerations:** Include a brief discussion on the computational complexity of the proposed method and its scaling with the number of data points `τ`.

**Conclusion:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a valuable and well-founded contribution. The fusion of Koopman theory and kernel methods is elegant and impactful. The primary weaknesses lie in the experimental section, which is more illustrative than evaluative, and in the positioning with respect to closely related work. However, the strengths of the core idea and theoretical development are substantial. The work is significant and likely to inspire follow-up research. Therefore, I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above to improve the paper's completeness and impact.
