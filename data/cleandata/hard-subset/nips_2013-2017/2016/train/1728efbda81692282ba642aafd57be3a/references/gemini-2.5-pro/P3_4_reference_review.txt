### Summary

This paper presents a method for performing Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems within the framework of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to generalize Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a popular data-driven technique from fluid dynamics, by replacing the linear space of observables with a high-dimensional feature space induced by a kernel function. This "kernel DMD" (kDMD) allows for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions using a rich, non-parametric set of basis functions. The authors derive a principled algorithm based on QR decomposition in the feature space and then present a more practical and robust version that projects the Koopman operator onto the principal components found via Kernel PCA. The utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues is demonstrated through several applications, including short-term prediction, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection.

### Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)

1.  **Principled Integration of Fields:** The paper provides an elegant and theoretically sound bridge between the Koopman operator theory/DMD, popular in the physical sciences, and kernel methods, a cornerstone of machine learning. This synthesis is highly valuable, making a powerful non-parametric tool (kernels) accessible for a fundamental problem in dynamics, and providing a new perspective on Koopman analysis for the ML community.

2.  **Methodological Soundness and Maturity:** The authors present two versions of their algorithm. The first (Section 3), based on a direct QR decomposition in the feature space, is theoretically clean and serves to establish the core principle. Crucially, the authors recognize its potential numerical instability—a well-known issue in related methods like Arnoldi iteration or standard DMD—and propose a robust alternative (Section 4) based on a projection onto Kernel PCA (POD) bases. This foresight and the provision of a robust, practical algorithm demonstrate a deep understanding of both the theory and its practical implementation challenges.

3.  **Compelling and Diverse Applications:** A major strength of the paper is Section 7, which goes far beyond a simple demonstration of the algorithm. The authors showcase how the learned spectral features can be used for sophisticated downstream tasks:
    *   **Prediction:** The use of a pre-image technique to map predictions back to the original space is a non-trivial and necessary step.
    *   **Dynamics Recognition:** Framing dynamics recognition as a problem of measuring the distance (via kernel principal angles) between subspaces spanned by Koopman eigenfunctions is a creative and powerful application.
    *   **Change-Point Detection:** The formulation of a change-point score based on the evolution of the Koopman operator is novel and addresses an important problem.
    These applications effectively argue that the proposed method is not just a new decomposition technique but a useful tool for feature extraction in the context of dynamical systems.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It starts with a clear introduction to the Koopman operator and DMD, smoothly transitions to the kernelized extension, addresses practical concerns with the robust version, and concludes with strong application examples. The mathematical derivations are clear and easy to follow for a reader familiar with the prerequisites.

### Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)

1.  **Relationship to Extended DMD (EDMD):** The most significant weakness is the underexplored relationship with Williams et al. (2015), "A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator: Extending dynamic mode decomposition" (Ref [35]). EDMD proposes generalizing DMD by using a user-defined dictionary of observable functions. The proposed kDMD can be viewed as a specific, elegant instance of EDMD where the dictionary functions are the kernel feature maps `{k(x_i, ·)}` centered at the data points. While the authors cite this work, the connection should be discussed in much greater detail. A key question is: what are the advantages and disadvantages of this implicit, kernel-defined dictionary versus an explicitly chosen one (e.g., polynomials, radial basis functions) in the EDMD framework? A more explicit comparison would better situate the paper's originality. The novelty then lies less in the general idea of using nonlinear observables (which is the core of EDMD) and more in the specific, powerful, and convenient formulation using the machinery of kernel methods.

2.  **Lack of Quantitative Baselines:** The empirical evaluation is illustrative but lacks rigor in comparing against alternative methods.
    *   For the eigenvalue estimation on the Hénon map, a comparison with standard DMD and EDMD (with a reasonable basis like polynomials) would be highly informative to show where kDMD excels.
    *   For the prediction task, comparing against a standard time-series model like a Gaussian Process dynamical model would provide a valuable baseline.
    *   In the motion capture recognition task, it is unclear how the proposed method compares to other time-series classification techniques.
    Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the practical performance benefits of the proposed method over existing approaches.

3.  **Scalability and Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper does not discuss two critical practical aspects of kernel methods.
    *   **Scalability:** The method relies on constructing and manipulating Gram matrices of size τ x τ, where τ is the number of data points. The computational complexity is likely O(τ³), which makes it prohibitive for very long time series. This limitation should be acknowledged, and potential remedies (e.g., subset of regressors/Nyström approximation) could be mentioned as future work.
    *   **Hyperparameter Choice:** The performance of kDMD will be sensitive to the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters (e.g., the width of the Gaussian kernel). The paper states the kernels used but provides little to no discussion on how these were selected or how sensitive the results are to this choice. A brief discussion or sensitivity analysis would significantly strengthen the paper's practical value.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution by connecting DMD-based Koopman analysis with the rich framework of kernel methods. The methodology is sound, the presentation is clear, and the application section is particularly strong and creative. The main weakness is the insufficient discussion of the relationship with the closely related Extended DMD and the lack of quantitative comparisons to strong baselines in the experiments. Despite these points, the strengths of the paper—its elegant formulation, methodological maturity, and compelling demonstration of utility—are substantial. The work is likely to be of high interest to researchers in both machine learning and applied sciences and has the potential to inspire follow-up work. I recommend acceptance. The authors should, however, be strongly encouraged to revise the paper to explicitly detail the relationship with EDMD and, if possible, include more direct quantitative comparisons.
