### **Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"**

#### **Summary**

This paper introduces a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a method for spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems via the Koopman operator. The core idea is to lift the system's state into a high-dimensional Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) and perform the DMD analysis in this feature space. This allows the method to approximate complex Koopman eigenfunctions that are not linearly expressible in the original state variables. The authors first present a theoretically sound algorithm based on a QR decomposition in the feature space, then propose a more practical and robust version that uses kernel Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to define a subspace onto which the Koopman operator is projected. The paper demonstrates the utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features for several downstream tasks, including state prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

The work builds upon the growing literature on DMD and Koopman operator theory, connecting it to the well-established framework of kernel methods in machine learning. It is closely related to "extended DMD" (eDMD) [35], which uses a pre-defined dictionary of basis functions, but this work provides a more flexible, non-parametric approach by implicitly defining the basis functions via the choice of a kernel.

---

#### **Strengths and Weaknesses (Evaluation Criteria)**

**1. Quality**

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical development of the method is sound. The paper correctly frames standard DMD as an Arnoldi-like method on a Krylov subspace and logically extends this concept to an RKHS. Theorem 1 establishes the principle in the feature space, and Theorem 2 provides a solid justification for the robustified algorithm, connecting the eigenvectors of the projected operator `F̂` to the true Koopman eigenfunctions. The recognition that a direct QR decomposition in RKHS (Section 3) would be ill-conditioned and the subsequent development of the robustified version using kernel PCA (Section 4) shows a good understanding of both theoretical and practical issues.

*   **Weaknesses:** The empirical evaluation, while demonstrating breadth, lacks depth and rigorous comparison.
    *   The toy examples in Section 6 serve as a good sanity check but do not explore the limits of the method or compare its performance against standard DMD or eDMD, which would be a crucial benchmark.
    *   In the applications (Section 7), comparisons are either qualitative or absent. For prediction, performance is not compared against standard time-series models like Gaussian Process Dynamical Models. For dynamics recognition, the MDS plot is illustrative, but no quantitative classification accuracy is reported. The change-point detection experiment compares against a "kernel change detection method" but the results are presented visually without statistical validation or comparison on a larger set of problems.
    *   The derivation for the change-point detection score is somewhat hand-wavy. The justification for ignoring the second term in the log-likelihood ratio ("the variation of the second term is much smaller than the first one") is asserted without proof or citation, which weakens the claim's technical soundness.

**2. Clarity**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The progression from the Koopman operator, to standard DMD, to the proposed kernelized DMD is clear and easy to follow. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithms are presented in a straightforward step-by-step manner. The distinction between the idealized algorithm in Section 3 and the practical one in Section 4 is helpful for the reader.

*   **Weaknesses:** The applications section feels rushed. The prediction via pre-image is explained very briefly, relying heavily on a citation [18] without providing sufficient detail for a reader to fully grasp the implementation. Similarly, the connection between the Koopman operator for stochastic systems and the non-parametric exponential family used for change-point detection could be explained more thoroughly to make the derivation more convincing.

**3. Originality**

*   **Strengths:** The synthesis of DMD/Koopman analysis with kernel methods is a natural, elegant, and novel contribution. While the idea of using richer basis functions exists in eDMD [35], this paper provides a principled, non-parametric way to do so without requiring the user to explicitly define a dictionary of functions. The kernel implicitly provides an infinitely rich basis. The applications, particularly the use of principal angles between subspaces of Koopman eigenfunctions for dynamics recognition and the novel change-point detection score, are creative and demonstrate original thinking about how to leverage the output of the analysis.

*   **Weaknesses:** The core idea is an application of the well-known "kernel trick." While the application context is novel, the machinery (kernel PCA, projections in RKHS) is standard in machine learning. The authors rightly point out the similarity to eDMD, but could do a better job of positioning their work by explicitly articulating the advantages of their implicit, kernel-based approach over the explicit basis selection required by eDMD.

**4. Significance**

*   **Strengths:** This work is highly significant. It provides a powerful, data-driven, and model-free tool for analyzing complex nonlinear dynamical systems, a problem of great importance across science, engineering, and machine learning. By making Koopman spectral analysis more accessible and powerful through the use of kernels, this method is likely to be used and built upon by researchers in various fields. The demonstration of the method's utility not just for analysis but for concrete tasks like prediction, classification, and change detection significantly broadens its potential impact. The proposed "Koopman eigenfunction features" could become a standard technique for representation learning in dynamical systems.

*   **Weaknesses:** The immediate significance is somewhat tempered by the lack of strong empirical evidence demonstrating that this method outperforms existing state-of-the-art techniques for the presented application tasks. Without such evidence, it remains a promising new tool, but its practical superiority is not yet established.

---

#### **Summary of Pros and Cons**

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
*   Presents a novel and elegant fusion of Koopman theory and kernel methods.
*   The method is theoretically sound and well-motivated.
*   Provides a powerful, non-parametric tool for analyzing nonlinear dynamics without requiring explicit model specification.
*   Demonstrates impressive versatility through a wide range of interesting applications (prediction, recognition, change-point detection).
*   The work is likely to have a high impact and inspire follow-up research.

**Arguments for Rejection (Cons):**
*   The experimental validation is weak and lacks rigorous quantitative comparisons to relevant baselines (e.g., eDMD, GP models).
*   The application sections, particularly the derivation for change-point detection, lack sufficient detail and rigor.
*   The originality, while present, should be more clearly distinguished from closely related methods like extended DMD.

#### **Final Recommendation**

This is a very good paper with a strong central idea. The theoretical contribution is solid, and the potential impact is high. However, its main weakness is the empirical evaluation, which does not meet the high standards of NIPS. The claims of utility in the application domains are not fully substantiated with strong quantitative evidence against competing methods. Despite this, the novelty and significance of the core method are compelling. I lean towards acceptance, with the strong recommendation that the authors significantly bolster their experimental section for the final version, providing quantitative comparisons to demonstrate the practical advantages of their approach over existing methods. The paper introduces a valuable tool to the community, and its weaknesses are primarily in the execution of the experiments rather than the core idea itself.
