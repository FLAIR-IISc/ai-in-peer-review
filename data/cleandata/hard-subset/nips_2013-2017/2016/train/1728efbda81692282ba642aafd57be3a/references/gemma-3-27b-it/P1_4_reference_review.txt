## Peer Review: Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis

**Summary:**

This paper extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to utilize reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), aiming to improve the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions beyond the limitations of linear monomials used in traditional DMD. The authors present an algorithm based on projecting the Koopman operator onto a Krylov subspace in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition. They address potential ill-conditioning issues with a POD-based projection step and demonstrate the method on both synthetic and real-world data, exploring applications like state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamic pattern recognition.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on the growing body of work surrounding DMD and Koopman operator theory. It correctly positions itself relative to related techniques like Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD), spectral methods, and various DMD variants (exact DMD, optimized DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD, extended DMD).  The paper acknowledges the connection to subspace identification methods but notes a need for further investigation. The references are generally relevant and cover key papers in the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The extension of DMD to RKHS is mathematically well-motivated. The theorems presented appear rigorous and provide a solid foundation for the proposed algorithm.  The connection to Krylov subspace methods and the derivation of Ritz values are clearly explained.
* **Addresses a Limitation of DMD:** The core idea of using kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with non-linear basis functions is a significant strength. This expands the applicability of DMD to systems where linear approximations are insufficient.
* **Robustification Strategy:** Recognizing the potential for ill-conditioning, the authors’ incorporation of POD projection is a practical and valuable addition, mirroring approaches successfully used in standard DMD.
* **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of both synthetic (toy system, Henon map) and potentially real-world data (locomotion data, though the source isn't fully detailed in the paper) provides some empirical support for the method.  The results on the toy system are particularly convincing.
* **Applications Demonstrated:** Exploring applications beyond mode extraction (prediction, change detection, dynamics recognition) highlights the practical potential of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Detail:** While the paper *demonstrates* applications, the empirical evaluation lacks detail.  The description of the locomotion data is weak (what is the dimensionality of the data? How many sequences? etc.).  There’s a lack of quantitative performance metrics for the prediction and change-point detection tasks.  Visualizations beyond the eigenvalue plots are sparse. The parameters used for the kernel functions (e.g., kernel width) are mentioned but not justified or sensitivity analyzed.
* **Computational Complexity Not Addressed:** The computational cost of performing DMD in RKHS is likely significantly higher than traditional DMD. The paper does not address this issue, nor does it discuss scalability to high-dimensional data or large datasets. This is a crucial practical concern.
* **POD Projection Justification:** While the POD projection improves stability, the justification for this step feels somewhat ad-hoc.  A more thorough discussion of why POD directions are suitable for pre-processing in this context would be beneficial. Is there a theoretical link between the dominant POD modes and the relevant Koopman modes?
* **Clarity of Notation:** The notation is sometimes dense and could benefit from more careful attention. For example, the distinction between different types of eigenvalues (λj vs. λ̃j) and vectors isn't always immediately clear.
* **Missing Baseline Comparisons:** The change-point detection application lacks comparison to other change-point detection methods, making it difficult to assess the advantage of the proposed approach.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (7/10):** The paper is technically sound and presents a novel extension of DMD. The theoretical foundations are strong, but the lack of thorough empirical evaluation weakens the overall assessment.
* **Clarity (6/10):** The paper is generally well-written, but the density of notation and occasional lack of explanation hinder comprehension.
* **Originality (8/10):** The combination of DMD and RKHS for Koopman spectral analysis is a novel contribution. It addresses a known limitation of existing DMD methods.
* **Significance (6/10):** The potential impact of this work is moderate. While the extension to RKHS is valuable, the practical limitations related to computational complexity and scalability need to be addressed to realize its full potential.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Presents a mathematically sound and novel extension of DMD.
* Addresses a known limitation of DMD by utilizing non-linear basis functions.
* Demonstrates potential for applications beyond mode extraction.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited empirical validation and lack of quantitative results.
* Computational complexity and scalability concerns are not addressed.
* Missing baseline comparisons in applications.
* Clarity of notation could be improved.

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Borderline Reject**. The paper presents a potentially valuable contribution, but the lack of thorough empirical evaluation and discussion of practical considerations (computational complexity) are significant weaknesses. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses identified above and resubmit. Specifically, a more detailed empirical study with quantitative metrics, comparisons to baselines, and a discussion of computational costs would significantly strengthen the paper.  If the authors can convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of their approach, it would warrant a higher rating.
