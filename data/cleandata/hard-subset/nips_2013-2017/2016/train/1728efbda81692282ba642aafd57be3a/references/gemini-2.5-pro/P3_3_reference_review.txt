This paper presents a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to leverage the kernel trick to lift the time-series data into a high-dimensional Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). In this space, the authors formulate a version of DMD that can approximate the Koopman operator and its eigenfunctions using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions implicitly defined by the kernel. The paper develops two algorithmic variants: a direct formulation based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the RKHS, and a more robust version that first projects the data onto its kernel principal components, analogous to common practices in standard DMD. The utility of the extracted Koopman spectral features is demonstrated through several applications, including short-term prediction, dynamics recognition on motion capture data, and sequential change-point detection.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The synthesis of Koopman operator theory, DMD, and kernel methods is elegant and well-motivated. Standard DMD is limited by its use of linear observables, effectively restricting its ability to approximate complex Koopman eigenfunctions. While methods like Extended DMD (eDMD) address this by using a user-specified dictionary of observables, the proposed kernel DMD (k-DMD) provides a principled and powerful way to work with an infinitely rich dictionary without explicit construction. This significantly broadens the applicability of DMD-style analysis to a wider class of nonlinear systems and is a valuable contribution to the machine learning literature on learning dynamical systems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development, culminating in Theorems 1 and 2, correctly establishes the connection between the proposed algorithm and the spectral properties of the Koopman operator in the RKHS. The authors astutely recognize that a direct implementation of DMD in the feature space (Section 3) would be ill-conditioned—a well-known pitfall—and propose a robust alternative based on Kernel PCA (Section 4). This demonstrates a deep understanding of both the numerical challenges of DMD and the mechanics of kernel methods.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with a concise yet informative introduction to the Koopman operator and DMD, making the work accessible to readers not deeply familiar with the fluid dynamics literature. The progression from the mathematically direct but impractical algorithm to the robust, practical version is clear and easy to follow.

4.  **Demonstrated Applications:** The applications section is a strong point. Instead of just showing eigenvalue recovery on toy problems, the authors showcase how k-DMD can serve as a powerful feature extraction tool for downstream tasks. The dynamics recognition via subspace angles and the novel formulation for change-point detection are particularly interesting and highlight the practical potential of the method for time-series analysis, which is highly relevant to the NIPS community.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Comparison with Extended DMD (eDMD):** The most significant weakness is the limited comparison to Williams et al. (2015), "A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator: Extending dynamic mode decomposition." Kernel DMD can be viewed as a specific instance of eDMD where the dictionary of observables is the feature space of the kernel. A thorough discussion and empirical comparison are crucial for positioning the paper's contribution. For instance, how does k-DMD with a Gaussian kernel compare to eDMD with a dictionary of Gaussian RBFs centered on the data points? The key trade-off is that k-DMD automates basis selection via the kernel choice at the cost of working with a dense kernel matrix, while eDMD requires explicit basis selection but may lead to sparser, more tailored representations. This critical comparison is currently missing.

2.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The proposed algorithm relies on the eigendecomposition of τ × τ matrices (where τ is the length of the time series), leading to a computational complexity of O(τ³). This is a major limitation for long time-series data common in many machine learning applications. The paper does not address this scalability issue. A discussion of potential approximation techniques standard in the kernel literature, such as the Nyström method or Random Fourier Features, would significantly strengthen the paper and broaden its practical impact.

3.  **Experimental Rigor:**
    *   **Prediction:** The prediction application (Section 7) lacks a comparative baseline. How does the proposed pre-image prediction fare against standard time-series models like Gaussian Process dynamical models or LSTMs? The pre-image problem for kernel methods is notoriously difficult, and the performance of the chosen MDS-based approach needs to be more thoroughly evaluated against alternatives.
    *   **Hyperparameter Selection:** The choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., the width of the RBF kernel) is critical to the performance of the method, but its selection is only briefly mentioned. A discussion of the sensitivity to these hyperparameters and the methodology used to select them in the experiments would be beneficial for reproducibility and practical use.
    *   **Change-Point Detection:** The derivation of the change-point score involves a heuristic simplification (ignoring the second term in the log-likelihood ratio). While plausible, this step could be better justified, perhaps with an empirical analysis showing the relative magnitudes of the terms or a reference to similar approximations in related literature.

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a high-quality, technically sound, and novel method for nonlinear dynamical systems analysis by elegantly combining DMD with kernel methods. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the applications demonstrated are compelling and relevant to the NIPS audience. The primary weaknesses are the lack of a direct comparison with the closely related eDMD framework and the unaddressed issue of computational scalability for long time series. Addressing these points, particularly the comparison with eDMD, would substantially strengthen the paper. Despite these shortcomings, the novelty of the approach and the quality of the execution make it a strong contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and elegant fusion of Koopman theory and kernel methods.
*   Provides a principled way to perform DMD with rich, nonlinear basis functions.
*   Technically sound, with a robust algorithm that addresses known numerical issues.
*   Showcases compelling and diverse applications beyond simple system identification.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Lacks a crucial empirical and theoretical comparison to Extended DMD.
*   Fails to discuss the significant O(τ³) computational complexity and potential solutions.
*   Experimental validation for applications could be more rigorous with stronger baselines.
