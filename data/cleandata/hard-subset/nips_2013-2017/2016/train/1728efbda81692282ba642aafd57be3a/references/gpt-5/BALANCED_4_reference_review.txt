Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) framework for Koopman spectral analysis. The central idea is to work in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), define the Koopman operator KH on the feature map ϕ(x), and approximate its spectral objects by (i) constructing a Krylov matrix Mτ = [ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)], (ii) orthogonalizing it in H via a kernelized Gram–Schmidt to obtain a finite-dimensional projection Pτ of KH, and (iii) extracting empirical Ritz values/vectors (λ̃j, φ̃j). A “robustified” variant projects onto kernel-PCA directions (akin to POD in DMD practice) to improve conditioning; the resulting finite-dimensional operator F̂ in the feature subspace is eigendecomposed, and its eigenvectors are mapped back to H to obtain a set of empirical Koopman modes/eigenfunctions. The paper then demonstrates applications to short-term prediction via a pre-image heuristic, dynamics embedding and recognition via principal angles between subspaces spanned by estimated Koopman eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection via a kernel exponential family likelihood-ratio score.

This line of work is closely connected to Extended DMD (EDMD) and its kernelized variant (often called KDMD). Williams, Kevrekidis, and Rowley (J. Nonlinear Sci., 2015) formalized EDMD with arbitrary dictionaries and explicitly described the kernel trick for large dictionaries, along with stabilized, SVD-based implementations. The present paper’s robustified algorithm—projection via kernel PCA/SVD followed by an eigendecomposition in a reduced subspace—is methodologically very close to KDMD. The connection to Koopman spectral theory and practical issues around ill-conditioning and truncation are extensively discussed in the EDMD/KDMD literature; the manuscript cites [35] but undersells the overlap.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: kernels provide richer nonlinear observables than linear monomials used by standard DMD.
- The RKHS formulation and the use of Gram matrices avoid explicit feature construction and lead to practical algorithms.
- Robustified variant via kernel PCA addresses known numerical issues and is consistent with best practices in DMD/EDMD.
- The paper sketches theoretical connections: an RKHS version of the Arnoldi/Ritz viewpoint (Theorem 1) and a consistency claim when Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the kernel PCA span (Theorem 2).
- Applications beyond spectral estimation (prediction via pre-image, subspace-based recognition, and change-point detection) are interesting and broaden impact.

Weaknesses
- Originality: The core algorithmic contribution overlaps substantially with EDMD/KDMD. The robustification via kernel PCA/SVD and the reduced operator F̂ are standard in KDMD; the paper does not delineate clear technical novelty beyond re-deriving known constructions in slightly different notation.
- Theory: Assumptions in Theorem 2 (Koopman eigenfunctions in Φ and in span(U)) are strong and essentially restate the standard EDMD/KDMD consistency premise. Proofs are sketched and mix objects (scalar eigenfunctions vs H-valued feature maps) in ways that could be made more rigorous; the operator being diagonalizable with distinct eigenvalues is a strong assumption; the identity MτF = KHMτ in infinite-dimensional settings warrants justification.
- Clarity/notation: The paper conflates multiple uses of φ: scalar Koopman eigenfunctions vs H-valued vectors (e.g., φ̃j). KH acts on H-valued maps (ϕ), while classical Koopman acts on scalar observables; this shift needs clearer, consistent notation and definitions. There are several typos and places where indices and matrix dimensions are unclear.
- Claims: “For a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on the unit circle” is not generally true for dissipative systems; point spectra commonly lie inside the unit disk while continuous spectra may occur on the unit circle. Later figures also show eigenvalues off the unit circle, contradicting the earlier claim.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments are small-scale and largely qualitative. There is no systematic comparison against EDMD/KDMD, exact/optimized DMD, or other Koopman-learning methods. No ablations on kernel choice, truncation rank, or noise robustness; no timing/memory analysis; no quantitative metrics for prediction, recognition, or change detection (e.g., RMSE, ROC/delay).
- Practicalities: The pre-image step relies on an MDS-like heuristic; its stability, computational cost, and sensitivity to kernel hyperparameters are not studied. Scaling to large τ or datasets is not discussed (e.g., Nyström approximations, mini-batching).

Quality
- Technical soundness is mixed: the algorithms are in line with KDMD practices, but the theoretical statements need tighter conditions and clearer operator setting. Empirics are not sufficient to validate superiority or robustness.

Clarity
- The paper is readable but notation is overloaded and some derivations are terse. The relationship between the RKHS Koopman operator and the classical Koopman operator should be clarified. Editing is needed to correct typos and improve organization.

Originality
- Limited. Kernelized DMD with SVD-based stabilization is established. The applications are reasonable but not novel in methodology.

Significance
- As written, significance is modest due to overlap with prior work and limited empirical validation. With stronger comparative experiments and clearer theoretical framing, significance could improve.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Solid, practically useful presentation of kernelized Koopman/DMD ideas.
- Interesting application avenues (recognition, change detection).

Cons:
- Overlaps substantially with EDMD/KDMD; unclear novelty.
- Theoretical claims need tightening; some incorrect statements.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and lacks comparisons.

Constructive suggestions
1) Positioning and novelty: Clearly distinguish your contributions from EDMD/KDMD. If your derivation via kernelized QR/Arnoldi or the POD-based robustification is new, formalize the differences. Otherwise, present the work as a unifying perspective and focus on novel applications or theory.
2) Operator/notation clarity: Define precisely the spaces and operators. Use distinct symbols for scalar eigenfunctions (ψj), H-valued eigenvectors (vj), and feature maps (ϕ). Clarify when you approximate the Koopman operator acting on a finite dictionary vs the feature map.
3) Theory: Provide rigorous conditions for Theorems 1–2 (boundedness, compactness, spectral assumptions), and discuss convergence (to Galerkin/EDMD-type limits). Fix the statement about eigenvalues on the unit circle.
4) Algorithms and complexity: Replace Gram–Schmidt with SVD-based orthogonalization in H via Gram matrices (numerically more stable); discuss computational complexity, memory, and scaling tricks (Nyström, randomized SVD).
5) Empirical validation: Add quantitative benchmarks against EDMD/KDMD, exact/optimized DMD on toy, chaotic, and noisy datasets; report prediction RMSE vs horizon, eigenvalue/mode errors, and robustness to noise. For recognition, report classification accuracy; for change detection, report ROC, average detection delay and false-alarm rate.
6) Pre-image: Compare pre-image strategies (fixed-point, gradient-based, Nyström regression); study sensitivity to kernel width and neighbor count.
7) Reproducibility: Include hyperparameter choices, truncation ranks, and code sufficient to reproduce all figures.

Recommendation: Reject (as is)
Rationale: The manuscript presents a kernelized DMD framework that closely parallels established EDMD/KDMD methods, with limited theoretical novelty and insufficient empirical evaluation and comparisons. With clearer positioning, strengthened theory, corrected statements, and comprehensive experiments, a future version could be competitive.
