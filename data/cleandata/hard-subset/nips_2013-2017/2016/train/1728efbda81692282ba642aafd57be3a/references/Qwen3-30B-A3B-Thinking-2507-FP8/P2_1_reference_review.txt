

# Review: "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors build upon the standard DMD approach, which approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials of observables (akin to a one-term Taylor expansion), by employing kernel functions to provide a richer set of nonlinear basis functions. The paper develops a modal decomposition algorithm based on this idea, presents a robust implementation using POD bases to avoid ill-conditioning, and demonstrates applications including state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The work relates to Williams et al.'s extended DMD (2015), which also uses pre-determined basis functions rather than monomials of observables. The authors position their kernel-based approach as providing a more expressive set of basis functions compared to extended DMD, though the paper could more clearly articulate this distinction.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical development is sound, with clear proofs of key theorems (Theorems 1 and 2).
- The algorithm is well-described, with a clear distinction between the theoretical approach and the practical implementation.
- The authors address a known issue with DMD (ill-conditioning) by proposing a robust implementation using POD bases, which is a practical contribution.
- The empirical validation on synthetic systems (toy system and Hénon map) and locomotion data provides reasonable support for the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough comparison with existing kernel-based DMD methods and extended DMD. It would be valuable to see how the proposed method compares in terms of accuracy and computational efficiency.
- There is no theoretical analysis of the approximation error or computational complexity compared to standard DMD or kernel-based variants.
- The empirical evaluation is limited to just two synthetic examples and one real-world application (locomotion data). More extensive experiments on diverse datasets would strengthen the claims.
- The paper claims that the method "fully recovers" Koopman eigenfunctions when the Krylov subspace is sufficiently large, but doesn't provide theoretical guarantees about the size of the subspace required for accurate approximation.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, progressing logically from theoretical foundations to algorithm development to applications.
- The mathematical notation is consistent and the proofs are clear.
- The figures (particularly Figure 1 showing estimated eigenvalues) effectively illustrate the results.

**Weaknesses:**
- The connection between the theoretical development and practical implementation could be clearer. The paper mentions that Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in RKHS can be reduced to operations on a Gram matrix, but doesn't provide explicit details on how this is done in practice.
- Some sections (particularly Sections 3 and 4) contain dense mathematical notation that might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with RKHS or Koopman operators. A more intuitive explanation of key concepts would improve accessibility.
- The empirical results section (Section 7) could better contextualize the results by comparing with standard DMD or other kernel-based methods.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The use of RKHS for Koopman spectral analysis represents a natural extension of existing DMD approaches, providing a more expressive set of basis functions than standard DMD.
- The robust implementation using POD bases is a practical contribution that addresses a known limitation of DMD.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty is somewhat limited. The paper builds directly on extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), and the kernel-based approach is a natural extension rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.
- The paper doesn't clearly articulate how the proposed method differs from other kernel-based DMD approaches in the literature (e.g., those using kernel PCA for DMD).
- The related work section (Section 5) cites Williams et al.'s extended DMD but doesn't thoroughly contrast it with the proposed method.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in nonlinear dynamics - approximating the Koopman operator for complex systems.
- The applications demonstrated (state prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) are relevant to multiple fields including fluid dynamics, control theory, and machine learning.
- The approach could potentially be useful for analyzing complex systems where linear approximations are insufficient.

**Weaknesses:**
- The significance is somewhat limited by the lack of clear demonstration of superior performance compared to existing methods.
- The applications shown are somewhat standard for DMD-based methods, so it's not clear how this specific kernel-based approach provides unique advantages.
- The paper doesn't establish how the proposed method advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way for the specific applications presented.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- The theoretical foundation is sound and well-developed.
- The practical implementation addresses a known limitation of DMD (ill-conditioning).
- The paper provides a clear exposition of the methodology and demonstrates reasonable results on synthetic and real-world data.
- The work is well-motivated and addresses an important problem in nonlinear dynamics.

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- The novelty is limited compared to existing DMD variants.
- The empirical evaluation is insufficient to fully establish the method's advantages.
- The paper lacks detailed comparisons with related methods that would better establish the contribution.
- The theoretical analysis of approximation quality and computational complexity is missing.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a thorough comparison with existing kernel-based DMD methods and extended DMD in terms of both accuracy and computational efficiency.
2. Provide more extensive empirical validation on diverse datasets to better demonstrate the method's strengths and limitations.
3. Include a theoretical analysis of the approximation error and computational complexity.
4. Clarify the distinction between the proposed method and Williams et al.'s extended DMD in the introduction and related work.
5. Consider adding a discussion of the practical limitations of the method (e.g., how kernel choice affects performance, scalability to large datasets).

## Final Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods that addresses a practical issue with the standard DMD approach. While the novelty is somewhat limited, the work is well-executed and provides a useful contribution to the growing literature on Koopman operator theory. With more comprehensive empirical validation and clearer articulation of the method's advantages over existing approaches, this paper would be significantly stronger. As it stands, it makes a reasonable contribution to the field but doesn't fully demonstrate the significance of the approach. I recommend weak acceptance with the expectation that the authors address the suggested improvements in a revised version.
