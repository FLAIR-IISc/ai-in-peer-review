

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors build upon the standard DMD framework, which approximates Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions using linear monomials of observables, by introducing kernel methods to allow for richer, nonlinear basis functions. The paper develops a theoretical framework for performing this analysis, provides a robust implementation via projection onto POD directions, and demonstrates applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The work relates to established literature on Koopman operator theory (Mezić, 2013; Rowley et al., 2009), DMD (Schmid, 2010), and kernel methods (Schölkopf et al., 1999), while extending these concepts to a more expressive functional space.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by extending DMD to RKHS, providing a mathematically rigorous framework for Koopman spectral analysis. Theorems 1 and 2 establish the theoretical foundations with appropriate proofs, demonstrating how kernel methods can recover Koopman eigenfunctions beyond the linear approximation of standard DMD. The connection to existing work, particularly Extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), is well-articulated, and the authors correctly identify the limitation of standard DMD in capturing global nonlinear dynamics.

**Practical Implementation:** The robustification step via POD projection addresses a known practical limitation of DMD (as noted in Chen et al., 2012), making the method more viable for real-world applications. The authors provide a clear algorithmic description for both the basic and robustified versions, which would be valuable for practitioners.

**Demonstrated Applications:** The paper successfully demonstrates multiple applications of the extracted features, including prediction via preimage, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection. The empirical examples on synthetic systems (toy nonlinear system, Hénon map) and real-world locomotion data provide reasonable validation of the approach's utility. The visualization in Figure 3 (MDS embedding of locomotion data) is particularly compelling.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Comparative Evaluation:** A significant weakness is the lack of comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods. While the paper references Extended DMD, it doesn't provide a side-by-side comparison of performance metrics (e.g., prediction accuracy, computational efficiency, or mode extraction quality) against existing methods. Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the kernel-based approach provides meaningful improvements over existing techniques.

**Narrow Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation is limited to two synthetic examples and one real-world dataset (locomotion data). More extensive testing on diverse, challenging benchmark datasets would strengthen the paper's claims. The paper would benefit from showing results on larger, more complex systems where standard DMD might fail due to its linear approximation limitations.

**Insufficient Differentiation from Related Work:** While Extended DMD is mentioned as a related approach, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution. The authors state that their method is "similar to the robust-version of our algorithm" but don't elaborate on the specific advantages of using kernel-based basis functions over the pre-determined basis functions in Extended DMD. A more detailed comparison of the mathematical and practical differences would strengthen the paper's originality claim.

**Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of the kernel-based approach compared to standard DMD. For large datasets, kernel methods can become computationally expensive due to the Gram matrix calculations. The authors should discuss the scalability of their approach and potential mitigations for large-scale applications.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance Assessment

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical derivations. However, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient depth to fully support the claims of superiority over existing methods. The authors should provide more comprehensive empirical validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation. The theorems and proofs are logically presented, though some technical details in the robustification step might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with kernel methods.

**Originality:** The use of kernel methods for Koopman spectral analysis is a novel combination of techniques. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish this contribution from Extended DMD and other kernel-based approaches to dynamical systems analysis. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the originality claim.

**Significance:** The Koopman operator framework is an important area of study, and the extension to RKHS could be valuable for practitioners. However, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this approach solves problems that cannot be addressed with existing techniques or provides substantially better results on challenging benchmarks. The significance would be greatly enhanced with more comprehensive comparisons and demonstrations on more complex systems.

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. **Expand Empirical Evaluation:** Conduct a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods (including Extended DMD) on multiple benchmark datasets, particularly those where standard DMD is known to perform poorly. Include quantitative metrics (e.g., prediction error, mode recovery accuracy) to demonstrate relative performance.

2. **Clarify Distinction from Related Work:** Provide a detailed comparison between the proposed kernel DMD and Extended DMD, highlighting specific advantages of the kernel-based approach. Discuss how the kernel method's flexibility in basis functions leads to tangible benefits in practice.

3. **Address Computational Considerations:** Analyze the computational complexity of the proposed method and discuss potential scalability solutions for large datasets. Consider including runtime comparisons with standard DMD.

4. **Enhance Real-World Validation:** Demonstrate the method on additional real-world applications where nonlinear dynamics are particularly challenging (e.g., climate modeling, neuroscience, or robotics), showing clear advantages over existing techniques.

5. **Improve Theoretical Justification:** While the theoretical foundation is strong, the paper could better articulate why the kernel-based approach provides a fundamental improvement over linear approximations in specific dynamical regimes.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods for Koopman spectral analysis. While the core idea is promising and the theoretical development is rigorous, the paper falls short in demonstrating the practical advantages of the proposed approach through comprehensive empirical validation and comparison with existing methods. With the suggested improvements, particularly a more thorough comparative evaluation and clearer differentiation from related work, this could be a valuable contribution to the field. As currently written, the paper's significance is somewhat limited by the lack of convincing evidence that the kernel-based approach provides substantial improvements over existing techniques.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution, the lack of comprehensive empirical validation and comparison with state-of-the-art methods prevents it from meeting NeurIPS's standard for significance and quality. The paper would benefit from substantially more thorough evaluation before it can be considered for publication.
