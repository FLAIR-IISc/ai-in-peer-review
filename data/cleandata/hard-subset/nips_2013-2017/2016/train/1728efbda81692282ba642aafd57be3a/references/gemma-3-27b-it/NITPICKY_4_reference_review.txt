This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions. While the core idea of leveraging kernel methods to enhance DMD's representational power is conceptually sound and potentially valuable, the paper suffers from a multitude of shortcomings in presentation, technical detail, and justification of choices, ultimately leading to a recommendation for rejection.

The introduction, while adequately framing the problem, is verbose and lacks conciseness. The connection between Bayesian approaches and decomposition methods feels somewhat forced, and the justification for extending DMD feels weakly motivated – merely stating linear monomials are insufficient doesn't constitute strong motivation without illustrating scenarios where this limitation demonstrably hinders performance.

Section 2 provides a necessary background but could benefit from a more critical discussion of DMD’s limitations beyond the stated one. The leap from theoretical eigenfunctions to practical approximations via finite data sequences isn't fully explained, and the comparison to the Arnoldi method feels cursory.

The core technical contribution in Section 3, while attempting to extend DMD with RKHS, is presented with concerning lack of rigor. The justification for assuming φ ∈ RKHS feels superficial (“almost of the theoretical claims… do not necessarily require… but this assumption should perform the calculation in practice”). This assumption is pivotal, and a more detailed explanation of its implications and alternatives is required. The theorem presented (Theorem 1) is stated without sufficient context – the conditions for its validity aren't entirely clear. The transition from the continuous operator to the discrete implementation via Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization is also glossed over, making it difficult to assess the algorithm's practical stability.  A key weakness is the absence of any discussion of kernel selection – what guidelines should practitioners follow? The computational cost of performing Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the potentially high-dimensional RKHS isn't discussed either.

Section 4, introducing POD projection, is presented as a remedy for an ill-conditioned algorithm *introduced by the authors' method*. This is a tacit acknowledgement of a significant problem with the proposed approach. While the idea of projecting onto POD directions is reasonable, it’s presented as a ‘fix’ rather than an integral part of the algorithm, and the justification isn’t compelling enough to offset the initial ill-conditioning. The connection between Theorem 2 and the overall algorithm’s effectiveness feels unclear – what are the conditions under which the approximation of κj holds?

The Related Works section is surprisingly brief considering the extensive literature on DMD, Koopman operator theory, and kernel methods. Several relevant works are missing, diminishing the paper's perceived novelty and positioning within the field (e.g., kernelized DMD variants are not discussed).

The empirical evaluation (Section 6) is the weakest aspect of the paper.  The ‘toy’ system is overly simplistic, and the Hénon map, while chaotic, doesn’t offer a stringent test of the algorithm's ability to handle complex nonlinear dynamics.  The figures are poorly labeled, and there’s a lack of quantitative analysis – simply *showing* eigenvalues doesn’t demonstrate accuracy. The choice of kernel parameters is arbitrary and not justified.  Crucially, there’s no comparison to existing DMD variants. How does the proposed method perform relative to optimized DMD or sparse DMD on these examples?  Without this comparison, the empirical results offer little convincing evidence of the algorithm’s benefits.

The applications section (Section 7) presents a wide range of potential applications but offers only superficial demonstrations. The prediction section lacks details about the pre-image recovery process, and the change-point detection example doesn't explain why the proposed method outperforms the kernel change detection method. The embedding and recognition of dynamics section uses a dataset (CMU Motion Capture) without a clear explanation of why this dataset is relevant or how the extracted features improve upon existing state-of-the-art methods.

Overall, the paper feels incomplete and lacks the technical depth and rigorous empirical validation expected of a NIPS submission. Numerous minor flaws (e.g., inconsistent notation, vague statements, missing details) detract from the clarity and impact of the work.



**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lack of rigorous theoretical justification for key assumptions.
*   Ill-conditioning of the proposed algorithm requiring a post-hoc POD projection.
*   Weak empirical evaluation with insufficient comparison to existing methods.
*   Superficial treatment of applications.
*   Insufficiently comprehensive related work section.
*   Numerous minor technical and presentational flaws.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The idea of extending DMD with RKHS is conceptually interesting.
*   The paper attempts to address a known limitation of DMD.



**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper, despite a potentially interesting idea, falls short of the NIPS standard due to its technical shortcomings, lack of thorough evaluation, and insufficient clarity. Substantial revisions addressing the aforementioned concerns would be necessary for reconsideration.
