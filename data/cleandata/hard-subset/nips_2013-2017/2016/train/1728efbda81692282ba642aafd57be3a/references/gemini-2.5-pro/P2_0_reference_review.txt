### Summary

This paper presents a method for performing Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to lift the system's state into a high-dimensional feature space using a kernel function, where the action of the nonlinear dynamics can be approximated by a linear Koopman operator. The authors propose two algorithms: a direct method based on a Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in the feature space, and a more numerically robust version that first projects the data onto its principal components in the feature space (via Kernel PCA) before computing the operator. The extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues are then demonstrated to be useful features for several downstream tasks, including short-term prediction, recognition of dynamic patterns, and sequential change-point detection.

### Review

This paper tackles the important problem of analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems from data. It successfully bridges the gap between DMD, a popular technique in the physics and fluid dynamics communities, and kernel methods, a cornerstone of machine learning. The proposed "kernel DMD" (kDMD) is a natural and elegant generalization of the standard DMD framework.

#### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development in Sections 3 and 4 is sound and logically progresses from a direct, but potentially unstable, formulation to a robust, practical algorithm. Theorem 1 correctly establishes the decomposition in the feature space, analogous to standard DMD. Recognizing the potential for numerical instability, the authors astutely propose a robustification strategy in Section 4 using Kernel PCA, which is analogous to the SVD/POD truncation used in many modern DMD variants. Theorem 2 provides a clear connection between the eigenvectors of the resulting low-rank operator and the true Koopman eigenfunctions, under the reasonable (though strong) assumption that the eigenfunctions lie within the span of the principal components of the data. The algorithms are presented clearly and appear to be correct derivations.

The experimental evaluation, while not exhaustive, effectively illustrates the method's capabilities. The eigenvalue recovery on the toy system and the Hénon map demonstrates that the core spectral analysis works as intended. The application sections show promising results and creativity in using the learned spectral features.

However, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened.
1.  **Prediction:** The prediction task is compared only against the ground truth, without any baseline methods. It would be valuable to see how kDMD compares against other standard nonlinear time-series forecasting models like Gaussian Process dynamical models or LSTMs, especially on the chaotic Hénon map. The pre-image reconstruction method is also non-trivial, and its performance and limitations are not deeply explored.
2.  **Change-Point Detection:** The derivation of the change-point detection score is somewhat heuristic, particularly the step of ignoring the log-partition function term. While this simplification may be practical, its justification is brief. The comparison is made against only one other kernel-based method on a single dataset. A more thorough evaluation on benchmark change-point datasets would make the claim more convincing.

#### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of first introducing the necessary background on Koopman operators and DMD, making the paper accessible to a broader machine learning audience. The progression from the standard DMD to the proposed kernelized version is clear and easy to follow. The inclusion of two distinct algorithms (the direct and the robust versions) and their theoretical justifications in Theorems 1 and 2 adds significant clarity. The figures are informative and support the claims made in the text. The remark that the method only requires pairs of consecutive states, not necessarily a single long trajectory, is a valuable practical clarification.

#### Originality

The idea of combining kernel methods with spectral analysis of dynamical systems is not entirely new. The paper's most significant connection is to Extended DMD (eDMD) by Williams et al. [35], which the authors cite. eDMD generalizes DMD to a user-defined dictionary of observable functions. The proposed kDMD can be viewed as a specific instance of eDMD where the dictionary functions are the kernel functions centered at the data points, i.e., $\{k(x, \cdot)\}_{i=1}^\tau$. The robust algorithm in Section 4 is procedurally very similar to applying eDMD with this kernel-based dictionary.

The paper would be stronger if it more explicitly discussed and distinguished its contribution from eDMD. While the formulation from an RKHS perspective is elegant and provides a different theoretical lens, the practical algorithm is highly related. The novelty of this work, therefore, lies less in the fundamental idea of using nonlinear basis functions for DMD (which eDMD introduced), and more in:
1.  The principled use of the kernel trick to implicitly work with a very rich, data-dependent dictionary of functions.
2.  The elegant connection to Kernel PCA for robustification.
3.  The novel applications, particularly the use of kernel principal angles for dynamics recognition and the CUSUM-like test for change-point detection, which showcase how to effectively use the features extracted by this method.

#### Significance

The work is significant as it provides a powerful, data-driven tool for nonlinear system analysis that is accessible and understandable to both the machine learning and dynamical systems communities. DMD is a highly impactful method, but its vanilla form is limited by linearity. This paper provides a non-parametric, flexible way to overcome this limitation. By leveraging the power of kernels, the method can potentially capture complex dynamics without requiring the user to manually engineer a suitable set of basis functions, which is a major challenge when applying eDMD in practice.

The applications presented are compelling and suggest that the learned Koopman eigenfunctions are indeed meaningful features. The dynamics recognition on motion capture data is a particularly strong example of the method's potential. Researchers working on time-series analysis, representation learning for dynamical systems, and control are likely to find this work useful and build upon it. The provided code is also a valuable contribution that will facilitate adoption.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Elegant and Principled Generalization:** The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated generalization of DMD to RKHS, bridging two important fields.
2.  **Practical Algorithm:** The authors provide a robust, practical algorithm based on Kernel PCA that addresses the known numerical issues of DMD-like methods.
3.  **Novel and Interesting Applications:** The paper demonstrates the utility of the extracted features on diverse tasks like prediction, embedding, and change-point detection, highlighting the method's versatility.
4.  **High Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, making a complex topic accessible.

### Arguments for Rejection:

1.  **Limited Discussion of Originality:** The contribution relative to Extended DMD [35] is not sufficiently articulated. The proposed algorithm is very similar to an application of eDMD with a kernel dictionary.
2.  **Preliminary Experimental Baselines:** The experiments lack comparisons to state-of-the-art alternative methods for the application tasks (prediction, change-point detection), making it difficult to assess the performance in a broader context.
3.  **No Discussion of Hyperparameters:** The choice of kernel and its parameters is critical for performance, yet there is no discussion of how these were selected or how sensitive the method is to them.

### Final Assessment

Overall, this is a very good paper. The strengths—a technically sound and elegant method, a practical algorithm, and compelling applications—outweigh the weaknesses. The primary concern regarding originality relative to eDMD is valid, but the RKHS formulation and the specific applications are novel enough to be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors revise the paper to more clearly situate their work with respect to Extended DMD and, if space permits, to add more comparative baselines to their experiments.
