This paper presents a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), termed kernel DMD (kDMD), for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems via the Koopman operator. The main idea is to lift the system's state space into a high-dimensional Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) and then apply the principles of DMD in this feature space. This allows for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions using rich, nonlinear basis functions derived from the kernel, in contrast to standard DMD which is typically limited to linear observables. The authors first present a direct formulation based on a Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in the feature space (Section 3), and then propose a more robust and practical algorithm based on projecting the Koopman operator onto the principal components in the feature space (i.e., Kernel PCA), which is analogous to the common practice of using POD modes to stabilize standard DMD (Section 4). The utility of the method is demonstrated on several applications: state prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

**Strengths**

1.  The core concept of extending DMD to RKHS is a logical and powerful step. It provides a principled way to move beyond linear observables and approximate Koopman eigenfunctions in a flexible, data-driven, non-parametric fashion.
2.  The theoretical development is sound. The paper provides formal theorems (Theorem 1 and 2) that connect the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the constructed finite-dimensional operators to the true Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions under specific assumptions. This provides a solid mathematical foundation for the proposed algorithms.
3.  The robustified version of the algorithm, which uses Kernel PCA for dimensionality reduction, is a crucial and practical contribution that addresses the well-known numerical stability issues of methods based on Krylov subspaces.
4.  The exploration of diverse applications demonstrates the potential of the extracted spectral features as a meaningful representation of the underlying dynamics. The change-point detection example, in particular, appears promising.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper presents a compelling core idea, it is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its execution, ranging from critical issues of originality to a general lack of rigor and care in its presentation.

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The most significant weakness is the insufficient discussion of the relationship with Extended DMD (eDMD) by Williams et al. [35]. The authors briefly mention that their robust version is "similar" to eDMD. This is an understatement. eDMD provides a framework for Koopman analysis using a general dictionary of observable functions. The proposed kDMD can be viewed as a specific instance of eDMD where the dictionary consists of kernel functions centered on the training data points (`ψi(x) = k(x, xi)`). This connection is not adequately explored or clarified. A thorough comparison is necessary to precisely delineate the novel contributions of this work over simply applying the eDMD framework with a particular choice of kernel-based dictionary. Without this, the originality of the core contribution is highly questionable.

2.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is written in a very dense style that hinders understanding and reproducibility.
    *   The algorithmic descriptions are terse. For instance, the kernelized Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in Section 3 is not explained; the reader is merely pointed to a textbook. Key details of the implementation are omitted.
    *   The application descriptions are vague. The pre-image technique for prediction is described at a very high level, referencing a similar idea in [18] without providing enough detail for the reader to understand the specific implementation. The derivation of the change-point detection score relies on hand-wavy arguments, such as claiming a term can be ignored because its variation is "much smaller" with reference to a different context in [7]. This lacks the rigor expected in a NIPS paper.
    *   The "Remark" at the end of Section 4 mentions that the method can use collections of state pairs instead of a single time series. This is a critical practical detail that fundamentally alters the data requirements and applicability of the method, and it should have been presented and discussed more prominently.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical results are not presented convincingly.
    *   The analysis of the Hénon map results is superficial. The text states that "the part of the eigenvalues of the equilibrium points seem to be approximately estimated," which is imprecise and uninformative. Which eigenvalues were found? Why not others? What does "approximately" mean in this context?
    *   The legend of Figure 1 (right) includes "Equilibrium DMD," a method that is never defined or discussed in the paper, which is confusing for the reader.

4.  **Attention to Detail and Formatting:** The manuscript exhibits a notable lack of care in its preparation, which is unacceptable for a top-tier publication.
    *   There are multiple grammatical errors (e.g., "almost of the theoretical claims", "as describe in").
    *   The bibliography is exceptionally sloppy. Numerous entries contain extraneous commas (e.g., [1], [2], [7]), are incomplete (e.g., [9]), or are inconsistently formatted. This carelessness detracts significantly from the paper's professional quality.
    *   Footnotes consist of raw URLs, which is not a standard or durable citation practice.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper is based on an elegant and theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHSs. It addresses a relevant problem and showcases the method's potential across several interesting applications.

*   **Arguments against Acceptance:** (1) The originality is in serious doubt due to the strong and undiscussed overlap with Extended DMD. (2) The paper is poorly written, with key algorithmic and applicative details being either omitted or vaguely described. (3) The experimental validation is weak and lacks depth. (4) The manuscript is riddled with formatting and grammatical errors, demonstrating a lack of care that falls far below the standards of NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Reject

While the core idea of this paper has merit, the execution is deeply flawed. The questionable originality, combined with the poor clarity, weak experimental support, and the sheer number of minor yet distracting errors, makes the paper unsuitable for publication at NIPS in its current form. The authors would need to perform a major revision, including a thorough and honest comparison to eDMD, a significant expansion and clarification of their methods and experiments, and a meticulous proofreading of the entire manuscript before it could be reconsidered.
