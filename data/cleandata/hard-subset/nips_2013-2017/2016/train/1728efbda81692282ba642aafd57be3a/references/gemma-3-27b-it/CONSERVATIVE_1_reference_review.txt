This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions, aiming to overcome limitations of traditional DMD which relies on linear monomials. While the core idea is conceptually sound – leveraging the expressiveness of kernel methods – the presentation lacks sufficient novelty and rigorous validation to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

The authors correctly identify the limitations of standard DMD and the potential benefits of using RKHS for improved approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions. The mathematical formulation appears correct, and the connection to existing DMD frameworks is clearly articulated. However, the paper largely *states* potential advantages rather than *demonstrating* them convincingly. The theoretical framework feels like a natural extension, but not a groundbreaking innovation. The core mathematical results (Theorems 1 & 2) are presented without significant insight beyond established principles in RKHS and spectral analysis. 

A major weakness is the limited empirical evaluation. The toy system example demonstrates the method’s ability to recover known eigenvalues, but offers little insight into its performance on more complex, realistic scenarios. The application to the Hénon map is also insufficient; merely *approximating* eigenvalues doesn't establish superiority over existing techniques.  The practical robustness enhancement via POD projection is a sensible addition, acknowledging the potential for ill-conditioning, but the discussion remains superficial. The application section, while diverse (prediction, change-point detection, dynamic recognition), lacks depth and comparative analysis. The results aren't presented with baselines to demonstrate a clear advantage over conventional methods like standard DMD, or kernel-based alternatives for system identification. The comparison to the kernel change detection method is minimal. The application section feels more like a proof-of-concept than a comprehensive evaluation.

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, but lacks critical discussion of how the proposed method distinctly improves upon existing spectral decomposition techniques, especially those incorporating kernel methods.  For example, kernel PCA, extended DMD, and subspace identification methods using kernel tricks share conceptual similarities and should be discussed in greater detail with respect to their strengths and weaknesses compared to the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

*   Mathematically sound extension of DMD.
*   Clear explanation of the underlying principles and connections to Koopman theory.
*   Addresses a potential limitation of standard DMD (reliance on linear monomials).
*   Inclusion of a practical robustness enhancement via POD.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty – the extension to RKHS feels natural and predictable.
*   Insufficient empirical validation – limited datasets and lack of comparative analysis.
*   Superficial discussion of robustness and practical considerations.
*   Inadequate comparison to related work, particularly kernel-based alternatives.
*   Application section lacks depth and convincing results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a mathematically sound extension of DMD.
*   The idea of using RKHS for Koopman eigenfunction approximation is reasonable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of compelling empirical evidence demonstrating a significant advantage over existing techniques.
*   Limited novelty – the contribution feels incremental.
*   Insufficiently rigorous validation and comparison to relevant literature.
*   The application section lacks depth and doesn't establish practical significance.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the work is technically sound and presents a logical extension of DMD, it lacks the novelty, rigorous validation, and compelling results necessary for acceptance at NIPS. The paper needs significantly more empirical work and a more thorough comparative analysis to demonstrate its value beyond a theoretical exercise. The authors should focus on showcasing concrete advantages over existing methods on challenging, real-world datasets.  Without substantial revisions addressing these concerns, the paper does not represent a significant contribution to the field.
