

# Review: Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors extend traditional DMD, which approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials of observables, to use nonlinear basis functions via kernel methods. The core contribution is a theoretical framework for performing modal decomposition in RKHS, where the Koopman operator is analyzed through the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization of the Krylov matrix in feature space. The authors further address practical implementation issues by incorporating kernel PCA to robustify the algorithm against ill-conditioning, which is a known challenge in DMD implementations.

The paper situates its work within the context of Koopman spectral analysis and DMD variants. It acknowledges that while DMD has been successfully applied to various dynamical systems, its traditional formulation uses linear basis functions that may not capture complex nonlinear dynamics. The authors note that related work includes extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), which uses pre-determined basis functions, and kernel PCA (Schölkopf et al., 1998), but their approach differs by directly operating in the RKHS without requiring pre-specified basis functions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a mathematically rigorous extension of DMD to RKHS, with clear theoretical derivations and proofs of key theorems (Theorem 1 and Theorem 2). The connection between the proposed method and traditional DMD is well-established, and the robustification approach using POD bases effectively addresses the ill-conditioning issue common in DMD implementations.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, with logical flow from theoretical foundations to algorithmic implementation and empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the relationship between the proposed method and prior work is clearly articulated. The algorithmic description (Section 4) is precise and executable.

3. **Novelty:** The use of RKHS for Koopman spectral analysis represents a meaningful extension beyond existing DMD variants. While extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) uses pre-specified basis functions, this work uses kernel functions to generate expressive nonlinear basis functions automatically, which is a distinct contribution. The paper demonstrates this novelty through both theoretical analysis and empirical results.

4. **Significance:** The proposed method has clear potential applications in nonlinear dynamics analysis, with demonstrated examples in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The empirical results on both synthetic (toy system, Hénon map) and real-world (locomotion data) datasets show promising performance, particularly in capturing eigenvalues that correspond to known dynamical features.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Empirical Comparison:** While the paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on several examples, it lacks comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods for Koopman spectral analysis. A more thorough comparison with alternative approaches (including extended DMD, sparse DMD, and other kernel-based methods) would strengthen the claims about the method's advantages.

2. **Sensitivity to Kernel Parameters:** The paper does not thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the method to kernel choice and hyperparameter selection (e.g., kernel width for Gaussian kernel). This is a critical consideration for practical implementation, as kernel parameters can significantly impact results.

3. **Computational Complexity:** The paper does not address the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to existing DMD variants. Given that kernel methods typically scale poorly with data size, this is an important consideration for real-world applications with large datasets.

4. **Limited Scope of Applications:** While the paper demonstrates three applications (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition), the evaluation of these applications is relatively brief. More extensive experimental validation would better establish the practical utility of the method.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of Koopman spectral analysis by extending DMD to operate in RKHS with nonlinear basis functions. The theoretical foundation is rigorous, the implementation is carefully designed to address practical challenges, and the empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness on relevant examples. The novelty of using kernel methods for Koopman spectral analysis is clear, and the paper appropriately situates its work within the existing literature.

While the paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, a more thorough exploration of kernel parameter sensitivity, and detailed discussion of computational complexity, these are minor limitations that do not undermine the core contribution. The work is well-suited for NIPS as it advances the theoretical understanding of Koopman spectral analysis and provides a practical algorithm with demonstrated utility across multiple applications.

The paper meets all the key criteria for NIPS: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), represents a meaningful extension of prior work (Originality), and has potential to advance the field (Significance). The main limitations are in the scope of empirical evaluation rather than the theoretical contribution.
