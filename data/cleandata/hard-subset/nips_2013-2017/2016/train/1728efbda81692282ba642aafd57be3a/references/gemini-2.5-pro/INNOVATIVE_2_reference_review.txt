### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) with the kernel trick. The core idea is to apply the Koopman operator theory, which provides a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics, in a high-dimensional Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). Standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear basis of observables, which is restrictive. By lifting the data into an RKHS via a kernel function, the proposed method, which can be called kernel DMD (kDMD), can approximate complex nonlinear Koopman eigenfunctions. The authors first derive a formulation based on a Gram-Schmidt process in the feature space and then present a more robust and practical algorithm based on kernel PCA, which projects the data onto dominant principal components before estimating the Koopman operator's projection. The paper demonstrates the potential of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features for several downstream tasks, including state prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

The work is situated at the intersection of machine learning and physics. It builds upon a rich literature on DMD and Koopman operator analysis from the physics community and connects it elegantly to the well-established framework of kernel methods in machine learning. It is conceptually related to extended DMD (eDMD), which also uses richer basis functions, but the proposed RKHS formulation offers a principled, non-parametric approach without the need to explicitly define a dictionary of basis functions.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Originality and Novelty:** The central contribution—the fusion of Koopman operator theory and kernel methods—is a bold, elegant, and highly novel idea. It addresses a fundamental limitation of standard DMD by providing a powerful, non-parametric way to enrich the basis functions for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions. This synthesis of concepts from two different fields is precisely the kind of creative and ambitious work that can open up new research avenues.

2.  **Significant Potential Impact:** By framing Koopman analysis within the familiar language of kernel methods, this work makes a powerful tool from the physical sciences much more accessible to the machine learning community. The potential applications are vast. Using Koopman spectra as features for dynamical systems is a compelling paradigm, and the paper provides intriguing proof-of-concept demonstrations in prediction, recognition, and change-point detection. This framework could have a significant impact on how we analyze and model time-series data, particularly in scientific domains where interpretability of dynamic modes (frequencies, growth rates) is valuable.

3.  **Conceptual Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the mathematical development is clear and logical. The authors do a good job of first presenting the core mathematical principle and then developing a robust, practical algorithm. The connection between the algorithm's steps (kernel PCA, eigendecomposition) and the underlying Koopman theory (approximating eigenfunctions) is well-established.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Preliminary Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation serves more as a demonstration than a rigorous validation. For the prediction task, the method is not compared against standard machine learning baselines for time-series forecasting (e.g., Gaussian Processes, LSTMs). While the paper's main contribution is the new methodology, a more thorough comparison would help to situate its practical performance. Similarly, the applications for recognition and change-point detection are illustrative but not fully developed or benchmarked.

2.  **Insufficient Discussion of Relationship to eDMD:** The paper acknowledges the similarity to extended DMD (eDMD), but the discussion is too brief. eDMD also allows for a dictionary of nonlinear observables. The key advantage of the proposed kernel method is that it implicitly uses an infinite-dimensional feature space, avoiding the difficult and often ad-hoc process of manually designing a finite dictionary of basis functions required by eDMD. This is a crucial point that should have been elaborated on more forcefully to better distinguish the contribution.

3.  **Heuristics in Applications:** Some of the proposed applications rely on heuristics or simplified assumptions. For instance, the pre-image calculation for prediction is a notoriously difficult problem for kernel methods, and the chosen approach is one of many possibilities. The derivation of the change-point detection score also involves a simplification that may not hold universally. While these do not undermine the core contribution, they highlight that the path from the proposed features to robust applications requires further research.

### Conclusion and Recommendation

This is a very strong paper that introduces a significant and exciting new idea. The conceptual leap of "kernelizing" Koopman analysis is a beautiful contribution that connects two powerful theoretical frameworks. It is a bold approach that prioritizes a novel formulation over incremental improvements on existing benchmarks.

The weaknesses identified—primarily the preliminary nature of the experiments and the brief comparison to related work—are minor in the face of the paper's novelty and potential impact. Methodological perfection and exhaustive validation are not always necessary in a paper that introduces a fundamentally new direction. The core framework is sound, elegant, and thought-provoking. This work provides a fertile ground for future research, both in refining the methodology and in developing more sophisticated applications.

I am highly enthusiastic about this submission. It represents exactly the type of creative, high-risk, high-reward research that NIPS should champion.
