- Paper extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) into Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to better approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using a Krylov-subspace-based algorithm with POD projection to address ill-conditioning.
- Demonstrations on toy problems and locomotion data show potential applications in prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.
- Strength: Theoretical soundness—well-motivated RKHS extension with a supporting theorem and clear Koopman connection.
- Strength: Addresses a core DMD limitation by using kernel methods to capture more expressive, nonlinear dynamics beyond linear monomials.
- Strength: Practical consideration of ill-conditioning via POD projection and attention to robustness.
- Strength: Presents several application scenarios, moving beyond purely theoretical exposition.
- Strength: Good overview and connections to related techniques (POD, DMD, spectral methods) in the literature.
- Weakness: Limited empirical evaluation—examples are simple and lack rigorous comparisons to state-of-the-art nonlinear system identification methods; kernel parameter choices are not justified.
- Weakness: Computational complexity and Gram matrix costs are not analyzed, creating concerns for large datasets.
- Weakness: No discussion of scalability to high-dimensional data or complex systems, limiting practical applicability.
- Weakness: Incremental novelty—kernelizing DMD is natural and relationship to extended DMD is insufficiently explored.
- Weakness: Writing is dense and occasionally hard to follow; clearer exposition and better visuals are needed.
- Review scores: Quality 7/10, Clarity 6/10, Originality 6/10, Significance 6/10.
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretically sound extension, addresses known DMD limitation, well-connected to related research.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical validation, unclear computational/scalability analysis, and moderate novelty requiring clearer positioning versus extended DMD.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept with major revisions—authors should expand empirical evaluation, analyze computational complexity and scalability, clarify novelty vs. extended DMD, and improve clarity/visuals.
