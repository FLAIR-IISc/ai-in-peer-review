

# Review: Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis

## Summary

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors extend traditional DMD, which uses linear monomials as basis functions, to employ nonlinear kernel functions for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions. They present a modal decomposition algorithm in RKHS based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization and provide a robustified version using POD (Principal Orthogonal Decomposition) bases to address numerical instability. The paper demonstrates applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using synthetic and real-world data.

The work builds on the growing literature of Koopman operator-based methods for nonlinear dynamical systems, particularly extending Williams et al.'s extended DMD (2015) by using kernel methods instead of pre-determined basis functions. It also relates to kernel PCA and the broader literature on kernel-based spectral analysis.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-justified theoretical claims. The authors provide clear theorems (Theorems 1 and 2) establishing the mathematical foundation of their approach, and the proofs appear rigorous. The extension of DMD to RKHS is a natural and well-motivated generalization that addresses a clear limitation of traditional DMD (reliance on linear approximations).

The empirical evaluation is adequate, with experiments on a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, and locomotion data. The results demonstrate that the kernel DMD can accurately estimate Koopman eigenvalues (Fig. 1), and the applications to prediction (Fig. 2), dynamics recognition (Fig. 3), and change-point detection (Fig. 4) are compelling. However, the paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, particularly with respect to computational efficiency and scalability to high-dimensional data.

The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations: "the data are sufficiently rich" (Theorem 2) and the method may suffer from numerical instability without the POD-based robustification. This demonstrates good scholarly practice.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are presented logically, with appropriate notation. The flow from background (Section 2) to proposed method (Section 3), robustification (Section 4), related work (Section 5), and applications (Sections 6-7) is coherent. The figures (1-4) effectively illustrate key results.

The paper could benefit from more detailed explanations of implementation details, such as how the kernel width is selected in practice. The connection between the theoretical development and the practical implementation could be slightly clearer. Some of the algorithmic steps (e.g., in Section 4) might be more accessible with a simple pseudocode.

## Originality

This paper makes a clear contribution to the field. While the extension of DMD to RKHS is conceptually straightforward, the authors provide a rigorous mathematical framework for this extension, which is novel in the context of Koopman spectral analysis. The use of kernel functions to capture nonlinear dynamics represents a meaningful departure from traditional DMD variants that rely on linear or polynomial bases.

The paper properly situates itself within the literature, clearly differentiating from Williams et al.'s extended DMD (which uses pre-determined basis functions) and other DMD variants. The authors have adequately referenced relevant work, including recent advances in kernel methods for dynamical systems analysis.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial for researchers working with nonlinear dynamical systems. The ability to use nonlinear basis functions for Koopman spectral analysis could enable more accurate modeling of complex systems where linear approximations fail. The demonstrated applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition show practical utility beyond the theoretical contribution.

The paper advances the state of the art by providing a more expressive representation for Koopman eigenfunctions. While the authors don't claim dramatic improvements over existing methods, they provide a theoretically sound and practically useful extension that could be valuable in applications where traditional DMD is insufficient.

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of Koopman spectral analysis. The extension of DMD to RKHS is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and supported by empirical results. The applications demonstrate the practical utility of the method.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical foundation with well-justified theorems
- Novel extension of DMD to RKHS for nonlinear basis functions
- Practical robustification using POD bases
- Demonstrated applications with compelling empirical results
- Adequate comparison with relevant literature

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison with state-of-the-art methods (beyond DMD variants)
- Some implementation details could be more explicit
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, valuable contribution to the field of Koopman spectral analysis, with solid theoretical grounding and practical applications. The extension to RKHS represents a meaningful advancement over traditional DMD, particularly for systems where nonlinear approximations are necessary. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a significant problem in dynamical systems analysis.

The main limitation is the lack of extensive comparison with alternative methods, but this does not undermine the core contribution. The paper is sufficiently complete and well-supported to warrant publication at NIPS. With minor improvements in the discussion of computational aspects, this work would be even stronger.

This paper would be of interest to the NIPS community working on machine learning for dynamical systems, kernel methods, and spectral analysis. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, with clear potential for impact in both theoretical and applied settings.
