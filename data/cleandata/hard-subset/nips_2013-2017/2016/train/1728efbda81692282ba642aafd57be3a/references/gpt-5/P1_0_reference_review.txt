Summary
The paper proposes a kernelized variant of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems via the Koopman operator. The key idea is to lift the data into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) using a feature map ϕ, define the Koopman operator KH(ϕ)(x) = ϕ(f(x)), and compute empirical Ritz values and vectors by projecting KH onto the Krylov subspace span{ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)}. Theoretical results (Theorem 1) connect this kernelized Arnoldi/QR construction to modal expansions analogous to standard DMD. Because Gram-Schmidt in RKHS can be ill-conditioned, a robust variant is proposed that projects onto truncated kernel PCA directions, leading to a finite-dimensional operator F̂ expressed purely in terms of Gram matrices (Theorem 2). The method is illustrated on a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map, and three application sketches are given: short-term prediction via feature-space extrapolation plus pre-image recovery, dynamics recognition using subspace angles between estimated Koopman eigenspaces, and sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-style score derived from a kernel exponential family view.

Relation to prior work
The paper is closely related to extended DMD (EDMD) and its kernelization (often called KDMD). EDMD constructs a finite dictionary of observables and estimates Koopman spectra from the least-squares operator on that dictionary; KDMD replaces the explicit dictionary by a kernel trick, expressing the needed Gram matrices between snapshots and their time-shifts (Williams et al., J. Nonlinear Sci. 2015; later KDMD follow-ups). The proposed robust variant with kernel PCA truncation is algebraically very similar to KDMD. The paper cites EDMD [35] but could position the connection more explicitly; many of the derived matrices (G = ΨX⊤ΨX, A = ΨX⊤ΨY, solve for eigenvalues of G+ A) match the presented F̂ expression up to centering/truncation.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: DMD with linear observables is often insufficient; kernelization increases representational power without handcrafting dictionaries.
- Conceptual simplicity: the kernel trick replaces inner products in Arnoldi/QR and in the projected operator with Gram matrices; the robustification via kernel PCA is natural and practically useful.
- Theoretical framing: the Ritz/vector expansions in RKHS (Theorem 1) and the connection to Koopman eigenfunctions under assumptions (Theorem 2) help ground the method.
- Breadth of potential applications: the paper outlines prediction, recognition, and change detection, which could be valuable to the NeurIPS audience.

Weaknesses
Quality/technical soundness:
- Some derivations are informal or rely on strong assumptions. In Theorem 1, the step F = R−1PτR = Mτ−1KHMτ is not well-posed since Mτ is rectangular; the argument should be confined to the projected subspace and use standard Arnoldi/companion matrix derivations rigorously.
- Notation is confusing: Koopman eigenfunctions are scalar-valued; here φj are elements of H (vector-valued in the feature space). Clarify the relationship between scalar eigenfunctions, feature map ϕ, and the vector-valued “modes” recovered.
- The statement “for a system evolving on an attractor, Koopman eigenvalues lie on the unit circle” is generally false; this holds for measure-preserving systems. Dissipative systems may have eigenvalues strictly inside the unit disk, and continuous spectra are common.
- Theorem 2 assumes φj ∈ Φ and κj = Uaj; this is a strong assumption tantamount to the kernel PCA subspace spanning the relevant eigenfunctions along data. Conditions (e.g., ergodicity, universal kernels, sampling) under which this holds are not discussed.
- The change-point detection derivation posits a density p(xt) ∝ exp(⟨ϕ∘f(xt−1), ϕ(xt)⟩H − g(·)), which is not a standard conditional density model; the approximation that drops one term lacks justification and no statistical guarantees are provided.

Empirical evaluation:
- Experiments are very limited and largely qualitative. The toy example and Hénon map show eigenvalue plots but no quantitative error, no comparison to EDMD/exact DMD/KDMD, and no robustness/noise studies.
- The application sections are proof-of-concept. Prediction via pre-image is notoriously ill-posed; an MDS-based heuristic is used with no accuracy metrics or baselines. Recognition via subspace angles is illustrated with an embedding but no classification accuracy. Change detection shows one plot without detection delay/false alarm comparisons.

Clarity:
- The paper contains typos and incomplete references. Several figures and equations are referenced but not fully described; important algorithmic details (regularization, rank selection, kernel choices) are missing.
- Centering via kernel PCA removes the constant mode; discuss whether centering is appropriate for Koopman analysis and whether the trivial eigenvalue 1 mode is preserved.

Originality:
- The core contribution overlaps substantially with EDMD/KDMD. The RKHS-Krylov/QR perspective is an alternative derivation, but the resulting algorithm and projected operator are essentially those of KDMD with truncation. The novelty claim should be narrowed and the relationship made precise.

Significance:
- Given the overlap and the limited empirical evidence, the current impact is constrained. With stronger theoretical clarity and rigorous experiments, the approach could be useful to practitioners wanting a kernelized Koopman/DMD pipeline.

Questions for the authors
1. Please clarify the exact relationship between your robust algorithm and EDMD/KDMD. Under what conditions are they algebraically equivalent? What is the effect of centering?
2. How do you choose the truncation rank p and regularize ill-conditioned Gram matrices (e.g., Tikhonov or spectral cutoff)? How sensitive are results to these choices and to kernel parameters?
3. Can you provide quantitative comparisons (e.g., eigenvalue error, prediction error) against DMD, EDMD, and KDMD on standard benchmarks (Duffing, FitzHugh–Nagumo, Lorenz) with/without noise?
4. Can you formalize the probabilistic model used for change detection and provide detection performance metrics and baselines?
5. What is the computational complexity and memory footprint as a function of τ and n? How does the method scale to longer sequences and multiple trajectories?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear problem framing and potentially useful kernelized viewpoint
- Theoretical connections to Ritz approximations in RKHS
- Practical robustification via kernel PCA/truncation
- Broad applicability to ML tasks involving dynamics

Cons for acceptance
- Significant overlap with EDMD/KDMD; novelty not clearly established
- Theoretical imprecision and some incorrect statements
- Insufficient empirical validation and lack of quantitative comparisons
- Application sections are heuristic and under-evaluated

Recommendation
While the direction is relevant to the NeurIPS community, the current version is not yet ready: it needs tighter theory, clearer positioning relative to EDMD/KDMD, and substantially stronger experiments with quantitative metrics and baselines. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to revise accordingly.

Confidence
High on methodological connections to EDMD/KDMD; medium on specific theoretical claims due to presentation ambiguities.
