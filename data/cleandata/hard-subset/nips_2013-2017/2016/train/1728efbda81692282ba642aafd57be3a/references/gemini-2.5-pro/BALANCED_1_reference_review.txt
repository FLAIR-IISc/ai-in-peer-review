### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a popular method for spectral analysis of dynamical systems. The core idea is to apply the principles of Koopman operator theory in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). By mapping the original state space data into a high-dimensional feature space via a kernel function, the authors aim to find a representation where the nonlinear dynamics become linear. The Koopman operator, which governs the evolution of observables, is then approximated in this feature space. The paper presents an algorithm, termed kernel DMD (kDMD), for estimating the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions of this lifted Koopman operator.

The authors first derive a formulation based on the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization of the Krylov subspace in the RKHS (Theorem 1). Recognizing potential numerical instability, they then present a more robust algorithm that leverages kernel Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to project the data onto a reduced-rank subspace before estimating the Koopman operator, analogous to the use of Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) in standard DMD (Theorem 2). Finally, the paper demonstrates the utility of the extracted Koopman spectral features for several downstream tasks, including short-term prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

This work builds directly upon the extensive literature on DMD [25, 26, 33] and Koopman operator analysis. While standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear combinations of the observed state variables, kDMD implicitly uses a rich, and potentially infinite, set of basis functions defined by the kernel. This connects the work to Extended DMD (eDMD) [35], which uses a user-defined dictionary of basis functions. The proposed kDMD can be seen as a specific, powerful instance of eDMD where the dictionary is implicitly defined by the kernel trick, offering a non-parametric way to enrich the basis. The paper also fits within the broader area of kernel methods for learning dynamical systems, such as the kernel subspace method by Kawahara et al. [15], but distinguishes itself by focusing on spectral decomposition rather than just system identification for prediction.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Principled Generalization:** The extension of DMD to RKHS is a natural and powerful idea. It provides a principled way to move beyond the limitations of standard DMD, which is fundamentally a linear analysis method, to handle strongly nonlinear systems without requiring the manual selection of a finite set of nonlinear observables. The theoretical grounding in Koopman operator theory is sound, and Theorems 1 and 2 clearly establish the connection between the algorithm's outputs and the true Koopman spectrum in the feature space.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with a clear review of Koopman theory and DMD, smoothly transitions to the proposed kernelized extension, addresses practical issues of numerical stability with a robust variant, and concludes with compelling applications. The mathematical derivations are easy to follow.

3.  **Practical Significance and Versatility:** By providing a non-parametric method for Koopman analysis, this work makes spectral decomposition techniques accessible to a wider range of complex systems. The demonstration of the method on diverse applications—prediction, classification of dynamics, and change-point detection—effectively highlights the utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues as general-purpose features for dynamic data. The application to motion capture data is particularly convincing.

4.  **Completeness:** The inclusion of the robust algorithm based on kernel PCA is a key strength. It shows an awareness of the practical numerical issues that plague Arnoldi-type methods like DMD and provides a viable, well-justified solution that is analogous to best practices in the standard DMD literature.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with Extended DMD (eDMD):** The paper acknowledges the connection to eDMD [35] but does not explore it in sufficient depth. The robust kDMD formulation is algorithmically very similar to eDMD. For instance, kDMD with a polynomial kernel is equivalent to eDMD with a basis of monomials. The primary advantage of kDMD seems to be the ability to use infinite-dimensional feature spaces (e.g., with an RBF kernel), but the practical benefits of this over a sufficiently rich finite dictionary in eDMD are not discussed or demonstrated. A direct experimental comparison would significantly strengthen the paper and clarify its specific contribution.

2.  **Scalability Concerns:** The proposed method requires the construction and eigendecomposition of Gram matrices of size `τ x τ`, where `τ` is the length of the time series. The computational complexity is at least O(τ³), which is prohibitive for long sequences common in many real-world applications. This is a well-known limitation of kernel methods, and the paper would be improved by acknowledging this and briefly discussing potential approximation strategies (e.g., Nyström method, random features) to enhance scalability.

3.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The performance of kDMD is critically dependent on the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters (e.g., the width of a Gaussian kernel). The paper provides no guidance on how to set these parameters, which is a crucial step for any practitioner seeking to apply the method. An analysis of the method's sensitivity to hyperparameter choice, or a discussion of potential selection strategies (e.g., cross-validation based on prediction error), would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Brief Treatment of Applications:** While the breadth of applications is a strength, the descriptions are somewhat brief. The prediction application relies on solving the pre-image problem, which is notoriously ill-posed. The paper cites an MDS-based approach but provides few details, leaving the reader to wonder about its robustness and limitations. Similarly, the derivation for the change-point detection score involves simplifications (e.g., dropping the log-partition function term) that could benefit from stronger justification.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   In Section 5, I suggest adding a more detailed paragraph comparing kDMD and eDMD. You could frame kDMD as providing a systematic, kernel-driven approach to constructing the dictionary for eDMD. An experiment comparing kDMD (with an RBF kernel) to eDMD using a finite basis of RBFs centered at data points could concretely illustrate the trade-offs.
*   It would be beneficial to add a short discussion on the computational complexity and potential scalability limitations. Mentioning that standard techniques like the Nyström approximation could be employed to handle larger datasets would show foresight and increase the paper's practical relevance.
*   For the experiments, please specify how the kernel hyperparameters were chosen. Even a brief note on this (e.g., "width was set to the median pairwise distance") would be helpful. A small experiment showing how the estimated eigenvalues change with the kernel width for the Hénon map would be very instructive.
*   The prediction section could be strengthened by providing more detail on the pre-image algorithm and discussing its potential failure modes. For instance, how does prediction accuracy degrade as the predicted point moves further from the training data manifold in the feature space?

### Decision

This paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound contribution that successfully merges Koopman operator theory with the power of kernel methods. The proposed kDMD algorithm is an elegant and significant extension of standard DMD. Despite some weaknesses regarding the comparison to related work and practical considerations like scalability and hyperparameter selection, the core ideas are strong, and the paper is well-executed. The demonstrated applications clearly show the potential impact of this method. The weaknesses are addressable and do not invalidate the central claims. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
