

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors claim their method approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using nonlinear kernel-based basis functions, as opposed to the linear monomial basis used in traditional DMD. They present a theoretical framework for this extension, including proofs of theorems relating to the Krylov subspace, and propose a robust variant using POD bases to address numerical instability. The paper demonstrates the method on toy systems and the Hénon map, and presents applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

**Strengths:**
- The mathematical derivation is sound, with clear theoretical connections to existing DMD methods.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good explanations of the Koopman operator and DMD fundamentals.
- The connection to Extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) is acknowledged, though not deeply differentiated.
- The robustification approach using POD bases is conceptually reasonable and addresses a known issue with DMD.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's primary weakness is its lack of convincing empirical evidence demonstrating clear advantages over established methods. The authors claim their kernel-based approach provides "richer basis functions" for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions, but fail to provide adequate evidence that this leads to substantially better performance on meaningful problems.

Key concerns:
1. **Minimal empirical validation:** The experiments are limited to a simple toy system and the Hénon map. These are insufficient to demonstrate the method's utility on real-world problems where nonlinear dynamics are more complex. The "empirical examples" in Section 6 don't provide quantitative comparisons with established methods.

2. **Lack of rigorous comparison:** The paper fails to compare against relevant baselines such as Extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), which already uses pre-determined basis functions. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to determine whether the kernel approach offers meaningful improvements over existing methods.

3. **Overstated novelty:** The authors claim their method "gives an approximation with a set of nonlinear basis functions due to the expressiveness of kernel functions," but Extended DMD (which they cite) already uses arbitrary basis functions (not just linear monomials). The paper doesn't clearly differentiate their approach from Extended DMD, making the claimed novelty questionable.

4. **Insufficient application demonstration:** The applications section (Section 7) shows limited results on simple datasets without demonstrating clear advantages over simpler methods. For instance, the prediction example (Figure 2) shows results for the toy system but doesn't compare against standard time-series forecasting methods.

5. **Missing ablation studies:** The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to quantify the contribution of the kernel component versus the DMD framework. Without this, it's unclear whether the kernel approach is necessary or beneficial.

**Overall assessment:**
This paper presents a mathematically interesting extension of DMD, but fails to meet NIPS standards for empirical validation. The claims about the advantages of kernel-based approximation are not substantiated with sufficient evidence. The paper would benefit from:
1. More comprehensive comparisons against established methods (Extended DMD, standard DMD variants)
2. Experiments on more complex real-world datasets
3. Ablation studies showing the contribution of the kernel component
4. Clearer differentiation from Extended DMD

While the mathematical framework is sound, the paper's primary contribution is theoretical rather than empirical, and it doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method offers significant practical advantages over existing approaches. The empirical validation is insufficient to justify the claimed novelty and advantages.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper does not provide sufficient empirical evidence to support its claims of superiority over established methods. The theoretical contribution is incremental and not sufficiently justified by meaningful empirical results. The minimal experimental validation on toy systems does not demonstrate the method's utility for real-world problems where nonlinear dynamics are more complex and challenging. The paper would need substantially more rigorous empirical validation against relevant baselines to be considered for publication at NIPS.
