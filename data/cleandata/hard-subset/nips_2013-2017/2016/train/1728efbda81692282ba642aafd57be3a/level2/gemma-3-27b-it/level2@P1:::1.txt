This paper introduces a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) technique extended to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), aiming to improve upon traditional DMD's reliance on linear monomials for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions. The core idea is sound – leveraging the expressiveness of kernels to capture potentially more complex dynamics. The paper is well-structured, presenting necessary background on Koopman theory and DMD before detailing the proposed method. The authors clearly outline the mathematical formulation and provide empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending DMD to RKHS is a worthwhile contribution. It addresses a key limitation of standard DMD by allowing for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations appear rigorous and build logically upon existing Koopman and DMD theory.  Theorem 1 and 2 are well-stated and provide a solid foundation for the proposed method.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the concepts in a relatively accessible manner, given the complexity of the underlying mathematics. The organization is logical, easing comprehension.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on both a toy system and the Henon map, showing reasonable agreement with expected results. They also explore several application areas (prediction, embedding, and change-point detection).
* **Robustification:**  The inclusion of a POD-based robustification step (Section 4) is crucial, as the authors acknowledge the potential for ill-conditioning in practical implementations. This adds practical value to the work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison:** While the authors compare to a kernel change detection method in one application, there is a relative lack of comprehensive comparisons to other relevant methods, particularly those addressing nonlinear system identification or spectral analysis (e.g., extended DMD, sparse identification of nonlinear dynamics (SINDy)). A more thorough comparison would solidify the advantages of the proposed approach.
* **Kernel Selection:** The choice of kernel (polynomial for the toy system, Gaussian for the Henon map) seems somewhat arbitrary. The paper lacks a discussion of kernel selection strategies or sensitivity analysis to different kernel parameters.  A more systematic exploration of kernel choices is needed.
* **Computational Cost:**  The computational complexity of the proposed method, especially involving the kernel matrix operations, is not explicitly discussed. While the POD projection helps, it's unclear how the method scales to high-dimensional data or long time series.
* **Application Depth:** The applications section feels somewhat cursory. While demonstrating feasibility, the results are not particularly compelling or transformative. More detailed analysis and comparison with baseline methods within each application area are needed.
* **Reference to Related Work:** While a considerable number of references are provided, some key works in related areas (e.g., kernel PCA, SINDy) could be more prominently discussed and positioned relative to the proposed method. The connection to the eigensystem realization method alluded to in Section 5 deserves further elaboration.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of DMD with the potential to improve the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems.
* The method is well-motivated and clearly explained.
* The empirical results, while not extensive, provide initial evidence of effectiveness.
* The inclusion of a robustification step addresses a practical concern.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of comprehensive comparisons to other relevant methods weakens the claim of superiority.
* The limited discussion of kernel selection and computational cost.
* The applications section could be significantly strengthened with more in-depth analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The idea of extending DMD to RKHS is promising, and the authors have provided a clear and rigorous presentation of their method. However, the limited comparisons and lack of detailed discussion regarding kernel selection and computational cost prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

1. **Expand the comparison** to include other relevant methods for nonlinear system identification and spectral analysis.
2. **Discuss kernel selection** strategies and perform a sensitivity analysis to different kernel parameters.
3. **Provide a discussion** of the computational complexity of the method and its scalability.
4. **Strengthen the applications section** with more detailed analysis and comparisons to baseline methods.
5. **Elaborate on the connection** to the eigensystem realization method.
