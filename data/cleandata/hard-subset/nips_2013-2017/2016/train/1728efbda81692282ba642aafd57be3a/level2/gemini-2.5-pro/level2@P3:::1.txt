This paper presents a method for performing a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The work extends the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) algorithm, a technique popular in the analysis of physical systems, to handle nonlinear dynamics more effectively by leveraging the power of kernel methods. The proposed algorithm approximates the Koopman operator's eigenvalues and eigenfunctions from finite time-series data. It involves constructing and finding an orthogonal basis for a Krylov subspace in the feature space, followed by an eigendecomposition of the operator projected onto this subspace. The paper also introduces a more robust variant of the algorithm that uses kernel Principal Component Analysis (PCA) for regularization, which is analogous to practices in the standard DMD literature. Finally, the utility of the extracted Koopman modes as features for nonlinear dynamical systems is demonstrated through applications in state prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection. This work connects Koopman operator theory with kernel methods, providing a data-driven tool for the analysis and representation of nonlinear dynamics relevant to the machine learning community.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development is sound, building a clear and logical bridge from the standard DMD framework to its RKHS counterpart. Theorem 1 provides a direct generalization of the DMD decomposition principle to the feature space. Recognizing the potential for numerical instability—a known issue in standard DMD—the paper proposes a robustified version based on projecting data onto kernel principal components (Section 4). This practical consideration is well-motivated and the connection to the Koopman eigenfunctions is formally established in Theorem 2. The claims are well-supported by experiments on both synthetic data (a toy system and the chaotic Hénon map) and real-world data (motion capture). The experiments convincingly illustrate the method's ability to extract meaningful spectral information and use it for downstream machine learning tasks. The work appears to be a complete contribution, presenting theory, a practical algorithm, and compelling applications.

**Clarity**

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It begins with an effective introduction to the Koopman operator and DMD, making the work accessible to readers not already familiar with these topics. The progression from the basic kernelized DMD algorithm in Section 3 to the robust version in Section 4 is logical. The mathematical notation is consistent and the derivations are easy to follow. The inclusion of algorithmic summaries at the end of Sections 3 and 4 is helpful for implementation. The figures are informative and support the claims made in the text. For instance, the MDS embedding in Figure 3 clearly shows the separation of different motion types, providing strong visual evidence for the quality of the learned representations.

**Originality**

The core contribution—the formulation of DMD in an RKHS—is a novel and significant extension of existing methods. While DMD itself is not new, its kernelization for Koopman analysis appears to be a new contribution. The work effectively combines ideas from dynamical systems theory (Koopman operators, DMD) and machine learning (kernel methods).

The paper appropriately cites related work, including different variants of DMD and spectral methods for learning dynamical systems. One area where the discussion could be expanded is the relationship with "Extended DMD" (Williams et al., 2015). The paper correctly identifies this as a similar approach but could benefit from a more detailed comparison. Kernel DMD can be viewed as a specific instance of Extended DMD where the dictionary of observables is implicitly and non-parametrically defined by the kernel function and the data points. Clarifying this relationship would help to better position the paper's contribution and highlight the advantages of not needing to pre-specify an explicit dictionary of functions.

**Significance**

The results presented in this work are important and likely to be influential. The proposed method provides a powerful, general-purpose tool for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems from data. By framing the Koopman modes as features, the paper makes a strong connection to representation learning, opening up new avenues for applying techniques from dynamical systems to machine learning problems. The demonstrated applications are compelling and relevant to the NIPS community:
- The recognition of locomotion patterns is a strong proof-of-concept for learning invariant representations of dynamic behaviors.
- The change-point detection algorithm is an innovative application that demonstrates the utility of the learned predictive model.

This work has the potential to be widely used by both researchers studying dynamical systems and practitioners looking for principled ways to extract features from time-series data.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is strong, but could be improved with the following additions:

1.  **Computational Complexity:** A discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method would be valuable. The algorithm relies on forming and decomposing a Gram matrix of size `τ x τ`, where `τ` is the number of data points. This could be a limitation for very long time series. Mentioning potential approximation techniques, such as the Nyström method, would strengthen the paper from a practical standpoint.
2.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of kernel methods is often sensitive to the choice of kernel and its parameters. The experiments use specific kernels (polynomial, Gaussian) but provide little discussion on how these were selected or how sensitive the results are to these choices. A brief discussion on parameter selection or a small sensitivity analysis would enhance the reproducibility and practical utility of the work.
3.  **The Pre-image Problem:** The prediction application relies on solving the pre-image problem, which is known to be ill-posed and challenging. While the chosen MDS-based approach seems to work for the examples shown, a sentence acknowledging the inherent difficulty of this step would provide a more balanced perspective on the limitations of the prediction framework.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound kernelization of Dynamic Mode Decomposition for Koopman analysis.
*   Addresses a fundamental problem: learning representations for nonlinear dynamical systems.
*   The robustified algorithm is practically sound and well-justified.
*   Demonstrates strong performance on a diverse set of applications, including dynamics recognition and change-point detection, with compelling results on real-world data.
*   The work is well-written, clear, and builds a valuable bridge between the dynamical systems and machine learning communities.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The relationship to and advantages over Extended DMD could be articulated more clearly.
*   Practical aspects such as computational scaling and hyperparameter selection are not discussed in detail.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It is a well-executed piece of research with high originality and significance. The suggested improvements are minor and could be incorporated into a final version.
