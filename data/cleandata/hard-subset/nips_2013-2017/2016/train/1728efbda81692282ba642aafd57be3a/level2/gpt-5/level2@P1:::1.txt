Summary
The paper proposes Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels (kernel DMD) to approximate Koopman spectral quantities for nonlinear dynamical systems from finite data. The key idea is to perform an Arnoldi/DMD-like Ritz analysis in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), where the Krylov matrix is built from feature maps ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1). The method computes orthonormal bases via Gram–Schmidt in RKHS (using kernel Gram matrices), forms the projected operator on the span, and eigendecomposes it to obtain Ritz eigenvalues/vectors that mimic Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions. To improve numerical stability, the paper proposes a robust variant using kernel PCA (POD in feature space) to truncate to principal directions before forming the projected operator. Theoretical statements (Theorems 1–2) connect the Ritz quantities to Koopman eigenpairs under assumptions on span and distinctness of eigenvalues. The paper then explores applications treating Koopman modes/eigenfunctions as features: short-term prediction via pre-image; dynamics recognition using kernel principal angles between subspaces; and sequential change-point detection via a CUSUM-like statistic derived from a kernel exponential family. Empirical illustrations include a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, motion capture sequences, and a Lorenz-map change-point example.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on DMD (Schmid 2010) and its refined variants (exact/optimized DMD, Tu et al. 2014; Chen et al. 2012), and on Koopman operator analysis (Mezić 2005/2013). It is closely related to Extended DMD (Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, J. Nonlinear Sci. 2015), which uses a user-specified dictionary and admits kernelization. The present paper’s contribution is to derive a kernelized Arnoldi/Ritz perspective directly in RKHS, and to advocate a data-adaptive dictionary via kernel PCA for robustness. The recognition/embedding and change-point applications relate to kernel subspace methods (Wolf & Shashua 2003) and kernel exponential families (Canu & Smola 2006).

Strengths
- Clear conceptual bridge: frames DMD as Arnoldi in feature space and provides a clean kernelized algorithm based on Gram matrices; the robust variant via kernel PCA is natural and practical.
- Theoretical connections: Theorems 1–2 give conditions under which the projected operator’s eigenpairs approximate Koopman spectra and link them to kernel principal components.
- Practicality: Works with collections of predecessor–successor pairs and supports truncation when Gram matrices are ill-conditioned; code is provided.
- Applications: Demonstrates how Koopman features can be used in prediction, recognition, and change detection, expanding the audience beyond fluid mechanics.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty relative to EDMD and kernel EDMD needs sharpening. EDMD already frames Koopman approximation with a dictionary and admits the kernel trick; a careful comparison (algorithmic, theoretical, and empirical) is needed. How does the proposed approach differ from choosing the dictionary as kernel PCA functions in EDMD? Please discuss links to the “kernel EDMD/KDMD” literature and clarify naming to avoid confusion.
- Assumptions and scope: Theorems hinge on distinct eigenvalues and on the Koopman-invariant subspace being captured by the kernel-PCA span. Many systems exhibit continuous spectrum or require rich dictionaries. Please discuss how the method behaves with continuous spectra, quasiperiodic vs mixing dynamics, and noise.
- Numerical robustness: Gram–Schmidt in RKHS can be unstable; while the kernel-PCA variant helps, an explicit regularization strategy (e.g., Tikhonov, rank truncation thresholds, conditioning analysis) and sensitivity studies are missing.
- Experimental evaluation is limited. The toy and Hénon examples are illustrative but small-scale; the applications lack baselines and quantitative metrics. For prediction, pre-image recovery is nontrivial and the MDS-based approach may be brittle; quantitative errors and comparisons to EDMD, kernel EDMD, and standard predictors are needed. For change-point detection, the simplification of the likelihood ratio and its robustness should be validated against strong baselines (e.g., residual CUSUM on AR/GP models, kernel change detection).
- Complexity and scalability: Please analyze computational and memory complexity in τ and number of points, and discuss out-of-sample evaluation and streaming updates.
- Notation/clarity: Some steps (e.g., derivation of F = R−1Q* M+; properties of the companion matrix; conditions for ψ = 0) could be detailed further. It would help to clearly separate infinite-dimensional operator statements from their finite-sample empirical counterparts.

Quality
The algorithmic derivations are largely sound and align with standard Ritz/Arnoldi reasoning adapted to RKHS. The robust variant is reasonable. Some proofs rely on strong assumptions; adding proofs to the supplement (e.g., the Vandermonde argument and equivalence steps) and discussing failure modes would strengthen the paper. Empirical support is currently qualitative.

Clarity
The paper is mostly well organized and readable. Suggestions: define all matrices when introduced (Mτ, M+, H), state assumptions before theorems, and include a schematic algorithm box for both variants with complexity notes. Please proofread for English style and minor typographical issues.

Originality
Moderate. The RKHS Arnoldi viewpoint and the explicit use of kernel PCA as a data-adaptive dictionary are appealing, but overlap substantially with EDMD/kernel EDMD concepts. The applications section is more novel in its breadth than in methodological depth.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners interested in Koopman analysis with kernels and for feature-based tasks on dynamical data. Significance would increase with stronger empirical comparisons and clearer positioning against EDMD/kernel EDMD.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a thorough comparison to EDMD and kernel EDMD: theory (objective, projection spaces), algorithms (matrices formed, role of kernel PCA), and experiments (eigenvalue accuracy, reconstruction error, prediction error, sensitivity to noise and kernel parameters).
- Add quantitative experiments on multiple nonlinear systems (e.g., Duffing, Lorenz63/96, Van der Pol), with noise and limited data, and ablations on truncation rank p and kernel width.
- Include regularization (ridge) in forming projected operators and study its effect.
- Discuss continuous spectrum and how to interpret/regularize modes in such cases.
- Detail pre-image computation, its success criteria, and alternatives (e.g., solving a pre-image optimization with regularization).

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant and practical kernelized DMD framework with a principled robust variant via kernel PCA.
- Clear connections to Koopman theory and data-driven modal analysis.
- Broad applicability illustrated across several tasks; code available.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental relative to EDMD/kernel EDMD with insufficient comparative evaluation.
- Limited quantitative experiments and lack of strong baselines.
- Theoretical assumptions may not hold in many realistic settings; limited discussion of continuous spectra and noise.

Overall assessment
Promising and well-motivated contribution that clarifies and packages kernel-based Koopman spectral analysis. With stronger positioning relative to EDMD/kernel EDMD and more rigorous empirical evaluation, this would be a solid NeurIPS paper. In its current form, I view it as borderline; I encourage the authors to address the above points.
