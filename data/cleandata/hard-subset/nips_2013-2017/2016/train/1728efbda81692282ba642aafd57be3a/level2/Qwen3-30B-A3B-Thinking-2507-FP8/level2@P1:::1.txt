

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors develop a modal decomposition algorithm that approximates the Koopman operator in RKHS, moving beyond standard DMD's use of linear monomials as basis functions. The key contribution is a method that computes orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performs eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projection, with a robust implementation via POD basis projection. The paper demonstrates applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The theoretical foundation is strong, with clear mathematical derivations and well-established theorems (Theorems 1 and 2) that demonstrate the equivalence with DMD in RKHS and the robust implementation. The extension to RKHS is a meaningful advancement over standard DMD, as it allows for richer basis functions capable of capturing more complex nonlinear dynamics. The connection to previous work on Koopman analysis (Rowley et al., 2009), DMD variants (Chen et al., 2012; Williams et al., 2015), and kernel methods is well-explained with appropriate citations.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, with examples on a toy nonlinear system, Hénon map, and real-world locomotion data. The results (Figures 1-4) demonstrate the method's effectiveness in estimating Koopman eigenvalues, prediction, and dynamics recognition. The paper's applications are particularly compelling, showing how the extracted features can be used for practical machine learning tasks. The robust implementation via POD (Section 4) effectively addresses the ill-conditioning issue noted in standard DMD implementations.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-organized sections and logical flow from theory to applications. The mathematical exposition is generally accessible, though some parts (particularly the connection to kernel PCA) could benefit from more intuitive explanations for readers unfamiliar with RKHS theory. The writing is professional and clear throughout.

Regarding originality, this work makes a significant contribution by extending DMD to RKHS, which provides a more expressive basis for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions. This distinguishes it from previous DMD variants, including Williams et al.'s extended DMD, which uses pre-determined basis functions but does not employ kernel methods. The paper clearly articulates how this approach differs from prior work, though a more detailed comparison with related methods would strengthen the contribution statement.

The significance of this work is evident in its potential applications across multiple domains where understanding nonlinear dynamics is crucial (fluid mechanics, control systems, machine learning). The demonstrated applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition provide concrete evidence of the method's utility. The paper's ability to extract meaningful features from nonlinear systems represents a valuable contribution to the field.

Some minor weaknesses are worth noting. The paper could provide more guidance on kernel selection for different types of systems, as the current examples use specific kernels (polynomial for the toy system, Gaussian for Hénon map) without discussing how to choose kernels for new applications. The computational complexity analysis is somewhat limited; while the paper notes that Gram-Schmidt QR can be reduced to operations on Gram matrices, a more thorough comparison of computational costs with standard DMD would be beneficial. Additionally, the connection to the eigensystem realization method (mentioned in Section 5) could be expanded to better contextualize the relationship between this work and subspace identification methods.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the Koopman spectral analysis literature. The extension to RKHS is theoretically sound, the implementation is robust, and the empirical results are compelling. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the minor weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing more detailed guidance on kernel selection and computational considerations.

The paper is suitable for NIPS as it presents a significant theoretical advancement with clear practical applications, addressing a fundamental problem in understanding nonlinear dynamical systems. The work aligns with NIPS' focus on machine learning methods with strong theoretical foundations and practical utility.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, meaningful extension of DMD, comprehensive empirical evaluation, clear writing
Con: Limited discussion on kernel selection, computational complexity analysis could be deeper, comparison with related work could be more thorough
