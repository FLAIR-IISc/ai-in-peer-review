

The paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors present a modal decomposition algorithm that operates in RKHS, which can be reduced to calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projection. The method contrasts with standard DMD, which uses linear monomials of observables as basis functions, while the proposed approach uses nonlinear basis functions enabled by kernel functions. The paper also provides a robustified version using POD bases to address numerical instability issues. The work relates to recent advances in Koopman spectral analysis, including Williams et al.'s Extended DMD, and extends the theoretical framework for dynamical system analysis in RKHS.

The paper demonstrates several strengths. The theoretical foundation is solid, with Theorem 1 establishing the equivalence with DMD in RKHS and Theorem 2 providing the connection between the proposed method and Koopman eigenfunctions. The robustification using POD bases is a practical contribution that addresses a known limitation of standard DMD, making the method more applicable to real-world data. The empirical evaluation includes synthetic examples (toy nonlinear system and Hénon map) and applications to real-world data (locomotion recognition, change-point detection), which effectively demonstrate the utility of the extracted features. The paper provides clear connections between the mathematical formulation and practical implementation, including detailed algorithm descriptions.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The empirical comparison with state-of-the-art methods is limited. While the paper mentions Extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), it does not provide quantitative comparisons with this or other recent methods on standard benchmarks. The applications section shows promising results but lacks rigorous comparison with existing approaches. For example, in locomotion recognition, the paper does not compare the performance with other feature extraction methods or classification techniques. The paper also does not thoroughly discuss computational complexity, which is important for practical applications.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, but some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The connection between the kernel-based approach and the physical interpretation of Koopman eigenfunctions could be made more explicit. The paper's relationship to Extended DMD is acknowledged but not sufficiently differentiated, despite the similarities between the methods.

The originality of the work is moderate. While extending DMD to RKHS is novel, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from Williams et al.'s Extended DMD. A more thorough discussion of how this work differs from and improves upon previous kernel-based methods for dynamical systems would strengthen the contribution.

The significance of the work is promising but not fully demonstrated. The applications shown are relevant, but the paper does not convincingly establish how this approach advances the state of the art beyond existing methods. For instance, it would be valuable to show cases where the kernel-based approach outperforms linear DMD due to the nonlinear basis functions.

To improve the paper, the authors should: 1) Add quantitative comparisons with state-of-the-art methods on standard benchmarks for the applications demonstrated; 2) Provide more detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability; 3) Clarify the relationship with Extended DMD and other kernel-based approaches, explicitly stating the advantages of the proposed method.

The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to Koopman spectral analysis with a practical extension to RKHS. The robustification using POD bases is a valuable practical contribution. With more comprehensive empirical evaluation and clearer differentiation from related work, this could be a significant contribution to the field. The method has potential for applications in machine learning and dynamical systems analysis, particularly for systems where linear approximations are insufficient. The paper's theoretical foundation is strong, but the empirical validation needs strengthening to fully demonstrate the method's advantages.
