This paper presents a method for performing Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The central idea is to generalize Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a method for approximating the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator, by leveraging the kernel trick. Standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear combination of observables, which can be insufficient for highly nonlinear systems. By mapping the data into a high-dimensional feature space, the proposed "kernel DMD" (kDMD) can implicitly work with a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions to find a more accurate approximation of the Koopman spectral decomposition. The authors present a direct formulation based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the RKHS and a more numerically robust variant based on projecting the data onto kernel principal components. The paper further demonstrates the utility of the learned Koopman modes as features for downstream machine learning tasks, including time-series prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

This work builds upon a solid foundation of Koopman operator theory for dynamical systems and the growing literature on DMD. It elegantly connects these ideas from physics and fluid dynamics with well-established techniques from machine learning, namely kernel methods. The proposed method can be seen as a sophisticated, data-driven instance of Extended DMD (eDMD), where the dictionary of basis functions is implicitly defined by the kernel and the data itself, rather than being specified a priori.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Principled Generalization:** The combination of DMD and kernel methods is a natural, elegant, and powerful idea. It provides a principled way to move beyond the linear subspace assumptions of standard DMD to model complex nonlinear dynamics without requiring the user to manually engineer a suitable set of basis functions.

2.  **Solid Theoretical Grounding:** The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for the proposed method. Theorems 1 and 2 establish the connection between the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the constructed matrices and the true Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions under reasonable assumptions. The derivations are clear and follow logically from the principles of DMD.

3.  **Practical and Robust Algorithm:** The authors correctly identify the potential numerical instability of a naive implementation and propose a robust version based on truncated kernel PCA. This is analogous to the use of POD/SVD truncation in standard DMD and makes the proposed method a viable and practical tool.

4.  **Demonstrated Versatility and Significance:** A key strength of the paper is that it goes beyond simply presenting the algorithm. By framing the method as a feature extraction technique and demonstrating its use in prediction, embedding, and change-point detection, the authors showcase its broad applicability and significance for the machine learning community. This bridges the gap between analysis tools for physical systems and practical machine learning tasks.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Comparison to Extended DMD (eDMD):** The relationship to eDMD (Williams et al., 2015) is mentioned but could be explored in greater detail. Kernel DMD can be interpreted as a specific case of eDMD where the dictionary consists of kernel functions centered at the data points. A more thorough discussion of the pros and cons of this implicit dictionary approach versus an explicit one would strengthen the paper. For instance, kDMD automates the choice of basis functions but introduces kernel hyperparameters and may have higher computational costs. An empirical comparison on one of the tasks would be highly valuable.

2.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The paper does not discuss the computational complexity. The core of the algorithm involves forming and computing the eigendecomposition of τ x τ matrices, where τ is the length of the time series. This O(τ^3) complexity could be a bottleneck for long sequences. It would be beneficial to discuss this limitation and mention potential avenues for scaling up the method, such as using Nystrom or other random feature approximations for the kernel matrix.

3.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The performance of the method likely depends on the choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., the width of the RBF kernel). The paper states which kernels were used but provides no details on how their parameters were selected. A discussion of this practical aspect, for instance mentioning the use of a median heuristic or cross-validation, would make the work more complete and reproducible.

4.  **Experimental Rigor:** While the application examples are compelling illustrations, their evaluation could be strengthened.
    *   For prediction, comparing against other common nonlinear time-series forecasting models (e.g., Gaussian Process models, LSTMs) would provide a clearer picture of the method's performance.
    *   For dynamics recognition, providing quantitative results (e.g., classification accuracy in a k-NN framework) would be more informative than the qualitative MDS visualization alone.
    *   The derivation for the change-point detection score involves a heuristic simplification. While plausible, some justification or empirical validation of this approximation would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the intersection of dynamical systems theory and machine learning. It presents a novel, well-motivated, and theoretically-grounded method for nonlinear system analysis. The paper is clearly written and well-organized. Despite the weaknesses mentioned above, which are largely addressable and point towards interesting future work, the core contribution is strong and likely to be influential. The proposed kDMD method is a powerful tool that could be widely used by both researchers studying dynamical systems and practitioners looking for effective feature extraction techniques for time-series data. I recommend acceptance.
