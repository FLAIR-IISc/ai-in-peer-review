This paper introduces a Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) method leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to move beyond linear monomials in traditional DMD to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with richer, nonlinear basis functions enabled by kernel methods. The authors present an algorithm based on calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition. They demonstrate the method on synthetic and real-world data, exploring applications like state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamic pattern recognition.

The paper is well-motivated, building on the increasing interest in Koopman operator theory and DMD for nonlinear system analysis. The connection to existing DMD is clearly articulated, and the authors rightly point out the limitations of monomial-based approximations, especially for strongly nonlinear systems. The extension to RKHS is a logical and potentially powerful step, and the theoretical formulation, particularly Theorem 1, appears sound. The inclusion of a robustness enhancement via POD projections is a pragmatic addition, addressing potential ill-conditioning issues. The empirical examples, while limited, provide initial validation of the approach. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the clarity could be significantly improved. The mathematical notation is dense, and the transitions between equations and their explanations are sometimes abrupt. Section 3, in particular, requires careful reading to fully grasp the details. While the authors attempt to bridge the gap between mathematical formalism and intuition, the explanation could be more accessible.

Second, while the algorithm is theoretically well-defined, the practical considerations are somewhat glossed over. The choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., kernel width in the Gaussian kernel) is crucial for performance but receives limited discussion. The sensitivity of the results to these choices is not thoroughly investigated. Similarly, the dimensionality reduction using POD (Section 4) requires a criterion for selecting the number of retained modes; this aspect isn’t detailed.

Third, the empirical evaluation feels somewhat superficial. The toy system serves as a basic validation but lacks complexity. The Hénon map example, while more interesting, doesn’t demonstrate a clear advantage over existing DMD methods. More compelling results on benchmark datasets or real-world applications would significantly strengthen the paper. The applications section (Section 7) presents several ideas but lacks a detailed comparative analysis against state-of-the-art techniques. The figures are generally small and lack detailed labeling, making it difficult to assess the results quantitatively. 

Fourth, the related work section, although referencing relevant papers, feels incomplete. While key DMD variants are mentioned, there’s less discussion of other kernel-based methods for dynamical systems or nonlinear system identification. The connection to subspace identification methods is acknowledged but not fully explored.  The citation of [36] regarding kernel principal angles appears somewhat out of place, as it’s only briefly mentioned in the applications section.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of DMD to RKHS, potentially enabling better approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions for nonlinear systems.
*   Solid theoretical foundation, with a clearly stated algorithm based on established concepts (Krylov subspaces, eigendecomposition).
*   Practical improvement via POD projection to address ill-conditioning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Clarity and readability issues, making the paper difficult to follow.
*   Limited empirical validation, lacking rigorous comparison with existing methods.
*   Insufficient discussion of practical considerations (kernel selection, dimensionality reduction).
*   Incomplete related work section.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. The core idea is promising, and the theoretical framework appears sound. However, the lack of clarity, limited empirical evaluation, and incomplete discussion of practical aspects prevent me from recommending acceptance. The authors should significantly revise the paper, focusing on improving clarity, conducting more comprehensive experiments, and thoroughly addressing the issues raised in this review. A more detailed comparative analysis against existing DMD variants and other relevant methods is crucial to demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.
