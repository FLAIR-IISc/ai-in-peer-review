**Summary**

This paper presents a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a technique for the spectral analysis of the Koopman operator associated with a nonlinear dynamical system. The central idea is to perform DMD in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) rather than the original state space. By implicitly mapping the data into a high-dimensional feature space, the method can approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions, overcoming the limitations of standard DMD which is based on linear observables. The authors derive an algorithm based on the principle of DMD in the feature space, which involves a Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization of a Krylov subspace. Recognizing potential numerical instability, they also propose a more robust version that first projects the data onto its kernel principal components (analogous to POD modes in the feature space). The paper demonstrates the utility of the extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features for several machine learning tasks, including state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and natural synthesis of Koopman operator theory, popular in the physics and fluid dynamics communities, with the kernel methods framework, which is central to machine learning. This extension of DMD to RKHSs is an important contribution that provides a principled way to perform nonlinear spectral decomposition of dynamical systems. The potential to use the learned spectral components as features for downstream machine learning tasks makes this work highly significant and relevant to the NIPS community.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is sound. The paper clearly establishes the connection between the standard DMD framework (based on the Arnoldi method and Krylov subspaces) and its proposed counterpart in the RKHS. Theorem 1 provides a direct extension, while Theorem 2 provides a formal justification for the robustified algorithm, connecting the eigenvectors of the projected operator to the true Koopman eigenfunctions under reasonable assumptions. The robustification procedure using kernel PCA is a well-motivated and practical choice that mirrors best practices in the standard DMD literature.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. It starts with a good introduction to the Koopman operator and DMD, making the work accessible to readers not deeply familiar with these topics. The derivation of the kernelized algorithms is clear and easy to follow for an audience familiar with kernel methods.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Relation to Extended DMD (eDMD):** The paper briefly mentions Extended DMD [35] but does not sufficiently differentiate its contribution. The proposed robust algorithm (Section 4) appears to be very similar, if not equivalent, to eDMD when the dictionary of basis functions in eDMD is chosen to be the set of kernel functions evaluated at the data points, i.e., $\{k(x, x_i)\}_{i=0}^{\tau-1}$. A more detailed discussion comparing the two frameworks is necessary. The authors should clarify whether their contribution is a novel theoretical perspective (framing it through Krylov subspaces in RKHS), a more convenient formulation, or if there are fundamental differences. An empirical comparison against eDMD on one of the tasks would significantly strengthen the paper's claim to originality.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the applications in Section 7 are compelling demonstrations of potential, the experimental evaluation lacks quantitative rigor and comparison to appropriate baselines.
    *   For the prediction task (Figure 2), the results are only shown qualitatively. It would be valuable to see quantitative error metrics (e.g., MSE) and a comparison against standard DMD, linear models, and perhaps other nonlinear time-series models like Gaussian Process Dynamical Models.
    *   The analysis of the Hénon map is vague. The paper states that "the part of the eigenvalues of the equilibrium points seem to be approximately estimated." This needs more precision. Which eigenvalues are captured? How accurate are they? How does this affect the quality of the reconstructed dynamics?
    *   For the locomotion data embedding (Figure 3), the result is visually appealing, but it is unclear how this compares to other manifold learning or time-series embedding techniques.
    *   The change-point detection experiment would be more convincing with a quantitative analysis (e.g., detection delay, false alarm rate) and a clearer explanation of the baseline method.

3.  **Practical Considerations:** The paper omits a discussion of hyperparameter selection, which is crucial for reproducibility and practical use. The performance of kernel methods is highly dependent on the choice of the kernel and its parameters (e.g., the width of the RBF kernel). Similarly, the rank `p` for the truncation in the robust algorithm is a critical parameter. The authors should include details on how these were selected for the experiments and discuss their sensitivity.

4.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** The derivation of the change-point detection score relies on a heuristic argument that "the variation of the second term is much smaller than the first one." While this may be reasonable, it should be stated more clearly as an approximation and perhaps justified further. The remark at the end of Section 4—that the method can work on pairs of data points rather than a single contiguous trajectory—is very important for practical applications and could be mentioned earlier and more prominently.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable core contribution. The kernelization of DMD is an elegant idea that successfully bridges two important fields. The theoretical exposition is solid and clear. The primary weaknesses lie in the insufficient positioning with respect to closely related work (Extended DMD) and the lack of quantitative, comparative experiments. Despite these shortcomings, the novelty and significance of the core idea are high. I believe the paper would be of great interest to the NIPS community, and I lean towards acceptance, with the strong recommendation that the authors address the aforementioned weaknesses in the final version, particularly the comparison with eDMD and the strengthening of the experimental evaluation.
