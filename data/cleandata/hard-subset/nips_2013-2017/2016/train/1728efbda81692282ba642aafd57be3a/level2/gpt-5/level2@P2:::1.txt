Summary
The paper proposes performing Koopman spectral analysis in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The authors present two closely related algorithms: (i) a DMD-like procedure in feature space obtained by orthogonalizing the feature-space Krylov matrix via Gram–Schmidt and computing a projected Koopman operator, and (ii) a robustified variant that projects onto truncated kernel-PCA (POD) directions before forming the finite-dimensional operator F̂ = U* K_H U, followed by eigendecomposition. Theorems establish exact feature-space reconstructions on the observed time window (with a residual orthogonal to the span) and connect the eigenpairs of F̂ to Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions under subspace richness assumptions. The paper also discusses three applications: short-term prediction via a pre-image construction, recognition of dynamics via subspace angles between estimated eigenfunction spans, and sequential change-point detection via CUSUM-like scores in an RKHS exponential-family model. Experiments include a simple nonlinear toy system, the Hénon map, a motion-capture embedding, and a change-point example.

Relation to prior work
DMD and its variants (exact DMD, optimized DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD) are well-summarized. The closest prior is Extended DMD (EDMD) and its kernelized variant (KEDMD) (Williams et al., J. Nonlinear Sci. 2015), which approximate Koopman spectra using a user-chosen dictionary or kernel features. Conceptually, the presented algorithms compute a Galerkin projection of the Koopman operator onto span{ϕ(x0),…,ϕ(xτ−1)} (or onto a truncated kernel-PCA basis U), yielding a finite-dimensional operator whose eigenpairs approximate Koopman spectra—this is essentially the EDMD/KEDMD construction with a data-driven dictionary (feature snapshots or their PCA basis). The Krylov/QR viewpoint is a useful re-derivation that ties DMD-style Arnoldi methods to kernel methods, but the practical operator (F̂) coincides with standard kernel EDMD forms G^{-1}A built from Gram matrices of snapshots.

Quality
- Theory: The statements parallel standard DMD/EDMD results. The reconstruction theorems are reasonable, but some steps are informal. For example, writing F = R^{-1}PτR = Mτ^{-1}K_H Mτ suggests an inverse of an operator-valued “matrix” in H; the subsequent use with an orthogonal projector Q Q* effectively builds in the residual term, but the presentation blurs when the action of K_H leaves the span. Assumptions such as distinct eigenvalues and eigenfunction representability within the chosen subspace are strong but common in this literature. The robust variant derives the usual projected operator F̂ = U* K_H U; the connection to Koopman eigenfunctions is standard EDMD theory.
- Applications: The pre-image prediction method is heuristic (MDS-like) and lacks error analysis; the change-point detection derivation (RKHS exponential family) is presented tersely and omits key implementation details (e.g., estimating log-partition, calibration of thresholds, handling noise). The recognition experiment uses kernel principal angles but reports no classification metrics.
- Empirics: Proof-of-concept plots are given, but there is no systematic, quantitative evaluation (prediction error curves, robustness to noise, sensitivity to kernel hyperparameters, comparison to EDMD/KEDMD/Exact DMD). The Hénon results are visually suggestive but not rigorous; “approximately estimated” eigenvalues of equilibria are not quantified.

Clarity
The manuscript is readable but notation-heavy and occasionally imprecise. Suggestions:
- Clearly distinguish operators in H from finite matrices; avoid writing inverses of non-square/tall matrices (instead, use projectors and pseudoinverses).
- Provide a concise pseudo-code for both algorithms with computational complexity and numerical stabilization (pivoted QR/SVD).
- Clarify assumptions (ergodicity/attractor measures, noise, stationarity). The statement “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on the unit circle” needs qualification (e.g., measure-preserving systems). Many dissipative attractors exhibit eigenvalues inside the unit disk.
- The pre-image section should outline the exact optimization and initialization, and state when the method fails (non-invertible feature map, ambiguity).

Originality
Moderate. The kernelization of DMD and use of kernel-PCA/POD to stabilize the operator is very close to EDMD/KEDMD, which already framed Koopman analysis in RKHS with Gram matrices and dictionary truncation. The new contribution is primarily a Krylov/Arnoldi perspective for the kernel setting and the explicit QR-based derivation, along with application sketches (pre-image prediction, change detection, and recognition). These are interesting but incremental relative to established EDMD/KEDMD methodology.

Significance
Potentially useful as an accessible bridge between DMD and kernel methods, but the impact is limited by overlap with KEDMD and the lack of strong empirical evidence demonstrating superior accuracy, stability, or scalability. Demonstrating clear advantages over EDMD/KEDMD (e.g., better spectral accuracy for a fixed number of features, improved robustness to noise, or lower computational cost) would enhance significance.

Pros
- Clear connection between DMD/Arnoldi and RKHS methods; helpful intuition for practitioners.
- Practical robustification via kernel-PCA truncation (mirrors common best practices).
- Breadth of application ideas (prediction, recognition, change detection).
- Kernel-trick-based implementation that avoids explicit feature maps.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to EDMD/KEDMD; F̂ matches standard kernel EDMD operators.
- Theory sometimes informal (operator equalities, distinct eigenvalues, coverage of multiplicities/Jordan blocks).
- Experiments are qualitative and lack rigorous comparisons, metrics, or ablations.
- Pre-image and change-point detection components are underdeveloped and could be standalone papers with more depth.

Questions for the authors
1. How does your method differ, in operator construction and outputs, from kernel EDMD? Under what conditions do they coincide?
2. Can you provide quantitative comparisons (e.g., eigenvalue/eigenfunction errors, multi-step prediction error) against EDMD/KEDMD and exact DMD on standard benchmarks (Duffing, Van der Pol, Lorenz, FitzHugh–Nagumo)?
3. How sensitive are results to kernel choice and hyperparameters? Any guidance on selecting kernel and truncation rank?
4. How do you handle noise? Does truncation via kernel PCA suffice, or is Tikhonov/TSVD regularization needed in practice?
5. Can you address multiplicity of eigenvalues and non-diagonalizable cases?

Minor comments
- Several typos and formatting issues in references and equations; please proofread.
- Clarify how Fig. 1 eigenvalues near equilibria were computed for the Hénon map (local linearization?).
- Provide runtime and memory scaling with τ and p (number of features/PCs).
- Release code with scripts to reproduce figures (URL is appreciated; ensure long-term accessibility).

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a useful synthesis and may be of interest to the DMD/Koopman community, but in its current form the novelty w.r.t. EDMD/KEDMD is limited, and the empirical validation is not strong enough for NeurIPS. Strengthening the theoretical distinctions (or proving equivalence and then arguing new benefits), and adding rigorous, quantitative experiments that demonstrate clear advantages would significantly improve the paper.
