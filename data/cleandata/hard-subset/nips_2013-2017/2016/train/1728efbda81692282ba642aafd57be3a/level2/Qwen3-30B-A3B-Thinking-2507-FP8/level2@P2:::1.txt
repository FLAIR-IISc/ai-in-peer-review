

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors present a modal decomposition algorithm that approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using nonlinear basis functions derived from kernel methods, rather than the linear monomials used in standard DMD. They further propose a robust variant using POD bases to address ill-conditioning issues.

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by extending DMD to RKHS, which could potentially provide more expressive approximations of Koopman eigenfunctions. The mathematical derivation of the algorithm (Theorem 1) and its robust variant (Theorem 2) appears sound, and the connection between the proposed method and Koopman spectral theory is well-established. The applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition demonstrate potential utility of the approach.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that limit its contribution to the field. First, the related work section (Section 5) inadequately compares the proposed method with existing approaches, particularly "extended DMD" (Williams et al., 2015), which also uses predefined basis functions rather than linear monomials. The authors note this similarity but fail to systematically compare their method with extended DMD, which would be crucial to establish the novelty and advantages of their approach.

Second, the empirical evaluation is insufficient to support the claimed advantages of the kernel-based method. The synthetic examples (toy system and Hénon map) are small-scale and don't demonstrate clear superiority over standard DMD. The eigenvalue estimation in Figure 1 appears to match the true values well for the toy system, but the paper doesn't show how this compares to standard DMD or extended DMD on the same data. The applications section (Section 7) lacks quantitative comparisons with baseline methods, making it difficult to assess whether the proposed approach provides meaningful improvements.

Third, the paper claims the method provides "a richer approximation" of Koopman eigenfunctions, but doesn't provide concrete evidence of when and why this approximation is better than linear approaches. The authors should demonstrate specific scenarios where the kernel-based method captures dynamics that standard DMD cannot, especially in regions where local linearization fails.

The robustification approach using POD bases (Section 4) is conceptually similar to existing DMD variants (e.g., Chen et al., 2012), but the paper doesn't clearly establish how this differs from or improves upon those methods. The empirical validation of this robust variant is also lacking.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. A comprehensive comparison with standard DMD, extended DMD, and other relevant methods on challenging datasets
2. Quantitative metrics (e.g., prediction error, change-point detection accuracy) to demonstrate advantages
3. Clearer identification of specific scenarios where the kernel-based approach outperforms linear methods
4. More detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability

The paper's theoretical contribution is promising, but the empirical validation is insufficient to demonstrate its significance for the field. The authors would benefit from conducting more thorough experiments to establish the practical advantages of their approach over existing methods. Without these improvements, the paper's contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting theoretical extension of DMD to RKHS, the lack of comprehensive empirical evaluation and insufficient comparison with related work limits its significance. The method shows potential but requires stronger evidence to demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches. I recommend rejection pending substantial revisions to address these empirical shortcomings.
