## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes a novel extension to Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) by incorporating reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to leverage the expressiveness of kernel functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, moving beyond the linear monomials used in traditional DMD. The authors present an algorithm based on calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition, effectively providing a feature extraction procedure for nonlinear dynamical systems. They demonstrate the method on synthetic and real-world data, including applications to state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper builds logically upon existing work in spectral analysis of dynamical systems, clearly situating itself within the context of Koopman operator theory, DMD, and related techniques like POD. The motivation for using RKHS is well-articulated, addressing a key limitation of standard DMD in handling highly nonlinear systems. The mathematical formulation is generally sound, and the connection to existing DMD approaches is clearly explained. The inclusion of a robustification step using POD bases is a valuable addition, addressing potential ill-conditioning issues. 

A strength of the paper is the thorough empirical evaluation. The results on both the toy system and the Henon map are promising, demonstrating the ability to estimate eigenvalues accurately. The application examples, while brief, showcase the potential of the extracted features for various tasks. The authors also provide a link to their Matlab code, promoting reproducibility. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical framework is presented, the practical considerations of kernel selection and parameter tuning (e.g., kernel width) are not discussed in detail. The application sections feel somewhat cursory; a more in-depth analysis of the performance compared to other state-of-the-art methods would strengthen the claims. The related work section, while comprehensive, could benefit from a more critical discussion of how this work differentiates itself from existing kernel-based approaches to system identification. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of DMD with potential for improved accuracy in nonlinear systems.
*   Sound mathematical foundation and clear explanation of the algorithm.
*   Promising empirical results on synthetic and real-world data.
*   Availability of code for reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of practical considerations (kernel selection, parameter tuning).
*   Application sections lack depth and comparative analysis.
*   Related work section could be more critical and highlight unique contributions.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of nonlinear dynamical systems analysis. While some aspects could be strengthened, the novelty of the approach and the promising results warrant acceptance, potentially with a request for minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above. I encourage the authors to expand on the practical aspects and provide a more thorough comparative analysis in a revised version.
