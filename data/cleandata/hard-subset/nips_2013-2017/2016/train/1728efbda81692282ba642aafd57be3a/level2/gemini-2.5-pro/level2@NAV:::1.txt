This paper proposes a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The central claim is that by leveraging the kernel trick, one can approximate the eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator using a richer, nonlinear set of basis functions, leading to a more powerful analysis of complex dynamics than standard DMD. This work builds upon a strong foundation of Koopman operator theory and DMD, which has seen increasing interest in physics and machine learning, and connects it to the well-established framework of kernel methods. The authors present a theoretically sound algorithm, derived from the Arnoldi method in RKHS, and a more numerically robust variant based on kernel PCA.

The primary strengths of this paper lie in its originality and significance. The fusion of Koopman theory with kernel methods is an elegant and principled extension that addresses a key limitation of standard DMD—its reliance on linear observables. The paper is technically solid, with clear theorems providing theoretical justification for the proposed method. A significant strength is the demonstration of the method's utility beyond pure analysis; by framing the decomposition as a feature extraction procedure, the authors showcase compelling applications in state prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection. The writing is clear, well-organized, and provides sufficient background for the reader to understand the contribution.

Despite these strengths, the paper has some weaknesses. The empirical evaluation, while illustrative, could be strengthened with more rigorous quantitative comparisons. The authors mention that extended DMD is a similar approach, but a direct experimental comparison is missing. For the prediction and change-point detection tasks, quantitative metrics against relevant baselines would make the results more convincing than the current qualitative plots. Furthermore, the paper lacks a discussion on the choice of kernels and their hyperparameters (e.g., kernel width), which is critical for the performance and reproducibility of kernel-based methods. Finally, the scalability of the method, which depends on Gram matrix computations, could be briefly discussed as a limitation for very long time series.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and novel contribution by developing a kernel-based framework for Koopman spectral analysis. The theoretical development is strong and the demonstrated applications are highly relevant to the NIPS community. While the experimental validation could be more thorough, the core ideas are significant and likely to inspire future work.
