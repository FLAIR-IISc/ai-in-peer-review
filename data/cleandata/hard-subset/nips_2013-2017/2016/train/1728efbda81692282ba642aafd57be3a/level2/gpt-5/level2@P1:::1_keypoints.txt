- Proposes kernelized Dynamic Mode Decomposition (kernel DMD) performing Arnoldi/Ritz analysis in RKHS to approximate Koopman spectral quantities from data.  
- Builds Krylov matrices from feature maps, computes orthonormal RKHS bases via Gram–Schmidt using kernel Gram matrices, and eigendecomposes the projected operator for Ritz values/vectors.  
- Introduces a robust variant using kernel PCA (POD in feature space) to truncate principal directions and improve numerical stability.  
- Theorems 1–2 connect Ritz quantities to Koopman eigenpairs under assumptions of span capture and distinct eigenvalues.  
- Applications demonstrated: short-term prediction via pre-image, dynamics recognition using kernel principal angles, and sequential change-point detection with a kernel exponential family CUSUM.  
- Empirical examples include a toy nonlinear system, Hénon map, motion capture, and a Lorenz-map change-point experiment.  
- Builds on DMD/Koopman literature and is closely related to EDMD/kernel EDMD; claims data-adaptive dictionary via kernel PCA as a contribution.  
- Needs clearer positioning and comparison to EDMD/kernel EDMD, including algorithmic, theoretical, and empirical differences.  
- Novelty is moderate due to substantial overlap with EDMD/kernel EDMD concepts.  
- Strength: clear conceptual framing of DMD as Arnoldi in feature space with a practical kernelized algorithm.  
- Strength: theoretical connections linking projected operator eigenpairs to Koopman spectra and kernel principal components.  
- Strength: practical handling of predecessor–successor pairs, truncation for ill-conditioned Gram matrices, and provided code.  
- Strength: breadth of applications showing potential beyond fluid mechanics.  
- Weakness: strong assumptions (distinct eigenvalues, Koopman-invariant span capture) limit applicability—needs discussion of continuous spectrum, quasiperiodic vs mixing dynamics, and noise.  
- Weakness: numerical robustness concerns for RKHS Gram–Schmidt; explicit regularization strategies and sensitivity studies are missing.  
- Weakness: experimental evaluation is limited, small-scale, lacks quantitative metrics and strong baselines (EDMD, kernel EDMD, AR/GP predictors).  
- Weakness: pre-image recovery approach may be brittle; quantitative errors and comparisons required.  
- Weakness: computational and memory complexity, out-of-sample evaluation, and streaming updates are not analyzed.  
- Clarity: derivations largely sound but some steps and notation (matrices, companion matrix, conditions) need clearer definition and separation of infinite vs finite-sample statements.  
- Quality: proofs rely on strong assumptions; recommend adding supplement proofs (e.g., Vandermonde argument) and discussing failure modes.  
- Originality: RKHS Arnoldi viewpoint and kernel PCA dictionary are appealing but overlap with existing methods.  
- Significance: potentially useful for practitioners but would benefit from stronger empirical comparisons and clearer EDMD positioning.  
- Suggestions: provide thorough EDMD/kernel EDMD comparisons (theory, algorithms, experiments) and clarify naming.  
- Suggestions: add quantitative experiments on more nonlinear systems with noise, ablations on truncation rank and kernel width, and regularization studies.  
- Suggestions: discuss continuous spectrum handling, detail pre-image computation and alternatives, and include complexity analysis and algorithm schematics.  
- Arguments for acceptance: elegant, practical framework with principled robust variant, clear Koopman connections, and code availability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental relative to EDMD/kernel EDMD, insufficient quantitative experiments and baselines, and reliance on strong theoretical assumptions.  
- Overall: promising and well-motivated but borderline; acceptance contingent on stronger EDMD positioning and more rigorous empirical validation.
