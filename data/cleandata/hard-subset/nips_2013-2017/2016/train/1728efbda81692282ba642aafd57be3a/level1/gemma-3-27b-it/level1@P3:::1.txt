This paper proposes a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) method leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to perform Koopman spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to extend standard DMD by approximating Koopman eigenfunctions with richer, kernel-induced basis functions, potentially overcoming limitations of DMD’s reliance on linear monomials. The authors present an algorithm based on Krylov subspace calculations and demonstrate its application to synthetic and real-world data, including prediction, change-point detection, and dynamic pattern recognition.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of related work. The introduction appropriately frames the problem within the context of learning dynamical systems, referencing Bayesian approaches, POD, and spectral methods. The discussion of DMD and the Koopman operator provides necessary background. The paper correctly positions its contribution as extending DMD’s expressive power via RKHS and accurately cites relevant literature (e.g., Arnoldi method, POD, DMD variants, kernel PCA). A particularly strong aspect is the explicit mention of connections to subspace identification methods used in system control, acknowledging an open research question regarding the relationship between these techniques.  The related work section is well-structured and comprehensively covers relevant literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of RKHS to Koopman spectral analysis and DMD is a genuine extension of existing techniques. It addresses a known limitation of traditional DMD by offering a more flexible basis function set.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the algorithm is mathematically well-motivated, building on the Krylov subspace framework and providing a clear connection to DMD.  The theorems presented are rigorously stated and contribute to a better understanding of the method.
* **Completeness:** The paper provides a complete description of the algorithm, including a discussion of potential ill-conditioning and a robustification strategy utilizing POD.
* **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world data demonstrations strengthens the validity of the proposed method. The presentation of applications beyond spectral analysis (prediction, change-point detection, dynamic pattern recognition) showcases the versatility of the technique.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are sufficiently detailed.  The algorithm steps are clearly outlined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the paper addresses ill-conditioning, it doesn't thoroughly discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly concerning kernel matrix calculations. The cost of RKHS-based methods can be significant, especially for large datasets, and a discussion of scalability would be beneficial.
* **Kernel Selection:** The choice of kernel (polynomial, Gaussian) appears somewhat arbitrary.  A more in-depth discussion of kernel selection strategies and their impact on performance is warranted. How does the choice of kernel parameter (e.g., width for Gaussian) affect the results? A sensitivity analysis would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:**  While the change-point detection example includes a comparison to a kernel change detection method, the paper lacks a comprehensive comparative evaluation against other state-of-the-art techniques for the tasks presented (prediction, dynamic pattern recognition). A more rigorous comparison would highlight the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method relative to existing approaches.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The empirical sections could benefit from a discussion on parameter tuning. What criteria were used to select the number of POD directions and the regularization parameters in the applications?
* **Practical Implementation Details:** While the code is available, the description of the practical implementation lacks some details. For example, how was the Gram matrix calculation optimized?  Were any specific libraries used for kernel calculations?


**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension to DMD that addresses a known limitation.
* The empirical results demonstrate the potential of the proposed method for analyzing and extracting information from nonlinear dynamical systems.
* The paper is well-written, complete, and includes a comprehensive review of related work.
* The availability of code promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost and scalability concerns are not adequately addressed.
* The lack of a comprehensive comparative evaluation limits the assessment of the method's effectiveness.
* The discussion of kernel selection and parameter tuning is insufficient.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the weaknesses mentioned above warrant attention, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical validation of the proposed method are compelling. The potential of RKHS-based DMD for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems is significant.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the computational complexity of the algorithm and potential scalability issues.
2.  Provide a more detailed discussion of kernel selection and parameter tuning strategies.
3.  Include a more comprehensive comparative evaluation against state-of-the-art methods for the applications presented.
4. Elaborate on practical implementation details (e.g., library usage, optimization techniques).
