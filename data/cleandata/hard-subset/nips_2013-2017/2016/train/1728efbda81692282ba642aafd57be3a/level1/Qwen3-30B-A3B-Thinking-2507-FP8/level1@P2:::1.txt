

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors build upon the Koopman operator framework, which provides a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics, and extend traditional DMD (which uses linear monomials as basis functions) by employing kernel-based nonlinear basis functions. The core contribution is a modal decomposition algorithm that performs spectral analysis in RKHS, reduced to calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. The paper further demonstrates applications in prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD with rigorous theoretical foundation. Theorems 1 and 2 provide clear mathematical justification for the proposed method, demonstrating how the kernel-based approach extends traditional DMD. The authors acknowledge and address numerical stability concerns through a POD-based robustification step, which is well-motivated by similar issues in standard DMD. The empirical validation on synthetic systems (toy nonlinear system, Hénon map) and real-world data (locomotion, Lorenz map) provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness. The comparison with existing methods (e.g., standard DMD, equilibrium DMD) in Figure 1 is particularly valuable. However, the paper could have provided more thorough comparisons with other kernel-based approaches to nonlinear system identification, such as Gaussian process dynamical models (GPs), which would strengthen the quality assessment.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction provides an excellent context for Koopman operator theory and DMD, with precise positioning of the paper's contribution relative to previous work. Mathematical derivations are presented with sufficient detail for experts to follow, and the connection between theoretical concepts and practical implementation is well-explained. The empirical examples (Figures 1-4) are well-chosen and effectively demonstrate the method's capabilities. The only minor clarity issue is the somewhat abrupt transition to the robustification step in Section 4, where the connection to POD could have been more explicitly motivated before presenting the technical details.

**Originality**: The paper makes a clear and meaningful contribution to the literature. While Koopman operator theory and DMD have been extensively studied (as noted in Section 5), the specific extension using reproducing kernels for nonlinear basis functions represents a novel approach to approximating Koopman eigenfunctions. The authors correctly distinguish their method from related work, particularly Williams et al.'s extended DMD, by emphasizing the kernel-based nonlinear basis functions rather than pre-determined functional maps. The paper's originality is well-justified by the detailed comparison with prior work in Section 5, where they properly cite and distinguish from related approaches like sparsity-promoting DMD and kernel-based system identification methods.

**Significance**: The significance of this work is substantial. By providing a more expressive representation of nonlinear dynamics through kernel-based nonlinear basis functions, the method addresses a clear limitation of standard DMD. The applications demonstrated (prediction via preimage, dynamics recognition, change-point detection) are relevant to both machine learning and scientific computing communities. The ability to extract meaningful features from nonlinear dynamical systems has broad applicability in areas like fluid dynamics, neuroscience, and robotics. The paper successfully demonstrates how the extracted features can be used for practical tasks, showing that this is not merely a theoretical extension but has tangible utility. The comparison with existing methods in the applications section (Figures 2-4) provides strong evidence of the method's practical significance.

**Strengths**:
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous theorems
- Clear connection to and extension of existing DMD literature
- Well-executed empirical validation across multiple domains
- Practical applications demonstrating the method's utility
- Good organization and clear writing

**Weaknesses**:
- Limited comparison with other kernel-based system identification methods (e.g., Gaussian process dynamical models)
- The robustification step could have been more thoroughly motivated
- Some technical details (e.g., the choice of kernel parameters) could have been more fully justified
- The discussion of computational complexity could have been more detailed

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:
Pro: This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the Koopman operator and DMD literature by extending the approach with kernel methods, providing a more expressive representation of nonlinear dynamics. The theoretical development is sound, the empirical results are convincing, and the applications demonstrate significant practical value.

Con: While the paper is strong, it would have been even better with more comprehensive comparisons to other kernel-based methods and a more detailed discussion of computational aspects.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of nonlinear dynamical systems analysis. It successfully extends DMD with kernel methods to provide a more expressive representation of dynamics, with clear theoretical justification and practical applications. The paper meets all the NIPS quality standards, demonstrating technical soundness, clarity, originality, and significance. I recommend acceptance for NIPS 2016.
