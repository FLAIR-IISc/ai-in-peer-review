This paper studies Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), proposing a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) that operates on Krylov subspaces of feature-mapped data. The core idea is to compute orthogonal bases in RKHS (via kernel Gram matrices), form the projected Koopman operator, and extract Ritz values/vectors that approximate Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions. A “robustified” variant projects onto kernel PCA/POD directions to address ill-conditioning. The paper connects its estimates to Koopman spectra through two theorems and demonstrates applications in short-horizon prediction (via preimage recovery), sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The work is related to extended DMD (EDMD) and kernel variants, Arnoldi/ERMs in control, and spectral learning methods discussed at NIPS (e.g., GPDM; spectral methods for latent-state models).

Quality: The algorithmic derivations are sound and align with Arnoldi/DMD theory. Theorems 1–2 are clearly stated and leverage standard kernel machinery. However, there is no finite-sample perturbation analysis, consistency result, or noise-robustness guarantee; the assumptions in Theorem 2 (eigenfunctions in span of kernel PCs) are strong and their practical validity is not quantified. Empirics are illustrative (toy system, Hénon) but lack rigorous baselines, metrics, or ablations (e.g., vs. EDMD/kernel EDMD, exact/optimized DMD).

Clarity: The exposition is generally clear, with helpful step-by-step procedures. Notation is occasionally dense (Mτ, Qτ, F̂) and complexity/storage costs for Gram operations are not discussed. A consolidated algorithm box and a brief guide to kernel/regularization choices would improve readability.

Originality: The formulation closely parallels EDMD and kernel EDMD (Williams et al., J. Nonlinear Sci. 2015), which also use RKHS/dictionaries to approximate the Koopman operator via finite-dimensional projections. The contribution here is an Arnoldi/QR view with a POD/kernel PCA robustification and concrete applications. The overlap with prior KDMD should be acknowledged and differences clarified.

Significance: Koopman analysis in RKHS is relevant to ML for nonlinear dynamics, and the applications are appealing. Yet, without stronger empirical comparisons and theoretical guarantees, it is hard to assess impact beyond existing KDMD/EDMD toolkits.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear kernelized DMD derivation with links to Koopman theory.
- Practical robustification via kernel PCA/POD.
- Useful application sketches (prediction, recognition, change detection) and released code.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty relative to EDMD/kernel EDMD; distinctions need sharpening.
- No quantitative evaluation or baselines; small, mostly illustrative experiments.
- Lacks finite-sample error/robustness analysis; scalability and kernel sensitivity not addressed.
