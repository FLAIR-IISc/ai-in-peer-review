Summary
This paper studies Koopman spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems in an RKHS and proposes a kernelized Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) procedure. The core idea is to (i) construct a Krylov matrix in feature space Mτ = [ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)], (ii) project the Koopman operator KH onto the span of these features via a Gram–Schmidt QR in RKHS, and (iii) compute empirical Ritz values/vectors as a finite-dimensional eigendecomposition. A “robustified” variant projects the data first onto kernel PCA (POD) directions, mitigating ill-conditioning and enabling truncation. The paper provides algebraic results (Theorems 1–2) linking the computed eigenvalues/eigenvectors to Koopman spectra under assumptions that the relevant eigenfunctions lie in the span of the chosen feature subspace. It also sketches three applications using the extracted modes: short-horizon prediction via a pre-image heuristic, dynamics recognition using kernel principal angles between subspaces spanned by estimated Koopman eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-style criterion in a nonparametric exponential family. Empirical illustrations include a toy system, the Hénon map, a locomotion embedding, and a Lorenz change-detection example.

Relation to prior work
DMD and its variants (exact, optimized, sparsity-promoting) are well covered. Extended DMD (EDMD) and kernel EDMD (often termed kernel DMD) by Williams, Kevrekidis, and Rowley are appropriately cited; the robustified method here is very close in spirit (using kernels to operate in an implicit feature space and low-rank projections via SVD/POD). The paper frames its contribution via an Arnoldi/Krylov viewpoint in RKHS; this is conceptually natural given well-known connections between DMD and Arnoldi, and it largely recovers the kernel EDMD procedure with a different derivation/emphasis. The applications (pre-image prediction, subspace-based recognition, CUSUM change detection) are reasonable but incremental.

Quality
- Theory: Theorems 1–2 are algebraically correct under their assumptions (distinct eigenvalues, inclusion of true eigenfunctions in the feature/POD span). As with DMD/EDMD, these are finite-sample identities and do not provide consistency or approximation guarantees for the Koopman operator with finite/noisy data. Important practical issues—regularization for nearly singular Gram matrices, choice of truncation rank, and kernel hyperparameter selection—are discussed only briefly.
- Algorithms: The QR/Gram trick and the kernel PCA projection are standard and appropriate. However, the proposed implementation details are thin: no complexity analysis, no discussion of numerical conditioning beyond POD truncation, and no treatment of noise (e.g., Tikhonov regularization).
- Experiments: The empirical section is qualitative and limited. Plots of eigenvalues for a toy system and the Hénon map are suggestive but lack quantitative metrics (e.g., eigenvalue errors vs. ground truth, sensitivity to noise, robustness across initializations). The prediction via pre-image is heuristic; there is no rigorous evaluation of prediction accuracy or comparison to baselines (EDMD/kEDMD, GPDM, RNNs). The dynamics recognition and change detection demonstrations are small-scale, without systematic benchmarks, ablations, or comparisons beyond one kernel change-detection baseline. Overall, the experiments fall short of NeurIPS standards for empirical validation.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and organized, with a clear statement of the main idea and step-by-step procedures. Some notation is heavy, and a few typos remain. The connection to EDMD/kernel EDMD could be made more explicit (e.g., showing algebraic equivalence under specific choices). Pseudocode and concrete implementation guidance (regularization, hyperparameter tuning, truncation criteria) would improve reproducibility. The inclusion of code is a plus.

Originality
The central method is very close to existing EDMD/kernel EDMD, with the main novelty being an Arnoldi/Krylov derivation in feature space and a practical pipeline centered on kernel PCA truncation. The applications are straightforward uses of learned features. While the perspective is cohesive, the methodological novelty over kernel EDMD appears incremental; a direct head-to-head comparison and a precise statement of differences would be necessary to clarify the contribution.

Significance
Koopman-based, kernelized spectral methods are useful for analyzing nonlinear dynamics, and a unifying presentation can be valuable. However, given substantial overlap with prior work and the limited empirical depth, the paper’s current impact is modest. Stronger theory (e.g., error bounds, sample complexity), rigorous experiments on challenging benchmarks with competitive baselines, and clearer differentiation from kernel EDMD would increase significance.

Pros
- Clear, cohesive presentation of Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS via a Krylov/Arnoldi perspective.
- Practical robustification via kernel PCA (POD) and use of Gram matrices for implementation.
- Code availability.
- Illustrative applications hint at broader utility.

Cons
- High overlap with EDMD/kernel EDMD; insufficiently articulated novelty and no direct comparisons.
- Limited and largely qualitative experiments; no quantitative benchmarking or ablations.
- Strong assumptions in theorems; no statistical guarantees with noisy/finite data.
- Sparse guidance on key practical choices (kernel selection, regularization, truncation).

Suggestions for improvement
- Explicitly derive and contrast the proposed method with EDMD/kernel EDMD, including equivalence conditions and differences (e.g., Krylov vs. least-squares formulations).
- Provide quantitative evaluations: eigenvalue/mode recovery on systems with known spectra, prediction error vs. EDMD/kEDMD and learning baselines, and systematic change-detection benchmarks.
- Analyze sensitivity to noise, kernel choice, and truncation; include regularization strategies.
- Add computational complexity and scalability analysis; discuss memory footprint with large τ.
- Clarify the pre-image method’s limitations and alternatives (e.g., learning inverse maps or predicting observables directly).

Overall assessment with respect to NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: Moderate; correct algebra but weak empirical and statistical validation.
- Clarity: Generally good, but needs tighter connection to prior kernel EDMD and more implementation details.
- Originality: Limited; overlaps with established EDMD/kernel EDMD.
- Significance: Moderate to low in current form.

Given these points, I lean toward rejection in its current form, but the line of work is relevant to the NeurIPS community and could become competitive with stronger empirical validation and a clearer differentiation from existing kernel EDMD approaches.
