Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis. The key idea is to lift observables via a feature map into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), define the Koopman operator on that space, and compute a finite-dimensional spectral approximation from finite data by operating entirely through Gram matrices. Two related procedures are presented: (i) a “feature-space Arnoldi/DMD” using Gram–Schmidt in the RKHS to form a Krylov subspace, followed by an eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator; and (ii) a robust variant that projects data onto kernel principal components (akin to POD truncation used in DMD) and then estimates a finite-dimensional operator F̂ whose eigenvalues/eigenvectors approximate Koopman spectra/eigenfunctions. Theoretical statements (Theorems 1–2) show that the feature-space Arnoldi yields Ritz values/vectors analogous to standard DMD and that, under subspace containment assumptions, the robust variant recovers Koopman eigenvalues and provides a modal expansion.

Beyond spectral estimation, the paper outlines applications that treat the extracted modes/eigenfunctions as features: short-term prediction via a pre-image computation, dynamics recognition via kernel principal angles between subspaces spanned by estimated eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection via a CUSUM statistic derived from a kernel exponential family. Empirical illustrations include a toy 2D nonlinear system, the Hénon map, CMU mocap sequences, and a Lorenz-map change-point toy.

This work is closely related to extended DMD (EDMD) and its kernelized instantiation (kernel EDMD), which approximate the Koopman operator using a dictionary of basis functions and can be implemented via Gram matrices. The robust, truncated operator F̂ constructed here is algebraically very similar to the finite-dimensional operator G−1A in EDMD/kernel EDMD (with G the Gram matrix and A the time-shifted cross Gram). The paper cites EDMD but does not deeply compare to its kernelized form.

Quality (technical soundness):
- The feature-space Arnoldi derivation is consistent with Krylov subspace methods and DMD theory: projecting the Koopman operator onto the span of {ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)} yields a finite operator whose eigenpairs provide Ritz approximations. The use of Gram matrices to implement QR/orthogonalization in RKHS is standard and sound in principle, assuming linear independence and numerical conditioning.
- Theorem 1 relies on distinct Ritz values and implicitly on invertibility/independence of the Krylov vectors (so that R is nonsingular). These conditions are not stated explicitly; clarifying them would improve rigor. The identification of F as a “companion matrix” is standard in Arnoldi contexts but the construction would benefit from more detail to avoid ambiguity in infinite-dimensional settings.
- The robust variant projects onto kernel PCA/POD directions, then forms F̂ = S̄−1/2 B̄* H (Mτ* M+) H B̄ S̄−1/2. This is well-defined and aligns with practice in DMD (POD truncation) and with kernel EDMD (finite operator in the feature space), though the effect of centering (H) on Koopman invariance is not discussed and may matter.
- Theorem 2 correctly formalizes recovery under subspace containment (κj in span(U)), yielding both eigenvalue recovery and a modal expansion. The proof sketch is reasonable, though some steps (use of Ḡ−1 and normalization conditions) could be presented more transparently.
- The application sections are heuristic. The pre-image method via MDS preserves local distances but can be unstable; complexity and convergence are not analyzed. The change-point detection formulation via a kernel exponential family introduces a likelihood with parameter ϕ∘f(xt−1) but does not address computation of the log-partition function g nor normalization; the subsequent approximation essentially reduces to a kernel-similarity CUSUM, which is plausible but not fully justified.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear in its high-level goals and algorithmic steps, with explicit procedures and reliance on Gram matrices. However, there are notational inconsistencies and typos, and some linear algebra steps (e.g., construction of F and the role of centering) would benefit from more precise assumptions. Figures are qualitative and sparsely labeled; quantitative evaluation is limited.
- The relation to EDMD/kernel EDMD is mentioned but not analyzed in depth; a clearer mapping would help readers situate the contribution.

Originality:
- Kernelizing DMD for Koopman analysis, operating in RKHS via Gram matrices, and using truncation/POD for robustness are natural extensions of DMD and EDMD. Prior work (EDMD, including kernelized versions) already provides a closely related framework. The paper’s feature-space Arnoldi perspective and theorems are useful, but the overall methodological novelty over kernel EDMD appears incremental. The application ideas (pre-image prediction, subspace angles for dynamics recognition, CUSUM change detection) are reasonable but not fundamentally new.

Significance:
- Accurate Koopman spectral estimation with kernels is impactful for nonlinear dynamics. If positioned and validated as an instance/variant of kernel EDMD with specific numerical procedures, the work can be a solid contribution.
- Empirical evidence is limited: eigenvalue plots for simple systems, qualitative mocap embeddings, and a toy change-point example. There are no quantitative benchmarks, error analyses, or comparisons to EDMD/exact/optimized DMD. Without such comparisons, it is hard to assess gains from the proposed procedures.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation to move beyond linear monomials via RKHS; clean Gram-matrix implementations.
- Theoretical connections (Ritz expansions in RKHS; recovery under subspace containment).
- Practical robustification via kernel PCA/POD truncation.
- Broad applicability and code availability.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty relative to EDMD/kernel EDMD; lack of explicit theoretical/empirical comparison.
- Incomplete assumptions in theorems (independence, invertibility, centering effects) and some imprecise statements (e.g., eigenvalues on unit circle claim is not generally true outside measure-preserving settings).
- Application sections are heuristic with limited quantitative evaluation and minimal baselines.
- Pre-image and change-point methods lack theoretical guarantees and reproducible details.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Explicitly relate the proposed operators to EDMD/kernel EDMD (derive equivalence or differences), and compare empirically on standard benchmarks (e.g., eigenvalue/mode recovery error, prediction error).
- State assumptions needed for Theorems 1–2 (linear independence, conditioning, distinct eigenvalues), and discuss centering’s impact.
- Provide quantitative experiments (noise sensitivity, kernel choices, truncation levels), ablations, and comparisons to exact/optimized DMD and EDMD.
- Clarify and justify the pre-image procedure (complexity, robustness) and the change-point detection likelihood (normalization, approximation quality); include baselines beyond a single kernel change detector.
- Correct or qualify statements about Koopman spectrum location.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid formalization of Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS with practical Gram-matrix algorithms.
- Useful robustification aligned with DMD practice; potentially valuable to practitioners.
- Broad applicability with illustrative examples and available code.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Overlap with EDMD/kernel EDMD is substantial; contributions appear incremental and not rigorously contrasted with prior art.
- Empirical validation is limited and largely qualitative; lack of baselines prevents assessing advantages.
- Some theoretical and presentation gaps (assumptions, centering, spectrum claims).

Overall, the work is technically plausible and practically motivated, but requires stronger positioning against existing EDMD/kernel EDMD and more rigorous, quantitative validation to meet NeurIPS standards.
