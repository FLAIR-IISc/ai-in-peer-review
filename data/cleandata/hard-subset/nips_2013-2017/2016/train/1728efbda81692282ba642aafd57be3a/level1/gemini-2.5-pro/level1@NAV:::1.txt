This paper presents a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is that standard DMD is limited by its use of linear observables to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions. This work, termed Kernel DMD (kDMD), leverages the kernel trick to implicitly work with a much richer, nonlinear set of basis functions in the feature space. The authors derive a principled algorithm based on the Krylov subspace in RKHS and propose a more robust variant that projects data onto kernel principal components to handle ill-conditioning. This work builds upon the extensive literature on DMD from the physics community and connects it to kernel-based and spectral learning methods previously explored at NIPS and in the broader machine learning literature, such as Extended DMD and kernel subspace methods.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its originality and significance. The synthesis of Koopman operator theory with kernel methods is an elegant and powerful contribution that bridges methods from physics and machine learning. The theoretical development is of high quality; the authors present clear theorems supporting their approach and thoughtfully provide a robustified algorithm that addresses practical implementation issues, demonstrating technical depth. The work is also clearly written and well-organized, making the novel concepts accessible. A further strength is the demonstration of kDMD as a feature extraction tool for various machine learning tasks, including prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition, highlighting its potential for broad impact.

The main weakness is in the experimental validation. While the applications are compelling, the empirical results are largely illustrative and lack rigorous quantitative comparisons against strong baselines. For instance, the prediction and change-point detection results would be more convincing if benchmarked against standard nonlinear time-series models or other DMD variants. Furthermore, while the paper acknowledges the similarity to Extended DMD, a direct empirical comparison would have better clarified the practical advantages and novelty of the proposed kDMD formulation.

Arguments for acceptance:
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound method combining Koopman analysis and kernel methods.
*   The work is of high technical quality, complete with a practical, robust algorithm.
*   Demonstrates significant potential impact through a diverse set of applications.

Arguments against acceptance:
*   Empirical validation could be strengthened with quantitative comparisons to relevant baselines.

Overall, the strong conceptual contribution and technical quality of the paper outweigh the weaknesses in the experimental section. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors incorporate more rigorous baseline comparisons in the final version.
