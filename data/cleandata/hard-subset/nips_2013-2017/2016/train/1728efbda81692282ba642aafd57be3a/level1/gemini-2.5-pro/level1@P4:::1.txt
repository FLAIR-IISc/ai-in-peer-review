### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The central idea is to analyze the Koopman operator, which linearly evolves observable functions of a nonlinear system's state, in a high-dimensional feature space defined by a kernel function. Standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear combination of observables, which can be insufficient for highly nonlinear systems. The proposed method, termed kernel DMD (kDMD), implicitly uses a rich set of nonlinear basis functions provided by the RKHS, enabling a more accurate approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions.

The authors first present a principled algorithm based on the Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition of the Krylov matrix in the feature space. Recognizing that this approach may be numerically unstable, mirroring known issues with standard DMD, they propose a robustified version. This second algorithm first projects the data onto its principal components in the feature space (via kernel PCA) and then performs the eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projected onto this truncated, lower-dimensional subspace. The work provides theoretical grounding (Theorems 1 and 2) that connects the eigenvalues and eigenvectors computed by the algorithm to the true Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions.

Finally, the paper frames the method as a feature extraction technique for dynamical systems and demonstrates its utility in three machine learning applications: short-term prediction via pre-image reconstruction, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection. The method's effectiveness is illustrated on synthetic data (a toy system, Hénon map, Lorenz map) and real-world motion capture data. This work builds upon the literature of DMD and Koopman operator theory, and connects it to the well-established field of kernel methods in machine learning. It is also related to Extended DMD (eDMD), which uses a user-defined dictionary of basis functions; kernel DMD can be viewed as providing a systematic and powerful way to define this dictionary implicitly.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality: Technical Soundness**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is technically sound. The extension of the DMD principle to an RKHS is well-formulated. The connection between the algorithm and the spectral properties of the Koopman operator is rigorously established in Theorems 1 and 2, which are the theoretical core of the paper. The motivation for the robustified algorithm using kernel PCA is logical, drawing a clear parallel to the use of Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) for robustifying standard DMD. The experimental results, particularly the accurate recovery of eigenvalues for the toy system, effectively demonstrate the method's correctness.
*   **Weaknesses:** The formulation of the change-point detection application appears somewhat heuristic. The derivation of the log-likelihood ratio score relies on a non-parametric exponential family assumption, and the final decision rule is simplified by dropping a term with the justification that its variation is smaller. While this may be reasonable, a more detailed justification or empirical validation of this simplification would strengthen the claim. Similarly, the prediction application relies on solving the pre-image problem, which is notoriously difficult. The paper cites an MDS-based approach but provides limited detail, leaving the robustness and accuracy of this prediction step as a potential concern.

**Clarity: Writing and Organization**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction and background sections (Sec 2) provide an excellent overview of the Koopman operator and DMD, making the paper accessible to readers not deeply familiar with the topic. The progression from the mathematically direct but potentially unstable algorithm in Section 3 to the robustified, practical version in Section 4 is easy to follow. The core algorithms are presented as clear, itemized steps.
*   **Weaknesses:** The applications in Section 7 are presented very concisely. While this demonstrates the breadth of the method's potential, each application could benefit from more detailed explanation. For instance, the derivation of the change-point detection score and the specifics of the pre-image algorithm for prediction are brief. In Figure 4, the caption refers to "kDMD-SVM", but the text discusses a CUSUM-based method, which could cause confusion.

**Originality: Novelty**

*   **Strengths:** The paper's main contribution—the formulation of DMD within an RKHS—is novel and significant. It elegantly merges two powerful frameworks: Koopman operator theory for linearizing nonlinear dynamics and kernel methods for representing complex data. While the idea of using kernels to handle nonlinearity is common in machine learning, its specific application to approximate the Koopman operator via a DMD-like algorithm is a new and valuable contribution. This provides a data-driven, non-parametric approach to choosing basis functions, which is a key advantage over methods like Extended DMD that require a pre-specified dictionary.
*   **Weaknesses:** The robustification technique (projecting onto kernel principal components) is a direct and logical extension of the established practice of using POD in standard DMD. As such, it is less of a conceptual leap, though it is a necessary and well-executed component for making the method practical.

**Significance: Impact**

*   **Strengths:** The proposed method has the potential for high impact. It provides a principled and powerful tool for the data-driven analysis of complex nonlinear systems, a fundamental problem in many scientific and engineering disciplines. By generalizing DMD, it makes Koopman spectral analysis applicable to a wider range of problems where linear observables are insufficient. The positioning of the method as a feature extraction technique for dynamics opens up numerous applications in machine learning, such as time-series classification, anomaly detection, and control. The provision of code also facilitates adoption by other researchers.
*   **Weaknesses:** The method's practicality may be limited by computational complexity, as it requires constructing and decomposing a Gram matrix of size τ x τ, where τ is the number of data points. This could be prohibitive for very long time series. Furthermore, like all kernel methods, its performance depends on the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters, an issue that is not explored in detail in the paper.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Contribution:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated synthesis of DMD, Koopman theory, and kernel methods, addressing a key limitation of standard DMD in handling strong nonlinearities.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The method is supported by a solid theoretical framework and demonstrated with clear, compelling empirical results on both synthetic and real data.
3.  **Broad Applicability:** The work is significant for both the machine learning community (as a feature extraction method for dynamics) and for scientific domains where DMD is already an established tool.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and structured, making a complex topic accessible.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Depth in Applications:** The application section, while demonstrating breadth, lacks sufficient detail in the methodology and experimental setup for each presented task, particularly for change-point detection.
2.  **Practical Considerations:** Important practical aspects, such as scalability with respect to the number of data points and guidance on kernel selection, are not substantially addressed.
