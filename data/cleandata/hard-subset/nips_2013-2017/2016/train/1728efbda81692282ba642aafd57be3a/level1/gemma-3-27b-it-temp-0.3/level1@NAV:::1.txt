## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) algorithm extended to utilize reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to move beyond linear monomials used in traditional DMD, leveraging the expressiveness of kernel functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions more effectively. The authors present a method based on calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition, effectively providing a feature extraction procedure for nonlinear systems. They demonstrate the method on both synthetic and real-world data, including applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of existing work in spectral analysis of dynamical systems, correctly positioning its contribution within the context of POD, DMD, and related techniques like subspace identification. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of traditional DMD and motivate the use of RKHS to overcome these. The mathematical formulation is generally sound, and the connection to existing DMD approaches through the Krylov subspace is well-explained. The inclusion of a robustification step using POD projections addresses a practical concern regarding ill-conditioning, demonstrating awareness of potential implementation challenges. 

A key strength lies in the exploration of applications beyond simply extracting Koopman modes. Demonstrating the utility of these extracted features in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition adds significant value. The empirical results, while limited in scope, provide initial validation of the proposed method. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The empirical evaluation is relatively shallow. While the toy system and Hénon map provide proof-of-concept, more complex and realistic datasets are needed to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the RKHS-based approach over existing DMD variants. The comparison with other methods (beyond a single kernel change detection method) is limited. Furthermore, the discussion of the computational cost associated with working in RKHS is lacking. The authors mention potential ill-conditioning but don’t thoroughly address the scalability of the proposed method to high-dimensional data. The related work section, while comprehensive, could benefit from a more critical discussion of how this work differs from existing kernel-based DMD approaches.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of DMD using RKHS, potentially improving accuracy in approximating Koopman eigenfunctions.
*   Sound mathematical formulation and clear connection to existing literature.
*   Exploration of practical applications beyond mode extraction.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical evaluation with relatively simple datasets.
*   Lack of detailed discussion of computational cost and scalability.
*   Insufficient comparison with other relevant kernel-based DMD methods.



**Overall:** I believe this paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field, but its impact is currently limited by the scope of the empirical evaluation. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on the authors expanding the experimental results with more complex datasets and providing a more thorough discussion of computational considerations and comparisons to related work.
