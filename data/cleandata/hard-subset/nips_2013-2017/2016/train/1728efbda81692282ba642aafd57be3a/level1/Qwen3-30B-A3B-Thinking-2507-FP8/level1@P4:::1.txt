

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors build upon the established framework of Koopman operator theory and DMD, which has been previously applied to dynamical systems analysis in physics and engineering. While standard DMD uses linear monomial basis functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, the proposed method employs kernel functions to create more expressive nonlinear basis functions. The approach is mathematically grounded, with Theorems 1 and 2 providing theoretical justification for the modal decomposition algorithm. The paper also addresses practical implementation challenges through a robustification step using kernel PCA (POD), which parallels techniques used in standard DMD.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical extension of DMD to RKHS is well-justified and supported by mathematical proofs. The proposed algorithm correctly generalizes the DMD framework to nonlinear basis functions while maintaining the essential properties of Koopman spectral analysis. The empirical evaluation on synthetic systems (toy nonlinear system, Hénon map) and real-world applications (locomotion data, change-point detection) provides reasonable validation of the approach. The robustification step via POD addresses a key practical limitation of the initial approach, making the method more applicable to real-world data. However, the paper could have provided a more thorough comparison with Williams et al.'s extended DMD [35], which also uses pre-determined basis functions rather than kernel-based ones.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are clear, with sufficient detail for experts to follow the theoretical development. The connection between the proposed method and previous work (DMD, kernel methods) is explicitly established. The applications section is particularly well-structured, clearly demonstrating how the extracted features can be used for prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The figures (Fig. 1, 2, 3, 4) effectively illustrate the key results. The paper provides enough information for an expert to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The core contribution—applying RKHS to Koopman spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems—is novel within the context of DMD. While Williams et al. [35] proposed extended DMD using pre-determined basis functions, this paper uses kernel functions to create a more expressive basis that can adapt to the data. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from previous work, noting that their method "works with operations directly in the functional space" rather than using pre-determined basis functions. This represents a meaningful extension of the DMD framework, particularly for systems where linear approximations are insufficient. The paper adequately references related work, including the Koopman operator literature and kernel methods.

**Significance:** The proposed method addresses a meaningful limitation of standard DMD by providing a more expressive basis for Koopman spectral analysis. The applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition demonstrate practical utility beyond the theoretical framework. The ability to extract meaningful features for nonlinear dynamical systems has potential value for practitioners in various fields (physics, engineering, machine learning). The empirical results on real-world locomotion data show promising applications in dynamics recognition, which could be valuable for human motion analysis. The method advances the state of the art in Koopman spectral analysis by providing a more flexible basis for representation.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical extension of DMD to RKHS with rigorous mathematical justification
- Well-structured presentation connecting theory to practical applications
- Effective robustification approach using kernel PCA to handle numerical instability
- Demonstrated utility across multiple applications (prediction, change detection, recognition)
- Strong empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world data

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison with Williams et al.'s extended DMD in terms of performance metrics
- Insufficient discussion of kernel selection criteria (why polynomial for toy system, Gaussian for Hénon map)
- Limited analysis of computational complexity compared to standard DMD
- The robustification approach using POD is conceptually similar to techniques used in standard DMD, reducing novelty in this aspect

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel extension of DMD using kernel functions for more expressive Koopman spectral analysis
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous proofs
- Clear connection between theory and practical applications
- Empirical results demonstrate utility across multiple domains
- Well-written and well-organized paper

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited comparison with the most similar prior work (Williams et al.)
- Some aspects of the approach (robustification) are not highly novel
- Limited discussion of computational considerations

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of Koopman spectral analysis, extending DMD with kernel methods to provide a more expressive representation of nonlinear dynamics. The theoretical development is rigorous, the applications are well-motivated, and the empirical results are compelling. While the comparison with Williams et al.'s extended DMD could be strengthened, the paper presents a meaningful extension of existing methods with clear practical utility. The work is well-suited for NIPS, advancing the state of the art in spectral analysis of dynamical systems with a novel kernel-based approach.
