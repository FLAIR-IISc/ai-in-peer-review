This paper proposes a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) algorithm leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to extend traditional DMD, which relies on linear monomials, to use nonlinear basis functions provided by kernel methods. The authors present a theoretical framework and algorithms for performing this analysis and demonstrate its application to both synthetic and real-world data, including prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper builds on the growing body of work surrounding Koopman operator theory and DMD, referencing key papers in the field (e.g., Schmid, Rowley, Mezić). It correctly identifies the limitations of traditional DMD in capturing highly nonlinear dynamics and positions the RKHS-based approach as a potential solution. The connection to existing methods like kernel PCA and the discussion of robustifying the algorithm with POD bases are also valuable. The inclusion of applications beyond simply extracting modes demonstrates a practical intent.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending DMD to RKHS offers a potential improvement over existing methods for analyzing nonlinear systems, allowing for more flexible basis functions. This represents a worthwhile contribution, especially given the increasing interest in data-driven approaches to dynamical systems.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical development, particularly Theorem 1 and 2, appears rigorous and well-motivated. The connection to existing DMD techniques and the inclusion of POD for robustness enhance the technical merit.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the proposed solution. The mathematical notation is consistent, and equations are generally easy to follow.
* **Completeness:** The paper presents a complete algorithm, including practical considerations for implementation (e.g., handling ill-conditioning with POD) and provides empirical validation with multiple applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Empirical Validation:** While the empirical examples are encouraging, they are somewhat limited. The toy system demonstrates the method's ability to recover known eigenvalues, but the Hénon map example lacks detailed comparison against other methods. The applications section feels somewhat cursory; further analysis and comparisons would strengthen the claims.  The choice of kernel parameters (e.g., Gaussian kernel width) is not thoroughly justified.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't explicitly address the computational cost of the RKHS-based DMD, which can be significantly higher than traditional DMD, especially for large datasets. This is an important consideration for practical applications and should be discussed.
* **Related Work:** While related work is acknowledged, the discussion could be more comprehensive.  For example, there is a growing field of kernelized DMD variants.  A deeper dive into how this approach differs from others, beyond just using kernel methods, is needed. More discussion on the connection to extended DMD as mentioned in the paper is also needed.
* **Assumptions & Limitations:** The discussion of the underlying assumptions (e.g., φj ∈ Φ in Theorem 2) and potential limitations of the method is somewhat brief. A more detailed analysis of these aspects would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel extension to a popular technique (DMD) with a clear theoretical justification.
* The proposed method has the potential to improve the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems.
* The paper is well-written and provides a comprehensive algorithm with practical considerations.
* The empirical results, while limited, are promising.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical validation is not sufficiently strong to fully support the claims of the paper.
* The computational complexity is not adequately addressed.
* The related work discussion could be more thorough.
* The assumptions and limitations of the method are not fully explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of data-driven dynamical systems analysis. The core idea of extending DMD to RKHS is novel and theoretically sound. However, the paper would benefit from more extensive empirical validation, a more detailed discussion of computational complexity, and a more comprehensive review of related work.  Given these minor weaknesses, I believe this paper is **acceptably good** and leans towards acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the comments regarding empirical validation and computational complexity to strengthen the paper. I recommend that the authors include more benchmarks and more detailed discussion of parameter tuning.
