**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems, termed Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels (kDMD). The core idea is to generalize Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a popular data-driven technique for Koopman operator analysis, to the setting of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs). Standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions as linear combinations of the observed state variables, which limits its applicability to highly nonlinear systems. By lifting the state data into an infinite-dimensional feature space via a kernel function, the authors linearize the dynamics under the Koopman operator in this space. They derive an algorithm to approximate the Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions by projecting the Koopman operator onto a data-spanned Krylov subspace within the RKHS.

Recognizing that a direct implementation can be numerically unstable, the authors propose a robustified version of the algorithm. This version first projects the data onto its principal components in the feature space (via kernel PCA) before performing the eigendecomposition. This is analogous to the SVD-based truncation used in "exact DMD" and related methods. The paper demonstrates that the learned Koopman spectra can serve as powerful features for downstream machine learning tasks. It showcases applications in short-term prediction (using a pre-image technique), recognition and embedding of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection. The method is validated on both synthetic examples (a toy system, the Hénon map) and real-world motion capture data.

This work builds directly upon the literature of DMD [25, 26, 33] and Koopman operator theory [16, 20]. It effectively synthesizes these ideas with well-established kernel methods like kernel PCA [28]. The contribution is closely related to Extended DMD (EDMD) [35], which also uses a dictionary of nonlinear observables to approximate the Koopman operator. kDMD can be seen as using an implicit, data-dependent dictionary defined by the kernel, a connection that is important for contextualizing the work.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Generalization:** The paper presents a theoretically clean and well-motivated extension of DMD. The use of kernel methods to implicitly handle nonlinearity is a natural and powerful idea, freeing the user from having to manually specify a dictionary of basis functions, which is a key requirement of EDMD. The connection between the Koopman operator in RKHS and the Arnoldi-like procedure is clearly established in Theorem 1.

2.  **Practical and Robust Algorithm:** The authors correctly identify the potential for numerical instability in a naive implementation and propose a robust variant based on kernel PCA (Section 4). This is a crucial step that makes the method practical. The algorithm is based on standard computations involving Gram matrices, making it relatively straightforward to implement for anyone familiar with kernel methods.

3.  **Demonstrated Significance for Machine Learning:** A major strength is the demonstration of the utility of the learned Koopman modes as features for various machine learning tasks. The applications to dynamics recognition (Fig. 3) and change-point detection are particularly compelling and highlight the method's potential impact beyond the traditional fluid dynamics community where DMD originated. This makes the paper highly relevant to the NIPS audience.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and logically structured. It begins with a clear review of the Koopman operator and DMD, systematically develops the kernelized extension, addresses practical implementation issues, and then presents compelling applications. The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) are clearly stated and provide a solid foundation for the algorithms.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Positioning with Respect to Extended DMD (EDMD):** The main weakness of the paper is its discussion of related work, particularly EDMD [35]. The authors mention EDMD only briefly, stating that "the resulting procedure is similar to the robust-version of our algorithm." This significantly understates the connection. The proposed kDMD method can be formally viewed as an instance of EDMD where the dictionary of observables is implicitly defined by the kernel basis functions `{k(·, x_i)}`. A more thorough discussion is needed to clarify the novelty and comparative advantages of kDMD. For instance, what are the trade-offs between implicitly defining a very high-dimensional feature space with a kernel versus explicitly choosing a smaller, potentially more interpretable, set of basis functions in EDMD? A deeper comparison would strengthen the paper's contribution.

2.  **Practical Guidance on Hyperparameter Selection:** The performance of kDMD is critically dependent on the choice of kernel and its associated hyperparameters (e.g., the width of a Gaussian kernel). The paper provides no guidance on this crucial practical aspect, simply stating the kernels used in the experiments. For the work to be reproducible and widely applicable, the authors should include a discussion on model selection strategies, such as cross-validation, for choosing these parameters.

3.  **Assumptions in the Theory:** Theorem 2 relies on the assumption that the true Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the subspace spanned by the kernel principal components of the data. This is a strong assumption, especially for complex or chaotic systems where the attractor may not be fully sampled. While this is a common type of assumption in kernel-based learning, the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of its implications. When might this assumption be violated, and how would that affect the quality of the estimated spectrum?

4.  **Heuristics in Applications:** While the application section is a strength, the derivation for the change-point detection score is somewhat heuristic. The claim that the variation of one term is "much smaller" and can be ignored warrants more justification or at least empirical validation. While this does not invalidate the result, a more rigorous treatment would be preferable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel, theoretically sound, and significant synthesis of Koopman operator theory and kernel methods.
*   Addresses a key limitation of standard DMD for nonlinear systems in a principled way.
*   The proposed robust algorithm is practical and implementable.
*   Demonstrates strong potential for impact in machine learning through diverse and compelling applications.
*   The paper is clear, well-written, and technically solid.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The novelty is somewhat diminished by the strong and under-discussed connection to Extended DMD.
*   Lacks crucial practical details on hyperparameter selection, which hinders reproducibility.

**Conclusion**

This is a high-quality paper that presents an important and elegant contribution. It successfully bridges the gap between the dynamical systems community's work on Koopman analysis and the machine learning community's toolkit of kernel methods. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the formulation and its demonstrated utility in machine learning tasks, clearly outweigh the weaknesses. The primary concerns regarding the relationship to EDMD and hyperparameter selection can be addressed with revisions to the text. I recommend acceptance.
