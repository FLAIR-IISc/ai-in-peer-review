

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors extend standard DMD, which approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials as basis functions, to use nonlinear basis functions derived from kernel methods. The core contribution is a modal decomposition algorithm that calculates orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performs eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. The authors also present a robustified version using POD bases to address numerical instability issues.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical grounding with proofs of key theorems connecting the proposed method to Koopman operator theory. It relates well to previous work, building on standard DMD (Schmid, 2010) and extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), while addressing limitations of linear approximations. The applications section demonstrates practical utility in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data, including locomotion data from CMU motion capture.

Strengths include the clear mathematical development, thorough empirical validation, and meaningful applications that go beyond typical DMD usage. The robustification approach with POD bases is particularly valuable for practical implementation. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Weaknesses include limited discussion of computational complexity compared to standard DMD, and the experimental section could better quantify performance gains over existing methods. While the method shows promise, the paper doesn't sufficiently address when this kernel-based approach would be preferable to standard DMD or other alternatives like extended DMD.

Pro: Novel extension of DMD using kernel methods; strong theoretical foundation; practical applications with empirical validation; robustification approach addresses numerical issues.

Con: Limited comparison with existing kernel-based approaches; computational complexity analysis lacking; applications could better demonstrate superiority over alternatives.

The paper makes a significant contribution to Koopman spectral analysis by enabling nonlinear basis function approximation, advancing the state of the art in dynamical systems analysis. It addresses a meaningful problem with a technically sound approach that would be valuable to researchers working at the intersection of machine learning and dynamical systems.
