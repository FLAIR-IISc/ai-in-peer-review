This paper introduces a Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) method leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to extend DMD beyond linear monomial basis functions to nonlinear functions defined by kernels, aiming to better approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, especially for systems where linear approximations are insufficient. The authors provide a theoretical framework and demonstrate the approach on toy and real-world data, exploring applications in prediction, pattern recognition, and change-point detection.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation and technical details of the proposed method. It appropriately positions the work within the existing literature on spectral analysis, DMD, and Koopman operator theory, citing relevant papers (e.g., Rowley et al., Schmid, Jovanović et al.). The mathematical derivation appears sound, and the connection to the standard DMD framework is well-established through the Krylov subspace analysis. The inclusion of applications provides a practical dimension, and the empirical results, while limited in scope, demonstrate the potential of the method. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending DMD to RKHS is a valuable contribution, addressing a limitation of standard DMD which relies on linear approximations. This allows for capturing potentially more complex dynamics.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is rigorous and well-explained, building on established concepts from functional analysis and numerical linear algebra.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with all the underlying concepts. Equations are clearly presented, and the connections between different parts of the method are articulated effectively.
* **Applications:** Demonstrating applications beyond spectral analysis adds value, showcasing the potential of the extracted features for prediction, classification, and anomaly detection.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While not explicitly discussed, the computational cost of working in RKHS can be significant, particularly for large datasets. The authors mention potential ill-conditioning, and the POD-based robustification adds another layer of computation. A discussion of scalability and computational complexity would be beneficial.
* **Kernel Selection:** The choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., bandwidth for Gaussian kernels) is crucial for performance. The paper only briefly mentions this and does not provide guidance on kernel selection strategies. A more thorough discussion of kernel selection and its impact on the results would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical examples are relatively simple. Applying the method to more complex and high-dimensional datasets would provide stronger evidence of its effectiveness and scalability. Comparing the performance against other state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Extended DMD, sparse identification of nonlinear dynamics (SINDy)) would also be beneficial.
* **Application Depth:** The application sections feel somewhat superficial. For example, the change-point detection results are shown, but the performance is not quantified or compared to alternative methods. Providing more detailed analysis and benchmarking of the applications would strengthen the paper.
* **Reference Gaps:** While the paper references many relevant works, some related literature seems missing. For example, the connection to kernel-based methods for system identification (e.g., kernel ridge regression for state prediction) could be further explored and referenced.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and potentially impactful extension to DMD that addresses a key limitation of the original method.
* The theoretical framework is sound and the mathematical derivations are clear.
* The paper demonstrates the potential of the method through empirical examples, even if limited in scope.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost and scalability of the method are not adequately addressed.
* The empirical evaluation is limited and lacks thorough comparison with existing techniques.
* The application sections are superficial and lack quantitative analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of dynamical systems analysis. The extension of DMD to RKHS is a valuable idea with the potential to improve the accuracy and applicability of this technique. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of computational issues, a more extensive empirical evaluation, and a deeper analysis of the applications.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method and potential strategies for improving scalability.
2.  Provide a more detailed discussion of kernel selection and its impact on the results.
3.  Expand the empirical evaluation with more complex datasets and comparisons to alternative methods.
4.  Provide a more thorough analysis of the applications, including quantitative results and comparisons to baseline methods.
