Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses Koopman spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems by lifting observables into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) and performing a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD)-style eigendecomposition on the projected Koopman operator. Section 3 derives a kernelized Arnoldi/DMD procedure: build the feature-space Krylov matrix Mτ = [ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)] from a trajectory, compute an orthogonal basis via Gram–Schmidt in the RKHS (implemented through Gram matrices), and eigendecompose the projected operator Pτ to obtain empirical Ritz values and vectors, which, by analogy with DMD, approximate Koopman eigenvalues and modes in feature space. Section 4 presents a robust variant by projecting onto kernel PCA directions (POD in feature space), yielding a finite-dimensional operator F̂ computed purely from centered Gram matrices, enabling truncation for numerical stability. The approach is then used as a feature extractor for three applications: short-term prediction via preimage estimation; dynamics recognition via subspace angles between estimated Koopman eigenspaces; and sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-like test built on kernel-exponential-family modeling.

This work is conceptually close to EDMD (Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, J. Nonlinear Sci., 2015), which approximates Koopman operators on a chosen dictionary of observables; using a kernel corresponds to an implicit, potentially infinite, dictionary. Related RKHS-based transfer operator methods (e.g., kernel Perron–Frobenius/Koopman operators in RKHS; Klus et al., circa 2015–2016) also approximate spectral objects via Gram matrices. The “exact DMD” and “optimized DMD” variants (Tu et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2012) address numerical robustness; the proposed kernel-POD truncation is analogous. The recognition via principal angles recalls kernel subspace angles (Wolf and Shashua, 2003). The preimage step leverages known techniques (e.g., Kwok and Tsang, 2004).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The kernelized DMD derivations largely mirror standard finite-dimensional Krylov/Arnoldi arguments, and the Gram-matrix implementation is standard for RKHS. Theorem 1 restates known DMD relations in feature space. Theorem 2 connects the eigenstructure of F̂ to Koopman eigenfunctions under strong assumptions (eigenfunctions are representable as inner products with elements in the span of selected kernel PCs). These assumptions are nontrivial and restrict when the method recovers true eigenobjects; no consistency guarantees (as data length grows or with noise) are provided. The claim that “eigenfunctions are fully recovered if the Krylov subspace is sufficiently large” is theoretically optimistic absent conditions on richness, mixing, and kernel universality, and ignores continuous spectrum issues.
- Numerical aspects: The robustification via kernel PCA is sensible, but the algorithm still scales O(τ^3) in time and O(τ^2) in memory due to eigendecompositions of τ×τ Gram matrices; this limits long trajectories unless further approximations (Nyström, randomized SVD) or streaming formulations are used. Regularization (e.g., Tikhonov in eigensolves, ridge on Gram inverses) is not discussed; sensitivity to kernel choice/width and truncation level is not analyzed.
- Experiments: The examples (toy bilinear system, Henon map) qualitatively show eigenvalue estimates, but quantitative evaluation is limited. The applications are promising but underdeveloped: prediction lacks RMSE/MAE vs. EDMD/DMD/Gaussian process baselines; recognition shows an MDS visualization without classification metrics; change detection compares to a kernel method but without details on thresholds, power/false alarm trade-offs, or ablations. No comparisons with EDMD (with polynomial/RBF dictionaries) or kernel-EDMD variants are provided, which is critical for assessing benefits of the proposed formulation.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but could be improved:
- Notation: ϕ (feature map) vs. φ (eigenfunction) is easy to confuse. Be consistent with adjoints in Hilbert space (⋅∗) vs. conjugate transpose in finite dimensions. Clarify when objects live in H vs. C^τ.
- Theory: State conditions under which Theorem 1–2 hold (e.g., linear independence in feature space, distinctness of eigenvalues, noise-free vs. noisy data), and discuss continuous spectrum and non-diagonalizable cases.
- Practicalities: Detail computational complexity, memory, and suggested regularization/truncation strategies; discuss kernel selection. The statement “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is inaccurate in general (dissipative systems have decaying modes with eigenvalues inside the unit disk; unit-modulus holds for measure-preserving dynamics).
- Applications: Provide algorithmic details for the preimage step, thresholds in CUSUM, and data preprocessing.

Originality
Positioning the method as DMD in RKHS is natural and useful, but overlaps with EDMD/extended DMD and kernel transfer operator literature. The specific recipe (kernelized Krylov with Gram–Schmidt; robustification via kernel PCA; application suite) is a reasonable synthesis rather than a fundamentally new operator-approximation framework. Clearer differentiation from EDMD with kernel dictionaries and from kernel Koopman/Perron–Frobenius methods would help.

Significance
Koopman-based learning continues to influence data-driven modeling. A kernel-based DMD that operates purely with Gram matrices is appealing and could broaden applicability, especially for nonlinearly separable observables. However, the current empirical evidence is insufficient to demonstrate clear advantages over EDMD/kEDMD or to guide practitioners on when and how to use the method. Adding strong quantitative comparisons and scalability strategies would substantially raise impact.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Conceptually clean kernelization of DMD with an implementable algorithm using only Gram matrices.
- Sensible robustification via kernel PCA/POD truncation.
- Links Koopman spectral analysis to ML tasks (prediction, recognition, change detection).
- Theoretical statements aligning kernel projections with Koopman spectra (under assumptions).

Cons:
- Limited novelty relative to EDMD and kernel operator literature; positioning and comparisons are weak.
- Strong assumptions in theory; no consistency, noise robustness, or generalization analysis.
- Experimental evaluation is qualitative and small-scale; lacks baselines and metrics.
- Scalability and regularization issues are not addressed; preimage step is ad hoc and unevaluated.
- Some inaccuracies (e.g., eigenvalues on unit circle) and notation/clarity issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative benchmarks against EDMD (polynomial/RBF dictionaries), exact/optimized DMD, and kernel-EDMD on standard systems (Henon, Lorenz, Duffing), reporting eigenvalue/mode errors, prediction RMSE, classification accuracy, and change-detection ROC.
- Discuss and implement regularization (ridge/Tikhonov), randomized/Nyström approximations for large τ, and noise handling.
- Correct theoretical statements about spectra on attractors; expand discussion of continuous spectrum and non-normality.
- Provide guidance on kernel choice, bandwidth selection, and truncation criteria; include sensitivity analyses.
- Clarify the relation to existing kernel transfer operator approaches and articulate distinctive advantages.

Overall, the paper presents a useful synthesis, but requires stronger empirical validation and clearer differentiation to meet NeurIPS standards.
