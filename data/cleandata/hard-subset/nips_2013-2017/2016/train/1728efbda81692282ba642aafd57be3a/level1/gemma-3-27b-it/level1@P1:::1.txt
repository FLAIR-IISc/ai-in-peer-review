This paper proposes a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) algorithm utilizing reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to perform Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to extend traditional DMD, which relies on linear monomials, to utilize nonlinear basis functions afforded by kernel methods, potentially capturing more complex dynamics. The authors provide theoretical justification and empirical demonstrations on both synthetic and real-world data, showcasing applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon the recent surge of interest in Koopman operator theory and DMD. It correctly identifies the limitations of traditional DMD regarding its reliance on local linearizations. The extension to RKHS is a logical progression, offering the potential for more accurate representations of nonlinear dynamics. The mathematical formulation is presented with reasonable clarity, and the authors make a good effort to connect the proposed method to existing DMD techniques. The inclusion of applications helps illustrate the practical relevance of the work. The related work section is relatively comprehensive, citing relevant papers in DMD, spectral methods, and kernel methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending DMD to RKHS offers a potentially significant improvement over existing methods, particularly for highly nonlinear systems. This expands the applicability of Koopman-based analysis.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical derivation, particularly Theorem 1 and 2, appears rigorous and provides a solid foundation for the algorithm. The connection back to DMD principles is clearly articulated.
* **Practical Applications:** Demonstrating the method's utility in prediction, change-point detection, and pattern recognition strengthens the paper's impact.
* **Clear Presentation:** While the math is complex, the authors do a good job explaining the core concepts and their implementation. The inclusion of a code repository is a valuable addition.
* **Robustification with POD:** Recognizing the potential for ill-conditioning and incorporating POD bases to address this is a practical and important consideration.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't thoroughly address the computational complexity of the RKHS-DMD approach. Kernel methods are notoriously expensive, and scaling to high-dimensional data or large datasets could be a significant bottleneck. A discussion of computational trade-offs is missing.
* **Kernel Selection:** The choice of kernel function (polynomial, Gaussian) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of kernel selection and its impact on performance would be beneficial.  The justification for the chosen kernel widths and degrees is limited.
* **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the comparison to kernel change detection is present, a more comprehensive comparison to other nonlinear system identification methods (e.g., neural networks, Gaussian processes) is lacking. Showing a clear advantage over these alternatives is crucial for justifying the complexity of the proposed approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical examples are relatively simple. Applying the method to more challenging, high-dimensional datasets would provide stronger evidence of its effectiveness and limitations.  The Hénon map is a well-studied chaotic system; demonstrating improvements over existing analyses on this benchmark isn't particularly compelling.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method likely has several hyperparameters (kernel parameters, truncation thresholds in POD) that can significantly influence its performance. The paper doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the results to these parameters or provide guidance on how to tune them effectively.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of DMD with the potential to improve the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems.
* The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of the method and its underlying principles.
* The empirical results, while limited, demonstrate the feasibility and potential utility of the approach.
* The inclusion of applications adds practical relevance to the work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational cost and scalability of the method are not adequately addressed.
* The comparative analysis is limited, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage over existing techniques.
* The empirical validation is insufficient to fully assess the method's performance on complex, real-world datasets.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameters is not discussed.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a worthwhile contribution to the field of Koopman operator theory and DMD. The extension to RKHS is a promising direction, and the authors have presented a solid theoretical framework. However, the lack of detailed discussion regarding computational cost, comparative analysis, and hyperparameter sensitivity raises concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above by: 

1.  Providing a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the method.
2.  Conducting a more comprehensive comparative analysis against other relevant nonlinear system identification techniques.
3.  Applying the method to more challenging, high-dimensional datasets.
4.  Investigating the sensitivity of the results to key hyperparameters and providing guidance on their tuning.
5. Justifying the choice of kernel parameters more thoroughly.
