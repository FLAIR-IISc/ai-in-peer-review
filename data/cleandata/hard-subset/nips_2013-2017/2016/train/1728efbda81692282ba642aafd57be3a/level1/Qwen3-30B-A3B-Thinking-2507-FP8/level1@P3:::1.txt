

# Peer Review: "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes a novel extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) by incorporating reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors build upon the foundational work of Rowley et al. (2009) and Williams et al. (2015), extending DMD from linear monomial basis functions to nonlinear kernel-based representations. The paper presents a theoretically sound algorithm that computes orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performs eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. The authors further address practical implementation issues by proposing a robust variant using POD bases, and demonstrate applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical extension of DMD to RKHS, with clear mathematical derivations (Theorems 1 and 2) that correctly establish the connection between the kernel-based approach and Koopman spectral analysis. The authors properly contextualize their work within the Koopman operator framework and the DMD literature.

2. **Practical Implementation Insight:** The authors recognize and address the ill-conditioning problem common in DMD implementations by proposing a robustification strategy using POD bases, which is a valuable practical contribution that aligns with established practices in the field (e.g., as seen in Tu et al., 2014).

3. **Applications:** The paper demonstrates promising applications in dynamics prediction, change-point detection, and motion recognition, showing how the extracted features could be useful for practical tasks in machine learning and dynamical systems analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is significantly underdeveloped. The paper only demonstrates results on two synthetic examples (a toy system and the Hénon map) without comparison to established methods like standard DMD, extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), or other kernel-based approaches. The lack of quantitative comparisons makes it difficult to assess the claimed advantages of the kernel approach.

2. **Lack of Comparison to Related Work:** The paper fails to clearly distinguish its contribution from Williams et al.'s extended DMD (2015), which also uses pre-determined basis functions. The authors acknowledge this connection but don't provide a thorough comparison of their kernel-based approach with extended DMD, making it unclear what specific advantages the kernel approach provides.

3. **Practical Implementation Gaps:** The paper lacks crucial implementation details. How are kernel parameters (e.g., width for Gaussian kernel) selected in practice? What is the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to standard DMD or extended DMD? The paper states that the kernel approach "gives an approximation with a set of nonlinear basis functions," but doesn't provide evidence that this leads to more accurate Koopman eigenfunction approximations for complex systems.

4. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims the method "can yield direct information concerning the dynamics such as growth rates and frequencies" but doesn't demonstrate that the kernel approach provides more accurate or meaningful dynamics information than linear DMD for non-trivial systems.

**Critical Assessment:**

While the theoretical extension to RKHS is mathematically sound, the paper falls short in demonstrating the practical significance of this extension. The field of Koopman spectral analysis has seen rapid development, with Williams et al.'s extended DMD (2015) already providing a framework for using arbitrary basis functions. The kernel-based approach proposed here appears to be a specific instantiation of this broader framework rather than a fundamentally new contribution.

The most significant gap is the lack of empirical evidence showing that the kernel approach provides meaningful improvements over existing methods. For a NIPS submission, it's crucial to demonstrate not just theoretical novelty but also empirical advantages. Without comprehensive comparisons on diverse datasets (including real-world applications beyond the limited locomotion data shown), it's difficult to assess the paper's contribution to the state of the art.

The robustification strategy using POD bases is a practical contribution, but this alone doesn't justify the paper's main claim of a novel kernel-based spectral analysis method. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with extended DMD and other kernel-based methods for dynamical systems.

**Recommendation:**

The paper presents a theoretically interesting extension of DMD to RKHS, but the lack of comprehensive experimental validation and unclear distinction from existing extended DMD approaches significantly weakens its contribution. The authors should substantially strengthen the empirical evaluation by:
1. Adding quantitative comparisons with standard DMD and extended DMD
2. Demonstrating advantages on more diverse datasets
3. Providing clear evidence that the kernel approach provides better Koopman eigenfunction approximations
4. Clarifying the relationship and differences between their method and Williams et al.'s extended DMD

Without these additions, the paper's contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. I recommend rejection for the current version, but with the potential for acceptance after substantial revision addressing these empirical shortcomings. The theoretical foundation is strong, but the paper doesn't meet NIPS' standard for demonstrating significant empirical contribution to the field.
