### **Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to leverage the Koopman operator theory, which provides a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics in an infinite-dimensional space of observables. Standard DMD approximates this operator using linear functions of the observed state variables. The authors argue this is restrictive and propose lifting the dynamics into an RKHS via a kernel function. In this feature space, they develop a "kernel DMD" (k-DMD) algorithm to find the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator.

The paper presents two algorithmic variants. The first is a direct extension based on a Gram-Schmidt process in the RKHS, which is shown to be theoretically sound but potentially numerically unstable, an issue also present in naive DMD implementations. To address this, the authors propose a second, more robust algorithm. This version first projects the data onto its principal components in the feature space (using kernel PCA) and then approximates the Koopman operator on this reduced-rank subspace. This is analogous to the common practice of using Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD)/SVD to regularize standard DMD. The paper demonstrates the utility of the extracted Koopman modes (eigenvalues and eigenfunctions) as features for various machine learning tasks, including short-term prediction, recognition of dynamic patterns, and sequential change-point detection.

This work builds upon a growing body of literature at the intersection of dynamical systems theory and machine learning. It directly generalizes the DMD framework [Schmid, 2010; Rowley et al., 2009], which has gained significant traction in fluid dynamics. It also relates to spectral learning methods for dynamical systems seen at NIPS and other ML venues [e.g., Kawahara et al., 2012], but provides a distinct perspective rooted in Koopman operator theory rather than system identification or state-space models. The most closely related work is "extended DMD" [Williams et al., 2015], which uses a user-defined dictionary of basis functions; the proposed kernel method can be viewed as using an implicit, and potentially much richer, dictionary defined by the kernel.

---

**Qualitative Evaluation**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development is sound, progressing logically from the Koopman operator to DMD and then to the proposed kernelized extension. Theorem 1 provides a solid foundation for the direct k-DMD algorithm, correctly extending the principles of the Arnoldi method to the RKHS. More importantly, the authors demonstrate practical awareness by identifying the numerical instability of this direct approach and proposing the robust variant in Section 4. Theorem 2 provides a clear theoretical justification for this robust algorithm, connecting the computed eigenvalues and eigenvectors to the true Koopman spectrum under reasonable assumptions (i.e., that the true eigenfunctions are well-approximated by the principal components of the data in the feature space). The claims are well-supported by this theoretical analysis.

The experimental validation is good, but could be strengthened. The toy example and Hénon map experiments serve as effective sanity checks, demonstrating that the method can recover known eigenvalues. The applications in Section 7 are compelling and showcase the method's versatility. However, the evaluation is primarily qualitative. For instance, the prediction task (Fig. 2) would be more convincing with quantitative error metrics (e.g., RMSE) compared against other nonlinear time-series forecasting methods. Similarly, the change-point detection result is promising, but the justification for the simplified CUSUM score is brief. Despite these minor points, the work is a complete and valuable contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors provide a concise yet sufficient background on the Koopman operator and DMD, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with either dynamical systems or kernel methods. The motivation for using kernels—to overcome the limitations of the linear observable space in standard DMD—is clearly articulated. The mathematical notation is consistent and the derivations are easy to follow. The distinction between the direct (Sec. 3) and robust (Sec. 4) algorithms is clear, and the procedural steps for each are laid out explicitly. The figures are illustrative and support the text well. The provision of code is a welcome addition that enhances reproducibility.

**Originality:** The core idea of combining DMD with kernel methods is novel and represents a significant conceptual advance. While "extended DMD" [Williams et al., 2015] explored using richer function bases, the proposed kernel approach is more elegant and general, as it avoids the need to explicitly define a dictionary of basis functions. Instead, it implicitly works with the infinite-dimensional basis of the RKHS, a hallmark advantage of kernel methods. The connection is mentioned in the related work, but a more in-depth discussion comparing the trade-offs between explicitly choosing a dictionary (as in extended DMD) versus implicitly defining one via a kernel would have been beneficial. The applications, particularly the formulation of a change-point detection score based on the Koopman representation, also demonstrate originality.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is highly significant. It bridges the gap between the DMD community (largely in physics and engineering) and the kernel methods community in machine learning. This opens up new avenues for both fields. For researchers in dynamical systems, it provides a powerful, data-driven tool to analyze complex nonlinear phenomena without resorting to hand-crafted observables. For machine learning practitioners, it offers a principled way to extract meaningful dynamic features from time-series data, which can be used for a variety of downstream tasks as demonstrated. The method addresses the difficult problem of understanding and modeling nonlinear dynamics in a more powerful way than standard DMD. The framework is general and likely to be built upon by future research. The potential for impact is high, as the analysis of time-series data from complex systems is a ubiquitous problem.

---

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Elegant Synthesis:** The paper presents a highly novel and natural combination of two powerful frameworks: Dynamic Mode Decomposition and kernel methods.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The proposed method is theoretically well-grounded, with clear connections to both Koopman theory and the practicalities of numerical implementation.
3.  **Practical Relevance:** The authors propose a robust, practical algorithm and demonstrate its utility on a diverse set of compelling applications, highlighting its value as a feature extraction tool for dynamics.
4.  **High Potential for Impact:** The work connects two previously disparate fields and provides a tool that is likely to be widely used and extended by researchers in both machine learning and applied sciences.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Quantitative Evaluation:** The experimental results are largely illustrative. A more rigorous quantitative comparison against state-of-the-art methods in the application domains (e.g., prediction, change-point detection) would make the paper stronger.
2.  **Insufficient Discussion on Hyperparameters:** The choice of kernel and its parameters is crucial for performance, yet this is not discussed in depth. A sensitivity analysis or more guidance on hyperparameter selection would improve the paper's practical value.

**Conclusion:**
Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It introduces a novel, theoretically sound, and practically significant method for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems. The fusion of Koopman operator theory with kernel methods is a powerful contribution that is likely to inspire considerable follow-up work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
