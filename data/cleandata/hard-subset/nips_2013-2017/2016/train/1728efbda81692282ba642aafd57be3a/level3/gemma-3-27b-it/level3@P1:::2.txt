This paper introduces a method for spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to operate within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The core idea is to leverage the Koopman operator, a linear operator representing the dynamics, and approximate its eigenfunctions using kernel methods, thus allowing for the capture of potentially complex nonlinear relationships. The authors present an algorithm based on calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition to extract modes representing the system's behavior. Applications to state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition are demonstrated on synthetic and real-world data.

The work builds upon a growing body of research into spectral methods for dynamical systems.  The authors correctly position their contribution within this landscape, referencing seminal work on the Koopman operator [16], POD [12], and DMD [25, 26]. They acknowledge connections to related techniques such as subspace identification [23, 14] and kernel methods for nonlinear system identification [11, 34].  The paper effectively outlines the existing challenges with linear approximations and how the kernelization approach addresses these limitations by enabling the use of nonlinear basis functions.  The connection to existing DMD algorithms is made clear, framing their work as a generalization utilizing the expressive power of kernel functions.

**Strengths:**

* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The paper presents a mathematically well-motivated approach, extending DMD to RKHS in a logically consistent manner. The inclusion of Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 demonstrates a rigorous theoretical grounding.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper provides a relatively clear explanation of the underlying concepts, particularly the Koopman operator and its relation to DMD. The organization is logical, beginning with background information and progressing towards the proposed method.
* **Practical Implementation Details:** The inclusion of the algorithm steps and the mention of the availability of Matlab code ([http://en.44nobu.net/codes/kdmd.zip](http://en.44nobu.net/codes/kdmd.zip)) enhances the reproducibility and potential impact of the work.
* **Robustness Improvement:** Addressing the potential for ill-conditioning by projecting data onto POD directions demonstrates an awareness of practical implementation challenges and a commitment to developing a robust algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** While the kernelization of DMD is a valid extension, the novelty appears incremental. The core algorithm (DMD) is well-established in the dynamical systems community, and the extension to kernel methods, while useful, doesn’t represent a fundamentally new paradigm.  This is acknowledged by the authors themselves.
* **Application Impact:**  The applications presented (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) are somewhat standard. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage of this approach over competing methods like recurrent neural networks or existing change-detection techniques [7]. The empirical results, while promising, are not particularly striking.
* **Kernel Choice:** The paper briefly mentions kernel choices but lacks a detailed discussion of the impact of kernel parameters or a comparison of different kernels. The choice of kernel (polynomial for the toy system, Gaussian for the Henon map) seems somewhat arbitrary.
* **Complexity & Scalability:** The computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly the Krylov matrix calculation and eigendecomposition in RKHS, is not thoroughly discussed. Scalability to high-dimensional datasets could be a limitation.
* **Comparison to Alternatives:** A more thorough comparison to other methods in terms of accuracy, computational cost, and parameter tuning would strengthen the paper's contribution.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and well-explained extension of a known algorithm (DMD).
* It provides a practical implementation with publicly available code.
* It addresses potential implementation challenges through the POD projection.
* The work may be of interest to researchers in both dynamical systems and kernel methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the approach is limited.
* The application demonstrations are not particularly compelling or demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* A more comprehensive comparison to alternative techniques is needed.
*  The lack of discussion regarding computational complexity and scalability is a concern.


**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid, well-executed technical contribution, but its overall impact is somewhat limited by its incremental novelty. While the kernelization of DMD is a worthwhile extension, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.  Given the current state of the paper, I would lean towards a **weak accept** with a strong encouragement to the authors to expand the application demonstrations, provide a more thorough comparison to alternative methods, and address the concerns regarding computational complexity and scalability in a revised version. A more in-depth analysis of the impact of different kernel choices would also be beneficial.
