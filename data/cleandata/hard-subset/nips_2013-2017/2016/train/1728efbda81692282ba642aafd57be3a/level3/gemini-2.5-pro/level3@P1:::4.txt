This paper presents a method for performing Koopman spectral analysis in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The central idea is to extend Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a technique for finding approximations to the eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes of the Koopman operator, by leveraging the kernel trick. This allows the method to implicitly work with a rich set of nonlinear basis functions, potentially leading to better approximations of the Koopman operator for highly nonlinear dynamical systems compared to standard DMD, which is often limited to linear observables.

The authors propose two algorithmic variants. The first is a direct extension of the Arnoldi-based interpretation of DMD to an RKHS, derived via Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space. Recognizing the potential numerical instability of this approach, they then present a more robust algorithm based on projecting the data onto principal directions found via kernel Principal Component Analysis (PCA). This second approach provides a principled way to regularize the problem by truncating the basis. The paper situates this work within the broader context of decomposition techniques for dynamical systems (e.g., POD) and connects it to related ideas in machine learning and system identification. A notable strength is the framing of the proposed method as a feature extraction technique for nonlinear time series, with subsequent applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition demonstrated on synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The exposition is clear, logical, and builds from a concise review of Koopman theory and DMD to the novel contributions. The mathematical derivations are easy to follow, and the overall structure of the paper makes the key ideas highly accessible.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The extension of DMD to RKHS is a natural and elegant idea, and the provided theorems rigorously connect the computed eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the finite-dimensional operator projection to the true Koopman operator's spectral properties under reasonable assumptions. The development of a robustified version using kernel PCA shows a thoughtful consideration of practical implementation issues.

3.  **Significance and Novelty:** While related work like Extended DMD also uses richer function dictionaries, the explicit formulation within the RKHS framework is a valuable and novel contribution. It provides a principled and flexible way to define the function space through the choice of kernel. The most significant contribution, however, is the application of this analysis tool to a range of machine learning tasks. By demonstrating its utility for prediction, clustering/embedding of dynamics, and change-point detection, the authors effectively bridge the gap between tools from the physical sciences and problems of interest to the machine learning community. This significantly broadens the potential impact of the work.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

The primary weaknesses of the paper lie in the empirical evaluation, which could be strengthened to make a more compelling case for the proposed method's advantages.

1.  **Lack of Comparative Baselines:** The experiments successfully show that the proposed "Kernel DMD" can be applied to several problems. However, they do not demonstrate *why* the kernelization is necessary or advantageous over simpler approaches. A direct comparison with standard DMD (or the closely related Extended DMD with a simple monomial basis) on the synthetic examples (the toy system and the Hénon map) would be invaluable. This would allow readers to quantify the performance gain afforded by the RKHS formulation and better understand the types of problems where this more complex method is required.

2.  **Absence of Discussion on Kernel Choice:** The choice of kernel and its hyperparameters is a critical, and often challenging, aspect of any kernel-based method. The authors use a polynomial kernel for one experiment and a Gaussian kernel for others, but they do not provide any justification for these choices or discuss how they were made. How sensitive are the estimated eigenvalues or the performance on downstream tasks to the kernel width or polynomial degree? A discussion of this issue, or ideally a sensitivity analysis in one of the experiments, would significantly enhance the paper's practical value and completeness. It would help practitioners understand how to apply this method effectively.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   The paper introduces a well-motivated and technically sound extension of Koopman spectral analysis to RKHS.
    *   The manuscript is written with exceptional clarity.
    *   The work provides a strong bridge between dynamical systems analysis and machine learning by demonstrating compelling applications in prediction, recognition, and change-point detection.
    *   The combination of theoretical elegance and practical applications makes it a strong fit for the NIPS audience.

*   **Arguments against Acceptance:**
    *   The empirical evaluation lacks comparisons to non-kernel baselines, making it difficult to assess the practical advantage of the proposed method.
    *   The crucial issue of kernel selection and hyperparameter tuning is not addressed.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting an interesting and significant contribution. The weaknesses are primarily related to the experimental section and are addressable. I believe the paper is a clear candidate for acceptance, and I strongly encourage the authors to incorporate a baseline comparison and a discussion on kernel selection into the final version to further strengthen their excellent work.
