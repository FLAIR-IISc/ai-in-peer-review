

This paper proposes a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors extend DMD by using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, replacing the standard linear monomial basis with nonlinear kernel functions. The method computes orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performs eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projection, producing a modal decomposition of the dynamics into interpretable modes.

The work builds upon existing Koopman operator theory and DMD literature. Koopman spectral analysis has been used in fluid dynamics and control for decades, with DMD emerging as a practical approximation method. The paper correctly cites Williams et al.'s Extended DMD, which also uses pre-determined basis functions, and the authors' kernel approach can be seen as a specific instantiation of that framework. The paper's main contribution is the kernelization of DMD, which allows for more expressive nonlinear basis functions compared to standard DMD's linear monomials.

The mathematical derivation is technically sound and well-structured. The authors provide clear theorems establishing the connection between the kernelized algorithm and Koopman spectral analysis. The robustification via POD bases (Section 4) addresses a practical implementation issue that has been noted in DMD literature. The empirical results on synthetic and real-world data (Section 6) demonstrate the method's ability to recover Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions, with the toy system and Hénon map examples showing reasonable accuracy.

The paper's strengths include:
- Clear mathematical formulation of the kernelized DMD algorithm
- Appropriate theoretical grounding in Koopman operator theory
- Practical implementation details addressing numerical stability
- Multiple application demonstrations (state prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition)

However, the paper has significant limitations in originality and significance. The core idea of kernelizing DMD is incremental rather than transformative, as the authors themselves acknowledge by noting their method is "analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point" versus the more expressive kernel approach. The novelty appears limited to the kernelization of an existing technique rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach to Koopman spectral analysis. This is particularly noteworthy given the extensive literature on DMD variants, including Williams et al.'s Extended DMD, which already uses pre-determined basis functions.

The applications section is the paper's weakest point. The authors demonstrate dynamics recognition using kernel principal angles between Koopman subspaces, but fail to convincingly demonstrate advantages over simpler approaches like standard PCA or even RNNs. The change-point detection application (Section 7) shows comparable performance to existing kernel-based methods without establishing clear superiority. The paper does not provide sufficient comparison to state-of-the-art methods in the relevant application domains, making it difficult to assess the method's relative contribution.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-organized sections and clear mathematical notation. The writing is precise and accessible to readers familiar with DMD and kernel methods. However, the connections to broader machine learning literature could be strengthened, particularly regarding the relationship to recent deep learning approaches for dynamical systems.

In terms of significance, the paper makes a modest contribution to the literature on Koopman spectral analysis, but the incremental nature of the novelty limits its impact. The applications do not convincingly demonstrate how this kernelized approach provides unique advantages over existing methods, particularly in the context of machine learning applications where deep learning approaches have proven highly effective for dynamical systems.

Pro:
- Clear mathematical formulation of kernelized DMD
- Practical robustification via POD bases
- Multiple empirical demonstrations across synthetic and real-world data
- Solid theoretical grounding in Koopman operator theory

Con:
- Novelty is incremental (kernelizing existing DMD rather than introducing new theory)
- Applications do not convincingly demonstrate advantages over simpler or existing methods
- Limited comparison to state-of-the-art approaches in relevant domains
- Fails to establish why kernel methods are necessary when other approaches (e.g., RNNs) exist

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound extension of existing DMD methods using kernel techniques. While the mathematical derivation is correct and the implementation details are well-considered, the novelty is limited to a specific application of kernel methods to an already well-studied problem. The applications do not sufficiently demonstrate the method's distinct advantages, and the paper fails to convincingly argue why this kernelized approach is preferable to alternatives. The contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the significance for the machine learning community is modest. The paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and clearer articulation of the unique advantages this approach offers.
