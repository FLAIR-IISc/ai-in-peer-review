

This paper proposes an extension of Koopman spectral analysis to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), presenting a modal decomposition algorithm that operates in RKHS rather than using linear monomials as in traditional Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD). The authors demonstrate how their approach can approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using nonlinear kernel-based basis functions, which provides a more expressive representation than standard DMD methods. The work builds on previous Koopman operator analysis (Rowley et al., 2009; Williams et al., 2015) and extends the DMD framework with kernel methods, positioning it within the growing literature on spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems.

The paper exhibits strong clarity with a well-structured presentation of the mathematical framework and a thorough empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The applications to state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition demonstrate practical utility. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the theoretical contributions.

However, two critical weaknesses require attention. First, the authors do not provide a comparative evaluation against non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis methods (e.g., standard DMD or extended DMD) in the simulations, which is essential to demonstrate the specific advantage of their RKHS approach. Second, there is insufficient discussion of kernel choice and its impact on results; the authors use specific kernels without exploring how different kernel parameters or types affect the decomposition quality. A systematic exploration of kernel selection via simulations would strengthen the empirical claims.

Strengths include the novel theoretical extension to RKHS, excellent writing quality, and meaningful real-world applications. Weaknesses center on the missing comparative analysis and kernel sensitivity discussion. The work makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending Koopman analysis to RKHS, but the empirical validation would be stronger with the requested comparisons.

Pro: Novel extension of Koopman analysis to RKHS, well-written, practical applications. Con: Missing comparative analysis against non-RKHS methods, insufficient kernel exploration. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing these two points.
