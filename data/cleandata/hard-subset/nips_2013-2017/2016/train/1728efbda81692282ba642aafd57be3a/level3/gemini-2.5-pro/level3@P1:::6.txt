### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems within the framework of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The central idea is to generalize Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a technique popular in fluid dynamics, by using kernel methods. The authors propose an algorithm that lifts the system's state into a high-dimensional feature space where the nonlinear dynamics are represented by a linear Koopman operator. By performing an eigendecomposition of this operator's projection onto a data-driven subspace, the method extracts Koopman modes, eigenvalues, and eigenfunctions. These components provide a linear decomposition of the nonlinear dynamics. The paper presents a robust version of the algorithm based on kernel Principal Component Analysis (PCA), which is analogous to the use of Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) in standard DMD. The work is positioned as a feature extraction technique for dynamical systems, and its utility is demonstrated through applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition on both synthetic and real-world data.

This work builds upon the growing body of literature on DMD (e.g., Schmid, 2010; Tu et al., 2013) and connects it to well-established kernel methods in machine learning, such as kernel PCA (Schölkopf et al., 1998). It is most closely related to "Extended DMD" (Williams et al., 2015), which also uses a dictionary of basis functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions; the present work frames this within the flexible and powerful RKHS framework.

### Strengths

1.  **Clarity and Pedagogical Value:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides a clear and accessible introduction to the concepts of the Koopman operator and DMD, which may be unfamiliar to parts of the NIPS audience. The theoretical development, linking the Koopman operator to DMD principles and then extending them to RKHS, is presented logically and is easy to follow.

2.  **Principled Generalization:** The proposed method is an interesting and theoretically sound generalization of DMD to nonlinear systems using kernels. By leveraging the representational power of RKHS, the approach can, in principle, approximate a richer set of Koopman eigenfunctions than standard DMD, which is limited to linear observables. This provides a principled bridge between linear spectral analysis techniques and nonlinear dynamics.

3.  **Promising Applications:** The authors propose several compelling applications for their method as a feature extraction tool, including prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. These applications move beyond the typical analysis-focused use of DMD in physics and suggest its potential relevance to the machine learning community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation and Baselines:** The experimental evaluation is the weakest part of the paper.
    *   The results on the toy system and the Hénon map demonstrate that the algorithm can recover known eigenvalues, but they lack a crucial comparison. How does standard DMD perform on these tasks? Or Extended DMD? Without these baselines, it is impossible to assess the practical benefit of the kernelized approach. The added complexity of the kernel method must be justified by superior performance.
    *   The applications are presented as proofs-of-concept rather than rigorous evaluations. For the locomotion data, the MDS embedding is visually illustrative but lacks quantitative comparison. How does this representation compare to one derived from kernel PCA on the raw data, or other time-series embedding methods? For prediction and change-point detection, comparisons against state-of-the-art methods in those specific areas are necessary to demonstrate significance.

2.  **Incremental Theoretical Novelty:** While the application of RKHS to DMD is new, the theoretical machinery itself—lifting data to a feature space to linearize a problem and then using the kernel trick—is a very well-established pattern in machine learning (e.g., kernel PCA, SVMs, kernel CCA). The derivation of the algorithm, particularly the robust version based on kernel PCA, will not be surprising to readers familiar with kernel methods. Therefore, the core originality rests more on the novel combination of ideas than on a fundamental theoretical breakthrough.

3.  **Relevance and Scalability for Machine Learning:** The paper does not make a strong enough case for the practical utility of this method in a typical machine learning context.
    *   The framework is entirely deterministic. Most real-world machine learning problems involving dynamics are stochastic. While the authors briefly mention that the Koopman operator can be extended to stochastic systems, the proposed algorithm and all experiments are deterministic, which limits its immediate applicability.
    *   The computational complexity, involving the construction and eigendecomposition of Gram matrices, scales with the number of data points. The authors should discuss the scalability of their method and its limitations for large datasets common in ML.

### Questions and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Baseline Comparisons:** I strongly urge the authors to include a direct comparison with standard DMD on the toy and Hénon map examples. This would clearly highlight the advantages (or disadvantages) of the kernel approach in situations where the dynamics are nonlinear.

2.  **Clarity on Implementation:** Could the authors clarify if the use of the learned modal decomposition (e.g., for prediction) requires explicit manipulation of vectors in the feature space? The representation `φ̄j = MτH...bj` is a linear combination of training points in the feature space. Does using this representation avoid the need to ever explicitly compute vectors in `H`? If not, this could be a significant limitation compared to methods like kernel PCA where one typically only works with projections.

3.  **Strengthening the ML Case:** To improve the paper's significance for the NIPS audience, the authors should focus on one application and provide a more thorough comparison with relevant state-of-the-art methods. For instance, in robotics, inferring and classifying system dynamics from sensor data is a key problem. Demonstrating a clear win over existing system identification or time-series classification methods in such a domain would make a much stronger case for the paper's contribution.

4.  **Discussion on Stochasticity:** A more detailed discussion of the challenges and potential paths to extending this framework to handle noisy or stochastic dynamics would be valuable and would increase the paper's relevance to the machine learning community.

In conclusion, this paper presents a clear, well-written, and interesting extension of DMD using kernel methods. However, its contribution is hampered by a lack of rigorous empirical comparison to relevant baselines and a limited discussion of its applicability to the stochastic, large-scale problems often faced in machine learning. While the idea is promising, the current manuscript does not sufficiently demonstrate its practical significance over existing techniques.
