This paper proposes a method for spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), presenting a novel modal decomposition algorithm. The framework serves as a direct extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a popular technique for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems, by employing the kernel trick. This allows for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions using a richer set of nonlinear basis functions than is possible with standard DMD. The authors derive the core algorithm, propose a robustified variant using projection onto principal component directions to improve numerical stability, and demonstrate its utility through numerical experiments on prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition tasks. The work relates to a significant body of literature in machine learning and physics on spectral methods for dynamical systems, such as extended DMD and subspace identification, but is novel in its direct formulation and analysis within the RKHS framework.

The paper's primary strengths lie in its clear presentation and technical ambition. The motivation is well-argued, and the manuscript is well-organized, providing sufficient background for readers familiar with DMD and kernel methods. The idea of kernelizing DMD is a natural and powerful extension that could have a significant impact on data-driven analysis of complex systems. The technical work is largely solid, and the numerical results, while lacking extensive comparison to state-of-the-art alternatives, successfully demonstrate the potential of the approach on a variety of interesting applications.

However, the paper suffers from a major lack of clarity in its core theoretical section (Section 3.1), which undermines the soundness of the main claims. Specifically, the mathematical formalism surrounding Equation (7) and the proof of Theorem 1 is confusing and requires substantial revision.
1.  The definition of the Koopman operator in RKHS, `(KHϕ)(x) = ϕ ◦ f(x)`, is ambiguous. It is unclear how this defines a linear operator on the Hilbert space `H`, as it is only specified by its action on the feature map `ϕ: M→H`. The distinction from the standard Koopman operator in Eq. (2) is not adequately explained.
2.  There appears to be a mathematical inconsistency. `KH` is stated to be an operator from `H` to `H`, but its eigenfunctions `φj` are also elements of `H`. It is not clear how an operator defined on a mapping `ϕ` can have eigenfunctions in `H`.
3.  The proof of Theorem 1 is difficult to follow. The step claiming `MτF = KHMτ` seems to be a significant logical leap and is not rigorously justified. The proof appears to mix operator and matrix algebra in a non-standard way and implicitly relies on strong, unstated assumptions about the invertibility of operators constructed from data.

Additionally, there are minor typos (e.g., "the the" in Line 55).

In summary, while the paper presents a promising and significant research direction, the lack of rigor and clarity in the central theoretical derivation is a major flaw. I would recommend rejecting the paper in its current form, but I would be willing to reconsider a revised version where these fundamental mathematical issues are thoroughly addressed.
