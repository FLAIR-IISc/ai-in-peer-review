The paper introduces a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) method extended to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), offering a kernelized approach to Koopman spectral analysis. The central idea is to perform modal decomposition based on orthogonal bases of the Krylov matrix in RKHS, ultimately providing a finite-dimensional representation of the dynamics of a nonlinear system. This builds upon the established framework of DMD, which approximates the spectra of the Koopman operator, a linear operator representing nonlinear dynamics. The authors clearly explain the theoretical underpinnings and present a practical algorithm, aiming to move beyond the limitations of traditional DMD which relies on linear monomials as basis functions. The paper then explores applications of the extracted features, including prediction, dynamic pattern recognition, and sequential change-point detection, demonstrating these with synthetic data (a toy system and the Henon map) and a real-world locomotion dataset.

This work builds upon a substantial body of related research. Spectral analysis of dynamical systems has a long history, including methods like proper orthogonal decomposition (POD), balanced modes, and more recent data-driven approaches like DMD itself. The authors appropriately cite key papers in these areas [25, 26, 12, 31, 17]. The extension to RKHS naturally connects to the wider field of kernel methods, drawing parallels with kernel PCA [28] and related techniques for nonlinear dimensionality reduction. The authors do a good job of positioning their method within this landscape, acknowledging the connections to subspace identification [23, 14] and existing DMD variants [8, 33].

The strengths of the paper lie in its clarity and pedagogical value. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the Koopman operator and DMD, making the material accessible to readers potentially unfamiliar with these concepts, particularly those from fields outside of traditional fluid dynamics. The mathematical derivations are well-presented, and the algorithm is clearly outlined. The inclusion of applications, even if preliminary, helps to illustrate the potential of the method.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. A primary concern is the limited and somewhat unclear comparison to existing methods. While the authors mention DMD and POD, they don't provide a rigorous comparative analysis, especially on the toy examples. Including standard DMD results alongside the kernelized DMD results would be beneficial for demonstrating the added value of their approach. Similarly, a comparison to kPCA, given the inherent similarities, would strengthen the justification for the proposed method. The application results are also somewhat weak – the locomotion data demonstration lacks specific metrics or comparisons to established state-of-the-art methods for dynamic pattern recognition.

The originality of the work is incremental. While extending DMD to RKHS is a natural progression, the theoretical results themselves are not entirely surprising given the established literature on RKHS and kernel methods for linear operators. The core contribution lies in the specific algorithm for performing the decomposition and its application to dynamical systems. However, a more compelling demonstration of novelty would require a more significant performance improvement over existing methods.

Regarding significance, the potential impact of this work is currently unclear. While the theoretical framework is sound, the paper lacks a convincing demonstration of practical utility, particularly in the context of machine learning. The authors should clearly articulate concrete use cases where this kernelized DMD approach outperforms existing techniques. Suggesting a target application domain, such as robotics or control systems, where dynamical system inference is crucial, would be a valuable addition. The current deterministic setup is a limiting factor for ML applications that typically involve noisy data; addressing this would broaden the applicability of the method. Furthermore, the authors need to clarify the computational cost of representing and manipulating data in the feature space – if explicit feature maps are required, this could be a significant limitation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Clear and well-written exposition of a kernelized DMD approach.
*   Solid theoretical foundation grounded in RKHS and Koopman operator theory.
*   Potential for advancing the field of nonlinear dynamical systems analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited comparative analysis against existing methods (DMD, kPCA).
*   Weak empirical validation, lacking rigorous performance metrics.
*   Unclear practical significance and limited demonstration of real-world utility.
*   Deterministic setup limits applicability to many ML problems.
*   Potential computational cost associated with feature-space representations.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and well-written contribution. However, the limited empirical validation and lack of a compelling demonstration of practical significance raise concerns. I recommend **weak reject** at this time. The authors should address the concerns outlined above – particularly strengthening the comparative analysis, demonstrating clear performance gains in a relevant application domain, and addressing the limitations related to the deterministic setup and feature-space representation – before resubmission.
