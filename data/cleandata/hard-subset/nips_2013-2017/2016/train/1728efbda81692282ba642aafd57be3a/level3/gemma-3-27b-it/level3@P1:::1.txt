This paper introduces a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) approach leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to extend traditional DMD, which relies on linear monomials, to utilize kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with potentially richer, nonlinear basis functions. The authors present an algorithm based on calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition. They demonstrate the method on synthetic and real-world data, showcasing applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper builds upon the growing body of work on Koopman operator theory and DMD. Prior approaches, like those by Rowley et al. (2005) and Schmid (2010), have focused on approximating the Koopman operator’s spectra to understand and predict the behavior of dynamical systems. DMD, specifically, has gained popularity for extracting dynamic modes from data, as highlighted in works by Bagheri (2013) and others. This work extends this by incorporating the power of kernel methods, a direction also explored, though less directly, by Williams et al. (2015) in their extended DMD formulation. However, the connection to Williams et al. (2015) is not sufficiently detailed. A clear comparison of the algorithmic differences, computational costs, and theoretical underpinnings of both approaches is needed, particularly regarding the treatment of basis functions and the resulting approximations of the Koopman operator.

The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical grounding and the potential for capturing more complex dynamics through the use of RKHS. The extension of DMD to a kernel setting is a natural and potentially powerful step, and the authors provide a mathematically sound framework for performing this analysis. The empirical results, while limited in scope, demonstrate the feasibility of the approach on relatively simple systems.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. A significant issue is the lack of a comprehensive discussion of related work in kernel-based dynamical systems. The authors neglect to cite several pertinent papers, including those by Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc (2005), Tuia et al. (2011), Rojo-Álvarez et al. (2015), Mouattamid & Schaback (2016) and Franz & Schölkopf (2006), which are directly relevant to the intersection of kernel methods and time-series analysis. This omission weakens the positioning of the work within the broader field.

Furthermore, the paper lacks exploration of stochastic settings. The analysis primarily focuses on deterministic systems.  The authors should address the performance and limitations of their method when applied to noisy data or systems with inherent stochasticity.  Koopman operator theory *can* be extended to stochastic systems (e.g., see Mezić, 2013), and discussing this would significantly strengthen the work.

Another concern is the practical implication of using pairs of consecutive observables instead of full sequences, particularly for multivariate time series. The causal assumptions inherent in this approach need to be clearly articulated and justified. Specifically, the authors should discuss scenarios where pointwise relationships between variables are weak, and how this impacts the performance of their method.

The experimental evaluation is also underwhelming. The prediction results are fairly standard, and the comparative analysis is lacking. The authors should compare their approach to established time-series clustering methods, such as kernel k-means or subspace kernel methods. Furthermore, more rigorous evaluation against state-of-the-art online kernel change-point detection techniques is necessary to demonstrate the advantages of their proposed method.  Simply showing results without a benchmark makes it difficult to assess the true contribution.

Finally, there are minor typographical errors. The indexing of 'tau-1' should be consistent throughout the paper, and the notation k(xi.xj) on page 7 should be corrected to k(xi, xj) using commas.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of kernel methods to Koopman spectral analysis, providing a potentially powerful extension of DMD.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and clear mathematical derivation of the proposed algorithm.
*   Demonstration of feasibility through empirical examples, albeit limited.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient comparison to related work, particularly in the realm of kernel-based dynamical systems.
*   Lack of analysis and experiments on stochastic systems.
*   Limited experimental evaluation and lack of benchmark comparisons.
*   Unclear discussion of the practical implications of using pairs of consecutive observables.
*   Minor typographical errors.



**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field of nonlinear dynamics and machine learning. However, the lack of thoroughness in related work, the absence of stochastic analysis, and the limited experimental evaluation prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the weaknesses outlined above. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and position it as a more impactful contribution to the NIPS community.
