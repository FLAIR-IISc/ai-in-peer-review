

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors develop a modal decomposition algorithm that operates in RKHS, providing a more expressive basis for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions compared to traditional DMD which uses linear monomials. The method involves calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projection, with a robust variant using POD bases to address numerical instability. The paper demonstrates applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data.

The work builds on significant prior contributions in Koopman spectral analysis, notably the DMD framework introduced by Schmid (2010) and extended by Williams et al. (2015). While previous work has explored variants of DMD, this paper makes a meaningful contribution by incorporating the expressiveness of kernel methods, which allows for richer approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions beyond local linearizations. The approach connects naturally to kernel PCA and spectral methods for dynamical systems, while addressing limitations of standard DMD in capturing global nonlinear dynamics.

The paper demonstrates strong quality through its mathematical rigor, clear theoretical development, and thorough empirical validation. The algorithmic steps are well-motivated and the theoretical claims are soundly supported. The experimental section provides compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness on both the toy nonlinear system and Hénon map, with accurate eigenvalue estimation. The applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition demonstrate practical utility, particularly in the locomotion data example where the method successfully distinguishes between different motion patterns.

The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, with clear organization and excellent technical exposition. The connection between RKHS theory and practical implementation is particularly well-executed, making complex mathematical concepts accessible. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including algorithmic steps and implementation considerations.

The originality of the work is significant. While there are connections to extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) and kernel-based methods, the specific integration of RKHS with Koopman spectral analysis represents a novel contribution that addresses a clear limitation in existing methods. The paper properly situates itself within the literature, with appropriate citations to related work on Koopman operators, DMD, and kernel methods.

The significance of this work is substantial. By providing a more flexible framework for Koopman spectral analysis, the method enables better characterization of nonlinear dynamics across various domains. The applications demonstrated (prediction, change detection, dynamics recognition) showcase practical utility, and the approach could potentially benefit fields such as fluid dynamics, neuroscience, and robotics where understanding nonlinear dynamics is crucial.

However, the paper could be strengthened in two specific areas. First, the comparison between the RKHS-based method and traditional DMD (or other non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis methods) is limited to the toy system and Hénon map. A more systematic comparison across multiple datasets with varying degrees of nonlinearity would better demonstrate the advantages of the kernel approach. Second, the paper mentions kernel choice as an important factor but does not thoroughly explore its impact on results. A brief analysis of how different kernel types and parameters affect the quality of the Koopman decomposition would be valuable for practitioners.

Pro-acceptance arguments: Significant theoretical contribution that extends Koopman spectral analysis with RKHS; mathematically sound and well-justified; excellent empirical validation across multiple applications; clear and well-written manuscript; strong potential for impact in both theoretical and applied domains.

Con-acceptance arguments: Limited comparison to non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis methods; insufficient exploration of kernel choice effects; some of the applications (e.g., prediction) could be better justified in terms of how the method compares to state-of-the-art approaches.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of dynamical systems analysis, addressing a clear limitation in existing Koopman spectral methods. While the suggested improvements would strengthen the paper, the current work is technically sound, well-executed, and demonstrates clear significance. The method provides a valuable tool for analyzing nonlinear dynamics with potentially broader applicability than existing approaches. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the suggested improvements in a revised version.
