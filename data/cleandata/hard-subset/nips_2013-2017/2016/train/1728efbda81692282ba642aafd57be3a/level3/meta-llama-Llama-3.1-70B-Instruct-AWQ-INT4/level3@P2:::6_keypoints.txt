- Paper presents spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in RKHS and a data-driven algorithm using finite-length trajectories.
- Algorithm reduces to computing orthogonal bases for the RKHS Krylov matrix and eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator.
- Demonstrations on a damped 1D system, the chaotic Henon map, and real locomotion data.
- Comparisons to DMD, PCA, and kPCA are unclear and unconvincing.
- Method is an interesting kernelized extension of DMD and related to existing kernel methods (kPCA, kernel ICA, kernel tests).
- Paper is clearly written and pedagogically useful for readers unfamiliar with fluid-dynamics concepts.
- Reviewer questions practical usefulness of the method for ML applications and requests concrete use cases and state-of-the-art comparisons.
- Suggests applying the method to robotics or other ML domains involving dynamical-system inference to demonstrate utility.
- Theoretical results are unsurprising given existing RKHS/kernel linear-operator literature.
- Requests clarification whether modal decomposition requires explicit feature-space representations (potential limitation vs kPCA).
- Asks to include standard DMD performance on the toy example and Henon map to highlight differences.
- Deterministic-only experimental setup (no stochastic/noise components) limits relevance to ML where randomness is common.
- Quality: technically sound and claims supported, but practical ML relevance is questioned.
- Clarity: well written, but would benefit from more intuitive explanations of technical concepts.
- Originality: kernelized extension is interesting but possibly not novel given prior RKHS work.
- Significance: provides a new perspective on spectral analysis, but practical significance for ML is uncertain.
- Arguments for acceptance: new perspective, interesting kernelized DMD extension, clear pedagogical presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear ML usefulness, deterministic-only setup, unclear comparisons to DMD/PCA/kPCA, need clarification on feature-space representation requirements.
- Suggestions for improvement: identify concrete use cases and compare to state-of-the-art, include standard DMD baselines, clarify feature-space decomposition requirements, and consider stochastic/noise in experiments.
