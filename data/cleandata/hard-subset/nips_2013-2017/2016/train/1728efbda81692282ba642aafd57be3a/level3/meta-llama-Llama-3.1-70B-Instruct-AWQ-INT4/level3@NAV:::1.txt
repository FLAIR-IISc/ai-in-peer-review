This paper proposes a novel approach to spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs). The authors introduce a modal decomposition algorithm that extends dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) to RKHSs, allowing for the use of kernel-based methods to approximate the Koopman eigenfunctions. The algorithm is shown to be efficient and experimentally validated through various applications.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and its connections to previous work. The use of kernel-based methods is a significant contribution, as it allows for the approximation of nonlinear dynamics using a set of nonlinear basis functions.

However, there are some areas that require improvement. Firstly, the authors should provide a clearer distinction between their work and the existing literature, particularly Williams et al. (2015). A more explicit comparison and discussion of the differences between the two approaches would be beneficial.

Additionally, the authors should include a more comprehensive review of relevant prior work in kernel-based dynamical systems. The current list of references is not exhaustive, and the authors should consider including other relevant papers, such as those by Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Álvarez et al., Mouattamid & Schaback, and Franz & Schölkopf.

The experiments presented in the paper are not ambitious enough, and the authors should consider including more challenging datasets and comparing their method to state-of-the-art time-series clustering methods and online kernel change-point detection methods.

Furthermore, the authors should clarify the practical implications of using pairs of consecutive observables instead of sequences, especially for multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relations. They should also discuss the causal assumptions underlying their approach.

Finally, the authors should address the issue of stochastic settings, where the performance and limitations of their method are not well understood. They should provide a more detailed discussion of how their method can be applied to stochastic systems and what the potential challenges and limitations are.
