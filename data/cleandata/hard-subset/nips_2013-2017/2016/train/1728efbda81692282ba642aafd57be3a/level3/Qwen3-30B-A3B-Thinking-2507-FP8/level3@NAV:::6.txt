

This paper proposes a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors derive a modal decomposition algorithm that performs spectral analysis of the Koopman operator using finite-length data sequences, essentially reducing to calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and eigendecomposition of the operator's projection. The work builds on the established connection between DMD and Koopman operator analysis, extending it with kernel methods to provide nonlinear basis functions rather than the linear monomials used in standard DMD.

The paper demonstrates the method on two simple dynamical systems (damped 1D and Hénon map) and real locomotion data, with applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The writing is clear and pedagogically valuable, particularly for readers unfamiliar with fluid dynamics concepts.

Strengths include the clear exposition of the algorithm and its theoretical foundation, the practical implementation details addressing numerical stability through POD projection, and the demonstration of potential applications. The paper makes a useful connection between kernel methods and Koopman analysis, extending prior work on kernel PCA/ICA.

However, several weaknesses limit the paper's significance. The theoretical results are unsurprising given existing RKHS literature, and the practical usefulness for machine learning lacks concrete justification. The comparisons to DMD/PCA/kPCA are unclear, and the deterministic-only setup (no stochastic components) significantly limits relevance to typical ML applications where randomness is common. The authors should identify specific ML use cases where this method offers clear advantages over existing techniques.

The paper would benefit from including standard DMD performance on the toy examples to highlight differences, clarifying whether explicit feature-space representations are required (which would be a limitation compared to kernel PCA), and providing stronger justification for the kernel choice. While the method is mathematically sound and well-presented, it lacks compelling evidence of superior performance or unique applicability in machine learning contexts. The significance of the work for NIPS is questionable without clearer demonstration of advantages over existing methods in ML domains.
