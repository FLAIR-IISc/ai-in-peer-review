

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors build upon the foundation of Koopman operator theory, which provides a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics, and extend it using kernel methods to obtain more expressive eigenfunction approximations than traditional DMD approaches that rely on linear monomials of observables. The work relates to recent NIPS papers on Koopman spectral analysis (e.g., Williams et al., "A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator," NIPS 2015) and kernel-based methods for dynamical systems (e.g., Kawahara et al., "A kernel subspace method by stochastic realization for learning nonlinear dynamical systems," NIPS 2011), but makes a novel contribution by combining these two approaches.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through rigorous mathematical development, including proofs of key theorems establishing the relationship between the proposed RKHS-based DMD and the Koopman operator. The theoretical framework is sound, and the authors carefully address practical implementation concerns through a robustified variant using POD bases. The experimental evaluation on both synthetic systems (a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map) and real-world data (locomotion recognition and change-point detection) shows promising results, with the method accurately estimating Koopman eigenvalues and demonstrating utility in downstream applications.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, with a logical flow from theoretical foundations to algorithms, implementation details, and empirical validation. The authors provide clear descriptions of the algorithms and make the mathematical content accessible to readers familiar with DMD and kernel methods. The paper also includes helpful visualizations of results, such as the eigenvalue estimation in Figure 1 and the dynamics recognition in Figure 3.

The originality of the work is significant. While DMD has been widely adopted for Koopman spectral analysis, this paper's extension to RKHS represents a meaningful advancement by enabling more flexible, nonlinear basis functions for eigenfunction approximation. This is a substantial improvement over traditional DMD approaches that rely on linear monomials, which are limited in their ability to capture complex nonlinear dynamics. The connection to kernel methods also opens up new possibilities for leveraging the rich literature on kernel-based learning in dynamical systems analysis.

The significance of the work is demonstrated through multiple applications: short-term prediction using pre-image reconstruction, dynamics recognition via kernel principal angles, and sequential change-point detection. These applications showcase the practical utility of the proposed method beyond basic spectral analysis. The locomotion recognition example (Figure 3) particularly demonstrates the method's value in a real-world machine learning context, where it achieves meaningful clustering of different motion types.

The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of direct comparison between the RKHS-based approach and traditional non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis methods in the empirical evaluation. While the paper shows the proposed method works well on the test cases, it would be valuable to see a quantitative comparison with standard DMD or extended DMD on the same datasets to demonstrate the specific advantages of the kernel approach. The authors should also discuss how kernel choice affects results, potentially through ablation studies with different kernels or bandwidth parameters.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field of Koopman spectral analysis. The method has clear potential for practical application in machine learning and dynamical systems analysis, and the authors demonstrate this through multiple compelling examples.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Novel integration of Koopman operator theory with RKHS
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous proofs
- Excellent clarity and organization
- Demonstrated utility in multiple machine learning applications
- Well-executed empirical evaluation with synthetic and real-world data

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Limited comparison with traditional non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis methods
- Insufficient discussion of kernel choice impact on results

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of nonlinear dynamical systems analysis, extending the capabilities of Koopman spectral methods through kernel-based representation. The technical quality is high, the writing is excellent, and the applications demonstrate clear significance. With minor revisions to include the requested comparisons and kernel impact analysis, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
