### Summary

This paper presents a novel method, Kernel Dynamic Mode Decomposition (KDMD), which extends the popular Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) technique to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to leverage the kernel trick to lift the observed data into a high-dimensional feature space where linear relationships corresponding to the Koopman operator can be more accurately identified. Standard DMD approximates the eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator using a basis of linear observables. In contrast, KDMD approximates these eigenfunctions using the much richer, nonlinear basis functions implicitly defined by the kernel. The authors provide a theoretical foundation for this extension, including an algorithm for performing the decomposition and a more numerically robust variant based on projecting the data onto kernel principal components. The method is demonstrated on synthetic toy problems and real-world data for applications in prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

This work relates to a growing body of literature on data-driven analysis of dynamical systems. It builds directly upon DMD [25, 26] and its variants [8, 33]. It is also closely related to Extended DMD [35], which uses a user-defined dictionary of basis functions. KDMD can be seen as providing a principled and powerful way of defining this dictionary through the choice of a kernel. The work also shares conceptual similarities with other spectral methods for learning dynamical systems in the machine learning literature [15, 17].

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The primary strength of this paper is the novelty and potential significance of the proposed method. The generalization of DMD to RKHS is an elegant and powerful idea that provides a non-trivial bridge between the dynamical systems community and the kernel methods community. The theoretical development appears sound, and the connection to Koopman spectral analysis is well-established. If presented more clearly, this method could become a standard tool and the paper could be highly citable.

However, the paper's main weakness is its clarity. The manuscript is exceptionally difficult to read for a broad machine learning audience like NIPS. The presentation is overly mathematical, dense, and verbose, which obscures the core concepts and intuition behind the method. This significantly hinders its accessibility and potential impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Originality & Significance:** The combination of Koopman operator theory, DMD, and kernel methods is novel and significant. It addresses a key limitation of standard DMD—its reliance on linear observables—by providing a systematic way to incorporate rich nonlinear feature mappings.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a solid theoretical basis for KDMD, including proofs for the main decomposition theorems (Theorems 1 and 2). The development of a robustified algorithm based on kernel PCA (Section 4) shows a practical awareness of numerical issues common in these methods.
3.  **Potential Impact:** The proposed method is a powerful tool for feature extraction from nonlinear time-series data. The demonstrated applications in prediction, embedding, and change-point detection highlight its versatility and potential for broad use in various machine learning tasks involving dynamics.

**Arguments against Acceptance (and suggestions for improvement):**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation (Major Concern):** The paper is not written for a NIPS audience. It is needlessly verbose and assumes a high level of familiarity with advanced concepts in dynamical systems. Many statements could be made far more concise. The authors should strongly consider a pedagogical rewrite. For example, Section 3 could start with a high-level paragraph explaining the intuition of using the kernel trick to find a linear operator in a feature space before diving into the formal theorem. The current presentation makes it very difficult to grasp the key ideas without significant effort.
2.  **Redundancy and Structure:** The paper could be organized more effectively for a conference format.
    *   Section 3 presents an algorithm based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization that is acknowledged to be ill-conditioned. Section 4 then presents the robust version that is used in practice. For a short paper, it would be more efficient to briefly motivate the need for robustness and focus primarily on the practical, robust algorithm, rather than dedicating two full sections to this.
    *   Section 6, the core empirical validation of the method, is overly condensed. The results on the toy system and Hénon map are presented with minimal discussion or analysis. This section should be expanded to give the reader a better sense of the method's performance, limitations, and sensitivity to parameters (like kernel choice).
3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The choice of kernels for the test problems raises questions about the method's practical application. The toy problem has polynomial dynamics, and a polynomial kernel is used. KDMD is most powerful when an appropriate kernel/feature map can be selected for the underlying dynamics. The paper would be much stronger if it included a discussion on kernel selection for systems where the underlying functional form is not known a priori. How does performance degrade with a mismatched kernel?
4.  **Related Work:** While the paper cites Williams et al. [35] on Extended DMD, the connection could be made more explicit. Furthermore, the authors might consider citing other relevant work on data-driven discovery of dynamics, such as Brunton et al. ("Discovering governing equations from data by sparse identification of nonlinear dynamical systems," PNAS, 2016), which shares the high-level goal of identifying structure in nonlinear systems from data.

### Minor Remarks:

*   The authors describe the toy system in Section 6 as nonlinear. This is correct due to the `x^2_t` term in the `y_t+1` equation. However, this could be stated more explicitly to avoid potential confusion, as the `x_t+1` dynamic is linear.
*   The applications in Section 7 are interesting but presented very briefly. For instance, the pre-image calculation for prediction is non-trivial and is described in a very condensed manner. Expanding on one of these applications with a more thorough experimental comparison would strengthen the paper.

In summary, this paper introduces a highly promising and elegant method. The core contribution is strong. However, its current form is not suitable for publication at NIPS due to major issues with clarity and presentation. I would be enthusiastic about this paper if it were rewritten to be more accessible to a machine learning audience, with a more focused structure and a more thorough empirical analysis.
