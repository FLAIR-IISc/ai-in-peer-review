**Summary**

This paper presents a method for performing Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The core idea is to extend the popular Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) algorithm, which approximates the spectrum of the Koopman operator, by leveraging the kernel trick. Standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear combination of the observed state variables. This work generalizes this by implicitly mapping the data into a high-dimensional feature space, allowing for the approximation of eigenfunctions with a much richer, nonlinear set of basis functions. The authors derive a "Kernel DMD" (kDMD) algorithm based on an Arnoldi-like procedure in the RKHS. They also propose a more numerically robust variant that projects the data onto its principal components in the feature space (via kernel PCA) before performing the decomposition. The utility of the method is demonstrated on several machine learning tasks, including state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using motion capture data. The work builds upon the extensive literature on DMD and connects it with established kernel methods and spectral learning techniques for dynamical systems.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper provides a novel and elegant synthesis of Koopman operator theory and kernel methods. This extension is a natural and powerful step, addressing the key limitation of standard DMD, which struggles with highly nonlinear dynamics that cannot be well-approximated by linear observables. By framing the Koopman analysis as a feature extraction problem for dynamics, the authors make a significant contribution that is highly relevant to the machine learning community and bridges the gap between physics-based modal decomposition and data-driven learning.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is sound and well-presented. The connection between the eigenvalues of the finite-dimensional matrix approximation and the true Koopman eigenvalues is clearly established through theorems that parallel the theory of standard DMD. The proposed robust algorithm, based on kernel PCA, is a well-justified and practical approach to handle the numerical stability issues common in these methods.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear introduction to the necessary background on Koopman operators and DMD before introducing their own contribution. The derivations are lucid, and the algorithms are stated precisely. This high level of clarity makes a technically dense topic accessible.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The primary weaknesses of the paper lie in the experimental evaluation, which, while demonstrating that the method works, does not sufficiently substantiate the advantages of the proposed kernelized approach over existing methods.

1.  **Lack of Comparative Baselines:** The experiments in Section 6 show that kDMD can estimate eigenvalues for a toy system and the chaotic Hénon map. However, to demonstrate the value of the kernel extension, a direct comparison with non-RKHS methods is essential. The evaluation would be significantly strengthened by comparing the performance of kDMD against standard DMD and perhaps Extended DMD (eDMD) on these nonlinear systems. For example, one could compare the accuracy of the reconstructed dynamics, the quality of the predicted states, or the precision of the estimated eigenvalues. Without such a comparison, the practical benefits of moving to an RKHS are not clearly demonstrated.

2.  **Discussion on Kernel Selection:** The performance of any kernel method is critically dependent on the choice of the kernel and its hyperparameters (e.g., the degree of a polynomial kernel or the width of a Gaussian RBF kernel). The paper uses a polynomial kernel for one experiment and a Gaussian kernel for others with minimal justification. There is no discussion of how these choices affect the results or how a practitioner should select an appropriate kernel for a given problem. This is a crucial practical detail. I strongly encourage the authors to add a section discussing the role of the kernel. This could include theoretical insights into how kernel properties relate to the class of eigenfunctions that can be represented, or, more practically, an empirical sensitivity analysis showing how the results (e.g., eigenvalue accuracy) vary with different kernel parameters on one of the test systems.

3.  **Details on Applications:** The applications in Section 7 are compelling and showcase the potential of the method. However, the descriptions are somewhat brief. For instance, in the change-point detection experiment, it would be helpful to provide more detail on the parameter settings and a more quantitative evaluation of the detection performance (e.g., detection delay, false alarm rate) compared to the baseline. Similarly, for the prediction task, providing quantitative error metrics (like RMSE over a prediction horizon) in addition to the qualitative plots would be beneficial and allow for a clearer comparison (as suggested in point 1).

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and important contribution. The theoretical work is strong and the writing is excellent. The main weakness is the lack of comparative experimental results that would firmly establish the superiority of this method over existing DMD variants. Addressing the points above, particularly the inclusion of baselines and a discussion on kernel choice, would elevate this work from a good paper to an excellent one.
