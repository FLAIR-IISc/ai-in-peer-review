**Summary**

This paper proposes a method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to leverage the Koopman operator, which linearly evolves observable functions of the system's state, even when the underlying dynamics are nonlinear. The authors formulate a "kernel DMD" algorithm that lifts the state-space data into an RKHS via a kernel map and then approximates the eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator within this feature space. This is achieved by finding the eigendecomposition of the operator's projection onto a data-spanned subspace, which is made robust using ideas from kernel PCA. The resulting Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions serve as features that characterize the system's dynamics. The paper demonstrates the utility of these features in applications such as short-term prediction, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection.

The work connects two distinct lines of research: the Koopman operator and DMD, which are well-established in the fluid dynamics and physics communities, and kernel methods, which are a cornerstone of machine learning. While DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear observables, this work extends it to a much richer, non-linear function class defined by the kernel.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound. It provides a good introduction to the Koopman operator and DMD for a machine learning audience. The derivation of kernel DMD, particularly the robust version based on kernel PCA, is principled and easy to follow for readers familiar with kernel methods. The connection between the eigendecomposition of the projected operator and the Koopman spectral properties is well-established in the theorems.

2.  **Elegant Conceptual Framework:** The paper introduces an elegant perspective for the NIPS community: analyzing function composition, the fundamental operation in dynamical systems, as a linear operator in a feature space. This framing provides a bridge between the spectral analysis of dynamical systems and the geometric and algebraic tools of kernel methods, which is a valuable conceptual contribution.

3.  **Principled Extension:** The proposed method is a natural and well-motivated extension of DMD. By replacing linear observables with an implicit and high-dimensional feature map, the method has the potential to capture a much richer set of Koopman eigenfunctions, thus allowing for a more accurate analysis of complex nonlinear dynamics without requiring predefined basis functions.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Originality:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in its originality. The core algorithmic idea, Dynamic Mode Decomposition, is not new and has been extensively developed in the dynamical systems literature (e.g., Schmid, 2010; Rowley et al., 2009). The main contribution of this paper is the application of the "kernel trick" to DMD. While a valid and useful combination, this can be viewed as an incremental step rather than a fundamentally new approach. The "extended DMD" method by Williams et al. (2015), cited by the authors, already proposes using pre-determined nonlinear basis functions, which is conceptually very similar. The paper could do a better job of distinguishing its contribution from this and other DMD variants.

2.  **Unconvincing Experimental Evaluation and Significance:** The significance of the proposed method is not clearly demonstrated by the experiments.
    *   **Lack of Competitive Baselines:** The applications shown (prediction, recognition, change-point detection) are standard machine learning tasks for which strong baselines exist. For time-series prediction and recognition, Recurrent Neural Networks (LSTMs, GRUs) or Gaussian Process Dynamical Models are state-of-the-art. The paper does not compare against any of these, making it impossible to assess the practical performance and utility of kernel DMD. Without such comparisons, the claim that this method is useful for these applications is not well-supported.
    *   **Generic Applications:** The chosen applications do not effectively showcase the unique advantages of a Koopman spectral decomposition. The strength of such a method should lie in its ability to provide interpretable modes (e.g., identifying frequencies, growth/decay rates) that are dynamically meaningful. The experiments, however, treat the method largely as a black-box feature extractor for generic downstream tasks. A more compelling application would have been one where the interpretability of the Koopman modes provides unique insights that other methods cannot.
    *   **Superficial Change-Point Detection:** The change-point detection experiment is particularly weak. The proposed score is heuristic, and it is only compared against one other kernel-based method. The field of change-point detection is mature, and a more thorough comparison against established statistical methods would be necessary to claim significance.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   To strengthen the paper's claims of originality, the authors should provide a more detailed discussion of how kernel DMD differs from and improves upon "extended DMD" and other methods that use nonlinear observables. Is the main benefit the avoidance of choosing explicit basis functions? If so, this should be highlighted and empirically demonstrated.
*   The experimental section requires significant revision to demonstrate the method's practical significance. I strongly recommend including comparisons with modern deep learning models for time series (e.g., LSTMs) on the prediction and recognition tasks. The authors should aim to identify scenarios where kernel DMD offers a clear advantage—perhaps in terms of data efficiency, interpretability, or performance on systems with specific oscillatory structures.
*   The applications could be re-framed to better highlight the strengths of the Koopman analysis. For instance, instead of just embedding motion capture data, can the learned Koopman modes be used to identify the fundamental frequencies of walking vs. running gaits? This would be a more direct and compelling use of the spectral information.

In summary, this paper presents a clear and technically sound kernelization of the DMD algorithm. It serves as a nice bridge between the dynamical systems and machine learning communities. However, its novelty is limited, and its practical significance is not convincingly established due to a lack of strong experimental baselines and a focus on generic applications that do not play to the method's unique strengths.
