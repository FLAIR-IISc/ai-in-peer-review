Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Koopman spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). It proposes a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) procedure: (i) form the Krylov matrix in feature space from a finite trajectory, (ii) compute orthogonal bases via Gram–Schmidt (implemented through Gram matrices), and (iii) compute the eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator to obtain empirical Ritz values and modes. Because all computations are expressed through kernels, the approach aims to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using rich, nonlinear basis functions rather than linear monomials (as in classical DMD).

To address numerical conditioning issues analogous to those in DMD, the paper derives a robust variant by projecting onto kernel-PCA (POD) directions and then performing eigendecomposition on a reduced operator. Theoretical statements (Theorems 1–2) relate the resulting eigenvalues/eigenvectors to Koopman spectra under assumptions on the span of the data and kernel principal components. Applications are sketched for short-term prediction via a preimage step, dynamics recognition via subspace distances (kernel principal angles), and change-point detection via a CUSUM-like statistic in an RKHS exponential-family model.

The work is closely related to DMD and extended DMD (EDMD), and more broadly to kernel methods that estimate linear operators in RKHS (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel CCA, and kernel transfer-operator literature). Compared to EDMD, the paper emphasizes the Krylov-subspace/Arnoldi viewpoint and a kernel-PCA-based robustification.

Strengths
- Clear, pedagogical exposition that will help readers unfamiliar with DMD/Koopman ideas, especially in ML.
- Conceptually neat kernelization of DMD with a principled robustness step (kernel-PCA truncation).
- Algorithmic pipeline operates entirely via Gram matrices; theoretical statements correctly mirror well-known finite-dimensional DMD facts.
- Code availability and illustrative examples (toy system, Hénon map, locomotion embeddings, change detection).

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to EDMD/”kernelized DMD” and RKHS operator estimation; theorems follow directly from standard Arnoldi/DMD and kernel-PCA machinery.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and mostly qualitative. There is no systematic comparison to standard DMD, EDMD (with comparable dictionaries), PCA/kPCA, or modern ML baselines for prediction/change detection/dynamics recognition.
- Deterministic, noise-free demonstrations limit relevance to ML scenarios with stochasticity and measurement noise. No robustness tests (SNR sweeps) are provided.
- The preimage-based prediction is ad hoc and lacks quantitative evaluation (e.g., NRMSE vs. DMD/EDMD/GPDM). Practical utility is unclear.
- No complexity/scalability discussion (kernel Gram matrices scale O(τ2) in memory and O(τ3) in time). No guidance on kernel/truncation selection.
- The change-point detection derivation drops terms without empirical justification; no detection-accuracy/false-alarm trade-off reporting.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivations are correct as extensions of DMD/Arnoldi to RKHS; the robustification via kernel PCA is standard and reasonable. However, there is no error analysis (e.g., consistency/convergence of Ritz values in RKHS, sample complexity, or sensitivity to kernel width and truncation).
- Empirical support: Illustrations are suggestive but insufficient to support claims of practical effectiveness. Lacks quantitative metrics and head-to-head baselines.
- Evaluation of limitations: Numerical ill-conditioning is acknowledged; other limitations (sensitivity to hyperparameters, preimage pitfalls, computational costs) are not adequately analyzed.

Clarity
- Well-organized and readable; definitions and algorithms are clear. Minor suggestions: explicitly state that all computations (except preimage) are kernelized and do not require explicit feature maps; move implementation choices (centering, truncation) into a consolidated algorithm box with complexity notes; clarify the relation to EDMD and whether your method can be seen as EDMD with an implicit infinite dictionary.

Originality
- The kernelization and robustification are natural extensions of DMD and EDMD; the contribution is primarily algorithmic packaging and exposition. The connection to kernel principal angles for recognition is known; the change-detection idea is interesting but preliminary.

Significance
- Potentially useful for domains where Koopman analysis is already used (e.g., robotics, control, fluid-structure analysis), but current experiments do not demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. Without quantitative gains or novel capabilities, impact for the ML community is limited.

Requested clarifications and questions for the authors
- Do you ever require explicit feature-space representations? My reading is that the decomposition itself is fully kernelized (no explicit ϕ), but the preimage step needs additional assumptions/heururistics. Please clarify.
- How does your method differ in practice from EDMD with kernel dictionaries? Can you position your approach with respect to Williams et al. (2015) and related “kernel DMD” variants?
- Complexity: Provide time/memory costs for each step and discuss scalability (e.g., Nyström, random features, streaming/online variants).
- Hyperparameters: How are kernel width and truncation rank chosen? Provide a principled selection criterion (e.g., validation on one-step prediction error, spectral residuals r).
- Noise/stochasticity: How robust are eigenvalues/modes under measurement noise or process noise? Please provide experiments with noise and compare to DMD/EDMD.
- Change-point detection: Justify neglecting the second term in the likelihood ratio; include quantitative detection metrics (ROC, average run length) vs. kernel change detection baselines.

Suggestions to strengthen the paper
- Add quantitative comparisons on the toy and Hénon examples against DMD, EDMD (with polynomial and RBF dictionaries), and kPCA-based baselines. Report eigenvalue errors, mode reconstruction errors, and prediction NRMSE.
- Include a stochastic/noisy setting and robustness plots vs. SNR.
- Demonstrate a concrete ML use case (e.g., short-horizon prediction or anomaly/change detection in robotics or human motion) with accuracy/latency comparisons to strong baselines (GPDM, LDS/SSMs, ESNs, spectral methods).
- Provide ablations: effect of kernel choice/width and truncation; sensitivity of preimage mapping; runtime and memory scaling.
- Discuss conditions for when kernel DMD is expected to outperform linear DMD (e.g., systems not well-approximated by local linearization).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and educational exposition of Koopman analysis in RKHS with an implementable algorithm.
- Unifies DMD, EDMD, and kernel PCA perspectives; potentially useful to practitioners in dynamics.

Cons against acceptance
- Incremental novelty over EDMD/kernel DMD; theoretical results unsurprising.
- Empirical evaluation lacks rigorous, quantitative comparisons and real ML relevance with noise.
- Practical issues (preimage, scalability, hyperparameters) not fully addressed.

Recommendation
- Weak reject. The paper is well-written and conceptually sound, but needs stronger empirical evidence, clearer positioning relative to EDMD/kernel DMD, and demonstrations in noisy, practical ML settings to meet NIPS significance expectations.
