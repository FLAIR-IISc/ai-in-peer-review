## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper presents a kernel-based extension to Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman operator spectral analysis. The authors leverage Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to enhance the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions, moving beyond the linear monomials used in traditional DMD. They propose a modal decomposition algorithm based on Krylov subspace calculation and provide a robustification method utilizing POD. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, presenting the theoretical concepts in a readily understandable manner. The technical aspects appear sound, and the numerical experiments demonstrate the functionality of the proposed approach, though comparison to state-of-the-art methods is limited.

The contribution builds on the growing interest in Koopman operator theory and DMD for nonlinear system analysis, extending the framework with the power of kernel methods. This allows for potentially more accurate approximation of the Koopman operator’s spectrum, crucial for understanding system dynamics and enabling data-driven modeling. The inclusion of a robustifying method using POD is a practical addition, addressing potential numerical instability issues common in DMD. The exploration of applications like state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition hints at the potential of this framework for broader use.

However, several points require attention. Section 3.1, specifically Equation (7), and the proof of Theorem 1 are unclear. Equation (7) seems remarkably similar to Equation (2), and the authors don’t sufficiently clarify the purpose and distinction between the two – what specific benefit does operating on the feature map ϕ(x) provide? Moreover, the mathematical justification in the proof of Theorem 1, particularly lines 144-157 ("applying the operator KH to both sides"), feels imprecise, as both sides of the equation appear to be scalars. 

A key concern is the seemingly strong, and not fully articulated, assumptions underlying Theorem 1. Additional assumptions appear within the proof itself, and their validity is not thoroughly discussed. Furthermore, there’s an inconsistency regarding the mathematical spaces: KH is an operator H→H, while φ is a map M→H, raising a question about whether φ necessarily belongs to H and thus can be directly operated on by KH.

Finally, minor typos were noted (line 55: “the the”, line 153: \kappaj / \lambdaj should be \kappai / \lambda_i).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-presented extension of DMD.
*   Clear explanation of theoretical concepts.
*   Demonstrated functionality through numerical experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Unclear mathematical justification in Section 3.1 and Theorem 1 proof.
*   Unspecified and potentially strong assumptions in Theorem 1.
*   Limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend a borderline acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding the clarity and rigor of the mathematical derivations and providing a more comprehensive discussion of the underlying assumptions. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and increase its impact.
