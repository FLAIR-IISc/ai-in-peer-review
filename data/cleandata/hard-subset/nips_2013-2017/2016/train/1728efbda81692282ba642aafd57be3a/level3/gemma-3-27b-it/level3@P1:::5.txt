This paper introduces a Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) method extended to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), termed KDMD. The core idea is to leverage the expressiveness of kernel functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, potentially capturing nonlinear dynamics more effectively than traditional DMD which relies on linear monomials of observables. The authors present an algorithm based on calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition on the projected Koopman operator. Applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition are explored with synthetic and real-world data.

The work builds upon the established field of spectral analysis of dynamical systems, referencing key contributions like the Koopman operator formalism (Koopman, 1963) and existing DMD variants (Schmid, 2010; Rowley et al., 2009). It also acknowledges related techniques like Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) (Sirovich, 1987) and subspace identification (Van Overschee & De Moor, 1996), positioning KDMD within a broader landscape of dimensionality reduction and system identification methods. The authors draw parallels to kernel methods in machine learning, referencing kernel PCA (Schölkopf et al., 1998) and related work on Gaussian processes (Wang et al., 2013).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Extension:** Extending DMD to RKHS offers a potentially powerful generalization, allowing the method to capture more complex dynamics beyond those effectively modeled by linear combinations of observables.
*   **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a mathematically rigorous formulation of KDMD, demonstrating its connection to the Koopman operator and DMD through theorems and proofs.
*   **Potential for Broad Applicability:** The proposed method has potential applications across various fields where data-driven modeling of nonlinear dynamical systems is crucial, as demonstrated by the explored applications.
*   **Robustification Technique:** The inclusion of POD-based projection for robustifying the algorithm addresses a practical challenge of ill-conditioning, increasing the method's reliability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is overly mathematical and verbose, making it difficult to follow the core ideas and understand the practical implications. Explanations often lack intuitive grounding and rely heavily on abstract notation.
*   **Organization and Redundancy:** Section 4, detailing the POD-based robustification, feels largely redundant and could be more concisely integrated into the main algorithm description. Its inclusion adds to the paper’s length without providing significant new insights.
*   **Insufficient Discussion of Related Work:** While related work is acknowledged, Section 5 lacks depth. It would benefit from concrete examples illustrating how KDMD differs from, and potentially outperforms, existing methods.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** Sections 6 and 7, detailing the empirical results, feel condensed and lack thorough analysis. More extensive experiments with diverse datasets and comparison to benchmark methods are needed to demonstrate the method's practical effectiveness. The examples presented are not fully compelling.
*   **Lack of Pedagogical Approach:** The paper reads more as a mathematical treatise than a contribution geared towards the NeurIPS audience. A more pedagogical rewrite, emphasizing intuition and practical considerations, would significantly enhance its impact and citability.
*   **Toy Problem Linearity:** The initial "nonlinear" toy example appears to be linear, potentially misleading the reader about the method’s capabilities.

**Overall Assessment:**

KDMD presents a mathematically sound and potentially valuable extension to DMD. However, its current presentation significantly hinders its accessibility and impact. The paper is technically strong but lacks the clarity and pedagogical approach expected of a NeurIPS contribution. The limited empirical validation and organizational issues further detract from its overall quality.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea of extending DMD to RKHS is novel and addresses a limitation of existing methods.
*   The theoretical foundation is solid and demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying principles.
*   The potential applications are broad and relevant to the NeurIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is difficult to read and understand due to its excessive mathematical formality and verbosity.
*   The organization is suboptimal, with redundant sections and limited empirical validation.
*   The current presentation is not suitable for a broad NeurIPS audience.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should prioritize clarity, conciseness, and pedagogical presentation. A more thorough empirical evaluation, expanded discussion of related work, and streamlined organization are also essential for a successful resubmission. Consider referencing Brunton et al. (2015) as a strong example of a well-written DMD paper. Rewriting the paper to be more accessible would dramatically increase its impact.
