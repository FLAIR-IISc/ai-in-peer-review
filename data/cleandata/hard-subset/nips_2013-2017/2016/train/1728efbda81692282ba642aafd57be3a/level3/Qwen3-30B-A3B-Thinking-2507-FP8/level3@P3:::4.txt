

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes a novel extension of Koopman spectral analysis using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), which significantly advances the field of dynamical systems analysis. Traditional Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) methods approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials of observables (a one-term Taylor expansion), which limits their applicability to systems where local linearization is valid. The authors extend this framework by incorporating kernel functions, allowing for richer, nonlinear basis functions that can capture more complex dynamics. The core contribution is a modal decomposition algorithm that operates in RKHS, essentially reducing to calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator's projection onto the subspace spanned by these bases. The paper also provides a robust variant using POD bases to handle ill-conditioned problems, similar to modifications made to standard DMD.

The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, with clear mathematical formulations, thorough theoretical development, and convincing empirical validation. The authors demonstrate their method on both synthetic systems (a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map) and real-world applications including state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using locomotion data. The results show clear improvements over traditional approaches, particularly in capturing complex nonlinear dynamics.

**Strengths:**

1. **Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The authors provide a rigorous extension of DMD to RKHS, proving key theorems (Theorems 1 and 2) that establish the connection between the proposed method and Koopman spectral analysis. The empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real-world data demonstrates the method's effectiveness. The robust variant using POD bases addresses practical implementation challenges, making the method more applicable in real scenarios.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are presented logically, with sufficient detail to allow for reproduction of the results. The connection between the proposed method and traditional DMD is clearly articulated, highlighting the key innovation (using nonlinear kernel functions instead of linear monomials). The applications section effectively demonstrates the utility of the extracted features.

3. **Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution by extending Koopman spectral analysis to RKHS, which represents a substantial conceptual advance over existing DMD approaches. While Williams et al. (2015) proposed extended DMD using pre-determined basis functions, this paper's use of kernel methods provides a more flexible and powerful framework for capturing nonlinear dynamics. The connection to kernel methods and RKHS is particularly novel in the context of Koopman spectral analysis.

4. **Significance:** The proposed method enables more accurate spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems, which has important implications for applications in physics, engineering, and machine learning. The ability to extract meaningful features from complex dynamics and use them for prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition demonstrates the practical significance of the work. The results on real-world locomotion data suggest potential applications in human motion analysis and robotics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper would benefit from a direct comparison to non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis (e.g., standard DMD or extended DMD) in the simulation experiments. The authors show that their method can accurately estimate eigenvalues for the toy system and Hénon map, but do not explicitly compare the accuracy of their method against alternative approaches. A quantitative comparison (e.g., error metrics) would strengthen the claims about the method's superiority.

2. The paper does not sufficiently explore the impact of kernel choice on the results. The authors use specific kernels (polynomial for the toy system, Gaussian for Hénon map) without discussing why these choices were made or how different kernel parameters affect the results. A systematic exploration of kernel choice (e.g., varying kernel parameters, comparing different kernel types) would provide valuable insights for practitioners and strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of Koopman spectral analysis and dynamical systems. The extension to RKHS is conceptually novel and technically sound, with clear advantages over existing methods. The manuscript is well-written and the empirical validation is thorough. The main weakness is the lack of direct comparison to non-RKHS approaches and insufficient exploration of kernel choice, which the authors should address in a revised version.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the two weaknesses mentioned above.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Conceptual novelty in extending Koopman spectral analysis to RKHS
- Strong theoretical foundation with well-proven theorems
- Clear practical applications with convincing empirical results
- Exceptionally well-written and organized manuscript

**Arguments against acceptance (to be addressed):**
- Need for direct comparison to non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis
- Need for exploration of kernel choice impact on results

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and would be of interest to the NIPS community, particularly those working on dynamical systems, machine learning, and data-driven modeling.
