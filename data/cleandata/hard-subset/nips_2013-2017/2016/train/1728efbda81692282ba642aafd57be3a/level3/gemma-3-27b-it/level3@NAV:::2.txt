## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes a kernelized version of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems via the Koopman operator. The core idea is to perform eigen-decomposition in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), allowing for the capture of more complex dynamics than traditional DMD which relies on linear monomials. The authors demonstrate the method on synthetic and real-world data, applying it to state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper builds upon a well-established line of work in spectral analysis of dynamical systems, including Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD), DMD, and related techniques (e.g., balancing modes, subspace identification). It correctly positions itself within this literature, acknowledging the existing limitations of linear approaches and motivating the use of kernel methods. The extension to RKHS is a natural progression, leveraging the benefits of kernel methods for nonlinear function approximation. The mathematical derivation appears sound, and the connection to existing DMD techniques is clearly explained.

However, the novelty of the work feels incremental. While kernelizing DMD is a logical step, it doesn’t fundamentally alter the underlying approach. DMD itself is already a well-known technique within the dynamical systems community, and the authors acknowledge this. The application demonstrations – state prediction, change detection, and system recognition – are standard benchmarks, and the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over competing methods such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or other change-detection techniques. The empirical results, while promising, aren’t presented with sufficient comparative analysis to substantiate claims of superiority. Furthermore, the application scenarios chosen don't showcase a particularly compelling reason *why* kernelized DMD would be the method of choice. 

**Strengths:**

*   Clear and mathematically sound presentation of the method.
*   Logical extension of existing DMD techniques using kernel methods.
*   Well-motivated within the context of Koopman operator theory and spectral analysis of dynamical systems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty - primarily a kernelization of an existing algorithm.
*   Lack of compelling demonstrations showing a clear advantage over existing methods (RNNs, alternative change-detection methods).
*   Applications are somewhat generic and don't highlight the unique benefits of the approach.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a technically sound extension of DMD with potential benefits in capturing nonlinear dynamics.
*   The method is clearly explained and well-motivated.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The incremental novelty may not warrant inclusion in a competitive conference like NIPS.
*   The lack of strong empirical evidence demonstrating superiority over existing methods weakens the contribution.



**Overall:** This paper presents a solid technical contribution, but its incremental novelty and lack of strong empirical justification make it a borderline submission. I would lean towards **reject** with encouragement to the authors to more thoroughly benchmark their method against state-of-the-art alternatives and to explore applications where kernelized DMD offers a unique advantage.
