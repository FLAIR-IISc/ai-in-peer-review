This paper presents a method for Koopman spectral analysis in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), proposing a data-driven algorithm that can be viewed as a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD). The work connects the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems, traditionally explored in fields like fluid dynamics, with the powerful framework of kernel methods from machine learning. This approach relates to prior work on kernelized linear algebra, such as Kernel PCA and ICA, by applying similar principles to a temporal decomposition operator.

The main strengths of the paper are its clarity and the elegance of its formulation. The authors provide a well-written, pedagogical introduction to Koopman operators and DMD, making these concepts accessible to a machine learning audience. The method itself is an interesting and principled fusion of kernel methods with DMD, offering a novel way to approximate the Koopman operator's spectrum using nonlinear basis functions.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that temper its contribution. The primary concern is its practical significance for the machine learning community. The experiments on two simple dynamical systems and locomotion data do not provide a clear picture of the method's advantages over alternatives like standard DMD, PCA, or Kernel PCA. To demonstrate utility, the authors should identify concrete ML tasks, such as in robotics or complex time-series forecasting, and benchmark their method against relevant state-of-the-art techniques.

On the technical side, the theoretical results are technically sound but not surprising, following expectedly from existing literature on linear operators in RKHSs. The evaluation would be substantially improved by including standard DMD as a baseline on the toy example and the Hénon map to precisely quantify the benefits of the kernelization. Furthermore, the paper should clarify whether the modal decomposition requires explicit feature-space representations, as this would be a significant limitation. Finally, the focus on purely deterministic systems limits the method's relevance for many ML applications where stochastic components are prevalent.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting theoretical connection, its impact is constrained by an evaluation that fails to convincingly demonstrate its value for practical machine learning problems.
