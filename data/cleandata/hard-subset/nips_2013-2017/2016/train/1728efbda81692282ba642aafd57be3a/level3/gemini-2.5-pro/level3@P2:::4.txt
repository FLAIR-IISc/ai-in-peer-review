### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending the Koopman operator theory and Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The central idea is that while standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear combination of observables, this work leverages the power of kernel methods to find approximations in a much richer, nonlinear function space. The authors present an algorithm, termed kernel DMD (kDMD), which performs a modal decomposition by finding an orthogonal basis for the Krylov subspace in the RKHS and then solving an eigenvalue problem for the projection of the Koopman operator. Recognizing potential numerical instability, a common issue in DMD methods, the authors also propose a robust version of their algorithm by projecting the data onto kernel principal component analysis (kernel PCA) directions. The paper effectively frames the resulting Koopman modes as features extracted from the dynamics and demonstrates their utility in several machine learning applications, including short-term prediction, sequential change-point detection, and the embedding and recognition of dynamical patterns. The method is evaluated on both synthetic data from chaotic maps and real-world motion capture data.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

This is a well-executed and clearly presented paper that makes a strong contribution by bridging the fields of Koopman operator theory and kernel methods.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development in Section 3, culminating in Theorem 1, provides a solid and elegant foundation for extending DMD into an RKHS. The follow-up in Section 4, which introduces a robustified algorithm based on kernel PCA (POD bases), demonstrates a careful consideration of the practical and numerical challenges involved, drawing a thoughtful parallel to similar robustification techniques in standard DMD. The work is presented as a complete contribution, with rigorous theory, a practical algorithm, and compelling applications.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors provide a concise but sufficient background on the Koopman operator and DMD, making the paper accessible to readers not deeply familiar with the topic. The progression from the theoretical principle to the robust algorithm and finally to applications is logical and easy to follow. The mathematical notation is clear and consistent throughout. This high level of clarity is a significant strength.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While DMD is an active area of research, and kernel methods are a staple of machine learning, their combination for Koopman analysis is a novel and powerful idea. This work differs significantly from previous contributions. For instance, while Extended DMD [35] also uses a dictionary of nonlinear observables, the proposed kDMD approach is arguably more flexible, as it implicitly works with the infinite-dimensional feature space defined by the kernel, rather than a pre-specified, finite set of basis functions. The paper correctly positions itself with respect to related literature in both physics and machine learning.

**Significance:** The results presented are important and likely to be impactful. By making DMD more powerful and applicable to a wider range of nonlinear systems, this work could be of great interest to both researchers and practitioners. The framing of Koopman modes as features for downstream machine learning tasks is particularly significant, as it opens up new avenues for analyzing and classifying complex time-series data. The applications in change-point detection and motion recognition demonstrate the practical utility of this approach. The method has the potential to advance the state of the art in data-driven analysis of dynamical systems.

Despite these significant strengths, the experimental validation could be strengthened in two ways:

1.  **Lack of Baseline Comparison:** The experiments effectively demonstrate that the proposed method works. However, they do not include a comparison against the most natural baseline: a standard (non-kernel) DMD algorithm. To fully appreciate the contribution, it would be invaluable to see a direct comparison on the synthetic examples (toy system, Hénon map) to understand in which regimes and for which types of dynamics the kernel-based approach offers a distinct advantage over its linear counterpart.

2.  **Kernel Selection:** The choice of kernel and its hyperparameters is fundamental to the performance of any kernel method. The paper uses a polynomial kernel for one task and a Gaussian kernel for another but does not discuss the sensitivity of the results to these choices. A brief discussion or a small-scale experiment illustrating how kernel selection (e.g., the width of the Gaussian kernel) affects the estimated eigenvalues or prediction accuracy would significantly improve the practical value of the paper for readers wishing to apply this method.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant theoretical extension of DMD to RKHS, bridging two important fields.
*   The paper is technically sound, with clear theorems and a well-motivated, robust algorithm.
*   The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   The proposed applications are compelling and demonstrate the potential of the method as a powerful feature extraction tool for dynamical systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The empirical evaluation lacks a direct comparison to standard DMD, making it difficult to precisely quantify the performance gains offered by the kernelization.
*   The paper does not address the practical issue of kernel selection and its impact on the results.

### **Conclusion**

Overall, this is an excellent paper. The theoretical contribution is novel and significant, and the presentation is of very high quality. The weaknesses identified are primarily in the experimental section and are addressable. They do not detract from the core value of the contribution. I believe this work will be of high interest to the NIPS community and strongly recommend acceptance. I encourage the authors to consider adding the suggested baseline comparisons and discussion on kernel choice to the final version of the paper to further enhance its impact.
