### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a method for performing Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The central idea is to extend Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a technique for extracting dynamically relevant modes, by leveraging the kernel trick. This allows the algorithm to implicitly work with a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions, overcoming the limitations of standard DMD which typically uses linear observables. The authors formulate an algorithm based on an Arnoldi-like procedure in the feature space and propose a more robust variant that projects the data onto kernel principal component analysis (PCA) directions. The method is framed as a feature extraction technique, and its utility is demonstrated through applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and the recognition of dynamic patterns on both synthetic and real-world data.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments for Acceptance (Strengths):**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The paper presents an elegant and theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHSs. Connecting the powerful framework of Koopman operator theory with kernel methods is a significant and timely contribution, opening up new avenues for data-driven analysis of nonlinear dynamics.
2.  **Computational Efficiency:** By leveraging the kernel trick, the proposed method can efficiently perform computations in a high-dimensional feature space by operating only on Gram matrices, making it practical for real-world application.
3.  **Demonstrated Versatility:** The authors successfully frame the Koopman eigenfunctions as features and demonstrate their utility across a diverse set of machine learning tasks (prediction, change-point detection, recognition), showing the broad potential of the approach beyond simple system analysis.

**Arguments for Rejection (Weaknesses):**

1.  **Unclear Contribution and Missing Literature:** The paper's novelty is significantly obscured by an inadequate discussion of related work. The distinction from Williams et al. (2015), "Extended DMD," is not clearly articulated. Furthermore, a substantial body of prior work on kernel-based methods for learning dynamical systems is overlooked.
2.  **Limited Scope and Assumptions:** The analysis and experiments are confined to deterministic systems. The brief extension to stochastic settings in the change-point section is hand-wavy and lacks theoretical or empirical rigor. Key practical assumptions, such as the use of data pairs instead of sequences, are not sufficiently discussed.
3.  **Unconvincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are illustrative but lack the ambition and rigor expected for a top-tier publication. The comparisons to baseline methods are either absent or insufficient, making it difficult to assess whether the proposed method offers a tangible improvement over the state of the art in the chosen application domains.

### Detailed Comments

**1. Originality and Relation to Prior Work:**

*   **Comparison with Extended DMD:** The paper fails to clearly differentiate its contribution from Williams et al. (2015), "A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator: Extending dynamic mode decomposition." Extended DMD also approximates the Koopman operator using a dictionary of observables (basis functions). The proposed kernel method can be viewed as a specific instance of this framework where the dictionary is implicitly defined by the kernel function. The authors state their method is "similar" but must provide an explicit and detailed comparison. Is it a special case? A generalization? What are the theoretical and practical advantages? This is particularly crucial for the robust version in Section 4, which closely resembles the procedure in Williams et al.
*   **Missing References on Kernel-based Dynamical Systems:** The paper overlooks a significant body of relevant work that has used kernel methods to learn operators for dynamical systems. This includes, but is not limited to:
    *   Learning dynamical systems with kernel embeddings of conditional distributions (e.g., works by L. Ralaivola & F. d'Alche Buc).
    *   Kernel-based methods for system identification and time-series analysis (e.g., works by T. Tuia et al.; J.L. Rojo-Álvarez et al.; E. Mouattamid & R. Schaback).
    *   Learning operators in RKHSs (e.g., M. Franz & B. Schölkopf).
    A thorough discussion of this related literature is necessary to properly situate the paper's contribution.

**2. Scope and Theoretical Depth:**

*   **Stochastic Settings:** The real world is noisy. The paper's focus on deterministic systems is a major limitation. The brief mention of extending the Koopman operator to stochastic systems in Section 7 is insufficient. The authors should include a formal discussion on how noise affects the estimation of Koopman spectra and provide experiments on systems with observational or process noise to characterize the method's performance and limitations.
*   **Data Structure and Causal Assumptions:** The remark that the method only requires pairs of consecutive observables {x_t, x_{t+1}} is an important practical point that needs deeper exploration. For complex, high-dimensional systems, the relationship between consecutive states can be weak or noisy. How does the method perform under these conditions? Moreover, by framing the problem this way, the method implicitly assumes a first-order Markov structure. This assumption should be stated and its implications discussed, especially in contexts where longer-term dependencies might be crucial.

**3. Experimental Evaluation:**

The experiments demonstrate the method's functionality but are not sufficient to establish its superiority.

*   **Prediction:** This is a standard task, but no baseline methods (e.g., Gaussian Process Dynamical Models) are compared against.
*   **Dynamics Recognition:** The embedding via MDS is visually appealing, but the comparison is qualitative. For a quantitative evaluation, the authors should compare against established time-series classification/clustering baselines, such as kernel k-means with appropriate time-series kernels (e.g., dynamic time warping) or methods based on subspace similarity.
*   **Change-Point Detection:** The comparison is made to a single method from 2006. The field of change-point detection has advanced significantly. To be convincing, the proposed algorithm should be benchmarked against state-of-the-art online kernel-based change-point detection methods. The derivation of the change score is also heuristic ("the variation of the second term is much smaller"), which weakens the claim.

**Minor Issues:**

*   There appear to be typos. In the algorithm summary in Sec 3, step (1) refers to M+ = [phi(x1),...,phi(x_tau)], but the Krylov subspace is built up to tau-1. Please check indexing consistency.
*   In the last equation on page 7, the kernel function appears as `k(xi.xj)`. This dot should likely be a comma: `k(xi, xj)`.

In conclusion, this paper presents a promising and elegant idea. However, its contribution is not clearly delineated from prior work, its scope is too narrowly focused on deterministic systems, and its empirical validation is weak. Major revisions are needed to address these points before it can be considered for publication.
