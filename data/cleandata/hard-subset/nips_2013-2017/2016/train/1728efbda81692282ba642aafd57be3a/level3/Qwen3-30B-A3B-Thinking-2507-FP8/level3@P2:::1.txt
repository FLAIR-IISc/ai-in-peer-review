

# Review for "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors present an algorithm that computes a modal decomposition of nonlinear dynamical systems by constructing orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projection. The method is theoretically grounded, with theorems establishing the connection between the kernel-based approach and Koopman spectral analysis. The paper demonstrates applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition, supported by synthetic and real-world examples.

The work builds upon the Koopman operator framework and DMD literature, particularly extending Williams et al. (2015) "A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator: Extending dynamic mode decomposition," which also uses pre-determined basis functions for DMD. However, the paper does not sufficiently clarify how the proposed kernel-based approach differs from Williams et al.'s extended DMD in terms of methodology, theoretical guarantees, or practical advantages. The authors should explicitly address this relationship in Section 4 and provide a more detailed comparison with related work.

**Quality**: The theoretical development is sound and well-justified. The algorithm is mathematically rigorous, with theorems supporting the proposed method. However, the paper lacks sufficient experimental validation against relevant baselines. For instance, the prediction experiments do not compare against standard time-series forecasting methods or kernel-based approaches. The change-point detection experiments should be benchmarked against state-of-the-art kernel-based change detection methods (e.g., [7]). Additionally, the paper does not discuss the performance of the method in stochastic settings, which would be important for real-world applications where noise is common.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written. The mathematical derivations are thorough, though the connection between the kernel-based approach and practical implementation could be strengthened. The explanation of how the algorithm handles the Krylov matrix in RKHS is clear, but the practical implications of using pairs of consecutive observables (rather than full sequences) need more discussion, especially regarding causal assumptions and applicability to multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relations.

**Originality**: The core idea of applying RKHS to Koopman spectral analysis has some novelty, but the paper fails to sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work. The authors do not adequately reference or compare to relevant kernel-based dynamical systems literature (e.g., Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Álvarez et al., Mouattamid & Schaback, Franz & Schölkopf), which would strengthen the novelty claim. The paper's main contribution appears to be the technical adaptation of DMD to RKHS, rather than a fundamentally new approach.

**Significance**: While the paper demonstrates potential applications in dynamics recognition and change-point detection, the experimental results lack convincing evidence of significant advantages over existing methods. The prediction experiments, for instance, use a standard pre-image approach that has been previously applied in kernel methods, without demonstrating clear performance gains. The applications section would be more compelling with stronger comparisons to state-of-the-art methods in time-series analysis, such as kernel k-means for clustering or subspace kernels for dynamics recognition.

**Strengths**:
- Solid theoretical foundation for extending DMD to RKHS
- Clear connection between kernel methods and Koopman spectral analysis
- Demonstrated applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition
- Well-structured mathematical presentation

**Weaknesses**:
- Insufficient differentiation from Williams et al. (2015) and other extended DMD approaches
- Missing comparison to state-of-the-art methods in relevant application domains
- Lack of discussion on stochastic settings and noise robustness
- Limited experimental validation with appropriate baselines
- Inadequate reference to relevant kernel-based dynamical systems literature

**Recommendation**: The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods, but the novelty is not sufficiently established, and the experimental validation is limited. The authors should strengthen the paper by:
1. Providing a clear, detailed comparison with Williams et al. (2015) and other extended DMD approaches
2. Adding experiments that benchmark against state-of-the-art methods in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition
3. Discussing the limitations and performance in stochastic settings
4. Including a more comprehensive literature review of kernel-based dynamical systems methods

Without these improvements, the paper's contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. While the theoretical development is strong, the practical significance is not sufficiently demonstrated. I recommend rejection in its current form, but with the potential for acceptance after addressing these concerns.
