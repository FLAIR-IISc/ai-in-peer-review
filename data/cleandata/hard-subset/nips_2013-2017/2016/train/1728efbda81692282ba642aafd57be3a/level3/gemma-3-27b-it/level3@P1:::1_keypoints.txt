- Proposes a kernelized DMD in RKHS for Koopman spectral analysis to approximate nonlinear Koopman eigenfunctions.  
- Algorithm: compute orthogonal bases for the RKHS Krylov matrix and perform eigendecomposition.  
- Demonstrated on synthetic and real data for state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.  
- Builds on DMD/Koopman literature (Rowley et al., Schmid, Bagheri) and relates to Williams et al. (2015) but the connection is underdeveloped.  
- Needs clearer comparison to Williams et al. (2015) regarding basis treatment, algorithmic differences, computational cost, and theoretical foundations.  
- Strength: solid theoretical grounding and natural extension of DMD using RKHS to capture richer nonlinear dynamics.  
- Empirically feasible but demonstrations are limited to relatively simple systems.  
- Major omission: insufficient review of kernel-based dynamical systems literature (missing Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc 2005; Tuia et al. 2011; Rojo-Álvarez et al. 2015; Mouattamid & Schaback 2016; Franz & Schölkopf 2006).  
- Lacks treatment of stochastic/noisy systems; no analysis or experiments on robustness to noise or inherent stochasticity.  
- Practical concern: using only pairs of consecutive observables may impose causal assumptions that are not justified, especially for multivariate time series with weak pointwise relationships.  
- Experimental evaluation is weak: prediction results are standard and there are no benchmarks against established methods.  
- Should compare to time-series clustering and change-point methods (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernel methods, online kernel change-point detection).  
- Comparative analysis and rigorous benchmarks are required to validate claimed advantages.  
- Minor issues: inconsistent indexing of "tau-1" and incorrect kernel notation k(xi.xj) should be k(xi, xj).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel kernel application to Koopman analysis, strong mathematical derivation, and empirical feasibility.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak related-work coverage, no stochastic analysis, limited experiments and benchmarks, unclear causal assumptions, and minor typographical errors.  
- Overall recommendation: reject in current form but encourage revision to address related work, stochastic settings, stronger empirical comparisons, and notation/typos.
