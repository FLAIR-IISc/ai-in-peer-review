Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes KDMD, a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition framework that performs Koopman spectral analysis in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The core contribution is to recast DMD in feature space: (i) construct the Krylov matrix from feature maps of consecutive states; (ii) compute orthonormal bases (via Gram–Schmidt using kernel evaluations); (iii) form and eigendecompose the projected Koopman operator to obtain empirical Ritz values/vectors; and (iv) reconstruct Koopman modes/eigenfunctions in the RKHS. To address ill-conditioning, the paper further introduces a robust variant using kernel PCA (POD) truncation, leading to an eigendecomposition of a low-rank, Gram-matrix–based projected operator. The authors also describe applications to short-term prediction via pre-image estimation, dynamics embedding/recognition using subspace angles, and sequential change-point detection. Empirical demonstrations include a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, motion capture locomotion data, and a change-point example.

This work is closely related to EDMD and kernel EDMD (Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, 2015/2016), exact DMD and its numerically robust variants (Tu et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2012), sparsity-promoting DMD (Jovanović et al., 2014), and optimized DMD. Brunton et al. (2015) also provide relevant developments connecting Koopman analysis to data-driven models. The presented robustification with kernel PCA and Gram-matrix manipulations appears particularly close in spirit to kernel EDMD.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity of the basic idea: lifting the Koopman analysis to RKHS provides a principled way to use rich nonlinear features with DMD-like Krylov methods.
- Technically sound derivation: the feature-space Arnoldi/QR perspective and projected-evolution operator are standard and credible; the kernel trick is applied correctly.
- Practical robustness via POD/kernel PCA truncation is well motivated, addressing known DMD conditioning issues.
- Potentially useful applications: feature-based prediction, dynamics recognition via subspace geometry, and change-point detection are interesting directions.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation: the manuscript is overly verbose and difficult to follow, with many statements that could be much more concise. The exposition feels cryptic for a NeurIPS audience and would benefit from a pedagogical rewrite, figures, and pseudocode.
- Originality relative to EDMD/kernel EDMD is not crisply articulated. Much of the kernelized projection and Gram-based operator construction appears to overlap with Williams et al. (2015/2016). Clear contrasts, advantages, or new guarantees are missing.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and lacks rigorous quantitative comparisons to baselines (exact/optimized DMD, EDMD, kernel EDMD, SP-DMD). Reported examples are small-scale and primarily qualitative.
- Guidance on kernel/feature selection and sensitivity is minimal, despite kernel choice being crucial. The first two test problems are polynomial, where polynomial kernels are natural; the paper does not show benefits in harder settings where kernel choice is nontrivial.
- Some sections are redundant or misbalanced (see detailed comments).

Quality
- Theory: The operator-projection constructions and theorems are plausible and consistent with known DMD/Arnoldi theory. However, proofs mostly repackage standard results; additional insights (e.g., approximation error bounds vs. EDMD; conditions for recovery; sample complexity; stability to noise) are not provided.
- Experiments: Demonstrations are illustrative but not comprehensive. There is little in the way of quantitative metrics, ablations (e.g., effect of truncation rank, kernel width), or comparisons.
- Evaluation honesty: The paper acknowledges conditioning issues and provides a robustification. Limitations around kernel choice and pre-image challenges could be discussed more candidly.

Clarity
- The paper is hard to read: too many dense, formal statements where shorter, clearer explanations would suffice. Several derivations can be streamlined, and notation could be standardized. Pseudocode, algorithm boxes, and schematic diagrams would help.
- Section balance: Section 4 feels redundant after the introduction and could be folded into the main method. Section 5 (Related Work) could be tightened or summarized in the conclusion. Section 6 (Empirical Example) is overly condensed; it should be expanded with clearer setups and quantitative results.

Originality
- Moderate. Kernelizing DMD/Koopman regression and projecting via Gram matrices is well known from EDMD and kernel EDMD. The paper’s contribution is a specific RKHS-Arnoldi/QR viewpoint and a kernel PCA robustification, which are incremental advances unless distinguished more clearly from Williams et al. (2015/2016) and related work (including Brunton et al., 2015).

Significance
- Potentially useful if clearly presented and empirically validated. As is, the significance is limited by overlap with existing methods and the lack of strong comparative results. A more pedagogical paper with thorough benchmarks would likely be impactful and citable.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Positioning vs. EDMD/kernel EDMD: Precisely explain how KDMD differs theoretically and algorithmically. If equivalent under certain choices (e.g., dictionary from kernel PCA), say so, and highlight any benefits (conditioning, complexity).
- Streamline Sections 3–4: Provide one unified algorithm with truncation options (rank selection), and move proofs to the supplement. Include clear pseudocode and complexity analysis.
- Section 5: Integrate key related work into the introduction/method, and keep Section 5 concise. Cite Brunton et al. (2015) explicitly as relevant to Koopman-based learning.
- Section 6–7: Expand experiments with quantitative comparisons to exact/optimized DMD, EDMD, kernel EDMD, and SP-DMD on standard benchmarks (e.g., cylinder wake, FitzHugh–Nagumo). Report accuracy of eigenvalues/modes, forecasting error, robustness to noise, and runtime/memory.
- Kernel choice: Provide guidance (e.g., bandwidth selection, polynomial degree), sensitivity analyses, and a discussion on when kernels help over linear DMD.
- Pre-image: The proposed MDS-based pre-image is heuristic; compare to standard pre-image methods and quantify forecasting performance.
- Minor: The “toy nonlinear system” looks partly linear; please clarify the role of the x_t^2 term and ensure consistency with the stated eigenfunctions. Fix typos and unify notation.

Arguments for acceptance
- Sound and principled extension of DMD to RKHS with a useful robustification.
- Timely topic with clear potential impact for nonlinear system analysis and ML applications.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited originality relative to EDMD/kernel EDMD; insufficient differentiation.
- Difficult-to-read presentation; several sections redundant or imbalanced.
- Insufficient empirical validation and lack of comparisons to strong baselines.
- Minimal practical guidance on kernel selection and robustness.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. With a substantial rewrite to clarify contributions beyond EDMD/kernel EDMD, stronger empirical comparisons, and better presentation, this could become a valuable and citable contribution.

Confidence: High.
