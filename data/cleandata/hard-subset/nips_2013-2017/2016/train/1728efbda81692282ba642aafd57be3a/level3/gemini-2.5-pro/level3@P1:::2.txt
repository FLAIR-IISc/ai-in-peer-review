This paper presents a method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) framework to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to leverage the Koopman operator, which provides a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics by acting on a space of observable functions. While standard DMD approximates the eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator using linear combinations of the observed states, this work proposes using the expressive power of kernel methods to find these eigenfunctions in an RKHS. The algorithm is derived from first principles, connecting it to the Arnoldi method and Krylov subspaces in the feature space. A robust version, analogous to DMD variants in the literature, is developed by projecting the data onto kernel principal components before performing the decomposition. The resulting Koopman modes and eigenvalues are then used as features for several machine learning tasks, including time-series prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection. This work builds upon the growing literature on DMD from the physics and fluid dynamics communities [25, 26, 33] and connects it with well-established techniques in machine learning like kernel PCA [28] and spectral methods for learning dynamical systems [15, 17].

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development in Section 3, which extends the principles of DMD to an RKHS, is clear and the proof of Theorem 1 correctly establishes the decomposition. The authors rightly identify the potential numerical instability of the direct Gram-Schmidt approach and propose a more robust algorithm in Section 4 based on a projection onto kernel principal components (POD bases). This practical consideration strengthens the work. The connection established in Theorem 2 between the eigenvectors of the projected operator and the Koopman eigenfunctions is well-argued, under the assumption that the eigenfunctions lie within the span of the principal components. The experiments on synthetic data (toy system, Hénon map) successfully demonstrate that the algorithm can recover the true eigenvalues of the system, validating the core method.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and logically organized. It begins with a helpful introduction to the Koopman operator and DMD, making the work accessible to a machine learning audience who may not be familiar with these concepts from the dynamical systems literature. The progression from the theoretical foundation of Koopman analysis, to its approximation via DMD, to the proposed kernelized extension is easy to follow. The algorithms are presented clearly, and the connection between the initial (less stable) and the final (robust) versions is well-motivated.

**Originality:**
The main contribution is the synthesis of two distinct fields: Koopman spectral analysis (specifically DMD) from dynamical systems and kernel methods from machine learning. While DMD itself is not new, its formulation and implementation within an RKHS is a novel contribution. The work is well-situated within the literature, and related methods like extended DMD [35] are acknowledged. The proposed method differs from extended DMD in that it does not require a pre-specified dictionary of basis functions, instead implicitly using the rich basis provided by the kernel function. The novelty for the NIPS community lies in framing the problem of learning from time-series data—which involves repeated function compositions (xt+1 = f(xt))—as the eigendecomposition of a linear operator that represents these compositions. This is an elegant and powerful perspective.

**Significance:**
The potential significance of this work is considerable, as it offers a principled, non-parametric method for extracting dynamically relevant features from nonlinear time series. However, the demonstrated significance in the current manuscript is limited by the experimental evaluation in the applications section. While the applications (prediction, recognition, change-point detection) are interesting proofs of concept, they lack rigorous comparison against standard and state-of-the-art machine learning baselines.
- For prediction on the Hénon map, how does the proposed method compare against standard time-series models like Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or LSTMs, which are designed for such tasks?
- For the recognition of motion capture data, the MDS embedding is visually appealing, but a quantitative comparison is needed. How does a classifier using these Koopman features compare to one using features from, for instance, a time-series autoencoder or a baseline classifier operating on raw trajectory data?
- The change-point detection experiment is compared to a kernel-based method, but it is not clear if the proposed method offers a distinct advantage in terms of accuracy, robustness, or computational cost.

Without these crucial comparisons, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed method provides a tangible advance over existing techniques for these specific tasks. The primary value of the Koopman decomposition is its interpretability (decomposing dynamics into modes with specific frequencies and growth rates), but this aspect is not the main focus of the application section, which instead treats the method as a black-box feature extractor.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper introduces an elegant and novel synthesis of Koopman operator theory from dynamical systems and kernel methods from machine learning.
2.  It provides the machine learning community with a new, non-parametric tool for analyzing nonlinear dynamics, which is a valuable contribution in itself.
3.  The theoretical development is sound, and the connection between the algorithm and the underlying theory is clearly established.
4.  The method is validated on synthetic examples where it successfully recovers the ground-truth system properties.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The main novelty is the "kernelization" of an existing algorithm (DMD), which, while valuable, may be seen as an incremental contribution.
2.  The experimental evaluation for the proposed applications is weak. The lack of comparison to standard machine learning baselines (e.g., RNNs) makes it impossible to judge the practical utility and performance of the method.
3.  The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate a unique advantage of this method over other feature extraction or modeling techniques for the chosen application domains. The results are illustrative rather than conclusive.

In summary, this is a well-written paper with a solid theoretical contribution that brings a powerful set of ideas to the machine learning community. However, its claims of utility for downstream tasks are not sufficiently supported by the experimental results. The work would be much stronger if it included rigorous quantitative comparisons against relevant machine learning baselines for the application tasks.
