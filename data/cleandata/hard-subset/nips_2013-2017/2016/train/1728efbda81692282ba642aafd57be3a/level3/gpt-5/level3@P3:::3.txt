Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies Koopman spectral analysis in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The key idea is to treat the feature map ϕ: M→H as an observable, form the RKHS-Krylov matrix Mτ = [ϕ(x0) … ϕ(xτ−1)], and perform a DMD-style modal decomposition by projecting a “Koopman operator in feature space” onto span{ϕ(x0),…,ϕ(xτ−1)}. The resulting eigenvalues (Ritz values) and eigenvectors (modes in H) provide a finite-dimensional approximation of Koopman spectra and modes. A robust variant projects the data onto kernel-PCA (POD) directions before eigendecomposition, addressing ill-conditioning. The paper then illustrates applications (short-term prediction via pre-images, dynamics recognition via kernel principal angles, and sequential change-point detection).

This line of work sits at the intersection of DMD/Koopman analysis and kernel methods. It extends DMD beyond linear monomials of observables by leveraging the kernel trick to implicitly use a rich dictionary, conceptually related to Extended DMD (EDMD) and to kernelized EDMD/kDMD approaches. The manuscript also connects to spectral learning of dynamical systems and subspace identification.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation and well-organized presentation. The core concepts—Koopman operators, Krylov subspaces, and kernelization—are introduced coherently.
- Technically solid overall: the RKHS-Krylov/projection viewpoint is sound, and the robust kernel-PCA projection mirrors best practices from exact/optimized DMD.
- The applications nicely showcase potential utility, despite being limited in scope.
- The approach provides a principled kernelized extension of DMD that many readers in ML will find useful.

Major concerns and questions:
1) Section 3.1, Eq. (7), and Theorem 1 proof clarity:
- Purpose and distinction of Eq. (7): As written, (KHϕ)(x)=ϕ◦f(x) appears to restate Eq. (2) but with an H-valued observable. Please explicitly state the space on which KH acts. In Eq. (2), K: G→G for scalar G = {g: M→C}, whereas in Eq. (7) it seems KH acts on H-valued functions F := {ψ: M→H} by composition, KH: F→F, with (KHψ)(x)=ψ(f(x)). Clarify that KH is not an operator H→H, but an operator on H-valued functions. This will also help distinguish vector-valued from scalar observables and reconcile Eq. (7) with Eq. (2).
- Domain/codomain mismatch: The text says “KH is an operator H→H” but ϕ: M→H, so ϕ∉H. Please correct the function spaces and notation (e.g., calligraphic F for H-valued functions), and specify the inner product used when you treat F as a Hilbert space (e.g., L2(μ;H)).
- Eigen-objects: Early in Sec. 3, φj are called “eigenfunctions of KH,” yet later φj are scalar Koopman eigenfunctions and φ̃j∈H are empirical modes. Please rigorously distinguish: scalar Koopman eigenfunctions φj: M→C vs. RKHS vectors/modes v∈H, and the coefficient κj∈H such that φj(x)=⟨ϕ(x),κj⟩.
- The phrase “applying the operator KH to both sides” (lines 144–157 in the current draft) is unclear because the displayed equality there is scalar-valued. If the intent is to apply KH to an identity in F or to vectors in H parametrized by x, that should be stated precisely.

2) Theorem 1 assumptions:
- The theorem requires more explicit and stronger assumptions than currently stated. Please state:
  - Full column rank of Mτ (or conditions ensuring the QR exists with invertible R).
  - That the companion form F exists/has distinct eigenvalues and a minimal polynomial of degree τ (for the Vandermonde diagonalization).
  - Whether noise is absent and whether the Krylov subspace is K-invariant along the trajectory.
- In the proof, Mτ−1KH Mτ implicitly assumes Mτ has a left inverse on its range; justify this or replace with a projection-based argument.
- Clarify the precise relationship between Pτ = Q*τ KH Qτ and F = R−1PτR, including domains.

3) Conceptual claim about “full recovery”:
- The statement that “the eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator are fully recovered if the Krylov subspace is sufficiently large, i.e., ϕ(xτ)∈span{ϕ(x0),…,ϕ(xτ−1)}” is too strong. Recovery requires that the invariant subspace spanned by the relevant Koopman eigenfunctions be contained in span{ϕ(x0),…,ϕ(xτ−1)} and that the data sufficiently excite that subspace. Please weaken and qualify this claim.

4) Experimental evaluation:
- The numerical examples are helpful but limited. For NIPS, please add comparisons to:
  - Exact/optimized DMD and EDMD (with carefully chosen dictionaries).
  - A kernelized EDMD baseline (if available) under the same kernels.
- Provide sensitivity to kernel choice/width and to truncation rank in the robust method.
- Report computational complexity and memory usage vs. τ and feature rank, and comment on scalability.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance:
- Quality: The core idea and algorithmic recipe are sound, and the robustification is practical. However, parts of Sec. 3 (Eq. 7, Theorem 1) need a careful rewrite to eliminate ambiguities and fix functional-analytic details.
- Clarity: Generally well written, but the notational overload (ϕ/φ) and function-space confusion reduce readability. Sharpening definitions and consistently distinguishing scalar eigenfunctions from RKHS modes would help experts reproduce results.
- Originality: The RKHS-Krylov viewpoint and the explicit Gram/QR derivation add a fresh angle beyond EDMD; the robust kernel-PCA step is a good practice carried into this setting.
- Significance: Potentially useful to practitioners who want richer dictionaries without manual feature design. Significance would be strengthened by broader comparisons and clearer theory.

Specific questions for authors:
- Please formalize the function space for H-valued observables and redefine KH accordingly; then restate Theorem 1 in that setting.
- Clarify the relationship between empirical Ritz vectors φ̃j∈H and Koopman modes uj, and when φ̃j approximates κj.
- In Eq. (11), specify conditions ensuring F̂ is a consistent estimator of a projected Koopman operator; discuss bias introduced by centering and truncation.
- For the change-point detection application, justify the approximation that drops the second term in the likelihood ratio and discuss robustness.

Minor issues and typos:
- Line 55: “the the” → “the”.
- Line 153: indices use κj/λj in place of κi/λi; please fix.
- Consider renaming: ϕ for feature map, vj (or ψj) for modes in H, φj for scalar Koopman eigenfunctions, κj for their RKHS representers.

Pros:
- Clear, well-organized exposition of a kernelized DMD framework.
- Solid algorithmic contributions with a practical robust variant.
- Interesting applications demonstrating utility.

Cons:
- Mathematical ambiguities in Sec. 3.1 (operator domains, eigen-objects).
- Theorem 1 relies on unstated strong assumptions; parts of the proof are unclear.
- Limited empirical comparison to state-of-the-art.

Recommendation: Weak accept, conditional on clarifying the operator/function-space definitions, tightening Theorem 1 (assumptions and proof), and adding at least a minimal EDMD/exact DMD comparison to substantiate empirical value.
