- Proposes kernelized DMD: extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition to RKHS to approximate the Koopman operator and eigenfunctions using kernel-defined feature spaces.  
- Presents two algorithms: a direct Gram–Schmidt on the RKHS Krylov subspace and a numerically robust version projecting onto kernel PCA (POD) directions.  
- Theoretical foundations (Theorems 1–2) are sound and link projected operator eigenvalues to Koopman eigenvalues under reasonable assumptions.  
- Robustification via kernel PCA is a practical and necessary improvement mirroring standard DMD developments.  
- Demonstrates applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition on synthetic and real data.  
- Empirical validation is limited: experiments are confined to deterministic systems with no assessment under noise or stochastic dynamics.  
- The brief remark about using collections of consecutive-observable pairs lacks detail on applicability to multidimensional series and implicit causal assumptions.  
- Fails to clearly distinguish or deeply compare the method with extended DMD (eDMD) by Williams et al., understating the close relationship.  
- Overlooks significant prior kernel-based work on dynamical systems and time-series (e.g., kernel CCA, RKHS system identification) that must be cited and discussed.  
- Experimental evaluation lacks comparisons to strong, recent baselines (e.g., kernel k-means/subspace kernels for recognition, modern online kernel change-detection methods, state-of-the-art nonlinear forecasting).  
- Without stronger baselines, it's unclear whether kernel DMD features outperform established techniques.  
- Minor clarity/typo issues: inconsistent indexing in kernel PCA description (x0..x_{tau-1} vs. M_tau * M_tau involving x_tau) and a typo k(xj . xi) instead of k(xj, xi).  
- Arguments for acceptance: well-motivated, theoretically solid unification of DMD and kernel methods offering powerful feature extraction for nonlinear dynamics.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear novelty relative to eDMD, missing related-kernel literature, weak empirical comparisons, and restriction to deterministic settings.  
- Recommendation: borderline — would reconsider if authors clarify relation to eDMD and prior kernel work and substantially strengthen experiments with appropriate baselines and stochastic/noisy evaluations.
