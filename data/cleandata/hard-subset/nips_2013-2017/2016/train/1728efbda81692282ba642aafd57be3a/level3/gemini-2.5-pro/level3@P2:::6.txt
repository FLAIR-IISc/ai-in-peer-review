### Summary

This paper introduces a method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to analyze the Koopman operator, an infinite-dimensional linear operator that governs the evolution of observables of the system, within an RKHS. The proposed algorithm, kernel DMD (kDMD), approximates the Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions from a finite sequence of data. This is achieved by performing an eigendecomposition of a finite-dimensional projection of the Koopman operator onto a subspace spanned by the data's feature maps. The authors present two algorithmic variants: one based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space and a more robust version based on projecting the data onto kernel principal components. The paper frames this decomposition as a feature extraction technique for dynamical systems and demonstrates its potential application in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

This work builds upon the growing literature on DMD, a technique that originated in fluid dynamics, and connects it to the well-established framework of kernel methods in machine learning. It is closely related to extended DMD, which uses a pre-determined dictionary of basis functions, and conceptually similar to other kernel-based methods for system identification and time-series analysis like kernel PCA and spectral methods for learning dynamical systems.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the derivation of the kernelized DMD algorithm appears correct. The connection between the eigendecomposition of the projected operator and the Koopman spectra is well-supported by the provided theorems. However, the paper's primary weakness lies in its evaluation and the handling of assumptions. The work is presented as a complete piece, but the experimental support is preliminary.
*   The framework is developed exclusively for deterministic systems. This is a significant limitation, as most machine learning applications involve systems with inherent stochasticity or observational noise. The authors briefly mention an extension to stochastic systems in the context of the change-point detection application, but this aspect is not sufficiently developed or evaluated.
*   The experimental claims are not strongly supported. The comparisons to other methods are either absent or unclear, making it difficult to assess the practical advantages of the proposed technique.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of introducing the concepts of Koopman operator theory and DMD in a manner that is accessible to a machine learning audience that may not be familiar with the fluid dynamics literature. The mathematical derivations are clear and easy to follow. This pedagogical quality is a significant strength of the manuscript.

**Originality:** The primary contribution is the novel combination of DMD with kernel methods to perform Koopman analysis in an RKHS. While the individual components (DMD, Koopman analysis, kernel methods for linear operators) are not new, their synthesis is. The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) are extensions of existing principles to the RKHS setting and, as such, are not surprising given the extensive literature on linear operators in Hilbert spaces. The work is most closely related to extended DMD [35], which also uses a dictionary of functions; the proposed method can be seen as using a data-dependent dictionary implicitly defined by the kernel. This relationship should be discussed more explicitly.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work is in bridging the gap between operator-theoretic methods for dynamical systems and mainstream machine learning. However, the paper falls short of demonstrating this significance convincingly.
*   The practical usefulness for machine learning is not clearly established. The authors suggest several applications, but they need to be benchmarked against state-of-the-art methods in those specific areas (e.g., Gaussian Process models for prediction, modern change-point detection algorithms). Without such comparisons, it is unclear what concrete problem this method solves better than existing techniques.
*   The experiments are illustrative rather than comparative. For the toy examples in Section 6, a comparison with standard DMD is crucial to demonstrate the value of the kernelization. It is not clear from the results whether kernel DMD provides a more accurate or insightful decomposition than simpler linear methods. For example, how does standard DMD perform on the Hénon map?

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

1.  **Baseline Comparisons:** To strengthen the paper, it is essential to include baseline comparisons in the experiments. For the toy and Hénon map examples (Fig. 1), please show the eigenvalues estimated by standard DMD. This would provide a direct assessment of the benefit gained by moving to an RKHS. For the applications in Section 7, comparisons against established machine learning methods are needed to demonstrate the practical relevance of the proposed approach.

2.  **Handling of Noise:** The deterministic setting is a major limitation. The authors should include a more thorough discussion on how noise (both process and measurement) would affect the algorithm. Does the POD-based robustification (Section 4) help mitigate the effects of noise? An experiment on a simple system with added noise would greatly enhance the paper's relevance.

3.  **Clarification on Implementation:** Could you please clarify if the algorithm, particularly step (4) in the robust procedure, requires the explicit computation of vectors in the feature space (`φ̄j`)? If so, this would be a major drawback compared to methods like kernel PCA that rely solely on the Gram matrix. If not, it would be helpful to explicitly state that the entire procedure can be "kernelized."

4.  **Application to ML Problems:** The paper would be more impactful if it focused on one application and demonstrated a clear advantage in a domain relevant to the NIPS community, such as robotics, reinforcement learning, or video analysis, where understanding and modeling dynamics is a core problem.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper is clearly written, introduces an elegant and interesting synthesis of DMD and kernel methods, and provides a useful bridge between the dynamical systems literature and machine learning. It has the potential to inspire further work in this direction.

*   **Con:** The practical contribution to the machine learning community is not well-supported. The experimental evaluation is weak and lacks necessary baselines, making the advantages of the proposed method unclear. The theoretical originality is incremental, and the strict focus on deterministic systems limits the method's immediate applicability to many real-world problems.

Overall, while the idea is interesting and well-presented, the paper in its current form feels more like a position paper than a complete contribution with demonstrated impact. The lack of rigorous empirical validation against relevant baselines is a significant concern.
