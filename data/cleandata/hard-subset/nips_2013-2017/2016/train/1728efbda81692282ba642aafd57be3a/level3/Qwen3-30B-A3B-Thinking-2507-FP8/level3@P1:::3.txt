

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors extend the traditional DMD framework, which approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials of observables, to use kernel-based nonlinear basis functions. The core contribution is a modal decomposition algorithm that reduces to calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projection onto these bases. The method is further robustified using kernel principal component analysis (PCA) to address numerical instability issues common in DMD.

The paper builds upon the Koopman operator framework, which has been increasingly popular in fluid dynamics and machine learning for spectral analysis of nonlinear systems. While DMD (proposed by Schmid and others) has gained traction in physics and engineering, the authors correctly identify its limitation in approximating eigenfunctions beyond local linearization regions. Their work extends Williams et al.'s extended DMD by using kernel methods to provide a more expressive basis for Koopman eigenfunction approximation. This aligns with recent trends in applying kernel methods to dynamical systems analysis, as seen in Kawahara et al. (NIPS 2010) and Williams et al. (J. Nonlinear Science 2015).

The paper demonstrates several applications of the extracted features: state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The empirical results on synthetic and real-world datasets (including the Hénon map and locomotion data) show that the method can accurately estimate Koopman eigenvalues and extract meaningful dynamics features.

The paper has significant strengths. It is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear theoretical exposition and detailed algorithm descriptions. The mathematical development is technically sound, and the robustification procedure using kernel PCA addresses a critical practical issue. The applications section demonstrates the utility of the extracted features in meaningful ways, with the locomotion recognition example particularly compelling.

However, the paper suffers from several mathematical inconsistencies that undermine its technical contribution. The most significant issue appears in Section 3.1, where Equation (7) defines KHφ(x) = φ∘f(x) as the Koopman operator on the feature map φ. The problem is that KH is supposed to be an operator from H to H, but φ maps M→H, meaning φ(x) is a vector in H, not a function in H. This creates a fundamental mismatch in the mathematical formulation. The authors state "KH is a linear operator in H," but the definition in Equation (7) doesn't establish how KH acts on elements of H.

The proof of Theorem 1 (lines 144-157) exacerbates this issue. The phrase "applying the operator KH to both sides" is mathematically questionable since both sides of the equation appear to be scalars (or vectors in C^τ), not elements of H. The proof assumes KH acts on the vectors vj, but these are not defined as elements of H in the context of the theorem statement.

Furthermore, Theorem 1 makes strong implicit assumptions that aren't explicitly stated. The proof requires that φj(x) = ⟨φ(x), κj⟩ for some κj ∈ H, but this isn't established as a general property of the kernel method. The paper also contains several typos that could confuse readers (e.g., "the the" on line 55, inconsistent use of λj vs λi in line 153).

The paper would benefit from a clearer mathematical foundation. The connection between the kernel-based approach and the Koopman operator framework needs more precise definition. The authors should explicitly clarify the relationship between the feature map φ, the RKHS H, and the Koopman operator KH. The current formulation blurs the distinction between functions in H and vectors in the feature space.

While the numerical results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient comparison to state-of-the-art methods. The authors reference Williams et al.'s extended DMD as similar, but don't provide a direct comparison of performance or computational complexity. A more thorough evaluation against existing kernel-based methods for dynamical systems analysis would strengthen the claims.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important limitation in DMD for nonlinear systems, and the kernel-based extension offers a theoretically sound approach to handle nonlinear dynamics. The applications section demonstrates practical utility, though more detailed comparisons with existing methods would better establish the method's advantages.

Overall, the paper presents a compelling extension of DMD using kernel methods with clear potential applications. The writing is excellent, and the core idea is well-motivated. However, the mathematical inconsistencies in the theoretical foundation significantly undermine the contribution. The paper would be much stronger with a more rigorous mathematical formulation that clarifies the relationship between the feature map, RKHS, and Koopman operator. Without addressing these foundational issues, the technical contribution remains questionable. The reviewers should consider this as a borderline paper, with the mathematical clarity issues being the main concern.
