- Proposes extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to an RKHS to approximate Koopman operator eigenfunctions using kernels.
- Develops an algorithm based on Krylov subspace computation and eigendecomposition.
- Demonstrates applications on synthetic systems and real locomotion data for prediction, embedding, and change-point detection.
- Paper is well-written with clear exposition of Koopman theory and DMD background.
- Mathematical derivations appear sound and logically presented.
- Good connection to relevant literature on DMD, POD, kernel methods, and spectral analysis.
- Novelty is incremental: extension is natural given existing kernel and RKHS operator literature.
- Empirical evaluation is weak: lacks direct comparisons to standard DMD and other state-of-the-art methods.
- Locomotion application lacks quantitative evaluation and comparisons to common dimensionality-reduction or pattern-recognition techniques.
- Unclear practical utility for ML tasks; authors do not convincingly show performance gains in real-world scenarios.
- Does not analyze the impact of kernel choice on performance or provide guidance for selecting kernels.
- Scalability and computational cost are not sufficiently addressed, especially for high-dimensional data.
- Exclusive focus on deterministic systems limits applicability to noisy/stochastic ML settings.
- Potential implementation limitation if modal decomposition requires explicit RKHS feature computation (unlike implicit kernel PCA).
- Arguments for acceptance: clear exposition, solid technical extension of DMD to RKHS, and demonstrated potential applications.
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, weak empirical validation, unclear ML relevance, and possible computational limitations.
- Overall assessment: weak accept, conditional on addressing comparisons to DMD, expanded experiments, treatment of noise, computational scalability, and concrete use cases.
- Recommended revisions: add direct DMD comparisons on toy/Hénon examples; expand rigorous benchmarks in ML domains; discuss noisy/stochastic extensions; clarify computational cost and feature-space handling; articulate concrete scenarios of advantage.
