Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a kernelized variant of Koopman spectral analysis, formulating Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The core idea is to approximate Koopman spectra by computing orthonormal bases of a Krylov matrix in RKHS and diagonalizing the projection of the Koopman operator onto that subspace. The authors also present a “robustified” variant that projects data onto kernel-PCA (POD) directions to address numerical ill-conditioning, and they demonstrate applications to short-term prediction via preimages, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The work sits among a family of spectral methods: classical POD/DMD, the Koopman operator viewpoint, and extended/optimized/sparse DMD. It is closest to extended DMD (EDMD) and kernelized EDMD/DMD (Williams et al., 2015), which approximate the Koopman operator using a dictionary of observables or the kernel trick. The paper’s Section 4 (projection to POD directions) resembles the “robust” SVD-truncation in DMD/EDMD and the kernelized formulation of EDMD. The authors also relate to spectral methods for dynamical systems in machine learning (e.g., predictive state representations, subspace identification), though this link could be strengthened.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The RKHS formulation is standard and the proofs building the connection to Ritz values/vectors and the kernel-PCA-projected operator are reasonable. The algorithmic steps reduce to Gram-matrix computations, which is practical. However, the paper needs a clear, explicit comparison to Williams et al. (2015), who derived kernel EDMD (KDMD): spell out where the proposed method is the same/different (operator estimated, centering, truncation, scaling, complexity, consistency). As written, it is difficult to assess what is novel versus a re-derivation of KDMD with slightly different numerics.
- Empirical support: Experiments are illustrative (toy system, Hénon map, locomotion clustering/embedding, change-point detection), but not sufficiently ambitious or comparative to support strong claims. No quantitative evaluation of stochastic/noisy settings is provided, despite Section 7 discussing stochastic extensions.
- Reproducibility: The algorithm is described in enough detail for an expert to implement. Hyperparameter selection (kernel type/width, truncation rank) and runtime/memory scaling are not reported.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and readable. Section 3/4 are technically clear, and the RKHS linear algebra is carefully presented. Major clarity gaps:
- Distinction to EDMD/KDMD (Williams et al., 2015) is not clear; Section 4 should explicitly compare formulations, proofs, and practical differences. In particular, please clarify whether your “robustified” method is equivalent to KDMD (up to centering and truncation), and how your Ritz construction relates to EDMD’s least-squares operator.
- The “pairs of consecutive observables” remark (end of Sec. 4) needs a careful discussion of causal/Markov assumptions, implications for multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relations, and when using sequences (or delay embeddings) is preferable. Please state the causal direction assumed, whether time-reversal symmetry is used, and whether delay coordinates (Hankel/KDMD with delays) would be more robust in weakly coupled systems.

Originality:
Kernelizing Koopman spectral analysis and connecting DMD to RKHS are by now established ideas (EDMD and KDMD). If there is novelty, it likely lies in the specific Krylov/QR/Ritz derivation and the particular projection via kernel-PCA. Without an explicit comparison, it is hard to judge incremental novelty. Related work is also incomplete in kernel-based dynamical systems and time-series analysis:
- Ralaivola & d’Alché Buc (e.g., time-series with kernels, RKHS autoregressions).
- Tuia et al. (kernel-based multitemporal/sequence modeling in remote sensing).
- Rojo-Álvarez et al. (kernel adaptive filters/kernels for time series).
- Mouattamid & Schaback (RKHS-based system identification/smoothing).
- Franz & Schölkopf (operator-valued kernels/RKHS for dynamical systems).
Please discuss how your approach relates to these streams and to delay-embedding-based Koopman methods (Hankel-DMD, subspace-DMD), GPDM, and PSR-style spectral learning.

Significance:
The RKHS formulation of Koopman spectral analysis is potentially impactful as a bridge between Koopman theory and scalable kernel learning, with applications to prediction, clustering, and change detection. However, significance is limited by the lack of rigorous empirical baselines and by unclear novelty over KDMD.

Pros:
- Clear RKHS formulation with derivations linking Ritz values/vectors to Koopman spectra.
- Practical robustification via kernel-PCA projection; kernel trick yields implementable algorithms.
- Multiple application sketches (prediction preimage, change-point detection, dynamics recognition).
- Code availability (though consider anonymization issues).

Cons:
- Unclear distinction from Williams et al. (2015) EDMD/KDMD; needs explicit theoretical and empirical comparison.
- Experiments are small-scale and largely qualitative; no comparisons to strong baselines.
- No stochastic/noisy experiments; performance and limitations in such settings are not demonstrated.
- Missing related work in kernel dynamical systems and time-series kernels.
- Practical guidance on using pairs vs. sequences/delays and causal assumptions is missing.

Requests for revision:
1) Explicit comparison to Williams et al. (2015): derive equivalences/differences, compare complexity and numerical behavior, and run head-to-head experiments on shared benchmarks.
2) Related work: add and discuss Ralaivola & d’Alché Buc; Tuia et al.; Rojo-Álvarez et al.; Mouattamid & Schaback; Franz & Schölkopf; as well as delay-embedding Koopman and subspace-DMD.
3) Stochastic settings: include experiments with process and measurement noise; discuss robustness, estimation bias, and regularization.
4) Pairs vs. sequences: clarify assumptions, when delay embeddings are required, and implications for multivariate series with weak pointwise relations.
5) Stronger evaluations: 
   - Prediction: quantitative metrics vs. EDMD/KDMD, Hankel-DMD, GPDM, and simple baselines.
   - Dynamics clustering: compare to kernel k-means, subspace kernels/principal angles, global alignment/DTW kernels.
   - Online change-point detection: compare to kernel-based SOTA (e.g., Harchaoui & Cappé/Moulines; MMD-based online CPD) with detection delay/false alarm trade-offs.
6) Report scalability (O(τ^3), memory), kernel/truncation selection strategies, and sensitivity analyses.

Minor issues:
- Typo: indexing “τ−1 vs. τ” (ensure consistency in Section 2/3 equations).
- Typo: replace dot with comma in k(xi, xj) (last equation on p.7).
- Consider anonymizing code links for double-blind review.

Overall evaluation: Promising and well-presented RKHS view of Koopman spectral analysis, but the contribution appears incremental over KDMD and lacks strong empirical validation. With a thorough comparison to Williams et al. (2015) and more convincing experiments (especially in stochastic settings and against strong baselines), this work could be significantly strengthened. My current recommendation is weak reject, with a clear path to improvement.
