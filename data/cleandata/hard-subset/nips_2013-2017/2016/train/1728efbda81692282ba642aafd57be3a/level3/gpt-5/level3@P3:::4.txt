Summary and relation to prior work
The paper extends Koopman spectral analysis to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) by formulating Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) directly in feature space. The core idea is to define the Koopman operator KH on the feature map and compute a modal decomposition via (i) Gram–Schmidt orthogonalization on the Krylov matrix in RKHS and (ii) eigendecomposition of the projected operator. The authors also provide a numerically robust variant that projects onto kernel-PCA directions (akin to standard DMD’s POD truncation). The resulting “kernel DMD” (kDMD) yields finite-dimensional estimates of Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions and modes that can be used as features for downstream tasks.

This work builds squarely on DMD and the Koopman operator literature (e.g., Schmid; Rowley et al.; Mezić), and is closely related to Extended DMD (EDMD; Williams et al.), which uses pre-specified dictionaries. The RKHS formulation can be viewed as an implicit, rich dictionary and the robustified variant mirrors EDMD’s least-squares operator estimation but with kernel PCA–based truncation. The paper also connects to kernel PCA and subspace identification methods, and illustrates applications in short-term prediction via pre-image estimation, dynamic pattern recognition using kernel principal angles, and sequential change-point detection.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: clean RKHS formulation of the Koopman spectral problem; elegant derivations (Theorems 1–2) tying Ritz values/vectors in feature space to Koopman spectra.
- Practical algorithm: numerically robust pipeline via kernel PCA truncation; implementation reduces to Gram matrix operations.
- Broad applicability: demonstrates use for prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection, with both simulated and real-world data (e.g., locomotion MoCap).
- Clarity: the manuscript is well organized and very clearly written; the relationship to DMD/Koopman is carefully explained; code is provided.

Weaknesses
- Missing baselines: no quantitative comparison to non-RKHS Koopman spectral methods (standard/optimized/exact DMD, EDMD) on the same simulations.
- Kernel dependence: no systematic study of how kernel choice (e.g., RBF bandwidth vs polynomial degree) and truncation affect spectra, stability, and downstream accuracy.
- Assumptions/consistency: theoretical results assume eigenfunctions lie in the RKHS span (and in the kernel PCA subspace); the paper does not quantify approximation error or provide convergence guarantees under noise.
- Scalability: Gram matrices/eigendecompositions scale poorly; the paper does not discuss complexity or large-sample remedies (Nyström, random features, incremental KPCA).
- Pre-image step: the MDS-like pre-image recovery is heuristic; the sensitivity to neighbors, noise, and kernel parameters is not analyzed.

Quality
Technically sound and carefully derived. The robustified algorithm is sensible and aligns with best practices in DMD. However, empirical evaluation is largely qualitative. For the toy and Hénon systems, please report:
- Eigenvalue errors vs ground-truth (where available), mode reconstruction error, and multi-step prediction error.
- Comparisons to DMD, exact/optimized DMD, and EDMD with matched dictionary sizes.
- Sensitivity to sample length, noise, and truncation rank p.

Clarity
Excellent exposition and notation; the pipeline is reproducible from the description and code. Minor suggestions:
- State computational complexity per stage and memory footprint.
- Clarify centering: its role when learning KH and its effect on spectra.
- Improve figure captions with quantitative metrics and uncertainty.

Originality
Moderately high. The RKHS-Koopman framing and the QR/Krylov viewpoint are nice; the robustification via kernel PCA is natural. Given the close connection to EDMD and kernelized EDMD variants, please sharpen the positioning: what is new beyond EDMD with a kernel dictionary? For instance, is the Krylov construction in RKHS providing different estimators or improved stability?

Significance
Potentially impactful for data-driven analysis of nonlinear dynamics, especially for practitioners who want Koopman features without hand-crafting dictionaries. The applications show breadth, but more rigorous comparisons and kernel studies would strengthen the case for adoption.

Specific requests for revision
- Add non-RKHS baselines on simulations:
  - Standard DMD, exact DMD, optimized DMD, and EDMD (with polynomial/RBF dictionaries matching kernel capacity).
  - Metrics: eigenvalue error, reconstruction error of observables or modes, and prediction RMSE vs horizon.
  - On MoCap recognition, report classification accuracy vs baselines (e.g., PCA+subspace angles, EDMD features).
- Kernel choice and sensitivity:
  - Study RBF bandwidth (median heuristic vs cross-validation), polynomial degree, and regularization (ridge in Gram matrix, Tikhonov on F̂).
  - Plot how the number/stability of identified eigenvalues, residual ψ, and prediction error vary with kernel parameters and truncation rank p.
  - Consider multiple-kernel combinations or bandwidth ensembles; discuss practical selection criteria (e.g., minimizing one-step prediction error, maximizing explained variance in feature dynamics).
- Scalability:
  - Provide complexity analysis; discuss and, if possible, demonstrate Nyström approximations, random Fourier features, or incremental kernel PCA in streaming settings.
- Robustness:
  - Evaluate under observation noise and model misspecification; report how POD truncation and regularization mitigate spurious unstable eigenvalues.
- Theory:
  - Discuss when ψ ≈ 0 is plausible and how continuous spectra (chaotic systems) affect estimation; any bounds relating approximation error to kernel PCA truncation and sample size?

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, well-written generalization of DMD to RKHS with practical, implementable algorithms.
- Clear connection to Koopman theory and to established machine learning tools (kernel PCA).
- Potentially useful features for downstream tasks; code available.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited quantitative evaluation and lack of direct comparisons to DMD/EDMD baselines.
- No systematic treatment of kernel selection and its impact on results.
- Scalability and robustness not thoroughly addressed; theory relies on strong inclusion assumptions without error analysis.

Overall assessment
Promising contribution with solid methodology and clear writing. With added comparisons to non-RKHS Koopman methods and a careful exploration of kernel choices and truncation, this paper would be significantly strengthened and better positioned within the NIPS literature.
