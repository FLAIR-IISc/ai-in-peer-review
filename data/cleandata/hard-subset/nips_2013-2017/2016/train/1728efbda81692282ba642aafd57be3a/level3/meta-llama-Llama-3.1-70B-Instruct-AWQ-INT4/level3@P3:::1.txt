The paper "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis" presents a novel approach to spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs). The authors propose a modal decomposition algorithm that extends the traditional dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) method, which is widely used in physics and engineering fields.

The main idea of the paper is to apply the Koopman operator to a nonlinear dynamical system in an RKHS, allowing for a more accurate representation of the system's dynamics. The authors show that the proposed algorithm can capture the nonlinear dynamics from finite-length data sequences generated from a dynamical system, and demonstrate its effectiveness through empirical examples.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a clear and concise explanation of the Koopman operator and its application to nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors also provide a thorough review of related works, including DMD and its variants, as well as other spectral analysis techniques.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. Firstly, the authors could provide a clearer distinction between their work and that of Williams et al. (2015), which is mentioned in the paper but not fully explored. Additionally, the authors could benefit from including more relevant prior work in kernel-based dynamical systems, such as Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc; Tuia et al.; Rojo-Álvarez et al.; Mouattamid & Schaback; Franz & Schölkopf.

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the practical implications of using pairs of consecutive observables instead of sequences, especially for multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relations. The authors could also provide more insight into the causal assumptions underlying their approach.

In terms of experiments, the paper could benefit from more ambitious evaluations, such as comparisons to time-series clustering methods (kernel k-means, subspace kernels) and state-of-the-art online kernel change-point detection. The authors could also provide more convincing evaluations of their method, such as demonstrating its ability to handle stochastic settings and performance limitations.

Minor issues with the paper include two typos (indexing tau-1 vs. tau and replacing dot with comma in k(xi.xj) in the last equation on p.7).

Overall, the paper presents a novel and interesting approach to spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems using RKHSs. While there are some areas for improvement, the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the underlying concepts and provide a thorough explanation of their method.
