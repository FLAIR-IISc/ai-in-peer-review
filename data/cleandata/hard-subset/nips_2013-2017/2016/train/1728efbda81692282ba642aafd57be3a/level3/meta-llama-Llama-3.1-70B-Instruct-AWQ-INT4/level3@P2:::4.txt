This paper presents a novel extension of Koopman spectral analysis to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), enabling the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors propose a modal decomposition algorithm that leverages the properties of RKHS to approximate the Koopman eigenfunctions. The method is evaluated on both simulated and real-world data, demonstrating its effectiveness in extracting meaningful features from nonlinear dynamical systems.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the background and related work. The presentation of the proposed method is also well-organized, making it easy to follow the mathematical derivations and algorithmic descriptions.

The key contribution of this paper is the extension of Koopman spectral analysis to RKHS, which allows for the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems using a more expressive and flexible framework. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through various experiments, including the analysis of a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, and real-world data from the CMU Graphics Lab Motion Capture Database.

One of the strengths of this paper is its ability to provide a clear and concise introduction to the background and related work. The authors provide a thorough review of existing methods for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems, including proper orthogonal decomposition (POD), dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), and other variants. This helps to situate the proposed method within the broader context of nonlinear system analysis.

The paper also provides a clear and well-organized presentation of the proposed method, including the mathematical derivations and algorithmic descriptions. The authors provide a step-by-step guide to the implementation of their method, making it easy to follow and replicate.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. One potential limitation of the paper is the lack of comparison to non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis in the simulations. It would be helpful to include a comparison of the proposed method to existing methods for Koopman spectral analysis, in order to better understand the benefits and limitations of the proposed approach.

Another area where the paper could be improved is in the discussion and exploration of kernel choice and its impact on results. The authors mention that the choice of kernel is crucial for the success of the method, but they do not provide a detailed analysis of how different kernel choices affect the results. This could be explored through simulations, where the authors could investigate the impact of different kernel choices on the accuracy and robustness of the method.
