

# Review for "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), which the authors term Kernel DMD (KDMD). The work builds on the Koopman spectral analysis framework, where the Koopman operator provides a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics. Standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials of observables, while KDMD leverages kernel methods to obtain richer, nonlinear basis functions. The paper presents theoretical developments for performing this analysis using finite-length data sequences and demonstrates applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work on Koopman spectral analysis (e.g., Rowley et al., 2009; Tu et al., 2013) and kernel methods (Schölkopf et al., 1999). It also connects to related work on extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), which uses pre-determined basis functions rather than kernel-based representations. The authors correctly identify that KDMD represents a significant generalization of standard DMD by moving from linear to nonlinear basis functions.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear mathematical development of the KDMD algorithm through Theorems 1 and 2.
2. The method offers a principled approach to spectral analysis of nonlinear systems using kernel methods.
3. The applications to prediction (via preimage reconstruction), dynamics recognition (using kernel principal angles), and change-point detection are well-motivated and demonstrate the utility of the extracted features.
4. The empirical results on synthetic data (toy system and Hénon map) validate the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Clarity:** The paper is overly mathematical and verbose, making it difficult to follow for a general NIPS audience. Key concepts could be explained more pedagogically. For example, the connection between the Koopman operator and the kernel-based approach could be made clearer with a simple illustrative example before diving into the technical details.

2. **Structure:** Section 4 (Robustifying with POD Bases) is largely redundant with the introduction and unnecessarily lengthens the paper. The core idea could be presented more concisely in the main algorithm description.

3. **Presentation:** The paper's presentation is cryptic and needs significant improvement. Many statements could be much more concise. For instance, the description of the toy example appears to be a linear system rather than nonlinear as claimed, which requires clarification.

4. **Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic data. While the toy system and Hénon map are standard benchmarks, the paper lacks comparison with state-of-the-art methods for the applications demonstrated (prediction, change detection, etc.). More detailed analysis of when KDMD provides clear advantages over standard DMD or other methods would strengthen the claims.

5. **Significance:** The paper would benefit from more discussion of when KDMD is particularly useful (e.g., when the kernel/feature map can be guessed from domain knowledge). The paper mentions this briefly but doesn't elaborate as suggested in the key points.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a theoretically interesting extension of DMD using kernel methods with potential applications in dynamical systems analysis. However, the current presentation is overly technical, verbose, and lacks sufficient clarity for the NIPS audience. With significant rewriting to improve clarity and conciseness, and additional empirical evaluation to demonstrate the practical advantages of KDMD over existing methods, this work could become a valuable contribution.

The authors should consider:
- Providing a more pedagogical introduction to the key ideas before presenting the mathematical details
- Removing redundant sections (particularly Section 4)
- Expanding Section 6 with more detailed examples and comparisons
- Clarifying the toy example and explaining when KDMD is most beneficial

Without substantial revisions to improve clarity and presentation, the paper's impact will be limited despite its theoretical soundness. With the suggested improvements, this work would be highly citable and useful for researchers working at the intersection of machine learning and dynamical systems.

**Summary of pros and cons:**
- Pro: Theoretical foundation is sound and well-developed; applications are well-motivated
- Pro: Extends DMD in a principled way using kernel methods
- Con: Overly technical and verbose presentation
- Con: Limited empirical evaluation with synthetic data only
- Con: Redundant sections and lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods
- Con: Unclear when KDMD provides clear advantages over standard DMD

I recommend rejection in its current form, but with strong encouragement for the authors to revise and resubmit after addressing the clarity issues. The core idea is promising and could be a significant contribution to the field with a more accessible presentation.
