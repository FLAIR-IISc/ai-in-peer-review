This paper presents a novel approach to spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to operate within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The core idea is to approximate the Koopman operator’s eigenfunctions using kernel functions, allowing for a richer, potentially more accurate representation of the underlying dynamics compared to traditional DMD which relies on linear monomials. The authors develop an algorithm based on Krylov subspace computation and eigendecomposition, and demonstrate its application to synthetic and real-world data, including a locomotion dataset.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the theoretical background of Koopman operator theory and DMD, making it accessible to readers not necessarily familiar with the fluid-dynamics origins of these concepts. The extension to RKHS is logically presented and the mathematical derivations appear sound. The inclusion of applications, such as prediction, embedding for dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection, demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of potential use cases.

A key strength of this work is its connection to existing literature. The authors appropriately cite relevant papers on DMD, POD, kernel methods, and spectral analysis, situating their contribution within a broader research context. The discussion of related work, while brief, acknowledges relevant efforts in subspace identification and spectral methods for dynamical systems.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the theoretical results are well-motivated, they are not entirely surprising given the well-established literature on kernel methods and linear operators in RKHS. The core contribution lies in applying these established techniques to the specific context of Koopman spectral analysis, but the novelty appears incremental rather than groundbreaking.

More significantly, the empirical evaluation lacks compelling comparisons to existing state-of-the-art methods. The demonstrations on the toy nonlinear system and the Henon map are encouraging, but a direct comparison to standard DMD performance on these examples is missing. This makes it difficult to assess the practical benefits of the proposed kernelized approach.  The application to locomotion data is suggestive, but lacks quantitative evaluation or a comparison to other dimensionality reduction or pattern recognition techniques commonly used in this domain. The paper would benefit significantly from a more rigorous experimental setup with clear performance metrics.

The practical utility of this method for machine learning applications remains unclear. The authors mention several potential use cases, but fail to convincingly demonstrate how this approach outperforms existing methods in a real-world ML task. Specifically, the paper doesn’t address how the choice of kernel impacts performance or how to efficiently scale the algorithm to high-dimensional data.  The exclusive focus on deterministic systems also limits the applicability to many ML scenarios where noise and stochasticity are prevalent.

Furthermore, the discussion of feature-space representations raises a concern. If the modal decomposition requires explicit computation and storage of features in the RKHS, this could be a significant limitation compared to kernel PCA, which operates implicitly in the feature space. Clarification on this point is crucial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Clear and well-written exposition of a technically sound method.
*   Logical extension of DMD to RKHS, potentially offering improved accuracy in certain scenarios.
*   Demonstration of potential applications in prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.
*   Solid connection to relevant literature in dynamical systems and kernel methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty – the theoretical results are not entirely surprising.
*   Weak empirical evaluation lacking comparison to state-of-the-art methods (DMD, PCA, kPCA).
*   Unclear practical utility for machine learning applications, with a focus on deterministic systems.
*   Potential computational limitations related to explicit feature-space representations.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the technical contribution is solid and the paper is well-written, the lack of compelling empirical evidence and the unclear practical utility raise significant concerns. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing the following points:

1.  **Include a direct comparison to standard DMD performance** on the toy example and the Henon map.
2.  **Expand the empirical evaluation** with a more rigorous comparison to relevant state-of-the-art methods in a relevant machine learning domain (e.g., robotics, control).
3.  **Address the issue of deterministic systems** and discuss potential extensions to handle noisy data.
4.  **Clarify the computational cost** and scalability of the algorithm, particularly concerning explicit feature-space representations.
5. **Clearly articulate concrete use cases** where the proposed method offers a significant advantage over existing techniques.
