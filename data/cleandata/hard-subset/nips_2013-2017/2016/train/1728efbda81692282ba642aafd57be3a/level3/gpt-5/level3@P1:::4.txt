This paper proposes a kernelized extension of Koopman spectral analysis by formulating Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The core idea is to view time-shifted feature maps ϕ(xt) as a Krylov sequence in H and to approximate the Koopman operator via its projection onto the subspace spanned by these snapshots. The authors derive an RKHS analogue of the Arnoldi/DMD machinery: compute an orthonormal basis in the feature space (via Gram–Schmidt on the Krylov matrix Mτ), form the projected operator, and extract empirical Ritz values/vectors as Koopman eigenvalues/modes. They further “robustify” the algorithm by projecting onto kernel-POD (kernel PCA) directions, which stabilizes the computation in rank-deficient or ill-conditioned regimes. Theoretical results (Theorems 1–2) relate the computed eigenvalues/eigenvectors to true Koopman spectra under span/richness assumptions. Applications include short-term prediction via a pre-image construction, dynamics recognition using subspace distances between estimated Koopman eigenspaces, and sequential change-point detection using a kernelized CUSUM-style statistic. Empirical demonstrations on a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, motion-capture locomotion sequences, and a Lorenz-map change-point scenario illustrate feasibility.

Relation to prior work: DMD and its variants (exact DMD, optimized DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD) estimate Koopman spectra from data; EDMD (Williams et al.) generalizes DMD to a user-chosen dictionary and introduces a kernelized version (often called KDMD). The present paper’s contribution is closest to EDMD/KDMD but is framed through Krylov subspaces and Ritz analysis directly in RKHS, with a principled robustification via kernel PCA. Connections to spectral learning for dynamical systems and subspace identification are appropriately noted.

Quality:
- The linear-algebraic development is sound and clearly motivated: the RKHS Arnoldi viewpoint, projected operator eigendecomposition, and the kernel-POD stabilization are standard, well-justified steps. Theorems 1–2 are reasonable under distinct eigenvalues and span assumptions.
- Practical aspects (centering, truncation, Gram matrices) are addressed. The pre-image step for prediction is plausible, though its accuracy and computational cost can dominate in practice.
- The empirical section shows the method working qualitatively, but quantitative evaluation and baselines are limited. Key missing comparisons include standard DMD, exact DMD, EDMD, and existing KDMD; without these, the advantage of the proposed variant over established non-RKHS and RKHS Koopman methods is unclear.
- No complexity or memory analysis is provided; kernel methods scale as O(τ^2) memory and O(τ^3) time, which can be prohibitive.

Clarity:
- The manuscript is very well written and organized. The notation is consistent, algorithmic steps are explicit, and the theoretical claims are clearly stated.
- Suggestions: add pseudo-code with complexity for both the basic and robustified procedures; clarify hyperparameter selection (kernel type/width/degree, number of retained components p, regularization); and provide guidance on pre-image computation (initialization, convergence, failure modes).

Originality:
- Kernelizing Koopman spectral analysis has been explored via EDMD/KDMD. The paper’s distinct angle is the explicit RKHS Krylov/Arnoldi derivation with a QR-based route to Ritz values and an RKHS POD projection, plus clear theorems connecting kernel PCA subspaces to Koopman eigenfunctions. The novelty is incremental relative to EDMD/KDMD but still of interest, especially for the robustification and the applications angle.

Significance:
- A stable, kernel-based Koopman spectral method is valuable for practitioners in fluids, neuroscience, and control, and the paper’s applications (recognition, change detection) broaden impact beyond physics. With stronger empirical comparisons and guidance on kernel choice, this has potential to be adopted.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
- Add a thorough empirical comparison to non-RKHS Koopman methods (DMD, exact DMD) and to EDMD/KDMD on the same datasets. Report eigenvalue errors (when ground truth is known), mode reconstruction error, one-step prediction error, and sensitivity to noise.
- Investigate kernel choice and hyperparameters: compare RBF kernels with different widths (median heuristic vs cross-validation), polynomial degrees, and other kernels (e.g., Laplacian). Show how kernel choice impacts spectrum accuracy, mode stability, and prediction.
- Quantify the effect of truncation p and regularization (e.g., ridge on Gram matrices) on stability and accuracy; include noisy/stochastic settings to test robustness.
- Provide runtime/memory scaling plots vs number of snapshots; discuss approximations (Nyström, random features, block processing) for larger datasets.
- For prediction, compare the MDS-style pre-image method to alternative pre-image solvers (e.g., Kwok & Tsang, fixed-point iterations) and report pre-image errors; also consider evaluating prediction directly in feature space to decouple pre-image errors from spectral estimation.
- Clarify assumptions in Theorem 2 and discuss how violations (e.g., insufficient richness, misspecified kernel) manifest in practice; provide practical diagnostics (e.g., residual r or ψ norms).

Pros:
- Clear, well-presented RKHS-based derivation of Koopman/DMD with supporting theorems.
- Practical robustification via kernel PCA; handles rank deficiency and ill-conditioning.
- Demonstrates applicability to prediction, recognition, and change detection.
- Code availability enhances reproducibility.

Cons:
- Limited quantitative experiments and no comparisons to DMD/EDMD/KDMD baselines.
- No analysis of kernel choice/hyperparameters or scaling.
- Pre-image step introduces additional complexity and potential error without evaluation.
- Theoretical assumptions are strong and not accompanied by error bounds or diagnostics.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper is clear, technically competent, and potentially useful, but it needs stronger empirical comparisons and an exploration of kernel choice/sensitivity to substantiate benefits over existing (K)DMD methods. If the authors can add these comparisons and kernel analyses, the contribution will be solidly above the bar.
