

# Review: Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis

This paper proposes a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors extend the Koopman operator framework to RKHS by defining a kernelized version of the Koopman operator, developing an algorithm to compute its spectral decomposition using finite-length data sequences. The method essentially computes orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performs eigendecomposition on the projection of the Koopman operator. The authors demonstrate applications including state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using extracted features.

The work builds on the well-established DMD framework (Rowley et al., 2009; Schmid, 2010), extending it with kernel methods to allow for nonlinear basis functions rather than the linear monomials used in standard DMD. The paper relates to kernel PCA (Schölkopf et al., 1998) and other kernel methods, though it doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from Williams et al.'s extended DMD (2015), which similarly works with pre-determined basis functions.

**Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and pedagogically clear, making the Koopman operator concept accessible to ML readers unfamiliar with fluid dynamics. The mathematical derivations are sound, and the theoretical foundation is solid. The organization is logical, with clear sections on background, theory, robustification, and applications. The authors provide a comprehensive treatment of the method's theoretical underpinnings.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant weakness is the lack of clear demonstration of practical utility for machine learning. The empirical evaluation uses simple dynamical systems (damped 1D and Henon map) and locomotion data, but comparisons to standard DMD, PCA, and kPCA are insufficient to establish advantages. The paper doesn't include standard DMD performance on the toy example to highlight differences, and the claimed "kernel DMD" results appear to be on par with basic DMD. The theoretical results are unsurprising given existing RKHS literature (e.g., Schölkopf et al., 1998; Shawe-Taylor & Cristianini, 2004), and the paper fails to clarify whether the modal decomposition requires explicit feature-space representations, which would be a significant limitation compared to kernel PCA.

The deterministic-only setup (no stochastic/noise components) is problematic for ML applications where randomness is common, and the paper doesn't address how this method would handle noisy data. The applications proposed (state prediction, change-point detection) don't convincingly demonstrate advantages over simpler methods. The authors should identify a concrete ML application domain where this method provides clear benefits over existing techniques.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, but the empirical evaluation lacks depth and comparative analysis to support claims of superiority. The theoretical results are correct but not particularly novel.

**Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear mathematical derivations and pedagogical explanations that make complex concepts accessible to ML readers.

**Originality:** The core idea is a natural extension of DMD with kernel methods, but it's not sufficiently novel to stand out from existing work. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from Williams et al.'s extended DMD (2015) or other kernel-based approaches.

**Significance:** The significance is questionable. The paper doesn't demonstrate a concrete ML application where this method provides demonstrable advantages over simpler techniques. The authors should identify a specific domain (e.g., robotics, time-series analysis of complex systems) where Koopman analysis with kernel methods provides clear benefits over existing approaches.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a technically sound but incremental extension of existing DMD techniques. While the theoretical framework is solid and the writing is excellent, the lack of clear demonstration of practical utility for machine learning applications significantly limits its impact. To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct more rigorous comparisons with standard DMD, PCA, and kPCA on the toy examples
2. Identify and demonstrate a concrete ML application where this method provides clear advantages
3. Clarify whether the method requires explicit feature-space representations
4. Address the deterministic-only limitation by demonstrating performance on noisy data

Without these improvements, the paper's contribution appears marginal compared to existing methods in the field. I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that with the suggested revisions, this could become a strong paper.
