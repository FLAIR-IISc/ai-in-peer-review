This paper introduces a kernel-based method for Koopman spectral analysis, effectively developing a version of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The central idea is to use the kernel trick to approximate the eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator, allowing for a principled modal decomposition of nonlinear dynamical systems from data. This work builds upon the growing interest in Koopman operator theory for data-driven analysis and connects it with well-established kernel methods in machine learning.

The primary strength of this work lies in its elegant formulation of Koopman analysis in an RKHS, which provides a non-parametric approach to feature extraction for nonlinear dynamics. The theoretical derivations are sound, and the proposed applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition demonstrate the potential utility of the extracted features.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that temper its contribution.

*   **Originality and Positioning:** The distinction from prior work is insufficiently articulated. The connection to Extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) is particularly unclear; the robustified method in Section 4 appears very similar, and an explicit comparison is necessary. The paper also overlooks a substantial body of relevant literature on kernel-based methods for dynamical systems (e.g., Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc, 2005; Tuia et al., 2016; Rojo-Álvarez et al., 2008; Mouattamid & Schaback, 2021; Franz & Schölkopf, 2006).

*   **Scope and Assumptions:** The analysis is restricted to deterministic systems. A discussion of the method's performance and limitations in stochastic settings is a critical omission. Furthermore, the practical implications of using pairs of consecutive states versus longer sequences need clarification, especially for high-dimensional time series where pointwise relations may be weak. The underlying causal assumptions should be made explicit.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are not sufficiently ambitious to demonstrate a significant advance. The prediction task is standard, and the comparisons are lacking. For dynamics recognition, the authors should compare against relevant time-series clustering methods like kernel k-means. For change-point detection, a benchmark against state-of-the-art online kernel change-point detection methods is needed to provide a more convincing evaluation.

**Minor Issues:**
*   There appears to be a typo in the indexing on page 4, where the text discusses τ-1 observables, while formulas like Eq. 6a seem to use τ.
*   In the last equation on page 7, the dot in `k(xi.xj)` should likely be a comma: `k(xi, xj)`.
