Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Koopman spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs). The main idea is to lift time-series data through a feature map into an RKHS and perform a Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD)-like analysis there. Concretely, the authors (i) build a Krylov matrix in feature space Mτ = [ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)], (ii) project the Koopman operator KH onto the span of these columns via a Gram–Schmidt QR factorization, and (iii) compute Ritz values/vectors that play the role of Koopman eigenvalues/modes. To address numerical conditioning, they propose a robust variant that projects onto kernel PCA directions (akin to truncated POD in DMD), yielding an estimator based on the eigendecomposition of a p×p operator assembled from Gram matrices. They illustrate the method on synthetic examples (a toy system and the Hénon map) and discuss applications to short-term prediction via pre-images, subspace-based recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection using a kernelized exponential family view.

This work is closely related to DMD [25, 26], exact/optimized/sparsity-promoting DMD [33, 8, 13], and extended DMD (EDMD) [35]. The robust kernelized variant is conceptually similar to EDMD with a kernel dictionary (often called KDMD), in which one works with Gram matrices and finite-dimensional projections in RKHS. The paper’s emphasis on a Krylov/Arnoldi interpretation in feature space and on kernel-PCA-based robustification positions it within that line of work.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: brings the power of RKHSs to Koopman spectral analysis, aiming to capture nonlinear structure beyond linear monomials.
- Generally well written and organized; the algorithmic flow is easy to follow, and the applications section is engaging.
- Technically plausible: the use of Gram matrices makes the computations practical; the robustified variant mirrors well-established practices in DMD.
- Experiments, while limited, indicate the approach can recover meaningful eigenvalues/modes and support downstream tasks.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality (technical soundness)
- Section 3 and Theorem 1 require clarification. Equation (7) defines KHϕ(x) = ϕ∘f(x), which appears notationally identical in spirit to Equation (2), but now ϕ: M→H. Please clarify the distinction: in (2), K acts on scalar observables g∈G; in (7), KH acts on H-valued observables. State precisely the function spaces and domains/codomains. Currently KH is said to be an operator H→H, yet ϕ is a map M→H (i.e., not an element of H), so the types do not match without additional structure. One consistent option is to define the Koopman composition operator K acting on vector-valued observables h: M→H by (Kh)(x)=h(f(x)). If so, KH should be an operator on a space of H-valued functions, not on H itself.
- Proof of Theorem 1: several steps rely on non-square or infinite-dimensional objects as if they were invertible matrices. For instance, writing F = R−1PτR and “MτF = KHMτ” suggests an identification M−1τ KHMτ that needs justification, given Mτ has τ columns in an infinite-dimensional space. The emergence of a companion matrix and Vandermonde diagonalization assumes minimal polynomial/shift structure and distinct Ritz values; these assumptions should be explicitly stated and the derivation made precise (e.g., via Arnoldi recurrences in a Hilbert space with bounded composition operator).
- The phrase “apply KH to both sides” around lines 144–157 is unclear: what are the objects (scalars, H-elements, or functions) on each side? Please rewrite those steps with precise types and inner products.
- The assumptions underlying Theorem 1 are strong but not explicit. For example, when do we have ψ=0 (i.e., ϕ(xτ)∈span{ϕ(x0),…,ϕ(xτ−1)})? Under what conditions on f, the kernel, and the data does the Ritz spectrum converge to the Koopman spectrum? No consistency or approximation error bounds are provided.
- The robust variant (Section 4) uses kernel PCA with Ḡ’s eigendecomposition and inverses of S̄. Please specify centering, handling of small eigenvalues (pseudoinverse vs truncation), and regularization (e.g., Tikhonov) to guarantee numerical stability. As written, steps that use Ḡ−1 implicitly assume full rank.
- Computational aspects are not analyzed. Memory/time complexity of forming Gram matrices O(τ^2) and eigendecompositions O(τ^3) should be discussed, including scalability strategies (e.g., Nyström, random features).

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear, but key notational issues (domain/codomain of KH and of ϕ, distinction between H and spaces of H-valued functions) should be fixed early in Section 3. A clean separation between scalar observables g∈Hk (RKHS of k), vector-valued observables, and the feature map would help.
- Please outline a consistent framework (e.g., assume scalar RKHS Hk, observables g∈Hk, define K on Hk, and use kernel trick to compute inner products of g evaluated on data) or, if using H-valued observables, cite standard results on composition operators on Bochner spaces and state boundedness assumptions.

Originality
- Framing DMD in an RKHS and operating with Gram matrices is closely related to EDMD and KDMD [35]. The paper cites EDMD but the connection could be discussed more deeply: what is novel beyond EDMD with a kernel dictionary and POD truncation? Is the Krylov/QR viewpoint leading to different estimators or guarantees? A direct empirical comparison to EDMD/KDMD would strengthen the case for originality.

Significance
- The approach is potentially useful to practitioners who already rely on DMD but need richer features. However, the empirical section is limited in scope and lacks comparisons to strong baselines (exact DMD, EDMD/KDMD, optimized DMD). No quantitative metrics (e.g., reconstruction error, prediction error, classification accuracy) are reported against baselines, which limits claims of improved performance.
- The applications (pre-image-based prediction, dynamics recognition via kernel principal angles, change-point detection) are interesting, but methods (e.g., pre-image via MDS-like reconstruction) are heuristic; more rigorous evaluation would help.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Clarify Equation (7) versus (2): explicitly define the function spaces and make the purpose of (7) clear (i.e., moving to vector-valued observables in RKHS).
- Make explicit all assumptions in Theorem 1 (distinct Ritz values, rank conditions, boundedness/compactness of KH, invertibility of R) and ensure matrix operations are well-posed in infinite dimensions.
- In Theorem 2, specify when κj∈span(U) holds and whether the assumption φj∈Φ is realistic; discuss how truncation (p<τ) impacts bias.
- Provide comparisons to EDMD/KDMD and exact/optimized DMD on the same datasets with quantitative metrics.
- Discuss kernel choice and hyperparameter selection; show sensitivity analyses.
- Add regularization in all inversions (R, S̄) and note the use of pseudoinverses when appropriate.

Typos and minor issues
- Line 55: “the the” → “the”.
- Line 153: uses κj/λj where indices should be κi/λi.
- Ensure consistent notation for complex conjugate/Hermitian transpose; define all matrices at first use.
- The statement “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” needs qualification (e.g., measure-preserving/ergodic settings).

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Timely topic; solid and practical extension of DMD to RKHS.
- Clear exposition overall; useful applications and accessible algorithm.
- Potential impact for practitioners analyzing nonlinear dynamics.

Cons:
- Theoretical development (KH, Theorem 1) has type/assumption ambiguities and informal derivations.
- Limited empirical evaluation and lack of comparison to EDMD/KDMD and other DMD variants.
- Novelty relative to EDMD/KDMD not fully articulated.

Overall recommendation
Borderline. If the authors clarify the operator-theoretic setup and proofs, explicitly state and justify assumptions, and include quantitative comparisons to EDMD/KDMD and exact/optimized DMD, the paper would be a strong contribution. As it stands, the promise is high but key theoretical and empirical gaps remain.
