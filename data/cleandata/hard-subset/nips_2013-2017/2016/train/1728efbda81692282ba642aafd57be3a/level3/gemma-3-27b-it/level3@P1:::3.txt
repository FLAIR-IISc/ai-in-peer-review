This paper presents a novel approach to spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to operate within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to leverage the power of kernel methods to approximate the Koopman operator's eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions, potentially offering advantages over traditional DMD's reliance on linear monomials. The authors propose an algorithm based on calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition. They also address potential numerical instability by incorporating a projection onto POD directions. The method is demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world data with applications to prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

The work builds on the recent resurgence of interest in Koopman operator theory and DMD (Bagheri & Schlatter, 2016; Schmid, 2010), which offer a data-driven approach to understanding the global behavior of nonlinear systems. It also connects to established techniques in kernel methods (Schölkopf et al., 1998) and subspace identification (Van Overschee & De Moor, 1996), presenting a bridge between these fields.  The paper is situated within a growing body of literature aimed at improving the robustness and applicability of DMD, with other approaches including optimized DMD (Chen et al., 2012) and sparsity-promoting DMD (Jovanović et al., 2017).

**Strengths:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly articulate the motivation, theoretical background, and algorithmic details.  The exposition of the core concepts, particularly the extension of DMD to RKHS, is commendable. The inclusion of applications beyond basic spectral analysis—prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection—demonstrates the potential utility of the proposed method.  The technical foundation appears sound, and the numerical results, while not exhaustive, illustrate the feasibility of the approach. The authors also correctly identify the connection to POD as a means of improving numerical stability, echoing concerns and solutions found in related DMD literature.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite its strengths, the paper suffers from some clarity and rigor issues. Specifically, Section 3.1, culminating in Equation (7), and the subsequent proof of Theorem 1, require substantial clarification. The purpose and distinction between Equation (7) and the standard Koopman operator definition in Equation (2) are unclear.  The mathematical treatment raises concerns.  KH is defined as an operator acting on functions in H, while φ is a mapping from the state space M to H.  This implies φ itself might not necessarily reside in H, a crucial assumption that needs explicit discussion. 

The proof of Theorem 1 contains a step described as “applying the operator KH to both sides” (lines 144-157) which is ambiguous given that both sides appear to be scalar quantities, making the application of an operator unclear. Furthermore, the assumptions underpinning Theorem 1 are stated weakly and several additional implicit assumptions appear within the proof itself, the validity of which is not adequately justified. This lack of precision undermines confidence in the theoretical results.  Minor typos throughout (e.g., line 55 “the the,” line 153) detract from the polished presentation, though they do not fundamentally impact understanding.  

Finally, while the paper presents empirical results, the comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods is limited. Demonstrating a clear advantage over other DMD variants or traditional system identification techniques would significantly strengthen the contribution.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of DMD using kernel methods.
*   Clear and well-written exposition of the theoretical framework.
*   Demonstration of potential applications beyond spectral analysis.
*   Addresses numerical stability concerns with a POD-based projection.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity and rigor in the mathematical derivations, particularly in Section 3.1 and the proof of Theorem 1.
*   Unjustified assumptions and ambiguous steps in the proof.
*   Limited comparison to existing methods.
*   Minor typographical errors.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach to spectral analysis with the potential to advance the field. However, the concerns regarding mathematical clarity and rigor cannot be overlooked. I believe the paper, in its current form, is borderline. If the authors can address the identified weaknesses—specifically clarifying the mathematical justifications and providing more thorough comparisons—it would be a strong contribution to NIPS. **I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the concerns raised in this review.** The core idea is valuable, but requires more rigorous development and validation.
