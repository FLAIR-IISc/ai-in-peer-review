

This paper proposes a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors introduce an algorithm that approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using nonlinear kernel-based basis functions instead of the linear monomials used in traditional DMD, theoretically extending the method to richer functional representations. The approach is validated on synthetic and real-world datasets, with applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to Koopman spectral analysis, with clear theoretical development of the kernel DMD algorithm and its robustified variant using POD bases. The theoretical foundation is well-supported by mathematical derivations (Theorems 1 and 2), and the experimental results demonstrate reasonable performance on benchmark systems like the toy nonlinear system and Hénon map.

However, several significant weaknesses limit the paper's impact. First, the distinction from Williams et al. (2015) is unclear, particularly regarding their extended DMD approach, which is nearly identical to the robustified variant described in Section 4. The authors should explicitly compare and contrast their work with this prior contribution. Second, the paper omits key prior work in kernel-based dynamical systems (e.g., Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Álvarez et al.), which undermines the claimed novelty. Third, the experiments lack ambition: prediction is standard, and the paper fails to compare with time-series clustering methods (kernel k-means, subspace kernels) or state-of-the-art online kernel change-point detection. The absence of stochastic case analysis also limits the applicability to real-world systems.

While the method shows efficiency gains over traditional DMD, the paper's significance is diminished by these omissions. The minor typos noted (tau-1 vs. tau indexing and k(xi.xj) notation) further reduce clarity. The practical implications of using consecutive observable pairs instead of full sequences remain inadequately explained, particularly for multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relations.

Pro: Solid theoretical foundation, clear algorithmic presentation, reasonable experimental validation on simple systems.
Con: Unclear novelty relative to Williams et al. (2015), missing key literature, limited experimental scope, insufficient discussion of practical implications.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough literature review, explicit comparison with Williams et al., expanded experimental validation including stochastic settings and relevant baselines, and clearer discussion of practical limitations. As currently written, it represents an incremental contribution rather than a significant advance for the field.
