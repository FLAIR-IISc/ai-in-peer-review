- Proposes kernelized DMD/Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS using Krylov subspaces of feature maps to compute Ritz eigenpairs via orthogonalization and projected eigendecomposition.  
- Presents two algorithmic variants: Gram–Schmidt/QR via Gram matrices and a numerically robust kernel-PCA (POD)-projected operator (akin to exact/optimized DMD).  
- Claims the computed eigenvalues/modes approximate Koopman spectra and yield features for prediction (via pre-image), dynamic pattern recognition (subspace angles), and change-point detection.  
- Empirical demonstrations: toy system, Hénon map, motion recognition embedding, and a single change-point example.  
- Paper is conceptually close to EDMD and kernel-EDMD; authors note connections to DMD variants, spectral learning, and subspace ID.  
- Mathematical development is mostly sound and Theorems link empirical Ritz objects to Koopman spectra under specific assumptions.  
- Key theoretical assumptions are strong: Koopman eigenfunctions must lie in the RKHS data span; kernel-PCA truncation can remove important dynamics; "full recovery" claims ignore noise and finite-sample/non-invariance issues.  
- Kernel-PCA robustification mirrors standard DMD practice but the paper lacks explicit regularization strategies (e.g., Tikhonov/ridge) and complexity analysis.  
- Pre-image-based prediction is heuristic and potentially brittle in practice.  
- Change-point detection derivation drops log-partition contributions and requires justification, calibration, and quantitative validation.  
- Exposition and proof sketches are clear, but notation is heavy and the distinction between H-eigenfunctions and scalar eigenfunctions should be clarified.  
- Algorithmic presentation needs pseudocode and explicit complexity/memory cost statements.  
- Empirical sections omit important details (kernel width selection, truncation criteria, noise levels) and lack strong baselines and quantitative metrics.  
- Novelty is incremental relative to EDMD/kernel-EDMD; RKHS/Krylov framing is a nice unifying perspective but not fundamentally new.  
- Significance is moderate: RKHS formulation is relevant to ML and dynamics communities, but limited empirical evidence and benchmarks reduce impact.  
- Empirical evaluation is small-scale, qualitative, lacks comparisons to EDMD/optimized DMD/subspace ID/GPDM/RNNs, and does not assess scalability (O(τ^2) memory, O(τ^3) compute).  
- Pros: clear operator-theoretic RKHS formulation, bridges Koopman and kernel methods, practical implementation details and public code, unified feature-extraction viewpoint.  
- Cons: limited novelty, weak experimental validation, strong assumptions without sample/noise analysis, heuristic pre-image/change-detection, and missing discussion of scalability/hyperparameter selection/regularization.  
- Suggested improvements: explicitly contrast with EDMD/kernel-EDMD; add quantitative benchmarks and baselines; incorporate regularization and model selection; provide consistency/error analyses as τ→∞; and expand/change-point experiments with ROC/ARL and sensitivity studies.  
- Overall: clearly written and technically competent but incremental over prior kernel-EDMD work and lacking sufficient theoretical and empirical validation to demonstrate clear advantages.
