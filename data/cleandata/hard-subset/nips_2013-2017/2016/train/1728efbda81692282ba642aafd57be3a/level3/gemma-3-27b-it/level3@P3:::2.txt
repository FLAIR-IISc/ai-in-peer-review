This paper proposes a kernelized version of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems via the Koopman operator. The core idea is to perform eigen-decomposition in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) to capture more complex dynamics than standard DMD, which relies on linear monomials. The authors demonstrate the method on synthetic data and apply it to state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition, reporting empirical results.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in spectral methods for dynamical systems. The Koopman operator framework, as highlighted, provides a way to linearize nonlinear dynamics on an infinite-dimensional space of observables. DMD, in turn, offers a finite-dimensional approximation of the Koopman operator’s eigenvectors, revealing important dynamical characteristics. The authors correctly position their work within this landscape, acknowledging existing techniques like Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD), balanced truncation, and various DMD variants (exact DMD, optimized DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD, extended DMD). The connection to subspace identification methods from control theory is also briefly noted, pointing towards potentially fruitful future research. Related kernel methods, particularly kernel PCA, are acknowledged, and the paper rightly frames its contribution as adapting DMD to leverage the power of kernel functions.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Presentation of Background:** The introduction and Section 2 effectively summarize the Koopman operator, DMD, and their theoretical foundations. The discussion of existing methods provides useful context.
* **Mathematically Sound:** The derivation of the kernelized DMD algorithm (Section 3) appears logically sound, and Theorem 1 establishes a clear connection to standard DMD. The inclusion of a robustification step using POD (Section 4) is a pragmatic addition, addressing a potential numerical issue.
* **Comprehensive Applications:** The paper explores a range of applications – prediction, embedding/recognition, and change-point detection – demonstrating the versatility of the proposed method.
* **Empirical Validation:** While not groundbreaking, the empirical results on the toy system and Hénon map provide some evidence that the method functions as expected.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** The central contribution – kernelizing DMD – feels incremental. The core algorithm (DMD) is well-established, and applying kernel methods is a natural extension. The paper doesn't fundamentally alter the underlying approach.
* **Uncompelling Applications:** The presented applications, while broad, don’t convincingly demonstrate advantages over existing methods. Prediction is a common benchmark but lacks a rigorous comparison. The dynamics recognition example, though visually suggestive, lacks quantitative evaluation. Change-point detection provides a slightly more novel application but the comparison method is not state-of-the-art.
* **Lack of Comparative Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison with competing techniques. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), for example, are a powerful alternative for learning dynamical systems, and a comparison would be valuable. Similarly, more sophisticated change-detection methods could be benchmarked.
* **Implementation Details:** The choice of kernel parameters (degree for the polynomial kernel, width for the Gaussian kernel) seems somewhat arbitrary and lacks sensitivity analysis. Justification for these choices would strengthen the empirical results.
* **Clarity in Notation:** At times, the notation is dense and could be simplified. The relationship between different formulations of the Koopman operator and its discrete-time approximation could be explained more clearly.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound extension of a known algorithm to a richer function space.
* The mathematical derivations are well-defined and provide a clear framework for understanding the kernelized DMD.
* The exploration of multiple applications indicates a potential for broader impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the approach is limited, and it doesn’t represent a significant breakthrough.
* The applications are not particularly compelling and lack a robust comparison to existing methods.
* The empirical evaluation could be strengthened with more rigorous benchmarks and sensitivity analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a solid technical contribution, but its limited novelty and lack of compelling applications reduce its overall impact. While the method is likely to be useful to researchers already working in the field of spectral methods for dynamical systems, it doesn't offer a paradigm shift or address a critical gap in the literature. I would rate this as a **weak accept**. Further strengthening the empirical evaluation with more comprehensive comparisons and more compelling applications would significantly improve the paper’s standing. I suggest the authors address the comparative analysis and clarity concerns before a final decision is made.
