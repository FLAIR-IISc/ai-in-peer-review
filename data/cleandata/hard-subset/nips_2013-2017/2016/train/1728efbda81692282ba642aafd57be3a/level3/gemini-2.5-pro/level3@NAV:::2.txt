This paper proposes a method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to leverage the Koopman operator, which linearly represents nonlinear dynamics in an infinite-dimensional function space. The authors formulate a kernel-based algorithm that performs an eigen-decomposition of the Koopman operator from observed trajectories, effectively using kernel PCA and subspace analysis ideas. This approach connects the well-established DMD method from the dynamical systems literature (e.g., Schmid, 2010; Rowley et al., 2009) with the powerful framework of kernel methods, familiar to the NIPS audience. The work is related to extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), which also uses richer basis functions.

The primary strength of the paper is its elegant framing of function composition as a linear operator in an RKHS, to which spectral decomposition can be applied. This provides a novel and insightful perspective for readers familiar with kernel methods and presents a technically sound algorithm for extracting dynamic modes from nonlinear systems. The method is demonstrated on several applications, including system embedding and recognition, with promising empirical results on the chosen datasets.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that limit its impact. The main concern is originality. The core algorithmic contribution is the kernelization of DMD, an existing and well-known algorithm. While this is a valid and useful extension, it feels more incremental than a fundamental advance. The connection and distinction from closely related work like Extended DMD could also be clarified further.

Furthermore, the significance of the work is not fully established by the experimental evaluation. For the applications of system recognition and change-point detection, the paper fails to compare against strong, standard machine learning baselines. For instance, recurrent neural networks (RNNs) are a dominant approach for modeling and classifying sequential data, and various robust change-detection methods exist. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the practical advantages of the proposed kernel DMD approach. The chosen applications do not compellingly demonstrate scenarios where this method is uniquely suited or offers a significant performance benefit over established techniques.

In summary, while the paper is technically solid and presents an interesting fusion of ideas, its limited novelty and an unconvincing experimental evaluation weaken the overall contribution.
