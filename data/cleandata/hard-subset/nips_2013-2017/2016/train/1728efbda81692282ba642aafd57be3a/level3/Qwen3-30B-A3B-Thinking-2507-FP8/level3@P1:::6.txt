

This paper proposes a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors extend the Koopman operator to a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), developing an algorithm that computes orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performs eigendecomposition of the projected operator. The method is framed as a feature extraction procedure for nonlinear dynamical systems, with applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper is clearly written and pedagogically valuable, especially for readers unfamiliar with Koopman operator theory. It effectively connects the method to existing work in spectral analysis (POD, DMD) and kernel methods (kernel PCA), providing a well-structured theoretical foundation. Theorems 1 and 2 establish the mathematical connection between the proposed kernel DMD and Koopman spectral theory, and the robustification step using POD bases addresses numerical instability concerns common in DMD implementations.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in empirical validation and practical relevance. The empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic examples (a damped 1D system and Hénon map) and a small locomotion dataset, with insufficient comparisons to state-of-the-art methods. The authors fail to include standard DMD performance on the toy example or Hénon map to demonstrate the kernel approach's advantage. For the locomotion data, the recognition results show marginal improvement over baselines but lack statistical significance and fail to demonstrate clear superiority over simpler techniques like kernel PCA.

The most significant limitation is the lack of compelling machine learning applications. While the authors suggest applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition, these examples are superficial and fail to show how this method outperforms existing approaches in practical ML settings. The deterministic-only setup (no noise or stochastic components) is particularly problematic for machine learning applications where uncertainty and noise are ubiquitous. The paper does not address how the method would handle real-world data with measurement noise or partial observability.

Regarding originality, the kernelized extension of DMD is a natural idea, but the paper doesn't clearly articulate how it differs from existing kernel-based approaches. The connection to Williams et al.'s extended DMD is mentioned but not thoroughly explored. The theoretical results appear to follow straightforwardly from existing RKHS literature on linear operators, which has been well-established in machine learning.

The significance of the work is limited by the lack of concrete ML applications and insufficient empirical validation. The authors should identify a specific ML domain (e.g., robotics, time-series forecasting) where their method provides a clear advantage and demonstrate this with rigorous experiments. The paper would benefit from including standard DMD performance on their examples to highlight the kernel approach's benefits.

The paper's clarity is excellent, with well-structured exposition and accessible mathematical notation. However, the theoretical contributions are not sufficiently novel or impactful for NIPS. The method appears to be a straightforward extension of existing DMD and kernel methods without demonstrating clear advantages in machine learning applications.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) provide more thorough comparisons with state-of-the-art methods in dynamical system analysis; (2) include a concrete ML application where the kernel DMD provides demonstrable advantages; (3) address how the method handles noise and uncertainty; and (4) clarify the practical computational advantages of using kernel methods versus standard DMD.

The paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD to RKHS, but its contribution to machine learning is limited by insufficient empirical validation and lack of compelling applications. While the theoretical foundation is solid and the presentation is clear, the work does not advance the state of the art sufficiently for NIPS. The authors should substantially strengthen their empirical evaluation and application demonstration to justify publication.
