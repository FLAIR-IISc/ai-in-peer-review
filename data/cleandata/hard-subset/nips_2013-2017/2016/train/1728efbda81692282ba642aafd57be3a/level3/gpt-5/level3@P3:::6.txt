Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors formulate the Koopman operator in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), approximate its spectral decomposition from finite data via a Krylov subspace/Arnoldi viewpoint, and give two algorithms: (i) a direct KDMD using Gram–Schmidt QR in feature space via kernel evaluations; and (ii) a robust version that projects onto kernel PCA (POD) directions before estimating the projected Koopman operator. The method returns eigenvalues and modal vectors in RKHS and is used as a feature extractor for prediction (via a preimage step), dynamics recognition, and change-point detection. Demonstrations include a simple damped nonlinear 2D system, the Henon map, and CMU mocap locomotion.

The paper sits at the intersection of Koopman/DMD methods (Schmid; Rowley & Mezic; exact/optimized/sparse DMD; EDMD), Krylov/Arnoldi theory, and classical kernel methods (kernel PCA; kernel ICA; kernel exponential families; kernel subspace distances). It is especially close to Extended DMD (EDMD) and kernel EDMD, which approximate Koopman spectra in a space spanned by user-chosen features; the present work chooses an RKHS and leverages the kernel trick to avoid explicit feature maps, with a POD truncation for numerical robustness.

Quality
- Theory: The derivations are technically sound and consistent with known results on linear operators in Hilbert spaces, Ritz values/vectors from Krylov subspaces, and kernel-PCA-based projections. Theorems 1–2 are reasonable under stated assumptions (richness of data; φj in the span). That said, the theoretical contributions are incremental: most steps (projection of Koopman to a Krylov subspace; kernelizing via Gram matrices; POD truncation) mirror standard results in Arnoldi/EDMD and RKHS operator literature. No sample-complexity, stability, or noise robustness guarantees are provided.
- Algorithms: The procedures are clear and implementable. Numerical concerns (ill-conditioning) are addressed via kernel POD, analogous to exact/optimized DMD. However, scalability is not analyzed: Gram matrices scale as O(τ2) memory/O(τ3) time; no Nyström/random feature approximations are discussed.
- Experiments: Toy and Henon examples illustrate eigenvalue estimation; locomotion provides a qualitative embedding; a change-point example is included. The comparisons to DMD/PCA/kPCA are not systematic and the utility over baselines is unclear. Quantitative metrics (e.g., spectral estimation error, short-horizon prediction error, classification accuracy) and state-of-the-art baselines are largely missing.

Clarity
The paper is well written, self-contained, and pedagogical, especially for readers new to Koopman/DMD and the fluid dynamics literature. The connection between Arnoldi/Ritz values in feature space and Koopman spectra is explained cleanly, and the robust POD variant is motivated well.

Originality
Kernelizing DMD and projecting onto kernel principal components is a natural extension of EDMD and known RKHS operator techniques. The paper’s contribution is primarily in assembling a coherent kernel DMD pipeline, providing proofs tailored to RKHS, and showcasing ML-oriented applications. Theoretical results are unsurprising given existing RKHS/kernels and EDMD literature, and related work on EDMD/kernelized variants should be discussed and compared more directly.

Significance
As a methodological bridge between Koopman/DMD and kernel methods, the work is interesting, but its practical impact for ML remains unclear from the current experiments. The paper would be stronger with convincing use cases and comparisons demonstrating meaningful advantages over DMD, EDMD, kPCA, and time-delay embeddings on realistic ML tasks with noise.

Specific comments, questions, and requests
- Comparisons: Please include standard (exact/optimized) DMD baselines on the toy system and the Henon map, reporting estimation errors of eigenvalues/eigenfunctions and sensitivity to noise/time-series length. Also compare to EDMD and kernel EDMD (if distinct), and to kPCA-only on locomotion (e.g., using kernel principal angles without Koopman estimation).
- Practical usefulness and target domain: Identify concrete ML use cases where Koopman features improve over strong baselines (e.g., robotic locomotion/gait segmentation and prediction; manipulation primitives; time-series anomaly detection in robotics; control from pixels). Report task metrics (classification accuracy, predictive RMSE, detection delay/false alarm) and state-of-the-art baselines (HMM/SLDS, GPDM, PSR/spectral methods, TICA/ICA, time-delay embeddings).
- Stochasticity/noise: The setup and theory largely assume deterministic dynamics and noise-free observations. Most ML settings have process/measurement noise. Please (i) add experiments with stochastic disturbances and measurement noise; (ii) clarify how the proposed estimators behave with noise; (iii) relate to the Koopman operator for stochastic systems and to subspace DMD/Hankel-DMD that explicitly handle noise.
- Modal decomposition and feature-space representations: Do downstream tasks require explicit preimages of modal vectors, or can one operate purely in RKHS (like kernel PCA projections)? If preimages are required to interpret modes or predict in the original observable space, this is a practical limitation given the ill-posedness of preimage problems. Please clarify when preimages are needed, and consider alternatives (e.g., linear readouts on kernels without inversion).
- Robustness and truncation: Provide guidance on choosing kernel, bandwidth, and truncation rank; ablation on these choices; and sensitivity analyses. Discuss bias–variance trade-offs vs EDMD with polynomial/radial bases.
- Scalability: Analyze computational complexity and memory, and discuss approximations (Nyström, random Fourier features) and streaming updates for long sequences.
- Evaluation clarity: The locomotion MDS plot is qualitative; please add quantitative recognition results using kernel principal angles vs baselines. For change detection, compare to kernel change-point tests and model-based detectors with proper evaluation (ROC/AUC, detection delay).

Pros
- Clear, pedagogical presentation bridging Koopman/DMD and RKHS.
- Sound algorithmic formulation with both direct and robust (POD) variants.
- Natural kernelized extension of DMD with potential utility as a feature extractor.
- Public code and illustrative demos.

Cons
- Incremental theory relative to EDMD and RKHS operator literature.
- Limited and largely qualitative experiments; unclear gains over DMD/EDMD/kPCA.
- Deterministic/noise-free focus; limited relevance to noisy ML settings as-is.
- No scalability strategy for large datasets; reliance on preimage may be limiting.

Recommendation
Leaning toward weak reject in its current form: the work is well written and technically correct but needs stronger empirical validation against state-of-the-art baselines, clearer positioning relative to EDMD/kernel EDMD, treatment of noise, and demonstration of concrete ML utility (e.g., in robotics or other dynamical inference domains). I encourage the authors to revise along the lines above; the paper could be impactful with a more compelling empirical case.
