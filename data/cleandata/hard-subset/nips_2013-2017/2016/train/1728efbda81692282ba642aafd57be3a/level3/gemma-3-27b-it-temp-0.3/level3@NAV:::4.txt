## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper presents an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to utilize reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), aiming to improve the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions beyond the limitations of linear monomials used in traditional DMD. The core idea – leveraging the expressiveness of kernel functions to capture more complex dynamics – is a natural and promising direction. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clearly explaining the mathematical foundations and algorithmic details. The presentation is logical and easy to follow, demonstrating a strong understanding of the relevant literature in both dynamical systems and machine learning.

The work builds upon the established framework of Koopman spectral analysis and DMD, connecting it to kernel methods and RKHS theory. This is a valuable contribution, as it bridges two powerful approaches. The authors correctly identify the potential for improved accuracy in representing nonlinear dynamics compared to standard DMD, and the proposed algorithm is a logical extension of the existing methods. The inclusion of applications to state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition further strengthens the practical relevance of the work. The empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real-world data provides initial validation of the method's effectiveness.

However, a key weakness is the lack of a direct comparison to non-RKHS Koopman/DMD methods on the simulation examples. While the paper demonstrates the method works, it doesn't definitively show *how much* improvement is gained by incorporating the RKHS framework. A side-by-side comparison with standard DMD on the toy system and Hénon map, using the same data and evaluation metrics, would significantly strengthen the claims. 

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of kernel choice and its impact on the results. The authors briefly mention using a polynomial kernel for the toy system and a Gaussian kernel for the Hénon map, but don’t provide justification for these choices. A small simulation study exploring the sensitivity of the results to different kernel parameters (e.g., kernel width for the Gaussian kernel, degree for the polynomial kernel) would be highly valuable. This would help users understand how to best apply the method to their own data.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of DMD to RKHS, offering potential for improved accuracy.
*   Clear and well-written manuscript, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field.
*   Empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   Potential for applications in various domains.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of direct comparison to standard DMD in simulations.
*   Insufficient discussion of kernel choice and its impact on results.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a clear contribution. Addressing the concerns regarding the comparison to standard DMD and kernel selection would significantly enhance its impact and justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
