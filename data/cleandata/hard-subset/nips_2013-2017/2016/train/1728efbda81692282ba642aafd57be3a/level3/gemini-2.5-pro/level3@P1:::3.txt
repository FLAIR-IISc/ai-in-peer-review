This paper presents an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for the spectral analysis of the Koopman operator by leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to approximate the Koopman operator and its eigenfunctions not in the space of observables, but in a high-dimensional feature space induced by a kernel function. This allows for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions with a richer set of nonlinear basis functions compared to standard DMD, which typically uses linear monomials of observables. The authors first propose a direct algorithm based on a Gram-Schmidt process in the feature space and then present a more practical and robust version that projects the data onto kernel principal component analysis (PCA) directions before performing the decomposition. The paper demonstrates the utility of the extracted Koopman modes as features for several machine learning tasks, including time-series prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection, with experiments on synthetic and real-world data.

This work builds upon a significant body of research on DMD, a technique that has gained popularity in fluid dynamics and is increasingly being explored in other fields. It connects this line of work with the well-established framework of kernel methods in machine learning. The proposed method is particularly related to Extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), which also uses a dictionary of basis functions, but the explicit use of the kernel trick here provides a principled and flexible way to work with infinite-dimensional feature spaces.

### Strengths:

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and elegant combination of Koopman operator theory and kernel methods. Extending DMD with the kernel trick is a natural and powerful idea that could significantly broaden the applicability of Koopman analysis to complex nonlinear systems where good basis functions are not known a priori. The framework for using the resulting Koopman modes as features for various downstream tasks is compelling and highlights the method's potential significance for the machine learning community.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. It provides a clear and concise background on the Koopman operator and DMD, which makes the motivation for the kernel-based extension easy to follow. The progression from the mathematically exact but potentially unstable algorithm in Section 3 to the robustified version in Section 4 is sensible.

3.  **Technical Soundness (in principle):** The high-level concept is technically sound. The robustified algorithm, which relies on kernel PCA for dimensionality reduction, is a practical approach to mitigating the numerical stability issues that often plague methods like DMD. The numerical experiments, though limited, successfully illustrate the method's capabilities and demonstrate that it can extract meaningful dynamic features.

### Weaknesses and Major Concerns:

The primary weakness of this paper lies in the clarity and rigor of its central theoretical presentation in Section 3. Several key definitions and mathematical steps are ambiguous, which makes it difficult to verify the correctness of the claims.

1.  **Unclear Definition of the Koopman Operator in RKHS:** The definition of the Koopman operator `KH` in Equation (7), `(KHϕ)(x) = ϕ ◦ f(x)`, is confusing. It appears to be a direct analogue of the standard Koopman operator from Equation (2), but its domain and codomain are not clearly specified. The paper states that `KH` is a linear operator in `H` (i.e., `H → H`), but it is defined as acting on `ϕ`, which is a map from the state space `M` to the RKHS `H` (`M → H`). The eigenfunctions `φj` of `KH` are then discussed, but their nature is ambiguous. Are they elements of `H`, or are they functions from `M` to `C` or `M` to `H`? This lack of precision makes the subsequent theoretical development difficult to assess. Theorem 2 suggests `φj` are scalar functions, which adds to the confusion.

2.  **Mathematical Inconsistency in Theoretical Claims:** The ambiguity in the definition of `KH` leads to potential mathematical inconsistencies. If `KH` is an operator on `H`, then its eigenfunctions must be elements of `H`. However, `ϕ` is a map, not an element of `H`, so applying `KH` to `ϕ` is not well-defined under this assumption. The authors need to provide a more rigorous formalization of the operator and its relationship with the feature map `ϕ`.

3.  **Unclear Proof and Unstated Assumptions in Theorem 1:** The proof of Theorem 1 is hard to follow, partly due to the aforementioned ambiguities. Furthermore, the theorem relies on several strong, unstated assumptions. For instance, the proof implicitly assumes that the Krylov matrix `Mτ` has full rank `τ`, which is necessary for the QR decomposition and the invertibility of `R`. This is a strong assumption that depends on the richness of the data sequence and the choice of kernel. These assumptions should be explicitly stated and discussed.

4.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** While the applications presented in Section 7 are interesting demonstrations, the experimental evaluation lacks rigorous comparison to state-of-the-art methods. For prediction, no baseline time-series models are compared. For change-point detection, the comparison is limited to one other kernel-based method. To establish the significance of the proposed approach, comparisons against other relevant methods, including other advanced DMD variants like Extended DMD or sparsity-promoting DMD, are necessary.

### Arguments For/Against Acceptance:

**Pro:**
*   Presents a novel and promising combination of Koopman theory and kernel methods.
*   The core idea is intuitive and could be highly significant for nonlinear system analysis.
*   The paper is well-written and provides a good introduction to the area.
*   The proposed applications demonstrate the potential utility of the method as a feature extraction tool.

**Con:**
*   The theoretical exposition in Section 3 is critically unclear and contains potential mathematical inconsistencies regarding the definition of the Koopman operator in RKHS.
*   Key assumptions for the main theorem are not stated, making it difficult to assess the validity and limitations of the method.
*   The experimental validation is preliminary and lacks comparisons to strong baselines, making it hard to judge the practical performance of the algorithm.

### Conclusion:

This paper introduces an exciting and potentially impactful idea. However, the lack of clarity and rigor in its core theoretical section is a major flaw that prevents a confident recommendation for acceptance. The mathematical framework needs to be carefully revised and clarified. If the authors can provide a rigorous and unambiguous presentation of their theory and bolster their experiments with more comprehensive comparisons, the paper would represent a strong contribution to the field. In its current form, it does not meet the standards for publication at NIPS.
