- Paper proposes kernel DMD (kDMD): extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition to RKHS to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions via the kernel trick.  
- Derives an algorithm finding an orthogonal basis for the Krylov subspace in feature space and solves an eigenproblem for a projected Koopman operator.  
- Proposes a numerically robust variant using kernel PCA (POD in feature space) to mitigate instability.  
- Frames the decomposition as feature extraction for dynamical systems and demonstrates utility in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.  
- Conceptually links DMD from physics/fluid dynamics with kernel methods from machine learning, avoiding explicit basis specification.  
- Correctly situates work relative to Extended DMD and notes similarity of the robust method to basis-function approaches; mentions links to subspace ID and spectral learning.  
- Strength: paper is well-written, organized, and provides clear background and separation of direct vs. robust methods.  
- Strength: technically novel and significant—kernelizing DMD is a powerful, principled extension with theoretical grounding (Theorems 1 and 2).  
- Strength: demonstrates practical relevance through multiple applications on synthetic and real data.  
- Weakness: ambiguous definition of the Koopman operator in RKHS—unclear distinctions between operators on feature maps vs. functions in H and the role of Ritz vectors.  
- Weakness: proof of Theorem 1 is hard to follow—unclear how KH acts on matrix Mτ and the derivation of the companion matrix F needs more rigor.  
- Weakness: unstated assumptions (e.g., linear independence of feature vectors for Mτ⁻¹) are strong and should be explicitly stated and discussed.  
- Weakness: experiments lack comparisons to relevant baselines (e.g., Extended DMD and other nonlinear time-series/system-identification methods).  
- Pro-accept: elegant, sound theoretical idea with appealing Koopman connection and relevant applications; overall a complete and compelling contribution.  
- Con-accept: significant clarity issues in theoretical formulation and proofs plus limited empirical benchmarking need to be addressed before final acceptance.  
- Minor issues: typographical errors (e.g., "the the") and notational inconsistencies in proofs (indices like i, κj, λj, δij) that should be fixed.
