

**Review**

This paper proposes a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors extend the standard DMD framework by performing decomposition in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), enabling nonlinear basis functions for representing Koopman eigenfunctions rather than the linear monomials used in traditional DMD. The method builds on established DMD approaches (Schmid, 2010; Tu et al., 2014) and extends them with kernel methods to address limitations of local linear approximations.

**Quality**  
The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical foundations. The authors provide two key theorems establishing the connection between kernel-based DMD and the Koopman operator, along with a robustified variant that addresses numerical stability through projection onto POD directions. The empirical results on synthetic data (toy system, Hénon map) and real-world applications (locomotion recognition, change-point detection) demonstrate the method's applicability. However, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), which also uses pre-determined basis functions. The paper would benefit from quantitative comparisons showing clear advantages of the kernel approach over existing methods for the same tasks.

**Clarity**  
The paper is well-organized and generally clear, with logical progression from theory to applications. The mathematical derivations are precise, though some sections (particularly Sections 3 and 4) would benefit from more intuitive explanations for readers unfamiliar with RKHS and Koopman operator theory. The algorithm descriptions are detailed and well-structured, though the connection between theoretical formulation and practical implementation could be strengthened. Figures 1-4 effectively illustrate key results, with Figure 3 particularly useful for demonstrating the dynamics recognition application.

**Originality**  
The main novelty lies in applying kernel methods to DMD for Koopman spectral analysis. While the kernel extension is mathematically valid, it represents an incremental rather than fundamental advance. The core DMD algorithm is well-established in dynamical systems literature (Schmid, 2010), and the kernel adaptation appears conceptually straightforward. The paper acknowledges related work but doesn't sufficiently distinguish this contribution from extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), which uses similar pre-determined basis functions. The claim that "our algorithm gives an approximation with a set of nonlinear basis functions" is accurate but doesn't clearly establish how this leads to significant improvements over existing approaches.

**Significance**  
The significance of the work is limited. While the applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition are relevant, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate advantages of the kernel approach over simpler or more established methods. For prediction, standard time-series models (e.g., ARIMA, RNNs) could serve as effective baselines. The change-point detection application references kernel-based methods (e.g., [7]), but doesn't show how the proposed method outperforms them. The locomotion recognition task (Fig. 3) shows promise but lacks comparison with state-of-the-art dynamic pattern recognition methods. Without clear evidence of superior performance or unique capabilities, the significance for the NIPS community is not well-established.

**Pro and Con Arguments**  
*Pro:* Well-motivated extension of DMD with strong theoretical grounding; robustified algorithm addresses practical implementation issues; relevant applications with promising empirical results; clear mathematical presentation.

*Con:* Incremental contribution rather than fundamental novelty; insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods; limited demonstration of clear advantages over existing approaches; applications don't convincingly showcase distinct benefits of the kernel approach.

**Overall Assessment**  
This paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods for Koopman spectral analysis. While the mathematical framework is rigorous and the applications are relevant, the novelty is incremental and the significance for the NIPS community is not convincingly demonstrated. The paper would benefit from stronger experimental comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and a clearer articulation of the method's distinct advantages. Given the limited novelty and insufficient demonstration of significance, I recommend rejection. However, with more thorough comparisons to existing methods and clearer demonstration of advantages, this work could be substantially strengthened for future submission.
