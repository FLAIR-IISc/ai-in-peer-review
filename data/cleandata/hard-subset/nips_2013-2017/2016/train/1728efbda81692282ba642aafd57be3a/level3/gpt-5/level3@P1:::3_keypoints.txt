- Proposes kernelized DMD by mapping states into an RKHS, building a Krylov subspace from feature snapshots, and computing a projected Koopman operator via QR/Gram–Schmidt.  
- Introduces a numerically robust variant projecting onto kernel-PCA (POD) directions to enable truncation and improved conditioning.  
- Sketches applications: short-term prediction via preimage reconstruction, sequential change-point detection (CUSUM-style), and dynamics recognition via kernel principal angles.  
- Empirical illustrations on toy systems, the Hénon map, motion capture sequences, and a Lorenz change-point task show feasibility but are small-scale and qualitative.  
- Method is closely related to EDMD/KDMD; the Krylov/QR framing and the kernel-POD robustification are the paper’s primary contributions.  
- Motivation is clear: kernels provide richer nonlinear basis functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions beyond local linearization.  
- Algorithmic framework is conceptually coherent and the robust variant mirrors best practices in DMD.  
- Locomotion dynamics-embedding example is appealing and experiments are generally well written and organized.  
- Operator-theoretic definitions and domains are imprecise, causing confusion between scalar observables, H-valued observables, and elements of H.  
- The paper alternates between calling objects “eigenfunctions of KH” and H-valued “Ritz vectors”; these should be clearly distinguished.  
- Theorem 1’s proof uses stronger unstated assumptions (noiseless data, KH mapping snapshots exactly, full column rank, invertibility/left-inverse of Mτ, distinct eigenvalues/minimal polynomial).  
- Expressions like M+ = KH Mτ and F = R−1 Pτ R = Mτ−1 KH Mτ are problematic without explicit assumptions or pseudo-inverse notation.  
- The phrase “applying KH to both sides” and some derivation steps mix scalars and functions — the operator’s action must be clarified.  
- Theorem 2’s assumption that Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the span of kernel principal directions is strong; truncation and noise make identities approximate.  
- Experiments lack quantitative baselines and metrics: no comparisons to exact DMD, EDMD (polynomial dictionaries), or KDMD; missing prediction RMSE, spectral error, reconstruction fidelity.  
- Computational complexity, memory scaling, kernel/hyperparameter selection, and regularization strategies (e.g., Tikhonov) are not discussed.  
- Section 3.1 (Eq. 7) and Theorem 1 need rewritten functional-analytic notation with explicit definitions: state space, scalar observable space, feature map, snapshot subspace, and projected operator.  
- Minor typos and notation inconsistencies (e.g., “the the”, index mismatches, conjugate/Hermitian notation, centering issues) should be fixed.  
- Novelty is moderate: kernelizing Koopman/DMD is known via EDMD/KDMD; the Krylov/QR perspective and kernel-POD robustification are useful but not radically new.  
- Significance is contingent on clarifying theory and strengthening empirical evidence; current application sketches are interesting but preliminary.  
- Requested revisions: clarify domain/codomain of KH, replace ambiguous inverses with pseudo-inverse/projection formulations, state assumptions for theorems, and indicate which equalities become approximations under noise/truncation.  
- Requested experiments: add quantitative comparisons to exact DMD and EDMD on toy and Hénon systems, report prediction performance and sensitivity analyses for preimage methods, and evaluate change-point detection against kernel baselines with false-alarm/delay metrics.  
- Requested methodological discussion: detail computational complexity, kernel/hyperparameter selection, regularization for ill-conditioned Gram matrices, and justification for approximations in the change-point model.  
- Pros: clear motivation, coherent framework, robust kernel-POD variant, interesting applications, and code availability.  
- Cons: mathematical inconsistencies and hidden assumptions, limited empirical evaluation and baselines, and unclear novelty relative to EDMD/KDMD.  
- Recommendation: Borderline — support acceptance if operator-theoretic setup and proofs are clarified, assumptions made explicit, and quantitative comparisons added; otherwise more suitable for a workshop.
