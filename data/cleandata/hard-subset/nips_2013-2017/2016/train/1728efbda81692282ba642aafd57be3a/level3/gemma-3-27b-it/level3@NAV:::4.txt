## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper presents an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to utilize reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), offering a potentially more powerful approach to Koopman spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea – leveraging the expressiveness of kernel functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions – is a logical and valuable extension of existing DMD techniques. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clearly articulating the mathematical foundations and algorithmic details. The connection to prior work in DMD, POD, and Koopman operator theory is well established, effectively situating the contribution within the broader field. 

The work’s strength lies in its theoretical soundness and clear presentation. The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of the underlying mathematical principles and provide a rigorous derivation of their algorithm. The inclusion of empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world data lends further credibility to the proposed approach. The discussion of potential applications, including prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection, highlights the practical relevance of the research. 

However, a key weakness lies in the limited comparative analysis. While the paper demonstrates the method's functionality, a direct comparison to *non-RKHS* Koopman spectral analysis (standard DMD) on the simulated data would be highly beneficial. This would clearly illustrate the advantages – or limitations – of incorporating RKHS. Specifically, showing how the kernel-based approach handles complexities where linear DMD struggles would strengthen the argument for its adoption. 

Furthermore, the choice of kernel and its impact on the results remains underexplored. A more detailed discussion – potentially supported by simulations varying kernel parameters (e.g., kernel width in the Gaussian kernel) – would be valuable. Understanding the sensitivity of the results to kernel selection is crucial for practical application.  Does a different kernel lead to more accurate mode extraction? How does the choice of kernel relate to the underlying dynamics of the system? Addressing these questions would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of DMD with clear potential benefits. The writing quality is excellent, and the empirical results are encouraging.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The lack of a direct comparison with standard DMD and limited exploration of kernel selection hinder a full assessment of the method’s advantages and robustness. 

**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the comparative analysis and kernel selection discussion. The paper represents a worthwhile contribution to the field and has the potential to advance research in Koopman spectral analysis and nonlinear dynamical systems.
