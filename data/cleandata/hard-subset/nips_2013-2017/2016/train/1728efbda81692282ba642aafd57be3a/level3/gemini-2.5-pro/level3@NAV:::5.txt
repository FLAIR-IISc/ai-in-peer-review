This paper introduces Kernel Dynamic Mode Decomposition (KDMD), a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to extend Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a technique for approximating the Koopman operator, into Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). By using feature maps, KDMD can identify nonlinear basis functions, which is a powerful generalization of traditional DMD that is typically limited to linear observables. This work connects the growing body of literature on Koopman analysis in dynamics (e.g., Williams et al., 2015) with well-established kernel methods from the machine learning community.

The main strength of this paper is its originality and significance. The proposed KDMD framework is an elegant and principled extension of DMD that holds considerable promise for data-driven feature extraction and modeling of complex nonlinear systems. The theoretical connection established between Koopman theory and kernel methods is a valuable contribution that is highly relevant to the NeurIPS audience. If made accessible, this technique is likely to be widely used and built upon.

However, the paper's primary weakness is its clarity. The manuscript is exceptionally dense, overly mathematical, and verbose, making it extremely difficult to follow for readers not already deeply expert in this specific niche. For a NeurIPS audience, the presentation is cryptic and would benefit greatly from a more pedagogical rewrite with clearer, more concise explanations. The paper's organization also needs revision. Section 4 is largely redundant, covering material from the introduction that is unnecessary in a short paper. Section 5 (Related Works) would be better integrated into the introduction and conclusion, while the overly condensed example in Section 6 should be expanded into a proper experimental section.

Minor issues include a potential typo where a "nonlinear" toy case appears to be linear. Furthermore, the initial test problems are polynomial, where guessing the appropriate kernel is straightforward. More discussion is needed on kernel selection strategies for cases where the underlying dynamics are unknown, a point better developed in Section 5. The authors should also consider citing broader work on data-driven dynamics, such as Brunton et al. (2015).

In summary, this paper presents a high-potential idea, but its current presentation is a major barrier. I strongly recommend a substantial revision focused on improving clarity and structure. With such a revision, this could be a highly impactful and citable paper.
