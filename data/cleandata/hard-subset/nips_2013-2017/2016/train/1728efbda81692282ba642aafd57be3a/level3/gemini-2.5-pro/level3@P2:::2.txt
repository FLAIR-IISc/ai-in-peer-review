This paper presents a method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) framework using kernel methods. The core idea is to analyze the Koopman operator, an infinite-dimensional linear operator that governs the evolution of observables of a nonlinear system. By operating in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), the authors develop an algorithm, Kernel DMD, to approximate the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator from finite data sequences. This provides a modal decomposition of the dynamics. The paper proposes a direct algorithm based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space and a more robust variant that first projects the data onto kernel principal components. The extracted Koopman modes and eigenvalues are then framed as features for machine learning tasks, with applications demonstrated in state prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

This work sits at the intersection of the dynamical systems literature and machine learning. The concepts of the Koopman operator and DMD are well-established in fields like fluid dynamics [25, 26], where they are used to extract dynamically relevant modes from complex data. This paper's contribution is to bring this powerful framework into the machine learning domain by "kernelizing" it, thereby allowing the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions with a rich set of nonlinear basis functions implicitly defined by the kernel. This connects DMD to spectral methods for learning, such as those based on subspace identification [15, 17].

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant Formulation:** The paper provides an elegant and principled fusion of Koopman theory with kernel methods. The insight of treating function composition (the core of a dynamical system) as a linear operator in an RKHS and applying spectral analysis is powerful. This provides a fresh perspective for the kernel methods community on modeling dynamical systems.

2.  **Bridging Communities:** This work serves as a valuable bridge between the dynamical systems community, where DMD is a popular tool, and the machine learning community. By formulating DMD in the familiar language of kernels, Gram matrices, and kernel PCA, it makes these powerful ideas more accessible to a NIPS audience.

3.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithms are well-founded. The connection between the Ritz values/vectors of the projected operator and the true Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions is clearly established in Theorems 1 and 2. The development of a robust version of the algorithm (Section 4) using a projection onto POD/kernel PCA modes is a crucial and well-justified step, as it addresses the known numerical instability issues of standard DMD and Arnoldi-type methods when dealing with rank-deficient data.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Originality:** The main novelty appears to be the application of the kernel trick to the DMD algorithm. While this is a non-trivial extension, the core algorithmic and theoretical machinery is inherited from DMD and kernel PCA. The paper acknowledges related work like "extended DMD" [35], which uses a pre-determined dictionary of basis functions. The proposed Kernel DMD can be seen as a specific instance of this more general framework where the dictionary is implicitly and flexibly defined by a kernel. The paper could be strengthened by more clearly delineating its contribution relative to this prior work.

2.  **Unconvincing Applications and Evaluation:** The experiments demonstrate the method's feasibility but do not convincingly establish its significance or superiority over existing machine learning techniques.
    *   **Prediction:** The prediction task is compared only against the ground truth, without benchmarking against standard time-series forecasting models like Gaussian Process Dynamical Models [34] or Recurrent Neural Networks (LSTMs), which are strong baselines for such problems.
    *   **Recognition of Dynamics:** The embedding and recognition application is interesting, but again lacks comparison. How does a classifier built on these Koopman features compare to end-to-end sequence models or even simpler methods using, for instance, Dynamic Time Warping kernels? Without such comparisons, it is hard to assess the practical utility of the proposed features.
    *   **Change-Point Detection:** The derivation of the change score relies on a simplifying assumption that may not always hold, and the comparison is limited to another kernel-based method. The performance gains are not quantified against a broader set of state-of-the-art change-point detection algorithms.

3.  **Clarity for a General Audience:** While the paper is technically clear for an expert, readers unfamiliar with Koopman theory or DMD might find it dense. For example, the motivation for transitioning from the mathematically elegant Gram-Schmidt algorithm (Section 3) to the POD-based one (Section 4) is due to practical numerical instability, a point that is crucial but perhaps not emphasized enough for the uninitiated.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   The paper introduces a powerful and elegant framework (Koopman analysis) to the machine learning community using the familiar language of kernel methods.
    *   The proposed method is technically sound, principled, and represents a novel feature extraction technique for dynamical systems.
    *   It successfully builds a bridge between two distinct but related fields, which can foster new research directions.

*   **Arguments for rejection:**
    *   The core contribution is arguably an incremental extension (kernelization) of an existing algorithm (DMD).
    *   The experimental evaluation is weak and lacks comparisons to relevant machine learning baselines, making it difficult to judge the practical significance and advantages of the proposed method.
    *   The applications, while interesting, feel more like proofs-of-concept than compelling use-cases that highlight a unique strength of Kernel DMD.

In conclusion, this paper presents a technically solid and interesting combination of ideas from dynamical systems and kernel methods. Its primary strength lies in its elegant formulation and its potential to introduce the NIPS community to the Koopman operator perspective. However, its novelty is somewhat limited, and the experimental section does not sufficiently demonstrate the method's practical value compared to existing, well-established machine learning approaches for the chosen application domains.
