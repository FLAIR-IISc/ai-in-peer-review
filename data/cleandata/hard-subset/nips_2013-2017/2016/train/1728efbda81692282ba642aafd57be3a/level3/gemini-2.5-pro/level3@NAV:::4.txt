This paper presents a novel method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending the analysis to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The approach effectively serves as a kernelized version of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a technique that has gained significant traction in physics and engineering. By leveraging the kernel trick, the proposed algorithm implicitly maps the system's state into a high-dimensional feature space, enabling the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions using a rich, non-parametrically defined set of basis functions. This work builds upon the foundational theory of the Koopman operator and DMD, drawing a clear connection to related techniques like Extended DMD and spectral methods for learning dynamical systems, which are of significant interest to the NIPS community.

The paper’s strengths are numerous.
**Quality & Originality:** The extension of DMD to RKHS is a technically sound and elegant contribution. The theoretical development, culminating in two distinct but related algorithms (one based on Gram-Schmidt, the other on a robust kernel PCA-based projection), is rigorous and well-justified. This novel combination of familiar techniques provides a powerful, data-driven tool for analyzing complex dynamics without requiring pre-determined basis functions.
**Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides clear background on the necessary concepts and walks the reader through the derivations in a logical manner, making the work accessible to a broad audience.
**Significance:** The method is shown to be effective on a range of applications—from prediction and change-point detection to dynamics recognition—using both synthetic and real-world motion capture data, demonstrating its practical utility and potential for broad impact.

While the paper is strong, its empirical validation could be enhanced in two key ways to solidify its contribution:
1.  **Baseline Comparisons:** The experiments convincingly show that the proposed method works, but they lack a direct, quantitative comparison to non-RKHS alternatives. Including results from standard DMD or Extended DMD (with a suitable dictionary of observables) on the simulated datasets would provide crucial context and clearly demonstrate the advantages of the kernelized approach.
2.  **Kernel Selection:** The choice of kernel and its hyperparameters is critical for any kernel-based method. The paper uses different kernels for different problems but offers little discussion on the rationale or the sensitivity of the results to this choice. An analysis, even on one of the synthetic examples, exploring how different kernels or parameter settings affect the estimated Koopman spectrum would greatly improve the paper’s completeness and practical guidance for other researchers.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting an important and well-executed idea. The identified weaknesses are addressable and primarily concern the depth of the experimental section. The work represents a significant advance in data-driven analysis of dynamical systems and is highly relevant to the NIPS audience.
