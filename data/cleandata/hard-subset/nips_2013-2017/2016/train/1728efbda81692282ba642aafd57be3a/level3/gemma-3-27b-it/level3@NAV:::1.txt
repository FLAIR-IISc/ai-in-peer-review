## Review: Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis

This paper introduces a kernel-based extension to Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis, aiming to improve the representation of nonlinear dynamics through the use of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to perform the analysis within an RKHS, allowing for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions with potentially richer, nonlinear basis functions compared to standard DMD’s linear monomial basis. The authors present a modal decomposition algorithm and demonstrate its application to both synthetic and real-world data, including prediction, dynamic pattern recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

The work builds upon the growing body of research utilizing Koopman operator theory and DMD for analyzing nonlinear systems. While DMD has gained traction, its reliance on linear approximations can limit its effectiveness in strongly nonlinear scenarios. This paper’s approach of embedding the analysis within an RKHS is a logical step towards addressing this limitation, potentially offering greater flexibility and accuracy in capturing complex dynamics. The inclusion of applications beyond simple prediction is also a positive aspect, demonstrating the potential of the extracted features for broader tasks.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Most critically, the distinction from and improvement over extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) is not sufficiently clear. A detailed, point-by-point comparison is required, specifically addressing how the RKHS formulation provides advantages beyond simply using pre-determined basis functions. The related work section is also lacking; significant contributions in kernel-based dynamical systems, such as those by Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Álvarez et al., Mouattamid & Schaback, and Franz & Schölkopf, are not discussed.  

Furthermore, the evaluation is primarily focused on deterministic cases. The performance and limitations of the proposed method in stochastic settings remain unexplored, which is a crucial consideration for real-world applications. The paper also lacks a justification for using pairs of consecutive observables rather than full sequences, especially for higher-dimensional time series where pointwise relationships might be weak.  The causal assumptions underlying this choice require clarification.  

Finally, the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited. While the presented results are encouraging, the comparisons are basic. Including comparisons to time-series clustering methods (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernels) and state-of-the-art online kernel change-point detection methods would strengthen the validation. Minor typos (indexing of τ and punctuation in the kernel definition) should be addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel application of RKHS to Koopman spectral analysis, potentially extending the capabilities of DMD.
*   Demonstrates the method’s feasibility with applications to prediction, dynamic recognition, and change-point detection.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficiently distinguishes the approach from existing work, particularly Williams et al. (2015).
*   Missing relevant literature on kernel-based dynamical systems.
*   Limited evaluation, lacking stochastic analysis and comparison to alternative methods.
*   Unclear justification for methodological choices and underlying assumptions.



**Overall Assessment:**  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The core idea has merit, but the paper requires significant improvements in clarity, related work coverage, and experimental validation before it can be considered a strong contribution to the field.
