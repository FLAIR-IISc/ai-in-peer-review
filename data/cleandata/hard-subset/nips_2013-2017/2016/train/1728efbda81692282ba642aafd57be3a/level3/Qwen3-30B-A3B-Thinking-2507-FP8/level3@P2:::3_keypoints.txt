- Proposal: novel extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition using RKHS for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems.
- Theoretical insight: algorithm approximates the Koopman operator in RKHS and uses kernel methods to provide richer nonlinear basis functions than standard DMD.
- Related work: correctly positioned relative to Williams et al. (extended DMD), Mezić, Rowley, and Kawahara (kernel methods).
- Strength — clarity: paper is well-written and clearly organized.
- Strength — theory: technically sound development with clear algorithmic steps for both basic and robustified methods.
- Strength — applications: empirical results on prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using synthetic and real data.
- Strength — robustification: POD-based robustification is well-motivated and addresses a known DMD limitation.
- Weakness — mathematical inconsistencies: confusion about domain/codomain of KH and φ (e.g., Equation 7) and problematic application in Theorem 1 proof.
- Weakness — theorem assumptions: additional/strong assumptions appear in proofs (e.g., φj(x)=⟨φ(x),κj⟩) but are not stated or justified in theorem statements.
- Weakness — comparisons: lacks comprehensive experimental comparisons to other kernel-based DMD variants and relevant spectral methods.
- Weakness — minor issues: typos and inconsistent notation (e.g., \kappaj/\lambdaj vs \kappai/\lambda_i).
- Originality: appears to be an original combination of kernel methods and Koopman spectral analysis, distinct from prior DMD extensions.
- Significance: method has potential for feature extraction in nonlinear dynamics but needs stronger empirical evidence to claim state-of-the-art advancement.
- Recommendation: promising work that requires substantial revisions to correct mathematical inconsistencies, state assumptions clearly, and improve experimental comparisons before acceptance.
- Pro/Con summary: Pro — novel approach, clear exposition, useful applications, practical robustification; Con — significant theoretical issues, weak experimental benchmarking, and unclear theorem assumptions.
