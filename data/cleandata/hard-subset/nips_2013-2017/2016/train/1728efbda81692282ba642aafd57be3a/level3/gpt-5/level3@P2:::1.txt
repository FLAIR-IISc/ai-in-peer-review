Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a kernel-based variant of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to embed observables into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) via a feature map ϕ and approximate the action of the Koopman operator KH: ϕ(x) → ϕ(f(x)) using Krylov subspaces constructed from finite sequences {ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ)}. The main algorithm orthogonalizes the feature-space Krylov matrix via Gram–Schmidt (implemented through the Gram matrix), projects KH onto this basis, and computes Ritz values/vectors as approximations of Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions. A “robustified” variant projects data onto kernel-PCA directions (analogous to POD truncation in standard DMD) to mitigate ill-conditioning. The paper illustrates the method on a toy system and the Hénon map, and discusses applications to short-term prediction via pre-image recovery, dynamics recognition using subspaces of estimated eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection via a CUSUM-style score built from kernel inner products.

Conceptually, this sits at the intersection of Koopman spectral methods, DMD (Schmid, Tu et al., Jovanovic et al.), and kernel methods (Schölkopf et al., Shawe-Taylor & Cristianini). The closest prior work is Extended DMD (EDMD; Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, 2015), which uses a dictionary of nonlinear observables to approximate the Koopman operator; kernelizing EDMD yields a KDMD procedure that also operates with Gram matrices and avoids explicit feature maps. The “robustified” approach via kernel-PCA truncation parallels “exact/optimized DMD” and SVD/POD-based stabilizations in the DMD literature.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of a kernel-based Koopman spectral method, with derivations connecting Ritz values/vectors in RKHS to Koopman spectra.
- Pragmatic robustification via kernel-PCA that mirrors best practice in DMD (POD truncation), with a fully kernelized recipe using only Gram matrices.
- Useful discussion of applications: prediction (with a concrete pre-image strategy), dynamics recognition via subspace angles, and sequential change detection.
- The method naturally accommodates non-linear observables without explicit feature engineering and can, in principle, recover richer eigenfunctions than linear DMD.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to EDMD/KDMD (Williams et al., 2015) is not made explicit. The robustification and use of kernel PCA appear very close to kernelized EDMD; a head-to-head comparison and a precise statement of differences (assumptions, operators, numerical procedures, and guarantees) are missing, especially in Sec. 4.
- Missing related work on kernel-based dynamical systems and time-series modeling. Consider citing and positioning against earlier works on kernel methods for dynamical systems/time series and RKHS operator learning (e.g., Ralaivola & d’Alché-Buc; Tuia et al.; Rojo-Álvarez et al.; Mouattamid & Schaback; Franz & Schölkopf), as well as kernel subspace/dictionary approaches for time-series clustering.
- Theoretical assumptions (e.g., Theorem 2: κj in span(U)) are strong; guidance on when these are plausible and how truncation affects bias/variance is limited.
- Experiments are modest and largely deterministic. There is no quantitative comparison against EDMD/KDMD, exact/optimized DMD, or non-Koopman baselines for prediction, recognition, or change detection. The change-point experiment compares to a single kernel method only, without state-of-the-art online detectors.
- Practicality and scalability are not assessed: Gram matrix methods scale as O(τ2) memory and O(τ3) time, which can be prohibitive. No runtime/complexity or approximation strategies (e.g., Nyström, random features) are discussed.
- The remark that pairs of consecutive observables suffice needs a careful discussion of causal and statistical assumptions (Markovianity, stationarity, ergodicity) and implications for multidimensional series with weak instantaneous coupling. One-step pairs may be insufficient for systems with memory; delay embeddings should be discussed and evaluated.

Quality
- Technically sound at a high level; the kernelization and POD-style stabilization are standard and the operator projections are well-motivated. However, claims about “fully recovering eigenfunctions” if ψ=0 are idealized; in finite data with truncation/noise, this needs nuance.
- Empirical validation is limited; no ablations (e.g., effect of kernel choice/width, truncation level), no robustness to noise, and no quantitative metrics (prediction error, clustering accuracy, detection delay/false alarm).

Clarity
- Generally well written with a clear algorithmic description and helpful pointers to kernel computations via Gram matrices.
- The relationship to EDMD/KDMD needs a dedicated subsection with equations side-by-side.
- Application sections would benefit from clearer experimental protocols and metrics.

Originality
- The kernelized Koopman/DMD idea is closely related to EDMD with kernels. The paper’s contribution seems to be an alternative derivation via Krylov subspaces in RKHS and a particular robustification. The novelty is incremental unless the authors can articulate and empirically demonstrate substantive differences.

Significance
- Potentially useful to practitioners who want a kernelized Koopman method with a clear recipe. Significance would be strengthened by comprehensive comparisons and scalable implementations, showing advantages on nontrivial benchmarks (e.g., noisy Lorenz/Rössler, fluid datasets).

Requests for clarification and additional experiments
1) EDMD/KDMD comparison: Provide a detailed theoretical and algorithmic comparison (operator being approximated, role of dictionaries vs implicit kernels, projection spaces, numerical stability), and empirical head-to-head results on all tasks.
2) Stochastic settings: Evaluate with process/measurement noise, report how eigenvalues/eigenfunctions and downstream tasks degrade; discuss known extensions of Koopman to stochastic systems and what your estimator approximates (expectation vs random operator).
3) Pairs vs sequences: State assumptions (first-order Markov, stationarity, ergodicity). Discuss when pairwise sampling is adequate, and when delay embeddings/Hankelization are needed; include experiments showing the effect of delay coordinates for systems with memory.
4) Baselines: 
- Prediction: compare to EDMD/KDMD, exact/optimized DMD, Gaussian Process dynamical models, and simple kernel autoregressors; report short-horizon RMSE/NRMSE.
- Dynamics recognition: compare to kernel k-means on Hankel features, subspace-angle methods with Hankel/DMD bases, and time-series kernels.
- Online change detection: compare to state-of-the-art kernel-based detectors (e.g., MMD-CUSUM/Scan-B, kernelized BOCPD variants) with detection delay and false alarm rates.
5) Scalability: Report computational complexity and wall-clock times; explore Nyström or random features; show how truncation p trades off speed and accuracy.
6) Pre-image: Discuss stability and complexity of the MDS-style pre-image; compare to alternative pre-image methods and to predicting directly in input space via learned regressors.

Pros
- Clear kernelized operator-projection framework with a practical robustification.
- Useful application ideas and interpretable modal decomposition.
- Conceptually appealing extension of DMD with richer nonlinear features.

Cons
- Overlaps conceptually and procedurally with EDMD/KDMD; insufficient differentiation and comparison.
- Limited, mostly deterministic experiments without strong baselines; unclear empirical advantage.
- Scalability and stochastic robustness not addressed.

Minor comments/typos
- Indexing: check occurrences of τ−1 vs τ in sequence definitions and summations (e.g., kernel PCA section).
- Typo: replace dot with comma in k(xi, xj) (last equation on p.7).
- Please standardize notation for Hermitian transpose and complex conjugation; ensure all matrices’ dimensions are clear when using centered Gram matrices.
- The statement “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is too strong outside measure-preserving settings; consider softening.

Overall
Promising kernelized Koopman/DMD formulation with clean derivations and practical stabilization. To be competitive for NIPS, the paper should clearly differentiate itself from EDMD/KDMD, strengthen its empirical evaluation with strong baselines (including stochastic/noisy cases), and discuss scalability and modeling assumptions more explicitly.
