Summary
This paper proposes a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) framework for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. Starting from time-ordered data, the authors embed observations in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), construct a Krylov matrix of feature vectors, and compute the eigen-decomposition of the Koopman operator projected onto the span of these vectors. They provide two procedures: (i) a direct RKHS Arnoldi/QR formulation yielding Ritz eigenvalues/vectors (Theorem 1), and (ii) a numerically robust variant that projects onto kernel PCA directions before estimating the projected operator (Theorem 2). The resulting eigenvalues/eigenfunctions (modes) are used for feature extraction and downstream tasks: short-term prediction via pre-image recovery, recognition/embedding of dynamical patterns using kernel principal angles between subspaces, and sequential change-point detection via a CUSUM-type rule defined from kernelized likelihood ratios. Experiments on a toy system, the Hénon map, and motion-capture locomotion sequences illustrate feasibility.

Relation to prior work
DMD and its variants have a rich literature in fluids and control (e.g., Schmid 2010; Tu et al. 2014; Jovanović et al. 2014). In machine learning, spectral learning, GPDM/GPSSM, and subspace identification are relevant. Closest to this submission are extended DMD (EDMD; Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, JNS 2015), which uses user-specified dictionaries to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, and kernel versions of EDMD/DMD that achieve implicit high-dimensional dictionaries. Additionally, kernel transfer operator methods approximate Koopman and Perron–Frobenius operators in RKHS (e.g., Klus et al., 2016/2017). The submitted approach is very close to these kernel operator approximations, differing mainly in algorithmic presentation (RKHS Arnoldi/QR vs. Gram-based operator estimation) and in the use of kernel PCA for robustness.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivations are largely standard linear algebra translated to RKHS. Theorem 1 restates the Arnoldi/Ritz relationship in feature space; Theorem 2 aligns with EDMD-style consistency under strong assumptions (eigenfunctions represented in the span of the retained kernel principal components). The assumptions (distinct eigenvalues; κj in span(U)) are restrictive and should be discussed critically.
- Rigor and evaluation: Convergence/consistency and sensitivity to noise are not analyzed. The “robustifying with POD bases” is reasonable but lacks guidance on truncation, regularization, and numerical stability (e.g., Tikhonov, pivoted QR, randomized SVD). The change-point detection section makes heuristic steps (e.g., dropping terms in the likelihood ratio) without guarantees. The pre-image method is heuristic, with no error analysis or success conditions.
- Experiments: The toy and Hénon examples are helpful sanity checks but minimal; the mocap embedding is qualitative; change detection is compared to one kernel baseline on synthetic data. No comparisons to EDMD/kernel EDMD, kernel transfer operators, Koopman-based RRR/OKDMD, GPDM/GPSSM, state-space RNNs/LSTMs, or modern spectral SSA/SFA baselines. Claims of advantage are not substantiated.
- Practicality: Computational complexity and memory scaling with time points and kernel choices are not discussed; hyperparameter selection (kernel width, rank truncation) is ad hoc; multi-trajectory handling is noted but not empirically assessed.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-organized, but there are several issues:
- Some claims need precision (e.g., “for a system evolving on an attractor, eigenvalues lie on the unit circle” is not generally true except under measure-preserving assumptions).
- Notation is occasionally inconsistent; indices and centering operations could be presented more cleanly. A concise pseudocode for both algorithms would help reproducibility.
- Typos/grammar (e.g., “robustly it”) and missing details (pre-image algorithm steps) should be fixed.
- The connection and differences to EDMD and kernel transfer operator work should be made explicit in the related work and introduction.

Originality
The main idea—approximating the Koopman operator in RKHS and extracting modes via Gram-based eigen-decomposition—is closely related to EDMD with kernels and to kernel transfer operator approaches predating or concurrent with this submission. Framing via RKHS Arnoldi/QR is a useful perspective but appears incremental. The applications (pre-image prediction, subspace-based recognition, kernel principal angles) draw on established techniques. The novelty is thus modest unless the authors can clearly delineate theoretical and practical advantages over EDMD/kernel EDMD and kernel transfer operators.

Significance
Koopman-based feature extraction is important and has seen broad uptake. A clear, efficient, and robust kernelized DMD pipeline could be impactful. However, the present empirical evidence is limited, and the lack of strong baselines and scalability analysis makes it hard to assess practical significance. Strengthening comparisons and demonstrating a compelling advantage (accuracy, robustness to noise, sample efficiency, or computational benefits) on standard benchmarks would substantially increase impact.

Pros
- Clear formulation of Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS; direct link to Krylov/Arnoldi and a numerically improved variant via kernel PCA.
- Conceptually attractive feature-extraction view; reasonable downstream applications.
- Provides code and illustrative examples.

Cons
- Limited novelty versus EDMD/kernel EDMD and kernel transfer operator methods; related work discussion is incomplete.
- Weak theoretical guarantees and heuristic steps in applications (pre-image, change detection).
- Sparse and mostly qualitative experiments; few and weak baselines; unclear advantages over alternatives (EDMD, GPDM/GPSSM, RNNs).
- Some inaccuracies/overstatements and clarity issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify novelty relative to EDMD and kernel transfer operators; add direct empirical comparisons and ablations (with/without kernel PCA, different kernels).
- Provide complexity/memory analysis and guidance for hyperparameters and truncation/regularization.
- Add quantitative benchmarks (e.g., standard chaotic systems, real-world time series) with predictive accuracy, mode quality, and robustness to noise; include uncertainty quantification where possible.
- Strengthen the change-point detection methodology with principled scoring and baselines; provide detection delay/false alarm metrics.
- Improve clarity: add pseudocode, fix typos, refine theoretical statements (conditions for eigenvalues), expand proofs or provide references.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is well-motivated and technically competent, but the main contribution appears incremental relative to existing EDMD/kernel operator literature, and the empirical section does not convincingly demonstrate distinct advantages. Strengthening comparisons, theory, and experiments could make this a solid contribution.
