

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) by incorporating reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in nonlinear dynamical systems. The Koopman operator provides a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics, and the authors propose a kernel-based extension that uses nonlinear basis functions instead of the linear monomials used in standard DMD. This approach allows for richer approximations of Koopman eigenfunctions, potentially capturing more complex dynamics beyond the local linearization scope of traditional DMD.

The paper builds upon the foundational work of Rowley et al. (2009) on Koopman spectral analysis and DMD, and extends it using kernel methods as pioneered by Schölkopf et al. (1998). It relates to Williams et al.'s extended DMD (2015), but differs by using kernel functions to define nonlinear basis functions in the RKHS rather than pre-determined functional maps. The authors present a modal decomposition algorithm that reduces to calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. They further provide a robust variant using POD bases, similar to techniques used in standard DMD (Kutz et al., 2013).

**Strengths:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear presentation of theoretical concepts. The mathematical framework is technically sound, and the numerical experiments effectively demonstrate the approach's usefulness on synthetic and real-world datasets. The applications to state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition are compelling and well-motivated, showing practical utility beyond theoretical interest. The paper makes a clear contribution to extending DMD using kernel methods, which is a natural progression from existing work.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary concern is with the mathematical foundations presented in Section 3.1. Equation (7) defines the Koopman operator on the feature map ϕ as (KHϕ)(x) = ϕ ◦ f(x), which appears nearly identical to Equation (2) defining the standard Koopman operator. The distinction between these two operators is unclear, especially since the paper claims KH acts on RKHS while the standard Koopman operator acts on scalar functions.

A critical mathematical inconsistency arises from the definition of ϕ: ϕ is defined as a map from M to H (M is the state manifold, H is the RKHS), but in Theorem 1, ϕ(xt) is treated as an element of H. However, as a map M→H, ϕ itself does not belong to H (H contains functions, not maps). This is a fundamental issue that requires clarification.

The proof of Theorem 1 (lines 144-157) contains an unclear step: "applying the operator KH to both sides" when both sides of the equation are scalars. This phrasing is problematic since KH is an operator on H, not on scalars. The assumptions in Theorem 1 appear strong and not fully explicit; additional assumptions seem to be introduced in the proof without justification.

There are also minor issues: "the the" on line 55, and inconsistent notation on line 153 where \kappaj / \lambdaj is used instead of \kappai / \lambda_i.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its applications and experimental results, but the theoretical foundation has significant gaps that require clarification. The numerical results are convincing but lack comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written overall, but the mathematical inconsistencies in Section 3.1 significantly undermine clarity in the theoretical foundation.

**Originality:** The extension of DMD using RKHS is a meaningful contribution that builds upon existing work while introducing a novel approach for capturing nonlinear dynamics through kernel-based basis functions.

**Significance:** The method has clear potential for applications in dynamical systems analysis, particularly where traditional linear methods fail. The applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition demonstrate significant practical value.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting extension of DMD with kernel methods that shows promise in capturing complex dynamics. However, the mathematical inconsistencies in Section 3.1 and the unclear proof of Theorem 1 are serious concerns that need to be addressed before the paper can be considered for publication. The authors should clarify the distinction between the standard Koopman operator and KH, resolve the mathematical inconsistency regarding ϕ, and provide a rigorous proof of Theorem 1.

With careful revision to address the theoretical issues, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the field. As it stands, the theoretical foundation requires substantial clarification to match the quality of the practical applications demonstrated.
