### Review ###

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) framework to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to apply the Koopman operator, a linear operator that describes the evolution of observables of a nonlinear system, to feature maps in an RKHS. This "kernel trick" allows the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions implicitly defined by the kernel, overcoming the limitations of standard DMD which typically uses linear observables. The authors derive a modal decomposition algorithm based on this principle, which involves a Gram-Schmidt process in the feature space. Recognizing potential numerical instability, they also propose a robust version of the algorithm that projects the data onto kernel principal components before analysis. The paper demonstrates the utility of the extracted Koopman modes as features for tasks such as short-term prediction, recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection, with experiments on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It provides a clear introduction to the Koopman operator and DMD before presenting its novel kernel-based extension. The separation of the direct formulation from its robustified version is sensible, and the application-focused sections effectively demonstrate the potential utility of the method.

2.  **Technical Contribution (Originality and Quality):** The extension of DMD using the kernel trick is an elegant and novel contribution. It provides a principled way to move beyond linear observables and capture complex nonlinear dynamics without explicitly defining a dictionary of basis functions, as required by methods like Extended DMD. The connection established between the Koopman spectral analysis and kernel PCA in the robustified algorithm is technically solid and practically important.

3.  **Potential Significance:** The work builds a valuable bridge between the data-driven fluid dynamics community, where DMD is a prominent tool, and the machine learning community. By framing Koopman analysis in the language of kernel methods, the paper makes these techniques more accessible and offers a powerful new tool for feature extraction from time-series data. The demonstrated applications are relevant and suggest a broad scope of potential impact for practitioners and researchers in various fields.

**Weaknesses and Major Concerns**

The primary weaknesses of this paper lie in the lack of clarity and potential mathematical inconsistencies in the core theoretical presentation in Section 3, which undermines the reader's confidence in the formalism.

1.  **Clarity of Operator Definition (Quality/Clarity):** There is significant ambiguity in the definition and use of the Koopman operator in RKHS, `KH`, in Section 3.1.
    *   Equation (7), `(KHϕ)(x) = ϕ ◦ f(x)`, is presented as the kernelized analogue of Equation (2). However, its formal definition is unclear. `KH` is described as a linear operator in H (i.e., H → H), but it is applied to `ϕ`, which is a map from the state space to the RKHS (M → H), not an element of H itself. This creates a mathematical inconsistency. The authors should provide a rigorous definition of the operator `KH` and its domain. Is it an operator on a space of functions that map M to H? Clarifying this is essential for the technical soundness of the paper.
    *   The distinction and relationship between the standard Koopman operator `K` (acting on scalar observables) and the proposed `KH` are not adequately explained. The authors should elaborate on what is gained by this specific formulation and how it relates to the spectral properties of the original operator.

2.  **Unclear Proof of Theorem 1 (Quality/Clarity):** The proof of Theorem 1, a central result, is difficult to follow and appears to contain problematic steps.
    *   The statement "Since `F = R−1PτR = M−1τ KHMτ`" is a critical step, but the term `M−1τ` is ill-defined, as `Mτ` is a non-square matrix of feature vectors. This appears to be a major flaw or, at best, a highly confusing notational shorthand that requires justification.
    *   The derivation of `V = MτT−1` as the matrix of empirical Ritz vectors is not sufficiently explained. The logic connecting the diagonalization of the companion matrix `F` to the final form of the Ritz vectors needs to be laid out more clearly for the reader to verify its correctness.

3.  **Implicit and Strong Assumptions (Quality):** Theorem 1 relies on several assumptions that are not made explicit or justified. For instance, the theorem statement assumes the empirical Ritz values (`λ̃j`) are distinct, but the conditions under which this holds are not discussed. Further assumptions about the rank of matrices and the richness of the data appear to be implicitly made throughout the derivation. A rigorous theoretical contribution should explicitly state all necessary assumptions and discuss their implications.

4.  **Limited Experimental Comparison (Significance):** While the experiments demonstrate the functionality of the proposed method, they lack comparisons to relevant state-of-the-art methods. A comparison with Extended DMD, which the authors cite as related work, would be particularly insightful for positioning the contribution. Similarly, for the application tasks (e.g., change-point detection), comparing against established baseline methods in those domains would be necessary to substantiate claims of the method's practical advantages.

5.  **Minor Typos:** There are minor typographical errors, such as "the the" on line 55 of the introduction, which suggests a final round of proofreading is needed.

**Conclusion and Recommendation**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially significant extension of DMD to RKHSs. The core idea is appealing, and the paper is generally well-written. However, the significant clarity issues and potential mathematical errors in the main theoretical section (Section 3 and Theorem 1) are a major concern that prevents a confident assessment of the work's technical soundness.

I would recommend rejecting this paper in its current form. However, I believe the work has considerable potential and would encourage the authors to revise it. A revised version should focus on:
(1) Providing a mathematically rigorous and clear definition of the Koopman operator in RKHS.
(2) Correcting and clarifying the proof of Theorem 1, especially concerning the use of matrix inverses.
(3) Explicitly stating and justifying all assumptions underlying the theoretical results.
(4) Strengthening the experimental evaluation with comparisons to relevant baselines.

If these major issues are addressed, the paper would represent a strong contribution to the NIPS community.
