- Proposes Kernel Dynamic Mode Decomposition (KDMD) to extend DMD into RKHS for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems.
- Frames the Koopman operator as a linear operator on RKHS observables and develops an algorithm computing orthogonal bases for the RKHS Krylov matrix and eigendecomposition of the Koopman projection.
- Generalizes traditional DMD by using kernel functions instead of pre-specified basis functions to capture more complex nonlinear relationships.
- Demonstrates KDMD on prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition with synthetic and real-world data (e.g., Hénon map, locomotion data).
- Technically sound contribution with a rigorous mathematical framework and well-structured theorems linking the algorithm to Koopman spectral analysis.
- Builds on Williams et al.'s extended DMD but provides a more general framework that avoids specifying basis functions.
- Empirical results show competitive performance versus existing methods across multiple domains.
- Presentation suffers from excessive mathematical verbosity and notation that obscures core contributions.
- Section 4 (POD bases) is largely redundant and could be removed to tighten the paper.
- Section 5 (Related Works) should be condensed and integrated into intro or conclusion rather than a separate section.
- Section 6 (Empirical Examples) is overly condensed and lacks detail, especially the "nonlinear" toy example that appears linear.
- Paper lacks a clear, pedagogical explanation of when KDMD is most useful (e.g., when a kernel/feature map can be reasonably guessed).
- Strong significance for dynamical systems analysis but current writing prevents broader NeurIPS audience uptake.
- Recommendation: substantially rewrite to prioritize clarity and conciseness, focusing on key insights and practical implications.
- Pro-accept: novel, timely extension of DMD to RKHS with sound math and demonstrated utility across applications.
- Con-accept: overly technical and verbose presentation requires significant revision before being suitable for NeurIPS.
