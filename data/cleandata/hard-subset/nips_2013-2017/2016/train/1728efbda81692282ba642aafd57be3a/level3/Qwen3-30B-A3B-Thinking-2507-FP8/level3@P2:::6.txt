

# Review for "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors build upon the established connection between DMD and the Koopman operator, which is an infinite-dimensional linear operator on observables that captures the dynamics of nonlinear systems. While standard DMD uses linear monomials as basis functions (analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion), the proposed kernelized approach leverages the expressiveness of kernel functions to provide nonlinear basis representations. The paper presents a modal decomposition algorithm that involves calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the projection of the Koopman operator onto the subspace spanned by these bases. The authors further demonstrate applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using extracted features.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written, making complex concepts accessible to readers unfamiliar with fluid dynamics. The mathematical derivations are sound and follow logically from previous work on Koopman spectral analysis and DMD. The authors properly contextualize their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers on Koopman operator theory, DMD variants, and kernel methods. The connection to kernel PCA and the extension of previous DMD frameworks (such as Williams et al.'s extended DMD) is well-established.

**Quality**: The technical content is sound and the algorithm is mathematically well-justified. The theoretical results are correct, though not particularly surprising given the established literature on kernel methods for linear operators. The paper would benefit from more rigorous experimental comparisons to standard DMD and kernel-based methods. The empirical examples on the toy system and Hénon map are well-chosen, but the comparison with standard DMD on these examples is missing, making it difficult to assess the relative performance of the proposed method. The locomotion data experiments are promising but lack comparison to state-of-the-art methods in motion recognition.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the explanation of complex concepts is pedagogical. The authors provide clear algorithmic descriptions and sufficient detail for readers to understand the implementation. The figures are informative and well-labeled, though the presentation of empirical results could be improved with more detailed performance metrics.

**Originality**: The paper extends DMD using kernel methods, which is a natural progression from prior work. However, the novelty is somewhat incremental. The connection to kernel PCA and the extension of Williams et al.'s extended DMD is clear, but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contributions from existing kernel-based approaches to dynamical systems. The theoretical results would benefit from more explicit discussion of how they differ from prior kernel-based spectral analysis methods.

**Significance**: The significance of this work for the machine learning community is somewhat unclear. While the paper demonstrates applications in dynamics recognition and change-point detection, it fails to convincingly demonstrate why this kernelized approach is superior to existing methods for these tasks. The deterministic-only setup of the algorithm is a limitation for practical machine learning applications, where stochasticity and noise are common. The authors would strengthen the significance by identifying concrete ML use cases where this method provides clear advantages over existing approaches (e.g., robotics or time-series analysis with complex dynamics), and by providing more substantial comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.

**Key concerns**:
1. The paper does not sufficiently clarify whether the modal decomposition requires explicit feature-space representations, which would limit scalability compared to kernel PCA.
2. The practical usefulness for machine learning is not convincingly demonstrated; the authors need to identify concrete applications where this method provides advantages.
3. Missing comparisons to standard DMD on the toy example and Hénon map make it difficult to assess the method's relative performance.
4. The deterministic-only setup limits relevance to real-world ML applications where noise and stochasticity are prevalent.

**Arguments for acceptance**: The paper presents a clear extension of DMD using kernel methods, with sound mathematical foundations. The writing is excellent, and the method is well-suited for applications in dynamics analysis. The connections to existing work are well-established, and the paper provides a valuable pedagogical resource for readers unfamiliar with Koopman spectral analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance**: The significance for machine learning is not sufficiently demonstrated, and the method appears to be an incremental extension rather than a substantial advance. The empirical results lack meaningful comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, making it difficult to assess the practical value of the proposed approach. The deterministic-only setup limits its applicability to real-world ML problems.

**Recommendation**: I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it fails to convincingly demonstrate the significance of the proposed method for the machine learning community. The authors need to substantially strengthen the empirical evaluation with comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, clarify the practical advantages of their approach, and address the limitations of the deterministic-only setup. The paper would benefit from a stronger focus on concrete machine learning applications where this method provides clear advantages over existing approaches.
