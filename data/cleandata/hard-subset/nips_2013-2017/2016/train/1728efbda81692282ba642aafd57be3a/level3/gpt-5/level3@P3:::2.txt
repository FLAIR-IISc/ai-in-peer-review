Summary
The paper proposes a spectral method for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems by approximating the Koopman operator in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The core idea is to treat the composition operator g → g∘f as a linear Koopman operator acting on observables, which makes repeated compositions (e.g., x(t+2) = f(f(x(t)))) amenable to linear spectral analysis. Algorithmically, the authors adapt Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to RKHS: they form a Krylov matrix from collected trajectories in feature space, compute an orthonormal basis (via Gram–Schmidt) and then perform eigendecomposition of the operator projected onto this subspace to obtain approximate Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes. To improve numerical stability, they propose a robust variant that projects onto kernel PCA (POD) directions before estimating the spectrum, tying the method to subspace-analysis ideas familiar in kernel methods.

Empirically, the paper demonstrates: (i) toy and Hénon-map examples for eigenvalue recovery; (ii) a short-horizon prediction procedure via a pre-image step; (iii) embedding/recognition of dynamics using subspace distances (kernel principal angles) between estimated Koopman eigenfunction subspaces; and (iv) a change-point detection application using a CUSUM-style statistic built from kernelized predictions. Results suggest the approach can extract meaningful modes and perform reasonably in these tasks.

Relationship to prior work is discussed: DMD and its variants (exact/optimized/sparsity-promoting DMD), extended DMD (EDMD, with prescribed dictionaries), POD/balanced truncation, kernel PCA, and subspace identification. In particular, the present contribution can be seen as “kernelized DMD” performed directly in the feature space, with a numerically robust implementation via kernel PCA.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual framing: compositions as a linear operator; dynamical systems as repeated compositions. This perspective will be intuitive to kernel-method readers and places Koopman analysis on familiar ground.
- Methodologically sound extension: casting DMD in RKHS via Krylov subspaces and orthogonal projections is natural and mathematically clean. Theorems connecting Ritz values/vectors and Koopman spectra are clearly stated.
- Practical robustness: the kernel PCA/POD step addresses ill-conditioning and enables truncation to dominant components, which is important in noisy or rank-deficient regimes.
- Breadth of applications: beyond modal analysis, the paper shows how the extracted Koopman features can be used for prediction, embedding/recognition via subspace angles, and change detection. Code availability helps reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Originality is incremental. Dynamic Mode Decomposition is well-established; EDMD (and kernelized variants) already propose moving beyond linear monomials by working with richer dictionaries or kernels. The present approach largely consolidates these ideas (Koopman+DMD+kernel PCA) rather than introducing a fundamentally new paradigm. The paper acknowledges related work (e.g., EDMD), but the positioning versus kernel EDMD and other recent Koopman-learning methods is not sharply distinguished.
- Theory stops short of finite-sample error guarantees. The key assumptions (e.g., eigenfunctions lying in the span of the feature map; data “rich enough” so ψ≈0) are strong and common in this literature, but the paper does not quantify approximation error, sample complexity, or robustness to noise/model mismatch.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and not compelling. The examples (toy system, Hénon map, MoCap embedding via kernel principal angles, change-point detection) do not clearly demonstrate advantages over strong alternatives. For prediction and recognition, comparisons against contemporary baselines such as GPDM, spectral subspace identification, PSRs, and recurrent neural networks (LSTM/GRU) are missing. For change detection, more competitive methods (e.g., BOCPD, MMD-based tests) should be included with careful calibration. The current experiments are small-scale and qualitative.
- Practical guidance is sparse. There is little analysis of kernel choice, bandwidth selection, the number of modes retained, sensitivity to hyperparameters, or computational complexity/scalability. Gram matrices grow with trajectory length, and no approximations (Nyström, random features) are discussed.

Quality
- The algorithmic development is technically sound and consistent with the DMD/Koopman literature. The robust kernel PCA variant is a reasonable numerical fix.
- Claims are qualitative and supported by illustrative experiments, but not by rigorous comparisons or quantitative ablations. No error bounds or stability analyses are provided.
- The work is a complete piece of research at the method level, but the empirical section is too thin to substantiate broad claims about practical superiority.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The exposition of Koopman/DMD and the RKHS generalization is clear.
- Suggestions for improvement: unify and summarize notation (Mτ, M+, Qτ, F, F̂) in a table; provide pseudocode for both variants; add computational complexity and memory costs; specify all kernel hyperparameters and selection procedures; clarify the pre-image construction and its limitations.

Originality
- Moderate. Treating composition as a linear operator and decomposing it is classic Koopman theory; the novelty here is largely the RKHS/DMD synthesis with a kernel PCA stabilization. The relation to EDMD (and kernelized EDMD) should be sharpened, with empirical or theoretical distinctions made explicit.

Significance
- Potentially useful to researchers who already apply DMD and want a kernelized alternative. However, without stronger evidence on real, high-dimensional tasks, the likely impact in the NeurIPS community is limited. The applications presented do not convincingly showcase unique advantages over established RNN/GPDM/subspace approaches.

Suggestions for the authors
- Provide head-to-head comparisons with EDMD (including kernelized EDMD), exact/optimized DMD, GPDM, PSR/spectral methods, and RNN baselines on prediction and recognition tasks.
- Add quantitative metrics (prediction error curves, classification accuracy, ROC for change detection) and sensitivity analyses (kernel width, number of modes, trajectory length, noise).
- Discuss computational scaling and propose approximations (Nyström/random features, streaming updates, block processing).
- Clarify theoretical assumptions; if possible, include perturbation/error analyses linking finite data, noise, and kernel truncation to spectral estimation error.
- Strengthen applications: higher-dimensional real systems (e.g., videos, robotics, fluid flows) where kernelization is crucial.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid and clean derivation; a unifying presentation of Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS.
- Practical robustification via kernel PCA and clear connections to subspace methods.
- Readable tutorial value for ML audiences unfamiliar with Koopman/DMD.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to EDMD and existing kernelized Koopman approaches.
- Limited, small-scale experiments with weak baselines; unclear practical advantage.
- No error or stability analysis; limited guidance on hyperparameters and scalability.

Overall, this is a technically sound and clearly written incremental contribution that kernelizes DMD for Koopman analysis and demonstrates several applications. To meet the bar for NeurIPS, the paper would benefit from stronger empirical validation against contemporary baselines and a clearer articulation of what is fundamentally new beyond prior EDMD/kernel Koopman work.
