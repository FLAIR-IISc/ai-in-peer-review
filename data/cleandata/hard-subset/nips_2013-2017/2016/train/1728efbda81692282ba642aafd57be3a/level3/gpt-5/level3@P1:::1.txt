This paper develops a kernelized variant of Koopman spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems. Building on Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) and the Arnoldi/Ritz viewpoint, the authors define a Koopman operator acting in an RKHS and derive an algorithm that computes orthogonal bases of the feature-space Krylov subspace from finite data, followed by eigendecomposition of the operator projected onto this subspace. A robust implementation is obtained by projecting onto kernel-POD (kernel PCA) directions, enabling truncation to mitigate ill-conditioning. Theoretical statements (Theorems 1–2) establish the relationship between empirical Ritz values/vectors and Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions under span assumptions. The paper further demonstrates three applications that treat the extracted modes as features: short-term prediction via a preimage procedure, dynamics recognition via kernel principal angles between subspaces of modes, and change-point detection via a CUSUM-like statistic derived from a nonparametric exponential family parameterized by the Koopman-predicted features. Experiments on a toy system, the Hénon map, human motion capture, and a synthetic change-point scenario illustrate feasibility.

Relation to prior work: The paper is conceptually close to DMD, exact/optimized DMD, and especially Extended DMD (EDMD; Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, 2015), which approximates the Koopman operator on a user-chosen dictionary. The present work implicitly uses a (potentially infinite) dictionary induced by a kernel, and its robust form with kernel PCA is algorithmically similar to EDMD with a kernelized dictionary. The paper situates itself among DMD variants, but the distinction from EDMD should be sharpened (see below). There is also a broader literature on kernel-based dynamical systems, system identification, and sequence kernels that deserves discussion, e.g., works by Ralaivola & d’Alché-Buc on kernels for dynamical systems/time series, Tuia et al. on kernel time-series in remote sensing, Rojo-Álvarez and colleagues on kernel adaptive filtering and system identification, Mouattamid & Schaback on kernel-based identification, and Franz & Schölkopf on preimage/structured dynamics in RKHS.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of DMD to RKHS with a clear Krylov/eigenspace derivation; the connection to Koopman spectral quantities is well explained.
- Practical robustification via kernel PCA/POD is sensible, aligns with established best practices in DMD, and allows truncation to cope with numerical issues.
- The applications section showcases how Koopman features can be used beyond visualization, touching prediction, classification of dynamics, and online change detection.
- The kernel machinery allows richer function classes than linear monomials; the feature-space view neatly unifies DMD and kernel PCA.

Weaknesses and questions
- Overlap with EDMD: The robust kernel variant appears very close to EDMD with a kernelized dictionary. Please provide an explicit side-by-side comparison (objective, operators being diagonalized, how dictionaries/feature subspaces are formed, and when the methods coincide or differ in bias/variance and computational cost). Section 4 would benefit from this.
- Missing related work in kernel-based dynamical modeling and time-series kernels (e.g., Ralaivola & d’Alché-Buc; Tuia et al.; Rojo-Álvarez et al.; Mouattamid & Schaback; Franz & Schölkopf). Adding and contrasting to these would strengthen positioning.
- Stochastic settings: The paper briefly mentions stochastic extensions but provides no analysis or experiments. How does kernel DMD perform under process and observation noise? What regularization is needed? Please include experiments with noisy chaotic systems and stochastic dynamics, and discuss limitations.
- Data modality and causal assumptions: The remark that pairs {(x1,x2)} suffice (rather than a single long sequence) deserves a careful discussion. What assumptions (e.g., stationarity, ergodicity, causal x2=f(x1)) are required? How robust is the approach for multidimensional time series where pointwise pairs are weakly predictive (e.g., systems with delays, partial observability)? Guidance on forming pairs and the impact on spectral estimates is needed.
- Experimental evaluation is limited. Prediction via preimage is standard and evaluated on simple systems; dynamics recognition lacks comparisons to time-series clustering baselines (kernel k-means, subspace/dynamic kernels); the change-point detection evaluation lacks comparisons to state-of-the-art online kernel methods (e.g., kernel CUSUM, direct density-ratio–based detectors). More ambitious, quantitative benchmarks with SOTA baselines are needed.
- Scalability and sensitivity: Kernel methods scale as O(τ^2) memory and O(τ^3) time for eigendecompositions of Gram matrices. Please discuss computational complexity, memory, and practical dataset sizes; consider approximations (Nyström, random features). Also report sensitivity to kernel choice/hyperparameters and truncation levels. The preimage step is nontrivial; alternatives and failure modes should be discussed.
- Theoretical assumptions (e.g., ψ=0, φj∈Φ, κj∈span(U)) are strong; a discussion of approximation error when violated would improve the paper.

Quality
- The derivations are largely sound and connect neatly to Arnoldi/Ritz theory. The robustness section is well motivated. However, empirical validation is modest and lacks competitive baselines and noise/stochasticity tests. Practical guidance on hyperparameters, regularization, and complexity is missing.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear, with helpful intuition about Koopman spectra. A clearer comparison to EDMD and a more explicit algorithmic summary (inputs/outputs/complexity) would help. Clarify conditions for eigenvalues on the unit circle (requires measure-preserving dynamics on an attractor, not general dissipative systems). Explain the preimage method’s assumptions and alternatives.

Originality
- Using RKHS/Krylov ideas for Koopman analysis is a natural and interesting step, but the novelty over EDMD is currently under-explained. The formulation via feature-space QR/Krylov and the explicit projection picture is a nice perspective, yet likely incremental without a clearer differentiation.

Significance
- If positioned and validated properly, kernelized Koopman analysis can be impactful for data-driven modeling of nonlinear dynamics. The current experiments are insufficient to demonstrate state-of-the-art performance or broad applicability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add an explicit comparison (conceptual and empirical) to EDMD and recent DMD variants (exact/optimized, sparsity-promoting).
- Expand related work to include kernel-based dynamical systems and time-series kernels as noted.
- Include stochastic/noisy experiments and discuss regularization.
- Provide quantitative comparisons for dynamics recognition (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernels) and online change detection (kernel CUSUM, density-ratio detectors).
- Discuss computational complexity, kernel selection strategies, and scalable approximations.
- Clarify the “pairs vs. sequences” setting and the causal/ergodicity assumptions.

Minor comments
- Two typos: indexing should be τ−1 (not τ) in the kernel PCA setup; replace the dot with a comma in k(xi,xj) in the last equation on p.7.

Reasons to accept
- Clear, principled extension of DMD into RKHS with theoretical grounding.
- Practical robustification and several application avenues.
- Potentially broad relevance across ML and physics for nonlinear dynamics.

Reasons to reject
- Insufficient distinction from EDMD; missing key related work.
- Limited, mostly qualitative experiments without strong baselines; no stochastic/noisy evaluation.
- Lacks discussion of scalability, hyperparameter sensitivity, and practical assumptions about data.

Overall recommendation
- Borderline reject in current form. With a clearer positioning relative to EDMD, stronger empirical evidence (including stochastic settings and competitive baselines), and expanded related work, this could become a strong contribution.
