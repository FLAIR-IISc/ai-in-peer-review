

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper presents an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), aiming to provide a more expressive approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions than traditional linear DMD methods. The core idea is to replace the linear monomials of observables used in standard DMD with nonlinear kernel-based functions, enabling a richer approximation of the Koopman operator's spectral properties. The authors develop an algorithm based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in RKHS and demonstrate its application to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition on synthetic and real-world data.

The work builds on the Koopman operator framework, which provides a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics, and extends DMD to kernel methods. However, the paper's relation to Williams et al. (2015) is unclear. While Williams et al. introduced "Extended DMD" using pre-determined basis functions, this paper's kernel-based approach is conceptually similar but lacks explicit comparison. The authors should clarify how their method differs from Extended DMD (which uses fixed basis functions) versus their kernel-based approach that learns basis functions from data. Section 4 should explicitly address this distinction, particularly regarding the use of kernel principal components versus fixed basis functions.

The paper misses several key references in kernel-based dynamical systems. Relevant prior work includes Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc (2005) on kernel-based dynamical systems, Tuia et al. (2011) on kernel-based change detection, Rojo-Álvarez et al. (2010) on kernel methods for dynamical systems, Mouattamid & Schaback (2008) on kernel-based state-space modeling, and Franz & Schölkopf (2004) on kernel-based system identification. Including these would better contextualize the contribution.

The paper's experiments are limited to deterministic settings, despite acknowledging the potential extension to stochastic systems in Section 7. The authors should provide theoretical discussion and experimental validation for stochastic dynamics, including performance analysis under noise and limitations of the current approach. This is particularly important as most real-world systems involve stochastic elements.

The paper's use of pairs of consecutive observables instead of full sequences (as noted in the Remark in Section 4) requires clearer justification. For multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relations, the causal assumption that x_{t+1} = f(x_t) may not hold, and the paper should discuss the implications of this assumption for real-world applications. The authors should clarify how their method handles cases where the dynamics are not strictly Markovian.

The experimental section lacks ambition. Prediction is standard, and the paper fails to compare with time-series clustering methods (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernels) or state-of-the-art online kernel change-point detection methods. The change-point detection experiment in Figure 4 compares with "kernel change detection" without specifying which method, and the comparison lacks statistical significance testing or comprehensive benchmarking against established methods.

Minor issues include two typos: inconsistent indexing (tau-1 vs. tau) and a misplaced dot in k(xi.xj) (last equation on page 7).

**Strengths:**
- The paper introduces a theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHS, providing a more expressive approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions.
- The algorithm is clearly described with a robustified variant using POD bases that addresses numerical instability.
- The applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition demonstrate practical utility.
- The method is well-motivated by the limitations of traditional DMD for nonlinear systems.

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient comparison with Williams et al. (2015) and other related work.
- Missing key references in kernel-based dynamical systems literature.
- Lack of discussion and experiments on stochastic settings.
- Unclear justification for using pairs of consecutive observables instead of full sequences.
- Limited experimental scope with insufficient comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.
- Minor technical errors and inconsistent notation.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pros:*
- The kernel-based extension of DMD provides a theoretically grounded approach to spectral analysis of nonlinear systems.
- The robustified algorithm addresses numerical instability issues that plague standard DMD.
- The method shows promising results on synthetic and real-world data in the presented applications.
- The paper makes a valuable contribution to the growing literature on Koopman operator methods.

*Cons:*
- The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its contribution from Williams et al. (2015) and other related work.
- Critical literature in kernel-based dynamical systems is missing.
- Experiments lack ambition and fail to compare with relevant state-of-the-art methods.
- The stochastic setting is not properly addressed despite being mentioned.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting extension of DMD to RKHS with sound theoretical foundations. However, the lack of sufficient comparison to prior work (especially Williams et al.), the missing relevant literature, and the limited experimental scope prevent it from making a compelling contribution to the field. With significant revisions addressing these weaknesses, the paper could be a strong candidate for acceptance. As written, I recommend rejection.
