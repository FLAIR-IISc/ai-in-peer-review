This paper introduces a Koopman spectral analysis framework within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), aiming to extend Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to capture nonlinear dynamics more effectively. The core idea is to represent the Koopman operator's eigenfunctions using kernel-based functions, potentially providing a richer basis than the linear monomials used in traditional DMD. The authors present an algorithm based on Krylov subspace calculations and eigendecomposition, demonstrating its application to synthetic and real-world data through prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in spectral analysis and dynamical systems. It correctly positions itself within the broader context of POD, DMD, and related techniques. The extension to RKHS is a natural progression, potentially addressing limitations of linear approximations inherent in standard DMD. The application examples are relevant and illustrate the potential of the approach.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, the distinction from Williams et al. (2015) – extended DMD – remains unclear. While the authors mention using nonlinear basis functions, a more explicit comparison detailing the differences in implementation, theoretical properties, and practical considerations is crucial. Section 4, describing the POD-based robustification, feels somewhat disconnected and lacks a clear motivation beyond addressing potential ill-conditioning. A stronger justification for this specific approach would be beneficial.

Secondly, the paper neglects relevant prior work in kernel-based dynamical systems. Several papers explore similar concepts (Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc; Tuia et al.; Rojo-Álvarez et al.; Mouattamid & Schaback; Franz & Schölkopf), and a discussion of how this work differs from and builds upon these contributions is essential. 

Thirdly, the evaluation is limited to deterministic settings. The performance and limitations of the proposed method in stochastic environments are completely unexplored. Investigating robustness to noise and its applicability to systems with inherent stochasticity would significantly strengthen the paper.

A critical point concerns the use of pairs of consecutive observables instead of sequences. While this simplifies the mathematical formulation, its practical implications for multidimensional time series, particularly those with weak pointwise relationships, are not adequately addressed. The causal assumptions underlying this approach, and how they might affect performance, need clarification. Specifically, the assumption that xt+1 is solely a function of xt could limit the method’s applicability.

The experimental evaluation is relatively unambitious. Prediction is a standard benchmark, and the lack of comparisons to time-series clustering methods (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernels) or state-of-the-art online kernel change-point detection algorithms weakens the claims of novelty and superiority. More convincing evaluations, perhaps on more challenging datasets or against stronger baselines, are needed.

Finally, there are minor issues: "tau-1" should be "τ-1" and the dot in "k(xi.xj)" on page 7 should be a comma (k(xi, xj)).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of Koopman spectral analysis to RKHS.
*   Potential for improved performance in capturing nonlinear dynamics compared to traditional DMD.
*   Clear presentation of the algorithm and its theoretical basis.
*   Demonstration of applicability through relevant examples.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Unclear distinction from existing work (Williams et al., 2015).
*   Omission of relevant prior work in kernel-based dynamical systems.
*   Lack of evaluation in stochastic settings.
*   Limited discussion of practical implications of using pairs of observables.
*   Unambitious experimental evaluation.
*   Minor typographical errors.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field of Koopman spectral analysis. However, the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the lack of a clear novelty claim, incomplete literature review, and limited evaluation, significantly detract from its impact. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and address these concerns. Addressing the comparison with Williams et al. (2015), incorporating related work, expanding the experimental evaluation (including stochastic settings), and providing a more thorough discussion of the method's limitations would substantially improve the paper’s quality and justify its inclusion in NIPS.
