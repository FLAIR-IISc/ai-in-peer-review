Summary: The paper proposes KDMD, an extension of dynamic mode decomposition that performs Koopman spectral analysis in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The method constructs a Krylov subspace in feature space via kernel evaluations and computes Ritz values/vectors by projecting the Koopman operator onto this subspace; a robust variant uses kernel PCA/POD truncation. The authors illustrate KDMD on toy systems and the Hénon map, and sketch downstream applications (short-term prediction via preimage recovery, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition). Relative to prior work, KDMD is closest to extended DMD and kernelized variants (e.g., Williams et al., 2015), exact/optimized DMD, and recent Koopman-based analyses in the dynamical systems literature (e.g., Brunton et al., 2015). The contribution is a clear kernel-RKHS formulation with an Arnoldi/Gram–Schmidt view that unifies DMD with kernel spectral methods.

Quality: The core algorithmic development is technically sound and well-grounded, with theorems that connect empirical Ritz objects to Koopman spectra. However, empirical validation is thin: results are qualitative, baselines (EDMD, exact/optimized DMD) are missing, and there is little discussion of sensitivity to kernel choice, truncation, noise, or sample size. Applications are promising but remain sketches.

Clarity: This is the main weakness. The manuscript is overly mathematical and verbose, with many statements that could be made far more concisely. As written, it reads cryptically for NeurIPS and would benefit from a pedagogical rewrite: tighten exposition, add intuition and diagrams, provide pseudocode, and clearly separate core ideas from derivations.

Originality: Moderate. The RKHS generalization is natural and known in spirit (EDMD with kernels), but the presented Krylov/QR perspective and robustification are useful and well-integrated.

Significance: Potentially high if presented clearly—KDMD is a powerful generalization that could be widely used in ML and physics.

Specific suggestions:
- Major: The presentation is needlessly verbose; substantially shorten and clarify. Section 4 appears largely redundant with the introduction and may be cut in a short paper. Section 5 should either be condensed and integrated into the conclusion or illustrated with concrete comparisons. Section 6 is overly condensed; expand with quantitative metrics and baselines (compare to EDMD, exact/optimized DMD).
- Minor: The “nonlinear” toy case as written appears linear; please check for a typo and clarify the source of nonlinearity. The first two test problems are polynomial; KDMD is most compelling when kernels/feature maps are not obvious—provide guidance and examples (as hinted in Sec. 5).
- Cite and position against Brunton et al. (2015) and Williams et al. (2015).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid, principled kernel extension of DMD with clear links to Koopman theory.
- Broad applicability; could become highly citable with improved exposition.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Clarity and organization issues hinder accessibility.
- Limited, mostly qualitative experiments without comparisons or guidance on kernel/truncation choices.
