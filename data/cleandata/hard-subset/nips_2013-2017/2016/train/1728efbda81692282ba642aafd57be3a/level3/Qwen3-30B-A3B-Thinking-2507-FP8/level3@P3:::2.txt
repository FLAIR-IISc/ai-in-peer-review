

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors extend the well-known DMD method from the dynamical systems literature by embedding the problem in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), allowing for nonlinear basis functions rather than the linear monomials used in standard DMD. The core approach involves performing eigen-decomposition on the Krylov matrix in RKHS and projecting the Koopman operator onto the subspace spanned by the resulting orthogonal bases.

The paper makes a clear connection between the Koopman operator (which linearizes nonlinear dynamics through function composition) and DMD, noting that DMD can be seen as approximating the Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials. The kernelized extension provides a richer set of basis functions, potentially capturing more complex dynamics. The authors present a robust variant using kernel PCA to handle ill-conditioning issues common in DMD implementations.

**Strengths:**
1. The mathematical formulation is sound and well-justified, with clear theorems establishing the connection between the kernelized DMD and Koopman spectral analysis.
2. The paper provides a reasonable extension of existing DMD methods, making the approach accessible to dynamical systems researchers while connecting to kernel methods familiar to machine learning audiences.
3. The empirical results on synthetic and real-world data (including locomotion recognition and change-point detection) demonstrate practical utility of the method.
4. The presentation is clear, with well-organized sections and helpful examples illustrating the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited novelty:** The core contribution appears to be kernelizing an existing method (DMD) rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach. The paper acknowledges that DMD already exists in the dynamical systems literature, and the kernel extension seems conceptually straightforward.
2. **Insufficient comparison with relevant baselines:** The paper does not adequately compare with strong competing approaches like recurrent neural networks (RNNs), which have demonstrated success in modeling dynamical systems and could serve as more relevant baselines than the traditional DMD variants mentioned.
3. **Underwhelming application demonstration:** The applications (dynamics recognition, prediction, change detection) do not compellingly demonstrate the distinct advantages of the kernelized approach over simpler methods. The paper shows "good empirical performance" but doesn't establish that the kernelized method provides significantly better results than standard DMD or other kernel-based methods for these tasks.
4. **Lack of theoretical analysis:** While the paper provides empirical results, it lacks deeper theoretical analysis of when the kernelized approach would outperform standard DMD or other methods.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with correct mathematical derivations and reasonable empirical validation. However, the evaluation could be strengthened by more thorough comparison with relevant baselines and deeper analysis of the method's limitations.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, making the mathematical concepts accessible to readers familiar with both kernel methods and dynamical systems. The connection between DMD and Koopman operator is clearly explained.

**Originality:** The main novelty is limited to kernelizing DMD, which is a natural extension of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new contribution. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work on kernel-based methods for dynamical systems (e.g., "A Kernel Subspace Method by Stochastic Realization for Learning Nonlinear Dynamical Systems" by Kawahara et al., NIPS 2009).

**Significance:** The applications are interesting but not compellingly demonstrated. The paper doesn't establish that this method solves a significant problem in a demonstrably better way than existing approaches. The potential for the method to advance the state of the art is limited, as the core technique (DMD) is already well-established, and the kernel extension appears to be a straightforward adaptation.

**Final Assessment:** While the paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods, its novelty is limited and the empirical demonstrations don't sufficiently justify the approach over simpler alternatives. The method is a natural but incremental contribution to the field, rather than a significant advance. The paper would be stronger with more thorough comparison to relevant baselines and clearer demonstration of the method's unique advantages.

**Recommendation:** Weak reject. The paper is technically sound but makes only a modest contribution to the field, with limited novelty and insufficient demonstration of advantages over existing methods. The applications do not compellingly showcase the method's distinct value, and the paper would benefit from more thorough comparison with relevant baselines.
