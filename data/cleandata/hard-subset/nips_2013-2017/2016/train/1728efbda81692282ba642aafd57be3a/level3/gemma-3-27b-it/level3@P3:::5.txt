This paper proposes KDMD, an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), aiming to provide a more powerful generalization for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea – leveraging kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions – is promising. However, the manuscript suffers significantly from a lack of clarity and excessive mathematical detail, hindering its accessibility and impact.

The work builds upon the recent surge of interest in DMD (Brunton et al., 2015; Schmid, 2010) and its application to various fields, recognizing the limitations of traditional DMD’s reliance on linear monomials for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions. Extending the analysis to RKHS offers the potential to capture more complex dynamics. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of spectral methods for dynamical systems (e.g., subspace identification – Van Overschee & De Moor, 1996) and related decomposition techniques like POD (Sirovich, 1990). 

However, the presentation obscures these strengths. The mathematical formalism is dense and verbose, making it difficult to follow the core logic. The paper frequently restates concepts without providing sufficient intuition or motivation. While mathematical rigor is valuable, it shouldn’t come at the expense of clarity, particularly for a NeurIPS audience. A more pedagogical approach, with clearer explanations and illustrative examples, is crucial.

Several specific weaknesses further detract from the paper's quality. Section 4, which discusses robustifying the method with POD bases, is largely redundant. The motivation for using POD is adequately explained in the introduction, and the details presented in Section 4 don't warrant a dedicated section in a short paper. This section should be significantly condensed or integrated into Section 3.

Section 5, "Related Works," is inadequate. It provides a list of papers but lacks a critical assessment of how KDMD differs from and builds upon these existing approaches. The connection to subspace identification, mentioned briefly, deserves further elaboration.

Section 6, the empirical example, is overly condensed. While the authors demonstrate KDMD’s effectiveness on a toy system and the Henon map, the presentation lacks detail. The choice of kernels (polynomial and Gaussian) is stated, but the reasoning behind these choices isn't provided. The results, while promising, need more thorough analysis and discussion. The impact of parameter choices (e.g., kernel width) should be investigated.

A minor point: the authors claim the "nonlinear" toy case is actually linear. This should be corrected. Furthermore, the first two test problems are polynomial, which doesn’t fully showcase KDMD’s strength. The algorithm’s benefit is most apparent when the kernel/feature map can effectively capture the underlying dynamics—this should be demonstrated with a more challenging example, as alluded to in Section 5. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of DMD to RKHS, offering a potentially powerful generalization.
*   Theoretical soundness of the proposed algorithm.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on test problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Poor clarity and excessive mathematical verbosity.
*   Redundant content (Section 4).
*   Insufficiently developed empirical evaluation (Section 6).
*   Inadequate discussion of related work (Section 5).
*   Lack of pedagogical approach.



**Recommendation:** Reject with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. 

The core idea presented in this paper is valuable and has the potential to be highly citable. However, the current manuscript is inaccessible and falls short of NeurIPS quality standards. A complete rewrite, focusing on clarity, conciseness, and a more pedagogical presentation, is essential. Specifically, the authors should: 1) streamline the mathematical notation and explanations; 2) condense or eliminate Section 4; 3) expand and contextualize Section 5; 4) provide a more detailed and insightful empirical evaluation in Section 6; and 5) address the minor typo regarding the “nonlinear” toy case. Addressing these concerns will significantly enhance the paper’s impact and make it a valuable contribution to the field.
