## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes KDMD, an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), aiming to improve the approximation of Koopman operator spectra for nonlinear systems. The core idea – leveraging kernel methods to capture more complex, nonlinear eigenfunctions – is a natural and potentially powerful generalization of DMD. The authors demonstrate the method on toy problems and highlight its potential for feature extraction and downstream machine learning tasks. While the concept is promising and represents a logical progression from existing DMD techniques (e.g., Brunton et al., 2015), the manuscript suffers from significant clarity and presentation issues.

The primary weakness is the overly mathematical and verbose writing style. Explanations are often dense and lack intuitive motivation, hindering understanding. For example, the derivation in Section 3, while technically sound, could benefit from a more pedagogical approach, explaining *why* certain steps are taken and their implications. Section 4 feels largely redundant, reiterating concepts already introduced and unnecessarily lengthening the paper. Its inclusion doesn’t substantially add to the core contribution. Similarly, Section 5, a “related works” section, feels disjointed and doesn’t synthesize the literature effectively. The empirical results in Section 6 are presented with minimal explanation; illustrating the method with more detailed examples or summarizing the key findings in the conclusion would significantly improve comprehension. Section 6 is also too condensed and lacks the detail provided in Section 5. 

The paper also contains minor issues. The “nonlinear” toy case appears to be linear, and the initial test problems are polynomial, diminishing the demonstration of KDMD’s advantage when kernel/feature map selection is crucial. More detail on this aspect, as hinted at in Section 5, is needed.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The core idea of extending DMD to RKHS is novel and represents a valuable generalization. The theoretical foundation appears solid.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The manuscript is exceptionally difficult to read due to its verbosity and lack of clarity. Significant portions (Sections 4 & 5) are either redundant or underdeveloped. The empirical validation lacks sufficient detail and doesn’t fully showcase the benefits of the proposed method.

**Overall:** This paper has the potential to be a highly citable contribution. However, a substantial rewrite focusing on clarity, conciseness, and pedagogical presentation is *strongly* recommended. As it stands, the manuscript is too cryptic for a NeurIPS audience. I recommend rejection with encouragement to resubmit after a thorough revision addressing the concerns outlined above.
