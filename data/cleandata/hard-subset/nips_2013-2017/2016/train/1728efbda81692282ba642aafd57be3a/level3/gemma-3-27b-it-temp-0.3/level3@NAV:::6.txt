## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper presents a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis, leveraging Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea – applying kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions – is a natural and potentially valuable extension of existing DMD techniques. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of spectral methods for dynamical systems, referencing relevant literature including POD, DMD, and kernel methods like kernel PCA. The paper is well-written and pedagogically sound, clearly explaining the underlying concepts, even for readers unfamiliar with fluid dynamics. The inclusion of the Koopman operator background is helpful for context.

The strength of this work lies in its theoretical grounding and the clear presentation of the algorithm. The derivation relating the kernelized DMD to the Koopman operator’s eigenspaces is well-articulated. However, the empirical evaluation is a significant weakness. While the toy example demonstrates the method’s ability to recover known eigenvalues, the comparison to standard DMD, PCA, and kPCA remains unclear. The authors need to provide a more rigorous and quantitative comparison, demonstrating where their method outperforms or complements existing techniques. The Henon map example is also limited; including standard DMD performance on this map would be highly informative.

A key concern is the practical significance of this work for the machine learning community. The authors mention potential applications but don’t convincingly demonstrate a compelling use case where this kernelized Koopman analysis provides a substantial advantage over existing methods. Robotics or other domains involving dynamical system inference would be a strong target for demonstrating utility. Furthermore, the current formulation is limited to deterministic systems, which restricts its applicability to many real-world ML problems involving noise. 

The theoretical results, while correct, feel somewhat unsurprising given the existing literature on RKHS and kernel linear operators. The paper would benefit from highlighting novel theoretical insights beyond the straightforward application of kernel methods to DMD. Finally, clarification is needed regarding whether the modal decomposition requires explicit feature-space representations; if so, this is a limitation compared to kernel PCA.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Sound theoretical foundation.
*   Clear and pedagogical writing style.
*   Logical extension of DMD using kernel methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Weak empirical evaluation and unclear comparisons.
*   Lack of demonstrated practical utility for ML.
*   Limited to deterministic systems.
*   Theoretical results lack significant novelty.



**Recommendation:** Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should focus on strengthening the empirical evaluation, identifying concrete ML applications, and addressing the limitations related to stochastic systems and theoretical novelty.
