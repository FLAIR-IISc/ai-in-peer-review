This paper proposes a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors derive a theoretical framework for performing this analysis in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) and present a corresponding algorithm. While the core idea is sound and the paper is clearly written, its significance to the NIPS community is unclear, and the empirical validation is limited.

The paper successfully extends DMD by leveraging the power of kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, potentially capturing more complex dynamics than traditional linear monomial-based DMD. The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of spectral methods for dynamical systems, referencing relevant work in physics, engineering, and machine learning, including POD, DMD, and subspace identification techniques. The explanation of the Koopman operator and DMD is particularly well-done, making the paper accessible to readers unfamiliar with fluid dynamics concepts.

However, the paper’s originality is incremental. The combination of RKHS theory and DMD, while technically correct, feels largely unsurprising given the existing literature on kernel methods and linear operator analysis. The theoretical results follow naturally from established RKHS principles. Furthermore, the empirical evaluation provides little compelling evidence of a clear advantage over existing methods. The demonstrations on the damped 1D system and Henon map are rudimentary, and the comparison to DMD, PCA, and kPCA lacks sufficient detail and statistical rigor to draw definitive conclusions. The applications section (prediction, embedding/recognition, change-point detection) feels more like a list of possibilities than a robust demonstration of practical utility. 

A key weakness is the lack of a convincing demonstration of the method's usefulness for machine learning tasks. The authors acknowledge this in their discussion of related work, and rightly point out the need for concrete use cases and comparison to state-of-the-art approaches. Robotics, as suggested, would be a relevant domain for demonstrating the utility of Koopman analysis, providing a more challenging and realistic testbed. 

Several specific points require clarification. First, the authors should explicitly state whether the modal decomposition requires explicit feature-space representations. If so, this represents a limitation compared to kernel PCA, which operates implicitly in the feature space. Second, including standard DMD performance results on the toy example and the Henon map would significantly strengthen the paper by providing a clear baseline for comparison and highlighting the benefits (if any) of the kernelized approach. Third, the deterministic-only setup limits the relevance of the method to many real-world ML applications where stochasticity is prevalent. Investigating extensions to handle noisy data would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Clear and pedagogical explanation of Koopman analysis and DMD.
*   Technically sound derivation of the kernelized DMD algorithm.
*   Potential for capturing more complex dynamics than traditional DMD.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental originality; results largely follow from existing RKHS theory.
*   Limited empirical validation and unclear performance gains over existing methods.
*   Lack of a compelling demonstration of practical utility for ML tasks.
*   Deterministic setup limits real-world applicability.
*   Unclear whether explicit feature space representation is required, potentially limiting advantage over kernel PCA.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound but ultimately incremental contribution. While the ideas are well-explained and the mathematics are correct, the lack of strong empirical evidence and a clear demonstration of practical utility make it difficult to justify acceptance to NIPS at this time. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit a revised version with a more compelling empirical evaluation and a stronger focus on machine learning applications. Specifically, addressing the comparison to state-of-the-art methods within a relevant ML domain (e.g., robotics) would be crucial for future consideration.
