This paper studies Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) procedure. The main idea is to map states via a feature map ϕ(x) into an RKHS H, form a Krylov subspace span{ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)}, and compute a projected Koopman operator on this subspace via Gram–Schmidt/QR. The resulting eigendecomposition yields “Koopman modes” and eigenvalues that decompose the evolution of ϕ(x) analogously to DMD. A numerically robust variant projects onto kernel-PCA (POD) directions, enabling truncation and improved conditioning. The paper also sketches applications: short-term prediction via preimage reconstruction, sequential change-point detection via a CUSUM-style statistic, and dynamics recognition via subspace embedding using kernel principal angles. Experiments on a toy system, the Hénon map, motion capture sequences, and a Lorenz map change-point task illustrate feasibility.

Relation to prior work: DMD approximates Koopman spectra from data (Schmid 2010; Rowley et al. 2009; Tu et al. 2014 exact DMD), with many variants addressing numerical robustness and basis selection. Extended DMD (EDMD; Williams et al. 2015/2016) generalizes DMD to arbitrary dictionaries and includes kernel versions; sparsity-promoting DMD (Jovanović et al. 2014) selects parsimonious modes. The present work is close in spirit to EDMD with kernels (“KDMD”), but is framed via Krylov subspaces in feature space and a QR/companion-matrix derivation; the robust variant aligns with truncated POD/exact DMD implemented with kernel PCA. The applications leverage standard machinery (preimage problems in kernel methods; kernel subspace distances). The paper’s positioning relative to EDMD and exact DMD could be made sharper, but the RKHS-Krylov perspective is useful.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for richer (nonlinear) basis functions via kernels to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions beyond local linearization.
- Conceptually coherent algorithmic framework; the robust kernel-POD variant is practical and mirrors best practices in DMD.
- Empirical illustrations across synthetic and real data; the locomotion “dynamics embedding” example is appealing.
- Generally well written and organized.

Weaknesses and technical concerns (Quality)
- Operator-theoretic definitions and domains are not precise, leading to confusion and possible inconsistencies:
  - Equation (7) defines KHϕ = ϕ ∘ f with ϕ: M→H; this is not an operator H→H but rather an operator on H-valued observables over M. In contrast, Eq. (2) defines K on scalar observables g: M→C. The paper should clearly distinguish these spaces and the objects on which each operator acts.
  - The text alternates between “eigenfunctions of KH” and vector-valued “Ritz vectors” in H. If the operator acts on functions M→H, its eigenobjects are H-valued functions, not elements of H. If the intent is to study the linear map induced by time-shift on the span of snapshot features {ϕ(xi)} ⊂ H (i.e., A ≈ argmin ||ϕ(xi+1) − Aϕ(xi)||), then stick to that finite-dimensional projected operator and avoid calling its eigenvectors “eigenfunctions.”
- Theorem 1 proof uses steps that require stronger assumptions than stated:
  - The identity M+ = KH Mτ is exact only for noiseless data and if KH maps each column ϕ(xi) to ϕ(xi+1). This needs to be stated explicitly.
  - The expression F = R−1 Pτ R = Mτ−1 KH Mτ is problematic because Mτ is tall and generally not invertible; if a left inverse is intended, specify the assumptions (full column rank) and whether one uses a least-squares/pseudo-inverse relationship. The companion-matrix argument also hinges on distinct eigenvalues and minimal polynomial properties; these assumptions should be stated up front.
  - The phrase “applying KH to both sides” (lines 144–157) appears to operate on scalars; please clarify what objects the operator acts on at each step.
- The robust variant (Section 4) is more convincing, but still assumes that Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the span of kernel principal directions. Theorem 2’s assumptions (κj ∈ span(U)) are strong and should be discussed, including the effect of truncation and noise; otherwise, Eq. (12) becomes an approximation rather than an identity.
- Experimental evaluation is qualitative, small-scale, and lacks baselines. There is no comparison against exact DMD, EDMD (with polynomial dictionaries), or KDMD, nor quantitative metrics (prediction error, spectral estimation error, reconstruction fidelity).
- Computational aspects (O(τ^3) eigendecompositions of τ×τ Gram matrices; memory scaling; kernel/hyperparameter selection; regularization) are not discussed.

Clarity
- The overall exposition is good, but Section 3.1 (Eq. 7) and Theorem 1 require careful rewriting with proper functional-analytic notation:
  - Define the spaces precisely: X ⊂ Rd; scalar observable space G (e.g., L2 w.r.t. an invariant measure); feature map ϕ: X→H; snapshot subspace Φτ = span{ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)} ⊂ H; projected evolution operator Aτ: Φτ→Φτ defined by least squares/projection. Use consistent symbols for eigenvectors in H versus eigenfunctions in G.
  - Make all assumptions explicit (noise-free dynamics, distinct Ritz values, full column rank of Mτ, ψ = 0 case for exact recovery, etc.).
- Minor typos and notation issues:
  - Line 55: “the the”.
  - Line 153: κj/λj should read κi/λi (index mismatch).
  - Use consistent notation for complex conjugate/Hermitian transpose and inner products; clarify when data are centered and how centering affects shift relationships.

Originality
- The idea of using kernels to enlarge the dictionary for Koopman/DMD is known (EDMD and kernel EDMD). The paper’s contribution is the Krylov/QR derivation and the explicit kernel-POD robustification. Novelty is moderate but nontrivial; a stronger differentiation from EDMD/KDMD and discussion of equivalences would help.

Significance
- If clarified and positioned correctly, the RKHS-Krylov perspective and robust kernel-POD implementation can be useful to practitioners. However, the current empirical evidence is limited, and the preimage and change-point sections are interesting but preliminary.

Specific questions and requests
- Clarify the domain/codomain of KH in Eq. (7) and reconcile the use of “eigenfunction” versus “eigenvector” in H.
- Replace Mτ−1 occurrences by a precise projection/pseudo-inverse formulation; justify F = R−1Q* M+ and conditions under which it equals the companion matrix.
- State all assumptions for Theorems 1 and 2 explicitly; indicate which equalities become approximations under noise/truncation.
- Provide quantitative comparisons to exact DMD and EDMD (with polynomial/RBF dictionaries) on the toy and Hénon systems (e.g., eigenvalue errors, prediction RMSE).
- Discuss computational complexity, kernel/hyperparameter selection, and regularization (e.g., Tikhonov) for ill-conditioned Gram matrices.
- In the preimage section, report quantitative prediction performance and sensitivity to the neighborhood size; compare to EDMD-based predictors.
- For change-point detection, justify the exponential family model and the approximation that drops the second term; compare against kernel CUSUM/BOCPD baselines and report false-alarm/delay trade-offs.

Pros
- Clear motivation and coherent algorithmic framework.
- Solid connection to DMD/Koopman ideas; practical robust variant via kernel POD.
- Interesting application sketches; code availability is a plus.

Cons
- Mathematical presentation has inconsistencies and hidden assumptions.
- Limited empirical evaluation and lack of baselines/metrics.
- Novelty relative to EDMD/KDMD needs clearer positioning.

Recommendation: Borderline. I would support acceptance if the authors substantially clarify the operator-theoretic setup and proofs, make assumptions explicit, and add quantitative comparisons; otherwise, this is better as a workshop paper.
