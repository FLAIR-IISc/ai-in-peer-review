

This paper proposes Kernel Dynamic Mode Decomposition (KDMD), extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproduce kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors frame the Koopman operator as a linear operator acting on observables in RKHS, then develop an algorithm that computes orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performs eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projection. This generalizes traditional DMD (which uses linear monomials as basis functions) by leveraging kernel functions to capture more complex nonlinear relationships. The authors demonstrate applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution by extending DMD to RKHS, which offers a more expressive representation than traditional approaches. The mathematical framework is rigorous, with well-structured theorems establishing the connection between the proposed algorithm and Koopman spectral analysis. The authors effectively demonstrate the method's applicability across multiple domains, including the Hénon map and locomotion data, showing competitive performance compared to existing methods. This work builds upon Williams et al.'s extended DMD but provides a more general framework that avoids the need for pre-specified basis functions, making it a meaningful advancement in Koopman spectral analysis.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues that undermine its impact. The presentation is overly mathematical and verbose, with excessive notation that obscures the core contributions. For instance, Section 4 (Robustifying with POD Bases) is largely redundant with the main algorithm description and could be eliminated from a concise NeurIPS paper. Section 5 (Related Works) should be condensed and integrated into the introduction or conclusion, rather than presented as a separate section. Section 6 (Empirical Examples) is overly condensed, lacking sufficient detail to understand the results' significance, particularly for the "nonlinear" toy example which appears to be linear. The authors would benefit from including a more pedagogical explanation of when KDMD would be most useful (i.e., when the kernel/feature map can be reasonably guessed), as this is crucial for practical adoption.

The paper's significance is strong in the context of dynamical systems analysis, but its presentation prevents it from reaching a broader NeurIPS audience. While the mathematical content is sound, the writing style is inaccessible to many machine learning researchers who would benefit from this work. The authors should significantly revise the manuscript to prioritize clarity and conciseness, focusing on the key insights rather than exhaustive mathematical derivations. The current presentation is not suitable for a NeurIPS audience, which values clear exposition alongside technical rigor.

The strengths of this paper include:
- A meaningful extension of DMD to RKHS, providing a more flexible framework for Koopman spectral analysis
- Rigorous mathematical foundation with well-structured theorems
- Demonstrated applications across multiple domains with empirical validation
- Strong connection to relevant work (e.g., Williams et al.'s extended DMD)

The weaknesses include:
- Excessive mathematical verbosity that obscures the core contributions
- Poorly organized sections that could be condensed (particularly Sections 4 and 5)
- Insufficient explanation of when KDMD is most useful
- Overly condensed empirical examples that lack sufficient detail
- The "nonlinear" toy example appears to be linear, which undermines the paper's claims

The paper is technically sound and makes a valuable contribution to the field of dynamical systems analysis, but its current presentation severely limits its accessibility and impact at NeurIPS. With a more focused, pedagogical rewrite that emphasizes the key insights and practical implications, this work would be highly citable and influential. As written, it fails to clearly communicate its contributions to the NeurIPS audience, despite having strong technical merit.

Pro-acceptance arguments: The paper presents a novel extension of DMD to RKHS with sound mathematical foundations and demonstrates its utility across multiple applications. The Koopman spectral analysis framework is increasingly important in machine learning and dynamical systems, making this contribution timely and relevant.

Con-acceptance arguments: The current presentation is overly technical and verbose, making it difficult for the NeurIPS audience to grasp the key contributions. The paper would benefit from significant revision to improve clarity and conciseness before being suitable for publication.
