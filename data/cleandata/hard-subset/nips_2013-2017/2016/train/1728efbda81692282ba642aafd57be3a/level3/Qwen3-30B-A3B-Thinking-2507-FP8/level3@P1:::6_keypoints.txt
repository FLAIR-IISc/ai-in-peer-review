- Proposes a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) in RKHS, computing orthogonal Krylov bases and eigendecomposition for Koopman spectral analysis.  
- Frames the method as a feature extraction tool for nonlinear dynamical systems with applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.  
- Paper is clearly written and pedagogically valuable, with accessible exposition for readers unfamiliar with Koopman theory.  
- Effectively connects to existing spectral analysis and kernel methods (POD, DMD, kernel PCA) and provides a well-structured theoretical foundation.  
- Theorems 1 and 2 link the proposed kernel DMD to Koopman spectral theory; POD-based robustification addresses common DMD numerical instabilities.  
- Empirical validation is weak: experiments are limited to synthetic systems and a small locomotion dataset.  
- Lacks comparisons to state-of-the-art methods and omits standard DMD baselines on toy examples and the Hénon map.  
- Locomotion recognition results show only marginal, statistically inconclusive improvements and do not clearly outperform simpler methods like kernel PCA.  
- Major shortcoming: no compelling machine learning applications demonstrated; suggested applications remain superficial.  
- Deterministic-only experimental setup ignores noise, stochasticity, and partial observability common in real-world ML problems.  
- Originality is limited: kernelizing DMD is a natural extension and the paper doesn’t clearly distinguish itself from prior kernel-based approaches or fully explore links to extended DMD (Williams et al.).  
- Theoretical contributions largely follow from established RKHS operator theory and are not sufficiently novel for a top-tier ML venue.  
- Significance is limited by insufficient empirical evidence and lack of concrete ML domain advantages.  
- Recommended improvements: add thorough SOTA comparisons, demonstrate a specific ML application where kernel DMD excels, address noise/uncertainty handling, and clarify computational/practical advantages over standard DMD.  
- Conclusion: technically sound and well-presented but empirically and practically underpowered, thus not ready for NIPS without substantial strengthening.
