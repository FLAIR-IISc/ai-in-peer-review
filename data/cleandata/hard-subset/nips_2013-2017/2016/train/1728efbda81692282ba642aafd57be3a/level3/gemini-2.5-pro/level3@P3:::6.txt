### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a method for the spectral analysis of the Koopman operator by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The central idea is to lift the system's state into a high-dimensional feature space where the action of the nonlinear dynamics can be approximated by a linear Koopman operator. The authors derive a data-driven algorithm, Kernel DMD (kDMD), to compute the eigenvalues (frequencies and growth/decay rates) and eigenfunctions (modes) of this operator directly from time-series data using kernel methods. The algorithm is presented in two forms: a direct approach based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space and a more robust variant using a projection onto kernel principal component analysis (kPCA) bases. This work connects the fluid dynamics literature on Koopman analysis with established machine learning techniques like kernel PCA and spectral methods for learning dynamical systems. The extracted Koopman modes are framed as features, and their utility is demonstrated on applications including prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

### Strengths

1.  **Clarity and Pedagogical Value:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and logically organized. It provides an excellent introduction to the concepts of the Koopman operator and DMD for readers in the machine learning community who may be unfamiliar with these tools from fluid dynamics. The connection between DMD, the Krylov subspace method, and the proposed kernelized extension is laid out very effectively.

2.  **Novel Combination of Ideas:** The core contribution—formulating a kernelized version of DMD for Koopman spectral analysis—is an interesting and natural extension. It successfully bridges the gap between linear operator theory for dynamical systems and the powerful non-parametric toolkit of kernel methods. This provides a principled way to extend DMD to capture a richer class of nonlinear dynamics.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

1.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The experimental validation is the weakest aspect of the paper. While the method is demonstrated on a simple damped system, the chaotic Hénon map, and locomotion data, the results are difficult to interpret without stronger baselines.
    *   A direct comparison with standard, linear DMD is essential for the toy example and the Hénon map. This would clearly highlight the scenarios where the additional complexity of the kernel method provides a tangible benefit.
    *   The comparisons to methods like kernel PCA are not quantitative, making it hard to assess the unique advantages of the Koopman perspective for feature extraction. The authors should provide concrete metrics showing where kDMD outperforms alternatives.

2.  **Unclear Practical Significance for Machine Learning:** The paper argues that the Koopman modes can serve as features for ML tasks, but the link to the state-of-the-art in those areas is not established.
    *   The applications in Section 7 (prediction, change-point detection, etc.) are presented as proofs-of-concept. To be compelling for a NIPS audience, these applications need to be benchmarked against established, high-performing methods in their respective fields. For example, how does the prediction method compare to Gaussian Processes or LSTMs on these time series?
    *   The authors should identify a concrete ML domain where this analysis provides a clear advantage. For instance, robotics (e.g., for system identification, model-based control, or imitation learning) seems like a natural fit where inferring the underlying structure of dynamical systems is critical. Demonstrating superior performance on such a task would significantly strengthen the paper's impact.

3.  **Assumption of Deterministic Dynamics:** The framework is developed exclusively for deterministic systems (`xt+1 = f(xt)`). This is a significant limitation for most machine learning applications, where noise and stochasticity are prevalent. While the authors briefly mention an extension to stochastic systems in the change-point detection section, this is not a core part of the proposed method or its evaluation. A more thorough treatment of how the method behaves under observation noise or process noise is needed to assess its real-world applicability.

4.  **Implementation Details of Modal Decomposition:** It is unclear whether the calculation of the Koopman modes (`φ̄j` in the robust version) requires explicit representation or manipulation of the data matrix `Mτ` in the feature space. The formula `φ̄j = MτHB̄S̄−1/2bj` suggests this might be the case. If the algorithm cannot be implemented using only the Gram matrix and its eigendecomposition, this represents a significant computational and memory drawback compared to standard kernel methods like kPCA, potentially limiting its scalability. Please clarify this point.

5.  **Modest Theoretical Novelty:** While the combination of ideas is novel, the core theoretical results are largely an application of well-established theory of linear operators in Hilbert spaces to the Koopman operator framework. Given the extensive literature on kernel methods for linear operators (e.g., kPCA, kernel CCA), the derivation of the algorithm, while correct and elegant, is not surprising. The contribution is more in the formulation and connection of fields rather than a fundamental theoretical advance.

### Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-written and interesting fusion of Koopman operator theory with kernel methods. The proposed kDMD algorithm is a logical and principled extension of DMD for nonlinear systems. However, the paper is currently let down by a weak experimental section that fails to demonstrate a clear advantage over simpler baselines (like standard DMD) or state-of-the-art ML methods. The focus on deterministic systems and potential computational limitations also narrows its immediate appeal.

To improve the paper, I strongly recommend the authors include direct comparisons to standard DMD, benchmark the application tasks against relevant ML methods, and clarify the computational requirements of the modal decomposition. Providing a compelling use case, for example in robotics, would significantly elevate the work's significance. While the paper is pedagogically valuable, in its current form, its contribution to the machine learning community feels preliminary.
