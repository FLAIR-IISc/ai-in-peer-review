This paper studies Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (kDMD) algorithm. The authors define the Koopman operator KH acting on feature maps ϕ and construct a Krylov matrix in H from a finite trajectory. They obtain empirical Ritz values/vectors by projecting KH onto the span of the feature snapshots via Gram–Schmidt in H, implemented with Gram matrices. To address ill-conditioning, they introduce a robust variant that projects onto kernel PCA directions, analogous to the standard DMD practice of truncating to POD modes. The method yields a finite modal decomposition usable as features for downstream tasks. Demonstrations include: (i) a toy system with known Koopman eigenvalues and the Hénon map, (ii) short-horizon prediction via a preimage heuristic, (iii) embedding/recognition of motion capture sequences using kernel principal angles between subspaces spanned by estimated Koopman eigenfunctions, and (iv) sequential change-point detection via a CUSUM-like score built from kernel expansions.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on DMD and its variants (exact/optimized/sparsity-promoting) and on extended DMD (EDMD), which approximates the Koopman operator in a finite dictionary; here, kernels implicitly define a rich dictionary and the robustification uses kernel PCA rather than POD/SVD. It also relates to kernel PCA and kernel exponential families. The observation that one can perform Krylov subspace projections and Ritz analysis in H via kernel Gram matrices is natural given existing RKHS operator literature, but the presentation is cohesive and accessible for a machine learning audience.

Strengths:
- Clear, pedagogical exposition of Koopman spectral analysis and DMD, including RKHS extension and a robust implementation based on kernel PCA.
- Concrete algorithms that use only kernel evaluations (no explicit feature maps), with proofs connecting the computed spectra/modes to the Koopman objects (Theorems 1 and 2).
- Multiple potential applications are sketched, and code availability is a plus for reproducibility.
- The paper helps bridge the fluid-dynamics DMD community and ML audiences.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty is moderate: the approach is close to EDMD with a kernel dictionary; the relationship to EDMD and “kernel EDMD” is not fully clarified and the theoretical results are unsurprising given RKHS operator projections.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and largely qualitative. There are no quantitative comparisons to DMD/EDMD/POD/kPCA or ablations, and the chosen examples are small-scale.
- No treatment of stochasticity/noise, which limits relevance for ML domains where data are noisy and systems stochastic; robustness and regularization are not analyzed.
- The preimage step for prediction is ad hoc and its efficacy vs standard alternatives is unclear.
- Scalability, complexity, and hyperparameter selection (kernel width, truncation rank p) are not addressed.

Quality:
- The derivations are technically sound and consistent with known Krylov/projection ideas in RKHS. Assumptions (e.g., distinct eigenvalues, ψ = 0 for exact recovery, eigenfunctions in span of principal components) are clearly stated but strong. There is no statistical consistency or finite-sample error analysis. Empirical validation lacks quantitative rigor.

Clarity:
- Well written and organized; background is thorough and likely helpful to non-specialists. Some notation (e.g., consistent use of conjugate transposes in complex RKHS) could be streamlined. Figures should report quantitative metrics (errors, confidence intervals) and be more readable.

Originality:
- A reasonable synthesis of DMD/Koopman analysis with kernels. The robust kernel PCA projection is a natural analogue to truncated POD. The core ideas resemble EDMD with an implicit kernel dictionary; the paper would benefit from explicitly positioning against that line of work and clarifying differences.

Significance:
- Potentially useful for bringing Koopman features to ML tasks (e.g., robotics, time-series classification). However, without stronger empirical evidence on modern ML benchmarks and noise robustness, the immediate impact is uncertain.

Questions for the authors:
- Do all steps operate purely via kernel evaluations (no explicit ϕ)? Please detail computational and memory complexity in τ and p, and out-of-sample evaluation.
- How are kernel and truncation hyperparameters chosen? Provide a sensitivity analysis.
- How does this differ, theoretically and computationally, from EDMD with a kernel dictionary? Under what conditions would your approach outperform EDMD?
- How robust are the spectra to noise? Can you include Tikhonov/τ-regularization and show performance under observational/process noise?
- Does projection onto kernel PCA directions bias the estimated spectra? Guidance for choosing p?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add baselines: standard DMD, exact DMD, EDMD with polynomial/RBF dictionaries, Hankel-DMD/time-delay embeddings, and simple autoregressive models.
- Quantify: eigenvalue errors vs ground truth on the toy and Hénon systems; multi-step prediction RMSE; classification accuracy for motion data; ROC/AUC, detection delay, and false alarm rates for change-point detection; runtime/memory vs τ.
- Include noise/stochastic experiments and show robustness and regularization effects.
- Clarify kernel selection and parameter tuning; provide ablations on kernel width and truncation rank.
- Consider a compelling ML application (e.g., robot dynamics identification/control, gait recognition) with strong baselines to demonstrate utility.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, pedagogical derivation of a kernel DMD framework with practical implementation details.
- Bridges communities and could spur adoption of Koopman-based features in ML.
- Code availability.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incremental novelty vs EDMD/kernel operator literature; positioning is incomplete.
- Weak empirical validation without quantitative comparisons or noise robustness.
- Limited evidence of practical usefulness on ML-relevant tasks and scales.

Overall, this is a well-written and sensible kernelized extension of DMD with potential value, but it needs stronger empirical evidence and clearer positioning relative to EDMD and kernel operator methods to meet the bar for NIPS.
