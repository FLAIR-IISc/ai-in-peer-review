This paper proposes Kernel Dynamic Mode Decomposition (KDMD): a kernelized extension of dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) that performs Koopman spectral analysis in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The core idea is to treat feature-space snapshots ϕ(xt) as a Krylov sequence, compute an orthogonal basis (via Gram–Schmidt/QR in RKHS using Gram matrices), and then eigendecompose the finite-dimensional projection of the Koopman operator to obtain empirical Ritz values and modes in feature space. A robust variant projects data onto kernel-PCA directions (akin to truncated POD in DMD), improving numerical conditioning and enabling rank truncation. The paper also discusses applications: short-term prediction via a pre-image construction, recognition using subspace angles between estimated Koopman subspaces, and sequential change-point detection using an exponential family model with features provided by the Koopman analysis. Empirical illustrations include a toy system, the Henon map, motion recognition, and a Lorenz change-point example.

Relation to prior work: DMD (Schmid 2010; Tu et al. 2014), exact and optimized DMD (Tu et al. 2014; Chen et al. 2012; Askham & Kutz 2018), sparsity-promoting DMD (Jovanović et al.), and extended DMD (EDMD; Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley 2015) all seek finite-dimensional approximations to Koopman spectra. KDMD is close in spirit to EDMD but leverages the kernel trick to implicitly work with rich nonlinear dictionaries with favorable computational structure (Gram matrices, kernel-PCA truncation). The robustification via kernel PCA mirrors standard practice in DMD (POD projection). The paper is well situated in the Koopman/DMD literature from physics and control, and should also cite Brunton et al. (2015) on DMD/Koopman invariant subspaces to more fully connect to recent NeurIPS-relevant work.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity of the contribution: KDMD is a natural and powerful generalization of DMD/EDMD using RKHS machinery, enabling richer dictionaries without manual basis selection.
- Solid linear-algebraic formulation: the equivalence to Arnoldi/Ritz values in feature space and the QR-based derivations are correct and useful, and the kernel-PCA robustification is standard and justified.
- Practicality: computation is reduced to Gram-matrix operations; truncation provides numerical stability; the “pairs of snapshots” remark broadens applicability.
- Appealing applications: the recognition and change-point detection examples demonstrate the utility of Koopman features beyond pure physics/fluids.

Weaknesses and recommendations
- Clarity and exposition: The manuscript is overly mathematical and verbose, making it hard to follow for a general NeurIPS audience. Many statements could be substantially shortened and rephrased more pedagogically. Including a simple end-to-end pseudocode block, a schematic diagram of the pipeline (data → Gram matrices → eigendecomposition → modes → applications), and a concise “intuition” paragraph for each derivation would help.
- Structure: Section 4 (robustification via POD/kernel PCA) repeats ideas already motivated earlier and could be folded into Section 3 or moved to an appendix; in the NeurIPS format, this would free space for empirical analysis. Section 5 (Related Work) is long; it should be tightened and/or its key takeaways summarized in the conclusion. Section 6 (Empirical Example) is too condensed—please expand the setup, metrics, and baselines and explain what each experiment demonstrates.
- Experiments: The first two tests are polynomial or nearly so; KDMD’s advantage is most evident when the appropriate feature map is unknown. Add experiments with non-polynomial dynamics (e.g., limit cycles with harmonics, piecewise nonlinearities), noisy and stochastic systems, and misspecified kernels to probe robustness and kernel choice. Compare against EDMD with hand-crafted dictionaries, exact/optimized DMD, and, where appropriate, Gaussian-process dynamical models, with quantitative metrics (eigenvalue error vs. ground truth, prediction error, recognition accuracy, change-detection delay/false alarm).
- Kernel and model selection: Provide guidance (and perhaps cross-validation) for kernel choice, bandwidth/degree selection, and truncation level. Discuss computational complexity and memory scaling with τ and p, and the impact of ill-conditioning/regularization (e.g., Tikhonov on Gram matrices).
- Assumptions and theory: Several results assume distinct eigenvalues and that true eigenfunctions lie in the span of kernel principal components. Discuss how multiplicities, continuous spectra, and finite-data/noise affect the estimates, and relate to Koopman theory on attractors and non-normal dynamics.
- Applications: The pre-image step is critical; the MDS-style pre-image is interesting but should be compared to standard pre-image methods (e.g., Kwok & Tsang 2004) and to directly predicting observables. The change-point detection section would benefit from a clearer derivation, a baseline (e.g., kernel CUSUM) with matched priors, and quantitative detection metrics.

Quality
- Technical content is sound and well grounded in linear algebra and RKHS theory. However, empirical validation is limited and lacks strong baselines and quantitative comparisons. Clarify possible typographical errors (see below) and provide more implementation details (centering, normalization, regularizers, stopping criteria).

Clarity
- The paper is difficult to read due to verbosity and dense notation. A shorter, clearer presentation with high-level intuition, pseudocode, and a streamlined related-work section would substantially improve accessibility.

Originality
- Kernelizing DMD and connecting to Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS is a meaningful and nontrivial synthesis building on EDMD and DMD variants. The specific robust kernel-PCA projection and application suite are valuable, though related ideas exist in EDMD and kernel subspace methods.

Significance
- If clarified and empirically strengthened, KDMD could become a widely used tool for learning and analyzing nonlinear dynamics, with impact in ML and application domains. The current experimental evidence is promising but not yet definitive.

Specific comments
- Possible typo in the “toy nonlinear system”: yt+1 = 0.5 yt + (0.92 − 0.5) x_t^2 seems inconsistent; did you intend (0.9^2 − 0.5) x_t^2? Please verify and clarify whether the example is truly nonlinear.
- Please discuss when KDMD reduces to EDMD and exact DMD, and add a citation to Brunton, Proctor, and Kutz (2015) on Koopman subspaces/DMD.
- Provide code and scripts to reproduce all experiments; report runtime and memory.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually strong extension of DMD to RKHS with a practical algorithm.
- Broad applicability and intriguing applications.
- Potential to be influential if presented clearly.

Cons for acceptance
- Exposition is hard to follow; redundancy and lack of pedagogical framing.
- Experimental section is thin, with limited baselines and quantitative results.
- Missing guidance on kernel/model selection and computational considerations.

Overall: Promising and potentially impactful, but the paper would benefit greatly from a concise, pedagogical rewrite and stronger empirical validation. I would support acceptance if the authors significantly improve clarity and expand the experiments; otherwise, I recommend revision.
