### **Summary**

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems via the Koopman operator. The core idea is to leverage the representational power of kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions, thereby overcoming the limitations of standard DMD which relies on linear observables. The authors present two algorithms: a direct formulation based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space, and a more robust, practical version that projects the data onto kernel principal component (POD) bases before estimating the Koopman operator. The utility of the learned Koopman modes and eigenvalues as features for downstream machine learning tasks is demonstrated through applications in state prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection. The work connects the physics- and engineering-centric DMD framework with well-established kernel methods from machine learning.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper's primary strength lies in its elegant and theoretically grounded fusion of Koopman operator theory with kernel methods. This is a natural and valuable contribution, providing a principled way to perform nonlinear spectral analysis of dynamical systems from data. The theoretical development, particularly Theorems 1 and 2, appears sound and provides a solid justification for the proposed algorithms. Furthermore, framing the output of the analysis (the Koopman eigenfunctions) as features for subsequent machine learning tasks is a compelling direction that highlights the potential significance of this work beyond pure system identification.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that temper its potential impact, primarily concerning originality, the experimental evaluation, and the scope of the claims.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Strengths):**

1.  **Novel Framework:** The paper introduces a clear and principled method for kernelizing DMD, which is a logical and important step in advancing data-driven analysis of nonlinear systems.
2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations for both the direct and the robustified algorithms are well-explained and appear correct, providing a solid foundation for the method.
3.  **Potential for Impact:** The idea of using Koopman eigenfunctions as features for various machine learning tasks is powerful. The applications presented, while needing stronger evaluation, effectively demonstrate the versatility and potential of this approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Weaknesses):**

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:**
    *   **Unclear Distinction from Extended DMD:** The paper's contribution, especially the robust version in Section 4, appears highly similar to the Extended DMD (eDMD) framework proposed by Williams et al. (2015, Ref [35]). In eDMD, the system state is lifted into a higher-dimensional space using a dictionary of basis functions. The proposed algorithm's use of a kernel map can be seen as a specific choice for this dictionary. The authors mention this work but do not provide a clear, explicit comparison. To establish originality, the paper must thoroughly discuss the similarities and differences, both theoretically and empirically, between the proposed kernel DMD and eDMD.
    *   **Missing Literature on Kernel-Based System Identification:** The literature review overlooks a substantial body of work on using kernel methods for learning dynamical systems and time-series analysis. Relevant works include, but are not limited to, those by Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc (kernel methods for regression on time series), Tuia et al. (kernel PCA for change detection), Rojo-Álvarez et al. (kernel methods in signal processing), and Franz & Schölkopf (kernel methods for causal inference from time series). Situating the current work within this broader context is essential.

2.  **Quality and Significance of Experiments:**
    *   The experimental validation is not sufficiently rigorous or ambitious for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The applications are treated as illustrative examples rather than thorough empirical evaluations.
    *   **Prediction:** The prediction task lacks comparison against any standard baseline models for time-series forecasting, such as Gaussian Process dynamical models or even simple autoregressive models. Without these comparisons, it is impossible to judge the method's practical performance.
    *   **Dynamics Recognition:** The MDS embedding is a nice visualization, but it is not a quantitative evaluation. This task is essentially time-series classification or clustering. The paper should compare its feature representation against established methods, such as using kernel k-means on the raw data or employing specialized kernels for time-series similarity (e.g., subspace kernels).
    *   **Change-Point Detection:** The comparison is made to a single, somewhat dated, kernel change detection method. The evaluation would be far more convincing with a comparison against more recent, state-of-the-art online kernel change-point detection algorithms to demonstrate a clear advantage.

3.  **Scope and Practical Considerations:**
    *   **Stochastic Systems:** The paper focuses almost exclusively on deterministic systems. While a brief mention is made of extending the Koopman operator to stochastic systems, there is no substantive discussion of how noise or inherent stochasticity would affect the algorithm's performance, stability, or theoretical guarantees. Given that most real-world data is noisy, this is a major limitation. The authors should include experiments on stochastic systems to assess the method's robustness.
    *   **Use of Data Pairs:** The remark at the end of Section 4 mentions that the method can be applied to a collection of consecutive pairs `(x_t, x_{t+1})` rather than a single long sequence. This is an important practical point that needs clarification. How does this formulation handle multidimensional time series where pointwise relationships might be weak or delayed? What are the underlying assumptions about causality and independence between these pairs? A more detailed discussion of the practical implications and limitations of this approach is needed.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the critical distinction from Williams et al. [35] must be clarified. A dedicated subsection comparing the two approaches would significantly improve the manuscript.
*   **Minor Typos:**
    *   In the proof of Theorem 1, the text mentions `M_τ = [ϕ(x_0) ... ϕ(x_{τ-1})]` but later refers to data up to `x_τ`. Please ensure consistent indexing.
    *   In the final equation on page 7, `k(xj, xi)` seems to have a dot instead of a comma in some drafts: `k(xj . xi)`. Please correct this.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting and theoretically appealing idea, its current form suffers from a lack of clear differentiation from prior work and an unconvincing experimental evaluation. The contribution would be much stronger if the authors could clearly articulate the novelty with respect to Extended DMD, broaden the literature review, and provide a much more rigorous and comparative experimental validation for the proposed applications.
