- Paper proposes kernelized DMD in an RKHS to perform Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems.
- Core idea: use kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions without a pre-specified dictionary.
- Algorithm derived from first principles, linking DMD to Arnoldi and Krylov subspaces in feature space.
- Addresses numerical instability of direct Gram–Schmidt by projecting onto kernel principal components (robust variant).
- Theoretical development (Section 3) is sound; Theorem 1 correctly establishes the decomposition.
- Theorem 2 correctly connects eigenvectors of the projected operator to Koopman eigenfunctions, assuming eigenfunctions lie in the PCA span.
- Experiments on synthetic systems (toy system, Hénon map) show the method can recover true system eigenvalues.
- Presentation is clear and accessible, with a helpful introduction to Koopman/DMD for ML audiences.
- Algorithms are clearly presented and the motivation for the robust version is well-argued.
- Novelty: synthesizes Koopman/DMD ideas with kernel methods; kernel formulation is new relative to classic DMD.
- Differs from extended DMD by avoiding a pre-specified basis and using the kernel's implicit basis.
- Potential significance: provides a principled nonparametric tool for extracting dynamic features from time series.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and lacks rigorous comparisons to standard ML baselines.
- Missing baselines: no comparison to RNNs/LSTMs for Hénon map prediction.
- Missing quantitative comparisons for motion-capture recognition against time-series autoencoders or raw-trajectory classifiers.
- Change-point detection comparison to a kernel method lacks clarity on advantage in accuracy, robustness, or cost.
- The interpretability advantage of Koopman decomposition is not emphasized in the applications, which treat the method largely as a feature extractor.
- Arguments for acceptance: elegant synthesis of fields, sound theory, clear connection of algorithm to theory, validated on synthetic examples.
- Arguments against acceptance: perceived incremental novelty (kernelization of DMD), weak experimental evaluation, and lack of demonstrated unique advantage over other methods.
