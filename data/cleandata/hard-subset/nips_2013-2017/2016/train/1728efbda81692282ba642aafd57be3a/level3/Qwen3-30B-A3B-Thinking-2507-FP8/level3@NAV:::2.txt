

This paper proposes a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors extend the existing DMD framework by embedding observable functions into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), enabling a more expressive basis for spectral decomposition compared to traditional linear monomials used in standard DMD. The method involves performing kernel PCA for robustness and applying eigendecomposition to the projected Koopman operator, yielding a modal decomposition that can serve as a feature extraction procedure for dynamical systems.

The paper demonstrates reasonable empirical performance on synthetic and real-world data for applications including dynamics recognition, state prediction, and change-point detection. The mathematical derivation is technically sound, and the paper is well-organized with clear presentation of the theoretical framework.

However, the primary novelty appears limited to kernelizing an existing DMD framework rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach. The core DMD algorithm has been extensively studied in the dynamical systems literature, and the kernel extension represents a natural, rather than groundbreaking, adaptation. The authors fail to sufficiently demonstrate the method's distinct advantages over competing approaches like recurrent neural networks or established change-detection methods. The applications section does not convincingly show how the kernelized DMD outperforms or uniquely contributes to these tasks compared to existing techniques.

While the quality of the technical content is good and the paper is clearly written, the originality is questionable given the incremental nature of the contribution. The significance is also limited, as the paper does not provide compelling evidence that this approach advances the state-of-the-art in a demonstrable way for the target applications. The work would benefit from more thorough comparisons with relevant baselines and a clearer articulation of the unique value proposition.

Overall, while technically sound, the paper's incremental contribution and lack of compelling evidence for distinct advantages make it less suitable for NIPS compared to more innovative work in the field.
