This paper presents a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis, aiming to capture nonlinear dynamics through a spectral representation in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The core idea is to leverage kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with potentially richer, nonlinear basis functions compared to traditional DMD’s reliance on linear monomials. The authors provide a theoretical framework and an algorithm based on Krylov subspace calculations and eigendecomposition. They also propose a robustification method using POD and demonstrate the approach with both synthetic and real-world data.

This work builds upon the growing literature surrounding Koopman operator theory and DMD, particularly its application to nonlinear system identification and control. Prior work, such as that by Rowley et al. (2005) and Schmid (2010), established DMD as a powerful tool for extracting dynamic modes. Subsequent research, including Jovanović et al. (2014) and Williams et al. (2015), explored improvements to DMD’s robustness and applicability to various systems. The extension to RKHS, as proposed here, is a natural progression, aiming to overcome DMD's limitations in representing complex nonlinearities. The connection to kernel methods like Kernel PCA (Schölkopf et al., 1998) is also clearly articulated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. The theoretical concepts are presented clearly, and the derivation of the algorithm is easy to follow.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical foundation appears solid, and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of Koopman operator theory, RKHS, and DMD. The numerical results, while not extensively compared to state-of-the-art methods, illustrate the usefulness of the proposed approach.
*   **Novelty:** Extending DMD to the RKHS framework using the kernel trick is a valuable contribution. It offers a potential route to improved accuracy in capturing nonlinear dynamics compared to standard DMD.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Section 3.1 and Theorem 1:** Section 3.1, specifically Equation (7), and the subsequent proof of Theorem 1 are difficult to understand. The reasoning behind defining the Koopman operator on the feature map ϕ in this way, and the justification for subsequent steps, are not sufficiently explained.
*   **Equation (7) Purpose and Distinction:** The purpose of defining the Koopman operator as (KHϕ)(x) = ϕ ◦ f(x) and its distinction from prior definitions, particularly the relationship with Equation (2), is unclear. A more detailed explanation of why this specific formulation is advantageous is needed.
*   **Mathematical Inconsistency:** There’s a mathematical inconsistency regarding the domain of functions. KH is an operator H→H, while φ is a map M→H, meaning φ may not necessarily belong to H. This requires further clarification and justification. Why is it valid to treat ϕ(x) as an element of H for the purposes of the Koopman operator?
*   **Proof Concerns:** Lines 144–157 of the proof of Theorem 1 are unclear. The phrase “applying the operator KH to both sides” is vague since both sides seem to be scalar values. It's not evident how the Koopman operator is applied in this context.
*   **Strong & Implicit Assumptions:** The assumptions in Theorem 1 are strong and not explicitly stated. Additional assumptions seem to appear within the proof itself without proper acknowledgement or justification. The validity of these implicit assumptions needs to be discussed.
*   **Minor Typographical Errors:** Line 55 contains the typo “the the,” and Line 153 uses “\kappaj / \lambdaj” instead of “\kappai / \lambda_i”.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially valuable extension of DMD.
*   The writing is clear and well-organized, making the work accessible.
*   The proposed method shows promise, even with limited comparisons.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Significant clarity issues in crucial sections (3.1 and the proof of Theorem 1).
*   Mathematical inconsistencies and unclear assumptions undermine confidence in the theoretical framework.
*   Lack of comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising extension of DMD but suffers from clarity issues in its theoretical development. Addressing the identified weaknesses, particularly regarding Section 3.1, the proof of Theorem 1, and the mathematical inconsistencies, would significantly strengthen the paper. A more thorough comparison to existing methods would also be beneficial. As it stands, the paper is a good contribution but requires revisions before acceptance. I recommend a **conditional acceptance** pending major revisions to address the points raised above.
