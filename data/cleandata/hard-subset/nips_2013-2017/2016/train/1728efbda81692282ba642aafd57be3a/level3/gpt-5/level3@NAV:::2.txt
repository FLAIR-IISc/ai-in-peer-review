This paper proposes a kernelized spectral method for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems through the Koopman operator. The main idea is to perform Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) in an RKHS: construct a Krylov matrix of feature-mapped observations, compute an orthonormal basis (via Gram–Schmidt/QR on the kernel Gram matrix), and eigendecompose the projection of the Koopman operator to obtain Ritz values/vectors. A robust variant projects onto kernel PCA/POD directions to mitigate ill-conditioning. The method is then used as a feature extractor for prediction (via a preimage heuristic), embedding/recognition (via kernel principal angles), and sequential change detection. Empirical demonstrations include a toy system, the Hénon map, motion capture sequences, and a Lorenz-map change-point task.

Quality: The derivations are sound and consistent with the Arnoldi/DMD viewpoint, and the RKHS formulation is clear. The robustification via kernel PCA is sensible. However, theoretical guarantees (e.g., consistency of eigenvalue/mode estimation, sample complexity, and conditions under which ψ=0 or small) are absent, and there is limited analysis of numerical stability, regularization, and kernel hyperparameter selection. The preimage step is heuristic and could fail in high dimensions. Experimental evaluation is qualitative; there is little quantitative comparison (e.g., eigenspectrum error, short-horizon prediction RMSE) or ablation (kernel choice, truncation rank).

Clarity: The paper is generally well written with helpful algorithmic summaries. Assumptions (ergodicity, richness/rank conditions, noise) should be made explicit. Computational complexity and memory costs of the kernel Gram operations and out-of-sample evaluation should be discussed.

Originality: Treating composition as a linear operator is standard in Koopman theory; DMD approximates Koopman spectra. The main contribution appears to be a kernelization of DMD with a POD-based stabilization and subspace-analysis applications. This space is close to extended DMD and kernel DMD (e.g., Williams et al., 2015) and exact/optimized/sparse DMD. The paper would benefit from a tighter comparison and clarification of what is genuinely new beyond EDMD/KDMD.

Significance: Dynamical systems are a natural fit for Koopman-based compositions, and the kernel view can increase expressivity. That said, the applications do not yet convincingly demonstrate advantages over strong baselines (EDMD/KDMD, GPDM, PSRs/HMM spectral methods, RNNs/LSTMs for recognition/change detection).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear RKHS reformulation with practical stabilization.
- Useful unifying perspective and accessible exposition.
- Diverse applications illustrating flexibility.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty relative to EDMD/KDMD and DMD variants.
- Weak empirical evidence and missing quantitative/baseline comparisons.
- No theoretical guarantees or sensitivity analyses.

Suggestions: Add rigorous comparisons to EDMD/KDMD, exact/optimized/sparse DMD; include quantitative metrics and ablations; analyze kernel/regularization choices; and clarify computational scaling and assumptions.
