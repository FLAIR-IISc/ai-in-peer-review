This paper proposes a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) method extended to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to leverage the Koopman operator, a linear representation of a nonlinear system, and perform modal decomposition in RKHS to capture complex dynamics beyond linear approximations. The authors present an algorithm based on calculating orthogonal bases for a Krylov matrix and performing eigendecomposition, demonstrating its application to state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in dynamical systems and machine learning. The Koopman operator and its spectral analysis are well-established concepts [16], with DMD being a popular technique for approximating its spectra [25, 26]. The authors correctly position their work within this context, acknowledging related approaches such as proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) [12] and spectral methods for system identification [11, 34]. Kernel methods, particularly kernel PCA [28], are also appropriately cited and integrated into the proposed framework. The connection to subspace identification methods [23, 14] is noted as an interesting direction for future research.

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear presentation of the theoretical foundation, extending DMD to a more expressive RKHS setting. The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of the underlying mathematics and provide a detailed algorithmic description. The inclusion of empirical examples, while relatively simple, helps illustrate the method’s functionality. The robustification technique using POD bases (Section 4) is a valuable addition, addressing a potential practical issue with the algorithm's conditioning. The applications section, though not groundbreaking, showcases potential use cases.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The primary limitation is the incremental nature of the contribution. While the kernelization of DMD is a logical extension, it doesn't represent a fundamentally new approach. DMD itself is already a well-known technique in the dynamical systems community, and extending it to RKHS, while mathematically sound, doesn’t dramatically alter the landscape. The novelty for readers familiar with kernel methods is highlighted by the authors – treating function composition as a linear operator – but this perspective isn't entirely novel within the context of kernelized learning. 

Furthermore, the empirical evaluation lacks depth. The synthetic examples confirm the method works as expected, but the real-world application to locomotion data (Section 7) feels somewhat superficial. The performance isn’t compared to state-of-the-art methods in dynamics recognition, and the advantages of using the proposed approach over existing techniques (e.g., recurrent neural networks or other kernel-based classifiers) are not convincingly demonstrated. The change-point detection application, while interesting, similarly lacks a comparative analysis against existing methods [7]. The reliance on a single dataset (CMU Motion Capture) and limited hyperparameter tuning further weaken the empirical evidence.

The clarity of the writing is generally good, although the mathematical notation can be dense at times. The organization is logical, following a standard structure for theoretical papers. However, the paper could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the core concepts and a clearer discussion of the practical implications of the algorithm's parameters.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a mathematically sound extension of DMD to RKHS, providing a more flexible framework for analyzing nonlinear dynamics.
*   The inclusion of the robustification technique using POD bases addresses a potential practical challenge.
*   The paper is well-written and demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The contribution is incremental, lacking significant novelty beyond kernelizing an existing algorithm.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited in scope and doesn’t convincingly demonstrate the method’s superiority over existing approaches.
*   The applications section feels superficial and lacks a comparative analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the technical soundness and clarity are commendable, the limited novelty and weak empirical evaluation prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The paper represents a reasonable extension to existing work, but it doesn't offer a compelling enough advance to warrant a high ranking at NIPS. I believe the paper could benefit significantly from a more thorough experimental evaluation, including comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, and a more focused discussion of the practical advantages of the proposed approach.
