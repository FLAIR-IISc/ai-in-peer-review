## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper proposes a kernelized version of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems through the lens of Koopman operator spectral analysis. The core idea is to perform DMD in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), allowing for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions, potentially overcoming limitations of traditional DMD which relies on linear monomials. The authors present an algorithm based on Krylov subspace calculations and demonstrate its application to state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data.

The paper builds upon a well-established line of work in spectral analysis of dynamical systems, referencing key contributions like Koopman operator theory, POD, and various DMD variants. It correctly positions itself within this landscape, acknowledging the existing literature and highlighting the potential advantage of kernel methods for capturing more complex dynamics. The extension to RKHS is a natural progression, leveraging the power of kernel methods to represent nonlinear functions. 

However, the novelty of this work appears incremental. While the application of kernel methods to DMD is a logical step, the core algorithm – DMD itself – is already well-established. The paper’s strength lies in the clear presentation of the kernelized adaptation and the theoretical justification, but it doesn’t introduce a fundamentally new approach. The empirical evaluation, while demonstrating functionality, doesn’t convincingly showcase a significant advantage over competing methods like recurrent neural networks or existing change-detection techniques. The applications presented – prediction, embedding, and change detection – are standard benchmarks and the paper doesn’t provide compelling evidence that this kernelized DMD outperforms existing solutions on these tasks. The robustness improvement using POD bases is a valuable addition, addressing a potential practical issue. 

**Strengths:**

*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the method.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and justification for the kernelized approach.
*   Addresses a potential practical issue with ill-conditioning through POD projection.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty – primarily a kernelization of an existing algorithm.
*   Empirical results do not demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   Applications are not particularly compelling or unique.
*   The connection to related work in subspace identification could be more thoroughly explored.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Presents a technically sound and well-explained extension of DMD.
*   May be of interest to researchers specifically working with kernel methods and dynamical systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty and limited impact on the field.
*   Lack of compelling empirical evidence demonstrating significant advantages.
*   The paper feels more like a technical extension than a breakthrough contribution.



**Overall:** I would lean towards **rejection**. While the paper is technically sound and clearly written, the limited novelty and lack of compelling empirical results do not justify acceptance to a competitive conference like NIPS. The work might be more suitable for a workshop or a more specialized venue.
