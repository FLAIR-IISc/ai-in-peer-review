- Proposes extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for richer Koopman spectral analysis using nonlinear kernel bases.  
- Core algorithm computes orthogonal bases for the RKHS Krylov matrix and performs eigendecomposition to extract features/eigenfunctions.  
- Demonstrated on synthetic and real-world tasks: state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.  
- Connects appropriately to prior work (POD, DMD, balanced mode decomposition, subspace identification) with adequate citations.  
- Provides a Matlab implementation, aiding reproducibility.  
- Novelty: applying RKHS/kernel methods to Koopman/DMD is a meaningful and original extension.  
- Soundness: theoretical foundation appears rigorous, with relevant theorems supporting the method.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, organized, and accessible even to non-experts.  
- Empirical validation: experiments show promising results across multiple applications.  
- Reproducibility: code release is a valuable contribution.  
- Missing baseline comparison: no direct quantitative comparison to standard (linear) DMD on the same synthetic datasets.  
- Lack of quantified error analysis for approximating Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions.  
- Insufficient exploration of kernel selection and sensitivity to kernel parameters (e.g., Gaussian width).  
- Computational complexity (Gram matrix computation, orthogonalization) and scaling are not discussed.  
- Practical limitations: assumptions in Theorem 2 (e.g., φj ∈ Φ and κj in span(U)) may be restrictive and are not explored for violations.  
- Acceptance argument: novel, well-motivated, mathematically solid, and reproducible work with promising empirical results.  
- Rejection concerns: limited comparative analysis, inadequate kernel discussion, and missing treatment of computational/practical issues.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to add DMD baseline comparisons, a kernel sensitivity study, and a brief discussion of computational complexity and practical limitations.
