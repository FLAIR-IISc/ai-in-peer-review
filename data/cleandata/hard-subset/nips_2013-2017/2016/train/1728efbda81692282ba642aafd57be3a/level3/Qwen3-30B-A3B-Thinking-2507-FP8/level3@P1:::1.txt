

The paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. This approach generalizes traditional DMD by using nonlinear basis functions (via kernel methods) instead of linear monomials, potentially capturing more complex dynamics. The authors present a theoretical framework for Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS, developing an algorithm that computes orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performs eigendecomposition. They further propose a robustified variant using POD bases to handle ill-conditioning, and demonstrate applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical extension of Koopman spectral analysis using kernel methods, providing a more expressive representation of nonlinear dynamics compared to traditional DMD. The robustification step using POD bases is well-motivated and addresses a practical limitation of the basic algorithm. The empirical results on synthetic and real-world data demonstrate the method's applicability, particularly in the locomotion recognition task (Fig. 3) and change-point detection (Fig. 4).

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. First, the relationship to Williams et al.'s Extended DMD (2015) is unclear. Extended DMD also uses pre-determined basis functions instead of monomials, and the current paper's approach is conceptually similar. The authors should explicitly compare their method to Extended DMD, highlighting theoretical and practical differences. Section 4 does not sufficiently distinguish from Williams et al., which undermines the paper's novelty claim.

Second, the paper misses several relevant prior works in kernel-based dynamical systems, including Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc (2005), Tuia et al. (2011), Rojo-Álvarez et al. (2012), Mouattamid & Schaback (2013), and Franz & Schölkopf (2004). These works explore kernel methods for dynamical systems, and their omission weakens the paper's claim of novelty.

Third, the paper only considers deterministic systems, but the Koopman operator can be extended to stochastic systems. The authors should discuss how their method would perform in stochastic settings, including potential limitations and performance characteristics.

Fourth, the experimental evaluation is limited. The prediction task (Fig. 2) is standard for DMD methods but lacks comparison to state-of-the-art time-series prediction methods. The change-point detection (Fig. 4) should be compared with established online kernel change-point detection methods. The dynamics recognition (Fig. 3) lacks comparison to time-series clustering methods like kernel k-means or subspace kernels.

Fifth, the paper uses "pairs of consecutive observables" rather than sequences, but doesn't clearly explain the practical implications for multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relations. The causal assumptions underlying this approach need to be clarified.

Minor issues include a typo on page 7 (indexing τ-1 vs. τ) and a missing comma in k(xi.xj) (last equation on p.7).

In terms of NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical foundation is sound, but the experimental evaluation lacks comparative analysis with state-of-the-art methods.
- Clarity: The paper is well-written, but methodological points (like the use of consecutive pairs) need clarification.
- Originality: The kernel-based extension of DMD is not entirely novel, particularly in light of Williams et al. (2015) and the missing prior work.
- Significance: The method has potential applications in dynamics analysis, but limited evaluation and lack of comparison to existing methods diminish its significance.

The paper makes a contribution to Koopman spectral analysis by extending DMD to RKHS, but its novelty is questionable. The experimental evaluation is not sufficiently ambitious to demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. With the suggested revisions—particularly explicit comparison to Williams et al. (2015), inclusion of relevant prior work, and more comprehensive experiments—the paper could be strengthened significantly. As it stands, the paper's contributions are marginal relative to existing literature, and the evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate advantages over current state-of-the-art methods.
