### Summary

This paper introduces Kernel Dynamic Mode Decomposition (KDMD), a powerful generalization of the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) algorithm for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to apply the DMD algorithm not to the raw data but to a feature map of the data in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This implicitly replaces the linear dictionary of observables in standard DMD with a much richer, nonlinear set of basis functions defined by the kernel. The authors present a formal algorithm based on the Arnoldi method in RKHS and a more practical, robust version using a projection onto kernel principal components (analogous to SVD truncation in standard DMD). The method is demonstrated on synthetic examples and applied to several machine learning tasks, including state prediction, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection.

This work builds upon a growing body of literature on Koopman operator theory for data-driven analysis of dynamics. It connects DMD, a method popular in fluid dynamics [25, 26], with kernel methods, a cornerstone of machine learning. The proposed KDMD is closely related to Extended DMD [35], where a pre-determined dictionary of functions is used; KDMD provides an elegant and powerful way to implicitly specify this dictionary through the choice of a kernel.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound. The theoretical derivations, particularly the extension of the DMD algorithm to an RKHS in Theorem 1 and the robustification via kernel PCA in Theorem 2, appear correct and provide a solid foundation for the proposed method. The connection drawn between the algorithm's outputs and the true Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions under certain assumptions is clear and well-argued. The work is a complete contribution, presenting theory, an algorithm, and several application examples.

### Clarity

The main weakness of this paper is its clarity. The manuscript is overly verbose and mathematically dense, which obscures the simple and elegant core idea. For a NIPS audience, who may not be deeply familiar with Koopman theory or DMD, the presentation is cryptic and difficult to penetrate. The paper would be significantly improved and likely have a much greater impact if it were rewritten in a more pedagogical style. Many of the mathematical statements could be made more concise, and more intuition could be provided to bridge the concepts for a broader audience.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
*   The connection between the robustification in Section 4 and the standard practice of using a truncated SVD in DMD [33] should be made more explicit. The section as it stands feels somewhat redundant, as this is a well-established technique for regularizing DMD-type algorithms. The content could be condensed and integrated with Section 3.
*   The application descriptions in Section 7 are overly condensed. These sections demonstrate the method's utility, but they read as brief proofs-of-concept rather than thorough experimental evaluations. Expanding on one or two of these with more detail, quantitative results, and comparisons to relevant baselines would be more convincing.

### Originality

The paper's core idea is novel and represents a significant contribution. While Extended DMD [35] previously proposed using general dictionaries of observables, the use of kernel methods to implicitly define an infinitely rich dictionary is a clever and powerful step forward. This work provides an important bridge between the dynamical systems community and the machine learning community, framing a powerful analysis tool in the language of kernel methods. The proposed applications, while not fully developed, also showcase novel uses of Koopman spectral analysis as a feature extraction tool for machine learning tasks.

### Significance

The results are potentially very important. DMD is a rising star in data-driven science, and its kernelization makes it applicable to a much broader class of problems and more accessible to the machine learning community. If the presentation were improved, this paper could become a highly cited and foundational work for nonlinear time-series analysis and representation learning for dynamical systems. The method provides a principled way to extract features (the Koopman modes and eigenvalues) that linearize the underlying nonlinear dynamics, which is a significant advance over purely geometric methods like PCA.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

*   **Rewrite for a Broader Audience:** I strongly encourage the authors to restructure the paper to be more accessible. For instance, start with the high-level intuition: DMD finds a linear model for the evolution of observables, and kernels allow us to use a very rich set of nonlinear observables. This would motivate the subsequent technical details.
*   **Discussion on Kernel Choice:** The performance of KDMD is critically dependent on the choice of kernel, yet this is not discussed in depth. The experiments use polynomial and Gaussian kernels, which work well for the chosen problems. However, some guidance on how to select a kernel based on prior knowledge of the system would be invaluable. For instance, the toy system has polynomial eigenfunctions, making the polynomial kernel an obvious choice; this connection should be explicitly stated.
*   **Minor Correction:** The toy system `xt+1= 0.9xt, yt+1= 0.5yt+(0.92−0.5)x^2_t` is described as nonlinear, which is correct due to the `x^2_t` term. Perhaps the original note I made was in error. The system is a good choice as its simple nonlinearity is perfectly captured by the chosen polynomial kernel.
*   **Strengthening Related Work:** The link to Williams et al. [35] is present but could be emphasized further by stating that KDMD is a specific, powerful instance of the Extended DMD framework. Additionally, consider citing recent work on data-driven discovery of dynamical systems, such as Brunton et al., "Discovering governing equations from data by sparse identification of nonlinear dynamical systems," PNAS (2016), which shares similar goals.

### Arguments For/Against Acceptance

**Pro:**
*   Presents a novel, powerful, and theoretically sound generalization of DMD using kernel methods.
*   The idea is elegant and bridges the fields of dynamical systems and machine learning.
*   The method has high potential for significant impact on nonlinear time-series analysis and representation learning.

**Con:**
*   The paper is poorly written and very difficult to understand, which severely limits its accessibility and immediate impact.
*   The experimental validation, particularly in the applications section, is preliminary and lacks depth and comparison with baseline methods.

**Recommendation:** While the core contribution is strong, the poor clarity is a major issue. I would recommend acceptance only on the condition of a major revision to improve the presentation. As it stands, the paper is unlikely to be understood or appreciated by much of the NIPS community. A more pedagogical version of this paper would be a fantastic contribution.
