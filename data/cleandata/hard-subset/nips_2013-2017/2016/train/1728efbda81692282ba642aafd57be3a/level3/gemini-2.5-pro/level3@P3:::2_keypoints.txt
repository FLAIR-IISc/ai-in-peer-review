- Proposes kernel DMD: lift state data to an RKHS and approximate Koopman eigendecomposition on a data-spanned subspace, using kernel PCA for robustness, to extract Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions as dynamical features.
- Well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound with accessible derivations for a machine learning audience.
- Establishes a principled theoretical connection between the eigendecomposition of the projected operator and Koopman spectral properties.
- Presents an elegant conceptual bridge between Koopman/DMD analysis and kernel methods, framing composition as a linear operator in feature space.
- Natural and well-motivated extension of DMD that replaces linear observables with implicit high-dimensional kernel features, potentially capturing richer eigenfunctions.
- Limited originality: kernelizing DMD is incremental relative to existing DMD variants (e.g., extended DMD) and the contribution risks being seen as the straightforward application of the kernel trick.
- Experimental evaluation is unconvincing: lacks comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., LSTMs, GRUs, Gaussian process dynamical models), so practical utility is unclear.
- Applications chosen are generic and do not showcase the unique interpretability advantages of Koopman spectral decomposition (modes, frequencies, growth/decay rates).
- Change-point detection experiment is weak: relies on a heuristic score and compares against only one kernel method rather than established statistical baselines.
- Recommend clarifying and empirically demonstrating how kernel DMD differs from and improves upon extended DMD and other nonlinear-observable methods (e.g., avoiding explicit basis selection).
- Recommend stronger experimental comparisons to modern time-series models and identification of scenarios where kernel DMD clearly outperforms (data efficiency, interpretability, oscillatory systems).
- Suggest reframing applications to exploit Koopman spectral insights (e.g., using learned modes to identify fundamental frequencies in gait) and expanding change-point comparisons to mature detection methods.
