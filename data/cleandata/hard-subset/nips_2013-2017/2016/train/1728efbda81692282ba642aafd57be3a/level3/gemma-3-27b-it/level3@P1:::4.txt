This paper proposes a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) method leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to extend traditional DMD, which approximates the Koopman operator’s spectra using linear monomials, to utilize the richer, nonlinear basis functions afforded by kernel methods. The authors present an algorithm based on calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition, effectively providing a feature extraction procedure for nonlinear dynamical systems. The method is demonstrated on synthetic data and real-world examples, including applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The work builds upon a growing body of research in spectral methods for dynamical systems, connecting to prior work on proper orthogonal decomposition (POD), DMD, and related techniques like balanced mode decomposition. The authors appropriately position their contribution within this context, highlighting the limitations of linear monomial-based approaches and the potential benefits of RKHS for capturing more complex dynamics. They also correctly point out the connection to subspace identification methods used in system control. The paper acknowledges related work adequately and provides sufficient citations to relevant literature, including key papers on DMD, Koopman operators, and kernel methods. A Matlab implementation of the method is provided, which is a valuable contribution for reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of RKHS to Koopman spectral analysis and DMD is a novel extension. Utilizing kernel methods provides a potentially more expressive framework for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions compared to traditional linear approaches.
*   **Soundness:** The mathematical formulation appears rigorous and well-motivated. The connection to the Krylov subspace method and the theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) provide a strong foundation for the algorithm.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanation of the method is easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with all the underlying concepts.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world examples provides initial evidence of the method's effectiveness. The applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition demonstrate the potential utility of the extracted features.
*   **Reproducibility:** Providing code promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research by the community.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Comparative Analysis:** While the empirical results are promising, a direct comparison to standard DMD (without RKHS) on the synthetic data is missing. Demonstrating an improvement in accuracy or performance compared to the baseline DMD on the same datasets would strengthen the argument for the benefits of the proposed method.  Specifically, quantifying the error in approximating the Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions would be beneficial.
*   **Kernel Selection:** The paper briefly mentions kernel choice but doesn't thoroughly explore its impact on the results. A more detailed discussion, perhaps with a sensitivity analysis demonstrating the effect of different kernel parameters (e.g., kernel width for the Gaussian kernel) or even different kernel functions on the accuracy of the approximation, would be valuable. Simulations exploring different kernel options and their performance would be helpful.
*   **Computational Cost:**  The computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly the calculation of the Gram matrix and orthogonal basis, is not explicitly discussed.  A brief discussion of the computational scaling with respect to the dataset size and potential strategies for mitigating computational cost would be useful.
*   **Practical Limitations:** The assumptions required for Theorem 2 (φj ∈ Φ and κj in the subspace spanned by U) appear restrictive. A discussion of the implications of these assumptions and potential approaches for dealing with situations where they are not fully satisfied would be useful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and mathematically sound extension of existing techniques for Koopman spectral analysis.
*   The method is well-motivated and clearly explained.
*   The empirical results, while preliminary, suggest promising performance.
*   The provision of code enhances reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a direct comparative analysis against standard DMD limits the ability to assess the true benefits of the proposed method.
*   The discussion of kernel selection is insufficient.
*   The computational complexity and practical limitations of the method are not fully addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution. The clarity of presentation and the mathematical rigor are particularly commendable. However, addressing the weaknesses regarding the comparative analysis and kernel selection would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should include a comparison to standard DMD on the synthetic data, conduct a more thorough investigation of kernel choice, and briefly discuss the computational complexity and practical limitations of their method.
