**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to approximate the eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator using the rich, nonlinear basis functions implicitly defined by a kernel function. The authors derive an algorithm, which they term kernel DMD (kDMD), that performs a modal decomposition by finding an orthogonal basis for the Krylov subspace in the feature space and then solving an eigenvalue problem for the projection of the Koopman operator. Recognizing potential numerical instability, a more robust version of the algorithm is also proposed, which leverages kernel PCA (equivalent to projection onto POD bases in the feature space). The paper effectively frames this decomposition as a feature extraction technique for dynamical systems and demonstrates its utility in several machine learning applications, including state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition, using both synthetic and real-world data.

**Relation to Prior Work**

The work is well-situated within the context of data-driven analysis of dynamical systems. It builds directly upon the recently popular DMD method, which approximates the Koopman operator's spectrum using linear observables. This paper's primary contribution is the application of the "kernel trick" to DMD, allowing it to capture complex nonlinear dynamics without explicitly defining a dictionary of basis functions. This conceptually links DMD, a method from the physics and fluid dynamics communities, with well-established kernel methods from machine learning. The authors correctly relate their approach to other DMD variants, such as extended DMD [35], noting the similarity of the robust version to methods that work on pre-determined basis functions. The connection to subspace identification methods and spectral learning for graphical models is also appropriately mentioned.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. It provides a good introductory background to the Koopman operator and DMD before carefully developing the proposed kernelized extension. The separation of the direct method (Section 3) and the robustified method (Section 4) is logical and helps the reader appreciate the practical challenges and solutions.

2.  **Novelty and Technical Contribution:** The combination of Koopman spectral analysis with kernel methods is a natural and powerful idea. This extension of DMD to RKHS is a significant contribution, providing a principled way to perform nonlinear spectral decomposition. The theoretical development, particularly in connecting the algorithm's output to the Koopman spectrum (Theorems 1 and 2), appears solid and provides a good foundation for the method.

3.  **Significance and Applications:** The paper goes beyond a purely theoretical presentation by demonstrating the proposed method on a diverse set of interesting machine learning tasks. The applications in prediction, change-point detection, and motion recognition show the practical potential of using Koopman modes as features, making the work highly relevant to the NIPS community. The empirical results, while not extensively benchmarked, effectively illustrate the method's capabilities.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

The main weakness of the paper lies in a lack of precision in some of the core theoretical definitions and proofs, which could lead to confusion for the reader.

1.  **Clarity of the Operator Definition (Sec. 3):** There is some ambiguity in the definition of the Koopman operator in RKHS, `KH`. Equation (7) defines `(KHϕ)(x) = ϕ ◦ f(x)`. This appears to be an operator acting on the feature map `ϕ: M → H`, rather than on functions in the Hilbert space `H`. However, later `KH` is treated as a linear operator on `H` with eigenfunctions `φj`. The eigenfunction `φj` is defined as a map from `M` to `C`, but the Ritz vectors `φ̃j` derived in Theorem 1 are elements of `H`. The relationship between these objects is unclear. Is `KH` an operator such that for any state `x`, `KH(ϕ(x)) = ϕ(f(x))`? If so, this should be stated more clearly. A more standard approach might be to define the Koopman operator `K` acting on scalar observables of the form `g_v(x) = <ϕ(x), v>` for `v ∈ H`, which would more clearly connect `KH` to `K`.

2.  **Proof of Theorem 1:** The proof is difficult to follow. In particular, the derivation of the companion matrix `F` involves the expression `Mτ⁻¹KHMτ`. It is not clear how the operator `KH` is applied to the matrix `Mτ`, whose columns are vectors in `H`. Please provide a more detailed and rigorous explanation of this step. Furthermore, the proof seems to rely on unstated assumptions. For instance, the existence of `Mτ⁻¹` requires that the vectors `{ϕ(x₀), ..., ϕ(x_{τ-1})}` be linearly independent in `H`. This is a strong assumption that depends on the data and the choice of kernel, and it should be stated explicitly and discussed.

3.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** While the experiments are illustrative, they lack comparison to relevant state-of-the-art methods. For instance, how does the proposed kDMD compare to Extended DMD [35], which the authors acknowledge is similar? A comparison on the prediction or recognition tasks against other nonlinear system identification or time-series modeling techniques would significantly strengthen the paper's empirical claims and provide a better sense of its practical advantages and limitations.

**Arguments Pro/Con Acceptance**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, elegant, and powerful extension of DMD using kernel methods. The theoretical idea is sound and the connection to Koopman operator theory is appealing. The demonstrated applications are relevant and compelling for the machine learning community. The paper is well-written and presents a complete piece of work.

*   **Con:** There are significant clarity issues in the core theoretical definitions and proofs that need to be addressed to ensure the work is fully understood and reproducible. The experimental validation could be strengthened with more rigorous comparisons to alternative methods.

**Minor Issues/Typos:**
*   Line 55: "the the" -> "the"
*   The proof of Theorem 2 (not 1 as in my initial notes, but similar logic may apply) seems to use `κj` and `λj` but then later refers to `δij` without defining `i`. This might just be a notational inconsistency. Please double-check the indices throughout the proofs.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution. The core idea is excellent and the applications are well-chosen. I would recommend acceptance, provided the authors can address the lack of clarity in the theoretical formulation in Section 3 and the proof of Theorem 1. Clarifying these points would substantially improve the quality and impact of the paper.
