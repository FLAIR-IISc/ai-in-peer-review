This paper presents a novel method for spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems based on the Koopman operator within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors extend dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) to leverage the benefits of kernel methods, aiming to provide a more expressive representation of Koopman eigenfunctions than traditional DMD, which relies on linear monomials. The core contribution lies in formulating a modal decomposition algorithm grounded in the calculation of orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and subsequent eigendecomposition. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough introduction to relevant concepts and a detailed presentation of the proposed methodology. Empirical validation is provided through synthetic and real-world examples, including applications to state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of previous work in spectral methods for dynamical systems. The authors appropriately cite relevant literature, including foundational papers on the Koopman operator [16], DMD [25, 26], proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) [12], and kernel methods [30, 58]. The connection to existing DMD variants, such as optimized and exact DMD [8, 33], is also acknowledged. The introduction effectively contextualizes the work within the broader landscape of nonlinear system identification and machine learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation appears technically sound, leveraging established concepts from functional analysis and numerical linear algebra. The extension of DMD to RKHS provides a theoretically principled way to incorporate nonlinear basis functions.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is remarkably well-written and organized. The concepts are explained clearly, and the notation is generally consistent. The step-by-step description of the algorithm is easy to follow.
* **Completeness:** The paper presents a relatively complete framework, including a robustification strategy using POD (Section 4) to address potential ill-conditioning issues and a demonstration of several potential applications.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide empirical results on both synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating the feasibility and potential of the proposed method. The applications showcased (prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition) are relevant and illustrate the versatility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Derivation (Section 3.1, Theorem 1):** The derivation in Section 3.1, particularly around Equation (7) and the subsequent proof of Theorem 1, lacks sufficient clarity. The purpose and distinction between Equation (7) and Equation (2) are not immediately apparent. The phrase “applying the operator KH to both sides” in the proof (lines 144–157) is unclear, as both sides appear to be scalar quantities. This section requires a more careful and detailed explanation.
* **Mathematical Consistency:** A potential mathematical inconsistency exists concerning the domain of the Koopman operator. KH is defined as an operator H→H, while the eigenfunction φ is a map M→H. This implies φ may not necessarily reside in H, which requires further clarification and justification.
* **Assumptions and Proof Validity:** The assumptions underlying Theorem 1 are not explicitly stated and appear restrictive. Furthermore, additional assumptions seem to be introduced within the proof itself, and their validity needs to be discussed. The jump in logic from stating φ ∈ Φ to applying certain operations feels abrupt.
* **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** While the empirical results are encouraging, the paper lacks a thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods for nonlinear system identification. Benchmarking against existing techniques like extended DMD [35] or sparse identification of nonlinear dynamics (SINDy) [44] would significantly strengthen the claims of novelty and performance.
* **Minor Errors:** The paper contains some minor typographical errors (e.g., "the the" on line 55 and a variable mix-up on line 153: \kappaj / \lambdaj instead of \kappai / \lambda_i). These are minor, but attention to detail is important.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded extension of DMD using kernel methods.
* The algorithm is well-defined and appears to be applicable to a wide range of nonlinear dynamical systems.
* The empirical results demonstrate the feasibility and potential of the approach.
* The clear writing style and organization make the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of clarity in the derivation of key results (Section 3.1, Theorem 1) raises concerns about the technical soundness of the approach.
* The absence of a comprehensive comparative evaluation limits the ability to assess the true significance of the contribution.
* The mathematical inconsistencies regarding the domain of the Koopman operator need to be addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a potentially valuable contribution to the field of nonlinear system identification. The extension of DMD to RKHS is a promising direction, and the proposed algorithm is well-motivated and clearly presented. However, the concerns regarding the clarity of the derivation, the mathematical consistency, and the limited comparative evaluation prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance in its current form. I recommend a **weak accept** with the strong encouragement that the authors address the identified weaknesses, particularly by clarifying the derivation in Section 3.1 and providing a more thorough comparative evaluation against existing methods. Specifically, a detailed explanation of Equation (7), its connection to Equation (2), and a rigorous justification of the mathematical assumptions are critical.
