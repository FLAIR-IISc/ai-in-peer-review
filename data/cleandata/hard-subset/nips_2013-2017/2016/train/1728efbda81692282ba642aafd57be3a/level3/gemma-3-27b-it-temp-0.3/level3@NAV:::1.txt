## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper introduces a kernel-based extension to Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) within the framework of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to leverage the expressiveness of kernel functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, potentially overcoming limitations of traditional DMD which relies on linear monomials. The authors present an algorithm based on Krylov subspace calculations and demonstrate its application to both synthetic and real-world data, including prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.

The work builds upon the growing body of research applying Koopman operator theory to nonlinear system analysis and control. While DMD has gained traction, this paper offers a potentially valuable extension by incorporating kernel methods, which are known for their ability to capture complex nonlinear relationships. The proposed approach is theoretically sound, and the empirical results, while limited in scope, demonstrate the feasibility of the method.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. A significant concern is the lack of a clear and detailed comparison to Williams et al. (2015) – extended DMD – which also utilizes pre-determined basis functions. The authors need to explicitly articulate the differences in approach, implementation, and performance. Furthermore, the paper overlooks a substantial body of relevant work in kernel-based dynamical systems, including contributions from Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Álvarez et al., Mouattamid & Schaback, and Franz & Schölkopf. A thorough review of this literature is crucial to properly position the work within the field.

The focus on deterministic systems is also a limitation. The performance and limitations of the method in stochastic settings are not discussed, which is a significant omission given the prevalence of noise in real-world applications. The choice to use pairs of consecutive observables instead of sequences is also not fully justified, particularly for multidimensional time series where pointwise relationships may be weak. A discussion of the causal assumptions inherent in this approach is necessary.

Finally, the experimental evaluation is relatively modest. While the prediction task is standard, the lack of comparison to time-series clustering methods (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernels) and state-of-the-art online kernel change-point detection limits the impact of the results. More ambitious and comprehensive evaluations are needed to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method. Minor typos (indexing of tau and punctuation in the kernel definition) should also be addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of kernel methods to Koopman spectral analysis.
*   Theoretically sound approach with a clear algorithmic description.
*   Demonstrated feasibility on synthetic and real-world data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Insufficient comparison to related work (Williams et al., kernel-based dynamical systems).
*   Lack of analysis in stochastic settings.
*   Limited experimental evaluation.
*   Unclear justification for using pairs of observables.



**Overall:** I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. Addressing the identified weaknesses, particularly the comparison to existing work and the expansion of the experimental evaluation, would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
