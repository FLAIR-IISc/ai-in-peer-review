Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes KDMD, a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for approximating Koopman spectral components of nonlinear dynamical systems. The key idea is to lift observables into a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) via a feature map, construct a Krylov subspace from feature snapshots, and compute Ritz eigenvalues/vectors of the projected Koopman operator. The authors first present a direct RKHS analogue of DMD via Gram–Schmidt in feature space, then derive a more numerically robust variant using kernel PCA/POD truncation. They illustrate KDMD on toy and canonical chaotic systems and sketch applications to short-term prediction via pre-images, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using subspace angles.

This work is closely related to DMD and extended DMD (EDMD) in the literature. DMD approximates Koopman spectra using linear monomials of observables; EDMD (Williams et al., 2015) generalizes this to an arbitrary dictionary of basis functions. KDMD can be viewed as EDMD with implicitly infinite dictionaries through kernels, coupled with a particular Krylov/POD realization and a clear operator-theoretic framing. The paper also intersects with work surveyed by Brunton et al. (2015), including exact/optimized DMD and sparsity-promoting DMD.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A principled RKHS formulation of Koopman spectral analysis that generalizes DMD beyond linear dictionaries; strong alignment with operator-theoretic learning.
- Algorithmic clarity (in principle): The connection to Krylov subspaces and Ritz pairs provides a clean spectral picture; the POD-regularized variant addresses well-known numerical issues from DMD.
- Potential impact: If presented pedagogically, KDMD could become a widely used, citable tool for learning dynamics and feature extraction in nonlinear systems.
- Breadth: The paper outlines several downstream ML applications (prediction, change detection, recognition) that showcase the flexibility of Koopman features.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation: The manuscript is difficult to read—overly mathematical, verbose, and cryptic for a NIPS audience. Many statements could be made far more concisely, with intuition and diagrams. A pedagogical rewrite is strongly encouraged.
- Redundancy and imbalance: Section 4 is largely redundant with the introduction’s motivation for robustness; Section 5 (Related Work) is long relative to the condensed experiments in Section 6; Section 6 needs development akin to Section 5. Section 5 could be tightened and some of its insights moved to discussion/conclusion, while Section 6 should be expanded with examples and comparisons.
- Empirical evaluation: Limited and largely illustrative. Lacks comparisons to baselines (DMD, exact/optimized DMD, EDMD/KEDMD). No ablations on kernel choices or robustness to noise.
- Practical guidance: KDMD is most useful when an appropriate kernel/feature map can be guessed (as the first two test problems are polynomial), but the paper provides little guidance on kernel selection or dictionary design (beyond brief comments in Sec. 5). This is crucial for practitioners.
- Minor concerns: The “nonlinear” toy example reads as linear in x; likely there is a typo in the coefficient (0.92 vs 0.9^2) and/or in calling the overall system nonlinear. Please clarify.

Quality
- Theoretical soundness: The operator-theoretic derivations and links to Ritz values, Krylov subspaces, and kernel PCA are standard and appear correct. However, proofs are terse and heavy on notation; moving some derivations to an appendix and adding intuition/pseudocode would help.
- Empirical support: Current experiments are too limited to substantiate robustness and superiority. Please add: (i) quantitative comparisons to EDMD and DMD variants on synthetic systems with known Koopman spectra; (ii) sensitivity to noise and to kernel width/degree; (iii) runtime/memory scaling vs number of snapshots and kernel bandwidth.
- Reproducibility: Code is provided, which is appreciated, but the paper should specify kernels, hyperparameters, data preprocessing, and model selection procedures in-line.

Clarity and organization
- The paper would benefit from:
  - A high-level figure showing the KDMD pipeline (data → Gram matrices → POD → projected operator → eigenpairs → modes/eigenfunctions).
  - One-page “intuitive” overview of Koopman in RKHS, deferring heavy Hilbert-space notation to an appendix.
  - Algorithm boxes with clear inputs/outputs and computational complexity.
  - Consolidating or removing Section 4 in the main text; trimming Section 5; expanding Section 6 with illustrative examples and ablations; moving ancillary details to supplementary material.

Originality
- KDMD as presented is a natural but meaningful step: kernelizing EDMD/DMD with a Krylov/POD framing. The novelty is moderate given EDMD (2015) and related kernelized variants, but the operator-centric derivation and applications are valuable if clarified and empirically substantiated. Please more clearly differentiate KDMD from EDMD/KEDMD and “exact/optimized” DMD variants (e.g., what is new beyond using kernels and POD?).

Significance
- Potentially significant for ML and scientific computing communities if made accessible. With better exposition, guidance on kernel design, and solid empirical comparisons, KDMD could be widely adopted.

Major comments
- Rewrite for pedagogy: shorten, clarify, and add intuition; reduce formalism in the main text.
- Section 4 feels redundant; either compress substantially or move to supplement.
- Section 5 is long; summarize and integrate key points into the introduction/conclusion; alternatively, add small illustrative examples tying related methods to KDMD.
- Section 6 is too condensed; expand with quantitative experiments, baselines, and ablations.
- Provide practical guidance: how to choose kernels/feature maps; when polynomial/RBF kernels succeed/fail; links to system properties. The first two test problems are polynomial; please discuss when kernel choice is informed vs agnostic (as hinted in Sec. 5).
- Add comparisons to EDMD and to DMD variants (exact, optimized, sparsity-promoting), as surveyed by Brunton et al. (2015).

Minor comments
- Possible typo in the toy example: “0.92” likely intended as 0.9^2; clarify the nonlinearity and notation so the example clearly reads as nonlinear.
- Clarify whether the “nonlinear toy” is linear in x and nonlinear in y; align the description with equations.
- Provide kernel hyperparameters and data normalization details for each experiment.
- Unify notation and remove redundant statements; several sentences can be made more concise.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid operator-theoretic foundation; natural and useful generalization of DMD.
- Broad applicability; promising bridge between control/operator theory and ML.
- Code availability.

Cons for acceptance
- Hard to read; presentation is verbose and cryptic for NIPS.
- Empirical evidence is limited; missing comparisons to standard baselines.
- Insufficient practical guidance on kernel/dictionary choice.

Questions for the authors
- How does KDMD compare quantitatively to EDMD and exact/optimized DMD on canonical benchmarks with known spectra?
- How sensitive are results to kernel choice and hyperparameters? Any heuristics for selecting kernels?
- What are the computational/memory complexities and practical limits in τ and n?

Recommendation
Borderline. The core idea is worthwhile and likely significant, but the paper needs a more pedagogical rewrite and stronger empirical validation. With these revisions, it could be highly citable and impactful. References such as Brunton et al. (2015) should be discussed more explicitly to situate KDMD among DMD variants.
