## Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper presents a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman operator spectral analysis. The core idea is to perform the analysis within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), allowing for a more flexible approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions compared to traditional DMD’s reliance on linear monomials. The authors propose an algorithm based on calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition. The presentation is clear and pedagogically sound, effectively explaining the underlying concepts, even for readers unfamiliar with fluid dynamics. The inclusion of the Koopman operator background and DMD review is particularly helpful.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in spectral methods for dynamical systems, DMD, kernel methods (kernel PCA, etc.), and RKHS theory. The extension to RKHS is a natural progression, and the theoretical foundation appears solid, though as the authors acknowledge, the results are largely consistent with existing literature on kernelized linear operators. The paper demonstrates the method on a damped 1D system, the Henon map, and real locomotion data.

However, the empirical evaluation is a key weakness. While the method *works* on the toy examples, the comparisons to standard DMD and other dimensionality reduction techniques (PCA, kPCA) are inconclusive. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods. The locomotion data application is presented, but lacks quantitative comparison or a compelling demonstration of benefits over more standard ML approaches. 

A significant concern is the limited scope regarding practical ML applications. The authors mention applications like state prediction, but don't provide compelling evidence of their utility in a broader machine learning context. Robotics, as suggested by the authors, or another domain requiring dynamical system inference would be a more impactful demonstration. Furthermore, the deterministic setup restricts the relevance to real-world ML problems where stochasticity is prevalent. The requirement for explicit feature-space representations, if present, would also be a limitation compared to kernel PCA's implicit mapping.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
* Clear and well-written explanation of a conceptually sound extension to DMD.
* Theoretically grounded and builds upon a strong foundation of related work.
* Pedagogically valuable for introducing concepts to a broader audience.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
* Empirical results lack convincing comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.
* Limited demonstration of practical ML applications and significance.
* Deterministic setup reduces relevance to real-world scenarios.
* The theoretical contribution, while valid, feels somewhat incremental given existing RKHS/kernel literature.

**Recommendations:**

* **Include standard DMD performance on the toy example and the Henon map** to highlight differences.
* **Clarify whether the modal decomposition requires explicit feature-space representations.**
* **Focus on a specific ML application domain (e.g., robotics) and provide a rigorous comparison** to existing methods.
* **Consider incorporating noise or stochastic elements** into the analysis to improve relevance to real-world applications.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting theoretical extension, but lacks the empirical validation and demonstrated practical utility necessary for a strong NIPS contribution. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to address the weaknesses outlined above.
