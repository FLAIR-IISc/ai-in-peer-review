This paper introduces Kernel Dynamic Mode Decomposition (KDMD), a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to extend the popular Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) method by leveraging the kernel trick. Where DMD provides a linear approximation of the Koopman operator in the space of observables, KDMD implicitly lifts the system's state into a high-dimensional Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) via a feature map. In this feature space, the Koopman operator can be approximated linearly, allowing for the extraction of its eigenvalues and eigenfunctions (Koopman modes). This provides a powerful, data-driven framework for identifying the fundamental frequencies, growth/decay rates, and coherent structures of nonlinear systems. The authors derive the algorithm, propose a robust variant using kernel PCA, and demonstrate its potential for feature extraction in applications like prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

This work connects the fields of nonlinear dynamics (Koopman operator theory, DMD) and machine learning (kernel methods). While DMD has been extensively explored in the fluid dynamics community, its connection to and potential for the broader machine learning audience at NeurIPS has been less developed. This paper makes a strong step in that direction by framing the Koopman analysis within the familiar language of kernel methods.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a powerful and elegant generalization of DMD. The synthesis of Koopman operator theory with kernel methods is a significant contribution that provides a principled, nonlinear extension to a widely used technique. If presented clearly, this work has the potential to be highly influential and citable, making Koopman spectral analysis more accessible to the machine learning community.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development of KDMD appears to be sound. The derivation, which frames the problem as an eigenvalue problem in the RKHS and solves it via operations on Gram matrices, is correct and well-founded. The robustified version based on kernel PCA is a sensible and necessary addition for practical application.
*   **Potential Impact:** The idea of using Koopman modes as features for downstream machine learning tasks is very appealing. The proposed applications—prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition—are important problems where a good representation of system dynamics is crucial.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The primary weakness of this paper is its lack of clarity. While the underlying ideas are strong, the manuscript is exceptionally difficult to read, which severely limits its accessibility and impact. A major revision focused on presentation is strongly recommended.

**Major Remarks:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is needlessly verbose and overly mathematical in its presentation. Many concepts are introduced with dense notation and terminology without sufficient intuition. For a NeurIPS audience, a more pedagogical approach is essential. For example, the connection between the DMD Arnoldi interpretation (Eq. 6) and the proposed KDMD formulation could be made much more explicit and intuitive. The authors should aim to simplify the language, shorten sentences, and add clear, high-level explanations before diving into mathematical formalism. A clear, well-written version of this paper would be a fantastic contribution.
2.  **Paper Organization and Redundancy:** The paper's structure could be streamlined. Section 5, "Related Works," is extensive and much of its content could be integrated more concisely into the introduction to better motivate the work from the outset. In a short conference paper, a long, separate related works section can disrupt the flow.
3.  **Underdeveloped Applications:** Section 7 presents several interesting applications, but the descriptions are too high-level and lack illustrative examples to be convincing. For instance, the prediction application is described in abstract steps without showing a concrete result on a simple system to build intuition. These ideas could be briefly summarized in the conclusion as future work, or alternatively, one application should be chosen and developed thoroughly with clear experimental validation.
4.  **Condensed Empirical Examples:** Section 6, the main empirical validation, is overly condensed. While the results on the toy system and Hénon map are promising, they are not explored in sufficient detail. The discussion is minimal and does not provide much insight into the method's behavior, strengths, or limitations (e.g., sensitivity to kernel choice or data size). This section should be expanded, perhaps by integrating one of the application examples from Section 7.

**Minor Remarks:**

1.  **Toy Example Description:** In Section 6, the toy system is described as nonlinear. While the system `xt+1= 0.9xt, yt+1= 0.5yt+(0.92−0.5)x^2_t` is indeed nonlinear overall, the `x` dynamics are linear and uncoupled. This could be stated more clearly to avoid confusion.
2.  **Kernel Selection:** The experiments on the toy and Hénon map use a polynomial and Gaussian kernel, respectively. In these cases, the choice of a suitable kernel is relatively straightforward given the polynomial nature of the dynamics. The paper would be much stronger if it included a discussion on the critical issue of kernel selection, especially for real-world problems where the underlying functional form of the dynamics is unknown. How sensitive is KDMD to this choice? Are there principles to guide selection?
3.  **Missing Reference:** The literature on DMD and its extensions is vast. The work of Brunton et al. (e.g., Brunton et al., 2015) has been influential in this area and should be consulted and potentially referenced to better situate this contribution.

In summary, this paper introduces KDMD, a technically sound and highly significant idea. However, the current manuscript is let down by a cryptic and verbose presentation that makes it inaccessible. The experimental sections are also too brief to be fully convincing. I strongly encourage the authors to undertake a major rewrite with a focus on pedagogical clarity and a more thorough empirical demonstration. With such a revision, this paper would represent a top-tier contribution.
