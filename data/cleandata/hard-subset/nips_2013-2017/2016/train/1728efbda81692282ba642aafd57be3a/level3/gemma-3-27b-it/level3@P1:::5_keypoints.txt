- Introduces KDMD: Dynamic Mode Decomposition extended to RKHS to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using kernel functions.  
- Algorithmic core: compute orthogonal bases for the RKHS Krylov matrix and eigendecompose the projected Koopman operator.  
- Applications demonstrated: state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition on synthetic and real data.  
- Strong theoretical grounding: rigorous formulation with theorems linking KDMD to Koopman operator and DMD.  
- Novelty: meaningful extension of DMD that can capture more complex nonlinear dynamics via kernels.  
- Broad applicability: potential across domains needing data-driven modeling of nonlinear dynamical systems.  
- Practical robustness: includes POD-based projection to mitigate ill-conditioning.  
- Clarity issues: overly mathematical, verbose, and lacking intuitive explanations for core ideas.  
- Organizational redundancy: Section 4 (POD robustification) feels repetitive and could be more concisely integrated.  
- Weak related-work discussion: Section 5 lacks depth and concrete comparisons to existing methods.  
- Limited empirical validation: experiments are condensed, lack breadth, and comparisons to benchmarks are insufficient.  
- Pedagogical shortcomings: reads like a mathematical treatise rather than an accessible NeurIPS contribution.  
- Misleading example: the initial “nonlinear” toy problem appears linear, weakening the empirical claims.  
- Overall: technically strong and promising but hindered by presentation, organization, and empirical weaknesses.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel idea, solid theory, and broad relevance to the community.  
- Arguments against acceptance: poor readability, suboptimal organization, and insufficient experimental evidence.  
- Recommendation: Reject but encourage revision and resubmission with clearer exposition, streamlined organization, stronger experiments, and improved related-work comparison.
