

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes KDMD (Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels), which extends the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) framework to reproduce kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors present a method for performing spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in RKHS, where the Koopman eigenfunctions are approximated using nonlinear basis functions derived from kernel feature maps rather than linear monomials used in standard DMD. This approach generalizes DMD by allowing for richer, nonlinear representations of the system dynamics. The paper relates to Brunton et al. (2015) and Williams et al. (2015) who developed extended DMD variants, but KDMD specifically leverages kernel methods to provide a more expressive feature space.

## Strengths

The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHS, which represents a meaningful generalization of the standard approach. The mathematical foundation is solid, and the authors correctly identify that standard DMD's linear basis functions limit its applicability to systems where linear approximations are sufficient. The proposed method theoretically allows for capturing more complex dynamics through the expressiveness of kernel functions. The empirical examples, including the toy system and Hénon map, demonstrate the method's ability to estimate Koopman eigenvalues accurately. The applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition showcase potential utility in machine learning and dynamical systems analysis.

## Weaknesses

The primary weakness of this paper is its poor presentation, which significantly hinders comprehension. The manuscript is overly mathematical and verbose, with unnecessary repetition of concepts. The writing style makes it difficult for readers to grasp the core contributions and practical implications.

Major presentation issues include:
- Section 4 is largely redundant with the introduction and should be removed or significantly condensed
- Section 5 (Related Works) is too brief and should be expanded to better contextualize the contribution against existing DMD variants and Koopman analysis methods
- Section 6 (Empirical Example) should be illustrated with examples rather than presented as a brief description
- Section 7 (Applications) is overly condensed and should be developed more thoroughly, similar to how Section 5 should have been

The paper contains a possible typo: the "nonlinear" toy case appears to be linear (x_{t+1}=0.9x_t, y_{t+1}=0.5y_t+(0.9^2-0.5)x_t^2), which is actually a polynomial system that could be handled with standard DMD using quadratic monomials.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of when KDMD provides advantages over standard DMD. The first two test problems are polynomial, which suggests that standard DMD might work well with appropriate polynomial basis functions. The authors should clarify when kernel-based approaches are genuinely superior to linear basis approaches.

## Quality

The technical content is sound and mathematically rigorous. The theoretical development of KDMD as an extension of DMD to RKHS is correct, and the empirical results demonstrate the method's ability to estimate Koopman eigenvalues. However, the paper would benefit from more thorough experimental comparison with standard DMD and other extended DMD variants to better establish the practical advantages of the kernel approach.

## Clarity

This is the paper's most significant weakness. The manuscript is needlessly verbose, with many statements that could be expressed much more concisely. The organization is poor, with redundant content in Sections 4 and 5. The core contribution (KDMD) is buried in excessive mathematical notation, making it difficult to grasp the essence of the method. A more pedagogical rewrite would make the paper accessible to the NeurIPS community and significantly increase its impact.

## Originality

The paper's core idea of extending DMD to RKHS is novel and represents a meaningful generalization of existing methods. While Williams et al. (2015) proposed extended DMD using pre-determined basis functions, KDMD specifically leverages kernel methods to create a more expressive feature space without requiring explicit basis function selection. This distinction is important and represents a genuine contribution to the field.

## Significance

The significance would be much higher if the paper were more clearly presented. The proposed method has potential applications in machine learning for feature extraction from dynamical systems, particularly in cases where nonlinear features are required. However, the current presentation makes it difficult to assess the practical advantages of KDMD over simpler approaches. The applications section is promising but insufficiently developed to demonstrate the method's true utility.

## Recommendation

This paper has strong technical merit but is currently unpolished and poorly presented. With a significant rewrite to improve clarity, conciseness, and organization, this would be a highly citable contribution to the field. As it stands, the excessive verbosity and poor organization make it difficult for readers to appreciate the paper's true value. The authors should remove redundant sections, develop the applications section more thoroughly, and provide clearer explanations of the method's advantages.

I recommend rejection in its current form, but with strong encouragement for the authors to revise and resubmit with a more pedagogical approach. The paper's technical contribution is valuable, but the current presentation prevents it from being properly evaluated and appreciated by the NeurIPS community.
