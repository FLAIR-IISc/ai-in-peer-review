

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using kernel methods for Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors develop a modal decomposition algorithm that approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using nonlinear basis functions derived from kernel functions, rather than the linear monomials used in conventional DMD. The method involves calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator, with a robustified variant using POD directions. The approach is theoretically grounded and demonstrated on synthetic and real-world data for applications like prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper excels in clarity and organization, with well-structured exposition of theoretical concepts that build upon previous work on Koopman operators and DMD (e.g., Williams et al. 2015, Rowley et al. 2009). The technical content is strong, and numerical experiments convincingly demonstrate the method's utility despite limited comparison to state-of-the-art. The robustification via POD directions is a practical contribution that addresses a known limitation of DMD.

However, several critical concerns undermine the paper's technical soundness. Section 3.1 contains significant mathematical inconsistencies: Equation (7) defines KH as an operator from H→H, but the subsequent proof treats φ(x) as a scalar (lines 144-157), creating a fundamental contradiction. The assumption that φ maps M→H implies φ may not belong to H, yet this is not clarified. The proof of Theorem 1 appears to introduce unstated assumptions, and the relationship between Equations (2) and (7) is unclear despite their superficial similarity. Additionally, typos (e.g., "the the" on line 55, inconsistent index notation on line 153) detract from professionalism.

The work shows strong potential for applications in dynamical systems analysis, but the mathematical shortcomings in the core theoretical contribution require resolution. While the numerical results are promising, the theoretical foundation must be solidified before this method can be confidently adopted by the community. The paper's clarity and practical applications are notable strengths, but the unresolved mathematical issues represent a significant barrier to acceptance.

Pro: Well-written, clear theory, practical applications, strong numerical results. Con: Critical mathematical inconsistencies in core theorem, unclear notation and assumptions, typos. The paper's significance is high, but quality is compromised by unresolved theoretical issues.
