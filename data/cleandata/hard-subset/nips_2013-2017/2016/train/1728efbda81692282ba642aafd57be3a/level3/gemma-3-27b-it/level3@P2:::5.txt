This paper proposes a Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) method extended to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), termed KDMD. The core idea is to leverage the power of kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with richer, nonlinear basis functions, overcoming a limitation of traditional DMD which relies on linear monomials. The authors demonstrate the method on both synthetic (a toy nonlinear system and the Henon map) and real-world data, and explore potential applications in prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

The work builds on a strong foundation of prior research in spectral analysis of dynamical systems. It correctly positions itself within the existing literature, referencing key works in proper orthogonal decomposition (POD), DMD, and Koopman operator theory (e.g., Koopman, 1963; Rowley & Mezić, 2008; Schmid, 2010). The connection to DMD is clearly established, framing KDMD as a generalization that allows for more flexible function approximation. The paper also references relevant work on kernel methods and subspace identification, establishing its place within a broader research context. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Extending DMD to RKHS is a conceptually sound and potentially powerful generalization. The use of kernel methods allows for capturing more complex dynamics than standard DMD.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical development appears rigorous, with a clear derivation of the algorithm and a theorem establishing the relationship between the estimated Ritz values and the Koopman eigenvalues.
*   **Potential Significance:** The ability to extract more informative features from nonlinear systems could have a significant impact in various fields, including control, system identification, and time-series analysis.
*   **Applications Exploration:** The paper demonstrates the potential of KDMD by exploring several applications, showing its versatility beyond just spectral analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper suffers from a lack of clarity and is overly verbose. The mathematical derivations, while correct, are presented in a dense and inaccessible manner. A more pedagogical rewrite is crucial for wider adoption. Many statements could be significantly more concise.
*   **Redundancy:** Section 4, which focuses on robustifying the algorithm with POD bases, feels largely redundant, especially given the discussion in the introduction regarding the limitations of standard DMD. Its inclusion adds unnecessary length to an already dense paper.
*   **Insufficient Discussion of Related Work:** Section 5, “Related Works,” is briefly mentioned but lacks depth. A more thorough discussion, possibly with examples illustrating how KDMD differs from existing approaches, would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the empirical examples demonstrate the method’s feasibility, they are underdeveloped. Section 6 is too condensed and lacks sufficient detail to fully evaluate the performance of KDMD. The choice of kernels and parameter settings could be better justified.
*   **Toy Problems:** The initial test cases are relatively simple (polynomial systems) and do not fully showcase the advantages of KDMD's nonlinear feature maps. More complex systems would be more convincing.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, but the presentation hinders full evaluation. The claims are supported by mathematical derivations, but the lack of clarity makes it difficult to assess the completeness of the work without significant effort.

**Clarity:** The paper is poorly written and difficult to follow. The organization could be improved, and the mathematical notation should be explained more carefully.

**Originality:** The extension of DMD to RKHS is original and potentially significant. However, the paper needs to more clearly articulate how this contribution differs from existing kernel-based methods for system identification.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is high, but it is currently limited by the lack of clarity and insufficient empirical validation. The method’s ability to extract more informative features could lead to improvements in various applications.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of a popular method (DMD) with potential for broader applicability.
*   Rigorous mathematical foundation.
*   Demonstrated feasibility on synthetic data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Poor clarity and overly verbose presentation.
*   Redundant section (Section 4).
*   Insufficient empirical validation and lack of detailed analysis.
*   Limited discussion of related work.




**Overall Recommendation:**

Reject. While the core idea is promising, the current manuscript is too difficult to understand and lacks sufficient empirical validation to warrant acceptance to NeurIPS. I strongly encourage the authors to rewrite the paper with a focus on clarity, conciseness, and a more thorough empirical evaluation. A more pedagogical presentation would significantly enhance the paper’s impact and make it more accessible to a wider audience. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above could lead to a highly citable contribution to the field. Consider citing Brunton et al. (2015) as a relevant work.
