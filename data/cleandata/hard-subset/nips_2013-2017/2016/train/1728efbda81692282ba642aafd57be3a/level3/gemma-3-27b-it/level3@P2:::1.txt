This paper introduces a dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) method leveraging reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors propose an algorithm to estimate Koopman eigenvalues and eigenvectors from data, moving beyond linear monomial basis functions used in traditional DMD by utilizing the expressiveness of kernel functions. They demonstrate the method’s applicability through synthetic and real-world examples, including applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The core idea is sound and builds upon the growing body of work applying Koopman operator theory to nonlinear system analysis. The extension to RKHS offers a potentially powerful way to capture complex dynamics without relying on explicit system identification. The connection to DMD, framing the proposed approach as a generalization leveraging the equivalent principle, provides a clear conceptual foundation. The empirical results presented demonstrate the method's feasibility and offer initial validation of its performance.

However, the paper exhibits several weaknesses that impact its overall strength. A significant concern is the limited discussion of related work, particularly within the context of kernel methods applied to dynamical systems. The authors cite foundational papers in DMD and Koopman analysis, but overlook crucial contributions. Specifically, the work of Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc (2004), Tuia et al. (2011), Rojo-Álvarez et al. (2015), Mouattamid & Schaback (2017) and Franz & Schölkopf (2006) explore kernel-based approaches to time series analysis, dimensionality reduction, and dynamical system modeling, and should be discussed to properly contextualize the novelty of the presented method. 

Furthermore, a clear comparison with extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) is missing. While the authors mention extended DMD briefly, they do not explicitly articulate how their approach differs or improves upon it, especially regarding the robustness enhancements described in Section 4. A direct comparison, both theoretically and empirically, is necessary to substantiate the claim of novelty. The practical implications of projecting onto POD directions, and why this differs from existing extended DMD formulations, needs to be clarified.

The paper also lacks a thorough exploration of its applicability to stochastic dynamical systems. The presented method appears primarily geared towards deterministic settings. A discussion of the method’s performance and limitations in the presence of noise, or how it could be adapted to handle stochasticity, would significantly strengthen the contribution.

The use of consecutive observables, as opposed to entire sequences, is an interesting choice. However, its limitations, particularly for high-dimensional time series where pointwise relationships might be weak, are not adequately addressed. A discussion on the causal assumptions inherent in this approach, and the potential for information loss, is warranted. 

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrative, is not ambitious enough. The prediction task is a standard benchmark. Comparisons to established time-series clustering techniques (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernels) and state-of-the-art online kernel change-point detection methods would provide a more rigorous assessment of the method’s performance. The locomotion data example is promising, but further analysis and comparison would strengthen the conclusions. Additionally, there are minor typos (e.g., “tau-1” should be “τ-1”, “k(xi.xj)” should be “k(xi, xj)”) that need to be corrected.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel extension of DMD to RKHS, offering a potentially powerful tool for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems.
*   The theoretical foundation is solid, building upon established concepts from Koopman operator theory and functional analysis.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the feasibility of the method and provide initial validation of its performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks a comprehensive review of related work in kernel-based dynamical systems.
*   A clear comparison with existing methods, such as extended DMD, is missing.
*   The applicability to stochastic systems is not adequately addressed.
*   The experimental evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of nonlinear dynamical systems analysis. However, the lack of a thorough literature review, the limited comparison to existing methods, and the somewhat superficial experimental evaluation raise concerns about its current state. I recommend **weak reject**. The authors should address the concerns outlined above, particularly by expanding the related work section, providing a detailed comparison to extended DMD, and conducting more comprehensive experiments, before resubmission. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a more compelling contribution to NIPS.
