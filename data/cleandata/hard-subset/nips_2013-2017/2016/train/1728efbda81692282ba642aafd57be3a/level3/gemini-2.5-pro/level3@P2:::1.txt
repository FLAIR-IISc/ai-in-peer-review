This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) for performing Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to approximate the Koopman operator and its eigenfunctions within a feature space defined by a kernel, thereby allowing for a richer, nonlinear set of basis functions compared to the linear observables used in standard DMD. The authors present two algorithms: a direct formulation based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization of the Krylov subspace in the feature space, and a more numerically robust version that projects the data onto principal directions found via kernel PCA. The method is framed as a feature extraction technique for dynamical systems, and its utility is demonstrated through applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition on both synthetic and real-world data. This work connects the DMD literature, popular in physics and engineering, with the powerful framework of kernel methods from machine learning.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper's theoretical foundation appears solid. The derivation of kernel DMD (Theorem 1) and its robustified version using POD bases (Theorem 2) provides a mathematically sound extension of the DMD principle to RKHS. The connection between the eigenvalues of the projected operator and the Koopman eigenvalues is clearly established under reasonable assumptions. The robustification via kernel PCA is a practical and necessary step that mirrors similar developments in the standard DMD literature.

However, the empirical validation is not fully convincing. The analysis is confined to deterministic systems. While the Koopman operator can be extended to stochastic systems, the paper does not discuss or evaluate how the proposed method would perform in the presence of noise or stochastic dynamics. This is a significant omission, as many real-world systems are inherently stochastic. Furthermore, the remark at the end of Section 4, stating that the method can use a collection of pairs of consecutive observables instead of a single sequence, needs more elaboration. It is unclear how this applies to multidimensional time series where pointwise relationships might be weak, and what causal assumptions are being made.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The background on the Koopman operator and DMD is introduced clearly, and the transition to the kernelized version is easy to follow.

A major point of confusion, however, is the lack of a clear distinction from the "extended DMD" (eDMD) method of Williams et al. (2015, Ref [35]). The authors briefly mention that the resulting procedure is "similar," but this understates the close relationship. The robust algorithm in Section 4, which relies on a projection onto a set of basis functions (the kernel principal components), seems conceptually very similar to eDMD, which works on a pre-determined dictionary of basis functions. A detailed paragraph explicitly comparing the two methods, highlighting both similarities and key differences in formulation and practical application, is necessary to properly situate this work.

There are also a couple of minor typos:
- The description of kernel PCA in Section 4 seems to use data from `x0, ..., x(tau-1)`, but the Gram matrix is defined as `M_tau* M_tau`, which involves `x_tau`. Please clarify the indexing.
- In the last equation on page 7, `k(xj, xi)` is written with a dot `k(xj . xi)`.

**Originality:**
The paper's primary contribution is the formalization of DMD within the RKHS framework. This is a valuable conceptual step that brings powerful tools from kernel methods to bear on Koopman analysis.

However, the originality is somewhat undermined by two factors. First, as mentioned above, the relationship with Williams et al. (2015) is not adequately clarified, making it difficult to assess the novel contribution. Second, the paper overlooks a significant body of prior work on learning dynamical systems using kernel-based methods. Relevant work includes, for example, kernel canonical correlation analysis for state-space model identification (e.g., Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc, ICML 2005), kernel methods for time series prediction and analysis (e.g., Tuia et al., IEEE TGRS 2009; Rojo-Álvarez et al., JMLR 2008), and system identification in RKHS (e.g., Mouattamid & Schaback, 2013; Franz & Schölkopf, 2006). Acknowledging and discussing this related literature is essential for contextualizing the paper's contribution.

**Significance:**
The paper addresses an important problem: extracting meaningful features from complex, nonlinear dynamical systems in a data-driven manner. The proposed method has the potential to be a useful tool for researchers and practitioners.

However, the significance of the presented results is limited by the experimental design. The applications, while interesting, lack comparisons to established, state-of-the-art baselines.
- For the "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" task, the results should be compared against standard time-series clustering/classification methods, such as kernel k-means or methods based on subspace kernels.
- The "Sequential Change-Point Detection" experiment compares against a method from 2007. It would be far more convincing to benchmark against more recent and competitive online kernel-based change detection algorithms.
- The prediction task serves as a basic sanity check but does not demonstrate a significant advance over existing nonlinear time-series forecasting methods.

Without these comparisons, it is difficult to judge whether the features extracted by kernel DMD offer a tangible advantage over other well-established techniques.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper presents a well-motivated and theoretically sound framework for performing Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS.
2.  It elegantly unifies DMD from the physics community with kernel methods from machine learning.
3.  The proposed method offers a powerful, data-driven approach to feature extraction for nonlinear dynamics.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The novelty and contribution relative to extended DMD [35] are not made clear.
2.  The paper fails to cite or discuss a substantial body of related work on kernel methods for dynamical systems.
3.  The experimental evaluation is weak due to the absence of comparisons with relevant state-of-the-art baseline methods.
4.  The analysis is restricted to deterministic systems, limiting its applicability for many real-world problems.

**Recommendation:**
While the core idea of this paper is promising, the weaknesses in its positioning relative to prior work and its empirical evaluation are significant. I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can convincingly address the issues regarding originality (especially the comparison with eDMD and other kernel methods) and significantly strengthen the experimental sections with appropriate baselines in a rebuttal.
