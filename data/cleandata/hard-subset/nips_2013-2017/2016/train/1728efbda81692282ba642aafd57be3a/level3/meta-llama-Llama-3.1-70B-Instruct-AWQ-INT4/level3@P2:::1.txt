The paper "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis" proposes a novel approach to spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The authors extend the dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) algorithm to work with kernel-based methods, which allows for more efficient and accurate analysis of nonlinear systems.

The main idea of the paper is to use the Koopman operator to analyze the spectral properties of nonlinear dynamical systems. The Koopman operator is an infinite-dimensional linear operator that acts on a scalar function, mapping it to a new function. The authors show that the Koopman operator can be used to decompose the dynamics of a nonlinear system into a finite number of modes, which can be used for feature extraction and analysis.

The authors propose a modal decomposition algorithm that uses the Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition to calculate a set of orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS. They then use the eigendecomposition of the projection of the Koopman operator onto the subspace spanned by the bases to obtain the empirical Ritz values and vectors. The algorithm is shown to be mathematically correct, but the authors also provide a robustified version of the algorithm that uses principal orthogonal directions (POD) to improve the stability of the algorithm.

The paper includes several empirical examples that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. The authors apply the algorithm to synthetic and real-world data, including a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map. They also demonstrate the use of the algorithm for prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The strengths of the paper include the novel approach to spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator in an RKHS. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the algorithm and its mathematical foundations. The empirical examples are well-chosen and demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm.

However, there are several weaknesses and areas for improvement. One of the main weaknesses is the lack of clear distinction between the proposed algorithm and the work of Williams et al. (2015). The authors should provide a more explicit comparison and discussion of the differences between the two approaches.

Another area for improvement is the lack of relevant prior work in kernel-based dynamical systems. The authors should include a more comprehensive review of the literature, including work by Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Álvarez et al., and Mouattamid & Schaback.

Additionally, the authors should discuss and experiment with stochastic settings, including the performance and limitations of the algorithm in these cases. The paper currently only considers deterministic cases, which may not be representative of real-world systems.

The practical implications of using pairs of consecutive observables instead of sequences should also be clarified, especially for multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relations. The authors should discuss the causal assumptions underlying the algorithm and provide more convincing evaluations of the algorithm's performance.

Finally, the experiments in the paper are not ambitious enough. The authors should compare their algorithm to time-series clustering methods, such as kernel k-means and subspace kernels, and to state-of-the-art online kernel change-point detection methods.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator in an RKHS.
* The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the algorithm and its mathematical foundations.
* The empirical examples are well-chosen and demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks a clear distinction between the proposed algorithm and the work of Williams et al. (2015).
* The authors do not provide a comprehensive review of the literature on kernel-based dynamical systems.
* The paper only considers deterministic cases and does not discuss or experiment with stochastic settings.
* The practical implications of using pairs of consecutive observables instead of sequences are not clarified.
* The experiments in the paper are not ambitious enough and do not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the algorithm's performance.
