This paper presents a novel extension of Koopman spectral analysis to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors propose a modal decomposition algorithm based on this extension, effectively leveraging the power of kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with potentially richer, nonlinear basis functions. The method is thoroughly evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its potential for feature extraction and subsequent applications in areas like state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamic pattern recognition. The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly articulating the motivation, methodology, and results.

The core idea builds on the well-established connection between the Koopman operator and dynamic mode decomposition (DMD). Existing DMD methods often rely on linear monomials of observables, limiting their ability to capture complex nonlinear dynamics. By embedding the analysis within an RKHS, the authors circumvent this limitation, enabling the use of kernel functions to represent more expressive basis functions. This extension, theoretically grounded and algorithmically sound, offers a potentially powerful tool for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems from data. The connection to DMD is explicitly made, and the algorithm is presented as a natural generalization.

The strengths of this work are numerous. The theoretical foundation is solid, with a clear explanation of the Koopman operator and its spectral properties. The proposed algorithm is well-defined and efficiently implemented, with a practical consideration for potential ill-conditioning addressed by incorporating a projection onto POD directions. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, showcasing the method’s performance on both a toy system and the Hénon map. Further, the exploration of applications – prediction, dynamic recognition, and change-point detection – demonstrates the practical utility of the extracted features. The inclusion of relevant related work, including discussions of POD, DMD variants, and kernel methods, situates the contribution within the broader landscape of spectral analysis techniques. The code availability is also a significant plus, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

However, the paper does have a few minor weaknesses. While the empirical results are promising, a direct comparison to standard (non-RKHS) Koopman spectral analysis or DMD on the presented datasets would strengthen the claims of improvement. Specifically, showing how the RKHS approach captures dynamics missed by traditional methods, even on these relatively simple examples, would be impactful. This would clarify the benefits of the added complexity of the kernel-based approach.

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of kernel choice and its influence on the results. While the authors specify the kernels used (polynomial for the toy system, Gaussian for the Hénon map), the rationale behind these choices and a sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different kernel parameters or even kernel types are absent. Including simulations illustrating how the performance changes with varying kernel parameters would significantly bolster the robustness of the conclusions. Is there a “best” kernel for a certain type of dynamical system? What are the trade-offs between kernel complexity and accuracy?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of Koopman spectral analysis to RKHS is a significant contribution, offering a potential improvement over existing methods.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical foundation is solid, and the algorithm is well-defined and implemented.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not deeply familiar with the field.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The method is thoroughly evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The exploration of applications demonstrates the potential utility of the method in diverse areas.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of direct comparison:** The absence of a direct comparison to standard Koopman spectral analysis or DMD on the same datasets limits the ability to assess the true benefits of the RKHS approach.
*   **Kernel selection discussion:** The lack of a detailed discussion of kernel choice and its impact on performance weakens the generalizability of the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite these minor weaknesses, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of nonlinear dynamical systems analysis. The novelty, soundness, clarity, and empirical validation all point towards a strong publication. Addressing the reviewer's suggestions regarding a direct comparison and a more thorough discussion of kernel choice would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The score should reflect strong positive evaluation, and I suggest a score of 8/10.
