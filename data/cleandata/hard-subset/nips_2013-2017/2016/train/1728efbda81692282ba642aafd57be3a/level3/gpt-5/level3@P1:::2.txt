This paper studies Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) algorithm for nonlinear dynamical systems. The key idea is to treat the Koopman operator as a linear composition operator acting on feature maps ϕ(x) in an RKHS, form a Krylov subspace from a finite trajectory ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1), and compute Ritz eigenvalues/vectors via an orthogonalization and projected eigendecomposition. The authors give two variants: (i) a Gram–Schmidt/QR formulation in feature space (implemented via Gram matrices), and (ii) a numerically robust variant that projects onto kernel-PCA (POD) directions before computing the projected operator F̂, akin to exact/optimized DMD stabilization. They argue that the resulting eigenvalues/modes approximate Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions and provide feature representations for downstream tasks (prediction via pre-image, dynamic pattern recognition via subspace angles, and change-point detection). Empirical demonstrations include a toy system, the Hénon map, a motion recognition embedding, and a change-point example.

Relation to prior work: DMD (Schmid, Rowley et al.) is a well-established approach for approximating Koopman spectra from data; variants include exact DMD, optimized DMD, sparse DMD, and extended DMD (EDMD) [33, 8, 13, 35]. EDMD in particular replaces raw observations by a dictionary of basis functions; Williams et al. [35] also describe a kernel-EDMD implementation that implicitly spans large dictionaries through Gram matrices. The present paper’s RKHS-based derivation and kernel-PCA-projected operator are conceptually very close to EDMD with a kernel trick. Connections to spectral learning and subspace identification are noted.

Quality: The mathematical development is mostly sound. The paper clearly derives the finite-dimensional projected operator in RKHS and presents Theorems 1–2 linking empirical Ritz objects to Koopman spectra under assumptions (distinct Ritz values; eigenfunctions in the feature span; κj lying in the kernel-PCA subspace). These assumptions are strong and should be discussed more critically: (i) φj(x)=⟨ϕ(x), κj⟩ requires that Koopman eigenfunctions lie in the RKHS span of the data; (ii) truncation in kernel-PCA may remove dynamically-important components; (iii) claims of “full recovery if the Krylov subspace is sufficiently large” do not account for noise, finite-sample error, or non-invariance of the feature subspace. The robustification via kernel-PCA is reasonable and mirrors DMD practice, but the algorithm would benefit from an explicit regularization strategy (e.g., Tikhonov, ridge in generalized eigenproblems) and complexity analysis. The pre-image-based prediction is heuristic and potentially brittle; the change-point detection derivation invokes a nonparametric exponential family and drops log-partition contributions—this needs justification, calibration, and quantitative evaluation.

Clarity: The exposition of Koopman/DMD background and the RKHS reformulation is clear and well-organized. Proof sketches are concise but understandable. Some notation is heavy (switching between operators on observables vs feature maps), and the distinction between eigenfunctions in H and scalar eigenfunctions could be sharpened. Algorithmic steps are provided but could use pseudocode and explicit complexity/memory costs. Empirical sections would benefit from more details (kernel width selection, truncation criteria, noise levels).

Originality: The central contribution is a kernelization of DMD/Koopman spectral analysis by operating directly in RKHS with Gram-Schmidt and a POD-robustified variant. However, EDMD [35] already formulates Koopman spectral estimation with arbitrary dictionaries and discusses kernel implementations; the robustification via projection onto dominant POD/kernel-PCA directions is also standard in the DMD literature. The paper’s framing via Krylov subspaces in RKHS is a nice unifying perspective, but the novelty relative to kernel-EDMD appears incremental. The application ideas (pre-image prediction, subspace-angle recognition, change detection) are sensible but not fundamentally new to kernel methods or time-series analysis.

Significance: Koopman-based, kernelized spectral methods are relevant to both ML and dynamical-systems communities, and a clear RKHS derivation can be valuable. That said, the empirical evidence is limited to small-scale demonstrations without strong baselines (EDMD/kernel-EDMD, exact/optimized DMD, subspace identification, GPDM, RNN/LSTM) or rigorous metrics. Without clearer performance gains or broader benchmarks (noise robustness, sample complexity, out-of-sample prediction, scalability), the impact for the NIPS audience is moderate.

Empirical evaluation: The toy and Hénon examples validate eigenvalue recovery qualitatively. The motion embedding via kernel principal angles is illustrative but lacks quantitative recognition accuracy or comparisons to EDMD or modern sequence models. The change-point experiment uses a single synthetic example and compares to one kernel change detection baseline; evaluation lacks detection delay/false alarm tradeoffs and sensitivity analysis. Scalability is not assessed (Gram matrices scale as O(τ2) memory, O(τ3) eigendecompositions).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear operator-theoretic formulation of DMD in RKHS with a principled projection and kernel-PCA stabilization.
- Bridges Koopman spectral analysis and kernel methods; accessible derivation for the ML audience.
- Practical implementation details (Gram computations, centering, truncation) and publicly available code.
- Provides a unified feature-extraction viewpoint enabling multiple downstream tasks.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty relative to EDMD and kernel-EDMD; robustification mirrors established DMD practice.
- Empirical evaluation is small-scale and largely qualitative; lacks strong baselines and quantitative comparisons.
- Assumptions for theoretical guarantees are strong; no analysis of sample complexity, consistency, or noise robustness.
- Pre-image and change-detection components are heuristic and insufficiently validated.
- No discussion of computational scalability, hyperparameter selection, or regularization.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify and contrast with EDMD/kernel-EDMD in depth: derive equivalences/differences between F̂ here and the EDMD generalized eigenproblem; position the contribution precisely.
- Provide quantitative benchmarks against EDMD, exact/optimized DMD, subspace ID, GPDM, and RNN/LSTM on synthetic (with noise) and real datasets; report recovery error, prediction horizons, and computational cost.
- Add regularization (e.g., ridge on Gram matrices), model selection for kernel and truncation, and sensitivity analyses.
- Strengthen theory: conditions for consistency as τ→∞, effects of truncation, and approximation error bounds in RKHS.
- Expand the change-point section with principled likelihood modeling or energy scores, and thorough ROC/ARL evaluations.

Overall, the paper is clearly written and technically competent, but the novelty over existing kernel-EDMD work appears limited and the experiments do not yet demonstrate compelling advantages or breadth.
