Koopman Spectral Analysis of Nonlinear Dynamical Systems with Reproducing Kernels

This manuscript extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), yielding a kernelized DMD (KDMD), and demonstrates it on several toy problems. By leveraging kernel-defined feature maps, the approach can target problem-specific observables, providing a powerful generalization of DMD.

However, the paper is difficult to read. Even basic concepts are presented with heavy mathematical formalism where concise prose would suffice, while other parts are unnecessarily verbose. As written, the exposition will challenge even specialists. I recommend a substantial rewrite with a more pedagogical focus; if clarified, the paper could be highly influential. In its current form, it is too cryptic for the Neural Information Processing Systems conference proceedings.

I. Major Remarks
1. The presentation is overly verbose and could be significantly simplified. For example, the sentence “Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point” can be replaced with a shorter, clearer statement (e.g., “DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear observables, akin to a first-order local expansion”).
2. Section 4 adds little in a short paper and substantially overlaps with the introduction. Consider removing it or integrating the essential points into earlier sections.
3. Section 5 should include at least one concrete example or figure to illustrate the claims. If detailed exposition is not possible due to space, summarize the key insights in the conclusion with clear takeaways.
4. Section 6 is too compressed. It should be expanded to match the clarity and level of detail of Section 5, with explicit links to the algorithmic and empirical implications.

II. Minor Remarks
1. There appears to be a typo or conceptual error in the description of the “toy NL” case; the system described is actually linear.
2. The first two examples are polynomial systems. This is precisely where KDMD should excel—when the kernel can be chosen to match the polynomial structure. Please provide more detail (as in Section 5) on:
   - How the kernel choice reflects the problem structure.
   - The impact of kernel degree and regularization on identified eigenvalues/modes.
   - Comparisons with standard DMD and polynomial observables without kernels.
3. Throughout, replace “features map” with “feature map,” and avoid phrases like “emphasize on”; use “overemphasize” or “focus excessively on” instead.
4. Improve readability by:
   - Introducing key ideas in plain language before giving formal statements.
   - Moving lengthy derivations to an appendix and keeping the main text focused on intuition, algorithmic steps, and practical guidance.
   - Providing a brief, self-contained algorithm box for KDMD with inputs, outputs, and complexity notes.

Recommendation
Promising contribution with significant potential impact, but requires a thorough rewrite to improve clarity, streamline redundant sections, and better illustrate the method on appropriately chosen examples.

Reference
1. Steven L. Brunton, Bingni W. Brunton, Joshua L. Proctor, J. Nathan Kutz, “Koopman invariant subspaces and finite linear representations of nonlinear dynamical systems for control,” arXiv:1510.03007v2 (2015).
