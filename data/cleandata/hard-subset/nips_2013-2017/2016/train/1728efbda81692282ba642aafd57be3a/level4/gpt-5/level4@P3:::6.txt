Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a kernelized variant of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to approximate the spectral objects of the Koopman operator for nonlinear dynamical systems. The key idea is to perform DMD in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), so that the Krylov subspace is spanned by feature evaluations ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1), and the finite-dimensional projection of the Koopman operator is formed via Gram matrices. The authors first present a QR/Arnoldi-style algorithm that yields empirical Ritz values and vectors in the feature space, and then propose a more numerically robust version that projects onto kernel PCA (POD) directions before eigen-decomposition. They show simple demonstrations on a toy system and the Hénon map, and sketch applications to short-term prediction via pre-images, change-point detection via a likelihood-ratio surrogate, and dynamics recognition via subspace distances.

The work sits in a lineage that includes DMD and variants (exact, optimized, sparsity-promoting), extended DMD (EDMD), and kernel methods (kernel PCA, kernel ICA, and related spectral techniques). The robust variant resembles EDMD with a data-adapted dictionary; the use of kernels provides a rich, implicit set of observables, similar in spirit to kernelized EDMD. The paper also connects to spectral approaches for system identification and predictive state methods.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A clear, principled extension of DMD to RKHS that enables nonlinear features without hand-designed dictionaries. The derivations of the kernelized Arnoldi/QR view and the POD-projected variant are well-motivated.
- Clarity of the main algorithmic pipeline: The steps are described in terms of Gram matrices, which makes the kernelization explicit and implementable without explicit feature maps.
- Potential utility: Kernelizing the observable space can improve expressiveness over linear monomials, bringing DMD closer to EDMD with large dictionaries while preserving a compact matrix formulation.
- Educational value: The paper provides a useful introduction to Koopman analysis for readers outside fluid mechanics and highlights links to machine learning.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation
Quality
- Theory: The main results (Theorems 1 and 2) rely on strong assumptions (distinct eigenvalues, φj ∈ span of features, and the Koopman eigenfunctions lying in the span of the retained kernel principal components). These assumptions are not verified in practice, and the implications for approximation/convergence in noisy, finite-sample settings are not analyzed. Please provide error bounds or at least a discussion of consistency and the effect of noise and finite τ (e.g., perturbation analysis of Ritz values in RKHS).
- Numerical stability and regularization: The robust variant projects onto truncated kernel PCA directions, but there is little guidance on truncation choice, Tikhonov regularization, or conditioning. Comparisons with exact DMD, optimized DMD, and EDMD (including kernelized EDMD) are needed to support robustness claims.
- Empirics: Experiments are limited and the figures are difficult to interpret. Beyond the toy system, the Hénon results appear qualitative. For the applications (prediction, recognition, and change detection), there are no quantitative metrics, no baselines beyond a brief kernel change detection comparison, and no ablations (e.g., kernel DMD vs standard DMD vs EDMD). This undermines the claims of practical utility.

Clarity
- Writing is generally clear, but there are typos (e.g., “robustly it”), inconsistent notation (e.g., φ used for both scalar eigenfunctions and feature-space vectors), and some statements that merit correction or qualification (e.g., “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is not generally true outside measure-preserving dynamics).
- Algorithmic exposition would benefit from a self-contained pseudocode with complexities (time O(τ^3), memory O(τ^2) for Gram operations) and practical guidance on kernel choice, bandwidth selection, truncation, and regularization.
- Some references are incomplete; please standardize and, where relevant, cite kernel EDMD explicitly and recent DMD variants.

Originality
- Kernelizing DMD is a natural extension in the spirit of EDMD and kernel methods. The QR/Arnoldi perspective in RKHS and the POD-projected variant are well-articulated, but the novelty relative to EDMD with kernels is not fully distinguished. A careful comparison (conceptual and empirical) to EDMD (Williams et al., 2015) and its kernelized implementations is needed.

Significance
- The approach is promising as a general-purpose, dictionary-free route to nonlinear Koopman spectral analysis. However, the current empirical evidence is not strong enough to establish impact for machine learning applications. Stronger, quantitative studies on standard nonlinear benchmarks (e.g., Duffing, Lorenz, Van der Pol, cylinder wake data) with clear metrics (eigenvalue error, reconstruction error, short-horizon prediction MSE, classification accuracy, change detection ROC) would significantly improve significance.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Clarify whether explicit feature maps are required: as written, all operations are via Gram matrices, so no explicit map is needed. Please make this explicit and provide computational complexity.
- Baselines: Include standard DMD, exact/optimized DMD, EDMD (with polynomial/RBF dictionaries), and kernel EDMD on the toy and Hénon examples, and in the application sections.
- Noise and stochasticity: Many ML settings involve process and measurement noise. Discuss the stochastic Koopman operator and demonstrate robustness to noise (e.g., additive Gaussian noise) empirically.
- Multiplicity and near-resonance: How does the method handle repeated or nearly repeated eigenvalues? Consider Schur-based variants or regularized eigen-solvers.
- Pre-image and prediction: Compare your MDS-style pre-image to standard methods (Kwok & Tsang, 2004; gradient-based pre-image). Report quantitative short-horizon prediction accuracy vs baselines.
- Change-point detection: The proposed exponential-family surrogate is unconventional; justify the modeling choice, clarify whether it defines a conditional vs marginal likelihood, and compare to kernel two-sample/MMD-based detectors and sequential variants (e.g., Harchaoui & Cappé, 2007; Gretton et al., 2012).
- Dynamics recognition: Provide classification accuracy, confusion matrices, and comparisons to subspace methods based on (kernel) PCA and EDMD features. Describe feature dimensionality and sensitivity to kernel parameters.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and principled kernelized formulation of DMD in RKHS.
- Useful bridge between Koopman spectral analysis and ML’s kernel toolbox.
- Potentially practical for nonlinear systems without hand-crafted dictionaries.
- Generally well-written and educational.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical validation and weak comparisons to strong baselines (EDMD, optimized DMD, kernel EDMD).
- Strong theoretical assumptions without error analysis; unclear robustness to noise.
- Some clarity and notation issues; incomplete references.
- Applications are interesting but currently anecdotal and lack quantitative evaluation.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The methodological idea is sound and potentially impactful, but the paper needs stronger empirical evidence, clearer positioning with respect to EDMD/kernel EDMD and DMD variants, and improved clarity and rigor around assumptions, robustness, and practical guidance. Strengthening these aspects could make this a solid contribution.
