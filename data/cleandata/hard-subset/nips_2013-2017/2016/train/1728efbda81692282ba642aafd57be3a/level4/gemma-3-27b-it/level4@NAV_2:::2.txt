The paper proposes a spectral analysis method for nonlinear dynamical systems based on the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs). The core idea is to perform a modal decomposition using finite-length data sequences, effectively reducing the problem to calculating orthogonal bases for a Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing an eigendecomposition. The authors extend existing Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) techniques by utilizing kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with potentially richer, nonlinear basis functions. Applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition are then demonstrated.

This work builds upon the established field of spectral analysis for dynamical systems, relating to techniques like Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) and DMD [25, 26]. It acknowledges the limitations of POD and seeks to overcome them by leveraging the Koopman operator, which provides a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics. The paper correctly positions its contribution as a kernelization of DMD, allowing for potentially more accurate approximations of Koopman eigenfunctions beyond local linearizations. It appropriately cites relevant prior work in dynamical systems, machine learning, and kernel methods [11, 34, 12, 15, 31, 17, 25, 26]. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its sound theoretical foundation and the clear connection drawn between kernel methods, the Koopman operator, and DMD. The extension of DMD to RKHSs is a logical and potentially valuable step, providing a mechanism to capture more complex dynamics. The inclusion of practical applications—even if preliminary—helps to illustrate the potential utility of the proposed method. The authors also demonstrate awareness of potential numerical challenges, such as ill-conditioning, and propose a robustification technique using POD bases. The detailed description of the algorithm and mathematical derivations are well-presented.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the theoretical framework is solid, the novelty of kernelizing DMD appears incremental. The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough discussion of how this approach differs from, and improves upon, existing kernel-based methods for dynamical systems analysis (e.g., kernel subspace identification [23, 14] or Gaussian process dynamical models [37]) would strengthen the contribution. The empirical evaluation, while demonstrating feasibility, is limited in scope. The toy system and the Henon map are useful for validation, but more complex and realistic datasets would be needed to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method. The application section feels somewhat cursory; a deeper investigation of the performance of the method compared to state-of-the-art techniques in each application area (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) is lacking. The comparison with kernel change detection [7] in Fig. 4 is a good start, but more extensive benchmarking is needed. The reference to related work could also be extended; for example, mentioning recent advances in Koopman operator learning with neural networks would be relevant.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods.
*   The algorithm is well-defined and mathematically justified.
*   The inclusion of practical applications, even preliminary, demonstrates potential utility.
*   The authors address potential numerical challenges and propose a robustification technique.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the approach is incremental.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited in scope and lacks a thorough comparison to existing methods.
*   The application section feels cursory and requires more in-depth analysis.
*   The related work section could be more comprehensive.



Overall, this paper represents a solid contribution to the field, but its impact is somewhat limited by the incremental nature of the work and the lack of a more comprehensive empirical evaluation. I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more thorough comparison to existing methods and a more detailed analysis of the performance on more complex datasets.
