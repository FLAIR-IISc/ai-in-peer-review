This paper introduces a spectral analysis framework using the Koopman operator defined in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. The authors then follow a standard approach to develop kernel methods for the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems from empirical data. Eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator leads to efficiency gains in both synthetic and real-world data analysis. This paper presents a significant kernel construction for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems; the contribution is well-motivated and experimentally validated. However, I have some concerns that I hope the authors will address.

It is unclear to me what the substantial difference is between the presented work and the paper by Williams, Kevrekidis, and Rowley [35], “A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator: Extending dynamic mode decomposition,” *Journal of Nonlinear Science*, 25:1307–1346, 2015, which already introduced Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for analyzing nonlinear system dynamics. I would appreciate comments and discussion clarifying the key distinctions, as well as an improved discussion in section 4. 

The field of nonlinear dynamical system analysis with kernels is extensive, and some relevant references are missing. The authors might consider papers by Ralaivola and d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., Mouattamid and Schaback, or Franz and Schölkopf.

Although the Koopman operator is generally defined for deterministic systems, I would appreciate comments on the performance of the method in stochastic settings, which are often more important and realistic. The authors briefly touch on this issue, but further discussion – and potentially a small-scale experiment – would be welcome. 

The authors clarify the remark on line 216, page 5, that “the estimation procedure itself does not necessarily require a sequence but rather a collection of pairs of consecutive observables, where each pair is supposed to be” nonlinearly pointwise related. I am curious about the implications of this assumption in multidimensional time series where pointwise relationships may be weak. What advantages does the proposed kernel construction offer in such cases? Related to this, I would also appreciate elaboration on the causal assumptions inherent in the setting.

Regarding the experimental setup, I find it somewhat unchallenging. The prediction task via time-series forecasting follows a standard, well-known approach. Under the "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" subsection, there is a lack of discussion connecting the proposed approach to the extensive literature on time-series clustering (e.g., via kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods), and crucially, no experimental comparison. Furthermore, for the sequential change-point detection experiment, a comparison to state-of-the-art online change detection methods with kernels would be more compelling (e.g., the method used by the authors is known to struggle with complex dynamics). More convincing experiments are needed to support the development.

*Minor*: The paper is generally very well written and clear. I identified two minor typos: please check the indices on τ−1 vs. τ, and replace the dot with a comma in k(xi.xj) (last equation on page 7).
