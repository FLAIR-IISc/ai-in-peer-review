## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title - *Please Insert Here*]

This paper proposes a spectral method for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems based on the Koopman operator, an operator representing function composition. The core idea is to apply a decomposition – analogous to kernel PCA and subspace analysis – to the Koopman operator representing the iterated dynamics of the system, specifically *x(t+2) = f(f(x(t)))*. Given trajectories collected from the system, the authors perform an eigen-decomposition of this operator and demonstrate its application to embedding and recognition of dynamical systems, achieving promising results.

The central contribution, for readers familiar with kernel methods, lies in the insightful observation that function composition can be effectively represented as a linear operator, allowing established techniques like eigen-decomposition to be applied to the analysis of nonlinear dynamics. Viewing a dynamical system as a specific instance of function composition provides a natural and compelling application of this representation. 

However, the novelty of this approach appears somewhat incremental. Existing work in dynamical systems, such as Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), already provides methods for extracting meaningful dynamics from observed trajectories. This paper essentially kernelizes DMD, and while the theoretical connection to Koopman operators and the use of kernel methods are well-articulated, the degree of novelty feels limited. 

Furthermore, the presented applications – embedding and recognition of dynamical systems – while demonstrably successful, are not particularly compelling in establishing a clear advantage over alternative methods. Several techniques already exist for these tasks, including recurrent neural networks and methods specifically designed for change detection in dynamical systems. The paper does not sufficiently articulate *why* this Koopman-based approach is superior, or uniquely suited, to these existing alternatives. A more thorough comparison, or application to a problem where existing methods demonstrably struggle, would strengthen the paper’s impact.

**Strengths:**

*   **Elegant Theoretical Framework:** The connection between Koopman operators, function composition, and kernel methods is clearly and elegantly presented.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The application of eigen-decomposition to the Koopman operator appears mathematically sound and well-motivated.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The authors demonstrate that the method performs well on the chosen tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core contribution feels incremental, building on existing work in DMD and kernel methods.
*   **Uncompelling Applications:** The selected applications do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.
*   **Lack of Comparative Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison to relevant techniques, such as DMD and recurrent neural networks, highlighting both strengths and weaknesses.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What specific scenarios or types of dynamical systems would particularly benefit from this Koopman-based approach compared to existing methods like DMD or recurrent neural networks?
*   Could the authors elaborate on the computational complexity of this method, particularly concerning the choice of kernel and the size of the resulting operator?  How does this scale with the dimensionality of the state space and the length of the trajectories?
*   Are there theoretical guarantees on the convergence or accuracy of the method, and if so, under what conditions?

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and theoretically interesting application of kernel methods to the analysis of dynamical systems. However, the limited novelty and uncompelling applications weaken its overall impact. While the work is valuable as a contribution to the growing field of Koopman operator theory, it does not currently rise to the level of a strong NIPS paper.  I recommend rejection with encouragement to expand the comparative analysis and explore more challenging or unique applications.
