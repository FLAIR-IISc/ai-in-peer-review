Title: Koopman Spectral Analysis of Nonlinear Dynamical Systems with Reproducing Kernels

Summary:
The paper extends dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), yielding a kernelized DMD (KDMD) method for approximating Koopman spectral objects from data. By defining a kernel (and thus an implicit feature map), the approach connects DMD to user-specified features of interest. The method is demonstrated on several toy problems. Conceptually, this is a compelling generalization that could broaden the applicability of Koopman-based analysis.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual value: kernelizing DMD to operate in RKHS is a natural and powerful idea that can tailor the observable space to the problem.
- Potential impact: if presented clearly, KDMD could become a widely used tool in data-driven analysis of nonlinear dynamics.
- The use of kernels provides flexibility and a pragmatic way to encode prior structure (e.g., polynomial observables).

Weaknesses:
- The paper is difficult to read. Exposition is dense and overly formal even for simple ideas, while other parts are verbose yet still unclear.
- Organizational issues: some sections are redundant with the introduction; important sections are too compressed to be useful.
- Empirical evaluation is restricted to simple toy examples, and several descriptions are ambiguous or possibly inconsistent.

Major comments:
1) Exposition and clarity. Many statements that should be communicated intuitively are instead written in a heavy mathematical style. For example, the notion that DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear observables can be stated succinctly without extended analogies. Please simplify and streamline:
- Introduce the key objects (observables, Koopman operator, DMD, kernel/RKHS) with brief, intuitive definitions before the formalism.
- Prefer short, direct sentences; move lengthy derivations to an appendix where possible.
- Provide a running example that threads through the method to anchor the notation.

2) Redundancy of Section 4. The content of Section 4 substantially overlaps with the introduction. In a short conference paper, this section could be removed or merged with the introduction to improve flow and conserve space for core contributions.

3) Section 5 presentation. The material in Section 5 would benefit from concrete illustration or, if it is primarily conceptual, a concise summary in the conclusion. As written, it does not sufficiently advance the reader’s understanding. Consider:
- Adding a worked example that shows how Section 5’s ideas change the algorithm or interpretation.
- If not essential, condensing the section and using the space to deepen earlier sections.

4) Section 6 is too condensed. This section should be developed to the level of detail of Section 5. Important components—how KDMD estimates eigenvalues/eigenfunctions, reconstructs modes, and interprets results—should be explained with clear steps and, ideally, a small illustrative example. Clarify assumptions, algorithmic steps, and any computational considerations.

5) Kernel choice and problem structure. The paper notes that the first two problems are polynomial. This is precisely where KDMD should excel with polynomial kernels (or finite-dimensional polynomial feature maps). Please:
- Make the kernel–dynamics match explicit, explain why polynomial kernels are effective here, and discuss how one might proceed when the kernel is mismatched to the dynamics.
- Provide guidance on kernel selection and its effect on approximation quality and numerical stability.

Minor comments:
1) Possible typo in the “toy NL” case: the system described appears to be linear. Please verify and correct the label or the system description.

2) Expand details for the first two polynomial examples. Since these are natural matches for KDMD, include more information (e.g., the exact kernel used, rank truncation, conditioning, and how results compare to standard DMD).

3) Editorial and style issues:
- Avoid phrases like “features map”; use “feature map.” Ensure consistent terminology (e.g., “nonlinear,” “reproducing kernel Hilbert space”).
- Reduce redundancy throughout, particularly where sections repeat content from the introduction.
- Tighten long sentences and remove nonessential asides to improve readability.

Overall assessment:
The paper addresses an important and timely problem with a conceptually strong contribution: extending DMD to RKHS via kernel methods. The potential impact is high, and with a clearer presentation and better-structured narrative, this work could be influential. However, in its current form the exposition is too cryptic and uneven for the NeurIPS audience. I recommend rejection at this stage, with strong encouragement to revise for clarity, streamline the organization, and expand specific sections as noted above. A more pedagogical rewrite, with targeted examples and guidance on kernel selection, would likely attract significant interest and citations.

Reference:
- Steven L. Brunton, Bingni W. Brunton, Joshua L. Proctor, J. Nathan Kutz, “Koopman invariant subspaces and finite linear representations of nonlinear dynamical systems for control,” arXiv:1510.03007, 2015.
