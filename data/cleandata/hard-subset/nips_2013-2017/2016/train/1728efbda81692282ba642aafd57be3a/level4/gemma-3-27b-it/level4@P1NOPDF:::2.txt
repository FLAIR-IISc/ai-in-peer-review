The paper proposes a spectral method for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems, leveraging the Koopman operator—an operator representation of function composition. Specifically, the authors apply Koopman operator decomposition to the forward nonlinear dynamics of the system, modeled as x(t+2) = f(f(x(t))). Given trajectories collected from the system, they perform an eigen-decomposition of this operator, drawing on ideas from kernel PCA and subspace analysis. The method is then applied to embedding and recognition of dynamical systems, with the authors demonstrating promising performance.

For readers familiar with kernel methods, the key novelty lies in representing function composition as a linear operator, and subsequently applying decomposition techniques to these compositions. Dynamical systems represent a specific instance of such function compositions, making this a natural and well-suited application. However, given the existing Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) algorithm within the dynamical systems literature, the paper’s novelty appears largely confined to a kernelized version of this approach. 

Furthermore, alternative methods exist for dynamical system recognition, such as recurrent neural networks, and for change detection within these systems. Consequently, the applications presented in the paper do not strongly demonstrate the advantages or unique capabilities of this method. While the results are encouraging, the presented applications are not particularly compelling in establishing *why* this approach is superior to existing techniques.
