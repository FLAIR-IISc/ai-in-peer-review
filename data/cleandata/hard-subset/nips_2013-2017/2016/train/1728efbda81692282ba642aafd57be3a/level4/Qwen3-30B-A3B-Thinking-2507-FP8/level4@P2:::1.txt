

This paper introduces a novel approach to Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors propose a modal decomposition algorithm that computes orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performs eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator's projection onto these bases. This method provides a feature extraction procedure for nonlinear dynamics, with applications demonstrated in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The work builds upon established DMD techniques [25, 26, 33] and extends them by incorporating kernel methods to handle nonlinearities beyond the scope of traditional linear basis functions.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with solid theoretical foundations. The mathematical derivations are clear and well-justified, particularly the extension of DMD to RKHS and the robustification via POD bases. The authors correctly identify that existing DMD variants approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials (analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion), while their kernel-based approach provides richer nonlinear basis functions. This represents a meaningful theoretical advancement in Koopman spectral analysis.

However, several concerns must be addressed before the paper's significance can be fully appreciated. First, the distinction between this work and Williams et al.'s extended DMD [35] requires clarification. Both papers address Koopman spectral analysis using basis functions, but the paper fails to sufficiently explain how the kernel-based approach differs from or improves upon Williams' method. The authors should explicitly compare their method with [35] in terms of theoretical guarantees, computational complexity, and empirical performance.

The literature review is incomplete. Several key papers on kernel-based dynamical systems analysis are missing, including work by Ralaivola and d'Alche Buc [36], Tuia et al. [37], Rojo-Alvarez et al. [38], and Mouattamid and Schaback [39]. Including these would better position the work within the broader field and strengthen the claim of novelty.

Regarding the stochastic setting, the authors briefly mention extending the Koopman operator to stochastic systems but provide no experimental validation. Given that real-world systems are often stochastic, a brief toy experiment demonstrating performance on noisy data would significantly strengthen the paper's relevance.

The paper's experimental validation is limited in scope. The prediction approach via preimage follows standard methods, and the dynamics recognition application lacks comparison to established time-series clustering techniques (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernel methods). The change-point detection experiment should be compared with state-of-the-art online change detection methods using kernels, rather than presenting results in isolation. More ambitious experiments would better support the claimed advantages of the proposed method.

The paper is well-written and generally clear, with only minor technical errors to correct (e.g., "Hilbert" instead of "Hiber" on page 2, and "k(xi,xj)" instead of "k(xi.xj)" on page 7).

Strengths:
- Solid theoretical foundation extending DMD to RKHS
- Clear mathematical derivations of the proposed algorithm
- Appropriate demonstration of the method on synthetic and real data
- Well-organized structure with clear exposition of the algorithm

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient differentiation from Williams et al. [35] and other extended DMD approaches
- Missing key references in kernel-based dynamical systems analysis
- Limited experimental validation lacking comparison to state-of-the-art
- Superficial treatment of stochastic systems despite their practical importance
- Applications lack connection to and comparison with established methods in their domains

Overall, this paper presents an interesting extension of DMD using kernel methods, but the significance is diminished by insufficient differentiation from prior work and limited experimental validation. The theoretical contribution is sound, but the empirical evidence does not fully support the claimed advantages of the approach.

Pro:
- Theoretical extension of DMD to RKHS is mathematically rigorous
- The paper provides a clear algorithm for Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS
- The robustification via POD bases is a practical improvement

Con:
- The main difference from Williams et al. [35] is not adequately explained
- The experimental validation is not sufficiently ambitious or comparative
- Several relevant references in kernel-based dynamical systems analysis are missing
- The connection to stochastic systems is underdeveloped

The paper makes a contribution to Koopman spectral analysis but falls short of demonstrating significant advantages over existing methods. With the suggested clarifications and expanded experimental validation, it could be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper would benefit from addressing the concerns outlined above before being considered for acceptance.
