The paper proposes a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for nonlinear dynamical systems. The Koopman operator is an infinite-dimensional linear operator that represents nonlinear dynamics without linearization. The authors develop an algorithm to perform this analysis using finite-length data sequences generated from a nonlinear system. The algorithm is essentially reduced to the calculation of a set of orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and the eigendecomposition of the projection of the Koopman operator onto the subspace spanned by the bases.

The paper provides a clear and well-written introduction to the Koopman operator and its relevance to machine learning. The authors demonstrate the algorithm and the analysis using two simple dynamical systems, a damped linear 1D dynamics and a chaotic map. They also apply their method to real-world locomotion data and compare the performance with standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA.

The paper derives an interesting extension of DMD, somewhat along the lines of older work on "kernelizing" techniques phrased in terms of linear operators (such as kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, GP bandits, etc.). The authors provide some experimental results on largely artificial problems. The paper has educational value for people foreign to concepts used in fluid dynamics.

However, to convince machine learning readers of the practical usefulness of these techniques, the authors need to do more work. What are potential use cases? Can you compete, at least to some extent, with the state of the art there? An ML discipline where inference and learning in dynamical systems is important, and where a lot of work has been done, is robotics. What would Koopman analysis buy somebody working in robotics? Or please pick any other domain.

Additionally, it is not quite clear whether in the modal decomposition algorithm one needs the explicit representation in feature space. If so, this is a limitation compared to kernel PCA, and the authors should make this more explicit. It would also be good if the authors would show the performance of standard DMD on the toy example and the Henon map to demonstrate the differences in this simple setup.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes an interesting extension of DMD using RKHS.
* The authors provide a clear and well-written introduction to the Koopman operator and its relevance to machine learning.
* The paper has educational value for people foreign to concepts used in fluid dynamics.

Weaknesses:

* The authors need to do more work to convince machine learning readers of the practical usefulness of these techniques.
* The paper lacks clear potential use cases and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods.
* It is not clear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires explicit representation in feature space.

Originality:

* The paper proposes a new extension of DMD using RKHS.
* The authors provide a novel application of the Koopman operator to machine learning.

Significance:

* The paper has the potential to contribute to the development of new methods for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems.
* The authors' work may have implications for various fields, including robotics and fluid dynamics.

Quality:

* The paper is well-written and clear.
* The authors provide a thorough introduction to the Koopman operator and its relevance to machine learning.
* The experimental results are well-presented, but more comparisons with state-of-the-art methods would be beneficial.

Clarity:

* The paper is well-organized and easy to follow.
* The authors provide clear explanations of the Koopman operator and its relevance to machine learning.
* The notation is clear and consistent throughout the paper.

Recommendation:

* Accept the paper with minor revisions.
* The authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above, including providing more comparisons with state-of-the-art methods and clarifying the requirements of the modal decomposition algorithm.
* The authors should also consider adding more potential use cases and implications of their work for various fields.
