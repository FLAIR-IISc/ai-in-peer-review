Summary
This paper develops a kernelized variant of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to perform Koopman spectral analysis in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The central idea is to model nonlinear dynamics via the linear Koopman operator acting on observables, and to estimate its spectral objects (eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes) from finite trajectory data. The authors propose two algorithms:

- A direct RKHS formulation: form the Krylov matrix Mτ = [ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)] in feature space, compute a Gram–Schmidt QR factorization, project the Koopman operator KH onto the resulting subspace, and eigendecompose the companion matrix. Theorems show that for distinct Ritz values and sufficiently rich data (ψ = 0), the representation recovers finite expansions analogous to classical DMD.

- A robustified variant: project data onto kernel PCA (i.e., POD) directions to address ill-conditioning, then estimate the finite-dimensional operator F̂ = U∗KH U entirely from Gram matrices, eigendecompose F̂, and reconstruct empirical Koopman modes. A theorem connects the eigenpairs of F̂ to true Koopman eigenpairs under span assumptions on the Koopman eigenfunctions relative to the kernel PCA subspace.

Beyond spectral estimation, the paper sketches three applications using the extracted modes/features: short-term prediction via a preimage reconstruction (using MDS-like distances), recognition/embedding of dynamics via kernel principal angles between subspaces spanned by estimated modes, and sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-style statistic derived from a nonparametric exponential family.

Relation to prior work
DMD and its variants (exact DMD, optimized DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD) are well established in fluids and control. Extended DMD (EDMD) formulates Koopman estimation with user-specified dictionaries; kernel EDMD realizes this with implicit feature maps and Gram matrices. The proposed approach is very close in spirit to EDMD with kernel dictionaries and to standard practices that use POD truncation for robustness. The paper appropriately cites EDMD (Williams et al.) and DMD variants, but does not empirically compare against them. The preimage step leverages classic kernel PCA preimage ideas.

Strengths
- Clear and useful RKHS formulation that enables nonlinear spectral analysis without manual dictionary design; all computations reduce to Gram matrices.
- The robustification via kernel PCA is practical and aligns with best practices in DMD/POD pipelines.
- Theorems articulate when the finite-dimensional projections recover Koopman-like expansions and connect feature-space eigenpairs to Koopman eigenfunctions.
- The application ideas (prediction, recognition, change detection) illustrate how Koopman features can support downstream ML tasks.
- Code availability is a plus.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to EDMD and kernelized variants. The method appears to be a kernel realization of EDMD/DMD with standard POD truncation, with limited differentiation beyond presentation.
- Theoretical guarantees rely on strong assumptions (distinct eigenvalues; eigenfunctions lying in the span of kernel PCA subspaces; ψ = 0). There are no error, consistency, or convergence rates, nor guidance on how truncation and kernel choice affect approximation quality.
- The preimage-based prediction procedure is heuristic; no guarantees are provided, and sensitivity to neighbor selection and kernel parameters is not analyzed.
- The change-point detection derivation is ad hoc. The move to a nonparametric exponential family with ϕ∘f as natural parameters, and the subsequent simplification by dropping a term in the likelihood ratio, lacks justification and empirical ablation.
- Experiments are minimal (toy system, Hénon map, locomotion sequences), qualitative, and omit quantitative comparisons to EDMD/exact DMD/optimized DMD. No analysis of noise robustness, stochastic dynamics, kernel bandwidth selection, rank truncation, or computational scaling is provided.
- Clarity issues: typos and grammar (“robustly it,” “almost of”), incomplete reference metadata, and figure descriptions that do not fully explain experimental setups and metrics.

Quality
The derivations are generally sound at the algorithmic level, and the RKHS manipulations via Gram matrices are standard. However, the paper would benefit from tighter theoretical statements (e.g., conditions under which the kernel PCA subspace approximates the span of Koopman eigenfunctions) and from a more rigorous treatment of the preimage and change-point sections. The empirical evaluation does not convincingly validate the approach against strong baselines or in realistic, noisy conditions.

Clarity
The paper is mostly readable but needs editing for grammar and organization. Several definitions and assumptions could be stated more precisely. Figures should include quantitative metrics and clearer captions. References need complete bibliographic information.

Originality
Moderate. The paper primarily repackages EDMD/DMD ideas in an RKHS with a POD-based stabilization that mirrors common DMD practice. The application sketches are interesting but not uniquely enabled by the proposed method.

Significance
Potentially useful to practitioners seeking a kernelized Koopman pipeline, but the current experimental scope and lack of comparative results limit demonstrated impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative comparisons to EDMD (with both handcrafted and kernel dictionaries), exact DMD, and optimized DMD on benchmark nonlinear systems (e.g., Duffing, Van der Pol, Hénon, Lorenz), reporting prediction error, mode recovery accuracy, and computational cost.
- Analyze sensitivity to kernel choice, bandwidth, and truncation rank; include cross-validation or heuristic selection strategies.
- Evaluate robustness to noise and short trajectories; include stochastic systems if claims extend beyond deterministic dynamics.
- Provide computational complexity and memory analysis; discuss scalability to large τ via low-rank approximations or randomized kernel methods.
- Strengthen the preimage section with ablations and, if possible, guarantees or at least failure cases.
- Justify the change-point statistic more rigorously or move it to an appendix with empirical validation.
- Edit for grammar and complete the references.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clean RKHS formulation and practical algorithm that many ML/NIPS readers may find accessible.
- Bridges Koopman analysis and kernel methods; could stimulate cross-community interest.
- Useful as a feature-extraction framework for dynamical systems.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to EDMD/kernel EDMD and DMD variants.
- Weak empirical validation and lack of baseline comparisons.
- Heuristic application sections without rigorous support.
- Clarity and presentation issues.

Overall
Borderline, leaning toward rejection in its current form due to incremental novelty and insufficient experimental evidence. Strengthening comparisons, theory, and evaluation could make this a solid contribution.
