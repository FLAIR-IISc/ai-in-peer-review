

This paper extends Koopman spectral analysis to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), presenting a modal decomposition algorithm that leverages kernel-based orthogonal bases for the Krylov subspace and eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. The work builds upon DMD by replacing linear monomial basis functions with nonlinear kernel representations, enabling more expressive approximations of Koopman eigenfunctions beyond local linearization. The authors demonstrate the method on synthetic systems (including a toy nonlinear system and Hénon map) and real-world applications like state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using locomotion data. The paper is technically sound, with clear exposition of the RKHS-based DMD framework, robust implementation via POD projection (Section 4), and thorough empirical validation across multiple domains. The connection to prior work on Koopman analysis (e.g., Williams et al.'s extended DMD) and spectral methods (e.g., POD, subspace identification) is well contextualized in Section 5.

Strengths include the elegant mathematical formulation of the kernel DMD algorithm, its practical robustness through POD-based regularization, and compelling applications that showcase the method's utility for feature extraction in nonlinear dynamics. The writing is exceptionally clear, with well-organized derivations and effective empirical examples (Figures 1–4). The significance lies in providing a more flexible spectral analysis framework that generalizes standard DMD while maintaining computational feasibility.

Two minor suggestions for enhancement: (1) Including a direct comparison between the proposed kernel DMD and non-kernel-based Koopman spectral methods (e.g., standard DMD or extended DMD) in the empirical evaluation would strengthen the demonstration of kernel advantages; (2) A brief exploration of kernel choice sensitivity (e.g., polynomial vs. Gaussian kernels) and its impact on eigenvalue estimation would provide valuable practical guidance for future applications. These additions would further highlight the method's distinct contributions without altering the paper's core technical merit. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, with clear significance, quality, and originality.
