

# Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

This paper extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproduce kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors propose an algorithm that approximates the Koopman operator's eigenfunctions using nonlinear kernel-based basis functions rather than the linear monomials used in conventional DMD. The method involves computing orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the operator's projection onto these bases. The paper demonstrates applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world locomotion data.

The paper builds upon established work in Koopman spectral analysis (Rowley et al., 2009; Mezić, 2005) and DMD (Schmid, 2010; Tu et al., 2014), as well as kernel methods in machine learning (Schölkopf et al., 1998; Shawe-Taylor & Cristianini, 2004). It properly contextualizes its contribution within the broader literature on spectral analysis of dynamical systems, acknowledging related work in fluid dynamics and machine learning.

**Quality:** The theoretical development is technically sound, with clear mathematical derivations and proofs. The extension of DMD to RKHS represents a mathematically valid approach to approximating Koopman eigenfunctions with richer nonlinear basis functions. The robustification via POD bases (Section 4) addresses a practical limitation of the direct Gram-Schmidt approach. However, the experimental validation lacks sufficient comparison with standard DMD and other state-of-the-art methods for dynamical systems in machine learning contexts. The paper demonstrates the method on simple synthetic examples and a toy locomotion dataset but does not establish clear performance advantages over existing techniques for practical ML applications.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making complex concepts accessible to readers unfamiliar with Koopman spectral analysis. The authors provide clear definitions, well-structured mathematical derivations, and helpful examples. The experimental section is logically presented with appropriate visualizations. The only minor clarity issue concerns whether explicit feature space representation is required for the algorithm's implementation (a limitation compared to standard kernel methods), which the authors should address more explicitly.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel extension of DMD to RKHS, which is a meaningful technical contribution. However, the core idea follows a well-established pattern in machine learning of "kernelizing" linear methods (as the authors acknowledge). While the specific application to Koopman spectral analysis is novel, the underlying approach is conceptually similar to kernel PCA and other kernel-based methods. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this approach differs from and improves upon existing kernel-based methods for dynamical systems.

**Significance:** The paper's significance to the NIPS community is somewhat limited. While the technique is mathematically interesting, the authors fail to convincingly demonstrate its practical utility for machine learning applications. The paper does not clearly articulate why Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS would provide advantages over existing ML approaches for dynamical systems (e.g., recurrent neural networks, Gaussian process dynamical models). The applications to locomotion data and change-point detection are promising but lack sufficient comparison with state-of-the-art ML methods. The authors should identify a specific ML domain where their method provides demonstrable advantages over current approaches.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Technically sound mathematical extension of DMD
- Well-written and accessible to ML researchers unfamiliar with Koopman analysis
- Demonstrates the potential of kernel methods for dynamical systems analysis
- Provides a useful educational resource for ML researchers interested in spectral analysis

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Fails to clearly establish practical advantages over existing methods for ML applications
- Limited demonstration of relevance to typical machine learning problems (e.g., robotics, time series analysis)
- Unclear why the RKHS approach provides benefits beyond standard DMD for the problems studied
- Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art ML methods for dynamical systems

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More detailed comparisons with standard DMD and other ML methods on benchmark datasets
2. Clearer articulation of specific ML applications where this approach provides demonstrable advantages
3. Discussion of computational complexity and scalability to larger datasets
4. More explicit identification of the limitations of the RKHS approach compared to standard kernel methods

While the paper makes a technically valid contribution to the mathematical theory of Koopman spectral analysis, its significance for the NIPS community would be substantially enhanced if the authors could better demonstrate its practical utility and advantages for machine learning applications. The current work represents a promising direction but requires more compelling evidence of its value to the machine learning community.
