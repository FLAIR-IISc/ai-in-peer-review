

**Review for "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"**

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to perform spectral analysis of the Koopman operator for nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors build upon the Koopman operator framework, which provides a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics, and extend the standard DMD approach—previously using linear monomials of observables—to leverage the expressive power of kernel functions. The core contribution is a modal decomposition algorithm that computes orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performs eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. The method returns a finite set of modes representing the dynamics, enabling feature extraction for applications like state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature. Koopman spectral analysis has been a topic of interest in recent years (e.g., Rowley et al., 2009; Williams et al., 2015), and DMD has gained traction in fluid dynamics and engineering (Schmid, 2010; Tu et al., 2014). The authors correctly acknowledge related work, including extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), which uses pre-determined basis functions, and kernel-based approaches like kernel PCA (Schölkopf et al., 1998). The proposed method extends these ideas by embedding the Koopman operator within RKHS, enabling nonlinear basis functions rather than the linear approximations used in standard DMD.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation is solid, with clear mathematical formulations and proofs (Theorems 1 and 2). The connection between the RKHS-based approach and standard DMD is well-explained, and the robustification via POD bases addresses practical implementation challenges.
2. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-structured, with clear sections explaining the Koopman operator, the proposed algorithm, and its applications. The empirical examples (Section 6) effectively demonstrate the method's ability to recover eigenvalues for both synthetic and chaotic systems.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The applications section (Section 7) demonstrates practical utility through state prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection. The locomotion data example (Fig. 3) is particularly compelling, showing how the method can extract meaningful features for classification.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Novelty:** While the mathematical extension to RKHS is technically sound, the core idea—kernelizing DMD—is not sufficiently novel. The authors acknowledge that extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) is similar, yet the paper does not convincingly distinguish their approach from existing kernel-based extensions. The novelty appears limited to applying kernel methods to DMD rather than introducing a fundamentally new technique.
2. **Comparative Analysis:** The applications section lacks thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods. For dynamics recognition, the paper does not compare against recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or other deep learning approaches that have shown strong performance in sequence modeling. Similarly, for change-point detection, the comparison with kernel-based methods (e.g., [7]) is limited, and the paper does not establish why kernel DMD outperforms simpler baselines.
3. **Practical Impact:** The empirical results are promising but do not sufficiently demonstrate why this method is preferable to alternatives. For instance, the prediction task (Fig. 2) shows reasonable performance but does not quantify improvements over standard DMD or other techniques. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**

*Pro:*
- Solid theoretical foundation with clear mathematical contributions.
- Well-organized and well-written, with strong empirical validation across multiple applications.
- Provides a meaningful extension of DMD using RKHS, addressing a limitation of linear basis functions.

*Con:*
- Novelty is limited to kernelizing DMD, with insufficient distinction from existing work (e.g., extended DMD).
- Comparative analysis with state-of-the-art methods is weak, reducing the impact of the empirical results.
- Applications lack compelling evidence of superiority over simpler or more established techniques.

**Recommendation:**
The paper makes a technically sound contribution to Koopman spectral analysis, extending DMD to kernel-based feature spaces. While the mathematical formulation is rigorous and the applications are well-motivated, the novelty is somewhat incremental, and the paper would benefit from a stronger comparative analysis against existing methods. The authors should more clearly articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) and other kernel-based methods. Additionally, a more thorough comparison with deep learning approaches for dynamics recognition would strengthen the case for the method's practical utility.

The paper aligns with NIPS scope, as it addresses a fundamental problem in nonlinear dynamics with a method grounded in machine learning theory. However, the limited novelty and lack of compelling comparative results make it a borderline submission. With revisions to clarify the novelty and strengthen the empirical comparisons, this could be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, I recommend a marginal accept.
