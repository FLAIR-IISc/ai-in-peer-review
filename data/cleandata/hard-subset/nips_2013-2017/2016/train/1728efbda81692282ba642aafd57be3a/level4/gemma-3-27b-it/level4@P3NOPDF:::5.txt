## Review of “Koopman Spectral Analysis of Nonlinear Dynamical Systems with Reproducing Kernels” (NIPS 2016)

This manuscript presents an extension of Dynamical Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), resulting in a novel algorithm termed KDMD. The core contribution lies in leveraging kernel methods to define feature maps within the DMD framework, offering a powerful generalization capable of aligning the analysis with specific features of interest. While the theoretical foundation appears solid and the potential impact significant, the paper currently suffers from issues of clarity and presentation that hinder its accessibility.

**I. MAJOR REMARKS**

The primary concern is the paper’s readability. The authors frequently employ overly complex mathematical phrasing where simpler, more direct language would suffice. Conversely, other sections are unnecessarily verbose. This inconsistency makes following the argument challenging, even for researchers familiar with the underlying concepts. A substantial rewrite focused on pedagogical clarity is strongly recommended.

Specifically:

1. **Presentation & Verbosity:** The paper often obscures key ideas within dense mathematical notation. For example, the statement: “Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point,” could be conveyed far more effectively with a concise sentence like, "DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials, resembling a first-order Taylor expansion." Such streamlining throughout would significantly improve comprehension.

2. **Section 4:** Section 4 feels extraneous given the paper’s length and scope. The material presented is largely a reiteration of concepts introduced earlier, and its inclusion does not meaningfully advance the core contribution. Its removal would tighten the paper and improve its focus.

3. **Section 5 – Lack of Illustration:** Section 5 presents interesting theoretical points, but lacks concrete illustration. Either a dedicated example demonstrating the discussed benefits, or a thorough summary of these points within the conclusion, is necessary to fully convey their significance to the reader.

4. **Section 6 – Insufficient Development:** Section 6 appears unduly condensed. The discussion feels incomplete, leaving the reader wanting a more detailed explanation akin to the style employed in Section 5. Expanding upon this section would substantially strengthen the paper.

**II. MINOR REMARKS**

1. **Typographical Error:** A potential typographical error exists in the description of the nonlinear toy problem; it appears to exhibit linear behavior. This should be carefully reviewed and corrected.

2. **Problem Selection:** The first two example problems are polynomial, which are naturally well-suited to kernel methods. While demonstrating KDMD's functionality is important, the paper would benefit from highlighting scenarios where the kernel's advantage is most pronounced – cases where the kernel is specifically chosen to capture relevant features, as suggested in the introduction. More detail regarding the kernel selection process and its impact on results is needed, building on the discussion hinted at in Section 5.



**Overall Assessment:**

The underlying idea presented in this manuscript is promising and has the potential to become a highly cited work in the field. However, in its current form, the paper is too difficult to navigate for a broad audience, including specialists. I recommend **rejection** for NIPS 2016, but with strong encouragement for the authors to revise and resubmit following a thorough focus on clarity and pedagogical presentation. Addressing the major remarks outlined above will be critical for unlocking the paper's significant potential.
