This paper extends Koopman spectral analysis to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), proposing a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition framework that operates on Krylov subspaces in feature space and performs eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. The method is evaluated on both simulated and real data. The manuscript is very well written and easy to follow.

Strengths:
- Clear and coherent presentation of Koopman spectral analysis within an RKHS, with an algorithmic procedure that mirrors DMD while leveraging nonlinear feature mappings via kernels.
- Empirical demonstrations on synthetic and real datasets that illustrate the feasibility of the approach.
- The framing of the method as both a modal decomposition and a feature extraction technique for downstream tasks is compelling.

Main suggestions for improvement:
1) Baseline comparisons: Please include a direct comparison to non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis in the simulations. A head-to-head evaluation against standard DMD/EDMD (or other common Koopman approximations without kernels) would make the benefits of the kernelized approach more concrete. For example, comparing estimated eigenvalues, modes, reconstruction error, and robustness across noise levels or sample sizes would help quantify the gains attributable to the RKHS formulation.

2) Kernel choice and sensitivity: The paper would benefit from a discussion and empirical exploration of how kernel selection affects outcomes. In particular:
- Effects of different kernel families (e.g., Gaussian/RBF vs. polynomial) on eigenvalue spectra, stability, and the number/quality of extracted modes.
- Sensitivity to kernel hyperparameters (e.g., RBF width, polynomial degree), including practical guidance on tuning strategies (median heuristic, cross-validation, or data-driven criteria).
- Robustness of the decomposition to ill-conditioning as a function of kernel and truncation thresholds, and how the POD-based “robustification” interacts with kernel choices.

These additions could be demonstrated within the existing simulation framework, and even a modest ablation would significantly strengthen the paper’s practical value.

Pros:
- Conceptually clean extension of Koopman spectral analysis to RKHS with a principled algorithmic pipeline.
- Well-motivated applications that highlight the utility of the extracted features (e.g., prediction, change detection, recognition).
- Strong writing quality and organization.

Cons:
- Lack of direct comparisons to non-kernel baselines leaves the empirical advantage of the method somewhat implicit.
- Limited guidance on selecting and tuning kernels, which is central to the method’s performance in practice.

Overall, this is a strong and well-presented contribution. Addressing the two points above—baseline comparisons and kernel-choice analysis—would improve the empirical rigor and practical guidance, making the paper more informative for both researchers and practitioners.
