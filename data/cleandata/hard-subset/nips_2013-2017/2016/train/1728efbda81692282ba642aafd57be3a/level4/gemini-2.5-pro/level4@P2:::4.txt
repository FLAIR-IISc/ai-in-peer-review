### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel method for performing Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The proposed approach is a principled extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a technique that approximates the Koopman operator's eigenvalues and eigenfunctions to understand system dynamics. While standard DMD relies on a linear combination of observables, this work leverages the "kernel trick" to implicitly use a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions. This allows for potentially more accurate approximations of the Koopman eigenfunctions for highly nonlinear systems. The authors present two algorithmic variants: one based on a direct Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space, and a more robust version that first projects the data onto kernel principal components (POD directions). The paper frames this decomposition as a feature extraction technique and demonstrates its utility on several machine learning tasks, including short-term prediction, sequential change-point detection, and recognition of dynamic patterns in motion capture data.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its elegant fusion of Koopman operator theory with kernel methods. This is a natural and powerful combination that extends the applicability of DMD-like spectral analysis to a broader class of nonlinear systems where linear observables are insufficient. The framing of the method as a feature extractor for dynamical systems is compelling and effectively broadens the potential impact of the work beyond the traditional fluid dynamics community, making it highly relevant to the NIPS audience.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The theoretical development is clear, building logically from the foundations of Koopman operators and DMD to the proposed kernelized extension (Theorems 1 and 2). The motivation for the robustified, POD-based algorithm as a solution to potential numerical instability is well-explained and practical.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical claims appear sound. The derivation of the robust algorithm via kernel PCA is a solid contribution that addresses practical numerical stability issues inherent in these methods, drawing a thoughtful parallel to similar developments in the standard DMD literature. The application examples, particularly for change-point detection and dynamics recognition, are creative and demonstrate the versatility of the extracted Koopman modes as features.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / Suggestions for improvement)**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Comparison:** A significant weakness is the absence of a direct empirical comparison to standard DMD or its variants (e.g., Extended DMD, which the authors acknowledge is similar to their robust version). The paper motivates the kernel-based approach by highlighting the limitations of linear observables, but it does not provide experimental results to demonstrate in which scenarios, and by how much, the proposed method outperforms these established baselines. For instance, on the Hénon map or a similar chaotic system, a quantitative comparison of prediction accuracy or eigenvalue estimation against standard DMD would be invaluable. Such a comparison is crucial to substantiate the claims of superiority and help readers understand the practical trade-offs of using the kernel framework.

2.  **Kernel Selection and Sensitivity:** The paper does not adequately address the critical issue of kernel selection and hyperparameter tuning. The experiments use a polynomial kernel for one task and a Gaussian kernel for others, with little justification for these choices or analysis of how sensitive the results are to them (e.g., the degree of the polynomial or the width of the Gaussian kernel). Since the choice of kernel fundamentally defines the feature space and thus the quality of the eigenfunction approximation, this is a major practical consideration for any user of the method. A discussion on kernel selection strategies or a sensitivity analysis in the experiments would greatly strengthen the paper and improve its reproducibility and practical utility.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality, well-written paper presenting a valuable and well-motivated extension to Koopman spectral analysis for the machine learning community. The theoretical contribution is solid and the demonstrated applications are interesting. The primary weaknesses lie in the experimental evaluation, which could be significantly strengthened by including comparisons to relevant baselines and providing an analysis of the sensitivity to kernel choice. Despite these shortcomings, the novelty and potential significance of the work make it a strong candidate for acceptance. The suggested improvements would elevate the paper from a good contribution to an excellent one.
