### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending the Koopman operator framework to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to perform Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) in the feature space induced by a kernel function, allowing for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions with a rich set of nonlinear basis functions. The proposed algorithm is based on calculating an orthogonal basis for the Krylov subspace in the RKHS and then performing an eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. A robust version of the algorithm is also proposed, which uses a projection onto kernel principal component analysis (PCA) directions to handle ill-conditioned problems. The authors frame this analysis as a feature extraction procedure and demonstrate its utility in several applications, including state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition, validating their approach on both synthetic and real-world data.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Approach:** The paper introduces a well-motivated and theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHS. By leveraging the kernel trick, the method can capture complex nonlinear dynamics without requiring pre-determined, explicit basis functions, which is a limitation of some existing DMD variants. The formulation is elegant, providing a clear connection between standard DMD, Krylov subspace methods, and kernel methods.

2.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The theoretical development, from the review of the Koopman operator and DMD to the introduction of the kernelized version and its robustification, is presented logically. The theorems provide a solid mathematical justification for the proposed algorithms.

3.  **Potential Significance:** The idea of using Koopman modes from an RKHS as features for downstream machine learning tasks is promising. If validated effectively, this approach could provide a powerful, data-driven tool for analyzing, comparing, and classifying complex dynamical systems in various fields.

### **Weaknesses**

While the core contribution is interesting, the paper could be significantly strengthened by addressing the following concerns regarding its originality, context, and experimental validation.

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with Closely Related Work:** The primary concern is the distinction from the "extended DMD" (eDMD) method proposed by Williams et al. [35]. eDMD already provides a framework for approximating the Koopman operator using a dictionary of user-defined basis functions. Since basis functions derived from kernels are a valid choice for the eDMD dictionary, the conceptual and practical differences between the proposed "kernel DMD" and eDMD with a kernel-based dictionary are not made clear. The current discussion of [35] is brief. A more detailed comparison, highlighting the specific novelties and advantages (e.g., computational, theoretical, or practical) of the proposed formulation over eDMD, is essential to properly situate the paper's contribution.

2.  **Incomplete Literature Review:** The field of nonlinear dynamical system analysis with kernels is extensive. The current literature review is missing several key references that have explored similar ideas. For instance, works by L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc on kernel-based regression for dynamical systems, as well as contributions from Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, or M. O. Franz and B. Schölkopf, would provide important context and help clarify the novelty of this work.

3.  **Limited Discussion of Stochastic Systems:** The paper primarily focuses on deterministic systems. While the authors briefly mention that the Koopman operator can be extended to stochastic settings, this point is underdeveloped. Given that most real-world systems are subject to noise, a more thorough discussion on the method's performance and theoretical properties in stochastic settings would greatly enhance the paper's relevance. A simple toy experiment demonstrating robustness to noise would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Ambiguity of Methodological Assumptions:** The remark on page 5 (line 216) states that the method can work with a collection of consecutive pairs rather than a full time sequence. This raises important questions that are not addressed:
    *   What are the implications of this assumption for multidimensional time series where pointwise relationships between consecutive states might be weak or complex?
    *   Could the authors elaborate on the inherent causality assumption in this setting and discuss how the proposed kernel construction helps in scenarios with weaker temporal dependencies?

5.  **Unconvincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments, while illustrating the applications, are not ambitious enough to convincingly demonstrate the superiority or unique advantages of the proposed method over existing techniques.
    *   **Prediction:** The prediction via preimage follows a standard, well-known approach in kernel methods. It serves as a proof of concept but does not establish a significant advance in forecasting.
    *   **Embedding and Recognition:** This section lacks a comparison to the vast literature on time series clustering and classification (e.g., methods based on kernel k-means, dynamic time warping, or other subspace kernel methods). Without experimental comparisons to established baselines, it is difficult to assess the practical benefits of using Koopman eigenfunctions as features.
    *   **Change-Point Detection:** The experiment compares against a kernel change detection method that is known to have limitations in complex dynamics. To be compelling, the evaluation should include comparisons against more modern, state-of-the-art online change detection algorithms with kernels.

### **Minor Comments**

*   The paper is well-written, but there are a few minor typos. For instance, please check for consistency in the indices (e.g., `τ-1` vs. `τ`).
*   In the last equation on page 7, the kernel notation `k(xj, xi)` seems to use a dot instead of a comma: `k(xj .xi)`. This should be corrected.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting and well-formulated method. However, its contribution is not clearly differentiated from prior work (particularly eDMD), and the experimental validation is not sufficient to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods for the presented applications. Addressing these points would be necessary to make a strong case for acceptance.
