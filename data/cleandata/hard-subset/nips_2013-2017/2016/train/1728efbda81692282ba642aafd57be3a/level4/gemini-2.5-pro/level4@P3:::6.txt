### Summary

This paper introduces a method for performing spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The core contribution is an algorithm that extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a technique popular in fluid dynamics, to a nonlinear setting using the kernel trick. The Koopman operator provides a way to analyze a nonlinear dynamical system using a linear operator that acts on observable functions. While standard DMD approximates this operator using linear combinations of observables, the proposed method leverages the expressive power of kernels to find Koopman eigenfunctions in a high-dimensional feature space, allowing for a richer, nonlinear representation of the dynamics. The authors present a theoretically-grounded algorithm based on a Gram-Schmidt process in the RKHS and a more practical, robust version that utilizes kernel Principal Component Analysis (PCA) for projection. The method is demonstrated on synthetic dynamical systems (a simple nonlinear system and the Hénon map) and applied to tasks such as prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition on real-world motion capture data.

### Strengths

1.  **Technical Quality and Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the theoretical derivations extending DMD to an RKHS framework are technically sound. The connection between the proposed algorithm, the Krylov subspace, and the Koopman operator is well-explained. The work has significant pedagogical value, as it provides a clear introduction to Koopman operator theory and DMD for a machine learning audience that may be unfamiliar with these concepts.

2.  **Novel Combination of Ideas:** The paper presents an interesting and natural synthesis of Koopman spectral analysis and kernel methods. While the "kernelization" of linear methods is a familiar theme in machine learning (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel ICA), its application to DMD for analyzing nonlinear dynamics is a valuable contribution.

3.  **Bridging Disciplines:** The work successfully bridges concepts from fluid dynamics and machine learning. Introducing powerful tools for dynamical systems analysis like the Koopman operator to the NIPS community is a commendable effort that could inspire new research directions.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significance and Motivation for Machine Learning:** The primary weakness is the limited justification for the method's relevance to mainstream machine learning problems. The Koopman framework is presented for deterministic dynamical systems. However, a vast body of work in machine learning on dynamical systems explicitly models stochasticity (e.g., Gaussian processes, state-space models, recurrent neural networks), which is often crucial for handling real-world data with process and measurement noise. The paper does not adequately address how this deterministic decomposition approach fits into or competes with the dominant stochastic paradigms. More work is needed to convince the reader of the practical utility of these techniques in a typical machine learning context.

2.  **Incremental Conceptual Novelty:** While the application is novel, the underlying idea of using the kernel trick to extend a linear method to a nonlinear one is a well-established pattern in machine learning. For readers familiar with the development of kernel PCA, kernel ICA, or kernel-based independence tests, the theoretical machinery and resulting algorithms, which involve eigendecompositions of kernel-related matrices, may not be surprising.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are not yet convincing enough to demonstrate a clear advantage of the proposed method.
    *   **Lack of Baselines:** The experiments on the synthetic data (toy system and Hénon map) are illustrative but lack a crucial baseline. Showing the performance of standard DMD on these same problems would have provided a direct and simple way to demonstrate the benefits of the kernel-based extension.
    *   **Unclear Real-World Impact:** The applications on real-world data are presented as proofs-of-concept, but their performance is not quantitatively compared to state-of-the-art methods in those domains. For example, in change-point detection, how does the proposed method fare against established kernel-based or Bayesian techniques? In dynamics recognition, how does it compare to other time-series classification methods? Without such comparisons, the practical significance of the extracted features remains unclear.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   It is not entirely clear from Section 3 whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires an explicit representation of the basis functions in the feature space. If this is the case, it would be a significant practical limitation compared to standard kernel methods like kernel PCA, which operate entirely through the Gram matrix. The authors should clarify this point.

*   The applications in Section 7 are interesting but feel disconnected from the state of the art in their respective machine learning subfields. For instance, what specific advantages would this approach offer to a researcher in robotics or time-series forecasting compared to existing, highly-developed models? A more focused application with a thorough, quantitative comparison to relevant baselines would significantly strengthen the paper's impact.

### Arguments For Acceptance

1.  The paper presents a technically sound and well-executed extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition using kernel methods.
2.  It is clearly written and serves as an excellent introduction to Koopman operator theory for the machine learning community.
3.  The proposed method offers a novel, principled approach for nonlinear feature extraction from dynamical systems.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

1.  The motivation and significance for the machine learning audience are underdeveloped, particularly regarding the common assumption of stochasticity in dynamical systems.
2.  The experimental evaluation is weak. It lacks direct comparisons to standard DMD and does not convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over existing state-of-the-art techniques in the proposed application areas.
3.  The conceptual novelty could be seen as incremental, following the established pattern of "kernelizing" linear algorithms.
