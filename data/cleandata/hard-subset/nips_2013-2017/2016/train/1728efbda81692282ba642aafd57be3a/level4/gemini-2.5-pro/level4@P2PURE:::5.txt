This manuscript extends the dynamical mode decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), proposing an algorithm termed Kernel DMD (KDMD) which is then demonstrated on several toy problems. This extension allows for the use of feature maps in the kernel definition, thereby linking the analysis to specific features of interest and establishing KDMD as a powerful generalization of DMD.

My primary concern is that the paper is very difficult to read. The authors frequently rely on dense mathematical formalism for concepts that could be explained more effectively with concise prose. Conversely, other sections are unnecessarily verbose. This imbalance makes the paper difficult to follow, even for specialists, and several key points require clarification. In its current state, the manuscript's presentation is too cryptic for publication at NIPS. Nevertheless, the underlying contribution is significant. I strongly encourage the authors to rewrite the paper with a focus on pedagogical clarity; a more accessible version of this work has the potential to be highly influential and widely cited.

**I. Major Remarks**

1.  The presentation is often verbose where it could be simplified. For instance, the sentence "Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions..." could be summarized in a much shorter and simpler statement.
2.  Section 4 seems redundant for a conference paper of this length, as its content is partially covered in the introduction.
3.  The discussion in Section 5 lacks concrete examples or illustrations. This section should either be visually illustrated or its key takeaways should be summarized in the conclusion.
4.  In contrast to other sections, Section 6 is overly condensed and would benefit from a more developed treatment.

**II. Minor Remarks**

1.  There appears to be a typo in the description of the non-linear (NL) toy example, as the system described is linear.
2.  The first two example problems are polynomial systems. These are cases where KDMD is expected to perform exceptionally well, particularly when an appropriate kernel can be selected (as suggested in Ref. [1]). This point should be elaborated upon with the level of detail seen in Section 5.

**References**

[1] Steven L. Brunton, Bingni W. Brunton, Joshua L. Proctor, J. Nathan Kutz, "Koopman invariant subspaces and finite linear representations of nonlinear dynamical systems for control," http://arxiv.org/abs/1510.03007v2, (2015)
