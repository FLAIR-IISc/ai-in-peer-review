Summary
This paper develops a spectral analysis of the Koopman (composition) operator within RKHSs and proposes a modal decomposition algorithm that leverages kernel methods. The authors also introduce a robustness-enhancing variant and provide numerical experiments to demonstrate the approach. Conceptually, the work can be viewed as a kernelized extension of dynamical mode decomposition (DMD), aiming to capture nonlinear dynamics via linear evolution in a lifted (feature) space.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and organization. The theoretical building blocks are introduced in a coherent way, and the writing is generally clear.
- Technically solid overall. The development appears mathematically careful, and the algorithmic pipeline is understandable.
- Practical relevance. Numerical experiments, while not exhaustive, indicate the method’s utility and are consistent with the paper’s claims.

Main concerns and requests for clarification
1) Section 3.1, Equation (7), and Theorem 1
- Relationship between Eq. (7) and Eq. (2): As presented, Eq. (7) looks essentially equivalent to Eq. (2), and it is unclear what new content Eq. (7) provides. Please clarify the purpose of introducing Eq. (7) and how it advances the derivation beyond Eq. (2).
- Domain/codomain issue: You denote KH as a linear operator from H to H, while φ is a map φ: M → H (the feature map). In Eq. (7), it appears KH is applied to φ itself. This seems inconsistent because φ, as a function on M, is not an element of H; rather, φ(x) ∈ H for each x ∈ M. If Eq. (7) intends to apply KH pointwise to φ(x), please state this explicitly and restate the equation in terms of elements of H (e.g., “for all x ∈ M, …”). As written, the notation suggests acting on the map φ rather than on elements φ(x).
- “Applying KH to both sides” (Line 149): The text indicates applying KH to both sides of an identity whose sides are scalar quantities. Since KH: H → H, it cannot be applied to scalars. If the intended operation is to act with KH on an H-valued identity prior to taking inner products, please rewrite the derivation to reflect the correct order of operations and the appropriate objects. Alternatively, if the operation is conjugated by evaluation or an inner product, please specify with which test function g ∈ H the inner product is taken and at which step the scalar equality is formed.
- Proof of Theorem 1 (Lines 144–157): The above notational ambiguities raise doubts about the validity of the current proof. Please provide a more explicit and rigorous argument that:
  - Clearly identifies the spaces in which each object lives (observables in H, feature maps, operators, and their domains).
  - States the equalities and operator actions pointwise in x or within H, as appropriate.
  - Avoids applying linear operators to scalars or to objects outside their domains.

2) Assumptions in Theorem 1
- Explicitness and strength: The assumptions in Theorem 1 are not stated with sufficient precision, and some additional assumptions appear only within the proof. Please:
  - List all assumptions explicitly before the theorem (e.g., properties of the underlying dynamical system, kernel and RKHS, compactness, boundedness, spectral properties, and any data-related conditions).
  - Justify their necessity and discuss whether they are standard in the literature (e.g., Koopman operator theory, kernel integral operators, covariance/Gram operators).
  - Avoid introducing additional assumptions implicitly in the proof; if they are needed, elevate them to the theorem statement.
  - Comment on how realistic or verifiable these assumptions are in practice (e.g., do typical kernels and regularization choices ensure them; are there counterexamples).

3) Relation to prior work and empirical comparisons
- While the experiments are informative, a stronger empirical comparison to closely related methods would clarify the incremental benefit of the proposed approach. In particular, comparisons against EDMD, kernel DMD (KDMD), extended/kernalized variants with regularization, and Hankel-based DMD approaches would help establish the practical advantages (accuracy, stability, robustness, and computational cost).
- If direct comparisons are infeasible for all benchmarks, consider at least one or two representative baselines with matched model capacity and hyperparameters.

4) Robustification details
- The robustifying method is promising, but please provide more detail on the formulation (e.g., the exact objective, regularizers, and how robustness is measured), hyperparameter selection, and sensitivity. Clarify whether robustness targets noise in states, outputs, or both, and discuss any trade-offs it introduces (bias vs. variance, spectral leakage, mode identifiability).

Suggestions to improve clarity and rigor
- Notation hygiene:
  - Be precise about when φ denotes the feature map φ: M → H and when φ(x) ∈ H is an element of H. Consider using different symbols (e.g., Φ for the map, φ(x) for elements) or explicitly quantifying “for all x ∈ M.”
  - When introducing KH, define it in operator-theoretic terms and state how it interacts with inner products and evaluation functionals. If KH is an empirical covariance or kernel integral operator, specify the construction.
  - Distinguish clearly between Koopman acting on observables Uf = f ∘ T and operators derived from data (e.g., empirical covariance operators, Gram matrices). If you deploy a representer-type argument, state the function class restriction explicitly.
- Proof structure:
  - Avoid steps that implicitly change the space (e.g., from H-valued equalities to scalar equalities) without indicating the test function or evaluation functional used.
  - If the proof relies on boundedness/compactness or spectral theorems for compact operators on Hilbert spaces, cite the specific results and verify their preconditions (e.g., self-adjointness or normality when used).
- Experimental reporting:
  - Include implementation details sufficient for reproducibility: kernel choice, feature dimensionality (if finite), regularization, numerical rank truncation, and computational complexity.

Minor comments and typos
- Line 55: “the the” → “the”
- Line 153: Use κj/λj instead of κi/λi if the surrounding text indexes with j.
- Ensure consistent indexing and notation across equations and proofs.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and potentially impactful contribution that extends Koopman spectral analysis via kernel methods with a practical modal decomposition algorithm. The paper is clearly written and technically promising. However, the notational inconsistencies and unclear operator manipulations in Section 3.1—particularly around Eq. (7) and the proof of Theorem 1—are substantive and hinder verification of the main theoretical claims. I encourage the authors to revise the presentation to resolve these issues, make all assumptions explicit, and, if possible, strengthen empirical comparisons. With these clarifications, the work would be significantly strengthened and more accessible to the community.
