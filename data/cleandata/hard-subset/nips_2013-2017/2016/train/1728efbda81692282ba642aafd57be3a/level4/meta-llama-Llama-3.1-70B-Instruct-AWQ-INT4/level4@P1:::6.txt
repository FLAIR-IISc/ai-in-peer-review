The paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs) and develops an algorithm to perform this analysis based on measurement data from a dynamical system. The Koopman operator, originating from fluid dynamics, is extended to RKHSs, providing a framework for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors demonstrate the algorithm and the analysis using two simple dynamical systems: a damped linear 1D dynamics and a chaotic map. They also apply their method to real-world locomotion data and compare the performance with standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA.

The paper derives an interesting extension of DMD, somewhat along the lines of older work on "kernelizing" techniques phrased in terms of linear operators (such as kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, GP bandits, etc.). The authors provide some experimental results on largely artificial problems. The paper is overall clearly written and has educational value for people foreign to concepts used in fluid dynamics.

The authors' work is supportive of introducing machine learners to techniques in different disciplines. However, to convince machine learning (ML) readers of the practical usefulness of these techniques, the authors need to do more work. Potential use cases and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods in relevant domains, such as robotics, would be beneficial. The authors should also clarify the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the need for explicit representation in feature space, which might be a limitation compared to kernel PCA.

Additionally, it would be helpful to demonstrate the performance of standard DMD on the toy example and the Henon map to highlight the differences in this simple setup. This would provide a clearer understanding of the advantages and limitations of the proposed method.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel extension of DMD to RKHSs, providing a framework for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems.
* The authors demonstrate the algorithm and the analysis using simple dynamical systems and real-world locomotion data.
* The paper is clearly written and has educational value for people foreign to concepts used in fluid dynamics.

Weaknesses:

* The authors need to do more work to convince ML readers of the practical usefulness of these techniques.
* Potential use cases and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods in relevant domains are lacking.
* The need for explicit representation in feature space might be a limitation compared to kernel PCA.
* The performance of standard DMD on the toy example and the Henon map is not demonstrated.

Originality:

* The paper presents a novel extension of DMD to RKHSs, which is an original contribution.
* The authors' work is supportive of introducing machine learners to techniques in different disciplines.

Significance:

* The paper has the potential to contribute to the development of new methods for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems.
* The authors' work could lead to new applications in domains such as robotics, where inference and learning in dynamical systems are important.

Quality:

* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
* The authors provide some experimental results on largely artificial problems.
* The theoretical derivations are well-executed, but not particularly surprising given the authors' background knowledge.

Clarity:

* The paper is clearly written, and the authors provide a good introduction to the concepts used in fluid dynamics.
* The notation is well-defined, and the equations are easy to follow.

Recommendation:

* The paper is a good fit for the NIPS conference, as it presents a novel extension of DMD to RKHSs and has the potential to contribute to the development of new methods for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems.
* However, the authors need to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly regarding the need for explicit representation in feature space and the lack of comparisons with state-of-the-art methods in relevant domains.
