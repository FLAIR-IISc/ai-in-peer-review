This paper introduces a spectral analysis method using the Koopman operator defined in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) to analyze nonlinear dynamical systems with empirical data. The authors follow the standard pathway to define a set of kernel approaches to perform this analysis. The eigendecomposition of the projection of the Koopman operator results in some efficiency gain in both synthetic and real data analysis problems. This paper introduces a significant kernel construction to analyze nonlinear dynamical systems.

The contribution is well motivated and experimentally validated. However, there are some concerns that the authors may want to address. Firstly, it is not entirely clear how the proposed method differs from the existing work by Williams et al. [35], where the Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes were already introduced to analyze nonlinear system dynamics. A more detailed discussion on the main differences between these two approaches would be beneficial. Additionally, the discussion in Section 4 could be improved.

Furthermore, the field of nonlinear dynamical system analysis with kernels is vast, and some relevant references are missing. The authors may want to consider including papers by L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, or M. O. Franz and B. B. Schölkopf in their literature review.

Although the Koopman operator is generally defined for a deterministic system, the authors briefly touch upon its performance in stochastic settings. However, a more in-depth discussion and a toy experiment would be welcome to demonstrate the applicability of the proposed method in realistic scenarios.

The authors mention in the remark (line 216, page 5) that the estimation procedure itself does not necessarily require a sequence but rather a collection of pairs of consecutive observables. The implications of this assumption in multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relations are not entirely clear. A discussion on what is gained with the proposed kernel construction in such cases would be beneficial. Additionally, the authors may want to elaborate on the causal assumption inherent in the setting.

Regarding the experimental settings, the current evaluation may not be ambitious enough. The prediction via pre-image follows a standard well-known approach, and the discussion on the connections between the proposed approach and the literature on clustering time series is lacking. Furthermore, the sequential change-point detection experiment would benefit from a comparison to state-of-the-art online change detection with kernels.

Minor issues include a few typos, such as incorrect indices on tau-1 vs. tau, and the replacement of the dot with a comma in k(xi,xj) (last equation on page 7).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel kernel construction for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems.
* The proposed method is well motivated and experimentally validated.
* The paper provides a good literature review, although some relevant references are missing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The difference between the proposed method and existing work by Williams et al. [35] is not entirely clear.
* The discussion in Section 4 could be improved.
* The experimental settings may not be ambitious enough, and more convincing experiments are expected to support the development.

**Quality:** The paper is well written and clear, with only a few minor typos.

**Clarity:** The paper is well organized, and the authors provide a good introduction to the topic.

**Originality:** The paper introduces a novel kernel construction for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems.

**Significance:** The proposed method has the potential to be applied to a wide range of nonlinear dynamical systems, and the experimental results demonstrate its efficiency gain in both synthetic and real data analysis problems.
