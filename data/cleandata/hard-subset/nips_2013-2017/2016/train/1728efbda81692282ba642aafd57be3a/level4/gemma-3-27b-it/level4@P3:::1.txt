This paper introduces a spectral analysis method leveraging the Koopman operator defined within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The authors present a framework for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems using empirical data, culminating in an eigendecomposition-based approach that demonstrates efficiency gains in both synthetic and real-world examples. The contribution is well-motivated and supported by experimental validation, offering a significant kernel-based approach to nonlinear dynamical systems analysis. However, several points warrant further clarification and refinement.

The primary concern revolves around the novelty of the proposed method relative to existing work, particularly Williams et al. [35]. While the paper clearly presents a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a more thorough discussion is needed to delineate the specific advantages and distinctions from prior approaches, such as extended DMD. A detailed comparison, perhaps highlighting differences in computational complexity, approximation accuracy, or applicability to specific system types, would significantly strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the discussion in Section 4 regarding robustification via POD could benefit from greater clarity. The rationale behind projecting data onto POD directions, and the theoretical implications of this step, should be more explicitly stated.

The field of nonlinear dynamical systems analysis with kernels is extensive, and the current literature review appears somewhat incomplete. Relevant works by researchers such as Ralaivola & d’Alche-Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., Mouattamid & Schaback, and Franz & Schölkopf should be considered and discussed. Incorporating these references would contextualize the contribution within the broader research landscape.

Although the Koopman operator is traditionally applied to deterministic systems, the applicability and performance of the proposed method in stochastic settings are crucial. While the authors briefly acknowledge this, a more detailed exploration, potentially with a toy experiment demonstrating performance on noisy data, would be highly valuable. This is especially important given the prevalence of stochasticity in real-world dynamical systems.

The authors state a key assumption regarding the pairwise relationship between consecutive observables (line 216, page 5), implying a pointwise nonlinear relationship. The implications of this assumption for multidimensional time series exhibiting weak pointwise correlations require further discussion. Specifically, what advantages does the proposed kernel construction offer in such scenarios? Additionally, the inherent causal assumption underlying the framework should be explicitly addressed.

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating the method’s functionality, lacks ambition. The prediction via preimage approach is relatively standard. The “Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics” section would be significantly improved by a discussion of the connection to existing time series clustering literature (e.g., kernel k-means and subspace kernel methods) and a comparative experimental analysis. Similarly, the sequential change-point detection experiment would benefit from comparison to state-of-the-art online change detection methods utilizing kernels. More compelling experiments are necessary to substantiate the claims of advancement.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel kernel-based approach to Koopman spectral analysis, extending existing DMD methods.
*   Demonstrates empirical validation on synthetic and real-world datasets, showcasing the method’s effectiveness.
*   The mathematical formulation is clear and well-presented, with a rigorous derivation of the core algorithm.
*   Potential for application in diverse fields, including system identification, control, and time series analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficiently clear differentiation from existing work, particularly Williams et al. [35].
*   Limited scope of the experimental evaluation, lacking comparison to relevant state-of-the-art methods.
*   Incomplete literature review, missing key references in the field of kernel-based dynamical systems analysis.
*   Limited discussion of the method's performance and applicability in stochastic settings.
*   Lack of detailed discussion regarding the implications of the assumed pointwise relationship between observables.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field of nonlinear dynamical systems analysis. However, several weaknesses, particularly regarding novelty, experimental validation, and literature review, need to be addressed before acceptance. I recommend a **weak reject** with a request for major revisions. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly a more thorough comparison to related work and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, would significantly improve the paper's quality and impact.
