This paper derives a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and develops a corresponding algorithm to perform this analysis from measurement data. The authors demonstrate their method on a damped linear system and a chaotic map, and apply it to real-world locomotion data, comparing its performance against standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA, with somewhat unclear results.

The paper is well-written and presents an interesting extension of DMD, following a line of research that "kernelizes" techniques based on linear operators (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel ICA). It also has educational value for machine learning researchers who may be unfamiliar with concepts like the Koopman operator from fluid dynamics. I am generally supportive of work that introduces the machine learning community to techniques from different disciplines.

However, the primary weakness of the paper is the lack of a compelling motivation for the practical utility of this technique within machine learning. The analysis focuses on deterministic dynamical systems, whereas many ML applications involve systems with stochastic components, such as innovations and noise. The authors need to do more to convince machine learning readers of the practical usefulness of their approach. What are the potential use cases? In what domains can this method compete with, or at least complement, the state of the art? For example, in a field like robotics, where learning and inference in dynamical systems are central, what specific advantages does Koopman analysis offer? Addressing these questions with a clear application would significantly strengthen the paper.

Furthermore, while the theoretical derivations are well-executed, they are not particularly surprising to a reader familiar with the kernel methods literature. The core idea—representing a linear operator in an RKHS, which ultimately leads to an eigendecomposition of a finite-dimensional matrix—is a well-established pattern seen in kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, and "kernel Bayes' rule."

A few specific points could also improve the paper:

*   It is not clear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires an explicit representation in the feature space. If it does, this is a significant limitation compared to methods like kernel PCA that rely solely on the kernel trick, and the authors should make this explicit.
*   To better demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method, the authors should show the performance of standard dynamic mode decomposition on the toy examples (the damped system and the Henon map). This would provide a direct and clear comparison in a simple, controlled setting.
