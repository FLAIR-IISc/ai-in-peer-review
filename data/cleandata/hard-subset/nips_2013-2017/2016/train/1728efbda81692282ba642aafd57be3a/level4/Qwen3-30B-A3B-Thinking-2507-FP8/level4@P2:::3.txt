

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors develop a modal decomposition algorithm that approximates the Koopman operator's spectral properties in RKHS, moving beyond traditional DMD's use of linear monomials as basis functions to leverage the expressive power of kernel methods. The approach involves computing orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator's projection onto this subspace. The paper further presents a robust implementation using POD bases to handle ill-conditioned matrices and demonstrates applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the growing body of work on Koopman operator theory and its applications. It builds upon previous NIPS work on Koopman spectral analysis (e.g., Williams et al. 2015, "A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator: Extending dynamic mode decomposition") and DMD variants (e.g., Chen et al. 2012, "Variants of dynamic mode decomposition"). The authors correctly identify that while traditional DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials (akin to a one-term Taylor expansion), their RKHS-based approach enables richer, nonlinear basis functions that can better capture complex dynamics.

Regarding the four NIPS evaluation criteria:

Quality: The theoretical framework is technically sound, with clear mathematical derivations for the proposed algorithm. The experimental results on synthetic and real-world datasets (including the Hénon map and locomotion data) demonstrate the method's effectiveness. However, I have significant concerns about the mathematical formulation in Section 3.1, particularly Equation (7) and the proof of Theorem 1. Equation (7) defines KHϕ as ϕ∘f(x), but KH is a linear operator from H to H, while ϕ is a function from M to H, making this definition inconsistent. The phrase "applying the operator KH to both sides" (Line 149) is problematic since KH acts on elements of H, not scalar values. The assumptions in Theorem 1 appear strong and not sufficiently clarified, with additional implicit assumptions appearing in the proof. These mathematical inconsistencies undermine confidence in the proposed method's theoretical foundation.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of complex concepts. The structure follows a logical progression from theoretical background to algorithm development, robustification, and applications. The figures effectively illustrate the method's performance, particularly the eigenvalue estimation results in Figure 1 and the dynamics recognition in Figure 3.

Originality: The extension of DMD using RKHS represents a meaningful contribution to the field. While previous work has explored kernel-based approaches for dynamical systems (e.g., Kawahara et al. 2010, "A kernel subspace method by stochastic realization"), this paper provides a novel spectral analysis framework specifically for Koopman operators using kernel methods. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from existing DMD variants, particularly extended DMD (Williams et al. 2015), by working directly in the functional space rather than using pre-determined basis functions.

Significance: The proposed method has clear practical applications in various domains where nonlinear dynamical systems are analyzed, including fluid dynamics, neuroscience, and robotics. The applications section (Section 7) demonstrates the method's utility in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The empirical results support the claim that the kernel-based approach provides more accurate spectral estimates than traditional DMD, particularly for complex nonlinear systems where local linear approximations fail.

Strengths: The paper is well-structured with a clear narrative, and the technical content is presented with exceptional clarity. The numerical experiments are thorough and well-chosen to demonstrate the method's advantages. The connection to existing DMD literature is well-maintained, and the authors appropriately acknowledge related work.

Weaknesses: The primary weakness is the mathematical inconsistency in Section 3.1, which casts doubt on the theoretical foundation of the proposed method. The proof of Theorem 1 requires significant clarification to address the operator application issue and the strong assumptions. While the experimental results are promising, the paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly those using kernel-based approaches for dynamical systems.

I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The mathematical concerns in Section 3.1 must be addressed before the paper can be accepted, as they undermine the theoretical contribution. The authors should provide a clearer definition of the Koopman operator in RKHS (Equation 7) and revise the proof of Theorem 1 to resolve the inconsistencies. Additionally, a more thorough comparison with related kernel-based dynamical system methods would strengthen the paper's position in the field.

The paper makes an interesting contribution to Koopman spectral analysis, but its technical soundness requires improvement before it can be considered for publication at NIPS. With careful revision of the theoretical foundations, this could become a valuable addition to the literature on dynamical systems and spectral analysis.
