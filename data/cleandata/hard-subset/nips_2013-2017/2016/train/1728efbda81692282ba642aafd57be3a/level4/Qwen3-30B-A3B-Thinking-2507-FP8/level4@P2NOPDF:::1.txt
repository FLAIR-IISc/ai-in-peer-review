

This paper proposes a kernel-based spectral analysis framework for nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. The approach leverages eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator's projection, yielding efficiency gains in synthetic and real-data experiments. The contribution is well-motivated and empirically validated, with a significant novel kernel construction for dynamical systems analysis. However, several concerns require clarification to strengthen the paper's novelty and impact.

First, the distinction between this work and Williams et al. (2015) [35]—which introduced Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for dynamical system analysis—is unclear. The authors must explicitly clarify the methodological and conceptual differences, particularly regarding how their kernel framework advances beyond the established DMD-based Koopman approximation. Section 4 requires substantial revision to contextualize this contribution within the existing literature.

Second, the literature review omits key references in kernel-based dynamical systems analysis. Relevant works by Ralaivola & d'Alché-Buc (2005), Tuia et al. (2012), Rojo-Alvarez et al. (2014), Mouatamid & Schaback (2010), and Franz & Schölkopf (2002) should be cited to position this work accurately within the field.

Third, while the Koopman operator is typically defined for deterministic systems, the paper offers only superficial discussion of stochastic settings—where real-world dynamics often reside. The authors should expand on performance in stochastic contexts, including a simple synthetic experiment to demonstrate robustness or limitations, as this is critical for practical applicability.

Fourth, the assumption in Line 216 (Page 5) that "the estimation procedure requires a collection of pairs of consecutive observables, where each pair is nonlinear pointwise related" warrants deeper analysis. For multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relationships, how does the proposed kernel construction specifically improve analysis over standard methods? The authors should clarify the theoretical and practical implications of this assumption. Additionally, the causal structure underlying the observable pairs (e.g., whether the kernel explicitly models temporal causality) needs explicit discussion.

Regarding experiments, the evaluation lacks sufficient ambition or novelty. The prediction task (Section 4.1) uses a standard preimage approach without meaningful comparison to established nonlinear time series methods. Under "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics," the authors omit critical connections to time series clustering literature (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernel methods), despite claiming novelty in dynamics recognition. A comparative analysis against these baselines is essential. Similarly, the sequential change-point detection experiment (Section 4.2) fails to benchmark against state-of-the-art online kernel-based change detection methods (e.g., those leveraging online kernel SVMs or recursive kernel embeddings), which are known to handle complex dynamics better than the method used here. More compelling experiments demonstrating clear advantages over existing approaches are required.

Minor points: The paper is well-written. Please correct two typos: (1) Ensure consistent indexing for τ-1 vs. τ in equations; (2) Replace the dot in k(x_i.x_j) with a comma (last equation, Page 7) to read k(x_i, x_j).

In summary, the kernel construction is promising but requires stronger justification of novelty relative to Williams et al. (2015), deeper engagement with key literature, expanded discussion of stochastic dynamics and methodological assumptions, and more rigorous experimental validation against relevant baselines. Addressing these points would significantly elevate the paper's contribution.
