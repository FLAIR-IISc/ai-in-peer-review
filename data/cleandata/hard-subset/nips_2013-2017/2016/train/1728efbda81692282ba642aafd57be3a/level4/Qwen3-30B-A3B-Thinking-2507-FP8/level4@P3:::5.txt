

The paper "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis" proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), resulting in a kernel-based DMD (KDMD) algorithm. The authors frame this as a generalization of conventional DMD, which uses linear monomials of observables as basis functions, by instead employing nonlinear kernel functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions. The method theoretically extends the Koopman spectral analysis to richer functional spaces, with the algorithm reduced to calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. The paper demonstrates applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**
1. **Originality:** The extension of DMD to RKHS represents a meaningful generalization that addresses a limitation of conventional DMD, which relies on linear approximations. This approach better captures nonlinear dynamics than standard DMD, as acknowledged by the authors' comparison to extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) and sparsity-promoting DMD (Jovanović et al., 2014).

2. **Significance:** The method provides a theoretically sound framework for spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems, with potential applications in various scientific domains. The authors convincingly demonstrate how extracted Koopman modes can serve as features for practical machine learning tasks (prediction, change detection, dynamics recognition), advancing the applicability of Koopman spectral analysis.

3. **Technical Quality:** The mathematical foundation is sound, with rigorous theoretical development of the algorithm and its connection to Koopman operator theory. The robustification via POD bases in Section 4 appropriately addresses numerical stability concerns, acknowledging similar approaches in conventional DMD literature (e.g., Kutz et al., 2016).

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Clarity:** The paper suffers from excessive technical verbosity and poor exposition, making it difficult to follow even for specialists. Key concepts like the connection between the RKHS formulation and standard DMD are buried in dense mathematical notation. For example, the explanation of Theorem 1 (Section 3) could be significantly simplified to clarify the core insight about the extension of DMD to RKHS.

2. **Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical validation is limited to toy examples (the toy nonlinear system and Hénon map) without sufficient comparison to state-of-the-art methods. The applications section (Section 7) lacks detailed quantitative comparisons to demonstrate the advantages of KDMD over existing approaches. The locomotion data example (Fig. 3) would benefit from clearer explanation of how the Koopman-based features outperform alternative methods.

3. **Novelty vs. Incrementalism:** While the RKHS extension is novel, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish this contribution from related work. The connection to extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015) is acknowledged but not thoroughly compared, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of the approach.

4. **Presentation:** The writing is unnecessarily complex in places, with verbose explanations of straightforward concepts (e.g., the description of the Koopman operator in Section 2 could be significantly condensed). The paper would benefit from more intuitive examples and clearer exposition of the algorithmic steps.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
- Provides a theoretically sound extension of DMD to RKHS, addressing a clear limitation of conventional approaches
- Demonstrates practical applications of the method in machine learning tasks
- The mathematical foundation is rigorous and well-developed
- Properly situates the work within the Koopman operator literature

**Con:**
- Poorly written, with excessive technical verbosity that obscures key insights
- Limited empirical validation with insufficient comparisons to baseline methods
- Fails to clearly articulate the advantages of KDMD over existing approaches
- Section 4 (robustification) appears redundant with material in the introduction
- Theoretical contribution is somewhat incremental compared to related work

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents an interesting theoretical extension of DMD that could be valuable for the community. However, the current presentation is too dense and poorly explained for NIPS publication. The authors should substantially revise the manuscript to improve clarity, streamline the presentation, and provide more comprehensive empirical validation. The core idea is promising and could be highly influential if properly communicated. I recommend a major revision to address the clarity issues and strengthen the empirical evaluation, rather than outright rejection.

The paper would benefit from a more pedagogical approach, with clearer explanations of the key insights, simplified mathematical notation where possible, and more detailed comparisons to relevant baselines. The authors should focus on making the contribution accessible to the broader NIPS audience, rather than emphasizing the mathematical details at the expense of clarity. With these improvements, the paper has significant potential to be a highly cited contribution to the field.
