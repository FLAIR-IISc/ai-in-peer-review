

This paper presents a compelling extension of Koopman spectral analysis to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), offering a theoretically grounded framework for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors demonstrate the method's efficacy through rigorous simulations and real-world applications, with clear exposition and strong empirical results. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, with logical flow, precise notation, and thorough technical justification—making it accessible to both theoretical and applied audiences in dynamical systems and machine learning.  

The core contribution—leveraging RKHS to overcome limitations of linear Koopman approximations—addresses a significant gap in the literature. The evaluation on synthetic data (including chaotic systems) and real-world datasets (e.g., fluid dynamics, climate data) convincingly illustrates the method's robustness and scalability. The choice of kernel-based representations is well-motivated, and the implementation details are sufficiently transparent for reproducibility.  

To further strengthen the work, the authors should address two minor points:  
1. **Comparison to non-kernel Koopman methods**: The simulations should explicitly compare the RKHS approach against established non-kernel techniques (e.g., Extended Dynamic Mode Decomposition, EDMD, or standard Koopman operator approximations using finite-dimensional bases). This would clarify the *relative* advantages of the kernel framework, particularly in capturing complex nonlinear dynamics where linear or low-dimensional approximations fail. For instance, a quantitative comparison of reconstruction error or prediction horizon across methods would solidify the paper’s claim that RKHS enables more accurate spectral decomposition.  
2. **Kernel selection and sensitivity analysis**: A deeper exploration of kernel choice—beyond the default Gaussian RBF used in experiments—is warranted. The paper should discuss how kernel hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidth in RBF, degree in polynomial kernels) influence the Koopman spectrum, stability, and interpretability. A brief empirical study varying kernels on a representative dataset (e.g., Lorenz system) would reveal whether the method is robust to this choice or requires careful tuning. Such analysis would guide practitioners in selecting kernels for specific applications and highlight potential pitfalls (e.g., overfitting with overly complex kernels).  

These additions would not only address a common concern in kernel-based methods but also elevate the paper’s practical utility. The current work already makes a valuable contribution, and these refinements would ensure it serves as a definitive reference for Koopman analysis in nonlinear systems. The writing quality and technical soundness are exemplary, and the proposed enhancements are feasible within the scope of the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to incorporate these points.
