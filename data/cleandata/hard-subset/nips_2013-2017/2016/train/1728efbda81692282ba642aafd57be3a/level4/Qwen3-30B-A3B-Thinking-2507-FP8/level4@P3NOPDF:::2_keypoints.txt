- Proposes a kernelized spectral method for Koopman operator analysis focused on the second iterate x(t+2)=f(f(x(t))).  
- Frames the Koopman operator as a linear representation of function composition and applies kernel/subspace methods for eigen-decomposition on trajectories.  
- Core technical contribution is essentially a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), an already well-established approach.  
- Focusing on the second iterate is unconventional, potentially confusing, and the paper fails to justify why it is necessary or advantageous over standard first-iterate analysis.  
- Does not clearly differentiate from or improve upon existing kernel-based Koopman methods and lacks theoretical guarantees for the composition-based decomposition.  
- The connection to kernel PCA is superficial; the method relies on standard kernel subspace techniques without novel theoretical insights.  
- Novelty claim is weak—applying kernel methods to composition is not convincingly a significant departure from prior work.  
- Experimental evaluation is insufficient: no comparisons to standard DMD or kernel DMD variants to show tangible benefits.  
- Fails to compare against state-of-the-art sequence models (e.g., RNNs, temporal CNNs), leaving performance claims unsubstantiated.  
- Missing ablation studies on kernel choice, trajectory length, and noise robustness, so key design decisions are unvalidated.  
- Dataset/context for recognition tasks are unclear; no evidence that chosen tasks are challenging for linear methods or how results compare to domain baselines.  
- Paper does not demonstrate unique value (e.g., handling high-dimensional or noisy data better than existing methods).  
- Writing is imprecise and conflates Koopman operator theory (observables evolution) with the DMD algorithm, risking reader confusion.  
- Recommendation: Reject — contribution is incremental, novelty insufficient, and evaluation fails to substantiate claimed advantages; needs stronger theory, clearer problem formulation, and rigorous baselines.
