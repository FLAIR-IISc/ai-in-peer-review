This paper proposes a spectral analysis framework for nonlinear dynamical systems by defining the Koopman operator in an appropriate reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). Building on this, the authors develop a family of kernel-based methods for data-driven analysis. The eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator yields computational efficiency gains, demonstrated on both synthetic and real datasets. Overall, the kernel construction is significant, well motivated, and empirically supported.

That said, I have several concerns and requests for clarification:

- Relation to prior work (Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, J. Nonlinear Science, 2015, [35]). It is not clear what the substantial differences are between this submission and [35], where Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes were already used to analyze nonlinear dynamics via extended dynamic mode decomposition. Please explicitly delineate the conceptual and algorithmic distinctions, the regimes where your approach meaningfully departs from or improves upon [35], and any implications for consistency, sample complexity, or computational scaling. An expanded and clearer discussion in Section 4 would be especially helpful.

- Missing context in the kernel literature for dynamical systems. The field is broad, and some relevant references appear to be missing. Please consider discussing related contributions by L. Ralaivola and F. d’Alché Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Álvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, and M. O. Franz and B. Schölkopf, and position your approach relative to these lines of work.

- Stochastic settings. While the Koopman operator is generally defined for deterministic systems, many realistic applications are stochastic. You briefly touch on this, but a deeper discussion of how your method behaves under stochastic dynamics (e.g., in terms of bias, variance, robustness, or identifiability) would be valuable. A small illustrative experiment would further strengthen the paper.

- Use of pairs of consecutive observables (line 216, page 5). You state that the estimation procedure need not use full sequences, but only pairs of consecutive observables that are nonlinearly pointwise related. Please clarify the implications of this assumption in multivariate time series where pointwise relations may be weak. What advantages does the proposed kernel construction provide in such settings (e.g., in terms of stability, expressivity, or sample efficiency)? Relatedly, please elaborate on the causal assumption implicit in the one-step mapping and how violations (e.g., latent confounding or delays) affect estimation and interpretation.

- Experimental scope and comparisons. The experiments are not very ambitious. Prediction via pre-imaging follows a standard approach. In the “Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics” subsection, there is little discussion of connections to (and no empirical comparison with) the extensive literature on time-series clustering, including kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods. For sequential change-point detection, it would be more compelling to compare against state-of-the-art online kernel change-detection methods; the baseline you use is known to struggle with complex dynamics. Stronger and more comprehensive experiments would better support the proposed framework.

Minor comments:
- The paper is clearly written. Please fix two minor typos: check indices for τ−1 vs. τ, and replace the dot with a comma in k(x_i, x_j) (last equation on p. 7).
