This paper proposes a spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator defined in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). Following this formulation, the authors develop a set of kernel-based methods for analyzing empirical data. The core of the approach involves the eigendecomposition of a projection of the Koopman operator, which is shown to yield efficiency gains on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The paper's main contribution is a significant kernel construction for this purpose, which is well-motivated and supported by experimental validation.

While the work is promising, I have several concerns that I would like the authors to address:

1.  **Novelty and Relation to Prior Work:** The relationship between the proposed method and the work of Williams et al. [35] is unclear. In [35], Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes were also used to analyze nonlinear system dynamics from data. A more detailed discussion clarifying the main differences and novel contributions of the present work is needed. This could be integrated into an improved discussion in Section 4.

2.  **Missing References:** The literature on kernel-based analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems is extensive, and several relevant works appear to be missing from the related work section. The authors may wish to consider and discuss the work of researchers such as L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, and M. O. Franz and B. B. Schölkopf, among others.

3.  **Applicability to Stochastic Systems:** The Koopman operator is typically defined for deterministic systems. While the authors briefly touch upon this, the paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the method's applicability and performance in stochastic settings, which represent many realistic scenarios. Including a toy experiment to demonstrate behavior in the presence of noise would be particularly valuable.

4.  **Assumptions on Data Structure:** On page 5 (line 216), the authors state that the estimation procedure requires only a collection of pairs of consecutive observables rather than a complete sequence. This raises two questions:
    *   What are the implications of this assumption for multidimensional time series where pointwise relations might be weak? Specifically, what is the benefit of the proposed kernel construction in such scenarios?
    *   Could the authors elaborate on the causal assumptions inherent in this setting?

5.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation could be made more compelling.
    *   The prediction task via pre-imaging appears to follow a standard, well-established approach.
    *   In the "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" section, the connection to the vast literature on time series clustering (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernel methods) is not discussed or experimentally compared against.
    *   For the sequential change-point detection experiment, a comparison against more state-of-the-art online kernel-based methods is needed. The baseline used is known to perform poorly on complex dynamics, which may not provide a challenging benchmark.
    Overall, more ambitious experiments against stronger baselines would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

**Minor Comments:**
*   The paper is generally very well-written and clear.
*   Please check the indices on τ-1 vs. τ.
*   In the last equation on page 7, k(xi.xj) should likely be k(xi, xj).
