This paper introduces a novel extension to Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) by applying it within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The resulting Kernel DMD (KDMD) aims to improve upon traditional DMD by enabling the use of richer, nonlinear basis functions for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions, potentially offering more accurate spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea – leveraging kernel methods to enhance DMD – is a promising generalization with potential for wider applicability.

However, the paper suffers significantly from issues of clarity and presentation. While the mathematical foundations are present, the writing is often overly dense and verbose, hindering understanding even for those familiar with the field. Conversely, certain sections feel rushed and lack sufficient detail, particularly regarding the practical implications and benefits of the proposed method.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The authors begin by providing background on Koopman operator theory and DMD. They then present their KDMD algorithm, which reformulates the problem in an RKHS, using kernel functions to define a feature map. This allows for a more flexible approximation of the Koopman operator's eigenfunctions. The approach involves calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition. The paper then proposes a robustification technique using POD to address potential ill-conditioning issues. Finally, the authors present empirical results on synthetic and real-world data, including applications to state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Extending DMD to RKHS is a valuable contribution, providing a potentially more powerful and flexible tool for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems. The use of kernel methods allows for the incorporation of richer features and potentially improved accuracy.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation appears rigorous and builds logically upon existing work in Koopman operator theory and DMD. The connection to established techniques like the Arnoldi method and POD is clearly articulated.
* **Potential Applicability:** The presented applications – state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition – demonstrate the potential utility of the method in various domains.
* **Robustification Technique:** Recognizing and addressing the potential for ill-conditioning with the POD projection is a practical and important consideration.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Readability:** This is the most significant weakness. The writing is often overly complex and mathematically dense, obscuring the core ideas. Simplifying the language and providing more intuitive explanations would greatly improve accessibility. The balance between mathematical rigor and pedagogical clarity is currently skewed too heavily towards the former.
* **Verbosity:** Some sections contain unnecessary detail and repetition. For example, the description of DMD in the introduction could be more concise.
* **Insufficient Detail in Applications:** The applications section (Section 7) feels underdeveloped. While the potential is shown, the results are presented too briefly, without sufficient discussion of the experimental setup, parameter choices, or comparative performance against other methods.
* **Section 4’s Value:** The addition of POD in Section 4 feels somewhat disconnected and adds complexity without a clear justification within the core paper narrative. It feels tacked on rather than integrated.
* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The examples primarily focus on relatively simple systems. Demonstrating the performance of KDMD on more challenging, high-dimensional datasets would strengthen the claims.
* **Missing Related Work Context:** While Section 5 touches upon related work, it lacks depth. A more thorough discussion of existing spectral methods, particularly those relevant to kernel-based approaches, would be beneficial. A citation to Brunton et al. (2015) on Koopman invariant subspaces would be valuable [1].

**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The core idea of extending DMD to RKHS is novel and theoretically sound.
* The paper presents a potentially valuable new tool for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems.
* The robustification technique addresses a practical challenge.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The poor clarity and readability significantly hinder understanding.
* The applications section lacks sufficient detail and analysis.
* The limited scope of the empirical evaluation weakens the claims.
* The paper feels incomplete and requires substantial revision.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution. However, its current state prevents a positive recommendation. The writing needs significant improvement to make the ideas accessible to a wider audience. The applications section requires expansion and more rigorous analysis. With substantial revision focusing on clarity, conciseness, and completeness, this paper could become a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, it is too cryptic for acceptance into NIPS. I encourage the authors to rewrite the paper with a focus on pedagogy and clear communication of their findings.
