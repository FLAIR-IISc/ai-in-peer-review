

This paper presents a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors propose a modal decomposition algorithm that operates in RKHS to extract Koopman eigenfunctions and eigenvalues from finite-length data sequences of nonlinear dynamical systems. The approach extends DMD by replacing linear monomial basis functions with kernel-induced nonlinear representations, enabling more expressive approximations of Koopman eigenfunctions. The paper also develops a robust variant using kernel principal component analysis (PCA) to address numerical instability issues common in DMD implementations.

The work relates to several strands of previous research. Koopman spectral analysis has been an active area in dynamical systems theory, with DMD emerging as a practical computational method for approximating Koopman eigenvalues and modes. The authors correctly position their work as extending DMD by incorporating kernel methods, which is conceptually similar to Williams et al.'s extended DMD but differs in its use of RKHS for more expressive basis functions. The paper also connects to kernel PCA and related spectral methods for dynamical systems analysis.

Strengths of the paper include its clear exposition of theoretical concepts, well-organized structure, and solid technical contributions. The mathematical derivations are mostly rigorous, and the connection between the proposed method and Koopman spectral theory is well-motivated. The empirical validation on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates the practical utility of the approach, with compelling applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The authors provide a clear implementation guide and make code publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

The paper excels in clarity. Each section builds logically on the previous one, with well-structured mathematical notation and effective use of figures to illustrate key concepts. The writing is precise and accessible to readers familiar with dynamical systems and kernel methods.

The originality is moderate but meaningful. While the kernel trick has been applied to other machine learning problems, its application to Koopman spectral analysis represents a novel combination of techniques. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from existing methods like extended DMD, particularly in how it handles nonlinear basis functions through RKHS. The authors adequately cite relevant prior work, including Williams et al. (2015) on extended DMD and Jovanović et al. (2014) on sparsity-promoting DMD.

The significance of the work is demonstrated through multiple applications that showcase the practical utility of the extracted features. The prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition tasks provide concrete evidence that the method can be used to solve real problems in nonlinear system analysis. The empirical results, particularly the accurate estimation of eigenvalues for both the toy system and Hénon map, suggest the method can provide valuable insights into complex dynamical behavior.

However, the paper contains several technical concerns that undermine its quality. The most serious issue is in Section 3.1, where Equation (7) defines the Koopman operator on the feature map as (KHφ)(x) = φ ∘ f(x). This formulation is problematic because φ is a map from the state space M to RKHS H (i.e., φ: M → H), making it impossible for φ to be an element of H on which KH would operate. The authors appear to be conflating the Koopman operator acting on functions with the operator acting on the feature map. The proof of Theorem 1 (lines 144-157) contains several logical gaps that stem from this fundamental confusion. For instance, the phrase "applying the operator KH to both sides" (line 149) is mathematically nonsensical since KH acts on functions in H, not on scalar values.

The assumptions in Theorem 1 are not clearly stated or justified. The proof requires that φj ∈ Φ, which means φj(x) = ⟨φ(x), κj⟩ for some κj ∈ H, but this is not established as a reasonable condition for general dynamical systems. The authors also introduce additional assumptions during the proof that are not mentioned in the theorem statement, raising questions about the generality of the results.

While the empirical results are promising, the paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors claim their approach is "a novel combination of familiar techniques," but they do not sufficiently demonstrate how it improves upon existing methods like extended DMD or sparsity-promoting DMD in terms of accuracy, computational efficiency, or applicability to different types of systems.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods with clear theoretical motivation. The method is well-explained, and the empirical applications demonstrate practical utility. The writing is clear and well-organized.

Con arguments against acceptance: The core theoretical foundation contains serious mathematical inconsistencies that undermine the validity of the main claims. The proof of the key theorem is flawed, and the assumptions are not adequately justified. The lack of comprehensive comparison with existing methods limits the paper's contribution to the field.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses an interesting problem with potentially useful applications, the fundamental theoretical issues in Section 3.1 represent a significant weakness that cannot be overlooked. The authors should clarify the relationship between the Koopman operator and the feature map, provide a rigorous proof of their main theorem, and demonstrate how their approach improves upon existing methods. Without addressing these concerns, the paper's contribution to the field remains questionable.
