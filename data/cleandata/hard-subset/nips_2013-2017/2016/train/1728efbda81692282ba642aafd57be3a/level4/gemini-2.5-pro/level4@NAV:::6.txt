This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), accompanied by an algorithm to perform this analysis on data from dynamical systems. The authors connect the Koopman operator, a concept originating in fluid dynamics, to potential applications in machine learning. The proposed algorithm and analysis are demonstrated on two simple dynamical systems—a 1D damped linear system and a chaotic map—as well as on real-world locomotion data. A performance comparison against standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA is provided, though the results of this comparison are somewhat ambiguous.

The work offers an interesting extension of dynamic mode decomposition, following a similar line of reasoning to established "kernelized" techniques that rephrase linear operator methods in RKHS, such as kernel PCA, kernel ICA, and kernel-based independence tests. The experimental validation is primarily conducted on synthetic problems. Nevertheless, the paper is well-written and serves an educational purpose for researchers in machine learning who may be unfamiliar with concepts from fluid dynamics. I am generally supportive of research that introduces the machine learning community to methodologies from other scientific fields.

The paper's focus is on deterministic dynamical systems, which can be viewed as functions mapping a space M to itself. This setup seems to diverge from many typical machine learning applications, where dynamical systems often incorporate stochastic elements like noise or random innovations. Consequently, the authors should make a more compelling case for the practical utility of their method to a machine learning audience. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing questions such as: What are the specific use cases where this technique would excel? How does it perform against state-of-the-art methods in those domains? For instance, in a field like robotics, where learning and inference in dynamical systems are crucial, what advantages does Koopman analysis offer? A detailed discussion focused on a specific application domain would be highly beneficial.

Furthermore, while the theoretical derivations are competently executed, they may not present a significant conceptual surprise to readers familiar with kernel methods. The general principle of representing a linear operator in an RKHS and subsequently deriving a tractable algorithm based on an eigendecomposition of a finite-dimensional matrix is a well-established pattern, seen in methods like kernel PCA, kernel ICA, and related follow-up work.

Regarding specific details, two points require attention. First, it is not entirely clear whether the modal decomposition algorithm necessitates an explicit representation of the data in the feature space. If it does, this would be a notable limitation compared to methods like kernel PCA, and this point should be explicitly clarified. Second, to better highlight the benefits of the proposed approach, it would be valuable for the authors to show the performance of standard dynamic mode decomposition on the toy example and the Henon map, thereby providing a direct baseline comparison in these simpler settings.
