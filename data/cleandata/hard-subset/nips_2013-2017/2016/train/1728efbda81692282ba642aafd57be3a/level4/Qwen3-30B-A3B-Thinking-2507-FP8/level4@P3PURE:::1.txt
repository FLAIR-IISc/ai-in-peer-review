

This paper introduces a spectral analysis framework based on the Koopman operator within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), followed by a standard kernel-based approach for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems using empirical data. The eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator yields efficiency gains in both synthetic and real-world data analysis. The proposed kernel construction represents a significant contribution to the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems, with well-motivated theoretical foundations and experimental validation. However, several concerns require clarification and addressing:

1.  **Fundamental Distinction from Williams et al. (2015):** It is unclear how this work fundamentally differs from Williams, Kevrekidis, and Rowley (2015) [35], where Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes were already introduced for nonlinear system dynamics analysis. The authors should provide a clear discussion highlighting the main distinctions and clarify the novelty of their approach relative to this foundational work. Additionally, Section 4 requires an improved discussion addressing these points.

2.  **Missing Relevant References:** The literature review overlooks several key contributions in kernel-based nonlinear dynamical systems analysis. The authors should incorporate and discuss relevant work by Ralaivola and d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., Mouattamid and Schaback, and Franz and Schölkopf.

3.  **Stochastic Systems Analysis:** While the Koopman operator is typically defined for deterministic systems, the authors should elaborate on the performance and applicability of their method in stochastic settings, which are ultimately the most relevant/realistic scenarios. The brief mention of this issue is insufficient; a more detailed discussion, and ideally a simple toy experiment, would be valuable.

4.  **Clarification of Key Assumptions:** The authors clarify the remark at line 216 (page 5) that "the estimation procedure itself does not necessarily require a sequence but rather a collection of pairs of consecutive observables, where each pair is supposed to be nonlinear pointwise related." However, the implications of this assumption for multidimensional time series with potentially weak pointwise relations remain unclear. The authors should discuss what specific advantages the proposed kernel construction provides under such conditions. Furthermore, the causal assumption inherent in the setting warrants explicit elaboration.

5.  **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental validation lacks ambition. The prediction via preimaging follows a standard, well-known approach. Under "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics," there is a notable absence of discussion and experimental comparison with the extensive literature on clustering time series (e.g., via kernel k-means or subspace kernel methods). Similarly, for the sequential change-point detection experiment, a comparison to state-of-the-art online change detection methods using kernels (e.g., methods known to outperform the one used by the authors in complex dynamics) would significantly strengthen the claims. More convincing experiments are required to support the proposed development.

*Minor:* The paper is well-written and clear. Please correct two minor typos: (i) verify the indices for $\tau-1$ vs. $\tau$; (ii) replace the dot with a comma in $k(x_i, x_j)$ (last equation on page 7).
