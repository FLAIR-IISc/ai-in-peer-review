Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), yielding a kernel DMD (KDMD) algorithm for Koopman spectral analysis. The core idea is to operate in feature space via a kernel, compute orthogonal bases of a Krylov matrix in RKHS, and then eigendecompose the projection of the Koopman operator onto this subspace. This provides a modal decomposition into a finite set of modes that can be treated as features of a nonlinear dynamical system. To improve numerical conditioning, the paper proposes a robust variant that projects onto kernel PCA (POD) directions. The authors also outline applications—short-term prediction via pre-image in feature space, sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-style statistic with a nonparametric exponential family, and dynamics recognition via kernel principal angles between subspaces—and illustrate results on a toy system and the Hénon map.

This line of work sits within Koopman operator methods for nonlinear dynamics and connects to the DMD literature in fluids and control (Schmid; Rowley et al.), as well as machine learning spectral methods for dynamical systems. Using kernels to enrich the observable space is a natural and powerful generalization of DMD, conceptually related to extended DMD, but here derived directly in RKHS with explicit use of Gram matrices and kernel PCA.

Strengths
- A timely and potentially impactful generalization of DMD that leverages the expressiveness of kernels to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions.
- Clear conceptual link between Krylov subspaces in feature space and Koopman eigenspectra; theorems articulate how Ritz values/vectors relate to Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions.
- Practical robustification via projection onto kernel PCA directions addresses known ill-conditioning in DMD-type methods.
- Interesting applications (prediction, change-point detection, recognition) that highlight the utility of Koopman-based features.
- If presented clearly, this work could be broadly useful in both ML and applied dynamics communities.

Weaknesses
- Clarity: The exposition is overly formal and verbose, making the paper difficult to follow even for experts. Many ideas that could be conveyed simply are buried in dense derivations. Key intuitions and algorithmic steps are not presented pedagogically.
- Structure: Some sections are redundant or misplaced for a short conference paper; others are compressed to the point of obscurity.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments are minimal and largely qualitative. There is no quantitative comparison to baselines (e.g., DMD variants, extended DMD), no robustness analyses (noise, sample size), and limited sensitivity to kernel choices.
- Notational issues and possible typos reduce confidence in the implementation details.

Detailed comments by review criteria

Quality
- The theoretical framing appears sound, and the RKHS formulation is natural. The robust variant based on kernel PCA/POD is standard and appropriate.
- However, the clarity of the proofs and algorithmic pipeline is insufficient for reproducibility. Provide concise pseudocode, state all assumptions explicitly (e.g., distinct Ritz values, conditions for ψ = 0), and clearly separate derivations from practical procedures.
- The experimental section does not convincingly demonstrate the advantages of KDMD or its robustness. Add quantitative error metrics for eigenvalues/eigenfunctions and prediction, and compare against exact/optimized/sparse/extended DMD.

Clarity
- The paper is hard to read due to excessive mathematical prose where short explanations would suffice, and conversely, some parts are too terse to understand.
- Example of verbosity: “Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point.” This could be succinctly stated as: “Standard DMD approximates eigenfunctions with linear observables; kernels provide richer, nonlinear bases.”
- Recommendations:
  - Introduce the algorithm at a high level first, then present the RKHS derivations.
  - Consolidate repeated explanations; define notation once.
  - Move technical proofs to an appendix and keep the main text focused on intuition and usage.
  - Provide a running toy example to anchor the mathematical steps.

Originality
- Extending DMD to RKHS is a meaningful generalization and aligns with current trends in data-driven Koopman analysis. While related in spirit to extended DMD, the RKHS-based derivation and robust formulation give this work a distinct angle.

Significance
- High potential impact if the method is made accessible and validated empirically. The applications section suggests broad utility beyond traditional DMD domains.

Major remarks
1) Simplify the exposition throughout. Replace long derivational passages with clear statements of results, intuition, and minimal equations. Provide concise pseudocode for both the basic and robust KDMD.

2) Section 4 feels unnecessary in its current length for a short paper and overlaps with ideas already foreshadowed in the introduction. Either condense it substantially or integrate its key points into the main algorithm section.

3) Section 5 (related work) should be better connected to the empirical sections—e.g., include illustrative comparisons or summarize key takeaways in the conclusion.

4) Section 6 is too condensed relative to Section 5. Expand experiments with quantitative metrics and baselines. Show cases where kernels make a tangible difference (e.g., polynomial vs. RBF kernels on systems with known polynomial nonlinearities).

Minor remarks
1) There is likely a typo in the toy nonlinear system description: as written, it appears linear. Please check and correct.

2) The first two problems are essentially polynomial; this is where KDMD should excel when the kernel matches the system structure (e.g., polynomial kernels). Provide more detail, as you do in Section 5, on how kernel choice impacts performance.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually strong and potentially influential extension of DMD to RKHS.
- Theoretical connections to Koopman spectra are carefully argued.
- Broad and relevant application domains.

Cons for acceptance
- Current presentation is too cryptic and verbose for NIPS proceedings.
- Insufficient empirical validation and lack of comparisons to strong baselines.
- Structural imbalance across sections and some notation/typo issues.

Recommendation
Reject in its current form due to clarity and evaluation shortcomings. I strongly encourage a thorough rewrite with a pedagogical focus and expanded experiments; the resulting paper could be highly impactful and well-cited.

Reference
- Steven L. Brunton, Bingni W. Brunton, Joshua L. Proctor, J. Nathan Kutz, “Koopman invariant subspaces and finite linear representations of nonlinear dynamical systems for control,” arXiv:1510.03007v2 (2015).
