This paper introduces a spectral analysis method for the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs), and develops a corresponding modal decomposition algorithm. The authors also address robustness concerns and demonstrate the method’s utility through numerical experiments. The core contribution extends dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) by leveraging the kernel trick.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:** The paper builds upon the established framework of Koopman operator theory [16] and DMD [25, 26] for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems. Koopman operator theory provides a way to linearize nonlinear dynamics when acting on observable functions. DMD approximates the spectra of this operator, revealing dynamic modes. This work extends DMD by operating within an RKHS, allowing for the use of richer, potentially nonlinear basis functions via kernel methods. This addresses a limitation of traditional DMD, which relies on linear monomials. The authors appropriately situate their work within the broader context of spectral methods [15, 31, 17], proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) [12], and related approaches for system identification [23, 14]. They also connect their method to kernel PCA [28] in their robustness enhancement (Section 4).

**Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors present the theoretical concepts thoroughly and logically, making the method accessible. The technical development appears sound. While a detailed comparative analysis with state-of-the-art methods is not extensive, the included numerical experiments effectively demonstrate the method’s capabilities. The connections made to existing literature are appropriate and demonstrate awareness of the field. The inclusion of applications beyond simply model estimation (prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) is a positive aspect.

**Weaknesses:** A central concern lies with the theoretical foundation presented in Section 3, specifically Equation (7) and the subsequent proof of Theorem 1. The purpose of Equation (7) is not immediately clear, given its similarity to Equation (2). More critically, the application of the linear operator *KH* to scalar values on Line 149 within the proof is questionable, as *KH* maps from *H* to *H*, and the right-hand side appears to be scalar. The assumptions underlying Theorem 1 are strong and lack sufficient explanation; clarifying their necessity and potential limitations is crucial. Additional, unstated assumptions appear during the proof itself, raising questions about their validity. Further clarification on the role of the feature map *ϕ* and its relationship to the RKHS is needed. The connection between the theoretical framework and practical implementation could be strengthened.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper demonstrates technical soundness, but the clarity of the theoretical derivation needs improvement, particularly regarding the application of the Koopman operator in the proof of Theorem 1. While the mathematical framework is present, the assumptions and logical flow could benefit from more rigorous justification. The work appears complete within its stated scope.
*   **Clarity:** The writing is generally excellent. However, as noted above, the explanations surrounding Equation (7) and the proof of Theorem 1 are unclear and require substantial revision. The organization is logical and aids understanding.
*   **Originality:** The extension of DMD to RKHSs represents a novel contribution. The paper clearly articulates how this differs from existing work, building upon established techniques but introducing a new approach to approximating Koopman eigenfunctions.
*   **Significance:** The method has the potential to be significant for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems where linear approximations are inadequate. The applications presented (prediction, change detection, and pattern recognition) suggest practical relevance. The ability to extract meaningful features from nonlinear data is valuable, and the combination of Koopman analysis with kernel methods is a promising direction.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel extension of DMD with potential for improved accuracy in nonlinear systems.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Solid theoretical foundation (with improvements needed, see cons).
*   Demonstrated through numerical experiments.
*   Potential for broad applicability.

**Con:**

*   Unclear justification of key theoretical steps (Equation 7 and the proof of Theorem 1).
*   Strong and poorly explained assumptions in Theorem 1.
*   Limited comparative analysis with existing methods.
*   Potential for numerical instability, even with the robustness enhancement.

**Recommendations:**

I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The authors should address the concerns raised regarding the theoretical clarity, specifically providing a more rigorous justification for Equation (7) and the proof of Theorem 1. A more detailed explanation of the assumptions underlying Theorem 1 is crucial. Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. Additionally, a more thorough comparative analysis with related techniques would be beneficial.
