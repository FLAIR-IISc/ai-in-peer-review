

This paper introduces a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), extending the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) framework for nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors propose an algorithm that performs modal decomposition in RKHS by computing orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix and conducting eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projection. The method yields a finite-mode decomposition of dynamics, functioning as a feature extraction procedure for nonlinear systems, with applications demonstrated in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The work builds on established Koopman operator theory (Koopman, 1931; Mezić, 2005) and DMD (Schmid, 2010), extending them to RKHS to enable nonlinear basis functions. The authors connect their approach to kernel methods (e.g., kernel PCA), noting that while standard DMD uses linear monomial basis functions (analogous to local Taylor expansions), their method leverages the expressive power of kernel functions to capture richer dynamics. This represents a meaningful extension of DMD that aligns with recent trends in kernelizing linear methods (e.g., kernel ICA, kernel independence tests).

The paper demonstrates the method on two synthetic dynamical systems (a damped linear 1D system and the Hénon map) and real-world locomotion data, comparing results with standard DMD, PCA, and kernel PCA. While the theoretical development is sound and well-executed, the experimental section lacks sufficient comparison to establish clear advantages over existing methods in machine learning contexts.

Regarding quality, the mathematical derivations are technically sound, with the authors addressing potential numerical instability through a POD-based robustification procedure. The algorithm is clearly described, though the need for explicit feature space representation (rather than working entirely in kernel space) is not sufficiently justified. The authors should clarify whether this is a necessary limitation compared to standard kernel methods.

Clarity is excellent—the paper is well-organized, with a logical flow from theoretical foundations to algorithmic implementation and applications. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the figures effectively illustrate key concepts (e.g., eigenvalue estimation in Figure 1).

Originality is moderate. While the extension of DMD to RKHS is non-trivial, the approach follows a pattern common in kernel methods (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel ICA), and the connection to existing work is well-documented. The paper would benefit from a more precise statement of how this differs from Williams et al.'s extended DMD (2015), which similarly operates on pre-determined basis functions.

Significance is the paper's main weakness. The authors fail to convincingly demonstrate why machine learning practitioners should care about this method. While the paper is clearly written and educational for those unfamiliar with Koopman theory (particularly from fluid dynamics), it lacks compelling evidence of practical utility in machine learning contexts. The locomotion data example shows promise but doesn't clearly establish advantages over simpler methods. The authors should address specific ML applications where this approach offers demonstrable benefits over existing techniques.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Explicit comparison of standard DMD and their method on the toy examples (Figure 1), to illustrate the advantage of kernel-based basis functions
2. Clearer articulation of potential ML applications (e.g., robotics, where dynamical system modeling is crucial), with concrete examples of how Koopman analysis with RKHS would outperform current approaches
3. Discussion of the trade-offs between explicit feature space representation and kernel trick implementation

The authors' claim that their method "could be considered as a feature extraction procedure for a nonlinear dynamical system" is accurate but underdeveloped. To demonstrate significance, they need to show how these extracted features improve performance on real ML tasks compared to standard techniques.

In conclusion, this is a technically sound extension of DMD that makes an important connection between kernel methods and Koopman spectral analysis. While the mathematical development is solid and the paper is clearly written, the lack of compelling evidence for practical significance in machine learning contexts limits its impact. The work would be substantially stronger if the authors could more clearly articulate how their method advances the state of the art for real ML applications, rather than just presenting a mathematically interesting extension. I recommend acceptance pending a more detailed justification of the method's practical utility in machine learning domains.
