

The paper presents a novel spectral analysis method for the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to incorporate kernel-based nonlinear representations. While Koopman operator analysis has been studied in fluid dynamics and applied to dynamical systems, the authors extend this framework to RKHS, providing a modal decomposition algorithm that uses kernel functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with richer basis functions than traditional DMD, which typically relies on linear monomials of observables. This approach offers a natural generalization of DMD by leveraging the expressiveness of kernel functions to capture nonlinear dynamics more effectively.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The mathematical derivations are clear and logically presented, with appropriate connections to existing methods like kernel PCA and standard DMD. The authors provide a detailed algorithm for their approach, including a robustification step using POD bases to address numerical instability in the direct implementation. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the connection between the proposed method and the Koopman operator framework is well-established. The paper also includes a detailed explanation of the computational implementation, making it accessible for practitioners.

However, the paper's significance for the machine learning community remains unclear. While the authors acknowledge the fluid dynamics origins of the Koopman operator, they do not sufficiently establish why this technique would be valuable for ML practitioners. The applications presented—state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition—are somewhat generic, and the comparison with standard DMD, PCA, and kernel PCA on the synthetic and locomotion data does not convincingly demonstrate superior performance or unique advantages. For instance, the results in Figure 3 for locomotion data show clustering by motion type, but it's unclear how this compares to other dimensionality reduction techniques commonly used in this domain.

The novelty claim is somewhat overstated. The approach essentially applies kernel methods to the Koopman operator framework, which is a natural extension given the established literature on kernelizing linear operators (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests). The theoretical derivations, while technically sound, do not offer substantial new insights beyond what would be expected from applying standard kernel techniques to this domain. The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison with related kernel-based methods in machine learning to clarify the unique contribution.

A critical limitation concerns the requirement for explicit feature space representations. Unlike kernel PCA, which operates entirely in the input space using the kernel trick, the proposed method appears to require explicit feature space calculations (as noted in the paper's derivation of the Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in RKHS). This is a significant practical limitation that should be more clearly acknowledged, as it would make the method computationally more expensive than standard kernel methods. The authors should clarify this point and discuss the computational trade-offs of their approach.

The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison with standard DMD on the toy examples and Hénon map to demonstrate the specific advantages of the kernel-based approach in these simple settings. The current results on the Hénon map (Figure 1) show reasonable eigenvalue estimation, but without a direct comparison to standard DMD, it's difficult to assess whether the kernel method provides meaningful improvements. The authors should include such comparisons to better establish the value of their approach.

The paper's significance for machine learning is its weakest aspect. The authors suggest potential applications in robotics and other domains where dynamical system modeling is important, but they do not provide specific use cases or demonstrate how their method would outperform existing approaches in these settings. To strengthen their case, the authors should identify a concrete ML application area where their method offers demonstrable advantages over current state-of-the-art techniques for dynamical system analysis.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods, with clear theoretical foundations and well-executed mathematical derivations. However, the significance for machine learning is not sufficiently demonstrated, the novelty is somewhat overstated given existing kernel-based techniques, and the practical limitations of the approach (particularly regarding explicit feature space representations) are not clearly articulated. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons with standard methods, clearer articulation of practical advantages for ML applications, and a more focused discussion of the specific contexts where this approach would provide meaningful improvements over existing techniques.
