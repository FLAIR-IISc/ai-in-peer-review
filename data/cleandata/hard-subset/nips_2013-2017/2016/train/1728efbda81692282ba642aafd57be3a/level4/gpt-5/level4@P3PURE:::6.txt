This paper derives a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes an algorithm that performs this analysis from measurement data of a dynamical system. The Koopman operator, rooted in fluid dynamics, is presented here with implied relevance to machine learning. The authors demonstrate their approach on two simple systems—a damped 1D linear system and a chaotic map—and additionally apply it to real-world locomotion data. They compare with standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA, though the empirical conclusions are somewhat unclear.

Strengths
- The paper presents an interesting extension of dynamic mode decomposition into an RKHS framework, in the spirit of prior work that “kernelizes” linear-operator-based techniques (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, GP bandits, kernel Bayes rule).
- The theoretical development is careful and well executed.
- The writing is generally clear and the paper has educational value, particularly for machine learning readers unfamiliar with concepts from fluid dynamics.

Concerns and clarity
- Practical relevance to machine learning is not convincingly established. The work focuses on deterministic dynamical systems defined by maps from M to M without stochastic components, whereas many ML applications (e.g., time-series modeling) involve random effects such as innovations and observation noise. I may be missing relevant precedents, but I am not aware of such deterministic setups being common in ML practice.
- The empirical results, especially on the locomotion data, are difficult to interpret; it is not clear what is gained relative to existing methods.
- While the derivations are solid, they are not especially surprising for readers familiar with kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, or kernel Bayes rule: once the problem is cast as a linear operator acting on functions in an RKHS, finite-dimensional eigendecompositions naturally arise.

Requests for clarification
- In the modal decomposition algorithm, do you require explicit representations in feature space, or can everything be expressed via kernels? If explicit feature maps are needed, this would be a substantive limitation compared to kernel PCA and should be stated clearly.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper
- Provide concrete use cases in machine learning where this methodology brings clear advantages. For example, in robotics—an area with substantial work on inference and learning in dynamical systems—what would Koopman-based analysis enable that current state-of-the-art methods do not? If robotics is not the right target, please identify another domain where the proposed approach is competitive and useful, and discuss whether you can match or approximate state-of-the-art performance there.
- On the toy examples and the Henon map, include results from standard DMD to make the differences explicit in a simple, controlled setting.
- Clarify the empirical takeaways on the locomotion dataset: what specific benefits or insights does the RKHS Koopman analysis provide over DMD, PCA, and kernel PCA?

Overall, this is a clearly written and thoughtful contribution that extends DMD into an RKHS framework and may help bridge ideas from fluid dynamics to machine learning. However, to convince an ML audience of its practical utility, the paper needs crisper empirical evidence, clearer baselines (including DMD on the simple benchmarks), explicit discussion of computational limitations (e.g., any need for explicit feature maps), and a stronger articulation of compelling ML use cases.
