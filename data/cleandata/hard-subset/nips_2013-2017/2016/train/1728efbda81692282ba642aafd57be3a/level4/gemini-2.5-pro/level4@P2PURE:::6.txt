This paper derives a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes an algorithm to perform this analysis on data from a dynamical system. The Koopman operator, a concept originating from fluid dynamics, is suggested to have relevance for machine learning. The authors demonstrate their algorithm on two simple systems—a one-dimensional damped linear system and a chaotic map—and apply it to real-world locomotion data. A performance comparison with standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA yields somewhat unclear results.

The work presents an interesting extension of dynamic mode decomposition, conceptually similar to prior work on "kernelizing" techniques for linear operators (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel-based independence tests). The experimental results are primarily demonstrated on artificial problems. The paper is clearly written and holds educational value for machine learning researchers unfamiliar with concepts from fluid dynamics; I am generally supportive of work that introduces the machine learning community to techniques from different disciplines.

A key point of consideration is the paper's focus on deterministic dynamical systems, which are constructed from functions mapping a space M to itself. This contrasts with typical machine learning applications where dynamical systems often incorporate stochastic components like innovations and noise. While I may be overlooking certain applications, the authors need to do more to convince a machine learning audience of the practical usefulness of these techniques. What are the potential use cases? Can this method compete with the state-of-the-art in those domains? For instance, in robotics—a field where learning and inference in dynamical systems are critical—what specific benefits would Koopman analysis provide? A clear articulation of its value in a specific ML domain would significantly strengthen the paper.

Furthermore, while the theoretical derivations are well-executed, they may not be particularly surprising to those familiar with kernel methods such as kernel PCA, kernel ICA, or kernel Bayes' rule. The general principle of representing a linear operator in an RKHS, which ultimately reduces the problem to an eigendecomposition of a finite-dimensional matrix, is a well-established pattern.

A few specific details require clarification:
*   It is not clear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires an explicit representation of data in the feature space. If so, this is a notable limitation compared to methods like kernel PCA, and the authors should make this explicit.
*   To better demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method, the authors should show the performance of standard dynamic mode decomposition on the toy example and the Hénon map. This would provide a direct and valuable baseline for comparison in these simple, controlled setups.
