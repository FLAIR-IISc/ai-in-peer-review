This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs) and develops a corresponding modal decomposition algorithm for nonlinear dynamical systems. The Koopman operator, originating in fluid dynamics, offers a way to represent nonlinear dynamics as a linear operator acting on observable functions. The authors extend dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) by utilizing RKHSs, enabling the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions. The method is demonstrated on a damped linear system, the Henon map, and locomotion data. 

The paper builds on the established field of spectral analysis for dynamical systems, including proper orthogonal decomposition (POD), DMD, and related techniques. It connects these approaches to kernel methods, reminiscent of kernel PCA, ICA, and related developments in machine learning. The core contribution lies in extending DMD to leverage the expressiveness of kernel functions, potentially allowing for more accurate approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions than methods relying on linear monomials. The paper is well-written and provides a valuable introduction to Koopman spectral analysis for readers unfamiliar with its roots in fluid dynamics. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Extension:** The work proposes a natural and logical extension of DMD by incorporating reproducing kernels, offering a potentially more powerful approach for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions in certain scenarios.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is clearly structured and well-written, making the concepts accessible to a broad audience. The mathematical derivations are presented with sufficient detail.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors effectively relate their work to existing literature in both dynamical systems and machine learning, providing context and highlighting the contributions of their approach.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical framework appears solid, and the authors provide a theorem establishing the validity of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, while demonstrating the algorithm’s functionality, are somewhat limited in scope. The comparison to standard DMD, especially on the toy examples, is missing and would provide important insight.
*   **Practical Relevance to ML:** The paper lacks a compelling argument for the practical applicability of this approach within mainstream machine learning. It’s unclear how this technique offers advantages over existing methods in common ML tasks.
*   **Computational Cost:** The potential computational cost of operating in the RKHS, particularly the need for explicit feature map representation, is not thoroughly discussed.
*   **Lack of Comparison:** The method's performance is compared with other related methods only on locomotion data, not on the synthetic examples.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of a known technique (DMD) with potential benefits.
*   The work bridges a gap between dynamical systems analysis and kernel methods in machine learning.
*   The presentation is clear, and the paper is well-written.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical validation is limited and doesn't strongly demonstrate the practical advantages of the method.
*   The paper lacks a convincing demonstration of applicability to a relevant machine learning problem.
*   The computational considerations related to operating in RKHS are not sufficiently addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution with potential for interesting applications. However, the lack of strong empirical validation and a clear articulation of its relevance to the machine learning community raise concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors: 1) Including a direct comparison with standard DMD on the toy examples, 2) providing a more compelling discussion of potential ML applications, and 3) addressing the computational implications of working in RKHS. A stronger focus on demonstrating practical advantages beyond the theoretical extension would significantly strengthen the paper.
