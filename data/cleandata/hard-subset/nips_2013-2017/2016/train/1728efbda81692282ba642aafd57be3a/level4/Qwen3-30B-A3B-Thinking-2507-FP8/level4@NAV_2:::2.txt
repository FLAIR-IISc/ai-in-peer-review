

The paper introduces a kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), building on established DMD methods [25, 26] and recent kernelized approaches like Williams et al.'s extended DMD [35] presented at NIPS 2015. It proposes a modal decomposition algorithm that computes orthogonal Krylov bases in RKHS and projects the Koopman operator onto this subspace, yielding a finite-mode decomposition of nonlinear dynamics. The authors demonstrate applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using synthetic and real-world data, with empirical validation on toy systems, the Hénon map, and locomotion data.

Strengths include strong theoretical foundations (Theorems 1–2), clear mathematical exposition of the RKHS extension, and a robust implementation via POD-based projection (Section 4) to address numerical instability—a significant improvement over standard DMD. The clarity of the algorithmic description and empirical examples (Figures 1–4) is commendable, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The method aligns with recent NIPS work on Koopman analysis [20, 35] and extends the theoretical framework for nonlinear dynamics.

Weaknesses center on limited novelty relative to the NIPS scope. While the kernelized approach provides richer basis functions than linear DMD, it fundamentally generalizes Williams et al.'s extended DMD [35], which also uses pre-specified basis functions (e.g., monomials) and was presented at NIPS 2015. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution from existing kernel-based spectral methods [13, 35] or justify why kernelization is essential over standard extended DMD. The applications (Section 7) lack compelling comparative evidence: no quantitative comparison to RNN-based dynamics recognition [e.g., 18] or extended DMD in prediction/change detection, and the locomotion recognition (Figure 3) uses a standard kernel principal angle distance without demonstrating superiority over simpler methods like PCA. The significance is thus weakened, as the method appears incremental for the NIPS audience.

Pro:  
- Rigorous theoretical development with clear connections to Koopman theory and DMD.  
- Practical robustification via POD (Section 4) addresses a known limitation of DMD.  
- Well-structured empirical validation on diverse datasets.  

Con:  
- Novelty is marginal; kernelization of DMD is a natural extension rather than a significant advancement.  
- Applications do not convincingly demonstrate superiority over existing methods (e.g., extended DMD [35] or RNNs).  
- Insufficient comparison to recent NIPS-relevant work (e.g., Williams et al. [35] and Jovanović et al. [13] are cited but not contrasted).  

The paper is technically sound (Quality) and clearly written (Clarity), but its Originality and Significance are limited for NIPS. It would benefit from stronger comparative experiments against extended DMD and RNN baselines, and a clearer articulation of why kernelization is indispensable for the claimed applications. While a solid methodological contribution, it does not advance the state of the art sufficiently to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
