This paper presents a framework for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator defined within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This formulation enables the development of several kernel-based methods for analyzing empirical time-series data. The core of the proposed approach is the eigendecomposition of a projection of the Koopman operator, which is shown to yield computational efficiencies in experiments on both synthetic and real-world data.

The primary contribution is a well-motivated kernel construction for this purpose, which is validated through a series of experiments. The paper is well-written, and the proposed kernel-based formulation of the Koopman operator is an elegant and potentially powerful tool for data-driven analysis of dynamical systems. However, there are several concerns that the authors should address to strengthen the paper.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Clarification of Novelty with Respect to Prior Work:** A major concern is the lack of a clear distinction between the proposed method and the work of Williams et al. [35], which also utilized Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for analyzing nonlinear dynamics. The authors should explicitly detail their novel contributions in this context. A dedicated discussion clarifying the key differences—both theoretical and practical—would significantly strengthen the paper, perhaps as part of an expanded discussion in Section 4.

2.  **Incomplete Literature Review:** The field of kernel methods for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems is extensive. The current literature review appears to omit several relevant lines of work. To better position their contribution, the authors are encouraged to review and discuss works by researchers such as L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc, D. Tuia et al., J.L. Rojo-Alvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, and M. O. Franz and B. Schölkopf, among others.

3.  **Applicability to Stochastic Systems:** The Koopman operator is traditionally defined for deterministic systems. While the authors briefly touch upon this, the paper would benefit greatly from a more thorough discussion of the method's robustness and performance in stochastic settings, which represent the majority of real-world scenarios. Even a small-scale experiment on a toy problem with stochastic dynamics would be a valuable addition to demonstrate the method's behavior under noise.

4.  **Elaboration on Key Assumptions:**
    *   The remark on page 5 (line 216), stating that the estimation procedure requires a collection of consecutive pairs of observables rather than a full sequence, warrants further clarification. What are the practical implications of this for analyzing multidimensional time series where pointwise relationships between consecutive states might be weak or complex? A discussion of what is gained by the proposed kernel construction under such conditions would be insightful.
    *   Furthermore, the setting implies an inherent causal assumption. The authors should elaborate on this assumption and discuss its potential limitations or how it shapes the applicability of their framework.

5.  **Scope of Experimental Validation:** The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating the method's functionality, could be made more compelling.
    *   The prediction task relies on a standard pre-imaging approach, which makes it difficult to assess the unique advantages of the Koopman framework itself.
    *   In the "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" section, the paper misses an opportunity to connect with and compare against the extensive literature on time series clustering (e.g., methods based on kernel k-means or other subspace kernel approaches).
    *   The sequential change-point detection experiment would be significantly stronger if compared against state-of-the-art online kernel-based change detection algorithms. The baseline used is known to have limitations with complex dynamics and may not provide a challenging benchmark. More ambitious and comprehensive experiments with stronger baselines are needed to convincingly support the paper's claims.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Please check for consistent indexing (e.g., `τ-1` vs. `τ`).
*   In the last equation on page 7, the dot in `k(xi.xj)` should likely be a comma: `k(xi, xj)`.
