This paper introduces Kernel Dynamic Mode Decomposition (KDMD), an extension of the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) algorithm to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). By leveraging the kernel trick, KDMD allows for the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems using a potentially infinite-dimensional feature map, representing a powerful generalization of the standard DMD framework. The method is demonstrated on several toy problems.

While the core contribution is significant and promising, the manuscript's primary weakness is its presentation. The paper is difficult to follow, even for a reader familiar with the subject. The authors often favor dense mathematical formalism where simpler, intuitive explanations would be more effective. Conversely, some sections are overly verbose without adding significant clarity. In its current form, the paper is too cryptic for publication at NIPS. However, the underlying work is of high quality, and I strongly encourage the authors to undertake a major revision focused on pedagogical clarity. A well-written version of this paper has the potential to be highly influential and widely cited.

**Major Remarks**

1.  **Clarity and Exposition:** The manuscript would benefit significantly from a more direct and concise writing style. For example, long descriptions such as, "Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point," could be distilled into a more straightforward sentence. This issue is prevalent throughout the paper and hinders readability.

2.  **Paper Structure and Content:** The organization of the paper could be improved.
    *   Section 4 appears redundant, as its content is largely covered in the introduction and could be omitted in a conference-length paper.
    *   Section 5 presents a discussion that lacks concrete illustration. This section should either be supported by examples or condensed and integrated into the conclusion.
    *   In contrast, Section 6, which details the experiments, is overly condensed. This section requires significant expansion to provide the reader with a clear understanding of the experimental setup and the implications of the results.

**Minor Remarks**

1.  There appears to be a typo in the description of the toy nonlinear (NL) case; the system as described is linear.

2.  The first two toy problems are polynomial systems. These are ideal scenarios for showcasing the strength of KDMD, particularly when an appropriate polynomial kernel can be selected. The paper should provide more detail on this connection and elaborate on why these examples are particularly well-suited for the proposed method.
