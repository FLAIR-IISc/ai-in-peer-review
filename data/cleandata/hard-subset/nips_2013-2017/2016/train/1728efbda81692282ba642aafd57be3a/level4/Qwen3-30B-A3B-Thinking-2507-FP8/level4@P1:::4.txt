

This paper introduces a novel approach to Koopman spectral analysis by extending it to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors propose a modal decomposition algorithm that operates in RKHS to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using nonlinear basis functions, rather than the linear monomials used in conventional dynamic mode decomposition (DMD). The method fundamentally involves calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projection onto the subspace spanned by these bases. The authors demonstrate that this approach can be viewed as a feature extraction procedure for nonlinear dynamical systems, and they illustrate its utility through applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data.

The work builds upon established Koopman spectral analysis and DMD techniques, which have gained significant traction in fluid mechanics and engineering applications over the past decade. While conventional DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials (analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion), the proposed kernel-based approach leverages the expressive power of kernel functions to capture more complex nonlinear dynamics. This represents a meaningful extension to the DMD framework, particularly for systems where linear approximations are insufficient.

The paper's strengths are numerous. First, the theoretical development is sound and well-motivated, with clear connections to both Koopman operator theory and DMD. The authors provide a rigorous mathematical foundation for their method, including a detailed theorem (Theorem 1) that extends DMD principles to RKHS. Second, the practical implementation addresses a key limitation of standard DMD through a robustification step using kernel principal component analysis (Section 4), which significantly improves numerical stability. Third, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, demonstrating the method's effectiveness on both synthetic systems (including a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map) and real-world applications (locomotion data analysis and change-point detection). The results in Figures 1-4 clearly show the method's ability to accurately estimate Koopman eigenvalues and modes while enabling meaningful applications.

The paper's most significant weakness lies in the limited comparative evaluation. While the authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness through specific examples, they do not include a direct comparison to non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis methods on the same datasets. Such a comparison would be crucial to quantify the benefits of the kernel-based approach over conventional methods. For instance, it would be valuable to see whether the proposed method consistently outperforms standard DMD in terms of eigenvalue estimation accuracy or feature extraction quality across multiple datasets.

Additionally, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of kernel selection and its impact on results. The authors use specific kernels (polynomial for the toy system, Gaussian for the Hénon map) without explaining the rationale for these choices or exploring how different kernel functions might affect the quality of the Koopman spectral decomposition. A systematic exploration of kernel selection would strengthen the paper's contribution and provide practical guidance for future applications.

The paper also has minor limitations in terms of theoretical completeness. While Theorem 2 provides a connection between the proposed method and Koopman eigenfunctions, the assumptions required for this connection (that the data are sufficiently rich and the Koopman eigenfunctions can be approximated by kernel principal components) are not thoroughly validated with empirical evidence. The authors might have strengthened their theoretical claims by including experiments that test these assumptions.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported experimental results. The clarity is excellent, with well-structured presentation and effective use of mathematical notation. The originality is strong, as the RKHS extension to Koopman spectral analysis represents a meaningful advancement beyond existing DMD variants. The significance is substantial, as the method enables more accurate modeling of nonlinear dynamics and provides a novel feature extraction framework for machine learning applications in dynamical systems.

Pro:
- Novel extension of Koopman spectral analysis to RKHS with clear theoretical foundation
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple applications
- Practical robustification step addressing numerical instability
- Well-written and clearly presented
- Demonstrates significant potential for real-world applications

Con:
- Lacks direct comparison to non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis methods
- Insufficient discussion of kernel selection and its impact on results
- Limited validation of theoretical assumptions regarding data richness

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of dynamical systems analysis. The proposed kernel-based Koopman spectral analysis method offers a valuable extension to existing DMD techniques, with demonstrated utility in several applications. The primary limitation is the absence of comparative analysis with non-kernel methods and insufficient exploration of kernel selection effects. These omissions prevent the paper from fully establishing the relative advantages of the proposed approach. However, the method is technically sound, well-motivated, and presents a clear path forward for future work in Koopman spectral analysis. With the suggested improvements, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
