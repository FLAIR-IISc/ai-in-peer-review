This paper proposes a spectral method for the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The approach is based on the Koopman operator, which provides a linear operator representation for function composition. Specifically, the authors apply an eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator to the system's forward dynamics, which can be viewed as a composition of the state transition function (e.g., x(t+2) = f(f(x(t)))). Given trajectories collected from the system, this decomposition is performed using an approach analogous to kernel PCA and subspace analysis. Finally, the method is applied to the tasks of embedding and recognizing dynamical systems, where the authors demonstrate that it performs well.

For an audience familiar with kernel methods, the core contribution is the insight that function composition can be represented by a linear operator, to which powerful decomposition techniques can be applied. The evolution of a dynamical system is a natural example of function composition, making it a fitting application for this framework.

However, the novelty of this paper appears to be limited. A closely related algorithm, Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), has already been established in the dynamical systems literature. Consequently, the main contribution of this work seems to be the application of a kernel method to DMD, or its "kernelization."

Furthermore, the paper could be strengthened by providing a more thorough comparison to other established methods. For instance, there are alternative approaches for recognizing dynamical systems and detecting changes, such as Recurrent Neural Networks, which are not discussed or compared against. As a result, the applications presented in the paper are not fully compelling in demonstrating the unique advantages of the proposed method over existing techniques.
