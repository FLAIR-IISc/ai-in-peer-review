This paper introduces a spectral approach for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems, leveraging the Koopman operator – an operator representing function composition. Specifically, the authors apply Koopman operator decomposition to the system’s forward dynamics, defined by a discrete-time evolution x(t+2) = f(f(x(t))). Utilizing trajectories obtained from the system, they perform an eigen-decomposition of this operator, drawing parallels with kernel Principal Component Analysis and subspace analysis techniques. The method is then demonstrated on embedding and recognition tasks for dynamical systems, achieving promising results. For readers acquainted with kernel methods, the key contribution lies in framing function composition as a linear operator amenable to decomposition; the application to dynamical systems, inherently defined by such compositions, is well-motivated. However, given the existing Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) literature within the dynamical systems field, the novelty appears concentrated in a kernelized extension of this established technique. Furthermore, alternative approaches, like recurrent neural networks, exist for similar tasks including system recognition and change detection. Consequently, the presented applications, while functional, do not fully establish a compelling advantage or unique capability of this method.
