This paper presents a spectral analysis framework for nonlinear dynamical systems based on the Koopman operator, formulated within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. The authors leverage this foundation to develop kernel-based methods for analyzing systems from empirical data. Eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator demonstrates efficiency gains in both synthetic and real-world applications, representing a significant contribution in kernel methods for nonlinear dynamics. The work is well-motivated and supported by experimental validation. However, several points require further clarification and development.

A key concern is the novelty of this approach relative to prior work, specifically Williams, Kevrekidis, and Rowley [35], which already introduces Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for analyzing nonlinear system dynamics. The authors should explicitly delineate the core differences between their method and [35], providing a more in-depth discussion in Section 4 to highlight the specific advantages and contributions of their kernel-based formulation.

The field of kernel methods for nonlinear dynamical system analysis is extensive. The authors should broaden their related work to include relevant contributions from researchers such as L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, and M. O. Franz and B. B. Schölkopf. Inclusion of these references would demonstrate a comprehensive understanding of the existing landscape.

While the Koopman operator is typically defined for deterministic systems, many real-world applications involve stochastic dynamics. The authors briefly acknowledge this, but a more thorough discussion of their method's performance in stochastic settings would be valuable. Including a simple toy experiment illustrating performance with noisy data would further strengthen the paper.

The authors’ clarification regarding the data requirement (line 216, page 5) – that the estimation procedure requires pairs of consecutive observables linked by a pointwise nonlinear relationship, rather than a strict time sequence – raises an important question. In multidimensional time series where pointwise relationships may be weak, what benefits does the proposed kernel construction offer? Expanding on this point and discussing the implications of this assumption would be beneficial. Furthermore, elaborating on the underlying causal assumptions inherent in the framework would enhance clarity.

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating the method’s functionality, feels limited in scope. The predictive modeling through time series imaging follows a standard approach. The “Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics” subsection lacks a discussion of connections to, and a comparative evaluation against, the substantial literature on time series clustering (e.g., utilizing kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods). Similarly, the sequential change-point detection experiment would be more compelling with a comparison to state-of-the-art online change detection methods employing kernels – the currently used method is known to struggle with complex dynamics. More robust and comprehensive experiments are needed to convincingly demonstrate the superiority and broad applicability of the proposed approach.

Finally, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. A few minor corrections are suggested: please verify the indices on τ−1 versus τ, and replace the dot with a comma in k(xi.xj) (last equation on page 7).
