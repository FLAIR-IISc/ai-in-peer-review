The paper presents a spectral analysis method for nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs). The authors propose a modal decomposition algorithm to perform this analysis, which is an extension of dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) via the Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in the feature space. The algorithm provides a decomposition of the dynamics into a finite number of modes, and thus it can be considered as a feature extraction procedure for nonlinear dynamical systems.

The paper begins by introducing the Koopman operator and DMD, and then extends the analysis with reproducing kernels. The authors provide a modal decomposition algorithm to perform this analysis, which is mathematically correct but may yield an ill-conditioned algorithm in practice. To robustify the algorithm, the authors propose a modification using the proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) basis.

The paper also discusses related works, including spectral analysis techniques, DMD, and subspace identification methods. The authors highlight the connection between their algorithm and the eigensystem realization method, and mention that the relation between DMD and other methods is an interesting open problem.

The authors illustrate their method using synthetic and real-world data, including a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, and locomotion data from the CMU Graphics Lab Motion Capture Database. They also consider applications using extracted features, such as prediction, sequential change-point detection, and recognition of dynamic patterns.

The strengths of the paper include its clear and well-organized presentation, as well as its empirical examples and applications. The authors provide a thorough discussion of related works and highlight the novelty of their approach.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The novelty of the paper seems to be limited to kernelizing the DMD algorithm, which has already been proposed in the dynamical system literature. The applications presented in the paper, while interesting, are not particularly compelling in demonstrating the uniqueness and advantages of the method. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other methods, such as recurrent neural networks, for recognizing dynamical systems and detecting changes.

In terms of the four criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on clarity and originality. The authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their method, and the use of reproducing kernels is a novel extension of the DMD algorithm. However, the paper could score higher on quality and significance if it provided more compelling applications and comparisons with other methods.
