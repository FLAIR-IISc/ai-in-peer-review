This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces, alongside an algorithm for performing this analysis using data from dynamical systems. The work bridges concepts from fluid dynamics with potential applications in machine learning. The authors validate their approach on a damped linear 1D system, a chaotic map, and real-world locomotion data, comparing performance against dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA, though the results from the latter comparison lack clarity. 

The contribution represents a noteworthy extension of DMD, conceptually similar to existing “kernelization” strategies found in kernel PCA, kernel ICA, and related methods. While the theoretical development is sound and the paper is well-written – offering valuable insight for those unfamiliar with fluid dynamics – the experimental evaluation primarily focuses on relatively simple, constructed examples.

I am generally positive towards efforts that introduce machine learning researchers to techniques from other fields. However, to strengthen the paper’s impact within the ML community, the authors should more explicitly articulate the practical benefits of this Koopman-based approach. Specifically, identifying concrete use cases and demonstrating competitive performance against existing state-of-the-art methods is crucial. A compelling example could be found in robotics, a field actively researching inference and learning in dynamical systems. What advantages does Koopman analysis offer in this context, or another relevant ML domain?

The theoretical results, while correct, are largely anticipated given the established principles of kernel methods. Applying a linear operator within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space naturally leads to eigendecomposition-based algorithms on finite-dimensional matrices, as seen in kernel PCA and related techniques. 

Regarding implementation details, it remains unclear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires explicit representation in the feature space. If so, this constitutes a limitation relative to kernel PCA and should be explicitly acknowledged. Furthermore, including a performance baseline for standard DMD on the toy example and the Henon map would help to better illustrate the differences between the proposed method and its predecessor in a straightforward setting.
