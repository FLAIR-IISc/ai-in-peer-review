### Summary

This paper presents a method for spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to analyze the Koopman operator, a linear operator that describes the evolution of observables under a nonlinear dynamic, within the rich functional space provided by an RKHS. The proposed algorithm, kernel DMD (kDMD), approximates the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator from finite data sequences. This is achieved by performing an eigendecomposition of the operator's projection onto a Krylov subspace constructed in the feature space. The authors present a direct formulation based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization and a more robust variant that projects data onto kernel principal component analysis (kPCA) directions. The resulting Koopman modes are presented as features for downstream machine learning tasks, which the authors demonstrate through applications in state prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled and Elegant Formulation:** The paper provides a clear and theoretically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods. Connecting the spectral theory of the Koopman operator with the functional analysis tools of RKHS is a natural and powerful idea. This allows the method to capture nonlinear dynamics by implicitly using a rich set of basis functions, moving beyond the linear observables typically used in standard DMD.

2.  **Technical Soundness (Quality):** The mathematical derivations, particularly Theorems 1 and 2, are well-presented and appear correct. They establish a clear connection between the algorithm's outputs (empirical Ritz values and vectors) and the true Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions. The proposed robustification using a projection onto kernel principal components is a sensible and necessary step, analogous to the use of POD truncation in standard DMD to handle noise and rank-deficiency.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with a helpful review of the Koopman operator and DMD, making the work accessible to readers who may not be experts in fluid dynamics or dynamical systems theory. The progression from the direct formulation in Section 3 to the robust version in Section 4 is logical and well-motivated.

4.  **Versatility of Application:** The authors effectively frame the proposed method as a feature extraction technique for time-series data. The demonstration of its use in diverse applications like prediction, embedding, and change-point detection highlights the potential utility of the learned spectral features.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Originality:** The primary contribution of the paper is the "kernelization" of the DMD algorithm. While this is a valid and useful extension, the idea of applying the kernel trick to linear algorithms is a well-established paradigm in machine learning. Furthermore, the proposed method is conceptually very similar to "extended DMD" (eDMD) [35], which allows for the use of a pre-determined dictionary of basis functions. The kernel method can be seen as a specific, albeit powerful, way of defining such a dictionary. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly differentiated its contribution from eDMD with, for example, a Gaussian RBF dictionary.

2.  **Insufficient Empirical Comparison (Significance):** The experiments are more illustrative than demonstrative of the method's superiority. While the applications are interesting, they lack rigorous comparison against relevant state-of-the-art baselines.
    *   **Prediction:** The prediction task could be compared against standard time-series models, such as Gaussian Processes or Recurrent Neural Networks (LSTMs, GRUs), which are also designed to handle nonlinear dynamics.
    *   **Dynamics Recognition:** The MDS embedding is qualitative. A quantitative comparison on a classification task against other time-series classification methods would be more compelling.
    *   **Change-Point Detection:** The comparison is made to one specific kernel-based method. It would be beneficial to compare against a broader range of established sequential change-point detection algorithms.

    Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed method for these tasks. The experiments successfully show that the method *can* be used for these applications, but not that it is a *better* way to do so.

3.  **Practical Considerations:** The paper does not discuss practical aspects such as the choice of kernel and its parameters, which is critical for the performance of any kernel method. Additionally, the computational complexity scales with the number of data points due to the construction and manipulation of the Gram matrix, which could limit its applicability to very long time series.

### Conclusion and Arguments for/against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a theoretically sound and elegant fusion of Koopman operator theory and kernel methods.
*   It provides a complete algorithmic pipeline, including a crucial robustification step.
*   The work is clearly presented and could inspire further research at the intersection of dynamical systems and machine learning.
*   The framing as a feature extraction tool for dynamics is valuable for the NIPS community.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The novelty is somewhat limited, as it can be viewed as an application of the standard kernel trick to an existing algorithm (DMD) and is conceptually close to prior work (eDMD).
*   The experimental validation is weak. The lack of strong baselines makes it difficult to judge the significance and practical utility of the proposed method compared to existing solutions for the demonstrated applications.

Overall, this is a well-written paper describing a technically sound and interesting method. The main concerns are its incremental novelty and the lack of compelling empirical evidence to demonstrate its advantages over other methods. While the theoretical contribution is elegant, its practical impact is not fully established. The paper is borderline, but the weakness in the experimental evaluation is significant enough to lean towards rejection. A revision with a stronger empirical section, including comparisons to relevant machine learning baselines, would make a much stronger case for acceptance.
