

The paper proposes a kernel-based spectral method for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems via Koopman operator decomposition, specifically targeting the second iterate dynamics \(x(t+2) = f(f(x(t)))\). The authors frame the Koopman operator as a linear representation of function composition, leveraging kernel methods and subspace analysis to perform eigen-decomposition on trajectory data. They demonstrate applications in dynamical system embedding and recognition, claiming competitive performance.  

While the Koopman operator framework is well-established for linearizing nonlinear dynamics (typically applied to \(x(t+1) = f(x(t))\)), the focus on the second iterate \(x(t+2)\) is unconventional and potentially confusing. The core technical contribution appears to be a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a method already widely used in dynamical systems literature. The paper does not clearly articulate how this kernelization differs from or improves upon existing kernel-based Koopman approaches (e.g., [1,2]), nor does it establish theoretical guarantees for the proposed decomposition under composition. The connection to kernel PCA is superficial; the method relies on standard kernel subspace techniques without novel theoretical insights.  

The novelty claim is weak. DMD itself is a specific Koopman-inspired technique, and its kernelization has been explored in prior work (e.g., [3]). The paper’s primary innovation—applying kernel methods to the *composition* of dynamics—is not convincingly justified as a significant departure from the literature. The authors assert that "the composition of functions can be represented as a linear operator," but this is a direct consequence of the Koopman operator’s definition, not a new observation. The paper fails to clarify why the second-iterate formulation is necessary or advantageous over standard first-iterate Koopman analysis, which is the norm in the field.  

The experimental section suffers from a lack of compelling motivation and insufficient comparison. The applications (embedding, recognition) are tested on synthetic and benchmark datasets, but the evaluation does not adequately address *why* the kernelized Koopman approach is superior to simpler alternatives. For instance:  
- No comparison is made against standard DMD or kernel DMD variants (e.g., [4]), making it unclear whether the kernelization provides tangible gains.  
- The paper overlooks established methods for dynamical system recognition, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or temporal convolutional networks, which are the state-of-the-art for sequence-based tasks. Without such comparisons, the claim of "good performance" is unsubstantiated.  
- Ablation studies on kernel choice, trajectory length, or noise robustness are absent, leaving key design decisions unvalidated.  
- The recognition tasks lack context: Are these datasets specifically challenging for linear methods? How do the results compare to domain-specific baselines (e.g., for motion capture or climate data)?  

Critically, the paper does not demonstrate *unique* value. Dynamical system analysis via Koopman operators is a mature area, and the kernelized DMD approach described is a straightforward adaptation of existing tools. The authors do not show that their method solves problems unaddressed by prior work—e.g., handling high-dimensional or noisy data more effectively than RNNs or standard DMD. The absence of a clear problem statement or gap analysis weakens the contribution.  

The writing is occasionally imprecise. For example, the phrase "the Koopman operator decomposition is applied to the forward nonlinear dynamics" misrepresents the Koopman operator’s role: it is a *linear representation* of the nonlinear dynamics, not a decomposition applied *to* them. The manuscript also conflates Koopman operator theory (which deals with the evolution of observables) with the specific DMD algorithm, which is a data-driven approximation. This ambiguity may mislead readers unfamiliar with the literature.  

In summary, the paper presents a technically plausible but incremental extension of kernelized DMD. The theoretical foundation is underdeveloped, the novelty is overstated relative to prior work, and the experimental validation fails to convincingly demonstrate advantages over simpler or established methods. The applications lack specificity and fail to justify the method’s necessity. While the kernel approach is a valid direction, the paper does not advance the field sufficiently to warrant acceptance at NIPS.  

**Recommendation: Reject**  
*Reason*: The contribution is incremental with insufficient novelty, and the evaluation does not substantiate the claimed advantages over existing methods. The paper would benefit from a stronger theoretical justification, clearer problem formulation, and rigorous comparisons against relevant baselines (including RNNs and standard kernel DMD).
