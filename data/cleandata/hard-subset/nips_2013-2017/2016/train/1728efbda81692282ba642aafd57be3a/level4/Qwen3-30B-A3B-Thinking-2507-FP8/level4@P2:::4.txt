

This paper presents a novel extension of Koopman spectral analysis to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), providing a modal decomposition algorithm that uses nonlinear basis functions instead of the linear monomials typically employed in dynamic mode decomposition (DMD). The authors frame their approach as a generalization of DMD, where instead of using linear basis functions (analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion), their method leverages the expressive power of kernel functions to capture more complex dynamics. This work builds upon the Koopman operator theory (Koopman, 1931; Mezić, 2005), DMD (Schmid, 2010), and extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015), while addressing limitations of previous approaches by introducing kernel-based nonlinear approximations.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-structured theoretical development. The authors establish the mathematical foundations through a series of theorems, showing how the Koopman operator can be analyzed in RKHS through orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix and eigendecomposition of the projected operator. The robustification approach using POD bases effectively addresses the numerical instability issues common in DMD implementations, making the method more practical for real-world applications. The empirical validation on synthetic and real-world datasets (including the Hénon map, Lorenz system, and locomotion data) provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness across multiple application domains.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-organized sections that logically progress from theoretical foundations to algorithmic implementation and practical applications. The mathematical derivations are presented with sufficient detail for experts to follow and reproduce the results. The authors provide clear descriptions of the algorithmic steps and effectively illustrate the method through multiple figures showing eigenvalue estimation and application results.

In terms of originality, this work makes a meaningful contribution by extending Koopman spectral analysis to RKHS, which represents a non-trivial generalization beyond existing DMD variants. While Williams et al. (2015) proposed extended DMD using pre-determined basis functions, this paper's approach using kernel functions provides a more flexible and data-adaptive basis selection mechanism. The connection between the proposed method and Koopman eigenfunctions is well-established, with clear theoretical justification for the approach.

The significance of this work is substantial, as the proposed method provides a feature extraction procedure for nonlinear dynamical systems with broad applicability. The authors demonstrate this through three compelling applications: short-term prediction via preimage reconstruction, dynamics recognition using kernel principal angles, and sequential change-point detection. These applications showcase the method's potential value for practitioners working with complex dynamical systems across various domains.

Strengths:
1. Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous mathematical development
2. Practical implementation details, including robustification via POD bases
3. Clear writing with well-structured organization
4. Multiple convincing applications with empirical validation
5. Effective connection between theory and practice

Weaknesses:
1. Limited comparison to non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis (e.g., standard DMD and extended DMD) in the empirical evaluation
2. Insufficient exploration of kernel choice and its impact on results
3. The theoretical connection between the RKHS approach and Koopman eigenfunctions could be more thoroughly explained
4. Some technical details (e.g., the choice of kernel parameters) could be better justified

For acceptance, I recommend the following arguments:

Pro:
- The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension to Koopman spectral analysis
- The method is well-motivated, well-developed, and practically implemented
- The empirical validation across multiple domains demonstrates the method's utility
- The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized

Con:
- The limited comparison to non-RKHS approaches weakens the evaluation
- The lack of exploration regarding kernel selection is a notable omission
- Some theoretical connections could be more thoroughly explained

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of nonlinear dynamical systems analysis. While the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the minor weaknesses noted above, the core contribution is technically sound, well-executed, and represents a meaningful advancement over existing DMD approaches. The method's potential applications in prediction, change detection, and dynamics recognition make it relevant to both theoretical and applied researchers. With minor revisions addressing the comparative evaluation and kernel selection discussion, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
