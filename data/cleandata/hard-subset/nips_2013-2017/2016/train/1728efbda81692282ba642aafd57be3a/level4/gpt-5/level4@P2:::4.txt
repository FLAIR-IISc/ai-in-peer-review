Summary
The paper proposes a kernelized approach to Koopman spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors formulate dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), deriving a modal decomposition by (i) constructing orthogonal bases of the Krylov matrix in feature space and (ii) eigendecomposing the projection of the Koopman operator onto the span of these bases. A robust variant projects data onto kernel POD (kernel PCA) directions to mitigate ill-conditioning, analogous to POD-truncated DMD. The method returns finite sets of modes (features) and is applied to short-term prediction via preimage reconstruction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. Empirical illustrations include a toy nonlinear system, the Henon map, and motion-capture locomotion sequences. Results show plausible recovery of eigenvalues and qualitative performance on applications.

Relation to prior work
This work builds on Koopman operator theory, classic DMD, and numerical variants (exact/optimized DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD). It is closely related to extended DMD (EDMD), which replaces monomials with user-chosen bases. Here, the RKHS formulation provides an implicit, rich basis via kernels, with computations expressed through Gram matrices. The robustification via kernel PCA is in the spirit of POD-based DMD improvements. Connections to spectral methods and subspace identification are noted.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a clean derivation of DMD in RKHS with rigorous statements (Theorems 1–2) linking empirical Ritz quantities to Koopman spectra.
- Practical algorithm: kernelization enables nonlinear features without explicit basis design; the POD variant addresses numerical stability and rank deficiency.
- Breadth of applications: shows how Koopman modes can be used for prediction, change detection, and recognition, illustrating the method’s versatility.
- Writing and organization: generally clear exposition and helpful step-by-step algorithms.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical evaluation: experiments are small-scale and largely qualitative. There is no quantitative comparison to strong baselines such as (exact) DMD and EDMD with polynomial/RBF dictionaries.
- Kernel sensitivity: there is no systematic study of how kernel choice and hyperparameters (e.g., RBF bandwidth) affect eigenvalue recovery, predictive accuracy, or robustness.
- Scalability: computational and memory costs scale with the number of snapshots via the Gram matrix; the paper does not discuss large-scale strategies (e.g., Nyström, random features, incremental updates).
- Assumptions and conditions: Theorem 2 requires eigenfunctions to lie in the span of kernel principal directions; practical conditions under which this holds (data richness, mixing, noise) are not examined. Noise robustness is not evaluated.
- Preimage step: the proposed MDS-style preimage method is reasonable but not compared to alternatives, and its stability and runtime are not reported.

Quality
The theoretical development is sound, with proofs and clear algorithmic recipes. However, the evidence for effectiveness is mostly qualitative. Adding quantitative metrics (eigenvalue errors on systems with known spectra, prediction RMSE, change-point detection ROC/AUC, classification accuracy for motion recognition) and ablations (sequence length, noise level, kernel parameters) would significantly strengthen the claims.

Clarity
The paper is well written overall. Suggestions:
- State computational complexity and memory requirements of both variants and discuss scalability.
- Be explicit about kernel and hyperparameter selection (e.g., median heuristic vs cross-validation).
- Clarify the relationship and differences to EDMD and “kernel DMD” nomenclature; a short comparative paragraph would help.
- Tighten notation around feature-space vectors and empirical Ritz vectors (φ̃ vs φ̄) and centralization (H, Ḡ).
- Minor typos: “a practical implementation could yield an ill-conditioned algorithm; therefore, in Section 4, we describe a way to robustify it...”.

Originality
Kernelizing DMD and connecting it to Koopman spectral analysis in RKHS is a natural but nontrivial extension. The explicit QR/Krylov construction in feature space and the kernel-POD robustification are technically solid. The work is close to EDMD; the main novelty is the kernel-centric derivation and practical algorithms expressed via Gram matrices.

Significance
If supported by stronger empirical evidence, the approach could be impactful for data-driven modeling and analysis of nonlinear dynamics, offering flexible, basis-free feature construction. The application sketches are promising but need quantitative validation and baseline comparisons to demonstrate clear gains.

Pro acceptance
- Clear, principled kernel extension of Koopman/DMD with theory.
- Practical robust variant; broadly applicable pipeline.
- Good exposition and relevant application scenarios.

Con acceptance
- Lack of quantitative comparisons to DMD/EDMD and other baselines.
- No study of kernel/hyperparameter sensitivity or noise robustness.
- Scalability and preimage practicality not addressed.

Questions for the authors
1. How does the method compare quantitatively to exact DMD and EDMD (e.g., polynomial degree 3, RBF dictionaries) on eigenvalue recovery and prediction?
2. How sensitive are results to kernel choice and bandwidth? Can you provide a simple heuristic or selection procedure?
3. What are the time/memory complexities for both variants, and can you scale to tens of thousands of snapshots (Nyström, random features, incremental updates)?
4. How robust is the method to measurement noise and process noise?
5. For recognition, can you report classification accuracy instead of only an embedding visualization?

Minor suggestions
- Include a brief guide for kernel selection and hyperparameter tuning.
- Add an ablation on the number of retained kernel-POD components.
- Consider a sparsity-promoting variant in RKHS to reduce the number of modes.

Overall
A well-written and principled contribution that extends DMD/Koopman analysis to RKHS with a practical algorithmic path. I would be inclined to accept if the final version includes quantitative baselines against non-RKHS Koopman spectral methods and a discussion/experiment on kernel choice and sensitivity.
