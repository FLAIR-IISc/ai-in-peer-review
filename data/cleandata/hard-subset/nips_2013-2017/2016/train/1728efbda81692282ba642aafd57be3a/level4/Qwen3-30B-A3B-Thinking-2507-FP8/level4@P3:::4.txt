

This paper presents a novel extension of Koopman spectral analysis using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), addressing the limitations of traditional dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) methods that rely on linear basis functions. The authors propose a modal decomposition algorithm that operates directly in RKHS, allowing for more expressive nonlinear approximations of Koopman eigenfunctions. The core contribution is a mathematically rigorous framework that reduces the spectral analysis to orthogonal basis calculation in RKHS and eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. The authors further demonstrate applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition, supported by empirical validation on synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper builds upon a rich body of work on Koopman operator theory and DMD methods. While traditional DMD (e.g., [25, 26]) approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials (effectively a one-term Taylor expansion), this work leverages the expressiveness of kernel functions to capture more complex dynamics. The authors properly contextualize their contribution relative to related work, including proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) [12], extended DMD [35], and kernel-based approaches [28]. The paper also relates to the broader NIPS community's interest in spectral methods for dynamical systems [15, 31, 17], and makes clear connections to recent advances in Koopman operator theory [35, 33].

The paper excels in quality, presenting a technically sound approach with rigorous mathematical derivations. The theoretical foundations are well-established, with clear theorems (1 and 2) demonstrating the mathematical equivalence between the proposed kernel-based DMD and standard DMD principles. The implementation details, particularly the robustification via POD bases (Section 4), address practical concerns about numerical stability that plague many DMD variants. The empirical validation across multiple datasets (toy system, Hénon map, locomotion data, and Lorenz map) provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness, with results comparable to state-of-the-art approaches. The paper demonstrates careful evaluation by including both strengths (accurate eigenvalue estimation) and limitations (sensitivity to kernel choice, computational complexity).

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The mathematical exposition is precise yet accessible, with well-structured sections that build logically from theoretical foundations to practical implementation and applications. The authors effectively use figures (e.g., Fig. 1 showing eigenvalue estimation, Fig. 3 demonstrating dynamics recognition) to illustrate key concepts. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results, including clear descriptions of the algorithm steps and empirical setups.

In terms of originality, this work makes a meaningful contribution by extending DMD to RKHS, which allows for more expressive modeling of nonlinear dynamics. While kernel-based methods have been applied to other areas of machine learning, their application to Koopman spectral analysis represents a novel combination of techniques. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous work, particularly noting that while extended DMD [35] uses pre-determined basis functions, their method operates directly in the functional space with kernel functions providing the basis. The paper is sufficiently distinct from prior NIPS work on Koopman analysis [35, 33] and related spectral methods.

The significance of this work is substantial. The ability to extract meaningful modal decompositions of nonlinear dynamical systems has broad implications across physics, engineering, and machine learning. The demonstrated applications—state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition—show tangible utility beyond theoretical interest. The method's potential to advance state-of-the-art in dynamical systems analysis, particularly for complex nonlinear systems where linear approximations fail, represents a meaningful contribution to the field.

The paper has two main weaknesses that would strengthen its contribution. First, while the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the kernel-based approach, it lacks a direct comparison to non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis (e.g., standard DMD or extended DMD) on the same datasets. Such a comparison would more clearly quantify the advantage of the kernel-based approach. Second, the paper does not sufficiently explore the impact of kernel choice on performance. The authors use specific kernels (polynomial for toy systems, Gaussian for Hénon map), but a systematic investigation of how different kernels affect the quality of the spectral decomposition would be valuable for practitioners.

The paper is well-written and meets NIPS standards for technical quality. The theoretical foundations are sound, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the applications demonstrate practical relevance. The authors have carefully addressed related work and positioned their contribution within the broader context of spectral analysis for dynamical systems.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to Koopman spectral analysis and dynamical systems, with clear theoretical foundations and practical applications. The two weaknesses identified (lack of direct comparison to non-RKHS methods and insufficient exploration of kernel choice) are important but not critical to the paper's core contribution. Addressing these in the revision would strengthen the paper significantly, but they do not prevent the paper from making a valuable contribution to the field.

Overall, this is a strong paper that advances the state of the art in Koopman spectral analysis by introducing a kernel-based extension that enables more expressive modeling of nonlinear dynamics. The work is well-executed, well-motivated, and has clear potential for impact across multiple scientific domains.
