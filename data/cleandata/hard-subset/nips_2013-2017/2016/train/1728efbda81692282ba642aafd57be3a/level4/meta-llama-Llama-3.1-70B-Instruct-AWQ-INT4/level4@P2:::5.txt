The manuscript, entitled "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis" extended the dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The resulting algorithm (KDMD) is then applied to a few toy problems. It hence allows to use features map as definition of the kernel, in order to link more the DMD to specific features of interest; it is then a powerful generalization of DMD.

The paper is well-motivated and relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly in the field of physics and engineering. The authors provide a clear summary of the main ideas and connect them to existing research on spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems.

The strengths of the paper include its originality and significance. The proposed algorithm provides a new approach to spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems, and the use of reproducing kernels allows for a more flexible and powerful framework. The authors also provide several applications of the algorithm, including prediction, sequential change detection, and recognition of dynamic patterns.

However, the paper is really hard to read. Even for a few simple notions, the authors emphasize on the mathematical writings when short sentences would have been more than enough. Some sections, on the other hand, are actually verbose. It might make it difficult to understand even for specialists, and a few points should be clarified.

Specifically, Section 4 seems unnecessary and was partially treated in the introduction. Section 5 should have been illustrated, or be summarized in the conclusion. Section 6 should have been developed in the way of Section 5, as it has been condensed too much.

Minor remarks include a probable typo in the description of the toy NL case, which is actually linear. The first two problems are actually polynomial, and it is where the KDMD should shine - when the kernel can be guessed. More details should be given, as actually in Section 5.

Overall, I encourage the authors to rewrite the paper in a more pedagogic way. The resulting article should actually be highly cited.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* Originality and significance of the proposed algorithm
* Powerful generalization of DMD
* Several applications of the algorithm

Arguments con acceptance:

* Difficulty in understanding the paper due to verbose writing style
* Unnecessary sections (e.g., Section 4)
* Lack of illustration and development in certain sections (e.g., Section 5 and Section 6)
