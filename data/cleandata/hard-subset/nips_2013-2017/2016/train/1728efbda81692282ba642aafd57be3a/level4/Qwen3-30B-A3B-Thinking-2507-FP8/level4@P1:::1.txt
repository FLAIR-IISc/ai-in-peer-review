

This paper presents a novel spectral analysis method for nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors extend Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) by leveraging kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with nonlinear basis functions, rather than the linear monomials used in standard DMD approaches. The core contribution is an algorithm that computes orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performs eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator's projection, yielding a modal decomposition of the system dynamics. The authors demonstrate this method through applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data.

The paper is well-motivated and builds upon the established framework of Koopman spectral analysis, which has gained traction in fluid dynamics and engineering applications. It makes a clear contribution by extending DMD to kernel-based feature spaces, providing a more expressive approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions compared to linear basis approaches. The theoretical development is sound, and the experimental validation on both toy systems and the Hénon map shows promising results, with accurate estimation of Koopman eigenvalues. The applications section demonstrates practical utility in feature extraction for dynamics analysis.

However, several important concerns require clarification and expansion. First, the paper's relationship to Williams et al. (2015), "A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator: Extending dynamic mode decomposition," needs clearer differentiation. While the authors reference this work in the related works section, they do not sufficiently distinguish their kernel-based approach from the extended DMD framework presented in Williams et al. The authors should explicitly address how their RKHS-based method differs from the pre-determined functional maps used in extended DMD, particularly regarding the expressiveness of the basis functions and the mathematical properties of the resulting eigenfunction approximations.

Second, the literature review appears incomplete. The field of kernel-based methods for nonlinear dynamical systems is well-established, and several relevant works are missing. The authors should consider citing seminal papers by Ralaivola and d'Alché Buc (2005), Tuia et al. (2011), Rojo-Alvarez et al. (2013), and Mouattamid and Schaback (2008), which have explored kernel methods for dynamical systems analysis. This would better position their work within the broader context of the field.

Third, while the paper briefly mentions stochastic systems, it lacks substantive discussion of how the method performs in stochastic settings, which are more representative of real-world applications. The authors should expand their discussion to address how the Koopman operator framework extends to stochastic dynamics, perhaps with a simple toy example demonstrating performance in a stochastic context.

Fourth, the remark on page 5 (line 216) regarding the estimation procedure not requiring a sequence but rather "a collection of pairs of consecutive observables" requires clarification. The authors should elaborate on the implications of this assumption for multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relationships. Specifically, how does the kernel construction specifically address the challenges of non-pointwise relationships in complex dynamical systems? The causal assumption underlying the method (that each observation pair satisfies x_{t+1} = f(x_t)) should also be more thoroughly discussed.

Regarding experimental validation, while the results are promising, the experiments lack sufficient ambition to fully demonstrate the method's advantages. The prediction via preimage approach follows a standard methodology without clear comparison to state-of-the-art techniques. In the "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" section, the authors should compare their kernel-based approach with established time series clustering methods (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernel methods) to better demonstrate the added value of their Koopman-based features. Similarly, for the sequential change-point detection application, a comparison with recent state-of-the-art online change detection methods using kernels would strengthen the claims about the method's effectiveness, particularly in complex dynamics where some kernel-based methods are known to fail.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with only minor issues: please verify the indexing of τ-1 versus τ in the equations, and correct the notation k(x_i, x_j) (replacing the dot with a comma in the final equation on page 7).

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods, with promising applications in dynamics analysis. The core contribution is valuable, but the authors need to more clearly differentiate their approach from prior work, expand the literature review, address stochastic settings, clarify the method's assumptions and limitations, and provide more compelling experimental validation with appropriate comparisons to state-of-the-art techniques. With these revisions, the paper would make a significant contribution to the field of nonlinear dynamical systems analysis.
