This paper presents a spectral analysis framework based on the Koopman operator defined in an appropriate reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). Building on this, the authors follow a standard pathway to define a set of kernel-based approaches for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems from empirical data. The eigendecomposition of the projection of the Koopman operator yields efficiency gains in both synthetic and real-data analyses. Overall, the paper introduces a significant kernel construction for nonlinear dynamical system analysis, and the contribution is well motivated and empirically validated.

That said, I have several concerns and requests for clarification:

- Relation to prior work (Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, 2015). It is not clear what the substantial differences are between this paper and [35] (M.O. Williams, I.G. Kevrekidis, and C.W. Rowley. A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator: Extending dynamic mode decomposition. Journal of Nonlinear Science, 25:1307–1346, 2015), where Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes were already introduced for analyzing nonlinear dynamics. Please provide a clear comparison that pinpoints the main differences in assumptions, methodology (especially the kernel construction and operator projection/eigendecomposition), and theoretical or empirical consequences. An improved and more explicit discussion in Section 4 would be particularly helpful.

- Missing references and positioning within the kernel literature. The field of kernel methods for nonlinear dynamical system analysis is broad, and several relevant works appear to be missing. Please consider including and discussing related work by L. Ralaivola and F. d’Alché-Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Álvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, and M.O. Franz and B. Schölkopf. Positioning your contribution with respect to this literature—both in terms of goals (e.g., prediction, representation, detection) and techniques (e.g., kernel embeddings, kernel regression, subspace methods)—would strengthen the paper.

- Stochastic settings. While the Koopman operator is generally defined for deterministic systems, practical scenarios are often stochastic. The paper briefly touches on this, but I would appreciate a more in-depth discussion of the method’s behavior under stochastic dynamics (e.g., measurement noise, process noise, or stochastic transition dynamics). If feasible, a small toy experiment contrasting deterministic versus stochastic regimes would help clarify robustness and limitations.

- Clarification on the estimation setup and causal assumptions. On line 216 (p. 5), you write that “the estimation procedure itself does not necessarily require a sequence but rather a collection of pairs of consecutive observables, where each pair is supposed to be” nonlinearly pointwise related. I am interested in the implications of this assumption for multidimensional time series where pointwise relations may be weak. What do we gain from the proposed kernel construction in this setting? Please elaborate on how the method behaves when the instantaneous dependence across dimensions is weak or heterogeneous, and discuss any causal assumptions inherent in the setup (e.g., directionality encoded by consecutive pairs, stationarity or Markovianity required by the operator approximation, and how these affect identifiability or performance).

- Experimental evaluation. The experimental settings are not very ambitious. The prediction via pre-imaging follows a standard, well-known approach. In the “Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics” subsection, there is little discussion of connections to—and no experimental comparison with—the extensive literature on time-series clustering using kernels (e.g., kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods). For the sequential change-point detection experiment, it would be more compelling to include comparisons to state-of-the-art online change detection methods with kernels, especially given that the baseline you use is known to fail in more complex dynamical regimes. Overall, stronger and more comprehensive experiments would better support the proposed development.

Minor comments:
- The paper is generally very well written and clear.
- Please check indices for τ−1 vs. τ.
- Replace the dot with a comma in k(xi, xj) (last equation on p. 7).
