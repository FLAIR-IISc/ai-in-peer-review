

The paper introduces a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes an algorithm for this analysis using measurement data from dynamical systems. While the Koopman operator originates in fluid dynamics, the authors suggest its relevance to machine learning. They demonstrate their method on two simple systems—a damped linear 1D system and a chaotic Henon map—and apply it to real locomotion data, comparing performance against dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA. The results for the real-world data are not clearly compelling.  

The work extends dynamic mode decomposition in a manner building on established kernelized techniques (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, GP bandits). The theoretical derivations are technically sound, and the paper is clearly written, offering educational value for machine learning researchers unfamiliar with fluid dynamics concepts. I generally support efforts to bridge interdisciplinary techniques, but the paper falls short in establishing practical relevance for machine learning.  

The authors focus on deterministic dynamical systems defined by maps \(f: \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{M}\) (without stochastic components), which contrasts with common ML settings where noise and random innovations are central. I am not aware of such deterministic setups being prevalent in machine learning applications, though I acknowledge I may be overlooking specific domains. The paper must clarify *why* this approach matters to ML practitioners. What concrete use cases exist? How does it compare to state-of-the-art methods in domains where dynamical system learning is critical, such as robotics? For instance, what specific advantages would Koopman analysis offer robotics researchers over existing techniques? Without addressing this, the contribution risks appearing as a mathematical extension without clear ML utility.  

The theoretical results, while well-executed, are unsurprising given prior work on kernelized linear operators (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel independence tests, or "kernel Bayes rule"). The core idea—working in RKHS to enable eigendecomposition of finite-dimensional matrices—aligns with established patterns. The paper would benefit from a stronger justification for why this particular formulation advances beyond these precedents.  

Two specific concerns require clarification:  
1. **Explicit feature space representation**: The modal decomposition algorithm’s reliance on explicit feature space representations appears to be a limitation compared to kernel PCA (which operates implicitly via the kernel trick). The authors should explicitly state this constraint and discuss its implications for scalability or applicability.  
2. **Baseline comparisons**: For the toy examples (damped 1D system, Henon map), the paper should include performance metrics for standard DMD to clearly demonstrate the relative advantages of the proposed method. This would strengthen the empirical validation.  

The experimental section on artificial problems is limited. While the locomotion data application is a step toward real-world relevance, the comparison with DMD, PCA, and kernel PCA lacks depth—e.g., no discussion of computational costs or error metrics that would clarify *why* the proposed method is preferable. The authors should explicitly address how their approach outperforms or complements existing methods in these benchmarks.  

In summary, the paper presents a technically valid extension of kernelized dynamical systems analysis but fails to convincingly establish its value for machine learning. The theoretical framework is sound, but the work needs sharper focus on *why* ML researchers should care. Addressing the gaps in practical motivation, comparative baselines, and explicit limitations would significantly strengthen the contribution. The current results, particularly on real-world data, do not sufficiently demonstrate competitive advantages over standard techniques. The authors should reframe the narrative around concrete ML applications and provide clearer evidence of added value.
