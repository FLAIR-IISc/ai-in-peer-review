### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a method for the spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The authors develop an algorithm, which can be thought of as a kernelized version of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), to perform this analysis using finite data sequences from a nonlinear dynamical system. The core idea is to extend DMD—a technique traditionally used in fluid dynamics for modal decomposition—by leveraging the expressive power of kernel methods to better approximate the Koopman eigenfunctions. The proposed method is demonstrated on synthetic dynamical systems (a damped linear system and the Hénon map) and is applied to real-world motion capture data for tasks such as prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Connection to Prior Work:** The paper presents a well-executed and interesting extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to RKHSs. This "kernelization" is a natural step, placing DMD in the same conceptual framework as other successful kernel-based methods like kernel PCA and kernel ICA. The theoretical derivations are sound and clearly presented.

2.  **Clarity and Educational Value:** The paper is well-written and serves as a valuable introduction to Koopman operator theory and DMD for readers in the machine learning community who may be unfamiliar with these concepts from fluid dynamics.

3.  **Cross-Disciplinary Contribution:** The work successfully bridges concepts from physics and machine learning. Such efforts are commendable and can foster new research directions by introducing powerful analytical tools from one field to another.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Motivation and Significance for Machine Learning:** The primary weakness of the paper is its motivation from a machine learning perspective. While the connection to fluid dynamics is clear, the authors do not make a compelling case for why this particular form of analysis is needed or advantageous for typical machine learning problems. Most dynamical systems studied in ML (e.g., in reinforcement learning, robotics, or time-series forecasting) are inherently stochastic, involving process noise and measurement error. The paper focuses exclusively on deterministic systems, and it remains unclear how the proposed method would apply or what benefits it would offer in more standard, stochastic settings. A more thorough discussion of potential use cases where kernel DMD could outperform existing state-of-the-art methods in machine learning is needed. For example, in a field like robotics, what specific problems could be solved more effectively with Koopman spectral analysis compared to established techniques?

2.  **Predictable Theoretical Contribution:** While the mathematical derivations are sound, the core idea of applying the kernel trick to a method based on linear operators is a well-established pattern in machine learning. For readers familiar with the lineage of kernel methods (kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, etc.), the extension of DMD to an RKHS, while valuable, may not feel particularly surprising. The algorithm ultimately reduces to an eigendecomposition on Gram matrices, which follows a familiar template.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments could be strengthened in the following ways:
    *   **Missing Baseline:** A crucial baseline is missing from the toy examples. The performance of standard, linear DMD should be shown on the toy system and the Hénon map. This comparison would clearly demonstrate the practical benefits of the kernel extension in a controlled setting where linear methods might fail.
    *   **Clarity of Real-World Results:** The results on the real-world locomotion data feel preliminary. It is not entirely clear from the presentation (e.g., the MDS embedding in Figure 3) what new insights or superior performance the proposed method provides compared to simpler alternatives like applying standard PCA or kernel PCA directly to the trajectory data. A more quantitative comparison or a clearer qualitative analysis would be beneficial.

**Questions for the Authors**

*   In Section 3, it is not immediately clear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires an explicit representation of the basis functions in the feature space. If the method can operate solely on Gram matrices (as kernel PCA does), this is a significant advantage. Could the authors clarify this point? If an explicit representation is needed, it would be helpful to discuss the potential limitations this might impose.
