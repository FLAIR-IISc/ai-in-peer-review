This paper presents a spectral analysis framework leveraging the Koopman operator, formulated within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space, to tackle the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems using empirical data. The authors follow a logical progression, ultimately demonstrating efficiency gains through eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator in both synthetic and real-world applications. The introduced kernel construction represents a valuable contribution to the field, and the work is well-motivated with solid experimental validation. 

However, I have several points for the authors to consider. The relationship between this work and previous research, particularly Williams et al. [35], needs further clarification. A more detailed comparison highlighting the distinct contributions of this approach – beyond a standard Koopman operator-based framework – would be beneficial, as would a more in-depth discussion in Section 4. 

Given the breadth of kernel-based methods for nonlinear dynamical system analysis, the related work section appears incomplete. The authors should consider including relevant papers by researchers such as Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., Mouattamid & Schaback, and Franz & Schölkopf. 

While the Koopman operator is typically defined for deterministic systems, the applicability and performance of this method in stochastic environments – which are often more representative of real-world scenarios – warrants further investigation. Expanding upon the brief mention of stochasticity with additional discussion and a simple illustrative experiment would strengthen the paper. 

I would appreciate clarification regarding the implication of the assumption (line 216, page 5) that only a collection of observable pairs, related nonlinearly pointwise, is required for estimation. Specifically, how does the proposed kernel construction perform with multidimensional time series exhibiting potentially weak pointwise relationships? Furthermore, a discussion of the inherent causal assumptions within this framework would be valuable.

The experimental evaluation, while sound, could be more ambitious. The prediction task is fairly standard, and the "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" section lacks engagement with the extensive literature on time series clustering (e.g., kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods), including a comparative analysis. Similarly, the sequential change-point detection experiment would be more compelling with a comparison to state-of-the-art online kernel-based change detection methods, as the currently employed method is known to have limitations in complex dynamics. More comprehensive experiments are needed to fully support the claims.

Finally, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. I noted two minor issues: please verify the indices for τ-1 versus τ, and replace the dot with a comma in the kernel notation k(xi, xj) on the last equation of page 7.
