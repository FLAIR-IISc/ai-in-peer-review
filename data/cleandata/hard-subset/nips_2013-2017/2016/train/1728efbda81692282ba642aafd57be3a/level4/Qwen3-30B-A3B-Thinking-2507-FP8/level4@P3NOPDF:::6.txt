

The paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes an algorithm for its computation from dynamical system measurements. While the Koopman operator originates in fluid dynamics, the authors position their work as relevant to machine learning (ML), though this connection requires stronger justification. The method extends dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) via kernelization, drawing conceptual parallels to established kernelized techniques (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel ICA). The theoretical derivation is technically sound and well-executed, building predictably on the framework of RKHS and linear operators—resulting in a finite-dimensional eigendecomposition algorithm. The paper is clearly written and offers educational value for ML researchers unfamiliar with fluid dynamics concepts. However, the core contribution’s significance to ML remains underdeveloped, and experimental validation is insufficiently compelling.

The authors demonstrate their approach on two synthetic dynamical systems (damped linear 1D dynamics and the Henon map) and real-world locomotion data, comparing against DMD, PCA, and kernel PCA. The results on synthetic systems are somewhat ambiguous: while the method correctly identifies spectral properties, it is unclear whether the proposed approach consistently outperforms standard DMD (which should be included as a baseline for the toy examples). The locomotion data experiments yield "somewhat unclear results" with no clear metric showing superiority over baselines. Crucially, the paper fails to articulate *why* ML practitioners should care about this specific Koopman-based analysis. The authors posit a deterministic dynamical system framework (functions from *M* to *M*), yet ML typically engages with stochastic systems involving noise and uncertainty (e.g., in robotics or time-series forecasting). The work does not address how this deterministic setting aligns with prevalent ML applications, nor does it justify the method’s advantages over existing stochastic dynamical system models.

To strengthen the contribution, the authors must provide concrete use cases where their technique offers tangible benefits over state-of-the-art ML approaches. For instance, they should specify a domain like robotics where Koopman analysis would resolve a known limitation of current methods. *Why* would a robotics researcher choose this over, say, Gaussian processes or recurrent neural networks for state estimation? The paper should explicitly address: (1) *What problem does it solve better?* (2) *In what specific ML context is this advantageous?* (3) *How does it compare to the best available alternatives in that context?* Without this, the work risks being perceived as a theoretical extension of fluid dynamics tools without ML relevance.

The theoretical section is robust but not novel. The derivation follows standard kernelized operator principles (e.g., as in kernel PCA or kernel independence tests), and the resulting algorithm resembles existing kernelized DMD variants. The authors should clarify whether their approach introduces new mathematical insights or merely rephrases known techniques. A critical technical gap exists regarding computational requirements: it is unclear whether the modal decomposition algorithm necessitates explicit feature space representations (as opposed to kernel tricks). If explicit mappings are required, this represents a significant scalability limitation compared to kernel PCA, which operates entirely in the kernel space. The paper must explicitly state this constraint and discuss its implications for real-world ML applications.

Additionally, the experimental section lacks depth. The synthetic examples should include standard DMD as a direct baseline to demonstrate the added value of the RKHS extension. For the Henon map, the authors should quantify performance differences (e.g., reconstruction error, spectral accuracy) and clarify why the kernelized approach is preferable. The locomotion data analysis should explicitly link results to a concrete ML task (e.g., predicting gait transitions or identifying latent states) and benchmark against ML-specific baselines like LSTM or Kalman filters, not just generic dimensionality reduction methods.

In summary, the paper makes a technically competent contribution to Koopman theory but falls short in establishing its ML relevance. The educational value for cross-disciplinary readers is notable, yet the work does not convincingly argue for its adoption in ML practice. To merit publication, the authors must: (1) define a clear ML use case with a compelling justification for why this method is preferable to existing alternatives; (2) provide rigorous experimental validation against state-of-the-art ML baselines in that context; and (3) clarify the computational limitations (explicit feature space requirement) and its impact on scalability. Without these revisions, the paper risks being viewed as a niche extension of fluid dynamics methodology with limited ML impact.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept (with major revisions)**. The core ideas have potential but require substantial reorientation toward ML applications and empirical validation. The authors must address all points above to justify the paper’s significance for the ML community.
