This paper introduces a spectral analysis using the Koopman operator defined in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. The authors then develop algorithms to perform this analysis using finite-length data sequences from nonlinear dynamical systems, effectively reducing the problem to calculating orthogonal bases of the Krylov matrix in the RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. This approach aims to improve efficiency in analyzing both synthetic and real-world data. The paper presents a significant contribution by introducing a kernel-based construction for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems. The motivation is clear, and the experimental validation is well-executed. However, several points warrant further consideration.

It is not immediately clear what distinguishes this work from prior research, particularly Williams et al. [35], which already introduced Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for analyzing nonlinear system dynamics. A clearer discussion highlighting the specific differences and advantages of the proposed method compared to [35] would be beneficial. Furthermore, Section 4 could benefit from more detailed justification and explanation. 

The field of nonlinear dynamical system analysis with kernels is extensive, and some relevant references are missing. The authors should consider including works by Ralaivola and d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., Mouattamid and Schaback, and Franz and Schölkopf to provide a more comprehensive context.

Although the Koopman operator is generally defined for deterministic systems, the paper briefly touches upon its applicability to stochastic settings, which are often more realistic. Expanding this discussion and potentially including a simple illustrative experiment would strengthen the work.

The authors clarify a key assumption regarding the data—that consecutive observables are nonlinearly related (line 216, page 5). Further discussion on the implications of this assumption in multidimensional time series, where pointwise relations might be weak, would be valuable. Specifically, it would be helpful to understand how the proposed kernel construction performs in such scenarios and to elaborate on the inherent causal assumptions within the framework.

The experimental evaluation, while sound, could be more ambitious. The prediction via preimage approach is standard, and the “Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics” section lacks discussion of connections to—and experimental comparisons with—the extensive literature on time series clustering (e.g., kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods). Similarly, the sequential change-point detection experiment would be more compelling with a comparison to state-of-the-art online change detection methods using kernels. More rigorous experiments are expected to substantiate the proposed development.

*Minor: The paper is well-written and clear. Two minor typos were noted: please review the use of τ-1 vs. τ and replace the dot with a comma in k(xi.xj) on page 7 (last equation).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of reproducing kernels to Koopman spectral analysis.
*   Clear theoretical development and a well-defined algorithm.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Potential for applications in various fields, including prediction, pattern recognition, and change-point detection.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of differences from related work, particularly [35].
*   Potential for improvement in the experimental evaluation with more rigorous comparisons.
*   Further elaboration needed on the implications of key assumptions and the applicability to stochastic systems.
*   Missing citations to relevant literature in kernel-based dynamical system analysis.
