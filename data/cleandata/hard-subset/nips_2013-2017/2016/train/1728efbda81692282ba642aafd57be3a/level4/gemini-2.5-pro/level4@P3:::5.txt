### Review of "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis"

#### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to apply the principles of Koopman operator theory not on the raw, low-dimensional state space, but in a high-dimensional feature space induced by a kernel function. While standard DMD approximates the Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear combination of observables, the proposed Kernel DMD (KDMD) leverages the kernel trick to construct approximations from a much richer, nonlinear set of basis functions.

The authors formulate two algorithms. The first is a direct extension of DMD based on a Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization of the Krylov subspace in the RKHS. Recognizing the potential numerical instability of this approach—an issue familiar from standard DMD—they propose a second, more robust algorithm. This version projects the data onto a truncated set of principal components in the feature space (via Kernel PCA) before performing the decomposition. The paper establishes theoretical connections between the eigenvalues and eigenvectors computed by this procedure and the true Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions. Finally, the authors frame KDMD as a feature extraction technique and demonstrate its utility in several machine learning applications, including short-term prediction, recognition of dynamic patterns, and sequential change-point detection, using both synthetic and real-world data.

#### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its central idea. The kernelization of DMD is an elegant, powerful, and natural step forward for Koopman spectral analysis. It provides a principled way to move beyond the limitations of linear observables inherent in classic DMD, potentially enabling the analysis of a much broader and more complex class of nonlinear systems. This contribution has the potential for significant impact, not only within the machine learning community but also in the physical and engineering sciences where DMD is already a popular tool.

2.  **Theoretical Foundation:** The theoretical development appears to be sound. The paper provides a clear derivation of the method, grounded in the established theory of Koopman operators and RKHS. Theorems 1 and 2 formally connect the computed "empirical Ritz values" to the Koopman spectrum, justifying the proposed algorithms.

3.  **Potential for Broad Applicability:** By framing the method as a feature extraction tool, the authors correctly identify its potential beyond simple system analysis. The proposed applications—while briefly explored—convincingly suggest that the Koopman modes extracted by KDMD could serve as meaningful representations of dynamics for downstream machine learning tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation (Major Issue):** The paper's most significant weakness is its lack of clarity. The manuscript is written in an exceedingly dense and formal style that makes it very difficult to follow, even for a reader familiar with the constituent concepts (DMD, Koopman theory, kernels). Many key ideas are buried in overly complex mathematical notation where simpler, more intuitive explanations would be far more effective. For example, the sentence in the introduction, "Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point," could be stated more simply and directly. This opacity is a serious barrier to the paper's accessibility and potential impact, especially for the broad NIPS audience.

2.  **Underdeveloped Experimental Evaluation:** While the paper proposes several interesting applications in Section 7, the empirical validation for each is superficial. The experiments serve as proofs-of-concept rather than a rigorous evaluation of the method's performance. For instance:
    *   The prediction task lacks comparison to standard time-series forecasting baselines (e.g., Gaussian Process models).
    *   The dynamics recognition experiment shows a qualitative MDS plot but lacks quantitative classification accuracy metrics or comparison to other dynamic texture recognition methods.
    *   The change-point detection example is presented with a single, qualitative plot.
    This thin evaluation makes it difficult to assess the practical advantages of KDMD over existing techniques for these tasks.

3.  **Imbalance in Detail:** The paper's structure feels imbalanced. A great deal of space is dedicated to the mathematical derivations, while the crucial experimental and application sections are highly compressed. Section 6 ("Empirical Example") is very brief, and Section 7 rushes through three distinct and complex applications. A more effective paper might have focused on a single application and explored it in depth.

#### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Improve Readability:** I strongly urge the authors to perform a major revision focused on pedagogy and accessibility. The goal should be to communicate the core intuition behind the method before diving into the formalism. A clearer connection between the steps of the algorithm and their conceptual purpose would be invaluable.

*   **Expand and Strengthen Experiments:** The experimental section needs to be significantly expanded. For at least one of the proposed applications, the authors should provide a thorough, quantitative comparison against relevant and strong baselines. This would more convincingly demonstrate the value of the features extracted by KDMD.

*   **Discuss Kernel Selection:** The paper would benefit from a discussion on the practical issue of kernel selection and hyperparameter tuning. The experiments use kernels that are well-matched to the underlying dynamics (e.g., a polynomial kernel for a system with polynomial nonlinearities). A discussion of how the method performs when the "correct" kernel is not obvious would make the work more compelling.

*   **Clarify Relationship to Extended DMD (eDMD):** The paper mentions eDMD [35] as related work. This connection could be elaborated upon. Both methods enrich the basis functions used for approximation. The key distinction appears to be that eDMD uses an explicit, user-defined dictionary of functions, whereas KDMD uses an implicit, and potentially infinite-dimensional, dictionary via the kernel trick. Highlighting this relationship more clearly would help situate the paper's contribution.

#### Summary of Evaluation

*   **Quality:** The technical and theoretical aspects of the paper appear sound. However, the quality of the empirical evaluation is low and does not sufficiently support the claims of practical utility.
*   **Clarity:** The clarity is very poor. The dense, formal writing style makes the paper unnecessarily difficult to understand and is the primary reason for my recommendation.
*   **Originality:** The core idea is highly original and represents a significant conceptual advance over standard DMD.
*   **Significance:** The potential significance is very high. If presented clearly and validated thoroughly, this method could become a widely used tool.

#### Recommendation

This paper introduces a powerful and elegant idea with high potential. However, in its current form, the manuscript is not ready for publication at NIPS. The severe lack of clarity obscures the contribution, and the underdeveloped experimental section fails to provide convincing evidence of its practical value.

I believe this work could form the basis of an excellent and highly-cited paper. I therefore strongly encourage the authors to undertake a substantial revision focusing on improving the clarity of the presentation and providing a more rigorous and quantitative empirical evaluation. For these reasons, I recommend rejection at this time.
