This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs) and introduces an algorithm for performing this analysis using data from nonlinear dynamical systems. The Koopman operator, originating in fluid dynamics, is leveraged to provide a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics, potentially bridging techniques from that field with machine learning. The authors demonstrate their method on synthetic systems (a damped linear system and the Henon map) and apply it to locomotion data, comparing it with dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA. 

The work extends DMD by utilizing kernel methods, allowing for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions with richer, nonlinear basis functions – a conceptually sound and potentially valuable extension. The paper is well-written and provides a useful introduction to Koopman operator theory for machine learning researchers. It successfully positions the method within the broader context of spectral analysis and decomposition techniques. The theoretical derivations are rigorous and align with expectations given existing knowledge of kernel methods like kernel PCA and kernel ICA.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Extension:** The application of reproducing kernels to Koopman spectral analysis offers a principled extension of existing DMD techniques, addressing limitations in representing complex nonlinear dynamics.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly explains the underlying concepts, making it accessible to readers from diverse backgrounds.
*   **Rigorous Derivation:** The mathematical derivations are sound and logically presented, building upon established principles of RKHS theory.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors effectively relate their work to established techniques in both dynamical systems analysis (e.g., POD, DMD) and machine learning (e.g., kernel PCA).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Practical Demonstration:** While the experiments are illustrative, they primarily focus on relatively simple systems. The results on locomotion data are not entirely convincing and lack a clear performance comparison with baselines. A more in-depth evaluation on challenging real-world datasets is needed.
*   **Lack of Application Focus:** The paper lacks a strong articulation of specific machine learning applications where this technique offers a significant advantage. While the authors acknowledge potential benefits, a detailed discussion of potential use cases (e.g., in robotics, control, or time series forecasting) is missing.
*   **Computational Considerations:** The computational cost of working in the feature space is not thoroughly addressed.  The authors should more explicitly discuss the practical implications of needing to represent data in the RKHS, and how this might scale to high-dimensional data.
*   **Comparison with Standard DMD:** A direct comparison of the proposed method against standard DMD on the toy examples (damped linear system and Henon map) would help to clarify the advantages and disadvantages of the kernelized approach in simpler scenarios.
*   **Feature Space Representation:** The necessity of explicitly representing data in the feature space is not sufficiently discussed.  If this representation is required, it represents a limitation that should be acknowledged and potentially addressed with approximation strategies.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel extension of DMD with the potential to improve the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems.
*   The theoretical contribution is solid and well-justified.
*   The work introduces a potentially valuable tool for researchers in both dynamical systems and machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical impact of the method is not clearly demonstrated, and the presented experiments are limited.
*   The lack of a strong application focus weakens the motivation for the work.
*   The computational considerations and scalability of the method are not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution with the potential to advance the field of nonlinear dynamical systems analysis. However, the limited empirical evaluation and lack of a strong application focus raise concerns about its immediate practical impact.  I believe the paper is currently borderline.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with the request that the authors significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation, particularly by: 

1.  Including a direct comparison with standard DMD on the toy examples.
2.  Demonstrating the method's performance on more challenging real-world datasets.
3.  Providing a more detailed discussion of potential applications in relevant machine learning domains (e.g., robotics, control).
4.  Addressing the computational challenges associated with working in the feature space.
