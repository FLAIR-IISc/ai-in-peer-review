Summary
This paper studies Koopman spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The authors develop a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) procedure that operates on Gram matrices. Starting from data x0,…,xτ, they form the Krylov matrix in feature space, compute orthogonal bases via Gram–Schmidt, project the Koopman operator onto the resulting subspace, and obtain empirical Ritz values/vectors by eigendecomposition. They further propose a numerically robust variant that projects onto kernel-PCA (POD) directions before eigendecomposition. The learned modes/eigenvalues are then used as features for several downstream tasks: short-term prediction via a pre-image reconstruction, change-point detection using a CUSUM-style score, and dynamics recognition via subspace distances (kernel principal angles). Experiments on a toy system, the Hénon map, motion capture sequences, and a Lorenz-style change-point example illustrate the approach.

Relation to prior work
DMD provides data-driven approximations to Koopman spectra; variants such as exact DMD, optimized DMD, and extended DMD (EDMD) are well known. This paper’s main contribution is to perform DMD in RKHS using kernel machinery, and to stabilize it via kernel-PCA truncation, closely related in spirit to EDMD with kernel basis functions. The paper situates itself among DMD and subspace/POD literature and briefly discusses EDMD.

Quality
- The operator-theoretic framing is sound. Theorems connecting Ritz values/vectors in RKHS to Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions are clearly stated, with proofs that leverage QR/companion form and kernel Gram matrices.
- The robustified variant using kernel-PCA/POD is practically important and aligns with best practices in DMD (truncation/regularization).
- However, several technical points need clarification or qualification:
  - Conditions under which the finite-dimensional projection recovers the true eigenfunctions (assumptions on Φ, richness of data, ergodicity, invariant measures) should be made explicit; finite-sample approximation error bounds are not provided.
  - The statement that “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is not generally true outside measure-preserving settings; please qualify and cite the precise conditions (e.g., Mezić).
  - Numerical stability and regularization are only partially addressed. Guidance on rank truncation, Tikhonov regularization, and handling noise/outliers would improve robustness.
  - The pre-image step is heuristic. A discussion of when pre-images exist/are unique, and practical alternatives (e.g., regression decoders) would strengthen the prediction section.

Clarity
- The core idea and algorithms are understandable, and the kernelization is clearly operationalized through Gram matrices.
- The exposition would benefit from improved organization and tighter notation:
  - Clearly separate assumptions (ϕ in RKHS vs general Hilbert space), distinguish φj as Koopman eigenfunctions vs empirical modes, and unify notation for feature maps and bases.
  - Fix minor typos and grammar; ensure consistent equation numbering and cross-references; define all matrices on first use (e.g., 1τ, H).
  - Provide pseudocode-level summaries of both the basic and robust algorithms, with input/output and computational costs.

Originality
- Kernelizing DMD and projecting onto kernel-PCA directions is natural and closely related to EDMD with kernel dictionaries. Williams et al. (EDMD) already discuss kernelized EDMD and the connection to Koopman operator approximations on feature spaces. The paper acknowledges EDMD but underplays the overlap.
- The contributions here appear to be: a) a direct Krylov/Ritz derivation in RKHS; b) an explicit Gram–Schmidt/companion-form viewpoint; c) a pragmatic robustification via kernel-PCA; and d) demonstration across several applications. The conceptual novelty is moderate; the formulation and stabilization are useful.

Significance
- The approach offers a scalable, nonparametric, operator-theoretic tool for extracting dynamical features without choosing explicit dictionaries. This is potentially impactful for practitioners who want Koopman features with kernel flexibility.
- The empirical section demonstrates feasibility but lacks rigorous, quantitative evaluation:
  - Compare against exact/optimized DMD, EDMD (with polynomial/RBF dictionaries), and kernelized EDMD on the same tasks.
  - Report forecasting errors, eigenvalue/mode estimation errors (where ground truth is known), classification accuracy for dynamics recognition, and detection delay/ROC for change detection.
  - Provide sensitivity analyses to kernel choice/bandwidth, truncation rank, sequence length, and noise.

Pros
- Clear operator-theoretic framework linking Koopman analysis and kernels.
- Practical algorithm entirely in terms of Gram matrices; no explicit feature maps required.
- Robustified variant via kernel-PCA/POD aligns with established practice in DMD.
- Broad applicability demonstrated on synthetic and real data; code availability is a plus.

Cons
- Novelty relative to EDMD and kernel EDMD is limited and insufficiently differentiated.
- Limited quantitative evaluation and lack of comparisons to strong baselines.
- Some theoretical claims need qualification; no finite-sample guarantees or error analysis.
- Pre-image step for prediction is heuristic; practical guidance is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Position more explicitly against EDMD and kernel EDMD, clarifying what is new (the Krylov/Ritz derivation, computational or statistical benefits) and when it matters in practice.
- Add quantitative benchmarks with baselines (exact/optimized DMD, EDMD, kernel EDMD, and simple RNN/GPDM baselines for prediction; standard change-point detectors for detection).
- Provide guidelines for kernel selection and hyperparameter tuning (e.g., bandwidth selection, cross-validation), and include sensitivity analyses.
- Discuss computational complexity and memory costs, and consider regularization strategies (rank truncation, ridge) for noisy data.
- Clarify the spectral assumptions (when eigenvalues lie on the unit circle, continuous vs discrete spectrum) and conditions for identifiability.
- For prediction, consider a learned decoder (e.g., ridge regression from feature-space coefficients to state space) as an alternative to pre-image optimization, and report ablations.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and well-motivated application of kernel methods to Koopman spectral analysis with a useful robustification. The paper’s main weakness is limited novelty relative to existing EDMD/kernel-EDMD work and the lack of strong empirical comparisons. With improved positioning, quantitative evaluation, and clearer assumptions, the paper would make a valuable contribution. At present, I view it as borderline, leaning toward rejection unless the authors can strengthen the empirical and comparative evidence and clarify the relationship to EDMD.
