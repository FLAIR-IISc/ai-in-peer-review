### **Review**

**Summary**
This paper introduces Koopman Dynamic Mode Decomposition (KDMD), a novel extension of the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) method to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). By lifting the system's state into a high-dimensional feature space via a kernel map, the proposed method allows for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The algorithm is demonstrated on several illustrative examples. This approach represents a powerful generalization of DMD, enabling the use of feature maps to connect the analysis to specific characteristics of interest in the data.

**Strengths**
The central contribution of this work is significant. The proposed KDMD framework is an elegant and powerful generalization of DMD, providing a principled method for approximating the Koopman operator using a much richer class of basis functions than standard DMD. The ability to leverage the kernel trick to implicitly work in high-dimensional feature spaces is a compelling advantage. The core idea is strong, and if presented clearly, has the potential to be a highly impactful and widely cited contribution to the fields of dynamical systems and machine learning.

**Weaknesses**
Despite the strength of the core idea, the manuscript's primary weakness is its presentation, which currently hinders its accessibility and impact. The paper is difficult to read and would likely be challenging even for specialists in the field. The writing style often prioritizes dense mathematical formalism over clear, intuitive explanations, making the key contributions difficult to parse.

My major concerns are as follows:

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Pedagogical Value:** The manuscript often obscures simple concepts with unnecessarily complex notation or verbose descriptions. A clear example is the sentence: "Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point." This important connection could be stated much more simply and directly. This issue persists throughout the paper, making the core methodology less accessible than it needs to be.

2.  **Ineffective Paper Structure:** The organization of the manuscript could be significantly improved to create a more streamlined and logical narrative.
    *   **Section 4:** This section appears largely redundant, with much of its content already treated in the introduction. Removing it and integrating any essential points elsewhere would improve the paper's flow.
    *   **Section 5:** This section contains potentially valuable discussion points, but its impact is diminished by a lack of concrete examples or illustrations. To be effective, this material should be directly supported by figures and results, or its key insights should be condensed and moved to the conclusion.
    *   **Section 6:** In stark contrast to the preceding sections, this section is overly compressed. If it contains key results or applications, it requires substantial expansion to adequately convey its findings and their significance. Developing this section with the illustrative detail that Section 5 lacks would greatly strengthen the paper.

**Minor Points**

1.  There appears to be a typographical error in the description of the "NL" toy problem, as the system dynamics presented are, in fact, linear. Please verify the equations.

2.  The first two experimental examples are based on polynomial systems. This is an excellent setting to showcase the strengths of KDMD, particularly when an appropriate kernel (e.g., a polynomial kernel) can be chosen to match the underlying dynamics. The paper would be significantly strengthened by providing more detail on this connection. A discussion of the rationale behind kernel selection and how it relates to finding a finite-dimensional invariant subspace for the Koopman operator (as discussed in, e.g., Brunton et al., 2015) would provide a clearer link between the theory and its practical application.

**Recommendation and Justification**
While the technical contribution of this paper is novel and potentially very valuable, the manuscript in its current form is not ready for publication. The severe issues with clarity, presentation, and structure make the work inaccessible and obscure its important contributions.

For these reasons, I recommend rejecting the paper at this time. However, I want to emphasize that this recommendation is based almost entirely on the quality of the writing, not the quality of the ideas. I strongly encourage the authors to undertake a major revision with a focus on improving pedagogical clarity, simplifying the exposition, and restructuring the narrative. A rewritten version that successfully communicates its powerful ideas would constitute a significant and highly citable contribution to the NIPS community.
