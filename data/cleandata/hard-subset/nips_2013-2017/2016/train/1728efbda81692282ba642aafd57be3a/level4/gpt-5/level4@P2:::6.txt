Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) and proposes an algorithm to estimate Koopman spectra from finite data. Building on dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), the authors construct a Krylov subspace in feature space, perform a Gram–Schmidt QR factorization, and compute the eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projected onto that subspace. They also provide a more numerically stable variant by projecting onto kernel principal components (POD in feature space), and discuss applications including short-term prediction via preimage reconstruction, sequential change-point detection, and recognition/embedding of dynamical patterns via kernel principal angles.

This line of work connects to a substantial literature: Koopman operator theory and DMD in fluid dynamics and control, variants such as exact and optimized DMD, extended DMD (EDMD) with user-defined dictionaries, and kernel methods that “linearize” operators in feature space (e.g., kernel PCA/ICA, kernel independence measures, and kernel Bayes rule). The presented method can be seen as a kernelized extension of DMD/EDMD that replaces fixed dictionaries with an implicit RKHS basis and uses Gram matrices for computation.

Strengths:
- Clear exposition that bridges Koopman/DMD ideas and kernel methods; useful for ML readers less familiar with dynamical systems in physics.
- Mathematically consistent extension of DMD to RKHS, with a principled Krylov-subspace view and a practical stabilization via kernel PCA.
- Algorithmic steps are concrete and, in principle, kernelizable (Gram-matrix-based), and the paper illustrates uses on synthetic and real data (toy nonlinear system, Hénon map, locomotion sequences).
- The applications section is thought-provoking and highlights potential use cases (feature extraction, prediction, change detection, dynamics recognition).

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Novelty relative to EDMD and closely related kernelized Koopman approaches is not fully delineated. Please clarify what is fundamentally new beyond “using kernels” and how your method differs from EDMD with large dictionaries or kernel EDMD-style constructions.
- Empirical validation is limited and, at times, unclear. The real-data results (locomotion) would benefit from quantitative metrics and stronger baselines. The comparisons to standard DMD, PCA, and kernel PCA are not systematically presented.
- It is not entirely clear whether the proposed modal decomposition requires any explicit feature-map computations or is fully expressible via Gram matrices. If explicit feature representations are needed anywhere, that is a limitation compared to kernel PCA; please state clearly which steps are kernelized, the computational complexity, and memory requirements (e.g., O(τ2) storage for Gram matrices).
- The practical benefit for machine learning applications is not convincingly demonstrated. Many ML dynamical-system settings include stochasticity (process/measurement noise). Please clarify how the method handles noise and randomness beyond the brief remark on stochastic Koopman operators. Robustness to noise (e.g., on the toy and Hénon systems with added noise) would be valuable.
- The prediction via preimage relies on an MDS-like reconstruction. Preimage methods can be unstable and kernel-dependent; more details, ablations, and error metrics would help.
- Scalability and conditioning: What are the limits on τ (sequence length) and p (effective rank)? How does truncation level in the kernel-POD step affect accuracy and stability?
- Some results are presented qualitatively (e.g., Figure 1). Please include numerical errors for eigenvalues/eigenfunctions/modes, reconstruction errors, and classification accuracy for dynamics recognition, with confidence intervals where possible.

Specific suggestions:
- Add direct comparisons with standard DMD and EDMD on the toy system and Hénon map, reporting eigenvalue/mode errors, reconstruction error, and robustness to noise.
- Provide quantitative results on the locomotion dataset (e.g., classification accuracy using kernel principal angles), and compare against DMD-, PCA-, and kernel PCA-based subspace methods.
- Clarify the algorithmic pipeline: exact kernelized steps, use of Gram matrices, conditioning strategies (e.g., regularization), runtime and memory complexity, and sensitivity to kernel choice/width.
- Discuss concrete ML use cases where Koopman spectral features offer practical advantages (e.g., robotics, control, anomaly detection). What can a practitioner gain over current state-of-the-art models in those domains?
- Expand the positioning relative to EDMD and any prior kernelized Koopman work. A short comparison table or structured paragraph would help.
- For the change-point detection section, provide a clearer derivation, justify approximations, and show detection delay/false-alarm trade-offs versus strong kernel baselines.

Evaluation:
- Quality: The derivations appear sound and build naturally from DMD/Koopman theory and kernel methods. However, empirical support is currently modest and lacks strong baselines and quantitative metrics.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and pedagogical; some algorithmic details (kernelization, preimage, complexity) need clarification.
- Originality: The kernelized DMD/Koopman framing is a reasonable extension, but feels incremental given existing EDMD and kernel operator literature. The precise novelty should be sharpened.
- Significance: Potentially useful as a feature-extraction tool for nonlinear dynamics, but current experiments do not yet demonstrate clear advantages or impact in ML practice.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear and instructive connection between Koopman/DMD and RKHS methods.
- Conceptually appealing kernelized algorithm with stabilization via kernel PCA.
- Interesting application ideas; code availability aids reproducibility.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incremental novelty relative to EDMD/kernel operator approaches.
- Limited and partly qualitative empirical evaluation; unclear real-world ML advantages.
- Missing strong baselines and quantitative comparisons; unclear scalability and robustness.

Overall, I am sympathetic to the goal of bringing Koopman methods into ML, but the paper would benefit from stronger empirical evidence, clearer positioning relative to EDMD and kernelized variants, and explicit guidance for practitioners on when and why to use this approach.
