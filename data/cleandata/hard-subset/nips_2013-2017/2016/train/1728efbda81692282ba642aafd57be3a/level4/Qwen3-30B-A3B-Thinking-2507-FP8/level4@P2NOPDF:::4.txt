

This paper presents a compelling extension of Koopman spectral analysis to the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) framework, offering a novel approach to modeling nonlinear dynamical systems. The method is rigorously evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating clear empirical benefits. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, with logical flow, precise technical exposition, and effective presentation of results. The contributions are significant for advancing Koopman-based methods in nonlinear dynamics, particularly through the integration of kernel techniques to handle complex state-space structures.  

To further strengthen the work, the authors could address two key aspects. First, a direct comparison with standard Koopman spectral analysis (i.e., non-RKHS approaches) in the simulation experiments would provide crucial context for the proposed method’s advantages. Such a comparison should include quantitative metrics (e.g., reconstruction error, spectral accuracy) and qualitative insights into when kernel-based extensions yield substantial gains over classical methods. This would help readers assess the practical necessity of the RKHS framework and clarify the scope of the method’s improvements.  

Second, the paper would benefit from a systematic investigation of kernel selection and its impact on results. While the authors employ specific kernels (e.g., Gaussian), a deeper exploration of how kernel choice—particularly hyperparameter tuning, kernel type (e.g., polynomial vs. radial basis), and feature-space mapping—affects the quality of the Koopman decomposition would be valuable. For instance, discussing trade-offs between kernel flexibility and computational cost, or analyzing sensitivity to kernel parameters via ablation studies, would offer practical guidance for practitioners. This could be integrated into the simulation section to illustrate concrete effects on performance.  

These additions would not only address potential limitations but also enhance the paper’s utility for the broader machine learning and dynamical systems communities. The current work is strong, and these refinements would elevate it to an even more impactful contribution. Overall, the paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a meaningful step toward scalable Koopman analysis. I recommend acceptance pending these minor clarifications.
