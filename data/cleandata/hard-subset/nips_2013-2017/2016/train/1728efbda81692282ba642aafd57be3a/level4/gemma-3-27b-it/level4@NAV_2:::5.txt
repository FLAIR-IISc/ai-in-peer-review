The manuscript, “Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis,” presents an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to utilize reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). This allows for a more flexible approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions compared to traditional DMD, which relies on linear monomials. The resulting algorithm, termed KDMD, is demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world data. This work represents a valuable generalization of DMD and has the potential to broaden its applicability to a wider range of nonlinear systems.

The paper begins by clearly outlining the motivation and context, positioning the work within the broader field of spectral analysis of dynamical systems and relating it to prior work such as Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) and other DMD variants [25, 26, 33]. The introduction effectively highlights the limitations of POD and standard DMD, setting the stage for the proposed kernel-based approach. The mathematical formulation of the Koopman operator and its extension to RKHS is presented rigorously, and Theorem 1 provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The inclusion of a robustification technique, addressing potential ill-conditioning issues via projection onto POD directions (Section 4), demonstrates an awareness of practical implementation challenges and a commitment to the algorithm's usability. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical soundness, its clear connection to established methods like DMD, and the potential for increased expressiveness afforded by the use of kernels. The detailed mathematical derivations are well-presented and contribute to the understanding of the underlying principles. The inclusion of empirical examples, although limited in scope, provides a preliminary validation of the method. The thorough discussion of related works (Section 5) demonstrates a good understanding of the current state of the field.

However, the paper’s clarity is a significant weakness. The writing style is often dense and mathematically focused, potentially hindering comprehension for readers not intimately familiar with the intricacies of Koopman operator theory and RKHS. While mathematical rigor is appreciated, greater emphasis on intuitive explanations and simplified descriptions would improve accessibility.  For instance, complex sentences like, "Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point," could be substantially streamlined.  Section 4, detailing the robustification technique, feels somewhat disconnected from the main flow of the paper and could potentially be integrated more seamlessly into the presentation or removed to maintain focus. The discussion of related works in Section 5 is comprehensive, but lacks sufficient integration into the broader narrative of the paper; elaborating on how KDMD differentiates itself from these works would strengthen the contribution.  Finally, the empirical evaluation (Sections 6 & 7) is relatively limited and lacks a detailed comparative analysis against existing methods. The toy examples, while illustrative, do not fully demonstrate the advantages of KDMD, and the application examples could benefit from more comprehensive results and discussion.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel extension of DMD using RKHS.
*   The method has the potential to improve the accuracy and applicability of Koopman spectral analysis for a wider range of nonlinear systems.
*   The inclusion of a robustification technique demonstrates practical considerations.
*   The related work section shows awareness of the existing literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper's clarity and writing style are significant weaknesses, potentially hindering comprehension.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited and lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing methods.
*   Section 4 feels somewhat disjointed and may not be essential to the core contribution.
*   The applications section could be significantly expanded to demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method.



Overall, while the technical contribution is promising, the paper requires substantial revision to improve its clarity and impact. I recommend rejection in its current form but encourage resubmission after addressing the concerns outlined above. A more accessible and comprehensive presentation, coupled with a more thorough empirical evaluation, would significantly strengthen the manuscript and increase its potential for acceptance.
