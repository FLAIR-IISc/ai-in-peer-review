This paper presents a novel method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending the popular Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) framework to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). Standard DMD approximates the Koopman operator using a linear dictionary of observables. In contrast, this work leverages the representational power of kernels to implicitly use a much richer, nonlinear set of basis functions, allowing for a more accurate approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions. The authors present a core algorithm for this "kernel DMD" and a robustified version that projects data onto principal components in the feature space, analogous to how standard DMD is often combined with Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD). The utility of the method is demonstrated on several machine learning tasks, including state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition, using both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and logically structured. The theoretical motivation for extending DMD to RKHS is well-argued, and the mathematical derivations are easy to follow. This makes the core contribution highly accessible.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is a natural and technically sound generalization of DMD. The authors provide a principled framework, complete with theoretical justification (Theorems 1 and 2), for performing Koopman analysis in feature space. The connection drawn to kernel PCA for robustification is insightful and practical.

3.  **Significance:** The work addresses the important problem of extracting meaningful features from nonlinear dynamical systems. By providing a data-driven method to approximate Koopman modes with nonlinear observables, the paper offers a powerful tool for system analysis. The demonstrated applications successfully highlight the potential of this method as a versatile feature extraction technique for complex time-series data.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the paper presents a valuable contribution, the experimental evaluation could be significantly strengthened to make a more compelling case for the proposed method. My main concerns are:

1.  **Lack of Baseline Comparisons:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method works on a few examples, but they do not establish *when* or *why* the kernel-based approach is superior to existing, simpler methods. A crucial omission is the lack of any direct comparison against standard (non-kernel) DMD or its modern variants (e.g., Extended DMD). Including such baselines in the experiments (for instance, on the toy system and the Hénon map) is essential. A clear demonstration of a scenario where standard DMD fails but kernel DMD succeeds would provide much stronger evidence for the utility of this work.

2.  **Kernel Selection:** A significant practical challenge for all kernel-based methods is the choice of the kernel function and its associated hyperparameters (e.g., the width of a Gaussian kernel or the degree of a polynomial kernel). This choice can have a profound impact on the performance. The manuscript currently lacks a discussion on this critical aspect. The paper would be greatly improved by including an analysis of the method's sensitivity to kernel selection. Providing some guidance, or even an empirical study on how to choose an appropriate kernel for a given dynamical system, would substantially increase the paper's practical value and reproducibility for other researchers and practitioners.

In summary, this is a well-written paper presenting a technically sound and interesting extension of DMD. The primary weakness lies in the experimental validation, which currently does not sufficiently demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches. Addressing the points above, particularly by adding baseline comparisons, would make this a much stronger paper.
