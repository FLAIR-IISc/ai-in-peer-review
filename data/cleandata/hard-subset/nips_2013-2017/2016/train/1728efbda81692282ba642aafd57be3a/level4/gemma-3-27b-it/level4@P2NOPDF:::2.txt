This paper introduces a spectral method for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems based on the Koopman operator, which provides a linear operator representation of function composition. The authors apply Koopman operator decomposition to the second-order forward dynamics of a system, specifically examining trajectories generated by iterating the nonlinear function *f* twice (x(t+2) = f(f(x(t)))). By leveraging techniques reminiscent of kernel Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and subspace analysis, they perform an eigen-decomposition of this operator from observed system trajectories. The proposed method is then demonstrated on embedding and recognition tasks, with results indicating strong performance. 

For readers familiar with kernel methods, the key contribution lies in framing function composition as a linear operator amenable to decomposition – a particularly effective application given that dynamical systems inherently represent repeated function composition. While Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) already exists within the dynamical systems literature, this work’s novelty primarily stems from its kernelized approach. 

However, the paper’s applications feel somewhat uncompelling in fully demonstrating the advantages of this method.  Alternative approaches, such as recurrent neural networks, already offer solutions for dynamical system recognition and change detection.  The paper doesn't convincingly establish why this Koopman-based method is superior or uniquely suited to these tasks compared to existing techniques.  The novelty, while present in the kernelization of DMD, appears incremental and the benefits are not clearly articulated through the presented applications.
