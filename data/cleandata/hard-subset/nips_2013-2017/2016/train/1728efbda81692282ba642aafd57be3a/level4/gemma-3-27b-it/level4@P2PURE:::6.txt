This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces and introduces an algorithm for performing this analysis based on data from dynamical systems. While originating in fluid dynamics, the authors suggest the Koopman operator framework holds relevance for machine learning. The proposed algorithm and analysis are demonstrated on a damped linear 1D system, a chaotic map, and real-world locomotion data, with comparisons to dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA yielding somewhat inconclusive results. The work represents an interesting extension of DMD, conceptually similar to prior “kernelization” efforts applied to linear operators – such as kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, and Gaussian process bandits. The experimental evaluation primarily focuses on relatively artificial problems.

The paper is well-written and serves as a valuable introduction to concepts from fluid dynamics for machine learning researchers. I am generally supportive of efforts bridging techniques across disciplines. This approach appears to decompose functions from M to M that define a dynamical system, without incorporating stochastic elements. While I am not currently aware of prevalent machine learning applications utilizing such deterministic dynamical systems (most commonly involving innovations or noise), my assessment may be incomplete. Currently, the authors need to more convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of their techniques to a machine learning audience. Specifically, articulating potential use cases and benchmarking against state-of-the-art methods is crucial.

Given the importance of inference and learning in dynamical systems within fields like robotics, it would be helpful to understand what advantages Koopman analysis offers to roboticists. Alternatively, the authors could focus on another relevant domain. The theoretical derivations are solid, but unsurprising given familiarity with kernel PCA, kernel ICA, or related kernel methods like kernel independence tests or kernel Bayes rule. The approach effectively leverages the properties of linear operators in RKHSs, resulting in algorithms that perform eigendecomposition on finite-dimensional matrices. 

Regarding implementation details, it remains unclear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires explicit representation in the feature space. If so, this represents a limitation compared to kernel PCA and should be explicitly acknowledged. Furthermore, demonstrating the performance of standard DMD on the toy example and Henon map would help clearly illustrate the differences between the proposed method and existing techniques in a simplified setting.
