This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors develop an algorithm to perform this analysis using finite-length data sequences generated from a nonlinear system. The algorithm is based on the calculation of a set of orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and the eigendecomposition of the projection of the Koopman operator onto the subspace spanned by the bases. The proposed algorithm provides a modal decomposition of the dynamics into a finite number of modes, which can be considered as a feature extraction procedure for nonlinear dynamical systems.

The authors relate their work to previous research in the field, including dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) and proper orthogonal decomposition (POD). They also discuss the limitations of existing methods and highlight the advantages of their approach. The paper is well-written, and the theoretical derivations are clear and concise.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their algorithm using two simple dynamical systems, a damped linear 1D dynamics and a chaotic map. They also apply their method to real-world locomotion data and compare the performance with standard dynamic mode decomposition, PCA, and kernel PCA. However, the results are somewhat unclear, and it is difficult to evaluate the performance of the proposed method.

The main strength of the paper is its educational value for people foreign to concepts used in fluid dynamics. The authors provide a clear introduction to the Koopman operator and its relevance to machine learning. However, to convince machine learning readers of the practical usefulness of these techniques, the authors need to do more work. They should provide more insights into potential use cases and demonstrate how their method can compete with state-of-the-art techniques in various domains.

Some minor points to consider:

* In the modal decomposition algorithm, it is not clear whether an explicit representation in feature space is required. If so, this is a limitation compared to kernel PCA, and the authors should make this more explicit.
* It would be beneficial to show the performance of standard dynamic mode decomposition on the toy example and the Henon map to demonstrate the differences in this simple setup.
* The authors should provide more details on the computational complexity of their algorithm and its scalability to large datasets.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is clearly written, and the organization is logical. However, the originality of the work is somewhat limited, as it builds upon existing research in kernel methods and dynamic mode decomposition. The significance of the paper is also limited, as it is not clear how the proposed method can be applied to real-world problems in machine learning.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel extension of dynamic mode decomposition using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces.
* The authors provide a clear introduction to the Koopman operator and its relevance to machine learning.
* The paper has educational value for people foreign to concepts used in fluid dynamics.

Arguments for rejection:

* The paper lacks clear insights into potential use cases and the practical usefulness of the proposed method.
* The results are somewhat unclear, and it is difficult to evaluate the performance of the proposed method.
* The originality of the work is limited, as it builds upon existing research in kernel methods and dynamic mode decomposition.
