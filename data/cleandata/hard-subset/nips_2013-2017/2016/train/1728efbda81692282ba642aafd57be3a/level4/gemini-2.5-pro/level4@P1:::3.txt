This paper presents a method for the spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The authors propose a modal decomposition algorithm, which serves as a kernel-based extension of the popular Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) technique. The core of the method involves calculating an orthogonal basis for the Krylov subspace in the RKHS, followed by an eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projected onto this subspace. The paper also introduces a robustified version of the algorithm using a projection onto POD (kernel PCA) bases and demonstrates the approach across several applications, including state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

**Strengths:**

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and presents the underlying theoretical concepts in a coherent manner. The proposed framework for extending DMD using the kernel trick is elegant and offers an interesting contribution. The work appears technically sound, and the connections to DMD, the Koopman operator, and kernel methods are well-established. The experiments, while not providing exhaustive comparisons to state-of-the-art alternatives, effectively illustrate the method's capabilities on a variety of tasks and demonstrate its practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**

My primary concerns relate to the clarity of some key theoretical derivations, particularly in Sections 3 and 4. I would ask the authors to clarify the following points to improve the manuscript:

1.  **Definition of the Koopman Operator in RKHS:** There appears to be an ambiguity in the definition provided around Equation (7). This equation defines the action of K<sub>H</sub> on the feature map `ϕ`, where `ϕ: M -> H`. However, the text also states that "the Koopman operator K<sub>H</sub> is a linear operator in H" (i.e., `K_H: H -> H`). These two descriptions seem inconsistent. Could the authors please clarify the precise definition of the operator, including its domain and codomain?

2.  **Proof of Theorem 2:** The derivation in the proof of Theorem 2 was difficult to follow. The step beginning with "Since K<sub>H</sub>φ<sub>j</sub> = λ<sub>j</sub>φ<sub>j</sub>, we have ⟨ϕ(f(x)), κ<sub>j</sub>⟩ = ..." is confusing. It is unclear how the first equality implies the second. If the intention is to apply the operator K<sub>H</sub>, this would not be well-defined on the scalar-valued function `φ_j`. A more detailed explanation of this step would greatly improve the paper's clarity.

3.  **Assumptions of Theorems 1 & 2:** The assumptions underlying the theorems could be stated more explicitly and discussed in greater detail. For instance, Theorem 1 assumes distinct Ritz values, but the proof seems to rely on other implicit conditions (e.g., regarding the invertibility of matrices). A discussion of when these assumptions are likely to hold in practice, and how restrictive they might be, would significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

**Minor Points:**

I also noticed a few minor typographical errors throughout the text (e.g., a duplicated "the" in the introduction). A careful proofreading would be beneficial.
