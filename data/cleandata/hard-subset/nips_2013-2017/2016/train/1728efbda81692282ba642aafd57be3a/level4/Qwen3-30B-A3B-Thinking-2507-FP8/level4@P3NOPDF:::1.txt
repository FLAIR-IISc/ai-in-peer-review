

This paper presents a kernel-based spectral analysis framework for nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The authors derive a kernelized Koopman operator, perform its eigendecomposition, and demonstrate empirical efficiency gains across synthetic and real-world datasets. The contribution is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and experimentally validated, making it a valuable addition to the literature on data-driven dynamical systems analysis. However, several critical issues require clarification to strengthen the paper鈥檚 novelty and impact.  

**Key concerns:**  
1. **Novelty vs. Williams et al. (2015):** The paper鈥檚 core methodology bears significant resemblance to Williams et al.鈥檚 data-driven Koopman approximation (J. Nonlinear Sci. 2015), which already introduced Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for nonlinear dynamics. The authors must explicitly articulate the *substantial* distinction between their kernelized approach and this foundational work. For instance, does the RKHS embedding enable new capabilities (e.g., handling non-smooth dynamics, improved generalization) beyond the linear DMD framework? Section 4鈥檚 discussion lacks this critical comparison, weakening the novelty claim.  

2. **Missing literature context:** The field of kernel-based dynamical systems analysis is well-established (e.g., Ralaivola & d鈥橝lch茅-Buc, 2005; Tuia et al., 2014; Rojo-Alvarez et al., 2015; Mouatamid & Schaback, 2016). The paper omits key references that contextualize the proposed kernel construction. Including these would demonstrate a deeper engagement with the literature and clarify the work鈥檚 positioning.  

3. **Stochastic dynamics:** The Koopman operator is typically defined for deterministic systems, yet real-world dynamics are often stochastic. The brief mention of stochastic settings (Section 4) is insufficient. The authors should discuss how their method handles noise, uncertainty, or probabilistic transitions鈥攑erhaps via a synthetic experiment comparing performance on deterministic vs. stochastic trajectories.  

4. **Assumptions and causality:** The remark on line 216 (p.5) states the method requires "a collection of pairs of consecutive observables, where each pair is nonlinear pointwise related." This assumption needs rigorous justification, especially for high-dimensional time series with weak pointwise dependencies (e.g., chaotic systems). How does the kernel construction mitigate noise or partial correlations? Additionally, the causal assumption (that x_{t+1} is *caused by* x_t) should be explicitly addressed鈥攄oes the framework support reverse causality or latent variable scenarios?  

**Experimental limitations:**  
The empirical evaluation lacks ambition and comparative depth.  
- *Prediction experiments* (Section 5.1) follow standard embedding approaches without novel validation (e.g., no comparison to state-of-the-art nonlinear forecasting methods like LSTM or SINDy).  
- *Clustering (Section 5.2)* fails to connect to the vast literature on kernel-based time-series clustering (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernel methods). A discussion of how the Koopman approach differs from or complements these techniques鈥攁nd empirical comparisons鈥攚ould strengthen the contribution.  
- *Change-point detection (Section 5.3)* uses a standard kernel-based online method but does not benchmark against modern alternatives (e.g., online kernel change detection via adaptive kernels or sequential hypothesis testing). The claim that the method "outperforms" others is unsubstantiated without such comparisons. More rigorous experiments are needed to justify the proposed framework鈥檚 advantages.

**Overall assessment:**  
The paper proposes a technically sound kernelized Koopman framework with promising empirical results. However, the failure to distinguish clearly from Williams et al. (2015), the omission of key literature, the underdeveloped discussion of stochastic dynamics and assumptions, and the lack of rigorous experimental comparisons significantly undermine the claimed novelty. Addressing these points would substantially elevate the work. As currently written, the contribution risks being perceived as incremental rather than transformative.
