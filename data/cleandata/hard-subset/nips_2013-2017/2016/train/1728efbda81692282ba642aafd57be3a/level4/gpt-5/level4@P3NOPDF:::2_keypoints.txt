- Summary: Proposes a kernel-based spectral (Koopman) approach to analyze nonlinear dynamics via eigendecomposition from trajectory data, applied to embeddings and recognition.

- Strength: Conceptual clarity connecting nonlinear evolution to linear spectral analysis on observables.

- Strength: Methodologically natural use of kernel methods (kernel PCA/subspace ID) to approximate the operator in feature space.

- Strength: Practical linear-algebraic pipeline (eigendecomposition) yields interpretable modes/embeddings for recognition/change detection.

- Strength: Empirical results show promising embeddings and recognition accuracy on presented tasks.

- Weakness: Limited novelty—appears to be kernelization of existing DMD/Koopman methods; relation to Extended/Kernel/Subspace DMD unclear.

- Weakness: Insufficient baselines—omits strong alternatives (RNNs/LSTMs, state-space models, HMMs, ARIMA/ARX, standard/extended DMD).

- Weakness: Unclear formulation—conflates state evolution with Koopman operator and gives weak motivation for two-step composition.

- Weakness: Lacks theoretical guarantees—no consistency, error bounds, sample complexity, or noise analysis.

- Weakness: Sensitivity/robustness not analyzed—dependence on kernel choice, regularization, number of components, trajectory length, and noise is unknown.

- Weakness: Scalability concerns—kernel eigendecomposition and Gram matrices may be computationally/memory expensive; no discussion of low-rank approximations.

- Weakness: Empirical scope limited—datasets and tasks do not convincingly show advantages over existing methods.

- Question: What is the precise novelty relative to standard/extended/kernel/subspace DMD and which algorithmic components are new?

- Question: Why focus on two-step composition; does multi-step composition help and how does it affect estimation/stability?

- Question: How sensitive is performance to kernel choice, kernel parameters, and regularization—provide sensitivity/ablation analyses?

- Question: Are there theoretical guarantees (convergence, identifiability, noise bounds) for the proposed estimator?

- Question: How does the method compare quantitatively to strong baselines (RNNs/LSTMs, state-space models, standard/extended DMD)?

- Question: What are the computational complexity and memory requirements; can it scale via Nyström or random features?

- Suggestion: Improve positioning with a thorough related-work comparison and clear statements of novelty vs existing DMD/Koopman kernels.

- Suggestion: Clarify formalism—define Koopman on observables, distinguish state vs function space, and justify multi-step composition.

- Suggestion: Strengthen experiments with strong baselines, sensitivity analyses, noise/limited-data robustness, and more challenging/real-world datasets.

- Suggestion: Address practicality—discuss computational costs, scaling strategies (low-rank kernels, minibatches), provide pseudocode and release code/data.

- Suggestion: Add theory where possible—consistency or error bounds under reasonable assumptions (ergodicity, richness of observables, bounded noise).

- Minor: Tighten exposition to avoid implying the Koopman operator acts on states.

- Minor: Clarify what “recognition” means (system identity, regime, or parameter setting) and downstream use of embeddings.

- Minor: Proofread and streamline notation to reduce ambiguity.

- Overall assessment: Promising kernel-based Koopman approach but limited novelty and insufficient theoretical/empirical support; needs stronger comparisons, clarity, and analysis — Score: 5 (borderline).
