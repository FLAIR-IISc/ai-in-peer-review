Summary
This paper studies Koopman spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The authors propose a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) procedure that (i) constructs a Krylov subspace in feature space, (ii) computes an orthonormal basis via Gram–Schmidt/QR using only Gram matrices, and (iii) obtains empirical Ritz values/vectors by eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. They also present a more numerically robust variant that projects onto kernel PCA directions (analogous to POD-truncated DMD), enabling truncation when the Gram matrix is nearly rank-deficient. The method yields a finite set of Koopman modes/eigenvalues and can be used as a feature extraction tool.

Applications include short-term prediction via a pre-image construction, sequential change-point detection using a CUSUM-style statistic derived from a kernel exponential family model, and recognition/embedding of dynamical patterns based on kernel principal angles between subspaces spanned by estimated Koopman eigenfunctions. Empirical illustrations cover a toy system with known eigenfunctions, the Hénon map, locomotion sequences (embedding/recognition), and change detection on a Lorenz map. The paper positions itself relative to DMD and its variants and provides MATLAB code.

Relation to prior work
DMD approximates Koopman spectra from data; exact/optimized DMD address numerical issues; extended DMD (EDMD) estimates Koopman operators on user-chosen dictionaries and includes kernel variants by implicitly using infinite-dimensional features. The proposed approach closely aligns with this line: it performs DMD-like computation in RKHS, leveraging Gram matrices and kernel PCA for robustness. The applications (prediction, recognition, change detection) leverage features derived from Koopman modes; alternative approaches include subspace identification and spectral learning methods as well as recurrent neural networks and Gaussian process dynamical models.

Strengths
- Clear framing within the Koopman/operator-theoretic viewpoint; the kernelization is natural and technically sound.
- Derivations are explicit, including connections between Ritz values/vectors in RKHS and Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions, and a robust formulation via kernel PCA (POD).
- The approach is practical: computations reduce to Gram matrices; truncation mitigates ill-conditioning; code is provided.
- The method is versatile and demonstrated on prediction, dynamics recognition via subspace angles, and change-point detection.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: DMD and EDMD (including kernelized variants) already capture the central idea of approximating Koopman spectra using richer function classes; the main contribution here is largely “kernelizing” DMD and adopting POD-style stabilization in feature space.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and largely qualitative. There are few datasets, minimal quantitative metrics, and no systematic comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., EDMD/kernel EDMD, exact/optimized DMD, RNN/GPDM for prediction, or established kernel change-detection methods).
- The pre-image step for prediction is heuristic and may be unstable; no quantitative evaluation of prediction accuracy vs. alternatives is provided.
- The change-detection derivation simplifies the likelihood ratio by dropping terms; the impact of this approximation is not analyzed, and performance is not compared to standard kernel change-point detectors.
- Important practical aspects remain unclear: choice of kernel and hyperparameters, sensitivity to noise, computational complexity and scalability (time/memory) with increasing τ and sample size, and conditions under which the assumptions in Theorems 1–2 hold in practice.

Quality
The mathematical development is generally correct and well-grounded. Theorems linking the projected operator’s eigendecomposition to Koopman spectra are appropriate. However, the empirical section does not convincingly establish superiority or robustness: there is little error analysis, no ablation on truncation rank, and no baseline comparisons. The change-detection and pre-image components would benefit from more rigorous justification and experiments.

Clarity
The paper is mostly well organized and readable, though some sentences could be tightened, and a few typos/phrases impede clarity (e.g., “robustly it”). The algorithms would benefit from explicit pseudocode, a brief complexity analysis, and practical guidance on kernel selection and truncation. The applications section would be clearer with quantitative metrics and baseline comparisons.

Originality
The core idea—approximating Koopman spectra using richer (kernel) feature spaces and stabilizing via POD/kernel PCA—has significant overlap with EDMD and kernel EDMD. The paper contributes a coherent RKHS-based derivation and algorithmic presentation, but the conceptual novelty beyond prior DMD/EDMD variants appears limited.

Significance
Koopman-based, kernelized feature extraction is potentially useful, but the paper does not yet demonstrate compelling advantages over existing methods. With stronger empirical validation (including comparisons to EDMD/kernel EDMD and modern learning baselines) and clearer guidance on practical deployment, the approach could be a useful addition to the practitioner’s toolbox.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the distinction from EDMD/kernel EDMD, exact/optimized DMD, and related methods; provide head-to-head comparisons on shared benchmarks.
- Report quantitative metrics (e.g., eigenvalue/mode estimation error on systems with known spectra, prediction RMSE, recognition accuracy, detection delay/false alarm rate).
- Analyze computational complexity and scaling; discuss memory/time vs. sample size and τ; include results on larger datasets.
- Provide guidance on kernel/hyperparameter selection and truncation; examine sensitivity and robustness to noise.
- Justify the pre-image approach and the CUSUM approximation more thoroughly, or compare to established alternatives.
- Improve writing in a few places and fix minor typos.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid operator-theoretic framework with clear RKHS-based derivation.
- Practical, Gram-matrix-based algorithm with a robust variant and available code.
- Versatile applications and intuitive connections to feature extraction.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to EDMD/kernel EDMD and DMD variants.
- Empirical evaluation is not compelling: few datasets, no strong baselines, minimal quantitative analysis.
- Practical considerations (scalability, hyperparameter selection, robustness) are insufficiently addressed.

Overall, while the kernelized Koopman/DMD perspective is sensible and the derivations are sound, the contribution currently feels incremental and under-evaluated. A more thorough empirical study and clearer differentiation from existing EDMD/kernel approaches would substantially strengthen the paper.
