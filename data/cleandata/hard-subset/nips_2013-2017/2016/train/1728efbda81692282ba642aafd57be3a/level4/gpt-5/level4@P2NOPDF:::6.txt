This paper develops a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes a data-driven algorithm to perform this analysis from measurement data. The authors illustrate the approach on a damped 1D linear system, a chaotic map, and a locomotion dataset, and compare against standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA. While the theoretical development is clear and the paper is well written, the practical value and novelty for the machine learning community remain insufficiently demonstrated.

Strengths:
- The paper provides a clear, well-structured derivation that extends DMD-style spectral analysis into the RKHS setting. The exposition has educational value for readers not familiar with operator-theoretic methods from fluid dynamics.
- The idea of leveraging kernel machinery to analyze linear operators on function spaces is natural and interesting, and the paper formalizes this connection cleanly.

Main concerns:
- Positioning and novelty for ML: Much of the methodology echoes established “kernelization” of linear operator methods (e.g., kernel PCA/ICA, kernel independence tests, and related RKHS operator estimators). As a result, the core insight—that linear operator analysis becomes tractable via Gram matrices and eigen-decompositions—is not particularly surprising to ML readers familiar with RKHS methods. The paper would benefit from a sharper articulation of what is fundamentally new beyond standard kernel operator theory or existing Koopman-based algorithms (e.g., distinctions from extended DMD or other kernel Koopman approaches), and why the contribution matters in ML.
- Practical usefulness and use cases: The paper does not convincingly demonstrate where this approach provides compelling advantages over existing ML tools for dynamical systems. Many ML applications involve stochasticity (process/measurement noise, innovations), whereas the paper appears focused on deterministic maps f: M → M. Please clarify:
  - What ML-relevant problems benefit from Koopman analysis in RKHS?
  - How does the method handle noise and stochastic systems?
  - Can it compete with state-of-the-art approaches in at least one concrete domain (e.g., robotics, control, time-series forecasting), and what does it buy practitioners there (better prediction, identification, interpretability, control synthesis)?
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are limited and the reported benefits over baselines are unclear. To strengthen the empirical case:
  - Include standard DMD results on the toy linear system and the Hénon map to directly illustrate differences in a simple, controlled setting.
  - Provide clearer quantitative metrics, confidence intervals, and ablations (e.g., effects of kernel choice, regularization, number of samples).
  - Evaluate on at least one application where ML researchers care about performance (e.g., predictive accuracy or control performance on a robotics benchmark), and compare against strong baselines commonly used in ML for dynamical systems.
- Algorithmic clarity and limitations:
  - It is not clear whether the modal decomposition requires explicit feature representations. If yes, this is a practical limitation compared to kernel PCA and should be made explicit. If no, please detail how the algorithm is carried out purely in terms of Gram matrices (including how modes/eigenfunctions are evaluated and extended out of sample).
  - Provide computational complexity and memory costs as a function of sample size and kernel rank, and discuss scalability. In practice, kernel methods can become cubic in sample size; how is this mitigated (Nyström, random features, low-rank approximations)?
  - Clarify the stability and identifiability of the estimated eigenvalues/eigenfunctions under finite samples and noise, and provide sensitivity analyses.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Articulate a concrete ML use case (e.g., robotic gait analysis, model reduction for control, nonlinear time-series forecasting) where the proposed method offers a clear advantage, and benchmark rigorously against relevant baselines (e.g., DMD/EDMD variants, state-space models, modern nonlinear forecasting methods).
- Expand the experimental section with:
  - Head-to-head comparisons with standard DMD on the toy linear system and Hénon map.
  - A real-world task with quantitative performance metrics and robustness tests (noise, sample size).
  - Discussion of hyperparameter selection (kernel, bandwidth, regularization) and its impact.
- Clarify technical details of the modal decomposition:
  - Whether it operates entirely in RKHS via kernel trick, or requires explicit features.
  - How eigenfunctions and modes are computed, normalized, and evaluated at new points.
  - Any regularization needed to ensure well-posedness.
- Provide a more explicit relationship to prior RKHS operator literature and Koopman-based ML methods, highlighting what is genuinely new here (theoretical results, algorithmic design, or empirical capabilities).

Overall, this is a clearly written and potentially useful bridge between operator-theoretic dynamics and kernel methods. However, to persuade an ML audience, the paper needs stronger positioning relative to existing RKHS/operator work and more compelling empirical evidence demonstrating practical benefits on ML-relevant tasks.
