

Review:  
This paper develops a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes an algorithm for its computation from dynamical system measurements. The Koopman operator, rooted in fluid dynamics, is positioned as relevant to machine learning (ML), though the paper’s justification for this connection remains underdeveloped. The authors validate their approach on two simple systems—a damped linear 1D dynamics and a chaotic Henon map—and apply it to real-world locomotion data, comparing results with dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA. However, the comparative performance on locomotion data is ambiguous, lacking clear evidence of superiority over baseline methods.  

The theoretical contribution extends DMD by leveraging RKHS, aligning with prior "kernelized" techniques like kernel PCA, kernel ICA, and kernel independence tests. While the derivations are mathematically sound, they largely follow established patterns: linear operators in RKHS enable eigendecomposition of finite-dimensional matrices, yielding algorithms analogous to kernel PCA. This incremental advance, though technically correct, does not introduce novel theoretical insights beyond known kernel methods. The paper’s educational value for ML researchers unfamiliar with fluid dynamics concepts is commendable, but its broader ML relevance requires stronger justification.  

Critically, the authors fail to articulate compelling ML use cases. The method targets deterministic dynamical systems (mappings from state space M to M), whereas mainstream ML applications typically involve stochastic dynamics (e.g., with noise or innovations). The paper should explicitly address: (1) *Why* this deterministic framework matters for ML, and (2) *Where* it outperforms state-of-the-art techniques. Robotics—where dynamical system modeling is pivotal—offers a natural test case. The authors should specify how Koopman analysis in RKHS advances tasks like motion prediction or control compared to existing ML methods (e.g., recurrent neural networks or Gaussian processes). Without such concrete examples, the work risks appearing disconnected from ML priorities.  

The experimental section has notable gaps. For the toy examples, the authors omit comparisons with standard DMD on the Henon map and damped dynamics, which would clarify the added value of their kernelized approach. Additionally, it remains unclear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires explicit feature-space representations. If so, this constitutes a significant limitation versus kernel PCA (which operates implicitly in RKHS), and the paper must explicitly acknowledge this trade-off.  

While the writing is clear and the mathematical exposition is rigorous, the paper’s core contribution—applying RKHS to Koopman analysis—lacks a persuasive narrative for ML audiences. The authors should:  
1. **Refocus the motivation** on a specific ML domain (e.g., robotics, time-series forecasting) where deterministic Koopman analysis provides measurable advantages.  
2. **Demonstrate concrete gains** over state-of-the-art methods in that domain, not just artificial toy examples.  
3. **Clarify the feature-space dependency** and its implications for scalability/comparisons to kernel methods.  

The current work is a technically competent extension of existing kernelized operator theory, but it falls short of establishing a compelling case for ML practitioners. With targeted revisions addressing these points, the paper could bridge fluid dynamics and ML more effectively. As-is, it serves better as an educational resource for cross-disciplinary audiences than as a contribution to ML methodology.
