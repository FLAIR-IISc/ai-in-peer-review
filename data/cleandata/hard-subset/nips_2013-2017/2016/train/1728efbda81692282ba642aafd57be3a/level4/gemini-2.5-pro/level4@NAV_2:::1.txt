This paper presents a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending the Koopman operator framework to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The core contribution is a kernel-based variant of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), termed kernel DMD (kDMD). The authors derive an algorithm that computes the eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator's projection onto a data-driven subspace within the RKHS. This procedure effectively extracts dynamic modes, Koopman eigenvalues, and eigenfunctions, providing a nonlinear feature extraction method for time-series data. The paper further proposes a robust version of the algorithm using a projection onto kernel principal component analysis (PCA) directions. The utility of these extracted features is demonstrated across several machine learning applications, including state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition, with experiments on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Elegance:** The extension of DMD to RKHS is a well-motivated and natural step, leveraging the power of kernel methods to capture complex nonlinearities that linear DMD might miss. The theoretical connection established between the Koopman operator framework and kernel methods is elegant.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The theoretical development, from the review of Koopman theory to the derivation of the kernelized algorithm (Theorems 1 and 2), is clear and easy to follow.
*   **Potential Significance:** The proposed kDMD provides a principled, data-driven tool for feature extraction from nonlinear dynamical systems. If proven robust and scalable, it could be highly valuable for researchers and practitioners in various fields who analyze time-series data, from physics to machine learning.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Originality and Relation to Prior Work:** The distinction from Williams et al. [35] (extended DMD) is not sufficiently clear. Extended DMD also approximates the Koopman operator using a dictionary of basis functions, which can be nonlinear. The authors should explicitly and thoroughly discuss the conceptual and practical differences between their RKHS-based approach and the use of pre-defined function dictionaries. A more direct comparison would be necessary to precisely position the paper's contribution and originality.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the applications are interesting, the experimental validation could be stronger to fully support the claims.
    *   The prediction experiments rely on a standard pre-imaging method, making it difficult to assess the specific advantages of the kDMD features.
    *   The dynamics recognition experiment lacks comparisons to established time-series classification/clustering baselines, such as kernel k-means or other subspace methods. Comparing against such methods would be crucial to demonstrate the significance of the proposed features.
    *   The change-point detection experiment uses a comparator known to have limitations. A more convincing evaluation would involve comparison with state-of-the-art online change detection algorithms, particularly those also based on kernels.

3.  **Handling of Stochasticity:** The paper primarily focuses on deterministic systems. While a brief mention is made of extending the framework to stochastic systems, this is a critical aspect for most real-world applications. A more detailed discussion on the method's robustness to noise and its performance in stochastic settings would greatly enhance the paper. A simple experiment on a stochastic toy system would be a welcome addition.

4.  **Scope of Literature Review:** The field of kernel methods for dynamical systems is broad. The literature review could be improved by including and discussing other relevant works, for example, from authors such as L. Ralaivola & F. d'Alche-Buc, D. Tuia, J.L. Rojo-Álvarez, or M. O. Franz & B. Schölkopf, to better contextualize the submission.

5.  **Clarification of Assumptions:** The remark in Section 4 (page 5) that the method only requires pairs of consecutive states `(x(i)1, x(i)2)` rather than a continuous sequence is intriguing. The implications of this for practical time-series analysis warrant further discussion. For instance, how does this assumption affect the analysis of high-dimensional systems with potentially weak pointwise relationships? An elaboration on the implicit causal assumptions would also be beneficial.

**Minor Issues:**

*   Please check for minor typos, such as inconsistencies in indexing with `τ` and `τ−1`, and the use of a period instead of a comma in `k(xi,xj)` in the final equation of Section 7.
