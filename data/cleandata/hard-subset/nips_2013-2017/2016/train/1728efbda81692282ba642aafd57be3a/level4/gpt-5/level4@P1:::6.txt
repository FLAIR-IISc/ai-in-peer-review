This paper develops a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes an algorithm—effectively a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD)—to estimate Koopman spectra from finite data. The method constructs a Krylov subspace in feature space, computes an orthogonal basis (via Gram–Schmidt/QR) for the feature-space snapshot matrix, and then performs an eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator. A robust variant projects data onto kernel PCA directions (analogous to POD truncation in DMD) to mitigate numerical ill-conditioning. The authors illustrate the approach on a toy 2D nonlinear system and the Hénon map, and present applications to short-term prediction via pre-image recovery, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition on locomotion data. The work relates to DMD and the Koopman operator literature in fluid mechanics and to kernel methods such as kernel PCA; it also connects to extended DMD (EDMD), exact/optimized DMD variants, and spectral learning for dynamical systems.

Strengths
- Clear and largely self-contained exposition that usefully introduces Koopman/DMD ideas to a machine learning audience.
- A principled extension of DMD to RKHS that replaces linear monomials with richer nonlinear basis functions via kernels.
- Sensible numerical stabilization through kernel PCA projection, closely paralleling best practices in DMD/POD.
- Illustrative examples and application sketches (prediction via pre-image, change detection, and recognition via subspace angles) that show how Koopman features can be used for downstream tasks.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practical usefulness for machine learning is not well established. Many ML applications of dynamical systems involve stochasticity (process/measurement noise), whereas the main development and most experiments here are deterministic. While the authors briefly mention stochastic extensions of Koopman (operator in expectation), the paper does not demonstrate performance under stochastic dynamics or noisy observations. Clear use cases (e.g., in robotics, forecasting, control) and comparisons to state-of-the-art ML methods are needed to justify significance.
- Relation to prior art, especially EDMD (Williams et al.) and exact/optimized DMD, is underdeveloped. EDMD also uses user-defined dictionaries (including kernels) and yields a finite-dimensional approximation of the Koopman operator. The paper should more carefully position its contributions relative to EDMD, emphasizing what is new (e.g., specific kernelization route, numerical procedure, theoretical guarantees) and providing empirical comparisons.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and somewhat unclear. The toy and Hénon examples are informative but small-scale, and the real-data locomotion results are not convincingly analyzed. There is no systematic comparison to standard DMD, exact/optimized DMD, or EDMD. Showing baseline DMD (and EDMD) performance on the toy system and Hénon map would help quantify the benefit of the kernel approach in simple, controlled settings.
- It is not entirely clear whether the algorithm requires explicit feature-space representations or whether all computations can be carried out via Gram matrices, as in kernel PCA. The reliance on Gram–Schmidt in RKHS suggests a fully kernelized implementation is possible, but the text should explicitly clarify this. If explicit features are required in any step, that is a practical limitation compared to standard kernel methods and should be discussed.
- Computational aspects and hyperparameter sensitivity are not discussed. Kernel methods scale cubically in the number of snapshots due to Gram matrix eigendecompositions; truncation helps but a complexity/memory discussion is needed. The sensitivity to kernel choice and bandwidth (especially for RBF) and the impact of truncation level on spectral estimates should be analyzed.
- The pre-image step for prediction is a known ill-posed problem; the MDS-style approach is reasonable but may be fragile. The paper would benefit from comparisons to alternative pre-image techniques and an assessment of prediction accuracy versus horizon, under noise.
- Some figures and empirical claims are difficult to interpret (“somewhat unclear results” also noted by the authors). Provide quantitative metrics (e.g., eigenvalue error, reconstruction error, prediction RMSE, change-point detection delay/false-alarm rates, classification accuracy) with variance across trials.

Quality
- The derivations appear correct and align with known properties of DMD, Krylov subspaces, and kernel methods. The robust variant via kernel PCA is sensible. However, for readers familiar with kernel PCA/ICA and related operator-theoretic kernel methods, the results will be unsurprising: linear operators in RKHS reduce to finite Gram-matrix eigendecompositions with appropriate truncation.

Clarity
- Generally well written and accessible. Please clarify:
  - Whether the entire procedure is kernelized (no explicit feature maps needed).
  - The precise differences from EDMD and exact/optimized DMD, both algorithmically and conceptually.
  - Experimental setup details (kernel selection, bandwidth/truncation choices, normalization), and provide quantitative evaluation.

Originality
- The idea of kernelizing operator-based decompositions is not new (e.g., EDMD/dictionary learning, kernel PCA). The contribution seems to be a specific RKHS formulation and numerical procedure for Koopman spectral analysis via DMD-like Krylov constructions and a POD-style stabilization. This is incremental but potentially useful if validated empirically and clearly distinguished from EDMD.

Significance
- Potentially useful for nonlinear modal analysis and feature extraction, but the current empirical evidence and application demonstrations are insufficient to convince ML practitioners. Stronger results on realistic tasks (e.g., robotics trajectories, control policy evaluation, forecasting under noise) and comparisons to established baselines are needed.

Specific suggestions
- Add direct comparisons to standard DMD, exact/optimized DMD, and EDMD on the toy system and Hénon map.
- Clarify kernelization: demonstrate that all steps can be performed with Gram matrices, and discuss numerical conditioning and truncation.
- Analyze sensitivity to kernel hyperparameters and truncation levels; report computational complexity and runtimes.
- For stochastic settings, include experiments with process/measurement noise to substantiate the claimed extension.
- In the applications:
  - Prediction: report quantitative short-horizon prediction errors and compare pre-image methods.
  - Change-point detection: provide detection delay/false alarm trade-offs against kernel change detection baselines.
  - Dynamics recognition: report classification accuracy and confusion matrices; compare to kernel PCA/PCA subspace methods.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear exposition and educational value for ML readers unfamiliar with Koopman/DMD.
- Principled RKHS extension with a practical stabilization strategy.
- Potentially useful feature extraction framework for nonlinear dynamics.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited and inconclusive empirical validation; unclear gains over existing methods (DMD/EDMD).
- Insufficient discussion of computational and practical aspects (kernel choice, scalability, stochasticity).
- Incremental originality relative to EDMD and prior kernel operator methods.

Overall, I am supportive of cross-disciplinary work bringing Koopman/DMD ideas to ML, but the paper needs stronger empirical evidence, clearer positioning relative to EDMD and DMD variants, and more explicit guidance on practical deployment to be compelling for the NIPS audience.
