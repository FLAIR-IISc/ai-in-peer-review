This paper introduces a method for the spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors derive a kernel-based algorithm to approximate the operator's eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes from measurement data of a dynamical system. Originating from fluid dynamics, the Koopman operator is presented here as a tool for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems, with potential applications in machine learning. The proposed method is demonstrated on two synthetic dynamical systems (a damped linear system and the chaotic Hénon map) and a real-world locomotion dataset. Its performance is compared against standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA, though the comparative results on the real-world data are not definitively conclusive.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper presents a well-founded extension of dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) to nonlinear systems, drawing a clear conceptual parallel to established kernel-based methods like kernel PCA, kernel ICA, and kernel independence tests.
*   The manuscript is well-written and serves an educational purpose by introducing the machine learning community to powerful concepts from dynamical systems theory and fluid dynamics. This cross-disciplinary approach is commendable and has the potential to enrich the set of tools available to machine learning practitioners.
*   The theoretical derivations appear to be sound and are executed with clarity.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Motivation for Machine Learning Applications:** The primary weakness of the paper is the insufficient motivation for its relevance to the machine learning community. The analysis is largely confined to deterministic dynamical systems, whereas many ML applications (e.g., in time-series forecasting, reinforcement learning, or state-space modeling) involve stochastic components like process noise and measurement error. The authors should more clearly articulate the specific machine learning problems where this Koopman operator analysis would provide a significant advantage over existing methods. What are the potential use cases where it could compete with, or even surpass, the state of the art? For instance, in a field like robotics, which heavily relies on learning and inference in dynamical systems, what specific challenges could this approach address better than established techniques? A more concrete positioning of the work within the ML landscape is needed to convince readers of its practical utility.

2.  **Perceived Novelty within the Kernel Methods Literature:** While the theoretical derivations are sound, the core idea of "kernelizing" a linear operator to handle nonlinearities is a well-established pattern in machine learning. For readers familiar with the development of kernel PCA, kernel ICA, or kernel-based independence tests, the theoretical contribution may appear more incremental than groundbreaking. The pattern of defining a linear operator on functions in an RKHS, leading to an algorithm involving an eigendecomposition of a finite-dimensional matrix, is a familiar one. The primary contribution thus seems to be the application of this established pattern to the Koopman operator, rather than a fundamentally new theoretical insight for kernel methods.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation could be strengthened to better support the paper's claims. To clearly demonstrate the advantage of the proposed kernel method, a direct comparison with standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) should be included for the synthetic examples (the 1D damped dynamics and the Hénon map). This would provide a crucial baseline and highlight the specific scenarios where the kernelized approach excels in a simple, controlled setup. Furthermore, the results on the locomotion data were described as somewhat ambiguous; a more thorough analysis or a clearer interpretation of these results would be beneficial.

**Questions and Suggestions for the Authors:**

*   A critical detail regarding the algorithm needs clarification: does the modal decomposition algorithm require an explicit representation of the data in the feature space, or can it be implemented solely using the kernel matrix (the "kernel trick")? If the former is true, this represents a significant computational limitation compared to methods like kernel PCA and should be explicitly discussed as a potential drawback.
*   Could the authors elaborate on how their framework might be extended to handle stochastic dynamical systems, which are more prevalent in machine learning contexts? This would significantly broaden the potential impact of the work.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting and clearly explained connection between Koopman operator theory and kernel methods. However, its impact could be substantially increased by providing stronger motivation for its application to specific machine learning problems and by strengthening the experimental evidence to more clearly demonstrate its advantages over existing techniques.
