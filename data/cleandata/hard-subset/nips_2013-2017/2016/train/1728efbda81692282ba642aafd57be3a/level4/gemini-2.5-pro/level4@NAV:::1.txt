This manuscript presents a spectral analysis framework grounded in the Koopman operator, which is defined within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. From this foundation, the authors develop a series of kernel-based methods for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems using empirical data, following a well-established methodology. The proposed approach, which relies on the eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator, demonstrates improved efficiency in experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets. Overall, the paper puts forward a valuable kernel construction for the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The contributions are clearly motivated and supported by experimental results.

Despite its strengths, there are several areas where the paper could be improved, which I encourage the authors to address:

*   The distinction between the proposed method and the work in [35] by M.O. Williams, I.G. Kevrekidis, and C.W. Rowley (Journal of Nonlinear Science, 2015) is not sufficiently clear. That paper previously introduced the use of Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for analyzing nonlinear system dynamics. A more detailed discussion highlighting the key differences would be beneficial, along with an expanded treatment of this comparison in Section 4.

*   The literature on kernel methods for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems is extensive. The current manuscript would be strengthened by acknowledging and citing additional relevant work. For instance, the authors might consider the contributions of researchers such as L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, or M. O. Franz and B. B. Schölkopf.

*   The Koopman operator is traditionally defined for deterministic systems. It would be valuable if the authors could comment on their method's applicability and performance in stochastic settings, which represent more realistic scenarios. While the paper briefly mentions this topic, a more thorough discussion, perhaps accompanied by a simple illustrative experiment, would be a welcome addition.

*   Regarding the remark on page 5 (line 216), the authors state that the estimation requires a collection of consecutive observable pairs with a nonlinear pointwise relationship, rather than a complete sequence. Could the authors elaborate on the implications of this assumption, particularly for multidimensional time series where such pointwise relations might be weak? A discussion on the specific advantages of the proposed kernel construction in this context would be insightful. In a similar vein, further clarification on the inherent causal assumptions of the framework would be appreciated.

*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive. The prediction task via pre-imaging is based on a standard, well-established technique. In the "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" section, the paper lacks a discussion of how the proposed method relates to the extensive literature on time series clustering, including methods like kernel k-means and other subspace kernel approaches; an experimental comparison would also be highly relevant here. Furthermore, for the sequential change-point detection experiment, a comparison against more modern, state-of-the-art online kernel-based change detection methods would be more compelling, especially since the baseline used is known to have limitations in complex dynamical settings. More rigorous experiments are needed to fully substantiate the claims of the paper.

*   **Minor:** On a minor note, the paper is well-written and clear. I noticed two small typos: the indices for τ-1 versus τ should be checked for consistency, and in the last equation on page 7, k(xi,xj) should use a comma instead of a dot.
