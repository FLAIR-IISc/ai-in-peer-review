The manuscript, entitled "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis," extends dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The resulting algorithm (KDMD) allows the use of feature maps to define the kernel, providing a powerful generalization of DMD and enabling a connection to specific features of interest. My primary concern is the paper's readability. The writing is often dense and mathematically focused even when simpler phrasing would suffice. Some sections are overly verbose, potentially hindering understanding even for specialists, and several points require further clarification. While currently unsuitable for publication in the Neural Information Processing Systems conference proceedings due to its complexity, I strongly encourage the authors to revise it for greater pedagogical clarity; a more accessible version of this work has the potential to be highly cited.

**I. MAJOR REMARKS**

1.  The presentation is often unnecessarily verbose. For instance, the sentence “Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point” could be expressed more concisely.
2.  Section 4 feels somewhat extraneous for a concise paper and partially reiterates concepts introduced earlier.
3.  Section 5 should either be expanded with illustrative examples or summarized more thoroughly in the conclusion.
4.  Section 6 is too condensed. The empirical results deserve more detailed development, similar in scope to Section 5.

**II. MINOR REMARKS**

1.  There appears to be a potential error in the description of the toy nonlinear system, as it seems to represent a linear system.
2.  The initial two examples are polynomial. KDMD's strength should be demonstrated with more complex cases where kernel choices significantly impact performance, and more details on these kernel choices should be provided as in Section 5.

**Detailed Review:**

This paper presents a novel extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) by performing the analysis within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The core idea – leveraging kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with richer, nonlinear basis functions – is promising and represents a potentially significant contribution. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature on spectral analysis of dynamical systems, including Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD), DMD, and related techniques. They accurately describe the limitations of POD and how DMD offers improved insight into system dynamics, and clearly articulate the motivation for extending DMD with kernel methods.

The mathematical formulation of the approach is rigorous and well-defined. The extension of DMD to RKHS, presented in Section 3, is clearly explained, and Theorem 1 provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithm. The robustness enhancement described in Section 4, by projecting data onto POD directions, is a sensible addition to address potential ill-conditioning issues. The connection to existing techniques like kernel PCA is also well-established.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in its presentation. The mathematical notation, while correct, is often presented without sufficient explanation, making it difficult to follow the logical flow of the argument. As noted in the major remarks, many sentences are overly complex and could be simplified without losing technical accuracy. The organization could also be improved; for example, Section 4 feels somewhat disconnected from the main flow of the paper.

The empirical evaluation in Sections 6 and 7 is a significant weakness. While the authors demonstrate the method on a toy system and the Henon map, the results are presented without sufficient detail. The choice of kernel parameters (e.g., the degree of the polynomial kernel or the width of the Gaussian kernel) is not fully justified, and the impact of these parameters on the results is not explored. The applications section (Section 7), while showcasing the potential of the method, lacks depth and feels more like a list of possibilities than a rigorous evaluation of the algorithm’s performance. The application examples are also not well explained and would benefit from a deeper discussion of the results.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed method represents a genuine extension of DMD with potential benefits for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical framework is well-defined and supported by a clear theorem.
*   **Potential Significance:** If successfully applied, this method could provide a powerful tool for extracting meaningful information from complex dynamical systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is difficult to read and understand, even for researchers familiar with the field.
*   **Insufficient Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical results are limited in scope and lack sufficient detail.
*   **Organization:** The organization of the paper could be improved.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe that this paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field, but its current state is not suitable for publication in a highly competitive conference like NeurIPS. I recommend rejection with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the concerns outlined in this review, particularly those related to clarity and empirical evaluation. The authors should focus on presenting their work in a more accessible and pedagogical manner, and providing a more comprehensive assessment of the algorithm’s performance on a wider range of problems.
