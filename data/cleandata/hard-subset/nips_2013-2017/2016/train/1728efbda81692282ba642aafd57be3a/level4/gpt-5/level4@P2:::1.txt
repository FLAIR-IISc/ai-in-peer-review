Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops a kernelized framework for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors define the Koopman operator on an RKHS feature map and construct a Krylov matrix from feature evaluations ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1). Using Gram–Schmidt in feature space (implemented via Gram matrices), they obtain an orthonormal basis and compute empirical Ritz values/vectors by eigendecomposing the projection of the Koopman operator onto this subspace. They also present a robust variant that projects onto kernel PCA/POD directions before eigendecomposition, analogous to the SVD/POD projection used in “exact DMD.” The resulting eigenvalues and modes serve as features for several tasks: short-horizon prediction via a preimage mapping, recognition by comparing subspaces spanned by estimated Koopman eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection using a kernel exponential-family view. Experiments on a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, and motion-capture data illustrate feasibility.

The work is closely related to DMD and its variants, especially extended DMD (EDMD; Williams et al., 2015), which formulates Koopman approximation with user-chosen dictionaries and solves a finite-dimensional eigenproblem. Here, the RKHS machinery implicitly defines a (potentially rich) dictionary via a kernel, and the algorithm is expressed through Krylov subspaces in feature space with a kernel-POD robustification.

Strengths:
- Clear and principled derivation linking Koopman theory, Krylov methods, and RKHS. Theorems connect feature-space Ritz values/vectors to Koopman spectra, and the robust variant addresses conditioning.
- Kernelization removes the need to handcraft dictionaries, potentially increasing expressiveness.
- Applicability to several downstream tasks is well motivated, and preliminary experiments are coherent.
- Writing is generally clear and the contribution is well motivated.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification:
- Originality vs EDMD [35]. The paper’s core seems equivalent to EDMD with a kernel dictionary and a standard POD/SVD projection; the Krylov/QR viewpoint is appealing but may be largely a re-expression. Please articulate the substantive differences: (i) Is your estimator algebraically equivalent to kernel-EDMD (generalized eigenproblem in Gram matrices)? (ii) Are there computational or statistical advantages (e.g., conditioning, complexity, memory) of your QR/Krylov route? (iii) How does your robust variant relate to exact/optimized DMD and EDMD with Tikhonov/truncation?
- Related work coverage. The kernel literature on dynamical systems and time series is broader than currently cited. Please consider adding and positioning with respect to work by L. Ralaivola and F. d’Alché-Buc (kernel autoregressive/time-series learning), Tuia et al., Rojo-Álvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback (meshless/kernel methods for dynamics), and M. O. Franz and B. Schölkopf (preimage and kernel dynamical models).
- Stochastic settings. While Koopman/transfer operators extend to stochastic dynamics, the paper offers only brief remarks. Please discuss how noise affects the Ritz spectrum (bias/variance), robustness of the Gram-based implementation, and whether the kernel-POD step mitigates noise. A small noisy example (e.g., Hénon with additive noise or a stochastic Lorenz map) would strengthen the empirical section.
- Data requirements and causality. The remark that only pairs of consecutive observables are needed (rather than a single long sequence) is important. In high-dimensional multivariate time series with weak pointwise relations, what are the implications for identifiability and sample complexity? Are you assuming first-order Markov structure? Would delay-embedding (stacking time lags) improve performance, and how would it be incorporated in your kernelized formulation? Please also comment on causal assumptions and whether the method distinguishes directionality vs pure correlation.
- Experiments and baselines. The empirical section is limited. For prediction, the preimage step follows standard practice; quantitative error curves, sensitivity to kernel choice/width, and ablations on truncation rank would help. For dynamics recognition, please connect to and compare with kernel k-means and subspace/kernel subspace methods on time series. For change-point detection, comparisons to stronger kernel-based online methods (e.g., MMD-based detectors, kernel CUSUM/relative density-ratio approaches) would be more convincing; the baseline used is known to struggle with complex dynamics. Comparisons to EDMD with polynomial/RBF dictionaries, exact DMD, and optimized DMD would clarify the added value of the RKHS approach.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The mathematical development is sound and the algorithms are clearly specified. Practical stability is addressed via kernel-POD, but more discussion of conditioning, complexity, and stochastic robustness would improve rigor. The preimage-based predictor is heuristic; a discussion of failure modes is desirable.
- Clarity: Generally well organized and readable. Section 4 could more explicitly connect to exact DMD and detail computational steps. The remark about pairwise samples warrants deeper explanation (see above).
- Originality: The kernelization of Koopman/DMD is interesting, but the contribution appears incremental relative to EDMD [35]. The paper would benefit from a sharper positioning of what is genuinely new (e.g., a provable link via Krylov/QR in RKHS, improved conditioning, or new guarantees).
- Significance: Potentially impactful, as kernelization offers flexibility and broad applicability. However, stronger empirical evidence and comparisons are needed to demonstrate practical gains.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical framing of Koopman analysis in RKHS with clear algorithms.
- Addresses numerical stability via a principled kernel-POD projection.
- Unifies Koopman/DMD with kernel methods and suggests useful applications.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty versus EDMD and related DMD variants; positioning needs sharpening.
- Experimental evaluation is modest and lacks strong baselines/comparisons.
- Insufficient discussion and testing under stochastic dynamics and in the pairwise-sample regime.

Minor comments:
- Please check indices for τ−1 vs τ in several formulas.
- Replace the dot with a comma in k(xi, xj) (last equation on p. 7).
- A brief complexity analysis (in τ and number of retained components p) would aid reproducibility.

Overall, this is a promising and well-written contribution. Clarifying its novelty relative to EDMD, expanding related work, and strengthening experiments (especially with competitive baselines and a stochastic example) would substantially improve the paper.
