The authors of this paper present a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and develop an algorithm to perform this analysis using measurement data from a dynamical system. The Koopman operator, originating from fluid dynamics, is applied to machine learning, although its relevance to this field is not explicitly demonstrated. The proposed algorithm and analysis are evaluated using two simple dynamical systems: a damped linear 1D dynamics and a chaotic map. Additionally, the authors apply their method to real-world locomotion data and compare its performance with standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA, yielding somewhat inconclusive results.

This paper extends dynamic mode decomposition in an interesting way, similar to previous work on "kernelizing" techniques that operate on linear operators, such as kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, and GP bandits. The authors provide experimental results for largely artificial problems. The paper is well-written and has educational value for readers unfamiliar with concepts from fluid dynamics. Introducing machine learning researchers to techniques from different disciplines is a valuable contribution, and this work presents a decomposition technique for functions from M to M used to construct a dynamical system without stochastic components.

However, it is unclear whether such setups are commonly used in machine learning, where dynamical systems typically involve random effects like innovations and noise. To convince machine learning readers of the practical usefulness of these techniques, the authors should provide more context on potential use cases and competitive advantages. For instance, in robotics, where inference and learning in dynamical systems are crucial, it is unclear what benefits Koopman analysis would offer. The authors should explore specific domains and provide concrete examples to demonstrate the value of their approach.

While the theoretical derivations are well-executed, they are not particularly surprising, given the existing work on kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, and kernel Bayes rule. The key idea is to apply a linear operator to functions in an RKHS, resulting in algorithms that perform eigendecomposition on finite-D matrices. Some technical details require clarification, such as whether the modal decomposition algorithm necessitates an explicit representation in feature space. If so, this limitation should be explicitly stated, as it differs from kernel PCA. Furthermore, the authors should demonstrate the performance of standard DMD on the toy example and the Henon map to highlight the differences in this simple setup.
