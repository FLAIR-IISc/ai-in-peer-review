## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title]

This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), accompanied by an algorithm for performing this analysis from data generated by dynamical systems. The authors effectively bridge concepts from fluid dynamics – specifically the Koopman operator – with techniques relevant to machine learning, offering a potentially powerful new lens through which to analyze dynamical systems. The paper is well-written and provides valuable educational material for machine learning researchers unfamiliar with concepts from the fluid dynamics domain. I am generally supportive of efforts to integrate methodologies from diverse disciplines into machine learning.

The core contribution lies in extending dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) using kernel methods. This work echoes a broader trend in machine learning – the “kernelization” of linear techniques, seen previously in kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, and Gaussian process bandits. The authors demonstrate the algorithm’s performance on two simple dynamical systems (damped linear 1D dynamics and the Henon map) and then apply it to real-world locomotion data, comparing it against DMD, PCA, and kernel PCA. The results on the locomotion data, however, are somewhat inconclusive.

While the theoretical derivations are sound and well-executed, they aren’t particularly surprising to someone familiar with kernel methods. The inherent benefits of operating within an RKHS – specifically, the ability to perform eigendecomposition on finite-dimensional matrices – are well-established. The novelty primarily resides in applying this framework to the Koopman operator.

However, the paper currently lacks a compelling argument for the practical utility of this approach within the mainstream machine learning community. The work focuses on deterministic dynamical systems, a scenario less common than those incorporating stochastic elements (such as innovations and noise). While I acknowledge I may be overlooking applications, the paper doesn’t articulate a clear use case where Koopman analysis offers a substantial advantage over existing methods.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should explicitly address potential applications within key machine learning domains. Robotics is a natural candidate; how would Koopman analysis benefit a robotics application, such as control or prediction of complex movements? Alternatively, the authors could explore other relevant areas like time series forecasting, or system identification. Crucially, they need to demonstrate competitive performance, or at least illustrate scenarios where their method provides unique insights, compared to state-of-the-art techniques in the chosen domain.

Several minor points should also be addressed: 

*   **Feature Space Representation:** It is unclear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires explicit representation in the feature space. If this is the case, it represents a significant limitation compared to kernel PCA and should be clearly stated.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** The authors should include a performance comparison of standard DMD against their proposed method on both the toy example and the Henon map. This would provide a clearer understanding of the improvements achieved through kernelization in these simple settings.
*   **Result Clarity:** The results on the locomotion data need to be presented more clearly. A more rigorous quantitative comparison, coupled with a detailed discussion of the observed differences, would significantly strengthen the paper.



Overall, this paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable extension of dynamic mode decomposition. However, to reach its full potential, it requires a more compelling articulation of its practical relevance to the machine learning community and a more thorough empirical evaluation demonstrating its advantages in real-world applications.
