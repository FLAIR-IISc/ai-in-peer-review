This paper proposes a method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems within the framework of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to extend Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a popular technique for analyzing such systems, by leveraging the representational power of kernels. This allows for the approximation of the Koopman operator's eigenfunctions using a rich, non-parametric set of basis functions, overcoming the limitations of standard DMD which typically relies on linear observables. The authors develop an algorithm that computes the eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projected onto a subspace of the RKHS. They also present a robust version of the algorithm using a projection onto POD (Principal Orthogonal Decomposition) bases, analogous to similar robustification techniques for standard DMD. The paper frames this analysis as a feature extraction procedure and demonstrates its application to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper presents a theoretically sound and well-motivated extension of Koopman spectral analysis to RKHS. This provides a principled, non-parametric approach for analyzing complex nonlinear dynamics.
*   The connection to the DMD framework is clear, and the proposed algorithm is a natural "kernelization" of existing concepts.
*   The development of a robust algorithm by projecting onto kernel PCA directions (Section 4) is a valuable practical contribution, addressing potential numerical instability issues.
*   The paper is generally well-written and the core technical ideas are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarification of Novelty with Respect to Prior Work:** My primary concern is the distinction from existing work, particularly the Extended DMD (EDMD) method of Williams et al. [35]. EDMD also allows for the use of pre-determined basis functions beyond linear observables. While the authors cite this paper, the novelty and specific advantages of the proposed kernel-based approach over EDMD (which could, in principle, use kernel-based features as its basis functions) are not clearly articulated. A more detailed discussion clarifying the key differences and contributions would significantly strengthen the paper's positioning.

2.  **Incomplete Literature Review:** The field of kernel-based analysis of dynamical systems is extensive. The literature review could be expanded to provide a more complete context for the paper's contributions by including and discussing relevant works from authors such as L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc, D. Tuia et al., J.L. Rojo-Alvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, or M. O. Franz and B. Schölkopf.

3.  **Scope of Application (Stochastic Systems):** The paper focuses on deterministic systems. While the authors briefly mention that the Koopman operator can be extended to stochastic settings, the implications for the proposed algorithm are not discussed. Given that many real-world systems are inherently stochastic, a more thorough discussion of how the method might perform or need to be adapted in the presence of noise would be valuable. A simple experiment on a toy stochastic system could also help demonstrate its robustness and would increase the paper's practical significance.

4.  **Assumptions on Data Structure:** The remark on page 5, stating that the method only requires pairs of consecutive data points rather than a full sequence, is interesting. However, it raises questions about its practical application. Could the authors elaborate on the implications of this for complex, multidimensional time series where pointwise relationships might be weak or delayed? Additionally, a brief discussion of the causal assumptions inherent in this framework would be helpful for practitioners.

5.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the paper presents several applications, the experimental evaluation could be more ambitious and rigorous to convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages.
    *   **Prediction:** The prediction task uses a standard pre-imaging technique. The evaluation does not make it clear whether the proposed features offer a significant advantage over other established nonlinear time-series prediction methods.
    *   **Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics:** This section lacks a quantitative comparison to the extensive body of work on time series clustering and classification. Comparing against relevant baselines, such as methods based on kernel k-means or other subspace approaches, is necessary to establish the proposed method's utility for this task.
    *   **Sequential Change-Point Detection:** The comparison is made against a kernel-based method that is known to have limitations in complex dynamics. A much stronger validation would involve comparing against more recent, state-of-the-art online change detection algorithms, particularly those also using kernels. Overall, more convincing experiments with stronger baselines are needed to support the claims of the paper.

**Minor Points:**
*   The paper is very well-written. I only spotted a couple of minor typos:
*   Please double-check the indices related to `τ` and `τ-1` throughout the text for consistency.
*   In the last equation on page 7, `k(xi.xj)` should likely be `k(xj, xi)`.
