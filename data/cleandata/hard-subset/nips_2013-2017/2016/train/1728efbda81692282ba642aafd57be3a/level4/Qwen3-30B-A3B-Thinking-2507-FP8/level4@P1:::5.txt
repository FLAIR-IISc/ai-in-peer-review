

This paper extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), introducing a kernel-based method for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors propose an algorithm (KDMD) that approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using kernel-based features rather than linear monomials, which they claim provides a more flexible and expressive representation. The paper demonstrates applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data. The approach builds on prior work in Koopman spectral analysis and DMD [25, 26, 35], extending the framework to incorporate kernel methods for more sophisticated feature extraction.

The paper's main strength lies in its technical contribution: extending DMD to RKHS provides a more general framework for Koopman spectral analysis. The theoretical development is sound, with appropriate mathematical foundations and clear connections to existing work on Koopman operators and DMD variants. The authors successfully demonstrate that their method can capture nonlinear dynamics more effectively than traditional DMD approaches, particularly when the underlying dynamics are not well-approximated by linear monomials. The empirical examples on synthetic and real-world data provide reasonable validation of the approach, and the applications to prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition showcase potential utility.

However, the paper suffers from significant issues with clarity and presentation that substantially undermine its impact. The writing is unnecessarily verbose and mathematically dense, making it difficult to follow even for specialists in the field. For example, the key insight that "DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point" could be stated concisely in a single sentence rather than being buried in multiple pages of dense mathematical notation. The paper would benefit greatly from a more pedagogic approach that emphasizes conceptual understanding before diving into mathematical details.

Several sections are redundant or could be significantly condensed. Section 4, which discusses robustification using POD bases, is largely covered in the introduction and could be integrated into the main algorithm description. Similarly, Section 5 on related work is overly brief and would be more effective as a concise summary in the introduction or conclusion rather than as a separate section. Section 6's empirical examples are presented too tersely and would benefit from more detailed explanation of the results and their significance, similar to how Section 5 could be expanded.

The paper's most significant weakness is its writing style, which makes it challenging to understand even for experts. Mathematical concepts are obscured by excessive formalism rather than being explained clearly. For instance, the description of the Krylov subspace and the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in RKHS could be presented with more intuitive explanations before introducing the mathematical machinery. The paper would be far more accessible if the authors focused on explaining the core ideas first, then providing the mathematical details as necessary.

The authors' claim that "the proposed algorithm provides a modal decomposition of the dynamics into a finite number of modes, and thus it could be considered as a feature extraction procedure" is important but is not sufficiently emphasized. This conceptual contribution is one of the paper's strongest points, but it gets lost in the mathematical details. A more focused presentation of this key insight would strengthen the paper significantly.

I also note a minor technical concern: the toy nonlinear system example described in Section 6 appears to be linear (xt+1 = 0.9xt, yt+1 = 0.5yt + (0.9² - 0.5)xt²), which contradicts the claim of nonlinear dynamics. This should be clarified or the example should be revised.

Strengths:
- Technical contribution extends DMD to RKHS, providing a more expressive framework for Koopman spectral analysis
- Sound theoretical foundation with appropriate connections to existing literature
- Demonstrates multiple applications with empirical validation
- Conceptual insight that the method serves as a feature extraction procedure for dynamical systems

Weaknesses:
- Poor writing style makes the paper difficult to understand even for specialists
- Excessive verbosity and mathematical density obscure key insights
- Redundant sections (particularly Section 4) could be condensed
- Empirical examples lack sufficient explanation and detail
- Minor technical error in the toy example description

While the paper's technical contribution is valuable and potentially significant, its current presentation is too obscure for publication in NIPS. The authors have a strong technical contribution that could be highly influential if presented more clearly. I strongly recommend rejection in its current form but with encouragement to substantially revise and resubmit. The authors should focus on creating a more accessible, pedagogic presentation that clearly communicates the conceptual advances of their work before delving into mathematical details.

The paper has the potential to be highly cited if the authors take the time to rewrite it with clarity and focus, emphasizing the conceptual contributions rather than burying them in dense mathematical notation. The core idea of using kernel methods to extend DMD for more expressive Koopman spectral analysis is promising and deserves a clearer presentation.
