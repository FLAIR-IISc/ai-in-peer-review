

The paper proposes a spectral method for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator, which provides a linear operator representation of function composition. Specifically, the authors apply the Koopman operator decomposition to the second iterate of the system dynamics, \(x(t+2) = f(f(x(t)))\), leveraging collected trajectories to perform a spectral decomposition inspired by kernel PCA and subspace analysis. The method is then applied to dynamical system embedding and recognition, with the authors demonstrating competitive performance. For readers familiar with kernel methods, the core novelty lies in representing function composition as a linear operator and applying spectral techniques to such compositions, with dynamical systems serving as a natural application domain. However, the novelty is limited: dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), a well-established method in dynamical systems literature, already addresses similar problems, and the proposed approach essentially kernelizes DMD. Furthermore, the applications lack compelling justification; alternative methods like recurrent neural networks (RNNs) already exist for dynamical system recognition and change detection, and the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why the kernelized Koopman approach offers significant advantages over these established techniques or provides unique insights for the tasks considered.
