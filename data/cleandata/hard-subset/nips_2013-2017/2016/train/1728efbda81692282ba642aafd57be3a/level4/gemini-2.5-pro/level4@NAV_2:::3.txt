This paper presents a method for spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The central contribution is a novel modal decomposition algorithm, effectively a kernelized version of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), for extracting features from nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors ground their work in the established theory of the Koopman operator and its connection to DMD, a technique that has gained significant traction in physics and engineering. They extend this analysis by leveraging the kernel trick to implicitly work with a richer set of nonlinear basis functions. The paper also proposes a practical, robust implementation that projects data onto principal orthogonal directions (via kernel PCA) to avoid numerical instability. The utility of the extracted Koopman modes as features is demonstrated through applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a principled and novel extension of DMD using kernel methods. By moving the spectral analysis into an RKHS, the method can potentially capture complex nonlinear dynamics far more effectively than standard DMD, which is limited by its underlying linear assumptions. This work builds a valuable bridge between the dynamical systems community's Koopman operator theory and the machine learning community's powerful kernel methods, representing a significant contribution with the potential for broad impact.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed algorithm is well-motivated. The insight to robustify the method by projecting onto POD bases (Section 4) is practical and addresses a known numerical issue in standard DMD, showing a thoughtful consideration for real-world implementation. The connections drawn between the empirical Ritz values/vectors and the true Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions in Theorem 2 are well-established.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It provides a concise yet informative background on the Koopman operator and DMD before detailing the proposed extension. The applications section effectively demonstrates the practical value of the method on a diverse set of tasks.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in the clarity of its core theoretical presentation, which raises some questions about its formal correctness.

*   **Clarity of the Operator Definition:** The definition of the Koopman operator in the feature space, `KH`, in Equation (7) is a significant point of confusion. `KH` is described as a linear operator in `H` (mapping `H` to `H`), yet in `(KHϕ)(x) = ϕ ◦ f(x)`, it appears to act on `ϕ`, which is the feature map from the state space `M` to the RKHS `H`, not an element of `H`. This notational ambiguity makes it difficult to rigorously follow the subsequent theoretical development and proofs. Could the authors please provide a more precise definition of `KH`, clarifying its domain and how it acts on elements within the RKHS?
*   **Rigor of Theoretical Proofs:** The lack of clarity in the operator definition impacts the reader's ability to verify the proofs. For example, the derivation leading to Theorem 1 requires a clear understanding of how `KH` operates within the Krylov subspace. A more detailed, step-by-step derivation in the proofs of both Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, with careful attention to the objects and spaces involved, would greatly strengthen the paper.
*   **Explicitness of Assumptions:** The assumptions underlying the main theorems could be stated more explicitly and their implications discussed. Theorem 2 assumes that the true Koopman eigenfunctions are contained within the subspace spanned by the kernel principal components. This is a strong but crucial assumption, as it posits that the data is sufficiently rich to capture the dynamically important modes. The paper would be improved by a discussion of the conditions under which this assumption might hold and the potential limitations of the method when it is violated.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper presents a highly interesting and significant extension of DMD to nonlinear systems via the kernel trick. The approach is well-motivated, and its practical utility is convincingly demonstrated through multiple applications.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The theoretical formulation, particularly the definition of the Koopman operator in RKHS and the subsequent proofs, lacks the clarity and rigor expected for a top-tier publication. These ambiguities must be resolved to ensure the work is technically sound.

Overall, this is a promising paper with a strong core idea. If the authors can address the points of confusion in the theoretical exposition, particularly regarding the operator definition in Section 3, it would constitute a solid contribution to the field.
