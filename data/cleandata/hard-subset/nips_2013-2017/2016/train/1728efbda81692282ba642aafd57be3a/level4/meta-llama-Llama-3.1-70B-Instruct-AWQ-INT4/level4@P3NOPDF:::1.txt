This paper introduces a spectral analysis using the Koopman operator defined in a proper reproducing kernel Hilbert space. The authors follow the standard pathway to define a set of (kernel) approaches to analyze nonlinear dynamical systems with empirical data. The eigendecomposition of the projection of the Koopman operator results in efficiency gains in both synthetic and real data analysis problems. This paper presents a significant kernel construction for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems, with a well-motivated contribution that is experimentally validated.

However, there are some concerns that the authors may want to address. Firstly, it is not clear what the substantial differences are between the current work and the paper by M.O. Williams, I.G. Kevrekidis, and C.W. Rowley (2015), where the Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes were already introduced to analyze nonlinear system dynamics. The authors would benefit from providing comments and discussion to clarify the main differences. Additionally, section 4 could be improved with more in-depth analysis.

The field of nonlinear dynamical system analysis with kernels is vast, and some relevant references are missing. The authors may want to consider citing papers by L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al, Rojo-Alvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, or M. O. Franz and B. B. Schölkopf. Although the Koopman operator is generally defined for deterministic systems, the authors should provide more comments on its performance in stochastic settings, which are more realistic and important. A toy experiment would also be welcome to further illustrate this point.

The authors clarify that the estimation procedure does not necessarily require a sequence but rather a collection of pairs of consecutive observables, where each pair is supposed to be nonlinear pointwise related. However, it is unclear what implications this assumption has in multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relations. The authors should discuss what benefits the proposed kernel construction provides in such cases. Furthermore, they should elaborate on the causal assumption inherent in the setting.

Regarding the experimental settings, they seem not very ambitious. The prediction via premiaing follows a standard well-known approach. Under the "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" subsection, there is a lack of discussion on the connections and experimental comparison between the proposed approach and the literature on clustering time series (e.g., through kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods). The sequential change-point detection experiment would be more convincing with a comparison to state-of-the-art online change detection with kernels. Some more convincing experiments are expected to support the development.

On a minor note, the paper is very well-written and clear. However, there are two minor typos: the indices on tau-1 vs. tau should be checked, and the dot should be replaced with a comma in k(xi,xj) (last equation on page 7).
