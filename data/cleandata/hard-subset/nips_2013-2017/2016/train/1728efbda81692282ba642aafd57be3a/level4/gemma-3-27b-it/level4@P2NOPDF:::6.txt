This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), and an associated algorithm for performing this analysis using data from dynamical systems. The authors draw connections between concepts originating in fluid dynamics and machine learning, positioning the Koopman operator as a potentially valuable tool for the latter. The proposed method is demonstrated on a damped linear 1D system, the Henon map, and a real-world locomotion dataset, with performance compared to dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), principal component analysis (PCA), and kernel PCA. The results on the locomotion data are not entirely conclusive.

The work offers a principled extension of DMD, conceptually similar to the broader trend of “kernelizing” existing techniques—a lineage that includes kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, and Gaussian process bandits. While the theoretical developments are sound, they feel largely anticipated given existing kernel methods; leveraging the structure of an RKHS naturally leads to algorithms involving eigendecomposition on finite-dimensional matrices when operating on linear operators. The paper is well-written and serves as a useful introduction to the Koopman operator for machine learning researchers unfamiliar with its origins in fluid dynamics, and I appreciate efforts to bridge disciplines.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more compelling articulation of its practical relevance to machine learning. The presented setup focuses on deterministic dynamical systems – function mappings from a space to itself without stochastic components – which is less common in many ML applications where noise and randomness are prevalent. While this is not necessarily a limitation, the authors need to more explicitly address the potential applicability of their method within the machine learning landscape. 

Specifically, I encourage the authors to highlight concrete use cases where Koopman analysis could provide an advantage. Robotics represents a relevant domain for inference and learning in dynamical systems, but the paper does not explain what benefits this approach might offer roboticists. A similar argument could be made for any other application area. Demonstrating competitiveness—even partial—with existing state-of-the-art techniques would be crucial.

Regarding the algorithmic implementation, it remains unclear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires explicit feature space representation. If this is the case, it represents a disadvantage compared to kernel PCA, and this limitation should be clearly stated. Furthermore, including the performance of standard DMD on both the toy example and the Henon map would facilitate a clearer understanding of the improvements offered by the proposed method in these simpler scenarios. 

In summary, while this paper presents a theoretically sound and clearly explained extension of DMD, its potential impact on the machine learning community is not yet fully justified. Addressing the questions of practical application and algorithmic clarity will be essential for increasing the paper’s value to a broader audience.
