Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a kernelized approach to Koopman spectral analysis: Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels (kernel DMD). The authors define the Koopman operator on an RKHS feature map and estimate its spectral objects from finite trajectories by: (i) computing orthogonal bases of the Krylov subspace in feature space via Gram–Schmidt/QR on the kernel Gram matrix, (ii) forming the projected Koopman operator, and (iii) extracting Ritz values/vectors as Koopman eigenvalues/modes. They provide two derivations: a direct RKHS Arnoldi/QR formulation and a numerically robust variant that projects onto kernel principal components (POD in feature space). The method returns a finite set of Koopman modes that serve as nonlinear features for downstream tasks (short-term prediction via preimage, dynamics recognition via subspace angles, and sequential change-point detection). Experiments on a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, and motion capture data illustrate feasibility.

This work sits within the DMD/Koopman literature [Schmid 2010; Rowley et al. 2009; Tu et al. 2014 (exact DMD); Chen et al. 2012; Jovanović et al. 2014; optimized DMD], and the use of richer observables via dictionaries as in Extended DMD (EDMD) [Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, J. Nonlinear Science, 2015]. EDMD explicitly includes “kernel EDMD,” where the dictionary is implicitly defined by a kernel map and computations proceed via Gram matrices. The present manuscript’s RKHS-Krylov/Arnoldi view and the robustified kernel-POD projection are very close in spirit to kernel EDMD; the paper would benefit from a precise positioning relative to EDMD’s kernel formulation, including when the proposed QR/Arnoldi and EDMD’s least-squares-based Galerkin approximation coincide or differ (e.g., assumptions on spanning sets, centering, and truncation).

Strengths
- Clear mathematical formulation connecting Koopman spectral analysis, Krylov subspaces, and RKHS machinery, with proofs linking projected operators to empirical Ritz values/vectors.
- Practical robustification via kernel PCA/POD that mirrors best practices in DMD for ill-conditioning.
- Conceptually appealing use of Koopman modes as nonlinear features for prediction, recognition, and change detection; code availability is a plus.
- Writing is generally clear and well organized.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to EDMD (including kernel EDMD) is not clearly articulated; the present contribution risks being a re-derivation unless key differences are emphasized.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: datasets are small, comparisons to strong baselines are absent, and metrics are largely qualitative.
- Stochastic/noisy settings—central in practice—are not empirically addressed; theoretical remarks are brief.
- Some assumptions are strong (distinct eigenvalues; ψ=0 or small; eigenfunctions contained in the RKHS and spanned by selected kernel PCs), and their practical implications are not discussed.
- Limited guidance on hyperparameter selection (kernel, bandwidth, truncation), regularization, computational complexity, and sensitivity.

Quality
The core derivations are sound given the stated assumptions. The move to kernel PCA (Section 4) is reasonable and aligns with exact DMD’s SVD truncation. However, more detail is needed on numerical aspects: regularization (e.g., Tikhonov in Gram inverses), truncation criteria, computational and memory complexity for long trajectories or large mini-batches, and sensitivity to kernel choice/hyperparameters. Experiments should include quantitative prediction errors, spectral approximation accuracy, and robustness to noise.

Clarity
The manuscript is largely clear. Section 4 would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of how kernel PCA bases mitigate ill-conditioning and how the procedure relates to EDMD’s Galerkin approximation. Please also add pseudocode and a complexity discussion for both variants. Minor typos are listed below.

Originality
The RKHS formulation of Koopman/DMD and kernel-based lifting are established ideas (EDMD, kernel EDMD). The paper’s QR/Krylov perspective and explicit theorems are useful, but the contribution will be stronger with a careful comparison to EDMD and exact DMD, highlighting any distinct theoretical guarantees, numerical benefits, or broader applicability.

Significance
Koopman-based features are relevant across ML and scientific computing. If the authors clarify novelty and provide stronger empirical evidence (including stochastic/noisy cases and comparisons to kernel time-series baselines), the work could be impactful.

Requests and suggestions
1) Positioning vs EDMD/Kernel EDMD (Williams et al., 2015):  
- Precisely compare the proposed RKHS-QR projection to EDMD’s least-squares/Galerkin approach. Under what conditions are they equivalent? When does your method provide advantages (e.g., numerical stability, sample efficiency, ability to exploit centering/POD, computational cost)?  
- Discuss “exact DMD” and optimized DMD connections and whether analogous kernel variants arise from your framework.

2) Stochastic/noisy dynamics:  
- Expand on the Koopman operator for random dynamical systems (expectation-based operator) and practical estimation under observation/process noise.  
- Include a toy experiment with injected noise (e.g., noisy Hénon or noisy linear system) to assess spectral bias/variance and prediction robustness.

3) Use of unordered consecutive pairs and causal assumptions:  
- Clarify the implications of using a collection of consecutive pairs rather than a single long trajectory, especially for multidimensional series with weak instantaneous coupling.  
- Discuss identifiability and sample complexity when pointwise relations are weak; consider time-delay embeddings (Takens-type delays) as a practical remedy, and whether your kernel construct readily supports delay coordinates.  
- Explicitly state the causal/Markov assumptions and how violations affect estimation.

4) Experiments:  
- Prediction: Compare to EDMD/kernel EDMD and to standard nonlinear predictors (e.g., kernel autoregression) with quantitative error metrics and confidence intervals.  
- Dynamics recognition: Relate to time-series clustering baselines (kernel k-means, subspace clustering with kernels/principal angles) and report classification accuracy.  
- Change-point detection: Compare to state-of-the-art kernel change-point methods (e.g., kernel CUSUM and MMD-based detectors; Harchaoui & Cappe, 2007; Harchaoui & Bach, 2007; Arlot et al., 2012), especially on complex/chaotic signals.  
- Provide ablations on kernel choice, bandwidth selection (e.g., median heuristic vs cross-validation), and truncation.

5) Related work:  
- Please include additional relevant references in kernel methods for dynamical systems/system identification and time-series analysis, e.g., works by L. Ralaivola and F. d’Alché-Buc (kernel state-space models), Tuia et al. (kernel methods for time series in remote sensing), Rojo-Álvarez et al. (kernel adaptive filtering), Mouattamid & Schaback (kernel-based system identification/sampling operators), and Franz & Schölkopf (polynomial kernels and Volterra/Wiener theory).

6) Practical details:  
- Describe selection of truncation rank in kernel PCA, regularization of Gram matrices, and computational complexity (time/memory) as functions of τ and the number of PCs.  
- Clarify preimage recovery: initialization, neighbor selection, convergence, and failure modes.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid and clear mathematical framing of Koopman spectral estimation in RKHS, with a practical, stabilized algorithm.
- Potentially useful feature extraction perspective with broad applicability.
- Code available; writing mostly clear.

Cons against acceptance
- Novelty relative to (kernel) EDMD and exact DMD is insufficiently delineated.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited and lacks strong baselines and quantitative metrics.  
- Noisy/stochastic settings and practical guidance are underdeveloped.

Minor comments
- Check indices τ−1 vs τ throughout, especially around centered Gram matrix definitions.  
- Replace the dot with a comma in k(xi, xj) (last equation on p. 7).  
- Proofread Section 1 (“a way to robustly it” → “a way to make it robust”).

Overall, this is a promising paper; with a clearer positioning vis-à-vis EDMD/kernel EDMD and a stronger, comparative empirical section (including stochastic scenarios), it could make a valuable contribution.
