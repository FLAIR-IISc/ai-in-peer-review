

The paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors build upon the Koopman operator framework, which represents nonlinear dynamics as an infinite-dimensional linear operator acting on observables, and extend it to RKHS. The core contribution is a modal decomposition algorithm that performs spectral analysis of the Koopman operator by calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the operator projection onto the subspace spanned by these bases. The authors demonstrate applications in feature extraction for dynamical systems, including state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

This work extends the well-established Koopman operator framework [16, 20] and Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) [25, 26], which has gained significant traction in fluid dynamics and engineering. The paper builds upon Williams et al.'s extended DMD [35], which also uses pre-determined basis functions, but introduces kernel functions to provide richer, nonlinear basis representations rather than the linear monomials used in standard DMD. The authors address numerical stability issues through POD-based robustification, a technique previously applied to standard DMD [8, 33].

Strengths:
1. Theoretical soundness: The paper provides a mathematically rigorous extension of DMD using RKHS, with clear theorems and proofs for the kernelized DMD algorithm.
2. Practical implementation: The authors address numerical instability through POD-based robustification, which is a well-justified and practical approach to improve algorithmic stability.
3. Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the connection between Koopman operator theory, DMD, and the kernelized extension.
4. Empirical validation: The method is tested on synthetic and real-world datasets, including the Hénon map and locomotion data, demonstrating reasonable performance.

Weaknesses:
1. Limited novelty: The core contribution is the kernelization of existing DMD techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach to spectral analysis. The connection to Williams et al.'s extended DMD [35] is not sufficiently clarified, making it difficult to distinguish the conceptual contribution.
2. Significance: The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why the kernelized approach offers substantial advantages over existing methods. The applications (state prediction, change-point detection, dynamics recognition) are common in the field, and the paper does not clearly show how the kernelized approach provides superior performance or new insights compared to standard DMD or other methods.
3. Comparison: The paper lacks thorough comparisons with state-of-the-art methods for dynamical system analysis, including recent approaches like recurrent neural networks [15, 19, 31], which have shown strong performance in similar tasks.
4. Related work: The paper does not adequately differentiate itself from Williams et al.'s extended DMD [35], which also uses pre-determined basis functions (though not necessarily kernel-based).

Quality: The technical content is sound, with proper mathematical treatment of the kernelized DMD algorithm. The theoretical foundations are solid, and the authors provide a detailed derivation of the algorithm. However, the paper would benefit from more extensive experimental comparisons with existing methods to better support claims about performance advantages.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and organized, with clear mathematical derivations. The connection between Koopman operator theory and the proposed algorithm is well-explained, and the empirical examples help illustrate the method's application.

Originality: The core contribution is the extension of DMD to RKHS, which is a natural extension of existing work. While mathematically sound, this does not represent a significant conceptual leap beyond previous work on Koopman spectral analysis. The novelty is limited to the kernelization aspect, which is a straightforward extension of existing techniques.

Significance: The applications presented are relevant, but the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why the kernelized approach offers substantial advantages over simpler or more established methods. The significance of the work is not clearly articulated, and it's unclear how this advances the state of the art compared to existing approaches.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- The paper makes a technically sound contribution to extending DMD using kernel methods.
- The robustification via POD bases addresses a practical issue in DMD implementations.
- The empirical validation on synthetic and real-world data provides evidence of the method's utility.

Con arguments for rejection:
- The novelty is limited to kernelizing an existing technique, with insufficient demonstration of advantages over existing methods.
- The paper fails to clearly articulate why this approach is better than alternatives, particularly extended DMD [35].
- The applications do not sufficiently demonstrate the unique value of the kernelized approach.

While the paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD using RKHS, its novelty is limited, and it fails to convincingly demonstrate the significance of the approach compared to existing methods. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons with state-of-the-art methods for dynamical system analysis and a clearer articulation of the unique advantages of the kernelized approach.
