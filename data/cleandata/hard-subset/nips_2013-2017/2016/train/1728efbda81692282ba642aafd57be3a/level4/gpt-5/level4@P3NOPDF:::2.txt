Summary
The paper proposes a spectral approach for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator framework. The core idea is to represent the composition of the system’s dynamics as a linear operator acting on observables, and to estimate an eigendecomposition of this operator from trajectory data using tools akin to kernel PCA and subspace methods. In particular, the paper considers forward dynamics through compositions (e.g., x(t+2) = f(f(x(t)))) and uses the resulting spectral objects for embedding and recognition of dynamical systems. Empirical results suggest the method performs well on the presented tasks.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Framing dynamical evolution as a linear operator on observables is elegant and connects nonlinear dynamics to linear spectral analysis.
- Methodological alignment: The use of kernel methods to approximate the operator in a high-dimensional feature space is natural and leverages well-established machinery (kernel PCA, subspace identification).
- Practical utility: A common linear-algebraic pipeline (eigendecomposition) provides potentially interpretable modes and embeddings useful for recognition and change detection tasks.
- Empirical promise: The reported experiments indicate the approach can produce meaningful embeddings and achieve good recognition accuracy.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited novelty: Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) and its extensions for nonlinear systems already exist in the dynamical systems literature. The paper’s main technical step appears to be a kernelization of DMD/Koopman operator estimation, which has been explored previously. The manuscript should more clearly articulate what is fundamentally new (beyond kernelization) and how it differs from existing DMD, Extended DMD, subspace DMD, and related kernel Koopman approaches.
- Insufficient baselines: The evaluation focuses on favorable examples but omits comparisons to strong alternatives for modeling and recognizing dynamical systems (e.g., recurrent neural networks, state-space models, HMMs, ARIMA/ARX, and standard or extended DMD). Without such comparisons, it is difficult to judge the method’s relative merits.
- Unclear formulation: The description conflates state evolution x(t+1) with the Koopman operator, which formally acts on observables g via (Kg)(x) = g(f(x)). The emphasis on the two-step composition x(t+2) = f(f(x(t))) is not well motivated; explain why two-step dynamics are essential, or clarify that the approach extends to arbitrary compositions.
- Theoretical guarantees: The paper does not provide consistency results, approximation error bounds, or conditions under which the kernel eigendecomposition recovers Koopman spectra. Practical guidance on sample complexity and the effect of noise is missing.
- Sensitivity and robustness: The method’s dependence on kernel choice, bandwidth/regularization, number of eigencomponents retained, and trajectory length is not analyzed. Robustness to measurement noise, process noise, and nonstationarities/change points is unclear.
- Scalability: Kernel methods can be computationally expensive in both time (eigendecomposition) and memory (Gram matrices). The paper should discuss complexity, low-rank approximations, and scalability to longer trajectories or higher-dimensional data.
- Empirical scope: The applications do not convincingly demonstrate uniqueness or clear advantages over existing methods. More challenging, diverse, or real-world datasets would strengthen the case.

Questions for the authors
1. What is the precise novelty relative to standard DMD, Extended DMD, kernel DMD, and subspace DMD? Which components of the algorithm are new?
2. Why focus on the two-step composition? Does the approach benefit from multi-step compositions, and how does this impact estimation error and stability?
3. How sensitive is performance to the choice of kernel, kernel parameters, and regularization? Provide sensitivity plots or ablations.
4. Do you have theoretical guarantees (e.g., convergence, identifiability under certain observability conditions, or bounds under noise)?
5. How does the method compare to strong baselines like RNNs/LSTMs, state-space models, and standard/extended DMD on recognition and change detection?
6. What is the computational complexity and memory footprint? Can the method handle large datasets via Nyström or random feature approximations?

Suggestions for improvement
- Positioning: Provide a thorough related-work comparison, with clear statements of what is novel and a side-by-side algorithmic comparison to existing DMD/Koopman kernel methods.
- Clarity: Formalize the Koopman operator on observables and carefully distinguish state space from function space. Explain the rationale for multi-step compositions and define all notation consistently.
- Experiments: Add strong baselines (RNNs, HMMs, AR models, standard/extended DMD), include sensitivity analyses (kernel, regularization, eigen-spectra truncation), and evaluate robustness to noise and limited data. Consider more challenging datasets and report standard metrics with confidence intervals.
- Practicality: Discuss computational costs and include strategies for scaling (low-rank kernels, minibatch approximations). Provide pseudocode and release code/data for reproducibility.
- Theory: If possible, add propositions on consistency or error bounds under mild assumptions (e.g., ergodicity, richness of observables, bounded noise).

Minor comments
- Tighten the exposition around the Koopman framework; avoid suggesting the operator acts directly on states.
- Clarify what “recognition” entails (classification of system identity, regime, or parameter setting) and how embeddings are used downstream.
- Proofread for grammar and streamline notation to reduce ambiguity.

Overall assessment
The paper taps into a powerful and timely framework for analyzing nonlinear dynamics and offers a kernel-based spectral approach with encouraging preliminary results. However, in its current form, the novelty appears limited and the empirical and theoretical support is not yet sufficient to establish clear advantages over existing methods. Strengthening the positioning, adding rigorous comparisons and analyses, and clarifying the formulation would substantially improve the contribution.
