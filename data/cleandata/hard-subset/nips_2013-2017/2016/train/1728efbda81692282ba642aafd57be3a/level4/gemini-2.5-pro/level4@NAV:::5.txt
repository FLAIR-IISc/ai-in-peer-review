This manuscript introduces an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), terming the resulting algorithm Kernel DMD (KDMD). The method is then demonstrated on several simple dynamical systems. This approach represents a powerful generalization of DMD, as it allows the use of feature maps, via the kernel definition, to connect the decomposition more directly to specific features of interest in the data.

My primary concern is the paper's overall lack of clarity. The manuscript is difficult to follow, even for a reader familiar with the topic. The authors frequently opt for dense mathematical notation to convey simple concepts that could be more effectively explained with concise sentences. Conversely, other sections are unnecessarily verbose. This imbalance makes the work challenging to penetrate, even for specialists, and several key points require further clarification.

In its current form, the paper is too cryptic for publication in the proceedings of the Neural Information Processing Systems conference. However, the contribution is significant, and I strongly encourage the authors to revise the manuscript with a focus on pedagogical presentation. A more accessible version of this paper would likely be a highly impactful and widely cited article.

**I. MAJOR REMARKS**

1.  The presentation is often verbose where it could be substantially simplified. For instance, the sentence, "Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point," could be communicated much more succinctly.

2.  Section 4 seems superfluous in the context of a short conference paper, as its material was partially addressed in the introduction.

3.  Section 5 should be supported with illustrations to be effective. Alternatively, its key takeaways could be summarized and integrated into the conclusion.

4.  In contrast to the previous point, Section 6 is overly condensed and would benefit from being developed with the more descriptive approach taken in Section 5.

**II. MINOR REMARKS**

1.  There appears to be a typographical error in the description of the nonlinear (NL) toy problem, as the system described is linear.

2.  The first two problem examples are polynomial systems, which is a domain where KDMD is expected to perform particularly well, especially when the kernel is well-chosen [1]. This point should be expanded upon with greater detail, similar to the style of discussion in Section 5.

**REFERENCES**
