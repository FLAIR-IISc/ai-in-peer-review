### Summary

This paper introduces a method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) with reproducing kernels. The central idea is to analyze the Koopman operator—an infinite-dimensional linear operator that describes the evolution of observable functions of a system's state—within the framework of a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). By lifting the state space into an RKHS, the authors derive a "kernel DMD" algorithm that can approximate the Koopman eigenfunctions using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions provided by the kernel. The method is presented in two forms: a direct approach based on Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization in the feature space, and a more numerically robust variant that projects the data onto principal directions found via kernel PCA. The authors frame this analysis as a feature extraction technique for dynamical systems and demonstrate its utility in several applications, including short-term prediction, sequential change-point detection, and the embedding and recognition of dynamics.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled Extension of DMD:** The paper presents an elegant and theoretically grounded extension of the DMD framework. By leveraging kernel methods, the approach overcomes a key limitation of standard DMD, which typically relies on a linear basis of observables. The formulation in RKHS allows for the modeling of highly nonlinear dynamics in a non-parametric way. The theorems provided clearly connect the algorithm's outputs to the underlying Koopman operator's spectral properties.

2.  **Novelty for the Machine Learning Community:** While DMD and Koopman operator theory are established in the fluid dynamics and physics communities, their connection to kernel methods is novel and highly relevant to the NIPS audience. The paper serves as a good bridge between these fields, framing a powerful technique from dynamical systems theory in the language of machine learning.

3.  **Thoughtful Algorithm Design:** The authors astutely identify the potential numerical instability of a direct kernelized DMD and propose a robust version based on projection onto kernel principal components (POD bases). This practical consideration significantly increases the potential applicability of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Originality in the Context of Kernel Methods:** The core technical contribution is the "kernelization" of the DMD algorithm. While elegant, this can be viewed as an application of a well-established technique (the kernel trick) to an existing algorithm. The paper acknowledges that the robust version is similar to Extended DMD [35], which uses pre-determined basis functions. A more thorough discussion of the distinctions and advantages over Extended DMD would be beneficial to better situate the paper's contribution.

2.  **Unconvincing Experimental Evaluation and Lack of Baselines:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in its empirical evaluation. While the applications are interesting illustrations of the method's potential, they lack the rigor needed to demonstrate its significance or superiority.
    *   **Prediction:** The short-term prediction results are not compared against standard time-series forecasting models like Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), LSTMs, or Gaussian Process Dynamical Models. Without these baselines, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers any practical advantage.
    *   **Change-Point Detection:** Similarly, the change-point detection experiment is compared to only one other kernel-based method. A comparison against more diverse or state-of-the-art change-point detection algorithms is needed.
    *   **Dynamics Recognition:** The embedding and recognition application is compelling, but again, lacks a comparison to standard approaches for time-series classification, such as using an RNN or extracting statistical features for a standard classifier.

3.  **Scalability and Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not discuss the computational complexity or scalability of the proposed method. The reliance on Gram matrices suggests a computational cost that scales poorly with the number of time steps (τ), potentially limiting its use on long time series. Furthermore, the choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., the width of a Gaussian kernel) is critical to performance, but the paper provides little guidance or analysis on hyperparameter selection.

### Detailed Comments

The paper is well-written and the mathematical derivations are clear. The concept of using Koopman theory for feature extraction is appealing. However, to strengthen the paper and make a more compelling case for its acceptance, I would suggest the following improvements:

*   **Clarify the Relationship with Extended DMD:** The authors mention that their robust procedure is "similar" to Extended DMD [35]. It would be very helpful to explicitly detail this relationship. Is kernel DMD a special case of Extended DMD where the basis functions are implicitly defined by the kernel? What are the theoretical and practical advantages of the kernel formulation over explicitly choosing a dictionary of functions?

*   **Strengthen the Empirical Validation:** The most critical improvement would be to include comparisons against relevant machine learning baselines in Section 7. Demonstrating superior or even competitive performance against established methods like RNNs or GPs on the tasks of prediction or classification would significantly bolster the paper's claims of significance.

*   **Address Practical Concerns:** A brief discussion on computational complexity (e.g., O(τ³ M) for τ time steps and M sequences) and potential strategies for scaling up the method (e.g., using random Fourier features or Nyström approximation) would be a valuable addition. A sensitivity analysis regarding the choice of kernel and its parameters on one of the tasks would also enhance the paper's practical value.

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically sound and interesting fusion of ideas from dynamical systems and machine learning. Its main weakness is the lack of a strong empirical demonstration of its utility compared to existing methods in the machine learning literature. While the theoretical contribution is noteworthy, the significance for the NIPS community would be much clearer with a more rigorous experimental evaluation.
