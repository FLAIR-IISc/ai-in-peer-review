This review provides a summary and evaluation of the paper "Dynamic Mode Decomposition with Reproducing Kernels for Koopman Spectral Analysis."

### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to analyze the spectral properties of the Koopman operator, a linear operator that globally describes nonlinear dynamics, within the rich feature space induced by a kernel function. The authors propose an algorithm, "kernel DMD," which approximates the Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions by performing a modal decomposition on finite-length time-series data mapped into an RKHS. This is achieved by calculating an orthogonal basis for the Krylov subspace in the feature space and then performing an eigendecomposition.

Recognizing potential numerical instability, the authors also introduce a robust version of the algorithm that projects the data onto its principal components in the feature space (via kernel PCA) before decomposition. The paper frames this analysis as a feature extraction technique for dynamical systems and demonstrates its utility across several machine learning applications, including state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and recognition of dynamic patterns, using both synthetic and real-world datasets.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

This is a well-written and well-organized paper that introduces an interesting and powerful extension of DMD. The proposed method appears to be technically sound, and the connection between Koopman theory and kernel methods is elegant.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally clear and logically structured. It provides the necessary background on the Koopman operator and DMD before carefully developing the proposed kernel-based extension. The flow from theory to algorithms and then to applications is easy to follow.

2.  **Originality and Significance:** The synthesis of Koopman spectral analysis and kernel methods is a significant and novel contribution. Standard DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using a linear combination of observables. By leveraging the kernel trick, this work provides a principled framework for using a much richer, nonlinear set of basis functions, which can potentially capture complex dynamics more accurately. Framing the resulting Koopman modes as features for downstream machine learning tasks is a valuable perspective that broadens the applicability of this analysis.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical development is solid, and the algorithms are well-motivated. The experiments, while not including exhaustive comparisons to all state-of-the-art methods, effectively illustrate the method's capabilities and usefulness in diverse applications.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Clarity of the Koopman Operator Definition in RKHS:** A key point of confusion lies in the definition of the Koopman operator in the feature space, `KH`, in Section 3. Equation (7), `(KH phi)(x) = phi o f(x)`, defines `KH` as an operator acting on the feature map `phi`, which is a function from the state space `M` to the RKHS `H`. However, the text describes `KH` as "a linear operator in H." This phrasing is ambiguous, as an operator *in* H would typically map elements of H to other elements of H. The current definition suggests `KH` operates on a space of H-valued functions, not on H itself. Clarifying the precise domain and codomain of `KH` would resolve this ambiguity and strengthen the reader's confidence in the subsequent derivations.

2.  **Assumptions and Proof of Theorem 2:** The proof and assumptions of Theorem 2, which connects the robust algorithm to the true Koopman spectral properties, could benefit from further explanation.
    *   The theorem assumes that a true Koopman eigenfunction `phi_j` can be represented as `phi_j(x) = <phi(x), kappa_j>` and, critically, that the element `kappa_j` lies within the subspace spanned by the kernel principal components of the data. This is a strong assumption, implying that the dynamically important eigenfunctions are well-captured by the directions of highest variance in the feature space. It would be valuable for the authors to discuss the conditions under which this assumption might hold and the potential limitations of the method when it is violated.
    *   The proof itself could be made more transparent. A more detailed walkthrough of the algebraic steps, particularly in translating the pointwise eigenfunction equation into the final matrix equation for the left eigenvectors of `F̂`, would enhance readability and make the argument easier to verify.

3.  **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper appropriately cites extended DMD (eDMD) as related work. However, the connection could be explored in greater detail. Since eDMD also uses a user-defined dictionary of basis functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, a direct comparison would be illuminating. Highlighting the conceptual and practical differences between using an explicit, pre-defined dictionary (as in eDMD) versus an implicit, potentially infinite-dimensional feature space via kernels would better position the contribution of this work.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and elegant combination of Koopman theory and kernel methods, extending an important technique in a principled way.
*   It is very well-written, with a solid theoretical foundation and clear algorithmic descriptions.
*   The work demonstrates significant practical utility by successfully applying the extracted dynamic features to a variety of relevant machine learning tasks.
*   The topic is of high interest to the NIPS community, bridging dynamical systems theory with mainstream machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   Key theoretical points, including the definition of the operator `KH` and the strong assumptions underpinning Theorem 2, lack sufficient clarity and justification.
*   A more in-depth comparison to closely related methods, such as extended DMD, would strengthen the paper's contribution.

### **Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a strong central idea. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarity in the theoretical exposition rather than fundamental flaws in the methodology. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the points of confusion regarding the operator definition and the assumptions of Theorem 2 in the final version. These clarifications would significantly improve an already excellent paper.
