This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and develops an algorithm to perform this analysis using measurement data from a dynamical system. The authors demonstrate the algorithm and analysis using two simple dynamical systems - a damped linear 1D dynamics and a chaotic map - and apply their method to real-world locomotion data. They compare the performance with standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA, obtaining somewhat unclear results.

The paper derives an interesting extension of DMD, somewhat along the lines of older work on "kernelizing" techniques phrased in terms of linear operators (such as kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, GP bandits, etc.). The authors provide some experimental results on largely artificial problems. The paper is overall clearly written and has educational value for people unfamiliar with concepts used in fluid dynamics.

I am generally supportive of work that introduces machine learners to techniques in different disciplines. This paper presents a decomposition technique for functions from M to M, used to construct a dynamical system without any stochastic components. While I am not personally aware of such setups being used in machine learning (typically, people use dynamical systems where random effects play a role, like innovations and noise), I may be missing something.

However, I believe the authors need to do more work to convince machine learning readers of the practical usefulness of these techniques. Potential use cases and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods in relevant domains are necessary. For instance, in robotics, where inference and learning in dynamical systems are crucial, what would Koopman analysis offer? How would it compete with existing methods? The authors should provide more context and demonstrate the benefits of their approach in a more concrete setting.

While the theoretical derivations are well-executed, they are not particularly surprising, given the similarity to kernel PCA or kernel ICA, or follow-up work on kernel independence tests or "kernel Bayes rule". The paper deals with a linear operator, and the results are a natural consequence of working in an RKHS, leading to algorithms that perform eigendecomposition on finite-D matrices.

Some details require clarification. It is not entirely clear whether the modal decomposition algorithm needs an explicit representation in feature space. If so, this is a limitation compared to kernel PCA, and the authors should make this more explicit. Additionally, it would be beneficial to show the performance of standard DMD on the toy example and the Henon map to demonstrate the differences in this simple setup.

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting extension of DMD, it would benefit from more context, comparisons, and demonstrations of its practical usefulness in relevant machine learning domains.
