The manuscript, “Koopman Spectral Analysis of Nonlinear Dynamical Systems with Reproducing Kernels,” extends dynamical mode decomposition (DMD) to the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), resulting in an algorithm termed KDMD. This allows for the use of feature maps to define the kernel, effectively linking DMD more directly to specific features of interest, and represents a powerful generalization of DMD. However, my primary concern is the paper’s significant lack of clarity. The authors frequently employ overly complex mathematical phrasing where simpler, more concise sentences would suffice. Conversely, other sections are unnecessarily verbose. This combination hinders understanding, even for specialists in the field, and several points require clarification. As it stands, the manuscript is too cryptic for publication in the Neural Information Processing Systems conference proceedings. Nevertheless, I strongly encourage the authors to revise it with a focus on pedagogical clarity; the resulting article has the potential to be highly cited.

**I. MAJOR REMARKS**

1. The presentation is often unnecessarily verbose and could benefit from simplification. For example, the statement: "Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point," could be readily summarized in a single, clear sentence.

2. Section 4 feels superfluous in a short paper and largely repeats material already presented in the introduction.

3. Section 5 would be more impactful if it included illustrative examples, or at least a concise summary of its key findings in the conclusion.

4. Section 6 appears overly condensed and would benefit from the level of detail provided in Section 5.

**II. MINOR REMARKS**

1. There appears to be a typographical error in the description of the nonlinear toy problem, as it appears to be, in fact, linear.

2. The first two example problems are polynomial. KDMD is expected to excel in scenarios where the kernel can be effectively chosen *a priori*, such as in example 1. Further details regarding these examples, similar to the presentation in Section 5, would be beneficial.
