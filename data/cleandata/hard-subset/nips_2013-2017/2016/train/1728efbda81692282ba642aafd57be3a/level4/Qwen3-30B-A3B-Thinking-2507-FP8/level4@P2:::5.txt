

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), creating a method termed Koopman Spectral Analysis with Reproducing Kernels (KDMD). The authors present a theoretical framework for spectral analysis of the Koopman operator using kernel-based feature maps, which generalizes standard DMD by replacing linear monomial basis functions with nonlinear kernel-based representations. The method computes orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS followed by eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator, yielding a modal decomposition of nonlinear dynamics that can serve as a feature extraction procedure. The authors demonstrate applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition using both synthetic and real-world data.

The paper builds on the growing literature around Koopman operator theory and DMD, which has gained traction in physics and engineering for analyzing complex dynamical systems. Previous work such as Williams et al.'s extended DMD uses pre-determined basis functions, while this work uses kernel functions to create more expressive representations. The authors also relate their method to spectral analysis techniques like Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD), noting that while POD identifies principal directions in data, DMD provides direct information about dynamics through eigenvalues and modes.

The paper's main strengths include the theoretical extension of DMD to RKHS, which offers a more flexible framework for capturing nonlinear dynamics. The proposed algorithm provides a principled way to incorporate domain-specific features through kernel selection, potentially enhancing applicability to specific problems. The empirical results on both synthetic and real-world data demonstrate the method's utility across different applications, including prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues that undermine its scientific contribution. The writing is overly verbose in places while being cryptic in others, making it difficult to follow even for specialists. For instance, the explanation of how KDMD generalizes DMD could be simplified: "DMD approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials (like a one-term Taylor expansion), while KDMD uses kernel functions for richer, nonlinear approximations." The current phrasing is unnecessarily complex and obscures the main idea.

Several sections require substantial improvement. Section 4 on "Robustifying with POD Bases" is redundant with the introduction's discussion of DMD robustness and could be removed or significantly condensed. Section 5 on "Related Works" should either be illustrated with concrete examples or summarized in the conclusion, as it currently reads like a literature review without clear connections to the paper's contributions. Section 6's empirical examples are condensed too much and should follow the more detailed structure of Section 5 to better demonstrate the method's value.

I also note a potential issue with the toy nonlinear example in Section 6, which appears to be linear rather than nonlinear as claimed. The first two examples (the toy system and Hénon map) are actually polynomial systems where KDMD should excel, but the paper fails to sufficiently explain why kernel selection matters for these cases. More details about kernel choice and its impact on performance would strengthen the paper.

The paper's quality is technically sound, with a correct mathematical derivation of the KDMD algorithm. However, the poor writing quality significantly reduces its accessibility and impact. The clarity criterion is not met, as the paper fails to adequately explain concepts in a way that would allow experts to reproduce the method or understand its significance.

The originality is moderate, as the extension of DMD to RKHS is a reasonable generalization of existing work, but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from related approaches like Williams et al.'s extended DMD. The significance is promising but not fully realized due to the paper's presentation issues.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1. Revising the writing to be more concise and pedagogical, replacing verbose mathematical descriptions with clear explanations
2. Removing redundant sections (particularly Section 4) or significantly condensing them
3. Expanding Section 6 with the same level of detail as Section 5 to better demonstrate the method's value
4. Clarifying the toy example and explaining why kernel selection matters for polynomial systems
5. Providing a more focused discussion of how this work differs from and improves upon extended DMD

The core idea of KDMD is valuable and has the potential to be highly influential in both theoretical and applied dynamics. However, as written, the paper's poor clarity prevents it from making its full scientific contribution. I strongly encourage the authors to substantially revise the manuscript to improve its presentation. With these changes, the paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS and likely to receive significant citations.
