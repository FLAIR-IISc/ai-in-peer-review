- Paper presents spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in RKHS and an algorithm to compute it from data.  
- Extends DMD by using kernel methods to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with richer nonlinear bases.  
- Demonstrated on synthetic systems (damped linear, Henon map) and on locomotion data; compared with DMD, PCA, and kernel PCA.  
- Writing is clear and accessible, providing a useful introduction to Koopman operator theory for ML researchers.  
- Mathematical derivations are rigorous and align with known RKHS/kernel method theory.  
- Effectively connects the method to existing techniques in dynamical systems (POD, DMD) and ML (kernel PCA).  
- Strength: novel, principled extension of DMD that can better represent complex nonlinear dynamics.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is limited and experiments (especially locomotion) are not fully convincing or compared to strong baselines.  
- Weakness: paper lacks a clear articulation of specific ML applications where the technique has major advantages.  
- Weakness: computational cost and scalability of operating in RKHS are not sufficiently addressed.  
- Weakness: direct comparisons with standard DMD on toy examples are missing and would clarify benefits/tradeoffs.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of whether explicit feature-space representations are required and how to approximate them.  
- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound, novel extension with solid theoretical contribution and potential value for researchers.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical impact unclear due to limited experiments, weak application focus, and unaddressed scalability concerns.  
- Overall assessment: valuable theoretical contribution but borderline due to limited empirical support and application focus.  
- Recommendation: weak accept, contingent on stronger empirical evaluation and clearer discussion of applications and computational issues.  
- Requested revisions: add direct DMD comparisons on toy examples; evaluate on more challenging real-world datasets; detail relevant ML applications; address computational/feature-space scalability and approximation strategies.
