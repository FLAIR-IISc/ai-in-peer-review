- Paper introduces RKHS-based spectral analysis for the Koopman operator, extending DMD via the kernel trick.  
- Contributions: modal decomposition algorithm, robustification strategy, and supporting numerical experiments.  
- Strengths: technically sound, well-structured, clearly written, with strong theoretical foundations.  
- Numerical results effectively demonstrate practical utility, though comparisons to state-of-the-art are limited (acceptable given focus).  
- Major concern: Section 3.1 contains significant issues around Equation (7) and the proof of Theorem 1 that undermine core theoretical claims.  
- Equation (7) appears redundant relative to Equation (2) and its purpose is unclear.  
- Notational inconsistency: Eq. (7) mixes scalars and operator application (κ_j vs. ⟨φ(x), K_H φ(x)⟩_H), implying an improper application of K_H to scalars.  
- The phrase "by applying the operator K_H to both sides" is incorrect since both sides are scalars; likely intent was to apply K_H to the RKHS function φ, not scalars.  
- Theorem 1 lacks explicit and justified assumptions (e.g., compactness, boundedness, normality of Koopman operator) required for spectral decomposition.  
- Proof implicitly relies on properties like existence of a complete eigenbasis and convergence of RKHS approximations, which are unstated and critical.  
- Authors should clarify which standard operator assumptions are needed and why they are plausible for typical dynamical systems.  
- Authors must distinguish theorem conditions from additional proof-specific assumptions and explain their relationships.  
- If required assumptions are overly restrictive (e.g., linearity or finite dimensionality), applicability of the method would be limited and must be addressed.  
- Minor issues: typo "the the" (Line 55) and incorrect index in κ_j/λ_j notation (Line 153).  
- Recommended fixes: re-express Eq. (7) to clarify K_H action on φ, explicitly state all assumptions for Theorem 1, and justify those assumptions in dynamical systems context.  
- Overall verdict: promising and well-presented work, but unresolved theoretical ambiguities constitute a major flaw; revise theoretical sections before acceptance.
