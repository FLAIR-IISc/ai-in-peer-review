

The paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and introduces an algorithm for this analysis using measurement data from dynamical systems. The authors demonstrate the method on two simple systems—a damped linear 1D system and a chaotic Henon map—and apply it to real-world locomotion data, comparing performance against dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA. The results are somewhat ambiguous, particularly in the real-world application.  

The work extends dynamic mode decomposition in a manner reminiscent of kernel-based techniques (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, and GP bandits), framed through the lens of linear operators in RKHS. While the theoretical derivations are technically sound and the paper is clearly written with educational value for machine learning audiences unfamiliar with fluid dynamics concepts, the practical relevance to machine learning remains underdeveloped. The authors focus on deterministic dynamical systems (functions from M to M without stochastic components), which contrasts with common machine learning applications involving noise or innovations. This raises a critical question: what specific use cases exist in machine learning where this approach offers tangible advantages over existing methods?  

The authors should explicitly address potential applications in domains where dynamical system learning is central, such as robotics. For instance, what unique benefits would Koopman analysis provide to robotics researchers compared to established techniques? Without such justification, the practical utility for machine learning practitioners remains unclear. While the theoretical framework builds logically on known kernel methods (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel independence tests, or "kernel Bayes rule"), the results do not demonstrate significant novelty or competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods in relevant domains.  

Specific technical concerns include:  
1. It is unclear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires explicit feature space representations, which would constitute a limitation relative to kernel PCA. The authors should clarify this.  
2. The paper lacks a direct comparison of standard DMD with their method on the toy examples (damped linear system and Henon map), which would better illustrate the algorithmic differences.  

Overall, the paper makes a reasonable contribution to bridging fluid dynamics and machine learning, but stronger justification for its relevance to machine learning practice—and clearer experimental validation against benchmarks—is needed to support its broader impact.
