This paper introduces a novel approach to Koopman spectral analysis by extending the framework to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs). The core idea is to leverage the expressiveness of kernel functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, potentially offering advantages over traditional methods relying on linear monomials. The authors present a modal decomposition algorithm based on calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition. The method is demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world data, showcasing its potential for feature extraction and subsequent applications like state prediction and dynamics recognition.

The paper is technically sound and well-motivated. The extension of Koopman analysis to RKHS is a logical and potentially powerful step, addressing limitations of existing methods in capturing complex nonlinear dynamics. The theoretical foundation is presented clearly, and the connection to dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) is effectively established. The inclusion of a robustification technique using POD bases is a pragmatic addition, addressing potential ill-conditioning issues. The empirical evaluation with both toy and real-world datasets demonstrates the feasibility of the proposed approach and provides initial validation of its performance.  The applications section, covering prediction, embedding/recognition, and change-point detection, further highlights the potential utility of the extracted features.

Strengths of the paper include its clear presentation, solid theoretical grounding, and practical relevance. The authors successfully bridge the gap between Koopman spectral analysis and kernel methods, offering a potentially valuable tool for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems. The inclusion of a robustification step based on POD demonstrates awareness of practical challenges and a commitment to developing a reliable algorithm.  The exploration of multiple applications expands the scope and impact of the work. 

Weaknesses lie primarily in the limited comparative analysis. While the paper motivates the use of RKHS by pointing out the limitations of linear monomial approximations, a direct comparison with traditional DMD or other Koopman-based techniques on the presented datasets would strengthen the claims of improvement.  Furthermore, the sensitivity of the method to kernel choice, and a discussion of guidelines for selecting appropriate kernels, is missing.  A more in-depth exploration of the kernel parameter (e.g., bandwidth in the Gaussian kernel) and its influence on the results would be beneficial.  While the provided code is appreciated, greater detail in the implementation, especially concerning computational cost and scalability to high-dimensional data, would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of Koopman spectral analysis to RKHS, offering a potentially more expressive framework.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and clear presentation of the method.
*   Practical relevance demonstrated through applications to prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection.
*   Inclusion of a robustification technique to address potential ill-conditioning issues.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of a direct comparative analysis against existing Koopman-based methods.
*   Limited discussion of kernel choice and its impact on performance.
*   Insufficient detail regarding computational complexity and scalability.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential. Addressing the identified weaknesses, particularly through a more comprehensive comparative analysis and a discussion of kernel selection, would substantially enhance its contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
