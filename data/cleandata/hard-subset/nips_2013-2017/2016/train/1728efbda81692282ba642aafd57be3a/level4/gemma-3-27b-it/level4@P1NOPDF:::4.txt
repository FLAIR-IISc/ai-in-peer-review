This paper extends Koopman spectral analysis to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The method is evaluated on both simulated and real data. Overall, this is a very well-written paper. I would have liked to see two additional aspects explored. First, a comparison to non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis within the simulations would be beneficial. Second, and potentially also addressable through simulations, would be a discussion or exploration of kernel choice and its impact on the results. 

The core contribution lies in bridging Koopman spectral analysis, traditionally applied to linear observables of dynamical systems, with the flexibility of RKHS. This allows for the analysis of nonlinear systems through the lens of Koopman theory, leveraging the powerful tools available within kernel methods. The authors clearly articulate the theoretical foundation and provide a compelling justification for this extension. 

The simulations presented demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed method in recovering underlying dynamics from noisy data. The application to real-world data further strengthens the claim of practical relevance. However, as mentioned above, the absence of a direct comparison to standard Koopman spectral analysis—without the RKHS extension—in the simulation setting leaves a gap in understanding the specific advantages conferred by this approach. While the authors implicitly suggest the benefits through their results, a more direct quantitative comparison would be valuable. It would help to illustrate *when* and *why* the RKHS extension is particularly advantageous.

My second point concerns the choice of kernel. The performance of any kernel method is sensitive to the kernel used. While the paper demonstrates good results with the chosen kernels, a more in-depth discussion of kernel selection would be helpful. For example, exploring the sensitivity of the results to different kernel parameters, or investigating the performance of alternative kernels (e.g., Gaussian, polynomial, or even data-driven kernel learning techniques) could provide further insight. This doesn't necessarily require extensive experimentation, but a thoughtful discussion of the trade-offs and potential pitfalls associated with kernel selection would enhance the paper. Specifically, it would be useful to discuss how the choice of kernel relates to the smoothness assumptions of the underlying dynamical system.

The writing is clear and concise, and the paper is well-organized. The mathematical notation is generally easy to follow. The related work section is comprehensive and accurately positions the paper within the existing literature. 

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field. The suggested additions—a comparison to non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis and a more detailed discussion of kernel choice—would further strengthen the work and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance.
