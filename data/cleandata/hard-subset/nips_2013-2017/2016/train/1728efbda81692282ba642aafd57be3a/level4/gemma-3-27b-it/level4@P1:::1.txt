This paper introduces a spectral analysis using the Koopman operator defined in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. The authors then follow a standard approach to analyze nonlinear dynamical systems with empirical data, with the eigendecomposition of the projection of the Koopman operator leading to efficiency gains in both synthetic and real data analysis. This work presents a significant kernel construction for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems. The contribution is well-motivated and experimentally validated. However, I have several concerns that I hope the authors will address.

It is not immediately clear what the substantial difference is between the presented work and Williams et al. [35], which already introduced Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for analyzing nonlinear system dynamics. I would appreciate comments and discussion clarifying the main differences between the two approaches. Additionally, the discussion in Section 4 could be improved.

The field of nonlinear dynamical system analysis with kernels is broad, and some relevant references are missing. The authors could consider papers by Ralaivola and d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., Mouattamid and Schaback, or Franz and Schölkopf.

Although the Koopman operator is generally defined for deterministic systems, I would appreciate comments on the performance in stochastic settings, which are often more realistic. The authors briefly touch upon this, but further discussion, and potentially a toy experiment, would be beneficial.

The authors clarify the remark (line 216, page 5) that the estimation procedure does not necessarily require a sequence but rather a collection of pairs of consecutive observables. I am curious about the implications of this assumption in multidimensional time series with potentially weak pointwise relationships. What benefits does the proposed kernel construction offer in this scenario? Furthermore, I would appreciate elaboration on the causal assumption inherent in the setting.

Regarding the experimental settings, I find them somewhat limited. The prediction via preimage follows a standard, well-known approach. Under the "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" subsection, there is a lack of discussion on connections to, and experimental comparison with, the vast literature on clustering time series (e.g., through kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods). For the sequential change-point detection experiment, a comparison to state-of-the-art online change detection methods with kernels would be more compelling (the method used by the authors is known to fail in complex dynamics). More convincing experiments are expected to support the development.

*Minor: The paper is generally well-written and clear. I spotted two minor typos: please check the indices on τ−1 vs. τ, and replace the dot with a comma in k(xi, xj) (last equation on page 7).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of reproducing kernels to Koopman spectral analysis.
*   Clear theoretical formulation and algorithm development.
*   Demonstration of effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   Potential for feature extraction and application to machine learning tasks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clear differentiation from existing work (e.g., Williams et al. [35]).
*   Limited experimental evaluation and comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
*   Insufficient discussion of limitations and assumptions, particularly regarding stochastic systems and causal inference.
*   Missing references to relevant work in kernel-based dynamical systems analysis.



**References:**
[35] M.O. Williams, I.G. Kevrekidis, and C.W. Rowley. A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator: Extending dynamic mode decomposition. *Journal of Nonlinear Science*, 25:1307–1346, 2015.
