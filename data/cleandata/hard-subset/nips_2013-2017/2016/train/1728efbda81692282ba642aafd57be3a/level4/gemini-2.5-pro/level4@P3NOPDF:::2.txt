This paper proposes a spectral method for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems based on the Koopman operator framework. The central idea is that a nonlinear dynamical system can be represented by a linear Koopman operator that acts on a high-dimensional space of observable functions. The paper develops a kernel-based method to approximate the eigendecomposition of this operator from collected system trajectories, drawing an analogy to kernel PCA and subspace analysis. The authors then apply this method to tasks such as the embedding and classification of dynamical systems, demonstrating strong empirical performance.

**Strengths:**

1.  The paper provides a clear and valuable introduction of the Koopman operator framework to the machine learning community. For an audience primarily familiar with kernel methods, the concept of representing function composition (such as the evolution of a dynamical system) as a linear operator is an insightful perspective.

2.  The connection established between Koopman spectral analysis and kernel methods is a significant strength. By framing the problem in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), the authors make this powerful tool from the dynamical systems literature accessible and readily extendable for machine learning researchers.

3.  The proposed method is applied to interesting problems, namely the classification and embedding of dynamical systems, and the empirical results presented are promising.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The primary concern with this paper is its novelty. The core algorithm for data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator, known as Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), is already well-established in the dynamical systems literature. The main contribution of this paper appears to be the application of the kernel trick to this existing method. While this is a valid and useful extension, the paper would be stronger if it more clearly contextualized its contribution with respect to the rich body of work on DMD and its variants. As it stands, the novelty seems confined to this "kernelization."

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Comparison:** The applications presented, while relevant, are not sufficient to demonstrate the unique advantages of this method over other established techniques. The experimental evaluation lacks comparisons to strong, relevant baselines from the machine learning literature for time-series analysis and system identification. For tasks like system recognition or change-point detection, methods such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or various state-space models are standard benchmarks. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to assess the practical significance and relative performance of the proposed approach.

3.  **Lack of Compelling Motivation for Applications:** The paper could better motivate why the Koopman operator approach is particularly well-suited for the chosen tasks. The experiments show that the method works, but they do not compellingly illustrate *why* this specific approach should be preferred over others. What unique properties of the Koopman spectral embedding make it superior for system classification? A more in-depth discussion linking the theoretical properties of the method to its practical utility would make the contributions more impactful.

In summary, the paper presents an elegant fusion of ideas from dynamical systems and kernel methods. However, its contribution is undermined by concerns about novelty and an experimental evaluation that fails to benchmark against standard machine learning methods. The paper would be significantly improved by a more thorough discussion of related work in the DMD literature and a more comprehensive set of experiments that clearly highlight the unique strengths of this approach.
