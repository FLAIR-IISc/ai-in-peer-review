This paper introduces a spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator defined within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). Following this formulation, the authors develop a set of kernel-based methods for analyzing empirical data. The proposed approach, which leverages an eigendecomposition of a projection of the Koopman operator, demonstrates efficiency gains in both synthetic and real-world data analysis tasks. The core contribution—a novel kernel construction for analyzing nonlinear dynamics—is well-motivated and the initial experimental results are promising.

However, there are several concerns that I hope the authors can address to strengthen the paper.

**Major Points:**

1.  **Novelty and Relation to Prior Work:** A primary concern is the precise distinction between the proposed method and the work of Williams et al. [35], "A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator: Extending dynamic mode decomposition." That paper already introduced the use of Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for analyzing nonlinear system dynamics from data. The authors should clearly articulate the novel contributions of their work in relation to [35]. An expanded discussion, particularly in Section 4, that pinpoints the specific theoretical and practical advantages of the proposed RKHS formulation over this and related data-driven Koopman methods is necessary.

2.  **Engagement with Relevant Literature:** The field of nonlinear dynamical system analysis with kernels is extensive. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of its relationship to the broader literature. Several relevant lines of work appear to be missing, and situating the current work within this context would be beneficial. I suggest the authors consider citing and discussing works by researchers such as L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc; Tuia et al.; Rojo-Alvarez et al.; M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback; or M. O. Franz and B. Schölkopf.

3.  **Applicability to Stochastic Systems:** The analysis is currently focused on deterministic systems, where the Koopman operator is classically defined. However, most real-world applications involve stochasticity. While the authors briefly touch upon this, the paper would benefit greatly from an expanded discussion on the method's robustness or applicability in stochastic settings. A simple experiment on a toy stochastic system would be a valuable addition to demonstrate its performance and limitations in the presence of noise.

4.  **Clarification on Data Assumptions and Causality:** The authors state (line 216) that "the estimation procedure itself does not necessarily require a sequence but rather a collection of pairs of consecutive observables." Could the authors elaborate on the implications of this? Specifically, in the context of high-dimensional time series where pointwise relationships between consecutive states might be weak, what are the theoretical and practical advantages of the proposed kernel construction? Furthermore, a discussion on the inherent causal assumptions of this pairwise model would be welcome, as the framework implicitly models a dependency of the future state on the present one.

5.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments provide a proof of concept, their scope could be expanded to more convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed framework.
    *   **Prediction:** The prediction task via pre-imaging follows a well-known approach, but its evaluation could be more comprehensive.
    *   **Clustering:** The "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" section lacks a comparison to the vast literature on time series clustering. It would be insightful to compare against established methods, including those based on kernel k-means or other subspace kernel approaches, to benchmark the performance.
    *   **Change-Point Detection:** For the change-point detection experiment, the chosen baseline is known to have limitations in complex dynamics. A comparison against more recent, state-of-the-art online change detection algorithms (especially other kernel-based ones) would provide a much stronger and more informative benchmark.

    Overall, the paper would be significantly strengthened by more rigorous experimental validation against relevant and challenging baselines that are representative of the state of the art in each application domain.

**Minor Points:**

The paper is generally very well-written and clear. I spotted only two minor typos:
*   Please check the consistency of indices on τ-1 versus τ.
*   In the last equation on page 7, the dot in `k(xi.xj)` should likely be a comma: `k(xi, xj)`.
