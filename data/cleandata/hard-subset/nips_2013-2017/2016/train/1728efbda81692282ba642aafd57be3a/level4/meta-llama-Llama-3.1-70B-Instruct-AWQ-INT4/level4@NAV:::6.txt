The authors of the present paper have developed a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces, accompanied by an algorithm for analyzing measurement data from dynamical systems. This concept, originating from fluid dynamics, appears to be relevant to machine learning. The authors demonstrate their algorithm and analysis using two simple dynamical systems - a damped linear 1D dynamics and a chaotic map. Additionally, they apply their method to real-world locomotion data, comparing its performance with standard dynamic mode decomposition, PCA, and kernel PCA, yielding somewhat ambiguous results. 

The paper presents an interesting extension of dynamic mode decomposition, somewhat analogous to earlier work on "kernelizing" techniques framed in terms of linear operators, such as kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, and GP bandits. The authors provide experimental results for largely artificial problems. The paper is well-written and offers educational value for those unfamiliar with concepts from fluid dynamics. 

I appreciate research that introduces machine learners to techniques from different disciplines. In this case, the decomposition technique appears to be applicable to functions from M to M used to construct a dynamical system without stochastic components. While I am not aware of such setups being commonly used in machine learning, where dynamical systems typically involve random effects, the authors need to demonstrate the practical usefulness of these techniques for machine learning readers. 

Potential use cases and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods should be explored. For instance, in the domain of robotics, where inference and learning in dynamical systems are crucial, it would be beneficial to explain what Koopman analysis offers to researchers in this field. Although the theoretical derivations are well-executed, they are not particularly surprising, given the similarities to kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, and kernel Bayes rule. 

Some technical details require clarification: it is unclear whether the modal decomposition algorithm necessitates an explicit representation in feature space, which could be a limitation compared to kernel PCA. The authors should address this point explicitly. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to demonstrate the performance of standard dynamic mode decomposition on the toy example and the Henon map to illustrate the differences in this simple setup.
