This paper introduces a spectral analysis using the Koopman operator defined in a proper reproducing kernel Hilbert space. The authors follow the standard pathway to define a set of (kernel) approaches to analyze nonlinear dynamical systems with empirical data. The eigendecomposition of the projection of the Koopman operator results in some efficiency gain in both synthetic and real data analysis problems. This paper introduces a significant kernel construction to analyze nonlinear dynamical systems. The contribution is well motivated and experimentally validated.

The main ideas of this paper relate to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly in the field of nonlinear dynamical system analysis with kernels. The authors propose a modal decomposition algorithm to perform spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The algorithm is essentially reduced to the calculation of a set of orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and the eigendecomposition of the projection of the Koopman operator onto the subspace spanned by the bases. This approach is an extension of dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) via the Gram-Schmidt QR decomposition in the feature space.

The strengths of this paper include its well-motivated contribution, experimental validation, and the introduction of a significant kernel construction to analyze nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors provide a clear and well-written explanation of their approach and its connections to previous work.

However, there are some concerns that the authors may want to address. Firstly, it is not entirely clear how the proposed approach differs from the work presented in [35] by M.O. Williams, I.G. Kevrekidis, and C.W. Rowley, where the Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes were already introduced to analyze nonlinear system dynamics. A more detailed discussion on the main differences and an improved discussion on section 4 would be beneficial.

Additionally, the field of nonlinear dynamical system analysis with kernels is vast, and some relevant references are missing. The authors could check papers by L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al, Rojo-Alvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, or M. O. Franz and B. B. Schölkopf.

The authors briefly touch upon the performance of the proposed approach in stochastic settings, but some further discussion and eventually a toy experiment would be welcome. They also clarify the remark that the estimation procedure itself does not necessarily require a sequence but rather a collection of pairs of consecutive observables, but the implication of this assumption in multidimensional time series with eventually weak pointwise relations is not entirely clear.

With regard to the experimental settings, the authors may want to consider more ambitious experiments. Essentially, prediction via pre-image follows a standard well-known approach, and the "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" subsection lacks discussion on the connections (and not to mention experimental comparison) between the proposed approach and the (vast) literature of clustering time series (e.g., through kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods). When it comes to the sequential change-point detection experiment, it would be more appealing to see a comparison to state-of-the-art online change detection with kernels.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper introduces a significant kernel construction to analyze nonlinear dynamical systems.
* The contribution is well motivated and experimentally validated.
* The authors provide a clear and well-written explanation of their approach and its connections to previous work.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The differences between the proposed approach and previous work, particularly [35], are not entirely clear.
* Some relevant references are missing, and the authors may want to consider a more comprehensive review of the literature.
* The experimental settings may not be ambitious enough, and the authors may want to consider more challenging experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.
