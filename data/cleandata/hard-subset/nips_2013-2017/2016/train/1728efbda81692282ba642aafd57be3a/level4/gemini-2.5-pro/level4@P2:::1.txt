### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a method for performing spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending the Koopman operator theory to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to approximate the Koopman operator's eigenvalues and eigenfunctions using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions implicitly defined by a kernel, in contrast to standard Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) which typically relies on linear observables. The authors propose an algorithm that finds a modal decomposition by projecting the Koopman operator onto a Krylov subspace within the RKHS. A second, more robust algorithm is also presented, which uses a projection onto kernel principal component analysis (POD) bases to handle potential numerical instability. The paper then demonstrates how the extracted Koopman modes can be used as features for downstream machine learning tasks, including state prediction, dynamics recognition, and sequential change-point detection, validating the approach on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The primary contribution—formulating DMD and Koopman analysis within the RKHS framework—is a novel and elegant extension of existing work. It provides a principled and powerful way to capture complex, nonlinear dynamics without requiring pre-defined, explicit basis functions. The theoretical development, particularly Theorems 1 and 2, clearly establishes the connection between the proposed algorithm and the underlying Koopman operator theory. The work is technically sound.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and clear. The authors do an excellent job of first reviewing the necessary background on Koopman operators and DMD, and then carefully building up their kernelized extension. The algorithmic descriptions are easy to follow.

3.  **Significance:** The concept of using Koopman eigenfunctions as a feature extraction procedure for dynamical systems is highly significant. It bridges the gap between physics-based modal decomposition techniques and data-driven machine learning. The paper successfully demonstrates the potential of these features in a variety of applications, suggesting a versatile tool for researchers and practitioners working with time-series data.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Comparison to Related Work (Originality/Clarity):** The distinction between the proposed method and the Extended DMD (eDMD) of Williams et al. [35] is not sufficiently clear. eDMD also allows for approximating the Koopman operator using a dictionary of user-defined basis functions. While the kernel method provides an implicit and potentially infinite-dimensional dictionary, the practical implementation (especially the robust version in Section 4) appears very similar to eDMD with a set of kernel-based features. A more detailed discussion clarifying the key conceptual and algorithmic differences would strengthen the paper's claim to originality.

2.  **Scope of Experimental Validation (Significance):** While the experiments demonstrate the method's feasibility, they could be more ambitious to fully support the claims.
    *   In the "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" section, the approach is essentially a form of time-series clustering or classification. However, there is no comparison to the vast literature on this topic, such as methods based on kernel k-means, subspace clustering, or other dynamic-aware distance metrics. A comparison against established baselines would make the results much more compelling.
    *   For the sequential change-point detection experiment, the comparison is made with a kernel-based method from 2006. More convincing evidence would be provided by benchmarking against more recent, state-of-the-art online change detection algorithms to better demonstrate the utility of the proposed features.
    *   The prediction via pre-imaging is a standard technique in kernel methods, and its application here is straightforward.

3.  **Handling of Stochasticity (Quality/Significance):** The paper focuses on deterministic systems. While the authors briefly mention that the Koopman operator can be extended to stochastic settings, this point is critical for most real-world applications. A more thorough discussion on the performance and robustness of the method in the presence of noise would significantly increase the paper's impact. A simple toy experiment demonstrating how the method behaves in a stochastic setting would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Positioning within Kernel Methods Literature (Originality):** The field of kernel methods for analyzing dynamical systems is broad. The paper would be better situated by acknowledging and discussing other relevant lines of work. For instance, contributions from authors like L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, or M. O. Franz and B. Schölkopf on related problems could be considered.

5.  **Clarification on Data Requirements and Assumptions (Clarity):** The remark on page 5 (line 216) states that the method can work with a collection of pairs of consecutive observables, not necessarily a single contiguous sequence. This is an interesting point, but its implications are not fully explored.
    *   I am curious about the benefits of this property for analyzing complex datasets, such as multi-dimensional time series where pointwise relationships might be weak. A discussion on what is gained with the kernel construction in such scenarios would be helpful.
    *   Relatedly, it would be beneficial if the authors could elaborate on the causal assumptions inherent in their `(x_t, x_{t+1})` pair-based setting.

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces a well-motivated and technically sound method for nonlinear system analysis by elegantly merging Koopman operator theory with kernel methods. The work is clearly presented and demonstrates promising applications. However, its contribution could be strengthened by clarifying its novelty with respect to Extended DMD, providing a more rigorous and comparative experimental evaluation, and discussing its robustness to stochasticity. Addressing these points would significantly improve the paper and solidify its place as an important contribution to the field.

**Minor Comments:**

*   The manuscript is excellently written.
*   There appears to be an inconsistency in the indexing of the data sequence (sometimes using `τ-1`, other times `τ`). Please check for consistency throughout the paper.
*   In the last equation on page 7, `k(xj, xi)` seems to use a dot instead of a comma. Please correct `k(xj .xi)` to `k(xj, xi)`.
