This paper introduces a method for spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems based on the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs). The core idea leverages the linearity of the Koopman operator – an operator representing function composition – to perform an eigen-decomposition, effectively analyzing the forward dynamics of a system given observed trajectories. The approach combines kernel methods and subspace analysis, presenting an algorithm related to dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) but extended to operate with potentially nonlinear basis functions due to the expressiveness of kernel functions. The authors demonstrate the method's efficacy through applications in dynamics embedding, recognition, and change-point detection.

The paper builds upon established concepts in dynamical systems and kernel methods. For researchers familiar with these areas, the central contribution lies in framing the composition of functions – inherent in dynamical systems – as a linear operator amenable to spectral decomposition. This allows leveraging kernel techniques to potentially capture more complex dynamics than traditional linear methods. Given the existing literature on DMD, the novelty primarily resides in this kernelization. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of spectral analysis for nonlinear dynamical systems and the motivation for using the Koopman operator.
*   **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The extension of DMD to RKHS provides a mathematically sound framework for analyzing nonlinear dynamics. The connection to existing techniques like kernel PCA is well explained.
*   **Potential for Capturing Nonlinearity:** Utilizing kernel functions offers the potential to represent eigenfunctions more accurately than linear monomials, particularly for systems where linearization is not valid.
*   **Illustrative Examples:** The empirical examples, while relatively simple, demonstrate the feasibility and basic performance of the proposed method.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper effectively positions its contribution within the broader context of dynamical systems analysis, referencing relevant techniques like POD and DMD.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** While the kernelization of DMD is a valid extension, the novelty is incremental. The paper doesn't strongly differentiate itself from existing approaches.
*   **Applications Lack Impact:** The chosen applications (prediction, dynamics recognition, change-point detection) are standard problems, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods (e.g., recurrent neural networks, kernel change detection).  A more compelling application, or a demonstration of superior performance on a challenging benchmark, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Computational Considerations:** The potential computational cost associated with kernel methods is not fully addressed. A discussion of scalability and practical implementation challenges would be beneficial.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of kernel methods is often sensitive to kernel parameter selection. The paper doesn’t adequately discuss this aspect or provide guidance on parameter tuning.
*   **Missing Related Work:** While DMD is acknowledged, related work on kernelized DMD variants (e.g., extended DMD) could be discussed more thoroughly to better position the contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a mathematically rigorous extension of DMD using RKHS, potentially improving its ability to handle nonlinear dynamics.
*   Presents a clear and well-structured approach to spectral analysis of dynamical systems.
*   Demonstrates the feasibility of the method through empirical examples.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty is relatively limited, and the paper doesn't offer a substantial advancement over existing techniques.
*   The applications are not particularly compelling and don't demonstrate a clear advantage over alternative methods.
*   The discussion of practical considerations (scalability, parameter sensitivity) is insufficient.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound but incrementally novel extension to existing methods for spectral analysis of dynamical systems. While the theoretical foundation is strong, the limited novelty and lack of a compelling application weaken the overall contribution.  I would lean towards a **weak reject** unless the authors can significantly strengthen the paper by: 1) demonstrating a clear advantage over existing methods on a more challenging problem; 2) providing a more thorough discussion of computational considerations and parameter sensitivity; and 3) better positioning the work within the broader landscape of kernelized DMD techniques.
