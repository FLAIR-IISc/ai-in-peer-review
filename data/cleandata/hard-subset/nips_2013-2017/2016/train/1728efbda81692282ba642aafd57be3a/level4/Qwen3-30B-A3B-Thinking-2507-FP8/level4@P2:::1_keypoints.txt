- Introduces a novel extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear systems.  
- Proposes a modal decomposition algorithm computing orthogonal Krylov bases in RKHS and eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projection.  
- Provides feature extraction for nonlinear dynamics with demonstrated applications in prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.  
- Builds on established DMD techniques and extends them by incorporating kernel methods to capture richer nonlinear basis functions.  
- Mathematical derivations are clear, well-justified, and the extension to RKHS with POD-based robustification is technically sound.  
- Correctly identifies limitations of traditional DMD (linear monomials) and motivates the kernel-based approach as a meaningful theoretical advance.  
- Fails to sufficiently distinguish the method from Williams et al.’s extended DMD [35]; needs explicit comparison of theory, complexity, and performance.  
- Literature review is incomplete — several key kernel-based dynamical systems papers (e.g., Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., Mouattamid & Schaback) are missing.  
- Stochastic extension is only briefly mentioned with no experimental validation; toy experiments on noisy data are recommended.  
- Experimental validation is limited and lacks comparisons to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernel methods, online kernel change detection).  
- Prediction via preimage uses standard approaches and dynamics recognition experiments do not benchmark against established time-series clustering techniques.  
- Paper is well-written and well-organized, with minor technical typos to correct (e.g., “Hiber” → “Hilbert”, kernel notation errors).  
- Strengths: rigorous theoretical extension of DMD to RKHS, clear algorithm, POD robustification, and demonstrations on synthetic/real data.  
- Weaknesses/cons: insufficient differentiation from prior work, missing references, underdeveloped stochastic treatment, and limited comparative experiments reducing empirical significance.
