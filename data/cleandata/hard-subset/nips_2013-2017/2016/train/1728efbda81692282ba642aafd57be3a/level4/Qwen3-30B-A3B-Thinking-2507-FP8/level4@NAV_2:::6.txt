

The paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and develops an algorithm for performing this analysis using finite-length data sequences from a nonlinear dynamical system. While the Koopman operator concept originates from fluid dynamics, the authors aim to demonstrate its relevance to machine learning applications. The authors validate their method on simple dynamical systems (a damped linear 1D system and the Hénon map) and apply it to real-world locomotion data, comparing results with standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), principal component analysis (PCA), and kernel PCA. The manuscript provides a theoretically sound extension of DMD that leverages the expressive power of kernel functions, building upon established kernel-based methods such as kernel PCA, kernel ICA, and kernel independence tests. The paper is clearly written and offers educational value for machine learning researchers unfamiliar with fluid dynamics concepts, which is commendable.

However, the paper would benefit from stronger justification of the method's practical utility in machine learning contexts. The authors should explicitly identify specific machine learning domains where their approach offers advantages over existing methods, particularly in applications where dynamical systems with deterministic dynamics (without stochastic components) are relevant. Robotics is a prominent example where inference and learning in dynamical systems is crucial; the authors should address what specific benefits Koopman spectral analysis would provide to roboticists. The current experimental results on synthetic and artificial datasets do not sufficiently demonstrate the method's competitive advantage in practical machine learning scenarios.

The theoretical developments are well-executed and consistent with established kernel methods, but the results are not particularly surprising given prior work on kernelizing linear operators. The paper does not clearly address whether the proposed modal decomposition algorithm requires explicit representation in the feature space, which would be an important limitation compared to standard kernel methods like kernel PCA. Additionally, the authors should provide a comparison of standard DMD performance on the toy examples and Hénon map to better illustrate the differences between their approach and existing methods.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound extension of DMD using RKHS, but would be significantly strengthened by clearer articulation of practical machine learning applications and more compelling experimental evidence of its advantages over existing methods.
