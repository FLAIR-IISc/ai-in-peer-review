## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title]

This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman (composition) operator within the framework of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs), and introduces a corresponding modal decomposition algorithm for performing this analysis. The authors also address the issue of robustness and validate their approach through several numerical experiments. The work builds upon and extends the concept of Dynamical Mode Decomposition (DMD) by leveraging the kernel trick, offering a potentially powerful tool for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems. 

**Strengths:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors demonstrate a strong command of the underlying theoretical concepts, presenting them in a manner that is easy to follow. The proposed method appears theoretically interesting and technically sound. While a detailed, comparative analysis against state-of-the-art techniques is limited, the presented numerical experiments effectively illustrate the practical utility of the approach, which, in my opinion, is sufficient for this initial presentation.

**Weaknesses:**

My primary concern lies with Section 3.1, specifically Equation (7) and the subsequent proof of Theorem 1. The purpose of Equation (7), given its similarity to Equation (2), is not immediately clear and requires further justification. Furthermore, the equation appears inconsistent; *K<sub>H</sub>* is defined as a linear operator mapping from *H* to itself, whereas *φ* is a function from the manifold *M* to *H*, and thus not an element of *H*. A more detailed explanation of the application of *K<sub>H</sub>* to *φ* would be greatly appreciated. 

This inconsistency raises concerns regarding the validity of the proof on Lines 144-157. The phrase “by applying the operator *K<sub>H</sub>* to both sides” (Line 149) is problematic, as both sides of the equation at that point represent scalar values. Applying *K<sub>H</sub> : H → H* to a scalar is not defined. 

The assumptions stated in Theorem 1 seem notably strong and lack precise definition. Clarification regarding the specific conditions imposed by these assumptions is essential. Additionally, the proof introduces further, unstated assumptions. The authors should explicitly state these additional assumptions, explain their necessity, and discuss their likelihood of holding in practical scenarios.  Addressing these points is critical for establishing the theoretical rigor of the proposed method.

**Minor Issues:**

*   Line 55: Typo – “the the” should be “the”.
*   Line 153: Typo – “\kappaj / \lambdaj” should be “\kappai / \lambda_i”.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could the authors elaborate on the purpose of Equation (7) and its relationship to Equation (2)?
*   Please provide a detailed explanation of how the operator *K<sub>H</sub>* is applied to the function *φ* in Equation (7), given the differing domain and range.
*   Can the authors provide more explicit definitions and justifications for the assumptions stated in Theorem 1?
*   Please clearly state and justify any additional assumptions made within the proof of Theorem 1.
*   Could the authors discuss the potential limitations of their method when the assumptions of Theorem 1 are not fully satisfied?



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the concerns raised above, this paper presents a promising and potentially impactful contribution to the field. The theoretical framework is well-motivated, and the initial numerical results are encouraging. However, addressing the issues outlined regarding the technical details of Section 3.1 and the assumptions underlying Theorem 1 is crucial before this work can be considered truly robust.  I recommend a revision addressing these points. The clarity of the exposition is a significant strength, and I believe with further refinement, this paper has the potential to be a valuable addition to the NIPS workshop.
