Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), yielding a kernelized DMD (KDMD) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The core idea is to work with a feature map ϕ(x) induced by a positive definite kernel, form the feature-space Krylov matrix from a data sequence, compute an orthonormal basis (via Gram–Schmidt or kernel PCA), project the Koopman operator onto this subspace, and eigendecompose the resulting finite operator to obtain approximate Koopman eigenvalues and modes. The paper presents two formulations: a direct QR-based approach and a numerically robust variant that projects onto kernel POD/kernel PCA directions. The authors prove that the empirical Ritz values/vectors obtained from these projections play the role of Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions in the feature space, under standard assumptions. They then sketch applications—short-term prediction via preimage, sequential change-point detection via a kernelized exponential-family score in a CUSUM framework, and dynamics recognition via principal angles between feature subspaces—and provide small-scale demonstrations on a toy system, the Hénon map, and a locomotion dataset. The work relates to DMD and its variants (exact/optimized DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD), extended DMD with user-specified dictionaries, subspace identification, and kernel PCA.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: A clear operator-theoretic generalization of DMD to RKHS that allows nonlinear feature dictionaries specified implicitly by kernels.
- Theoretical grounding: Precise statements connecting Ritz values/vectors in feature space to Koopman spectra; a robust variant using kernel PCA/POD that addresses ill-conditioning.
- Versatility: The framework naturally supports feature extraction for prediction, change detection, and dynamics recognition.
- Practicality: Implementation is kernelizable using Gram matrices; code availability is a plus.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Clarity and organization
- The exposition is dense and often cryptic. Many points could be conveyed with simpler prose and explicit algorithmic steps.
  - Suggest providing concise algorithm boxes for both KDMD variants, listing inputs, outputs, and computational steps (with kernel-only operations highlighted).
  - Move proofs and technical derivations (e.g., Vandermonde/companion-matrix details) to an appendix, keeping the main text focused on intuition and usage.
  - Unify notation and assumptions (e.g., centered vs. uncentered features; when distinct eigenvalues are required; whether data must be from a single trajectory or can be pairs).
- Section-level feedback:
  - The robustification section could be trimmed and consolidated with the main method, highlighting when projection to kernel POD directions is essential; detailed derivations can be deferred to the appendix.
  - The related work section is lengthy; condense it and sharpen the distinctions with extended DMD (Williams et al.), exact/optimized DMD, and kernel subspace methods, emphasizing what is genuinely new here (e.g., specific projections, theory, or applications).
  - The empirical section is too brief; expand experiments with clear protocols, metrics, and comparisons.

Experimental evaluation
- Current experiments are illustrative but limited. To assess quality and significance, add quantitative evaluations and baselines:
  - Compare KDMD against exact/optimized DMD and extended DMD with polynomial/RBF dictionaries on synthetic systems with known spectra (report eigenvalue errors, mode reconstruction errors, and predictive RMSE).
  - Perform sensitivity analyses over kernel choices and hyperparameters (e.g., RBF bandwidth, polynomial degree), and over truncation levels in the kernel POD step.
  - Report computational complexity and scaling (time/memory vs. number of snapshots and features), and any numerical stabilization used (regularization, rank truncation, centering).
- Applications:
  - Prediction: Provide quantitative short-horizon prediction results vs. baselines, and clarify the preimage method (objective, neighbors, optimization details, failure modes).
  - Change-point detection: Include ROC/delay analyses and comparisons to kernel change detection methods beyond a single trace; justify the approximation that drops the second term in the likelihood ratio.
  - Dynamics recognition: Report classification accuracy and confusion matrices; explain kernel principal angle computation and dimensionality choices.

Originality
- The kernelization of DMD is closely related to extended DMD (EDMD) with kernel dictionaries. Please clarify the precise differences: e.g., the use of feature-space Krylov subspaces, the projection operators, and theoretical guarantees that go beyond EDMD. Explicitly position KDMD relative to EDMD in terms of assumptions, computational steps, and outcomes.

Quality and correctness
- The derivations appear sound, but key assumptions should be stated prominently (distinct eigenvalues, φj ∈ span of feature images, richness of data). Note when results hold for collections of transition pairs vs. single trajectories.
- The preimage and CUSUM constructions need more detail to be reproducible and to evaluate robustness.

Minor comments
- The “toy nonlinear” example reads as linear in the stated form; please revise or clarify where the nonlinearity enters (the second observable is polynomial).
- Since the first two examples are polynomial, emphasize that KDMD should excel when a suitable kernel matches the anticipated nonlinearity; provide practical guidance for kernel selection.
- Clean up typos and redundancies; simplify sentences like the long comparison of DMD to a one-term Taylor expansion.
- Ensure figures are readable (axes, legends) and that all footnotes (e.g., code link, “procedure summarized as follows.1”) are correctly referenced.
- Provide pseudo-code with kernel-only operations (Gram matrices, centering, regularization).

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, general framework linking Koopman analysis and kernel methods.
- Theoretically grounded with practical algorithms and code.
- Potentially impactful across ML and dynamical systems communities.

Cons for acceptance
- Exposition is overly dense and hard to follow; organization needs streamlining.
- Limited empirical validation and lack of comparisons to strong baselines (EDMD/exact/optimized DMD).
- Originality relative to EDMD is not fully established.

Recommendation
Promising work, but as written it is too cryptic for NIPS and lacks sufficient empirical and comparative evaluation. I recommend revision focusing on clarity, positioning relative to EDMD and DMD variants, and a more thorough experimental section. With these changes, the paper could be a strong and widely cited contribution.
