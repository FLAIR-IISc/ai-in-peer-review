- Paper develops RKHS-based spectral analysis of the Koopman operator and an algorithm to estimate its spectrum from data, extending DMD into an RKHS framework.
- Demonstrations on a 1D damped linear system, a chaotic map, and a locomotion dataset, with comparisons to DMD, PCA, and kernel PCA.

Strengths
- Clear, accessible exposition and careful derivations.
- Conceptual contribution linking Koopman theory with kernel methods, following successful “kernelizing” precedents.
- Potentially useful toolbox for analyzing nonlinear dynamics via linear operators in feature space.

Main concerns / weaknesses
- Practical relevance to core ML problems is not convincingly demonstrated; experiments are primarily toy and real-data results are hard to interpret.
- Novelty seems incremental relative to existing RKHS operator frameworks; algorithmic pipeline feels expected.
- Limited treatment of stochasticity and measurement noise, leaving robustness unclear for common ML tasks.
- Empirical evaluation insufficient: missing ablations, baselines, and clear takeaways relative to competing methods.

Questions needing clarification
- Modal decomposition: are explicit feature maps required or is everything implemented via kernel evaluations; how are modes computed/interpreted without explicit features?
- Computational complexity and scalability: time/memory costs versus sample size and kernel; applicability of Nyström/random features and dataset sizes used.
- Hyperparameters and regularization: how are kernel, bandwidth, and regularization chosen and how sensitive are results?
- Out-of-sample evaluation: how are eigenfunctions/modes evaluated on new points and how stable are projections?
- Stochastic systems: can the method extend to stochastic dynamics (e.g., Markov processes) and how would estimators/guarantees change?

Experimental suggestions
- Add standard DMD baselines on the simple toy systems to isolate RKHS benefits.
- Include robustness experiments with measurement noise and model misspecification.
- Provide more interpretable analysis of locomotion data and evaluate on downstream tasks (segmentation, prediction, control).
- Benchmark against state-of-the-art dynamical-model methods (LSSMs, subspace ID, GP-SSMs, modern DMD variants, HMMs) on relevant ML tasks.
- Report quantitative metrics with uncertainty (reconstruction/prediction error, mode stability) and include ablation studies to isolate kernelization effects.

Positioning and contribution clarity
- Sharpen theoretical novelty: state any new identifiability or consistency results specific to Koopman spectra in RKHS or novel connections to kernel conditional operators.
- Articulate concrete use cases where Koopman spectral structure outperforms black-box predictors or classical IDs, especially under limited data or for interpretability.

Minor comments
- State required assumptions (ergodicity, compactness, observability) and link them to finite-sample implications.
- Clarify whether Koopman modes are obtained in original data space and how to reconstruct/forecast observables from estimated eigenfunctions.
- Improve comparison plots to make differences statistically supported and unambiguous.

Overall assessment
- Well written and technically solid with a promising bridge between Koopman theory and kernel methods, but novelty is modest and empirical evidence of practical benefit is insufficient; reviewer leans toward rejection pending stronger experiments, clearer positioning, and a compelling ML application.
