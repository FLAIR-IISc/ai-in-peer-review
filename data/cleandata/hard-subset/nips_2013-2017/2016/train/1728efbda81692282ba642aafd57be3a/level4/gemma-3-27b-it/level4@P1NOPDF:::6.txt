In this paper, the authors present a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces, and develop an algorithm for performing this analysis using measurement data from dynamical systems. The Koopman operator originates in fluid dynamics, and the authors suggest its relevance to machine learning. They demonstrate their algorithm and analysis on two simple dynamical systems – a damped linear 1D system and a chaotic map – and apply their method to real-world locomotion data, comparing performance with dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA. The results of this comparison are somewhat inconclusive. 

The paper offers an interesting extension of DMD, reminiscent of earlier work on “kernelizing” techniques expressed in terms of linear operators (such as kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, and Gaussian process bandits, among others). The authors support their approach with experimental results, though these largely focus on artificial problems. Overall, the paper is clearly written and provides educational value for readers unfamiliar with concepts from fluid dynamics. I generally support work that introduces machine learning researchers to techniques from other disciplines.

Here, the work appears to be a decomposition technique for functions mapping from a space M to itself, used to construct a dynamical system without stochastic components. I am not currently aware of such setups being common in machine learning, where dynamical systems typically incorporate random effects like innovations and noise – though I may be overlooking something. As it stands, I believe the authors need to more persuasively demonstrate the practical utility of these techniques to a machine learning audience. What potential use cases exist? And can this method compete, even partially, with state-of-the-art approaches in relevant fields?

Robotics is one machine learning discipline where inference and learning in dynamical systems are crucial areas of research. What advantages would Koopman analysis offer to a robotics researcher? Or, alternatively, could the authors highlight another applicable domain? While the theoretical derivations are well-executed, I wasn’t particularly surprised by them, given my familiarity with kernel PCA, kernel ICA, and subsequent work on kernel independence tests or the “kernel Bayes rule.” The approach involves a linear operator, and well-defined operations are possible when functions reside in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. This ultimately leads to algorithms that perform a type of eigendecomposition on finite-dimensional matrices.

Some specific points require clarification: It’s unclear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires an explicit representation in the feature space. If so, this represents a limitation compared to kernel PCA, and the authors should explicitly acknowledge this. Additionally, it would be beneficial to present the performance of standard DMD on the toy example and the Henon map, allowing for a clearer comparison and demonstration of the differences between the methods in a simple setting.
