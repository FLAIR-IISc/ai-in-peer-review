The paper derives a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes an algorithm to perform this analysis using measurement data from a dynamical system. The Koopman operator originates in fluid dynamics, and the authors suggest it has relevance for machine learning. They illustrate the approach on two simple dynamical systems—a damped 1D linear system and a chaotic map—and also apply it to real-world locomotion data, comparing against standard Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA. The empirical results, however, are somewhat unclear.

Conceptually, the work is an interesting extension of DMD, in the spirit of earlier efforts to “kernelize” techniques framed around linear operators (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, Gaussian process bandits, etc.). The paper is generally well written and has educational value for readers not familiar with tools from fluid dynamics. The experiments are primarily on artificial problems, with a brief foray into locomotion data.

At a high level, the method appears to be a decomposition technique for deterministic maps from M to M used to define a dynamical system, without stochastic components. I am not aware of many such setups being used in mainstream machine learning, where random effects (innovations, noise) typically play a central role, though I may be missing relevant strands of work. In its current form, the paper needs to do more to persuade an ML audience of the practical utility of these techniques. What are the concrete use cases where this approach is advantageous? Can it compete, at least partially, with the state of the art in those areas?

Robotics is a domain where inference and learning in dynamical systems are crucial and where substantial prior work exists. What would Koopman-based analysis offer practitioners in robotics? If robotics is not the right domain, please make the case in another application area where the assumptions match practice and where the method delivers a clear benefit.

While the theoretical derivations are careful and technically sound, they are not particularly surprising to readers familiar with kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, or even “kernel Bayes’ rule.” Once one recognizes a linear operator acting on functions in an RKHS, it is natural to obtain algorithms that reduce to eigendecompositions of finite-dimensional matrices.

Some specific points:
- It is not entirely clear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires an explicit representation in feature space. If so, this is a limitation relative to kernel PCA, and the paper should make this requirement explicit and discuss its implications.
- It would strengthen the empirical section to include standard DMD on the toy linear system and the Hénon map, to highlight differences in a simple, controlled setting and clarify what the kernelized Koopman approach contributes.

In summary, the paper offers a neat synthesis connecting Koopman analysis with RKHS machinery and provides a clear exposition. To make the case for NeurIPS, however, the authors should (i) sharpen the empirical narrative with clearer, more competitive comparisons, (ii) articulate compelling application domains and practical advantages over established ML approaches to dynamical systems (especially those handling stochasticity), and (iii) clarify implementation details around feature-space requirements in the modal decomposition.
