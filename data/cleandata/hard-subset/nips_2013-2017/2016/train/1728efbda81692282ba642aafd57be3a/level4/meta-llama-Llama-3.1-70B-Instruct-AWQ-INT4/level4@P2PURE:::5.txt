The manuscript "Koopman Spectral Analysis of Nonlinear Dynamical Systems with Reproducing Kernels" presents an extension of dynamical mode decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), resulting in the Koopman DMD (KDMD) algorithm. The authors apply KDMD to several toy problems, demonstrating its potential to link DMD to specific features of interest by using feature maps as kernel definitions. This represents a powerful generalization of DMD.

However, my primary concern is that the paper is challenging to read. The authors often prioritize mathematical notation over concise explanations, which can make even simple concepts difficult to grasp. Some sections are verbose, potentially hindering understanding even for specialists, and certain points require clarification. The current writing style makes the paper unsuitable for publication in the Neural Information Processing Systems conference proceedings. Nevertheless, I strongly encourage the authors to rewrite the paper in a more pedagogical manner, as the resulting article has the potential to be highly cited.

I. MAJOR REMARKS

1. The presentation is overly verbose in some areas and could be simplified. For example, the sentence "Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point" could be condensed into a shorter, more straightforward statement.
2. Section 4 seems unnecessary in a short paper, as it partially overlaps with the introduction.
3. Section 5 would benefit from illustrations or a summary in the conclusion to enhance clarity.
4. Section 6 feels condensed and could be expanded, similar to Section 5, to provide more comprehensive information.

II. MINOR REMARKS

1. There appears to be a typo in the description of the toy nonlinear case, which is actually linear.
2. The first two problems are polynomial in nature, making them ideal scenarios for KDMD to excel, particularly when the kernel can be guessed, as in the first problem. More details, similar to those provided in Section 5, would be beneficial.
