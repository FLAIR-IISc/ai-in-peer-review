This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs) and develops an algorithm for performing this analysis using data from dynamical systems. The Koopman operator, originating in fluid dynamics, offers a way to analyze nonlinear systems as linear operators acting on observable functions. The authors demonstrate their algorithm on both synthetic and real-world locomotion data, comparing its performance to standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), principal component analysis (PCA), and kernel PCA. The work proposes an interesting extension of DMD, analogous to kernel methods prevalent in machine learning (kernel PCA, kernel ICA, etc.), offering a way to leverage the expressive power of kernels within a spectral decomposition framework. The paper is generally well-written and provides valuable educational material for readers unfamiliar with concepts from fluid dynamics. I support efforts to bridge techniques between different disciplines, and this work introduces a decomposition technique for functions mapping from a manifold to itself, used to construct a dynamical system. While I am not immediately aware of common applications in machine learning that precisely fit this setup – many ML dynamical systems incorporate stochastic components – I may be overlooking relevant areas. Currently, the paper would benefit from further exploration of practical applications to convince machine learning researchers of the technique’s utility. What specific problems can this approach address, and how does it compare to state-of-the-art methods? Robotics, as a field heavily reliant on dynamical systems, presents a potential area for application. A clear articulation of the benefits of Koopman analysis within a domain like robotics, or another relevant field, would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, while the theoretical derivations are sound, they are largely expected given familiarity with kernel PCA, kernel ICA, or related work on kernel methods. The core idea – applying kernel methods to a linear operator and performing eigendecomposition on finite-dimensional matrices – is well-established. 

Several points could be addressed to improve the manuscript. It is not entirely clear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires explicit representation in the feature space. If so, this is a significant limitation compared to standard kernel methods, and the authors should explicitly acknowledge this. A direct comparison of standard DMD’s performance on the toy example and the Henon map would also be beneficial, allowing for a clearer demonstration of the proposed method’s advantages in these simpler scenarios. 

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Novel Extension of DMD:** The paper offers a principled extension of DMD using reproducing kernels, potentially allowing for the capture of more complex dynamics.
*   **Clear Writing & Educational Value:** The paper is well-written and accessible, providing a good introduction to Koopman operator theory for readers from diverse backgrounds.
*   **Bridging Disciplines:** The work successfully brings together concepts from fluid dynamics and machine learning.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Demonstration of Practical Utility:** The application of the proposed method to real-world problems remains somewhat unclear and lacks compelling demonstration of its advantages over existing techniques.
*   **Lack of Comparison to Standard DMD:** A direct comparison of the proposed method with standard DMD on the test systems is missing.
*   **Expected Theoretical Results:** The theoretical results, while correct, are largely predictable given prior work in kernel methods.
*   **Potential Computational Limitations:** The dependence on feature space representation (if present) may limit scalability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel extension of DMD.
*   The method offers a potentially powerful tool for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems.
*   The work encourages cross-disciplinary research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical utility of the method is not convincingly demonstrated.
*   The paper lacks a thorough comparison to existing methods, particularly standard DMD.
*   The theoretical contributions, while correct, are not groundbreaking.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and interesting extension of dynamic mode decomposition. However, its impact is currently limited by a lack of compelling demonstration of its practical utility and a comparison to standard DMD. I believe the paper would benefit from further investigation of potential applications and a more thorough evaluation of its performance. I would lean towards a **weak accept** contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above in a revision. Specifically, providing compelling examples of the method’s advantage in a real-world setting (perhaps robotics) and showing a direct comparison to standard DMD would significantly strengthen the paper.
