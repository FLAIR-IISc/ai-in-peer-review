

This paper proposes an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors build upon existing work in Koopman operator theory, where the Koopman operator provides a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics through function composition. While standard DMD methods approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using linear monomials (equivalent to a first-order Taylor expansion), this paper introduces a kernel-based approach that can capture more complex nonlinear relationships. The core contribution is a modal decomposition algorithm that operates in RKHS, involving orthogonal basis calculation for the Krylov matrix and eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projection. The authors also provide a robustified version using POD bases to address numerical instability issues common in DMD implementations.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. Koopman spectral analysis has been explored in fluid dynamics and machine learning (e.g., Rowley et al., 2009; Williams et al., 2015), and DMD variants have appeared in recent NIPS proceedings (e.g., Chen et al., 2012). The kernel extension builds on kernel methods in machine learning (Schölkopf et al., 1998; Shawe-Taylor & Cristianini, 2004), though the specific application to Koopman spectral analysis represents a novel combination.

**Strengths:**
1. **Quality:** The theoretical development is sound, with rigorous proofs of theorems connecting the RKHS-based DMD to Koopman spectral analysis. The robustified version addressing numerical instability is particularly valuable, as this is a known issue in standard DMD implementations (e.g., Chen et al., 2012).
2. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and technically clear, with a logical progression from theoretical foundations to algorithmic implementation and applications. The mathematical notation is consistent and properly defined.
3. **Significance:** The applications to dynamics recognition, state prediction, and change-point detection demonstrate potential utility for machine learning practitioners working with time-series data.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Originality:** The core contribution is incremental. While the kernel extension to DMD is nontrivial, it builds directly on existing DMD methods (Williams et al., 2015; Jovanović et al., 2014) and kernel methods. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from other kernel-based methods for dynamical systems analysis.
2. **Significance:** The empirical evaluation is limited in scope. The applications are demonstrated on synthetic data and a few small-scale real-world examples, but the paper fails to convincingly show how kernel DMD outperforms existing methods (e.g., standard DMD, RNNs, or kernel-based approaches like those in Kawahara et al., 2011). The comparison with state-of-the-art methods is insufficient to demonstrate the method's unique value.
3. **Clarity:** While the mathematical content is clear, the connection between the theoretical framework and practical applications could be strengthened. The paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of how the kernel choice affects performance and when this method is preferable to alternatives.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Mathematically sound extension of DMD to RKHS with a robust implementation
- Addresses a known numerical limitation in standard DMD
- Provides a novel feature extraction procedure for dynamical systems
- Applications to dynamics recognition and change-point detection are relevant to machine learning

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Incremental contribution with limited novelty compared to existing DMD and kernel methods
- Insufficient empirical evidence to demonstrate superiority over simpler alternatives
- Applications lack compelling comparison with state-of-the-art methods in the field
- The paper doesn't clearly articulate why kernel-based Koopman analysis is necessary when linear DMD or other methods could suffice

This paper makes a technically correct contribution to the literature on Koopman spectral analysis, but its incremental nature and limited empirical validation reduce its significance for NIPS. The kernel extension to DMD is a natural step, but the paper doesn't sufficiently establish why this specific approach is superior to existing methods for the applications presented. While the robustified algorithm is a useful technical contribution, the broader significance of the work for machine learning research is not compellingly demonstrated. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons with alternative approaches (e.g., recurrent neural networks, standard DMD, or other kernel-based methods) and a clearer articulation of the specific advantages of the kernel-based approach over simpler alternatives.

In summary, while the paper is technically sound and addresses a meaningful technical challenge, its contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. The empirical results, while suggestive, do not convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority for the stated applications. The paper would be stronger with more comparative analysis against relevant baselines and a clearer justification for why kernel methods provide unique advantages in this context.
