This paper proposes a spectral method for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator, interpreted as a linear operator that represents function composition. Specifically, the method applies a Koopman operator decomposition to the forward nonlinear dynamics x(t+2) = f(f(x(t))). Given observed trajectories, the authors perform an eigen-decomposition of this operator using ideas akin to kernel PCA and subspace analysis. They then apply the approach to embedding and recognition of dynamical systems and report good empirical performance.

For readers familiar with kernel methods, the main conceptual point is that compositions of nonlinear functions can be represented as linear operators, enabling spectral decompositions over function compositions. A dynamical system is a natural instance of such a composition, making this a fitting application.

However, the contribution appears incremental. Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) and its variants have been developed in the dynamical systems literature as operator-based spectral methods for nonlinear dynamics via linearization in lifted spaces. In this context, the novelty here seems largely limited to a kernelized formulation of the approach. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what is fundamentally new relative to existing DMD frameworks and closely related kernel-based operator methods, and from a precise characterization of the technical differences (algorithmic steps, assumptions, guarantees) beyond reframing the composition as a linear operator.

Moreover, the empirical evaluation is not fully compelling in demonstrating why this method is particularly advantageous or uniquely suited to the proposed tasks. There are established alternatives for recognizing and modeling dynamical systems, including recurrent neural networks, as well as standard approaches for change detection. Without strong comparisons to such baselines, it is difficult to assess the practical value of the proposed technique. More comprehensive experiments, ablations, and analyses highlighting scenarios where the method clearly outperforms or offers interpretability or robustness benefits would strengthen the case.

In summary, the paper presents a clean operator-theoretic framing and applies spectral ideas familiar from kernel methods to dynamical systems, but its novelty appears limited and the applications do not convincingly demonstrate distinct advantages over existing approaches.
