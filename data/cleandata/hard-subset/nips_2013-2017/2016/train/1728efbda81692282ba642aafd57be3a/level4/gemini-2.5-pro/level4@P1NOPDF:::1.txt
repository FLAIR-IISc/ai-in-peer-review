This paper introduces a kernel-based method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The approach is based on the Koopman operator, defined within an appropriate Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), which allows the authors to develop a set of kernel techniques for analyzing empirical data from such systems. The eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator is shown to yield efficiency gains in experiments on both synthetic and real-world data. Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution in constructing kernels for analyzing nonlinear dynamics, with a well-motivated and experimentally validated approach.

There are, however, several concerns that I hope the authors can address:

1.  **Novelty with respect to prior work:** The distinction between the proposed method and the work of Williams et al. [35] is not sufficiently clear. Reference [35] also introduced the use of Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for analyzing nonlinear system dynamics. A detailed discussion clarifying the key differences and novel contributions of the present work is needed. This comparison could be effectively integrated into Section 4.

2.  **Missing references:** The field of nonlinear dynamical system analysis with kernels is vast, and the related work section could be strengthened by including several relevant references. The authors may wish to consider citing the work of L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, or M. O. Franz and B. B. Schölkopf, among others.

3.  **Applicability to stochastic systems:** The Koopman operator is generally defined for deterministic systems. While the authors briefly touch upon stochastic settings, these are arguably more representative of real-world scenarios. Further discussion on the method's expected performance in stochastic environments would be valuable. An illustrative toy experiment in such a setting would be a welcome addition.

4.  **Assumptions on data structure:** The authors state (line 216, page 5) that the estimation procedure requires "a collection of pairs of consecutive observables" rather than a complete sequence, where each pair is assumed to have a nonlinear pointwise relationship. Could the authors elaborate on the implications of this for analyzing multidimensional time series, particularly those with potentially weak pointwise relationships between components? Specifically, what advantages does the proposed kernel construction offer in such cases? In a related vein, a discussion of the causal assumptions inherent in this framework would be appreciated.

5.  **Experimental evaluation:** The experimental validation, while useful, could be more ambitious to fully demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method.
    *   The prediction task via pre-imaging appears to follow a standard, well-known approach.
    *   The "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" section lacks a discussion of the connections to the vast literature on time series clustering. An experimental comparison with established methods, such as kernel k-means or related subspace kernel techniques, would significantly strengthen this part of the evaluation.
    *   For the sequential change-point detection experiment, a comparison against state-of-the-art online kernel-based methods is necessary. The baseline used is known to perform poorly on complex dynamics, which weakens the reported results. More convincing experiments are needed to fully support the paper's contributions.

**Minor Points:**
The paper is well-written and clear. I noticed two minor typos:
*   Please check the indices for consistency (e.g., τ-1 vs. τ).
*   In the last equation on page 7, please replace the dot with a comma in the kernel argument: k(xi, xj).
