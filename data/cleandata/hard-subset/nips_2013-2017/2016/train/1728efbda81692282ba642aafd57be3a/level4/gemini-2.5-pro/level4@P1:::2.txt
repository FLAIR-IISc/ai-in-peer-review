This paper presents a method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to analyze the Koopman operator, an infinite-dimensional linear operator that governs the evolution of observables of a nonlinear system. By working in an RKHS, the proposed algorithm can approximate the eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions defined by the kernel, in contrast to standard DMD which is limited to linear observables. The method effectively performs an eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projected onto a subspace derived from the data, analogous to how Kernel PCA finds principal components in a feature space. The resulting Koopman eigenvalues and modes are then proposed as features for downstream machine learning tasks, which the authors demonstrate through applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and the classification of dynamics.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Conceptual Contribution:** The paper provides a valuable and elegant bridge between the theory of Koopman operators, a topic of significant interest in the physical sciences and dynamical systems communities, and the well-established framework of kernel methods in machine learning. For a machine learning audience, framing the analysis of function composition (as represented by the Koopman operator) as a spectral problem in an RKHS is an insightful perspective.

2.  **Principled Extension:** The proposed kernel DMD is a natural and well-motivated extension of the standard DMD algorithm. The use of an RKHS provides a principled way to move from linear approximations of Koopman eigenfunctions to much more expressive nonlinear ones, potentially allowing the method to capture a wider range of complex dynamics.

3.  **Technical Soundness:** The derivation is clear and mathematically correct. The authors thoughtfully propose a robustified version of the algorithm (Section 4) that uses a truncated Kernel PCA basis. This addresses potential numerical instability, a known issue in standard DMD, and makes the method more practical.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Novelty:** The primary contribution is the application of the kernel trick to the DMD algorithm. While this is a logical and well-executed step, the novelty could be viewed as somewhat incremental, given that "kernelization" is a standard technique for extending linear algorithms in machine learning. The authors cite Williams et al. (2015), whose Extended DMD also uses more general dictionaries of basis functions. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly articulated the advantages of the proposed kernel-based approach over such methods.

2.  **Significance and Experimental Evaluation:** The significance of the method is not fully established by the experiments. While the applications are interesting proofs-of-concept, they lack the rigorous comparison to state-of-the-art baselines necessary to demonstrate a clear advantage.
    *   For tasks like prediction and recognition of dynamics from sequential data, the machine learning community has developed powerful and widely used models, most notably Recurrent Neural Networks (e.g., LSTMs, GRUs) and Gaussian Process Dynamical Models. The paper does not include any comparisons against these standard baselines. Without such a comparison, it is difficult to assess the practical utility of the proposed method for these tasks. Is it more data-efficient? More interpretable? More robust? These questions remain unanswered.
    *   Similarly, for change-point detection, a comparison against a broader suite of modern detection algorithms would be more convincing.
    *   The experiments are conducted on relatively simple synthetic data and one real-world dataset without a clear benchmark. To make a compelling case for the NIPS audience, the paper needs to demonstrate its value on more challenging problems and show where it offers a unique advantage over existing, well-established machine learning approaches.

**In summary:**

The paper introduces a technically sound and well-motivated kernel-based extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition. It successfully connects concepts from dynamical systems theory with mainstream machine learning. However, its primary weakness lies in the limited experimental evaluation, which fails to position the method relative to strong, existing machine learning baselines for time-series analysis. The applications are demonstrated but not benchmarked, making it difficult to judge the method's practical significance and unique contributions to the field.
