This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs) and develops an algorithm to perform this analysis using measurement data from a dynamical system. The Koopman operator, a concept originating in fluid dynamics, is presented here as a relevant tool for machine learning. The authors demonstrate their method on two simple dynamical systems: a damped linear 1D system and a chaotic map. They also apply their method to real-world locomotion data, comparing its performance against standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA. However, the results of this comparison are inconclusive.

The paper derives an interesting extension of dynamic mode decomposition, reminiscent of previous work on "kernelizing" techniques for linear operators, such as kernel PCA, kernel ICA, and kernel independence tests. The experimental results are primarily based on artificial problems. Overall, the paper is clearly written and has educational value for readers unfamiliar with concepts from fluid dynamics. I am generally supportive of work that introduces the machine learning community to techniques from other disciplines.

The primary weakness of the paper is its motivation for a machine learning audience. The proposed method appears to be a decomposition technique for deterministic dynamical systems (i.e., those without stochastic components). I am not personally aware of many applications for such deterministic setups in machine learning, where dynamical systems typically incorporate stochastic effects like process noise and measurement error, though I may be overlooking some relevant areas. As such, the authors need to do more to convince machine learning researchers of the practical utility of their approach. What are the potential use cases? How does this method compare to the state-of-the-art in those areas? For example, robotics is a domain where learning in dynamical systems is critical; what advantages would Koopman analysis offer a robotics researcher? The authors could significantly strengthen the paper by addressing such questions and grounding the method in a specific application domain.

Furthermore, while the theoretical derivations are well-executed, the results are not particularly surprising for those familiar with similar kernelized methods. The general principle is familiar: a linear operator acting on functions in an RKHS leads to a practical algorithm involving an eigendecomposition of a finite-dimensional matrix derived from the data.

I have a few specific suggestions for improvement:

1.  It is unclear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires an explicit representation of the data in the feature space. If it does, this would be a significant limitation compared to methods like kernel PCA, and the authors should state this explicitly.

2.  To provide a clearer baseline, the authors should show the performance of standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) on the toy example and the Hénon map. This would help to clearly demonstrate the benefits of their kernelized approach, even in these simple settings.
