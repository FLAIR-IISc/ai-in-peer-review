This manuscript develops a spectral treatment of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces and proposes an algorithm to carry out this analysis from observed data generated by a dynamical system. The Koopman framework originates in dynamical systems and fluid mechanics, and the authors suggest it bears relevance for machine learning. They demonstrate the procedure on two simple systems—a damped one-dimensional linear dynamics and a chaotic map—and further test it on real-world locomotion data, comparing against standard dynamic mode decomposition, PCA, and kernel PCA, with results that are somewhat inconclusive. Conceptually, the work extends dynamic mode decomposition in a way reminiscent of prior “kernelized” operator-based methods (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, Gaussian process bandits). The empirical studies are largely on synthetic settings. The paper is generally clearly written and provides pedagogical value for readers unfamiliar with tools common in fluid dynamics. I am, in principle, supportive of work that introduces the ML community to techniques from other fields. Here, the approach amounts to a decomposition for maps M→M used to specify a deterministic dynamical system (without stochastic components). I am not aware of such setups being widely used in mainstream ML, where stochastic effects (innovations, noise) often play a central role, though I may be overlooking some lines of work. As it stands, more is needed to persuade ML readers of the practical utility of these ideas. What concrete use cases are compelling? Can the method compete, at least partially, with current state-of-the-art approaches in such areas? Robotics is one ML domain with substantial prior work on learning and inference in dynamical systems—what advantage would Koopman analysis provide there? Alternatively, please motivate another domain in comparable depth. While the theoretical development is careful, none of it is particularly surprising to readers familiar with kernel PCA/ICA, kernel independence testing, or “kernel Bayes’ rule”: one has a linear operator on an RKHS, enabling algorithms that reduce to eigendecompositions of finite-dimensional matrices.

Some details: It is unclear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires an explicit feature-space representation. If it does, this is a limitation relative to kernel PCA and should be stated explicitly. It would also help to include baseline results from standard DMD on the toy system and the Hénon map to make the differences evident in these simple settings.
