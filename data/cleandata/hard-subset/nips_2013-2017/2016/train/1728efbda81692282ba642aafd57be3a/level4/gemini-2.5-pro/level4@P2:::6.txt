This paper presents a method for performing spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The authors develop an algorithm, Kernel Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), to extract modes from data generated by a nonlinear dynamical system. This approach extends the standard DMD, a technique popular in fluid dynamics, by leveraging the "kernel trick" to implicitly use a rich set of nonlinear basis functions. The proposed method is framed as a feature extraction technique for dynamical systems, and the authors demonstrate its use in applications such as state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The method is evaluated on a simple synthetic system, the chaotic Hénon map, and a real-world motion capture dataset.

The work's main contribution is to connect the theory of Koopman operators and DMD with the well-established framework of kernel methods in machine learning. This follows a successful tradition of "kernelizing" linear algorithms (e.g., PCA, ICA) to handle nonlinearities, making the underlying principles accessible to a machine learning audience.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Quality & Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the theoretical derivations are clearly presented and appear technically sound. The authors successfully bridge concepts from dynamical systems theory (Koopman operators) with machine learning (kernel methods), providing educational value for researchers unfamiliar with the former.

2.  **Originality:** The paper introduces a novel combination of familiar techniques by extending DMD to RKHS. While the general idea of applying the kernel trick to linear operators is not new, its specific application to Koopman spectral analysis is a valuable and interesting contribution.

3.  **Potential for Cross-Disciplinary Impact:** The work introduces the machine learning community to powerful tools from other scientific domains. This cross-pollination of ideas is commendable and has the potential to inspire new research directions.

### **Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance and Motivation for Machine Learning:** The primary weakness is the paper's limited motivation for a machine learning audience. The work focuses on deterministic dynamical systems, whereas most dynamical models in machine learning (e.g., state-space models, Gaussian process models) explicitly account for stochasticity through noise and innovations. The authors need to provide a more compelling argument for why this deterministic, decomposition-based approach is relevant and useful for solving concrete problems in machine learning. It is unclear what advantages this method offers over established techniques in domains like robotics or time-series forecasting.

2.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is not sufficiently convincing.
    *   The synthetic experiments on the toy system and Hénon map would be much stronger if they included a direct comparison with the standard (linear) DMD to clearly demonstrate the benefits of the kernelized extension.
    *   The results on the real-world locomotion data are presented via a qualitative MDS plot, which makes it difficult to assess performance. A quantitative comparison on a defined task (e.g., classification accuracy for recognizing dynamics) against relevant baselines (like Kernel PCA or other time-series feature extractors) is needed to substantiate the claims of the method's utility.

3.  **Incremental Novelty:** While the combination of ideas is novel, the core technical insight—using kernels to linearize a problem in a high-dimensional feature space—is a well-known concept. Given this, the burden of demonstrating practical significance through strong empirical results is higher, and the current experiments do not fully meet this standard.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   **Motivation:** Could you elaborate on specific machine learning applications where the analysis of deterministic dynamics is paramount and where your method would outperform existing stochastic models? For instance, what specific tasks in robotics, control, or signal processing would benefit from a Koopman spectral analysis over, say, a Gaussian Process Dynamical Model?

*   **Comparison to Baselines:** To better highlight the contribution, please add results for standard DMD on the toy example and the Hénon map. This would provide a direct and clear demonstration of the advantages conferred by the kernel-based approach in handling nonlinearity.

*   **Implementation Detail:** In your modal decomposition algorithm, is an explicit representation of the data in the feature space required at any point? Or does the entire algorithm rely solely on the Gram matrix (the kernel trick)? If the former, this would be a significant limitation compared to methods like Kernel PCA and should be explicitly discussed.

*   **Handling of Stochasticity:** The paper briefly mentions that the Koopman operator can be extended to stochastic systems. This point is crucial for relevance to the machine learning community. Could you expand on how your framework would handle or relate to systems with process or measurement noise?

In summary, the paper presents an elegant and theoretically sound extension of DMD using kernel methods. However, its contribution is weakened by a lack of compelling motivation for the machine learning community and an empirical evaluation that does not sufficiently demonstrate its advantages over existing or simpler methods. Strengthening the experimental validation and clarifying the target applications within machine learning would significantly improve the paper.
