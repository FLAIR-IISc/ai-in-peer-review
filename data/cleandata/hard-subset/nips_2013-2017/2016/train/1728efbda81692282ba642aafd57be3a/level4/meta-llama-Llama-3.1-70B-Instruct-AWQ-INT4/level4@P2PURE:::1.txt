This paper introduces a spectral analysis using the Koopman operator defined in a proper reproducing kernel Hilbert space, followed by a set of kernel approaches for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems with empirical data. The eigendecomposition of the projection of the Koopman operator results in efficiency gains in both synthetic and real data analysis problems. This paper presents a significant kernel construction for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems, with well-motivated contributions and experimental validation.

However, I have several concerns that I hope the authors can address. The key differences between this work and the paper by M.O. Williams, I.G. Kevrekidis, and C.W. Rowley (Journal of Nonlinear Science, 2015) are unclear, as the latter already introduced Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for nonlinear system dynamics analysis. I would appreciate a discussion clarifying the main differences and an improved discussion in Section 4.

The field of nonlinear dynamical system analysis with kernels is vast, and some relevant references are missing, including papers by L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, or M. O. Franz and B. B. Schölkopf. Although the Koopman operator is generally defined for deterministic systems, I would appreciate comments on its performance in stochastic settings, which are more realistic. The authors briefly touch upon this issue, but further discussion and a toy experiment would be welcome.

The authors clarify the remark on page 5, line 216, that the estimation procedure does not necessarily require a sequence but rather a collection of pairs of consecutive observables, where each pair is supposed to be nonlinearly pointwise related. I am curious about the implications of this assumption in multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relations: what do we gain with the proposed kernel construction? Could you discuss this? Related to this, I would appreciate it if the authors could elaborate on the causal assumption inherent in the setting.

Regarding the experimental settings, I find them not very ambitious. The prediction via premising follows a standard well-known approach. Under the "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" subsection, there is a lack of discussion on the connections (and experimental comparison) between the proposed approach and the literature on clustering time series (e.g., through kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods). The sequential change-point detection experiment would be more appealing with a comparison to state-of-the-art online change detection with kernels (e.g., the one used by the authors is known to fail in complex dynamics). More convincing experiments are expected to support the development.

Minor issues: the paper is very well-written and clear. I spotted two minor typos: please check the indices on tau-1 vs. tau, and replace the dot with a comma in k(xi,xj) (last equation on page 7).
