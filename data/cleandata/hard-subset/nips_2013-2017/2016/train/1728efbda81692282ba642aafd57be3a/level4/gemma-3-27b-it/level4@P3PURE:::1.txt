This paper introduces a spectral analysis framework leveraging the Koopman operator defined in a proper reproducing kernel Hilbert space. The authors then proceed to define a set of kernel methods for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems from empirical data. Eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator yields efficiency gains in both synthetic and real-world data analysis. This work presents a significant kernel construction for nonlinear dynamical system analysis, with a well-motivated contribution and experimental validation. However, I have several concerns that I hope the authors will address. 

First, the substantial difference between this work and the paper by Williams, Kevrekidis, and Rowley [35], “A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator: Extending dynamic mode decomposition,” Journal of Nonlinear Science, 25:1307–1346, 2015, which already introduced Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for analyzing nonlinear system dynamics, is not clear. I would appreciate clarification and a detailed discussion outlining the key distinctions. An improved discussion of Section 4 would also be beneficial.

Second, the field of nonlinear dynamical system analysis with kernels is extensive, and several relevant references are missing. I suggest the authors consult papers by Ralaivola and d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., Mouattamid and Schaback, or Franz and Schölkopf. 

Third, while the Koopman operator is generally defined for deterministic systems, I would appreciate comments on performance in stochastic settings, which are often more important and realistic. The authors briefly mention this issue, but a more thorough discussion, and potentially a simple experiment, would be valuable. 

Fourth, the authors clarify (line 216, page 5) that the estimation procedure requires a collection of pairs of consecutive observables, not necessarily a sequence, where each pair is assumed to be nonlinear pointwise related. I am curious about the implications of this assumption in multidimensional time series where pointwise relationships may be weak. What advantages does the proposed kernel construction offer in such scenarios? Relatedly, I would appreciate elaboration on the causal assumptions inherent in this setting.

Finally, the experimental evaluation, while sound, appears somewhat limited in scope. The prediction via pre-imaging follows a standard approach. The “Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics” subsection lacks discussion of connections to, and experimental comparison with, the extensive literature on time series clustering (e.g., via kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods). Furthermore, for the sequential change-point detection experiment, a comparison to state-of-the-art online change detection methods with kernels would be more compelling (the method currently used is known to struggle with complex dynamics). More convincing experiments are expected to bolster the claims.

*Minor: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. I identified two minor typos: please check the indices on τ−1 versus τ, and replace the dot with a comma in k(xi.xj) (last equation on page 7).
