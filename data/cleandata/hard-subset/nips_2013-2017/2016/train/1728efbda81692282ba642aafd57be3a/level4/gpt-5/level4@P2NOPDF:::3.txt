This paper develops a spectral analysis of the Koopman (composition) operator in RKHSs and proposes a modal decomposition algorithm to carry out that analysis. It also introduces a robustness enhancement and presents several numerical experiments demonstrating the method’s utility. Overall, the framework extends dynamical mode decomposition via the kernel trick.

Strengths: The paper is very well written and well structured. The theoretical components needed for the derivation are presented clearly, and the methodology appears technically sound. While the paper does not include an in-depth comparison with state-of-the-art alternatives, the experiments adequately illustrate the method’s usefulness; in my view this is acceptable.

Major concerns: My primary concern is Section 3.1, particularly Equation (7) and the proof of Theorem 1, which I find unclear.

- Equation (7) seems essentially the same as Equation (2). What additional content or insight does Eq. (7) provide beyond Eq. (2)?
- More importantly, Eq. (7) appears inconsistent: KH is a linear operator from H to H, whereas φ is a mapping M → H, not an element of H. In what sense is KH being applied to φ in Eq. (7)? Please specify the spaces for all objects involved and clarify whether Eq. (7) is an operator identity, a pointwise identity in H, or something else (e.g., after evaluating at x ∈ M).
- This issue carries over to the proof on Lines 144–157. What does “applying the operator KH to both sides” (Line 149) mean when both sides are scalars? Since KH: H → H, it cannot be applied directly to scalar quantities. If there is an implicit identification (e.g., via representers or Riesz isomorphisms), please state it explicitly and justify its use.

Assumptions in Theorem 1: The assumptions appear strong and are not stated explicitly enough. Please:
- List all assumptions clearly in the theorem statement (e.g., boundedness/compactness, injectivity, domain/codomain specifications, spectral properties).
- Avoid introducing additional assumptions only in the proof; if they are necessary, include them in the theorem statement and discuss their plausibility.
- Explain when these assumptions hold in practice (typical kernels, data regimes, or dynamical systems), and whether they can be verified or enforced.

Despite these concerns, I believe the overall direction is promising and the technical development is compelling once the above points are clarified.
