Summary
The paper proposes a spectral analysis framework for nonlinear dynamical systems by defining the Koopman operator in an appropriate reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) and performing eigendecomposition of its projected form. Building on this, the authors develop kernel-based algorithms to analyze empirical data, demonstrating efficiency gains on both synthetic and real datasets. The kernel construction is interesting, the motivation is clear, and the empirical results support the approach.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing formulation of the Koopman operator in an RKHS, enabling spectral analysis with nonlinear observables.
- Empirical demonstrations indicating computational and practical benefits.
- Clear exposition; the paper is generally well written.

Main concerns and suggestions
1) Relation to prior work (Williams, Kevrekidis, Rowley, 2015).  
It is not clear what the substantive differences are between this paper and Williams et al. (2015), which already introduced Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes via Extended Dynamic Mode Decomposition (EDMD) to analyze nonlinear dynamics. Please clarify:
- How the proposed RKHS-based projection and eigendecomposition differ from (or generalize) EDMD’s finite dictionary approach. Is the kernel here simply an implicit, possibly infinite dictionary, or are there theoretical or computational distinctions (e.g., consistency, regularization, spectral convergence, stability)?
- Whether the efficiency gains stem from kernelization, from a different estimator for the Koopman operator, or from new numerical techniques for eigendecompositions.
- What is new in Section 4 relative to EDMD and kernel-DMD variants; a more explicit comparison would help position the contribution.

2) Positioning within the kernel/dynamics literature.  
The literature on kernel methods for dynamical systems is extensive, and some relevant references appear to be missing. Please discuss and situate your work in relation to, for example:
- L. Ralaivola and F. d’Alché-Buc
- D. Tuia et al.
- J. Rojo-Álvarez et al.
- M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback
- M. O. Franz and B. Schölkopf
A brief taxonomy of related kernel approaches (e.g., kernel-based system identification, kernel time-series embeddings, operator-theoretic kernel methods) and how your method differs would strengthen the paper.

3) Deterministic versus stochastic settings.  
While the Koopman operator is classically defined for deterministic systems, realistic data are often stochastic (process noise, observation noise, or both). The manuscript briefly mentions this, but further discussion would be valuable:
- What operator is your estimator converging to under stochastic dynamics (e.g., the stochastic Koopman operator in expectation, or a Markov transfer operator)?
- How sensitive are the spectral estimates to observation noise and to process noise? Are there regularization or denoising strategies tailored to your kernel construction?
- A small synthetic experiment (e.g., a noisy nonlinear map or a Markovian system) could illustrate robustness and limitations.

4) Data pairing assumption and causality.  
You state that the estimation procedure “does not necessarily require a sequence but rather a collection of pairs of consecutive observables,” each assumed to be nonlinearly pointwise related. Please clarify:
- In multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relations (e.g., where dynamics depend on unobserved state or history), what do we gain from the kernel construction? When are pairs sufficient versus when is temporal context (lags, embeddings) necessary?
- What causal assumptions are required for identifiability (e.g., that x_{t+1} depends only on x_t in the observed variables)? How would hidden confounding or partial observability affect the estimates?
- Practical guidance on when to use paired samples versus embedded sequences would be helpful.

5) Experimental scope and baselines.  
The experimental section could be more ambitious and comparative:
- Prediction via pre-imaging largely follows standard practice; please discuss methodological choices, stability of the pre-image step, and how performance scales with kernel and regularization parameters.
- For “Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics,” there is no discussion or comparison to the extensive literature on time-series clustering with kernels (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernel methods). Including such baselines or at least analytical comparisons would clarify advantages.
- For sequential change-point detection, it would be more compelling to compare against state-of-the-art online kernel change detection methods. The baseline used is known to fail on complex dynamics; stronger baselines would better support the claimed benefits.
- Consider adding ablations (e.g., choice of kernel, dictionary size if applicable, regularization) and reporting computational costs to substantiate the claimed efficiency gains.

Clarification and technical details
- Provide guidance on kernel selection and hyperparameter tuning in this context. Are there criteria linked to Koopman spectral properties?
- Discuss sample complexity and numerical stability of the eigendecomposition, particularly for large-scale datasets and near-degenerate spectra.
- If possible, add theoretical guarantees (or cite existing ones) for consistency or error bounds of the projected Koopman operator in the RKHS setting.

Minor comments
- The paper is generally very clear and well written.
- Please check indices involving τ−1 versus τ.
- Replace the dot with a comma in k(x_i, x_j) in the last equation on page 7.
- A careful pass for minor typographical issues (e.g., “preimaing” → “pre-imaging”) would further polish the manuscript.

Overall assessment
This is a promising and well-motivated contribution that brings RKHS machinery to Koopman spectral analysis with encouraging empirical results. However, to fully establish novelty and impact, the paper should more clearly articulate its distinctions from EDMD and related kernel methods, expand its discussion of stochastic settings and causal assumptions, and strengthen the experimental evaluation with more competitive baselines and analyses. Addressing these points would substantially improve the paper’s clarity, positioning, and empirical credibility.
