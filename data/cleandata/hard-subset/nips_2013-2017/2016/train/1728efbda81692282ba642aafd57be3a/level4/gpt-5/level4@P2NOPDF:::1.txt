This paper proposes a spectral analysis framework for nonlinear dynamical systems using the Koopman operator in a suitable reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). Building on this setup, the authors follow a standard line of development to derive kernel-based methods for analyzing nonlinear dynamics from empirical data. The eigendecomposition of a projected Koopman operator is used, and the resulting algorithms exhibit computational gains on both synthetic and real datasets. The kernel construction is interesting and appears to be effective. Overall, the contribution is well motivated and empirically supported. That said, several issues merit clarification and further development.

Major comments:
- Relation to prior work: The differences from Williams, Kevrekidis, and Rowley (Journal of Nonlinear Science, 2015), which already introduced Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for analyzing nonlinear dynamics via extended DMD, are not sufficiently clear. Please delineate what is novel here—conceptually, algorithmically, and in terms of guarantees—beyond that work and related DMD extensions. In particular, clarify how the RKHS construction and the projected operator’s eigendecomposition go beyond the EDMD framework, both in theory and in practice. An expanded discussion in Section 4 would be helpful.
- Missing connections to kernel literature: The field of kernel methods for dynamical systems is extensive, and several relevant works are not cited or discussed. Please consider including and positioning your approach relative to contributions by L. Ralaivola and F. d’Alché-Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Álvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, and M. O. Franz and B. B. Schölkopf, among others. A brief taxonomy explaining where your method sits with respect to kernel time-series modeling, kernel PCA/KCCA dynamics, and kernel-based spectral methods would improve context.
- Stochastic dynamics: While the Koopman operator is classically defined for deterministic systems, realistic applications are stochastic. You briefly mention this, but a more substantive discussion is needed. How does the proposed estimator behave under measurement noise or process noise? Are you approximating the stochastic Koopman operator (or Markov/transfer operators) in any sense? What failure modes arise as the signal-to-noise ratio decreases? A small toy experiment illustrating robustness to stochasticity would strengthen the paper.
- Use of unordered pairs vs. sequences: The statement that the estimation procedure “does not necessarily require a sequence but rather a collection of pairs of consecutive observables” raises questions. In high-dimensional time series where pointwise relations can be weak or partially observed, what advantage does the kernel construction provide over linear or finite-dimensional feature maps? How sensitive is the approach to violations of a strong pointwise (causal) relationship? Please elaborate on the implicit causal assumption and discuss whether your method can accommodate delays, multistep dependencies, or hidden-state effects (e.g., via lag embeddings).
- Experimental scope and comparisons: The experimental design feels conservative.
  - Prediction via pre-imaging follows standard practice; please clarify what is novel in this part and whether pre-image artifacts affect performance.
  - Embedding and recognition of dynamics: There is little discussion of links to the time-series clustering literature (e.g., kernel k-means and subspace kernel methods) and no empirical comparison. Including baselines from this literature would make the case stronger.
  - Change-point detection: The chosen baseline is known to struggle with complex dynamics. Please compare to state-of-the-art online kernel change detection methods. Analysis of detection delay vs. false-alarm trade-offs would be valuable.
  - Overall, more ambitious experiments and stronger baselines would better substantiate the benefits claimed for efficiency and accuracy.

Strengths:
- Clear formulation that leverages RKHS machinery to approximate Koopman spectra.
- Demonstrated computational gains through operator projection and eigendecomposition.
- Well-written and accessible presentation.

Suggestions for clarification:
- Provide theoretical or empirical guidance on kernel choice, feature map richness, and regularization for stable eigenstructure estimation.
- Discuss sample complexity: how many pairs are needed to recover leading eigenvalues/modes with acceptable error?
- Comment on the stability and conditioning of the projected operator, and whether techniques like spectral filtering or Tikhonov regularization are required.

Minor comments:
- The manuscript is very well written overall. Two minor typos: check indices on τ−1 vs. τ; and replace the dot with a comma in k(x_i, x_j) (last equation on p. 7).

Overall assessment:
This is a promising and well-presented contribution that brings RKHS tools to Koopman spectral analysis with encouraging empirical results. To reach its full impact, the paper should more clearly differentiate itself from EDMD and related Koopman/DMD work, situate itself within the broader kernel literature on dynamical systems, address stochastic settings more substantively, and include stronger experimental comparisons.
