### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a method for performing spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The proposed approach, which can be seen as a kernelized version of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), aims to find the eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes of a nonlinear dynamical system from data. By leveraging the kernel trick, the method implicitly uses a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions to approximate the Koopman eigenfunctions, moving beyond the linear observables typically used in standard DMD. The authors first derive an algorithm based on a QR decomposition in the feature space and then present a more practical, robust version based on Kernel Principal Component Analysis (KPCA). The utility of this "Kernel DMD" (KDMD) is demonstrated as a feature extraction technique for applications such as short-term prediction, recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution—extending DMD to RKHS—is both novel and significant. It elegantly connects the well-established framework of DMD from the fluid dynamics and dynamical systems communities with the powerful tools of kernel methods from machine learning. This generalization provides a principled way to perform Koopman spectral analysis using a much richer class of basis functions than standard DMD, without requiring the user to specify them explicitly.

2.  **Potential Impact:** The paper has the potential for high impact. KDMD could become a valuable tool for analyzing time-series data across various fields. By framing the method as a feature extraction pipeline, the authors demonstrate its applicability to core machine learning tasks (prediction, classification, change-point detection), which will likely attract interest from a broad audience. The connection it builds between two distinct but related fields is a major strength.

3.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical development in Sections 3 and 4 appears to be sound. The derivation is a logical extension of the principles underlying DMD (i.e., its relationship to the Arnoldi method and Krylov subspaces) to the feature space. The inclusion of a robust version based on projecting onto principal components (Section 4) is crucial for practical implementation and addresses known numerical stability issues in DMD-type algorithms.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The main weakness of the paper is its clarity and presentation, which currently hinders its accessibility and impact. I believe the work is promising, but it requires a substantial revision to be suitable for the NIPS audience.

1.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is very difficult to read, even for a reader familiar with the underlying concepts. The writing is often overly dense with mathematical formalism where more intuitive explanations would be more effective.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should aim to make the paper more pedagogical. For example, the sentence, "Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point," could be simplified to clarify the motivation more directly. The addition of more high-level intuition before diving into the proofs and equations would greatly benefit readers who are not experts in Koopman operator theory.

2.  **Paper Organization and Focus:** The paper's structure feels imbalanced. The core contributions are spread thinly across several sections, and the experimental validation could be more focused.
    *   **Section 4 (Robustifying with POD Bases):** While technically important, this section describes a kernelized version of a standard technique used to stabilize DMD. Its presentation could be more concise, framing it clearly as a necessary practical adaptation rather than a standalone conceptual contribution.
    *   **Sections 6 & 7 (Examples & Applications):** The initial examples in Section 6 are very brief, while the more compelling applications in Section 7 feel rushed. The paper would be stronger if these sections were better integrated. For example, one of the toy systems could be used as a running example throughout the methods sections to illustrate the steps of the algorithm, and more space could then be dedicated to a deeper analysis of the real-world applications.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments demonstrate the method's potential, but they could be strengthened to better highlight its unique advantages.
    *   **Baselines:** The paper lacks comparisons to relevant baselines. A particularly important comparison would be against Extended DMD (EDMD), which also uses a dictionary of basis functions. Comparing KDMD (with a polynomial kernel) to EDMD (with an explicit polynomial basis) on the toy problems would help quantify the practical advantages of the kernel approach.
    *   **Choice of Examples:** The toy systems are polynomial, making a polynomial kernel a natural and perhaps obvious choice. It would be more convincing to show an example where the underlying dynamics are not easily represented by a simple class of functions, demonstrating the power of a general-purpose kernel like the RBF kernel. The connection to recent work on discovering Koopman-invariant subspaces for control (e.g., Brunton et al., arXiv:1510.03007) could also be discussed to better position the work.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents an exciting and powerful generalization of DMD using kernel methods. The core idea is strong, and the potential applications in machine learning are compelling. However, the paper is severely hampered by its lack of clarity and poor presentation. It is currently too cryptic for a broad audience. I believe that with a significant revision focused on improving readability, restructuring the narrative, and strengthening the experimental validation, this could be an excellent and highly-cited paper. I encourage the authors to undertake this revision.
