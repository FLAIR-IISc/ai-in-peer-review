Summary
This paper studies Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs) and proposes a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) algorithm. The method constructs orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in an RKHS and then computes an eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projected onto the span of those bases. A robust variant projects data onto kernel POD (kernel PCA) directions to mitigate ill-conditioning, and the paper illustrates applications including short-term prediction via preimage reconstruction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition. The approach extends standard DMD by replacing linear monomials with richer kernel-induced features, which is well aligned with trends in extended DMD and spectral methods used in machine learning and control.

Strengths
- The paper is generally well written and well organized, with a clear high-level narrative and a technically solid derivation.
- The core idea—casting Koopman spectral analysis in an RKHS and implementing a kernelized DMD via Gram-Schmidt/QR and eigendecomposition—is interesting and timely.
- The robustification via kernel POD is motivated and practically useful, addressing well-known numerical weaknesses of vanilla DMD.
- The empirical demonstrations on synthetic and real data convey the method’s potential, and the applications section usefully illustrates how the extracted modes can be used in downstream tasks.

Major concerns and requests for clarification
My main concern is the notation and functional-analytic setup in Section 3 (in particular Eq. (7)) and the proof of Theorem 1:

1) Eq. (7) and operator domains/codomains:
- You define ϕ: M → H (a feature map) and then define (KHϕ)(x) = ϕ ∘ f(x). As written, KH is presented as a linear operator from H to H, but ϕ itself is not an element of H; it is a map into H. This is different from the standard Koopman setup, where K acts on scalar observables h ∈ H (with H an RKHS of scalar functions on M) via (Kh)(x) = h(f(x)).
- Please clearly specify whether H is an RKHS of scalar-valued functions on M, with reproducing kernel k, and whether ϕ(x) denotes the representer κx = k(x, ·) ∈ H. If so, the Koopman operator KH should act on elements h ∈ H by composition, and your Krylov matrix Mτ = [ϕ(x0) … ϕ(xτ−1)] consists of representers κx. In that setting, Eq. (7) should be interpreted accordingly, not as KH acting on the feature map ϕ.
- Alternatively, if H is a Hilbert space of H-valued functions and KH is a composition operator acting on H-valued functions, please define the spaces and domains precisely. As it stands, Eq. (7) looks identical to Eq. (2) but with a type mismatch.

2) Theorem 1 and “applying KH to both sides”:
- In the proof (Lines 144–157), you state “by applying the operator KH to both sides,” but the preceding identity involves scalars/finite-dimensional vectors, and KH: H → H would not apply. Please rewrite this step with the correct linear-algebraic identities. From what follows, it seems you use MτF = KHMτ with F = R−1PτR, not KH acting on scalar quantities.
- More generally, the bridge from the infinite-dimensional composition operator to the finite-dimensional companion matrix F via QR/Arnoldi-style relations is plausible, but the notation needs to ensure all objects live in the right spaces at each step.

3) Assumptions in Theorem 1:
- The assumptions appear strong and are not fully explicit. Please state them clearly upfront. For example: distinct eigenvalues λ̃j; full column rank of Mτ (so R is invertible); that the Krylov subspace has dimension τ; that ϕ(xτ) admits the decomposition in Eq. (10); and any assumptions about invariance of the span under KH.
- The proof also introduces additional assumptions (e.g., diagonalizability, Vandermonde structure) that should be stated with justification. Under what conditions are these expected to hold, and are they generic for typical kernels and data?

Quality
- Technical soundness hinges on clarifying the operator domains and the use of representers versus feature maps. The numerical procedures (QR in feature space via Gram matrices; kernel POD truncation) are standard and sound.
- The experimental section is modest but demonstrates the core claims. A deeper comparison with related methods (e.g., extended DMD) would strengthen the evaluation but is not strictly necessary for acceptance.

Clarity
- Overall clear, but Section 3 needs careful rewriting to resolve the type/notation issues around Eq. (7), KH, and ϕ. The proof of Theorem 1 should avoid ambiguous statements like “apply KH to both sides.”
- Please make all assumptions explicit when theorems are stated.

Originality
- The paper extends DMD to RKHSs, closely related to extended DMD and kernel methods for dynamical systems. The formulation and robustification are sensible and incrementally novel.

Significance
- The method could be useful in practice for extracting Koopman modes with nonlinear features, and the robust variant addresses real numerical issues. The applications suggest potential impact for short-term prediction, change detection, and recognition.

Pros
- Clear motivation and technically coherent approach
- Practical robustification via kernel POD
- Useful applications showcasing the extracted modes

Cons
- Notation/definitions around KH and ϕ are confusing and may be incorrect as written
- Assumptions in Theorem 1 are strong and not fully stated
- Limited comparison to closely related state-of-the-art (e.g., extended DMD)

Minor typos and edits
- Line 55: “the the” → “the”
- Line 153: use κj/λj instead of κi/λi (indexing mismatch)

Overall
I find the idea and execution promising, and the paper is generally well presented. However, Section 3’s functional setup and the proof of Theorem 1 need careful clarification and correction to ensure mathematical consistency. If these issues are addressed, I would be positive about acceptance.
