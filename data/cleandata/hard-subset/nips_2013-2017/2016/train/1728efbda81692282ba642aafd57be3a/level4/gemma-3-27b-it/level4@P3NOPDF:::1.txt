This paper presents a spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems based on the Koopman operator defined within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. The authors leverage this framework to develop a set of kernel-based methods for analyzing systems from empirical data. Eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator yields efficiency gains demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The work introduces a significant kernel construction for this analysis, and the contribution is well-motivated and supported by experimental validation. However, several points warrant further clarification and consideration.

A primary concern is the novelty of the approach relative to existing work. Specifically, the paper shares similarities with Williams, Kevrekidis, and Rowley [35], which already introduced the use of Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for nonlinear system dynamics analysis. The authors should explicitly address this overlap by providing a detailed discussion of the key distinctions between their method and the approach presented in [35]. A more thorough comparison would significantly strengthen the paper. Furthermore, section 4 could benefit from expansion and a more in-depth discussion of the presented results.

The field of nonlinear dynamical system analysis using kernels is extensive, and the current bibliography appears incomplete. I recommend the authors consider relevant works by Ralaivola & d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., Mouattamid & Schaback, and Franz & Schölkopf, among others, to provide a more comprehensive contextualization of their contribution.

While the Koopman operator is typically defined for deterministic systems, many real-world applications involve stochastic dynamics. I would appreciate a more detailed discussion of the method's performance in stochastic settings, despite the authors briefly acknowledging this point. A simple toy experiment designed to evaluate performance under stochasticity would be a valuable addition.

I seek clarification regarding the assumption outlined on line 216, page 5, stating that the estimation procedure requires only pairs of consecutive observables related by a nonlinear pointwise relationship, rather than a full sequence. In the context of multidimensional time series exhibiting potentially weak pointwise relationships, what advantages does the proposed kernel construction offer? Further elaboration on this point, and a discussion of the inherent causal assumptions within the framework, would be beneficial.

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating the method’s functionality, feels somewhat limited in scope. The prediction task, utilizing a standard time-series embedding approach, lacks ambition. The “Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics” subsection would be significantly enhanced by a discussion of the connections to, and ideally a comparison with, the extensive literature on time series clustering (e.g., using kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods).  Similarly, the sequential change-point detection experiment would be more compelling with a comparison to state-of-the-art online change detection methods employing kernels; the method currently used is known to struggle with complex dynamics. More rigorous and comprehensive experimental validation is expected to support the claims of the paper.

Finally, the paper is generally well-written and clear. I noted two minor points: please verify the indices on τ-1 versus τ throughout the text, and replace the dot with a comma in k(xi.xj) in the last equation on page 7.
