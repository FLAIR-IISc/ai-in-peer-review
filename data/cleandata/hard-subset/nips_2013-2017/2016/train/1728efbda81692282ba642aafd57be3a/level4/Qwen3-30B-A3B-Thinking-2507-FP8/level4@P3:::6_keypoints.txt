- Extends Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems.  
- Proposes an algorithm that approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using nonlinear kernel-based basis functions instead of linear monomials.  
- Method computes orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performs eigendecomposition of the operator's projection onto these bases.  
- Demonstrates applications in state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition on synthetic and locomotion data.  
- Properly situates work within Koopman/DMD and kernel-method literature, citing relevant prior art.  
- Theoretical development is technically sound with clear derivations and proofs.  
- Robustification via POD bases addresses practical limitations of a direct Gram–Schmidt approach.  
- Experimental validation is weak: lacks sufficient comparison to standard DMD and state-of-the-art ML methods.  
- Demonstrations use simple synthetic examples and a toy dataset, failing to show clear practical advantages.  
- Clarity and exposition are excellent; only minor ambiguity about whether explicit feature-space representations are required.  
- Originality: novel application of kernelization to Koopman analysis, but conceptually follows standard kernelizing patterns (e.g., kernel PCA).  
- Significance for the ML community is limited because practical utility over existing ML approaches (RNNs, GP dynamical models) is not convincingly shown.  
- Arguments for acceptance: mathematically sound, well-written, educational resource, and shows potential of kernel methods for dynamical systems.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear practical benefits, limited relevance to typical ML problems, and insufficient benchmarking against modern methods.  
- Recommended improvements: add rigorous comparisons with DMD and ML baselines on benchmarks, clarify target ML applications, discuss computational complexity and scalability, and state RKHS approach limitations relative to standard kernel methods.
