### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) framework to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is to leverage the Koopman operator, a linear operator that describes the evolution of observables of a nonlinear system. Standard DMD approximates the eigenfunctions of the Koopman operator using linear combinations of observables, which can be insufficient for highly nonlinear systems. The authors propose "kernel DMD" (kDMD), which implicitly maps the state-space data into a high-dimensional feature space via a kernel function. In this RKHS, the Koopman operator can be approximated more effectively, allowing for the identification of Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions that correspond to complex, nonlinear modes of the underlying dynamics.

The paper develops two algorithmic variants. The first is a direct extension of DMD based on a Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization of the Krylov subspace in the RKHS. Recognizing the potential for numerical instability, which is a known issue in standard DMD, the authors propose a second, more robust algorithm. This version projects the data onto the principal components in the feature space (via kernel PCA) before performing the decomposition. The authors demonstrate that the extracted Koopman modes can be viewed as powerful features of the dynamical system. They showcase the utility of these features in several machine learning applications, including short-term prediction, embedding and recognition of dynamics, and sequential change-point detection, using both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Synthesis:** The paper provides an elegant and theoretically sound integration of two powerful concepts: Koopman operator theory for dynamical systems and kernel methods for nonlinear data analysis. This synthesis addresses a key limitation of standard DMD—its reliance on a linear dictionary of observables—by enabling the use of a rich, implicitly defined set of nonlinear basis functions.

2.  **Practical and Robust Algorithm:** The authors astutely identify the potential for numerical instability in a direct kernelization of DMD. The proposed robust algorithm, which uses kernel PCA for dimensionality reduction and denoising, is a practical and crucial contribution that makes the method viable for real-world data. This shows a thoughtful approach to both theory and implementation.

3.  **Broad Applicability:** By framing the Koopman analysis as a feature extraction procedure, the paper successfully bridges the gap between the physics/fluid dynamics community (where DMD originated) and the machine learning community. The demonstrated applications in prediction, classification, and change-point detection are compelling and effectively highlight the method's potential significance for a NIPS audience.

4.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and logically organized. The authors provide a concise background on Koopman theory and DMD before smoothly transitioning to their kernel-based extension. The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) are presented clearly and connect the algorithm's outputs back to the underlying Koopman spectral properties.

**Weaknesses**

While this is a strong paper, there are areas that could be improved to make the contribution even more impactful.

1.  **Lack of Empirical Comparison to Baselines:** The primary weakness is the absence of a direct, quantitative comparison to existing methods. The experiments convincingly show that the proposed method works, but they do not establish *how much better* it performs than alternatives. A comparison against standard DMD and, importantly, Extended DMD (eDMD) [35]—which also allows for user-defined basis functions—would be necessary to properly contextualize the contribution. For instance, how do the estimated eigenvalues or prediction accuracy compare on the Hénon map example?

2.  **Discussion on Kernel Selection:** The performance of any kernel method is highly dependent on the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters (e.g., the width of a Gaussian kernel or the degree of a polynomial kernel). The paper provides little discussion on how to select an appropriate kernel for a given dynamical system or how sensitive the results are to this choice. A section discussing the intuition behind kernel selection, or even a brief sensitivity analysis on one of the datasets, would significantly strengthen the paper's practical value.

3.  **Quantitative Evaluation in Applications:** The evaluation of the applications in Section 7 is largely qualitative. While the results are illustrative, the claims would be stronger if supported by quantitative metrics. For example:
    *   **Prediction:** Report a standard error metric (e.g., RMSE) over multiple prediction horizons and compare it to a baseline.
    *   **Change-Point Detection:** Compare the detection delay and false positive rate against the baseline method under a controlled experimental setup.
    *   **Recognition:** Report classification accuracy for the motion capture data.

**Evaluation based on Review Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical derivations are sound, and the connection between the proposed algorithm and Koopman theory is well-established. The work is complete and carefully presented. The main limitation is in the experimental validation, which lacks rigorous baselines.

*   **Clarity:** The clarity is outstanding. The paper is a model of good scientific writing—well-organized, easy to follow, and with concepts explained lucidly from first principles.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. The synthesis of DMD with the kernel trick to perform Koopman analysis in an RKHS is a novel and significant contribution. It clearly differentiates itself from prior work by providing a principled, data-driven way to construct a rich basis for approximating Koopman eigenfunctions.

*   **Significance:** The potential significance is high. This method provides a powerful new tool for nonlinear feature extraction from time-series data. It is likely to be used by researchers and practitioners in machine learning for tasks involving system identification, time-series analysis, and control. It advances the state of the art in data-driven analysis of nonlinear dynamics.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and theoretically grounded extension of a powerful spectral analysis technique.
*   The combination of Koopman theory and kernel methods is elegant and impactful.
*   Addresses a key limitation of prior DMD methods.
*   Demonstrates broad applicability to core machine learning problems.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The empirical evaluation lacks comparisons to relevant baselines (e.g., standard DMD, Extended DMD), making it difficult to assess the practical gains.
*   The critical issue of kernel choice and hyperparameter tuning is not adequately addressed.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is a very strong paper with a high-quality contribution. The strengths—particularly the novelty, theoretical soundness, and clarity—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the experimental section. These weaknesses could be addressed in a final version of the paper. I recommend acceptance.
