The paper proposes a spectral analysis method for nonlinear dynamical systems based on the Koopman operator. The Koopman operator provides a linear operator representation of nonlinear function composition, enabling the decomposition of the forward dynamics of a system, x(t+1) = f(x(t)). Given trajectories collected from the system, the authors perform an eigen-decomposition of the operator leveraging concepts from kernel PCA and subspace analysis. The method is then applied to embedding, recognition, and change-point detection in dynamical systems, demonstrating promising results. For readers familiar with kernel methods, the key contribution lies in framing function composition – and, specifically, dynamical systems as a particular instance of this – as a linear operator amenable to decomposition. While dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) is already established in the dynamical systems literature, this work extends it by incorporating kernel methods. However, alternative approaches exist for recognizing dynamical systems (e.g., recurrent neural networks) and detecting changes, and the applications presented do not fully demonstrate a compelling advantage or unique capability of the proposed method.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound, building upon established concepts from dynamical systems and kernel methods. The theoretical extensions of DMD using RKHS seem well-motivated, and the proposed algorithm is clearly outlined. The empirical results, while limited, support the claims regarding the method's ability to estimate Koopman eigenvalues and apply them to prediction and system recognition. However, the authors could strengthen the analysis by providing more detailed discussion on the choice of kernel parameters and their impact on performance. It’s not entirely clear how sensitive the method is to parameter selection, or how the authors addressed this. The connection to existing change-point detection methods is acknowledged, but a more thorough comparison would be beneficial.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although some sections could benefit from further clarification. The notation is consistent, but the mathematical derivations could be more accessible to a broader audience. For example, a more intuitive explanation of Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 would be helpful. The organization, while logical, feels slightly repetitive – particularly in the lengthy description of the algorithm in Sections 3 and 4. Streamlining these sections would improve readability.

**Originality:** The core idea of extending DMD with reproducing kernels is a novel contribution, providing a way to capture nonlinear relationships more effectively than traditional DMD, which relies on linear monomials. However, the overall approach builds heavily on existing work in kernel methods and dynamical systems analysis. The originality lies in the combination of these concepts rather than a fundamentally new theoretical breakthrough. A more extensive discussion of related kernel-based approaches for dynamical systems, beyond what is currently presented in Section 5, would further clarify the paper's unique contribution.

**Significance:** The potential significance of the work lies in its ability to provide a more accurate and flexible framework for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems, particularly those where linear approximations are inadequate. The applications demonstrated—prediction, system recognition, and change-point detection—are all important problems with broad relevance. However, the empirical evidence presented is relatively limited. Demonstrating the method's effectiveness on more complex, real-world datasets would significantly strengthen its impact. While the method shows promise, it’s not yet clear if it provides a substantial advantage over existing techniques in practical applications.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of DMD using reproducing kernels.
*   Sound theoretical foundation and clear algorithmic description.
*   Demonstrated ability to estimate Koopman eigenvalues and apply them to relevant tasks.
*   Potential for improved analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical validation and comparison with existing methods.
*   Applications are not particularly compelling or demonstrate significant advantage.
*   The originality is incremental, building heavily on existing work.
*   The writing, while generally clear, could be more concise and accessible.
