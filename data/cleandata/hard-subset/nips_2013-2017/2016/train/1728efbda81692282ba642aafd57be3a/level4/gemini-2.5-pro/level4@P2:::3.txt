This paper introduces a method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by extending the Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) framework to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs). The core idea is to analyze the Koopman operator, a linear operator that governs the evolution of observables of the system, within the rich functional space provided by kernels. The authors present an algorithm for this kernel-based DMD (kDMD), propose a robust version based on projecting data onto kernel principal components (analogous to POD-based robustification in standard DMD), and demonstrate its utility across several applications, including prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

The paper connects the growing literature on DMD, popular in fluid dynamics, with well-established kernel methods from machine learning. This synthesis allows for the estimation of Koopman modes and eigenvalues for highly nonlinear systems by implicitly using a rich, nonlinear set of basis functions, overcoming a key limitation of standard DMD which is often restricted to linear observables.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a novel and elegant extension of DMD using the kernel trick. This provides a principled and powerful framework for applying Koopman spectral analysis to complex nonlinear systems, a topic of significant interest to the machine learning community. The ability to extract features that linearize nonlinear dynamics has clear potential for a range of tasks, as convincingly demonstrated in the applications section.
2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and logically structured. The authors provide a clear introduction to the Koopman operator and DMD before carefully developing their kernel-based extension. The theoretical concepts are presented methodically, making a potentially dense topic accessible.
3.  **Technical Quality:** The core theoretical development appears solid, and the connection drawn between the proposed method and the spectral properties of the Koopman operator is compelling. The experiments, while not exhaustively compared against all state-of-the-art methods for dynamical systems, effectively illustrate the method's capabilities on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

Despite the strengths, the technical presentation has some points of ambiguity that hinder a full assessment of the work's soundness and reproducibility. Addressing these would significantly strengthen the paper.

1.  **Definition of the Koopman Operator in RKHS:** My primary concern lies with the formal definition of the Koopman operator in the feature space. Equation (7) defines `(KH phi)(x) = phi o f(x)`. This notation is confusing. `KH` is described as a linear operator in `H`, meaning it should map elements of `H` to `H`. However, `phi` is the feature map `M -> H`, not an element of `H` itself. Is the intended definition that `KH` acts on elements `h` in `H` such that `(KH h)(x) = h(f(x))`? Or is the operator defined such that it maps the feature representation of a state, `phi(x)`, to the feature representation of the next state, i.e., `KH(phi(x)) = phi(f(x))`? Clarifying the domain, codomain, and action of `KH` is crucial, as this ambiguity makes the subsequent theoretical claims difficult to verify.

2.  **Clarity of Proofs and Assumptions:** The ambiguity in the operator's definition carries over to the proofs. For instance, in Theorem 2, it is assumed that `phi_j` is a Koopman eigenfunction and that `phi_j(x) = <phi(x), kappa_j>`. This implies `phi_j` is a scalar-valued function, while `kappa_j` is its representer in `H`. However, the eigen-equation `KH phi_j = lambda_j phi_j` would suggest `phi_j` is an element of `H`. A more precise distinction between the scalar eigenfunctions and their vector representations in `H` would greatly improve the clarity of the proofs.

3.  **Discussion of Assumptions:** Theorem 2 relies on the assumption that the representer `kappa_j` of a true Koopman eigenfunction lies within the subspace spanned by the top `p` kernel principal components (`U`). This is a strong assumption, essentially stating that the finite dataset is rich enough for its principal components to approximate the true underlying eigenfunctions. While this is necessary for the method to work, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the conditions under which this assumption is likely to hold and the potential failure modes when it does not.

**Minor Points:**

*   Line 55: "the the" -> "the"
*   In the text accompanying Theorem 2 (which I assume the draft's mention of Line 153 refers to), the proof uses `a_j` as the left eigenvector. It might be helpful to clarify the notation used for left and right eigenvectors in the main text.

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a promising and well-motivated method for nonlinear system analysis by merging Koopman theory with kernel methods. The potential impact is significant. The presentation is clear and the experimental results are encouraging. However, the paper's central theoretical claims are clouded by notational and definitional ambiguities, particularly concerning the Koopman operator in RKHS. I would be inclined to recommend acceptance if the authors can rigorously clarify these points in a revised manuscript, as doing so would solidify the paper's technical contribution.
