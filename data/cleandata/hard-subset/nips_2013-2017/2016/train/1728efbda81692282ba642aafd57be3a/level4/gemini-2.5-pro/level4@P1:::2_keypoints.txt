- Proposes kernel DMD: extending Dynamic Mode Decomposition to RKHS to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions using nonlinear kernel-defined bases.  
- Core idea: analyze the infinite-dimensional Koopman operator in an RKHS so eigenfunctions can be approximated with rich nonlinear observables instead of linear ones.  
- Algorithm performs an eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projected onto a data-derived subspace, analogous to Kernel PCA.  
- Outputs Koopman eigenvalues and modes as features for downstream tasks and demonstrates applications in state prediction, change-point detection, and dynamics classification.  
- Conceptual contribution: nicely bridges Koopman operator theory and kernel methods, offering an insightful spectral framing for a ML audience.  
- Principled extension: natural, well-motivated move from linear DMD to expressive nonlinear approximations via RKHS.  
- Technical soundness: clear, correct derivation and a robustified algorithm using truncated Kernel PCA to mitigate numerical instability.  
- Novelty concern: kernelization is an incremental step and similar ideas (e.g., Extended DMD) reduce perceived novelty; advantages over such methods are not clearly articulated.  
- Weak experimental significance: experiments do not convincingly establish practical benefits or superiority over strong ML baselines.  
- Lacks comparisons to standard time-series models (LSTMs/GRUs) and probabilistic dynamical models (Gaussian Process Dynamical Models) to assess data-efficiency, interpretability, or robustness.  
- Change-point detection experiments miss comparisons to a broader set of modern detection algorithms.  
- Empirical evaluation limited to simple synthetic examples and one unbenchmarked real dataset; needs more challenging benchmarks to demonstrate unique advantages.  
- Overall: technically solid and well-motivated contribution that connects dynamical systems and ML, but limited experimental benchmarking undermines claims of practical significance.
