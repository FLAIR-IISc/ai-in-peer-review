**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces an extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) to Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), a method the authors term Kernel DMD (KDMD). The central idea is to perform a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within a high-dimensional feature space defined by a kernel function. This allows for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions using a richer, nonlinear set of basis functions than is possible with standard DMD. The authors first present a formulation of KDMD based on a Gram-Schmidt process in the feature space and then propose a more robust variant that leverages kernel Principal Component Analysis (PCA) for projecting the data. The utility of KDMD is demonstrated through several applications on synthetic and real-world data, including state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and the recognition of dynamic patterns.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this work is its core contribution: the generalization of DMD to RKHS. This is a significant and elegant extension that connects the well-established theory of Koopman operators with the powerful framework of kernel methods. By allowing the analysis to be performed in a feature space, the method can capture complex nonlinear dynamics more effectively than standard DMD, which is limited to linear combinations of observables. The potential impact of this work is very high; if presented clearly, this method could become a standard tool for data-driven analysis of nonlinear systems and the paper is likely to be highly influential and widely cited.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

Despite the strength of the core idea, the paper suffers significantly from issues with clarity and presentation, which currently hinder its accessibility and impact. The writing is often overly dense and cryptic, making it difficult for even a specialist to follow the key arguments.

1.  **Clarity and Writing Style:** The manuscript would benefit greatly from a more pedagogical approach. Many concepts are presented with dense mathematical formalism where simpler, more intuitive explanations would be more effective. For example, the description of DMD's conceptual basis on page 3 ("Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation...") is verbose and could be summarized in a much shorter, clearer sentence. I strongly encourage the authors to revise the paper with the goal of making their important contribution more accessible to the broader NIPS audience.

2.  **Paper Structure and Balance:** The paper's structure and the level of detail across sections are imbalanced.
    *   Section 5 (Related Works) feels disconnected from the main narrative. This section could be shortened and its key points better integrated into the introduction to provide more immediate context.
    *   In contrast, Section 6 (Empirical Example) and Section 7 (Applications) are overly condensed. These sections are crucial for demonstrating the value of the proposed method, but they lack the necessary detail for the reader to fully appreciate the results. These sections should be expanded to provide a clearer explanation of the experimental setup and a more thorough discussion of the outcomes.

**Detailed Comments**

*   **Clarification on Toy Example (Section 6):** Please clarify the description of the toy nonlinear system. While the system as a whole is nonlinear due to the `x^2_t` term in the equation for `y_{t+1}`, the evolution of `x_t` itself is linear. This could be a source of confusion, and it would be helpful to state explicitly where the nonlinearity lies.

*   **Choice of Kernels:** The first two examples involve systems with polynomial dynamics. These are ideal cases for KDMD, especially when a matching polynomial kernel is chosen. The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of this connection. The authors might consider citing and discussing related work on Koopman invariant subspaces and finite linear representations, such as Brunton et al. ("Koopman invariant subspaces and finite linear representations of nonlinear dynamical systems for control," arXiv:1510.03007, 2015), which is highly relevant here.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a novel and potentially high-impact generalization of DMD using kernel methods. The theoretical contribution is strong. However, the paper is currently held back by significant clarity issues and an imbalanced presentation. The writing is too cryptic for a broad audience, and the crucial application sections are underdeveloped. I believe this work is not yet ready for publication at NIPS, but I strongly encourage the authors to revise it. A rewritten, more pedagogical version of this paper would be an excellent contribution and would likely have a significant impact on the field.
