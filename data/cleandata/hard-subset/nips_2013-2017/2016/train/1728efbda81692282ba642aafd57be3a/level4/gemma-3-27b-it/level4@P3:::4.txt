This paper proposes an extension of Koopman spectral analysis using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs). The authors aim to improve upon existing dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) techniques by leveraging the expressiveness of kernel functions to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions, particularly in scenarios where linear monomials of observables are insufficient. The method is presented with a clear theoretical foundation and evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The core idea is to perform a modal decomposition by calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and then performing eigendecomposition on the projected Koopman operator. This approach allows for a potentially richer representation of the dynamics compared to traditional DMD, which relies on linear approximations. The paper also addresses potential practical issues, such as ill-conditioning, by proposing a robustification technique based on projecting data onto POD directions. Finally, the authors demonstrate potential applications of the extracted features, including state prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the concepts are explained in a straightforward manner.
*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The extension of Koopman analysis to RKHS is well-motivated and mathematically sound. The connection to DMD is clearly established, providing a helpful context for readers familiar with the latter.
*   **Practical Considerations:** Addressing the potential for ill-conditioning and providing a robustification strategy using POD is a significant strength. This demonstrates an awareness of practical challenges and a commitment to developing a usable method.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world data demonstrates the versatility of the proposed approach. The exploration of applications further highlights its potential impact.
*   **Related Work Discussion:** The paper provides a thorough review of relevant literature, situating the contribution within the broader context of spectral analysis and dynamical systems modeling.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the paper clearly explains the advantages of using RKHS, a direct comparison to non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis (or standard DMD) on the same datasets would strengthen the evaluation. Demonstrating a clear performance improvement, or highlighting scenarios where the RKHS approach excels, would be impactful.
*   **Kernel Selection Discussion:** The choice of kernel (polynomial for the toy system, Gaussian for the Hénon map) is presented without extensive justification. A more detailed discussion of kernel selection, including the impact of kernel parameters on the results, would be beneficial. Exploring different kernels or providing guidelines for kernel selection could enhance the practical utility of the method.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While robustness is addressed, the computational cost associated with kernel methods, particularly for large datasets, is not explicitly discussed. A brief analysis of the algorithm's scalability would be valuable.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and well-supported contribution. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are convincing. The authors demonstrate careful consideration of both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The writing is precise and accessible, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and understand the proposed method.

**Originality:** The extension of Koopman spectral analysis to RKHS represents a novel contribution. While building upon existing work, the authors present a unique combination of techniques that offers a promising new approach to nonlinear dynamical systems modeling.

**Significance:** The results are potentially significant, particularly for applications where nonlinear dynamics are prevalent and accurate modeling is crucial. The ability to extract meaningful features from complex data could have a broad impact on various fields, including engineering, physics, and machine learning.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and clear mathematical presentation.
*   Practical relevance and potential for real-world applications.
*   Well-written and easy to understand.
*   Addresses an important problem in nonlinear dynamical systems modeling.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited direct comparison to existing non-RKHS methods.
*   Lack of detailed discussion on kernel selection and its impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Koopman spectral analysis. The strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance. Addressing the concerns regarding comparative analysis and kernel selection in a revised version would further strengthen the work. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
