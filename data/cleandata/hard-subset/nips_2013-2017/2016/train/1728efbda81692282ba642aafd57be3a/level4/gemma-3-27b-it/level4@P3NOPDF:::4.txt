This paper presents an extension of Koopman spectral analysis to the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors demonstrate the utility of this approach through experiments on both simulated and real-world datasets. Overall, this is a very well-written and clearly presented contribution. The motivation is sound, the methodology is well-explained, and the experimental results are convincing. 

The core idea of lifting dynamics into an infinite-dimensional feature space via kernel methods offers a powerful way to apply Koopman spectral analysis to nonlinear systems where traditional, observable-based approaches may struggle. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this kernel-based extension provides a flexible and effective tool for system identification and prediction. The mathematical development is rigorous and accessible, and the authors appropriately connect their work to relevant literature on both Koopman operator theory and kernel methods.

The empirical evaluation is a strong point of this paper. The simulations showcase the method’s ability to accurately identify the underlying dynamics of a nonlinear system. The application to real-world data further validates the practical relevance of the proposed approach. The visualizations effectively illustrate the benefits of the kernel-based Koopman analysis.

While the paper is strong, I believe two additions would significantly enhance its impact. First, a comparative analysis against non-RKHS Koopman spectral analysis within the simulation framework would be valuable. Specifically, demonstrating the performance gains (or limitations) of the kernelized approach relative to traditional methods, using the same simulated systems, would clarify the benefits of this extension. This would allow readers to directly assess the advantages gained by incorporating kernel methods.

Second, the paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of kernel selection and its influence on the results. While the authors utilize a Gaussian kernel, exploring the impact of different kernel choices – including potentially data-driven kernel learning methods – and discussing their respective strengths and weaknesses, would provide further insight. This could be explored primarily within the simulation studies, systematically varying the kernel and observing the effect on the accuracy of the identified Koopman operator and the resulting predictions. Consideration of kernel parameters (e.g., bandwidth in the Gaussian kernel) and sensitivity analysis could also be beneficial. 

Addressing these points would not only strengthen the technical contribution but also provide more practical guidance for researchers intending to apply this method to their own problems.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of nonlinear system identification. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. The suggested additions represent relatively minor improvements that would further enhance the paper’s overall impact and usefulness. I recommend acceptance.
