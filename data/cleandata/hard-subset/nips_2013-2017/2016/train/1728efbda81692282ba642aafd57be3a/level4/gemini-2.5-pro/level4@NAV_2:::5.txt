This paper presents a method for performing Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The work extends the well-known Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) algorithm to operate within the feature space defined by a kernel function. The proposed algorithm, kernel DMD (kDMD), calculates the eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projected onto a data-driven subspace of the RKHS. This allows for the approximation of Koopman eigenfunctions using a rich set of nonlinear basis functions, offering a powerful alternative to the linear observables or predefined dictionaries used in standard DMD and its variants. The authors present a theoretically-grounded algorithm and a more practical, robust version based on projection onto kernel principal components. The utility of the method is then demonstrated on several applications, including prediction, sequential change-point detection, and dynamics recognition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The generalization of DMD to RKHS is a novel and significant contribution. It elegantly connects the powerful frameworks of Koopman operator theory and kernel methods, providing a principled, non-parametric approach to analyze complex nonlinear dynamics. The proposed method has high potential to become a widely-used tool for feature extraction and system identification.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development, including Theorems 1 and 2, provides a solid foundation for the algorithms and clearly connects the computed quantities to the underlying Koopman eigenvalues and eigenfunctions. The inclusion of a robust algorithm using kernel PCA is a thoughtful and important addition that addresses practical numerical stability issues.
*   **Scope of Applications:** The paper effectively demonstrates the versatility of the extracted Koopman modes as features across a diverse set of machine learning tasks, using both synthetic and real-world datasets. This successfully highlights the method's broad applicability beyond simple spectral analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's primary weakness is its clarity. The writing is often dense and relies heavily on mathematical formalism where more intuitive, high-level explanations would greatly aid comprehension. For example, verbose sentences such as "Conceptually, DMD can be considered as producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point" could be stated more concisely. The current presentation style may make the paper inaccessible even to experts in the field, thereby hindering its impact.
*   **Experimental Depth:** While the range of applications is commendable, the core empirical example in Section 6 is presented too briefly. A more detailed analysis would strengthen the paper significantly. For instance, discussing why a polynomial kernel is a good choice for the toy system and how this relates to the structure of its true eigenfunctions would provide valuable insight into the method's mechanics.
*   **Paper Organization:** The overall structure could be rebalanced. The "Related Works" section is comprehensive but could be condensed for a conference paper format. In contrast, the key experimental validation in Section 6 feels underdeveloped and would benefit from expansion.

**Detailed Suggestions:**

1.  I strongly encourage the authors to perform a thorough revision to improve the paper's readability and pedagogical value. Simplifying complex sentences and integrating more explanatory text to motivate the mathematical derivations would make the contribution much clearer and more impactful.
2.  The experimental analysis in Section 6 should be expanded. For the toy system, explicitly connecting the choice of the polynomial kernel to the successful recovery of the known polynomial eigenfunctions would serve as a powerful and intuitive illustration of the method's strengths.
3.  The paper would benefit from a more direct discussion on the practical implications of kernel selection. Since this choice is critical to the method's success, providing guidance on how to select an appropriate kernel based on prior knowledge of the system would be extremely useful for practitioners. This could be linked to recent work on Koopman invariant subspaces, such as [1].
4.  In Section 6, the description of the toy nonlinear system could be clarified. While the `x_t^2` term makes the coupled system nonlinear, the phrasing might lead a reader to overlook this. A minor rewording could prevent potential confusion.

In summary, this paper introduces a valuable and highly original contribution to the field. However, its current presentation obscures its quality. With a substantial revision focused on improving clarity and expanding the experimental discussion, I believe this work has the potential to be a highly cited and influential paper.
