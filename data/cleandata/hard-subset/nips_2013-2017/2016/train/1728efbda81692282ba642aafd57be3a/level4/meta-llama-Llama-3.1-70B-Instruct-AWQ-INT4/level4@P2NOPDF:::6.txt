The authors of this paper present a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and develop an algorithm to perform this analysis using measurement data from a dynamical system. The Koopman operator, originating from fluid dynamics, is explored for its relevance to machine learning. The proposed algorithm is demonstrated on two simple dynamical systems: a damped linear 1D dynamics and a chaotic map. Additionally, the authors apply their method to real-world locomotion data and compare its performance with standard dynamic mode decomposition, PCA, and kernel PCA, yielding somewhat unclear results.

The paper provides an interesting extension of dynamic mode decomposition, building upon older work on "kernelizing" techniques framed in terms of linear operators, such as kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, and GP bandits. The authors offer experimental results on largely artificial problems, and the paper is well-written, providing educational value for those unfamiliar with fluid dynamics concepts. Introducing machine learners to techniques from different disciplines is a commendable pursuit, and this work presents a decomposition technique for functions from M to M used to construct a dynamical system without stochastic components.

However, it is unclear whether such setups are commonly used in machine learning, as most dynamical systems in this field incorporate random effects like innovations and noise. To convince machine learning readers of the practical usefulness of these techniques, the authors should provide more context on potential use cases and comparisons with state-of-the-art methods. For instance, in the domain of robotics, where inference and learning in dynamical systems are crucial, it would be beneficial to explain how Koopman analysis can contribute to this field.

While the theoretical derivations are well-executed, they are not particularly surprising, given the existing knowledge of kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, and kernel Bayes rule. The application of a linear operator to functions in an RKHS yields algorithms performing eigendecomposition on finite-D matrices. Some details require clarification, such as whether the modal decomposition algorithm necessitates an explicit representation in feature space. If so, this limitation should be explicitly stated, especially in comparison to kernel PCA. Furthermore, demonstrating the performance of standard dynamic mode decomposition on the toy example and the Henon map would help illustrate the differences in this simple setup.

Overall, the paper has educational value, and its contribution to the intersection of fluid dynamics and machine learning is acknowledged. Nevertheless, to fully appreciate the practical implications of the proposed method, additional context and comparisons with existing techniques are necessary.
