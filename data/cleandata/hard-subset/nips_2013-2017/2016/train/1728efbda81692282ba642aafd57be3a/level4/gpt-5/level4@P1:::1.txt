Summary
The paper studies Koopman spectral analysis in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes a kernelized dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) algorithm. The core idea is to analyze nonlinear dynamics by lifting observations into an RKHS via a feature map ϕ, and then approximating the Koopman operator KH through its projection onto the finite-dimensional subspace spanned by a Krylov sequence {ϕ(x0), …, ϕ(xτ−1)}. The method computes orthogonal bases in feature space (via Gram–Schmidt/QR using Gram matrices), forms the projected operator, and performs an eigendecomposition to obtain empirical Ritz values and vectors, which play the role of Koopman eigenvalues and modes. To address numerical instability, the paper proposes a robust variant that projects data onto kernel PCA (POD) directions before eigendecomposition.

The authors position this as a feature extraction procedure for nonlinear dynamical systems and demonstrate applications: short-term prediction via preimage recovery, embedding/recognition of dynamics using kernel principal angles between subspaces of estimated Koopman eigenfunctions, and sequential change-point detection via a CUSUM-like criterion using a nonparametric exponential family model in RKHS. Experiments on a toy nonlinear system, the Hénon map, motion capture sequences, and a Lorenz map illustrate the approach.

Relation to prior work
DMD and Koopman spectral analysis have a strong presence both in fluid dynamics and in the ML literature. Compared to standard DMD (POD-based or Arnoldi-based) and its variants (exact DMD, optimized DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD), the present work replaces linear monomials of observables with nonlinear RKHS features, enabling richer approximations of Koopman eigenfunctions. This is close in spirit to extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015, [35]), which uses user-specified dictionaries; here, the kernel implicitly defines a rich dictionary and keeps computations in terms of Gram matrices. The robust variant via kernel PCA parallels the truncation used in practical DMD implementations.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of Koopman spectral analysis into RKHS, with clear derivations of the projected operator and its eigendecomposition using only kernel evaluations.
- Practical robustification via kernel PCA/POD that mirrors best practices in DMD, and permits truncation when the Gram matrix is near rank-deficient.
- The method is flexible: it works with sequences or, more generally, with collections of consecutive pairs, and supports several downstream tasks (prediction, recognition, change detection).
- The paper is generally well written and organized; the exposition of the algorithmic steps is clear.

Weaknesses and questions
- Relation to extended DMD (Williams et al., 2015, [35]): The technical and practical differences are not sufficiently discussed. Both frameworks approximate Koopman spectra using a feature/dictionary space. Please clarify what is novel here beyond using kernels as implicit dictionaries: for example, differences in operator estimation, handling of projection/truncation, numerical properties, and theoretical guarantees. Section 4 could be expanded to position the robust kernel-POD variant relative to extended DMD with regularization/truncation.
- Missing related work: The kernel literature on dynamical systems is broad. Please include relevant references, e.g., L. Ralaivola and F. d’Alché-Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Álvarez et al., M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback, and M. O. Franz and B. Schölkopf. This will help situate this contribution within kernel-based modeling of time series and dynamical systems.
- Stochastic settings: While the paper notes that Koopman operators can be extended to stochastic systems (acting on expectations), the empirical section does not explore this. Given the practical importance of stochastic dynamics, please add discussion of how noise affects eigenvalue estimates and mode recovery, and consider a small stochastic example.
- Data requirements and causal assumptions: The remark that the estimation procedure only requires pairs of consecutive observables (rather than a single contiguous sequence) is appealing. However, in multidimensional time series with weak pointwise relations, what is gained by the kernel construction? Please discuss robustness to weakly causal or partially observed dynamics, and clarify the causal assumptions implicit in x(i)2 = f(x(i)1). How does performance degrade if the mapping is only approximately pointwise (e.g., due to exogenous inputs or delays)?
- Experiments: The current experiments are not very ambitious. Prediction via preimaging follows standard practice; the embedding/recognition section lacks comparison to strong baselines from the time-series clustering literature (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace/kernel subspace methods); and the change-point detection experiment would be more convincing with comparisons to state-of-the-art online kernel change detection methods (the chosen baseline is known to fail in complex dynamics). More comprehensive comparisons would strengthen the empirical case and the significance of the contribution.
- Minor: Please check indices involving τ − 1 vs. τ, and replace the dot with a comma in k(xi, xj) (last equation on p. 7).

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The methods are technically sound; derivations and algorithmic steps are clear. However, the experimental evaluation is limited and lacks comparison to strong baselines.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well organized. Section 4 and the relation to extended DMD should be expanded, and the assumptions around paired data clarified.
- Originality: Using kernels to approximate Koopman spectra is a natural and useful idea; the specific combination of RKHS projection, QR in feature space, and a POD-based robustification is plausible but close to extended DMD with kernelized dictionaries. The novelty claim should be sharpened relative to [35].
- Significance: Potentially significant as a practical, flexible approach to Koopman spectral analysis with nonlinear features. Empirical breadth and stronger comparisons are needed to fully demonstrate impact.

Pros
- Clear RKHS formulation of Koopman spectral analysis and practical algorithm.
- Robust kernel-POD variant aligned with best practices in DMD.
- Versatile applications demonstrated (prediction, embedding/recognition, change detection).

Cons
- Insufficient differentiation from extended DMD; related work coverage incomplete.
- Limited experimental depth and lack of comparisons to strong kernel baselines.
- Minimal treatment of stochastic dynamics and of assumptions behind pairwise data.

Minor issues
- Fix τ − 1 vs. τ index inconsistencies.
- Replace k(xi.xj) with k(xi, xj).
