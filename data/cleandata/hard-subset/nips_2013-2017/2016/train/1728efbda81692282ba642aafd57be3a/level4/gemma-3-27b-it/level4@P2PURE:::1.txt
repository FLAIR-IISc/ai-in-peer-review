This paper presents a spectral analysis framework leveraging the Koopman operator defined within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. The authors then follow a standard approach to apply this to the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems using empirical data. Eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator yields efficiency gains demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world datasets. This work introduces a significant kernel construction for nonlinear dynamical systems analysis, with a well-motivated contribution and experimental validation. However, several points warrant further attention. Specifically, the distinction between this work and the 2015 paper by Williams, Kevrekidis, and Rowley [35] – “A data-driven approximation of the Koopman operator: Extending dynamic mode decomposition” – which already introduces Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for analyzing nonlinear system dynamics, is not sufficiently clear. I request the authors provide further clarification and discussion outlining the key differences. An improved discussion of Section 4 would also be beneficial. Given the extensive literature on nonlinear dynamical system analysis with kernels, several relevant references appear to be missing; the authors might consider papers by Ralaivola and d'Alche Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al., Mouattamid and Schaback, or Franz and Schölkopf. Although the Koopman operator is generally defined for deterministic systems, I would appreciate comments on the method's performance in stochastic settings, which are often more relevant in practical applications. The authors briefly address this, but a more detailed discussion and a corresponding toy experiment would strengthen the work. The authors clarify the remark on line 216, page 5, regarding the estimation procedure requiring only pairs of consecutive observables with a pointwise nonlinear relationship. I am interested in the implications of this assumption for multidimensional time series exhibiting potentially weak pointwise relationships: what benefits does the proposed kernel construction offer in such scenarios? Furthermore, elaboration on the causal assumption inherent in this framework would be welcome. Regarding the experimental evaluation, I find the settings somewhat limited in ambition. The prediction task via preimaging follows a standard approach. The “Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics” subsection lacks discussion connecting the proposed method to the substantial literature on time series clustering (e.g., kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods), and a comparative experimental analysis would be valuable. Similarly, for the sequential change-point detection experiment, a comparison to state-of-the-art online change detection methods with kernels (given that the current method is known to struggle with complex dynamics) would be more compelling. More substantial experiments are needed to fully support the developments presented. * Minor: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. I identified two minor typographical errors: please verify the indices on τ-1 vs. τ, and replace the dot with a comma in k(xi.xj) (last equation on page 7).
