Summary
The paper develops a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes an algorithm to estimate this spectrum from measurement data of dynamical systems. The approach extends dynamic mode decomposition (DMD) into an RKHS framework and is demonstrated on a 1D damped linear system, a chaotic map, and a real locomotion dataset, with comparisons to DMD, PCA, and kernel PCA.

Strengths
- Clear exposition and careful derivations. The paper is well written and accessible, especially for readers unfamiliar with Koopman analysis from fluid dynamics.
- Conceptual contribution that connects Koopman operator theory with kernel methods, aligning with prior successes in “kernelizing” linear-operator techniques (e.g., kernel PCA/ICA, kernel independence tests).
- Potentially useful toolbox for analyzing nonlinear dynamics via linear operators in feature space.

Concerns and weaknesses
- Practical relevance to core machine learning problems is not convincingly demonstrated. The current demonstrations are primarily toy systems, and the real-data results are difficult to interpret. It remains unclear where this method provides a tangible advantage over existing approaches.
- Novelty appears incremental relative to established RKHS operator frameworks. Given the rich literature on kernelized linear operators (kernel PCA/ICA, kernel conditional mean embeddings, kernel Bayes’ rule), the main algorithmic pipeline—estimating operators in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition on finite matrices—feels expected rather than surprising. The paper would benefit from a crisper statement of what is fundamentally new.
- Limited treatment of stochasticity and noise. Many ML applications of dynamical systems (e.g., time-series forecasting, control, robotics) are inherently stochastic or noise-contaminated. The current setup and experiments seem tailored to deterministic maps, leaving unclear whether the method handles noise robustly or relates to Koopman operators for stochastic processes.
- Empirical evaluation is insufficient to establish utility. The comparisons to DMD, PCA, and kernel PCA do not provide a clear takeaway. Missing ablations and baselines make it hard to attribute gains to the proposed components.

Specific questions and requests for clarification
- Modal decomposition and feature representation: Does the modal decomposition require explicit feature maps, or is everything implemented via kernel evaluations? If explicit features are required, this is a substantial limitation compared with kernel PCA and should be made explicit. If not, please clarify how modes are computed and interpreted without instantiating the feature space.
- Computational complexity and scalability: What are the time and memory costs in terms of the number of samples and chosen kernel (e.g., O(n^3) eigendecompositions)? Are approximations such as Nyström or random features applicable without breaking the operator estimates? How large were the datasets in practice?
- Hyperparameters and regularization: How are kernel choice, bandwidth, and regularization selected? How sensitive are the results to these choices?
- Out-of-sample evaluation: How are eigenfunctions and modes evaluated on new points? Is there a principled way to project new data, and how stable are the estimates?
- Stochastic systems: Can the method be extended to stochastic dynamical systems (e.g., Markov processes) via appropriate operator formulations? If so, please discuss how the estimator and guarantees would change.

Experimental suggestions
- Include standard DMD on the toy 1D system and the chaotic map (Henon) to isolate the benefits of the RKHS extension under clean, controlled conditions.
- Add robustness experiments with measurement noise and model misspecification. Many ML settings face noise, and showing that the approach degrades gracefully would strengthen the case.
- Provide more interpretable analysis on the locomotion data: what do the learned eigenfunctions/modes capture? Are there downstream tasks (segmentation, prediction, control-relevant features) where the method provides measurable gains?
- Benchmark against state-of-the-art methods in a domain where dynamical systems matter to ML, such as robotics. For instance, compare to linear state-space models, subspace identification, Gaussian process state-space models, modern DMD variants, and HMMs on tasks like gait phase estimation, behavior segmentation, or short-horizon prediction. Clarify what Koopman analysis buys a practitioner (e.g., better linearizability for control, more interpretable modes, improved prediction).
- Report clear quantitative metrics with uncertainty (e.g., reconstruction error, prediction error, mode stability), not only qualitative plots.
- Provide an ablation study disentangling the effect of kernelization from other algorithmic choices, and clarify when kernel PCA alone suffices versus when the Koopman operator adds value.

Positioning and contribution clarity
- More sharply delineate the theoretical novelty beyond existing RKHS operator theory. For example, are there new identifiability or consistency results specific to Koopman spectra in RKHS, or novel connections to kernel conditional operators that justify the approach?
- Articulate concrete use cases where Koopman spectral structure offers a decisive advantage over black-box predictors (RNNs, GP-SSMs) or classical linear system IDs, especially under limited data or interpretability constraints.

Minor comments
- Ensure that assumptions (e.g., ergodicity, compactness, observability) required for the spectral analysis are stated and linked to implications for finite-sample estimators.
- Clarify whether the approach yields Koopman modes in the original data space and how to reconstruct or forecast observables from estimated eigenfunctions.
- Improve the presentation of the comparison plots to make differences unambiguous and statistically supported.

Overall assessment
The paper is clearly written and technically solid, and it offers an appealing bridge between Koopman operator theory and kernel methods. However, in its current form, the novelty relative to existing RKHS operator frameworks is modest, and the empirical evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate practical benefits for machine learning. I would be more enthusiastic with stronger, noise-robust experiments, clearer positioning against prior kernel operator work, explicit comparisons to standard DMD on the simple setups, and at least one compelling ML application (e.g., in robotics) where the method demonstrates an advantage. As it stands, I lean toward rejection but believe the line of work is promising and could be impactful with a more compelling empirical and application-driven narrative.
