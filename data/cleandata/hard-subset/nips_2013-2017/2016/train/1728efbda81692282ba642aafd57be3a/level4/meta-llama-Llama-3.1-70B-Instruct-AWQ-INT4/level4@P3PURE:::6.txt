The authors of this paper derive a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and develop an algorithm to perform this analysis based on measurement data from a dynamical system. The Koopman operator, a concept from fluid dynamics, is explored in the context of machine learning, although its relevance to the field is not thoroughly established. The authors demonstrate their algorithm and analysis using two simple dynamical systems: a damped linear 1D dynamics and a chaotic map. Additionally, they apply their method to real-world locomotion data and compare its performance with standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA, yielding somewhat unclear results.

The paper presents an interesting extension of DMD, akin to older work on "kernelizing" techniques phrased in terms of linear operators, such as kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, and GP bandits. The authors provide experimental results on largely artificial problems, which, while valuable for educational purposes, may not be representative of real-world applications. The paper is well-written and has educational value for readers unfamiliar with concepts from fluid dynamics. The introduction of machine learners to techniques from different disciplines is a worthwhile pursuit.

However, the authors need to do more to convince machine learning (ML) readers of the practical usefulness of these techniques. Potential use cases and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods are essential to establish the relevance of Koopman analysis. For instance, in robotics, a domain where inference and learning in dynamical systems are crucial, it is unclear what advantages Koopman analysis offers. A more thorough explanation of the benefits and limitations of this approach in real-world applications is necessary.

While the theoretical derivations are well-executed, they are not particularly surprising, given the similarities to kernel PCA, kernel ICA, and other related work. The use of a linear operator in an RKHS leads to algorithms that perform eigendecomposition on finite-D matrices, which is a familiar concept.

Some specific details require clarification. It is unclear whether the modal decomposition algorithm necessitates an explicit representation in feature space. If so, this limitation should be explicitly stated, as it may impact the comparison to kernel PCA. Furthermore, demonstrating the performance of standard DMD on the toy example and the Henon map would help illustrate the differences between the two approaches in a simple setup.
