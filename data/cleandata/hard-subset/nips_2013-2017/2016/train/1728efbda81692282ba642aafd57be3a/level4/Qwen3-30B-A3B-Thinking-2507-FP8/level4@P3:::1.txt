

This paper proposes a novel extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) using reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) for Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. The authors present a modal decomposition algorithm that approximates Koopman eigenfunctions using kernel-based nonlinear basis functions, rather than the linear monomials used in standard DMD. The method involves calculating orthogonal bases for the Krylov matrix in RKHS and performing eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator, which can then be applied to feature extraction for various machine learning tasks. The paper builds upon the foundational work of Williams et al. (2015) on extended DMD, but extends it through the use of kernel methods to capture more complex nonlinear relationships.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with well-structured theoretical development. The mathematical derivations are correct and the algorithmic procedure is clearly presented. The authors demonstrate the approach on synthetic and real-world data, showing improved eigenvalue estimation compared to standard DMD on the toy nonlinear system and Hénon map. The applications to prediction, dynamics recognition, and change-point detection are well-motivated and demonstrate the utility of the extracted features.

Regarding originality, the kernel-based extension of DMD is conceptually novel, but the paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with Williams et al. (2015), which already introduced extended DMD using pre-determined basis functions. The authors should clarify how their kernel-based approach differs from and improves upon this prior work, particularly in terms of the expressive power of the basis functions and the resulting approximation quality. Additionally, the paper misses several relevant references in the kernel-based dynamical systems literature (e.g., Ralaivola & d'Alché-Buc, Tuia et al., Rojo-Alvarez et al.), which would strengthen the context of their contribution.

The significance of the work would be enhanced by more ambitious experimental validation. The prediction via preimage approach follows standard kernel methods and doesn't clearly demonstrate advantages over existing techniques. The dynamics recognition application lacks comparative analysis with established time-series clustering methods (e.g., kernel k-means, subspace kernel methods), making it difficult to assess the novelty of their approach. Similarly, the change-point detection experiment should include comparisons with state-of-the-art kernel-based online change detection methods to better demonstrate the advantages of their proposed approach.

The paper is generally well-written and clear, though Section 4 (robustification with POD bases) could be strengthened with more detailed discussion of the conditions under which this approach provides significant benefits over the basic algorithm. The authors' brief mention of stochastic settings (page 8) is insufficient; a more thorough discussion of how their method extends to stochastic dynamical systems, along with a simple toy experiment demonstrating this, would substantially strengthen the paper.

The authors should address the following specific concerns:
1. Clarify the key differences between their kernel-based approach and Williams et al. (2015) in the introduction and related work sections
2. Add references to recent kernel-based dynamical systems literature (Ralaivola & d'Alché-Buc, Tuia et al., etc.)
3. Expand the discussion of stochastic systems and provide a simple example
4. Improve the experimental validation by including comparative analysis with established methods in time-series clustering and change-point detection
5. Correct minor issues noted in the draft review (index notation, kernel function notation)

The paper makes a valid technical contribution to the field, but its significance would be substantially enhanced by addressing these concerns. The kernel-based extension to DMD is promising, but the current presentation doesn't sufficiently demonstrate its advantages over existing methods. With the suggested improvements, this work could make a meaningful contribution to the analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems.

Overall, the paper is technically sound (Quality), well-organized (Clarity), and introduces a novel kernel-based approach (Originality), but the significance (Significance) of the contribution would be strengthened by more thorough comparisons with existing methods and more ambitious experimental validation. I recommend a weak acceptance with the understanding that the authors address these concerns in the final version.
