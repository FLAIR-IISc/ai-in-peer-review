This paper presents a method for performing spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The authors introduce an algorithm to achieve a modal decomposition for nonlinear dynamical systems from finite data sequences. This is effectively a "kernelized" version of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), a technique with origins in fluid dynamics. The proposed method approximates the Koopman operator's eigenfunctions using a rich set of nonlinear basis functions induced by the kernel, in contrast to standard DMD which typically uses linear observables. The algorithm's core involves computing an orthonormal basis for the Krylov subspace in the RKHS and performing an eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projected onto this subspace. The approach is evaluated on synthetic data from a simple nonlinear system and the chaotic Hénon map, and applied to tasks like prediction and clustering on real-world motion capture data.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Quality:** The primary contribution is an elegant and theoretically sound extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition to RKHSs. This generalization connects the well-established Koopman operator theory with the powerful framework of kernel methods, allowing for the modeling of complex nonlinear dynamics in a principled manner.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It does an excellent job of introducing concepts from Koopman operator theory, which may be unfamiliar to a machine learning audience, and framing them in the familiar context of kernel methods. This makes the paper accessible and gives it significant educational value.
*   **Significance (Interdisciplinary):** The work serves as a valuable bridge between the fluid dynamics and machine learning communities. Introducing machine learning researchers to powerful techniques from other disciplines is a commendable goal, and this paper executes that well.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Significance (ML Context):** The paper could be significantly strengthened by better motivating its relevance and utility for the machine learning community. The analysis is presented for deterministic dynamical systems, whereas many ML applications, such as in robotics or time-series modeling, involve stochastic systems with process and observation noise. The authors should make a more compelling case for where their deterministic approach provides a distinct advantage over existing stochastic models in common ML problem domains.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical results are illustrative but lack depth. To demonstrate the practical advantage of the proposed kernel method, a direct comparison against standard DMD on the synthetic toy system and the Hénon map is essential. Currently, it is difficult to assess the performance gain offered by the kernelization. Furthermore, for the applications (prediction, recognition), the method is not benchmarked against relevant state-of-the-art machine learning techniques, making it hard to judge its practical significance.
*   **Originality of Technique:** While the application to Koopman analysis is novel, the theoretical machinery itself—applying the kernel trick to an algorithm involving linear operators and eigendecompositions—will feel familiar to readers versed in methods like Kernel PCA, Kernel ICA, or Kernel CCA. The contribution is more in the novel formulation for this specific problem rather than a fundamental advance in kernel methods.

**Detailed Comments:**
*   It would be beneficial to clarify whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires an explicit feature space representation or if all computations can be performed using only the Gram matrix. If the latter is true, this is a key advantage that should be highlighted; if not, this potential limitation should be discussed.
*   The connection to robotics is mentioned as a potential area where learning dynamical systems is important. It would strengthen the paper to elaborate on this, perhaps by discussing a specific robotics problem where Koopman analysis could outperform existing methods for system identification or control.
