Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes to perform Koopman spectral analysis in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The main idea is to lift data via a feature map ϕ: M→H and compute a finite-dimensional approximation of the Koopman operator by (i) constructing a Krylov matrix in feature space Mτ = [ϕ(x0),…,ϕ(xτ−1)], (ii) orthogonalizing its columns, and (iii) eigendecomposing the projection of the “Koopman operator in H” onto the span of these columns. This yields modal decompositions analogous to Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD), but with nonlinear features induced by kernels. The paper also presents a numerically robust variant using kernel PCA/POD, and demonstrates applications to prediction (via a preimage heuristic), change-point detection, and dynamics recognition on synthetic systems (toy system, Hénon, Lorenz) and motion capture data.

The work sits alongside DMD and its variants (exact/optimized DMD, sparsity-promoting DMD) and extended DMD (EDMD). It is especially close to kernelized EDMD/KDMD in Williams et al. (2015), which uses a kernel trick to define a finite-rank approximation of the Koopman operator on a subspace of observables. The robust POD-based variant here is also conceptually aligned with exact/optimized DMD practices.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for using RKHS features to approximate Koopman eigenfunctions with richer, nonlinear bases.
- The derivations are largely well structured, and the connection to Krylov subspaces/Arnoldi is conceptually sound.
- The POD-based robustification is practical and addresses conditioning issues known in DMD.
- The applications section is broad and illustrates potential utility beyond physics, including change detection and recognition.

Weaknesses and main concerns
1) Definition and domain/codomain of the “Koopman operator in H” (Eq. (7)) and Theorem 1:
- As written, KH is introduced by (KHϕ)(x)=ϕ∘f(x), and then called “a linear operator in H.” However, ϕ is a function M→H, so KH maps H-valued functions to H-valued functions; it is not an operator H→H. This mismatch propagates to expressions like Q*KHQ, which require KH: H→H.
- A standard way to make this rigorous is to define the Koopman operator K to act on scalar observables g: M→C, and relate scalar observables to H via linear functionals g_w(x)=⟨ϕ(x),w⟩H, w∈H. Then K g_w(x)=⟨ϕ(f(x)),w⟩. If there exists a bounded linear operator AH: H→H (on a suitable subspace) such that ϕ(f(x))=AH ϕ(x) (exactly or in least squares over data), one can write K g_w(x)=⟨ϕ(x),A∗H w⟩ and proceed to project AH onto span{ϕ(xi)}. Please clarify which of these assumptions you make and where they are used.
- In the proof of Theorem 1 and surrounding text, phrases like “apply KH to both sides” appear when the quantities are scalars. This is not meaningful if KH acts on functions. Please revise these steps to avoid applying KH to scalars and to keep the operator domains consistent throughout.
- Theorem 1 assumptions are not explicit. It seems you assume (i) distinct Ritz values; (ii) full rank of the Krylov matrix; and (iii) that the companion-matrix construction is well-posed in the (possibly infinite-dimensional) feature space via kernel Gram matrices. Please state all assumptions up front and explain when they hold (e.g., generic position of samples, no exact linear dependence, noise-free vs noisy data).
- Notational confusion between eigenfunctions φj and vectors κj in H: later (Theorem 2) you effectively assume scalar eigenfunctions of the form φj(x)=⟨ϕ(x),κj⟩. If that is your standing assumption, please adopt it consistently from Section 3 onward and avoid calling κj an “eigenfunction.”

2) Relation to prior kernel/DMD literature:
- The proposed approach appears very close to kernelized EDMD (Williams et al., 2015), which also operates in an RKHS via Gram matrices to obtain a finite-rank approximation of the Koopman operator and its modes. Please clarify the precise differences (algorithmic, theoretical guarantees, computational aspects) and include direct empirical comparisons. Without this, the originality is difficult to assess.
- Likewise, please position the robust POD-based variant relative to exact/optimized DMD and sparsity-promoting DMD, and discuss when your variant is preferable.

3) Claims about eigenvalues on attractors:
- The statement “for a system evolving on an attractor, the Koopman eigenvalues always lie on a unit circle” is not generally true; it holds for measure-preserving/unitary Koopman operators. Dissipative systems typically have eigenvalues inside the unit disk. Please correct or qualify this statement and its citation.

4) Experimental evaluation:
- The experiments are illustrative but largely qualitative. To assess significance, please include quantitative metrics (e.g., prediction error vs horizon, reconstruction error, stability of eigenvalues vs sample size, robustness to noise) and comparisons to EDMD/kernel EDMD and exact/optimized DMD on standard benchmarks.
- The preimage step is critical for prediction. Please provide details (neighbor count, distance normalization, sensitivity to kernel width) and a comparison to other preimage or regression-based approaches.
- For the motion recognition and change-point detection tasks, include baselines beyond a single kernel change detector, report accuracy/precision–recall/AUC, and provide ablations (effect of number of modes, truncation level p).

Quality
- The central idea is promising and technically plausible, but the operator-theoretic formulation needs to be made rigorous (domains, adjoints, projections) and some proofs clarified. The robustification is well motivated. Experiments need stronger baselines and quantitative evaluation.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but Section 3 suffers from domain/codomain ambiguities and overloaded notation (functions vs vectors in H). Making the scalar-observable viewpoint explicit (g_w(x)=⟨ϕ(x),w⟩) would resolve several issues. Please also make all assumptions explicit in Theorems 1–2 and align the notation κj/φj consistently.

Originality
- The kernelization of Koopman spectral analysis is known (EDMD/KDMD). The paper’s QR/Krylov framing and the specific robust kernel-POD projection may offer a slightly different perspective, but the novelty relative to kernel EDMD should be clarified and demonstrated.

Significance
- If the theoretical formulation is clarified and the empirical study strengthened with solid baselines, the work could be useful to researchers applying Koopman/DMD ideas in nonlinear settings, especially for feature extraction and short-term prediction. As is, the contribution appears incremental without clear advantages over kernel EDMD.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually appealing extension of DMD to RKHS with practical robustification.
- Broad applicability and interesting demonstrations across tasks.
- Clear connection to Krylov/Arnoldi methods and modal analysis.

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguities in the operator-theoretic formulation and proof details in Section 3.
- Limited distinction from existing kernel EDMD; no direct comparisons.
- Experiments are primarily qualitative, with few baselines and limited metrics.
- Some inaccurate theoretical claims (unit-circle statement).

Minor comments and typos
- Clarify Eq. (7) domain/codomain; avoid saying “KH is a linear operator in H.”
- Line 55: “the the.”
- Line 153: κj/λj (not κi/λi).
- Several references have incomplete bibliographic information and typos (e.g., “Aohnson”). Please standardize citations.
- Provide computational complexity and memory costs for both algorithms, and guidance on choosing kernel and truncation p.

Recommendation
Weak reject unless the authors substantially clarify the operator definitions/proofs in Section 3, correct theoretical statements, and add quantitative comparisons to EDMD/kernel EDMD and exact/optimized DMD. With these improvements, the paper could be a solid contribution.
