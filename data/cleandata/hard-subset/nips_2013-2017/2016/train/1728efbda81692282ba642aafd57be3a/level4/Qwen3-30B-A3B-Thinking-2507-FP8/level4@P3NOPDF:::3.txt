

This paper introduces a spectral analysis framework for the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs), leveraging the kernel trick to extend dynamical mode decomposition (DMD). The authors present a modal decomposition algorithm, a robustification strategy, and numerical experiments demonstrating practical utility. The work is technically sound, well-structured, and clearly articulated, with strong theoretical foundations. The numerical results effectively support the method’s efficacy, though the comparison to state-of-the-art approaches is limited—this omission is acceptable given the focus on methodological novelty. The paper’s clarity and rigor are commendable, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

However, significant concerns arise in Section 3.1, particularly regarding Equation (7) and the proof of Theorem 1 (Lines 144–157). These issues undermine confidence in the core theoretical claims. Equation (7) appears redundant relative to Equation (2), raising questions about its purpose. Crucially, Equation (7) states:  
$$\kappa_j = \langle \phi(x), K_H \phi(x) \rangle_H,$$  
where $K_H$ is a linear operator mapping $\mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{H}$. The notation suggests $\phi(x)$ is an element of $\mathcal{H}$ (which is correct, as $\phi: \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{H}$), but the left-hand side $\kappa_j$ is a scalar, while the right-hand side involves $K_H$ acting on $\phi(x)$. This is inconsistent: the expression $\langle \phi(x), K_H \phi(x) \rangle_H$ is valid (as it computes a scalar via the inner product), but the equation as written misrepresents the operator application. The phrase "by applying the operator $K_H$ to both sides" (Line 149) is problematic. Since both sides of Equation (7) are scalars, $K_H$ cannot be applied to them. Instead, the derivation likely intends to apply $K_H$ to the *function* $\phi$ in the RKHS, not to scalar quantities. This notational ambiguity must be resolved to avoid confusion.

Furthermore, the assumptions in Theorem 1 are insufficiently specified. The theorem states conditions under which the spectral decomposition holds, but these are neither explicit nor justified. For instance, the assumption that the Koopman operator is compact or normal is not stated, yet the proof implicitly relies on properties like the existence of a complete eigenbasis. Additional unstated assumptions emerge in the proof (e.g., convergence of the RKHS approximation), which are critical for the result’s validity. The authors should clarify:  
1. Which standard assumptions (e.g., compactness, boundedness) are required for the Koopman operator in this context?  
2. Why are these assumptions plausible for typical dynamical systems?  
3. How do the proof-specific assumptions relate to the theorem’s stated conditions?  
Without this, the theoretical contribution lacks grounding. If the assumptions are overly restrictive (e.g., requiring systems to be linear or finite-dimensional), this would limit the method’s applicability—a point the authors should address.

Minor issues include:  
- Line 55: "the the" → "the"  
- Line 153: "$\kappa_j / \lambda_j$" should be "$\kappa_j / \lambda_j$" (currently written as "$\kappa_i / \lambda_i$")  

The paper’s strengths—clarity, technical depth, and experimental validation—make it a strong candidate for acceptance. However, the unresolved theoretical ambiguities in Section 3.1 constitute a major flaw that must be addressed. The authors should:  
1. Re-express Equation (7) to clarify the role of $K_H$ (e.g., by defining the operator action on the feature map $\phi$).  
2. Explicitly state all assumptions for Theorem 1, distinguishing between theorem conditions and proof requirements.  
3. Provide justification for these assumptions in the context of dynamical systems.  

If these points are clarified, the paper would be a significant advancement. As written, the theoretical foundation is questionable, and the review process cannot endorse the current version. The authors are encouraged to revise the manuscript to resolve these issues before publication. The numerical experiments are compelling but cannot compensate for a weak theoretical core. The method’s promise hinges entirely on the correctness of the spectral decomposition, which remains unverified due to the ambiguities noted.  

In summary, this is a technically promising work with a clear exposition, but its core contribution is undermined by unresolved mathematical inconsistencies. Addressing these concerns would elevate it to a high-quality NIPS submission.
