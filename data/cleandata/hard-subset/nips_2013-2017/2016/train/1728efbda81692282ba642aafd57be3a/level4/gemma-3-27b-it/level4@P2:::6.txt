This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs), developing an algorithm for performing this analysis on data from dynamical systems. The Koopman operator, originating in fluid dynamics, is leveraged to provide a linear representation of nonlinear dynamics. The authors demonstrate their method on a damped linear system, the chaotic Henon map, and locomotion data, comparing it to dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA. 

The core contribution is an extension of DMD, leveraging kernel methods to potentially capture more complex dynamics than traditional linear approaches. This work aligns with a broader trend of “kernelizing” techniques commonly found in machine learning – analogous to kernel PCA, kernel ICA, and related methods – by applying kernel methods to a linear operator framework. The paper is well-written and provides a valuable introduction to Koopman operator theory for machine learning researchers. It successfully bridges concepts from fluid dynamics and signal processing with potential applications in learning-based dynamical systems modeling.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear exposition:** The paper is generally well-written and provides a good explanation of the underlying theory, particularly for readers unfamiliar with the Koopman operator.
*   **Novel Extension:** The application of RKHS to Koopman analysis offers a potentially powerful extension of existing DMD methods, allowing for the capture of more complex nonlinearities.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivations are well-executed and logically presented, building upon established concepts in kernel methods.
*   **Interdisciplinary Bridge:** The paper effectively introduces techniques from fluid dynamics to the machine learning community.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Practical Demonstration:** The empirical evaluation primarily focuses on relatively simple, synthetic systems. The results on the locomotion data are not clearly presented or convincingly demonstrate an advantage over existing methods.
*   **Lack of Application Context:** The paper lacks a compelling articulation of practical use cases within the machine learning field. While the theoretical foundation is strong, the benefits for typical ML applications (e.g., robotics, control, time series forecasting) are not fully explored or justified.
*   **Comparison to Baseline DMD:** The performance of standard DMD is not shown on the same datasets, hindering a clear assessment of the proposed method's improvements.
*   **Feature Space Representation:** The requirement of representing data in feature space could limit the scalability and practicality of the approach compared to standard kernel methods that operate implicitly in feature space. This limitation isn't adequately discussed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The work presents a theoretically sound and novel extension to dynamic mode decomposition.
*   It effectively bridges concepts between fluid dynamics and machine learning.
*   The paper is clearly written and educational.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation lacks sufficient evidence of practical advantage over existing methods in relevant machine learning applications.
*   The paper does not convincingly demonstrate the real-world utility of the proposed technique.
*   The limitations related to feature space representation need further discussion.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution, but falls short of demonstrating its practical significance within the machine learning community. While the extension of DMD using RKHS is interesting, the lack of compelling application scenarios and comprehensive empirical results weakens the overall impact. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak reject**. The paper has merit and could be improved with a stronger focus on practical applications, a more thorough empirical evaluation (including comparisons to baseline DMD), and a clearer discussion of limitations. If the authors can address these concerns, particularly by demonstrating a compelling use case or improvement over existing techniques in a relevant machine learning domain, a resubmission would be welcome.
