This manuscript extends the dynamical mode decomposition (DMD) method to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), resulting in a new algorithm the authors term Kernel DMD (KDMD). The method is demonstrated on several toy problems. By using the kernel trick, KDMD allows the feature map to be implicitly defined, providing a powerful mechanism to connect DMD with specific features of interest. This represents a powerful generalization of the standard DMD framework.

My main concern with this paper is its readability. The core ideas are excellent and potentially very impactful, but the presentation is difficult to follow. The writing is often opaque, relying heavily on dense mathematical formalism where simpler, more intuitive explanations would be more effective and sufficient. In contrast, some sections are unnecessarily verbose. This lack of clarity may be a barrier to comprehension even for readers familiar with the field, and several key points require clarification.

For these reasons, I believe the paper in its current form is not ready for publication at NIPS. However, I want to strongly encourage the authors to undertake a significant revision. A version of this paper rewritten with a focus on pedagogical clarity would be a valuable contribution to the community and would likely be highly cited.

**MAJOR REMARKS**

1.  **Presentation and Clarity:** The presentation is often verbose where it could be concise. For example, the description of DMD as "producing an approximation of the Koopman eigenfunctions using a set of linear monomials of observables as basis functions, which is analogous to a one-term Taylor expansion at each point" could be summarized in a much shorter and simpler sentence. The authors should review the manuscript to identify similar instances where the exposition could be streamlined.

2.  **Section 4:** This section on background material feels redundant, as much of its content is already (and appropriately) treated in the introduction. In a short conference paper, this section seems unnecessary and could be removed or significantly condensed to save space for more substantive discussion.

3.  **Section 5:** This section introduces interesting conceptual points but lacks illustration, making it difficult to grasp their full significance. The arguments would be much more compelling if supported by examples or figures. Alternatively, if space is a concern, the key insights from this section could be summarized in the conclusion.

4.  **Section 6:** In sharp contrast to other parts of the paper, Section 6 is overly condensed. The important results presented here warrant a more detailed development, perhaps following the more thorough conceptual style used in Section 5.

**MINOR REMARKS**

1.  There appears to be a typo in the description of the nonlinear (NL) toy problem, as the system described is actually linear.

2.  The first two experimental problems are based on polynomial dynamics. This is a setting where KDMD should excel, especially when the kernel can be chosen to match the underlying structure of the data [1]. More detailed analysis and discussion should be provided for these examples, perhaps in the style of Section 5, to better highlight the method's strengths in these ideal conditions.
