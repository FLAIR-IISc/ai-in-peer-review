This paper presents a method for the spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems by defining the Koopman operator within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). Following a standard methodology, the authors develop a set of kernel-based approaches for analyzing empirical data from such systems. The efficiency of the proposed method, which stems from the eigendecomposition of the projected Koopman operator, is demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world data.

The primary contribution is a novel kernel construction for this analysis, which is well-motivated and supported by experimental results. However, there are several concerns that the authors should address:

*   **Novelty and Relation to Prior Work:** The main concern is the distinction between the proposed method and the work of Williams et al. [35], who previously introduced the use of Koopman eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes for analyzing nonlinear system dynamics. The authors should provide a detailed discussion clarifying the substantial differences and novel contributions of their work relative to [35]. An improved discussion in Section 4 would be particularly helpful.

*   **Missing References:** The literature on kernel-based analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems is extensive, and several relevant lines of work appear to be missing. The authors are encouraged to review and cite relevant papers, for instance, by L. Ralaivola and F. d'Alche Buc; Tuia et al.; Rojo-Alvarez et al.; M. Mouattamid and R. Schaback; or M. O. Franz and B. Schölkopf.

*   **Performance in Stochastic Settings:** While the Koopman operator is formally defined for deterministic systems, most real-world applications involve stochasticity. The authors briefly touch on this, but a more thorough discussion on the method's expected performance in stochastic settings would be valuable. The inclusion of a simple toy experiment demonstrating this behavior would significantly strengthen the paper.

*   **Data Requirements and Causal Assumptions:** The authors state (line 216) that the estimation procedure requires only a collection of consecutive observable pairs, rather than a contiguous time series. This raises a question about the method's application to multidimensional time series where pointwise relationships between consecutive states may be weak. What are the advantages of the proposed kernel construction in such scenarios? Furthermore, a more explicit discussion of the causal assumptions inherent in this framework would be appreciated.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation could be made more compelling. Specifically:
    *   The prediction task via pre-imaging follows a standard, well-established approach.
    *   In the "Embedding and Recognition of Dynamics" section, the paper would benefit from a discussion and experimental comparison against the vast literature on time series clustering (e.g., kernel k-means and related subspace kernel methods).
    *   For the sequential change-point detection task, the chosen baseline is known to perform poorly on complex dynamics. A comparison against state-of-the-art online kernel-based change detection methods would provide a more convincing demonstration of the proposed method's capabilities.
    Overall, more ambitious experiments are needed to fully support the paper's contributions.

**Minor Points:**
*   The paper is generally well-written and clear.
*   Please check the indexing in the use of `tau-1` versus `tau`.
*   In the last equation on page 7, `k(xi.xj)` should be corrected to `k(xi,xj)`.
