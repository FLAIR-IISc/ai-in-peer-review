This paper presents a method for performing Koopman spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). This work effectively generalizes the popular Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) method, which typically operates on linear functions of the state, to a much richer, nonlinear function space defined implicitly by a kernel. The proposed algorithm computes a modal decomposition by finding the eigendecomposition of the Koopman operator projected onto a Krylov subspace within the RKHS. Recognizing potential numerical instabilities, the authors also introduce a more robust variant of the algorithm that projects the data onto kernel principal component analysis (POD) directions before performing the decomposition. The paper correctly situates this contribution relative to prior work in DMD and its variants, as well as broader techniques for analyzing dynamical systems like POD and subspace identification methods.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, presenting a rigorous and complete theoretical development. Theorem 1 provides the mathematical basis for the direct kernel DMD method, while Theorem 2 justifies the robustified version, demonstrating a thoughtful consideration of practical implementation challenges. The claims are well-supported by the provided theoretical analysis. The experimental results, while not exhaustive, successfully illustrate the method's functionality on both synthetic and real-world problems.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clear, and logically organized. It effectively guides the reader from the necessary background on Koopman theory and DMD to the novel contributions and their applications. The mathematical derivations are presented with clarity, and the algorithmic procedures are easy to follow.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—the formal extension of DMD to RKHSs—is novel and well-motivated. While related methods like extended DMD allow for user-specified basis functions, this work provides a principled framework for implicitly using the rich function classes associated with kernels. The connection to the Koopman operator is maintained throughout, and the work is clearly distinguished from prior art.

**Significance:** The proposed method has the potential for significant impact. By moving beyond linear observables, it offers a powerful tool for analyzing complex nonlinear dynamics. The authors compellingly frame the method as a feature extraction procedure for time-series data and demonstrate its utility on a diverse set of important machine learning tasks: short-term prediction, sequential change-point detection, and recognition of dynamic patterns in motion capture data. These applications suggest that the ideas presented are likely to be used and built upon by both researchers and practitioners in machine learning and other scientific fields.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces an elegant and theoretically well-grounded generalization of DMD to RKHSs, addressing a key limitation of the standard approach.
*   The work is thorough. The inclusion of a numerically robust algorithm based on kernel PCA demonstrates a mature understanding of the practical challenges involved.
*   The manuscript is of high quality in its presentation, clarity, and organization.
*   The practical value of the proposed method is convincingly demonstrated through a diverse range of applications, highlighting its potential as a general-purpose tool for analyzing nonlinear systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance / Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The empirical evaluation could be strengthened by including direct comparisons to relevant baseline methods. Showing results for standard DMD or extended DMD on the same tasks would help to quantify the performance gains achieved by the kernel-based approach and better justify its added complexity.
*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the impact of kernel selection. As with all kernel methods, the choice of the kernel and its hyperparameters is critical. An analysis of the method's sensitivity to this choice, or at least some guidance for practitioners on how to select an appropriate kernel for a given dynamical system, would make the contribution more complete.
