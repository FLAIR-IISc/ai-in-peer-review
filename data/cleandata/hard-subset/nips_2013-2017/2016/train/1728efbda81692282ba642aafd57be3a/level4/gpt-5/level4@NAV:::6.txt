This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces and introduces an algorithm to carry out this analysis from measurement data of a dynamical system. The Koopman operator originates in fluid dynamics, and the authors suggest it has relevance to machine learning. They demonstrate the approach on two simple dynamical systems: a damped linear 1D system and a chaotic map. They also apply the method to real-world locomotion data and compare with standard dynamic mode decomposition, PCA, and kernel PCA, with results that are somewhat unclear. The paper proposes an interesting extension of dynamic mode decomposition, reminiscent of earlier work on “kernelizing” linear-operator methods (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, GP bandits, etc.). The authors provide some empirical results primarily on artificial problems. Overall, the paper is clearly written and has educational value for readers unfamiliar with concepts from fluid dynamics. I am generally supportive of work that introduces machine learners to techniques from other fields. Here, the method appears to be a decomposition technique for functions from M to M that are used to construct a dynamical system (without stochastic components). I am not personally aware of such setups being commonly used in machine learning (typically, dynamical systems include random effects such as innovations and noise), though I may be missing relevant work. In this case, the authors should do more to convince ML readers of the practical utility of these techniques. What are potential use cases? Can you compete, at least to some extent, with the state of the art there? Robotics is an ML area where inference and learning in dynamical systems are important and heavily studied. What would Koopman analysis offer to someone working in robotics? Or pick another domain and make the case. While the theoretical derivations are well executed, they are not particularly surprising to readers familiar with kernel PCA or kernel ICA, or follow-up work on kernel independence tests or even “kernel Bayes rule.” You have a linear operator, and if functions live in an RKHS, one can do standard things. You end up with algorithms that perform some form of eigendecomposition on finite-dimensional matrices. Some details: It is not clear to me whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires an explicit representation in feature space. If so, this is a limitation compared to kernel PCA, and the authors should make this explicit. It would also be beneficial to show the performance of standard dynamic mode decomposition on the toy example and the Henon map to demonstrate the differences in this simple setup.
