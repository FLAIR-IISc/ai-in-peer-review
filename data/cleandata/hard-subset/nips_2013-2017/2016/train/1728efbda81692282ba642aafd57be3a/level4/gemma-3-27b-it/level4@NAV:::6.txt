This paper presents a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator within reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces, alongside an algorithm for performing this analysis using data from dynamical systems. The work bridges concepts from fluid dynamics with potential applications in machine learning. The authors validate their approach on both simple dynamical systems – a damped linear 1D system and a chaotic map – and on real-world locomotion data, comparing performance against dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA, though the results lack definitive clarity. 

The core contribution extends DMD, resembling prior “kernelization” strategies applied to linear operators found in areas like kernel PCA, kernel ICA, and Gaussian process bandits. While the theoretical development is sound, the experimental evaluation largely focuses on constructed examples. The paper is well-written and serves as a valuable introduction to fluid dynamics concepts for machine learning researchers. I support efforts to introduce techniques across disciplines, and this work represents a function-space decomposition approach relevant to dynamical systems lacking stochastic elements. 

However, to resonate more strongly with a machine learning audience, the authors need to better articulate the practical utility of this technique. Identifying specific application domains and demonstrating competitive performance against existing state-of-the-art methods is crucial. For example, exploring the benefits of Koopman analysis in a field like robotics, where inference and learning in dynamical systems are well-established, would be highly beneficial. Given the authors' use of kernel methods, the theoretical results are largely expected – leveraging the properties of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces naturally leads to eigendecomposition-based algorithms.

Specifically, it remains unclear whether the proposed modal decomposition algorithm requires explicit feature space representation, a potential limitation compared to kernel PCA that should be addressed. Furthermore, including a performance baseline for standard DMD on the toy example and Henon map would help to clearly delineate the advantages of the proposed method in a simplified setting.
