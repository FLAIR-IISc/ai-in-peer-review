Summary
The paper extends Koopman spectral analysis to a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) framework, providing a kernel-based approach to estimate spectral properties of dynamical systems. The method is evaluated on both simulated and real datasets. The paper is clearly written, well organized, and addresses an important problem at the intersection of dynamical systems and machine learning.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and positioning: The connection between Koopman operator theory and RKHS methods is timely and relevant, and the paper articulates this link well.
- Technical soundness: The derivations appear careful and principled, with an appropriate balance between theory and algorithmic development.
- Empirical validation: Experiments on simulated and real data substantiate the practical utility of the approach.
- Presentation quality: The exposition is lucid, and the results are easy to follow.

Areas for Improvement
1) Baselines against non-kernel Koopman methods:
- To contextualize the gains from the RKHS formulation, please include comparisons with standard non-kernel Koopman spectral analysis methods on the same simulated systems. Representative baselines could include DMD/EDMD-style approaches or other widely used finite-dimensional approximation schemes.
- Such comparisons would help isolate the benefits of kernelization (e.g., handling nonlinear observables implicitly) versus potential costs (e.g., computational overhead), and clarify when the kernel approach is preferred.

2) Kernel choice and hyperparameter sensitivity:
- The paper would benefit from a systematic exploration of kernel families (e.g., Gaussian/RBF, polynomial, Laplacian) and their hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidth, degree), including how they affect estimated spectra, eigenfunctions, and predictive performance.
- Please discuss practical strategies for kernel selection and tuning, such as median heuristic, cross-validation, or criteria based on reconstruction/prediction error or spectral stability.
- A sensitivity analysis or ablation study on kernel parameters and regularization strength would help practitioners understand robustness and trade-offs.

Additional Suggestions and Questions
- Computational complexity and scalability: Provide a more explicit complexity analysis with respect to the number of samples and the effective feature dimension (or rank), and discuss memory requirements. If applicable, comment on the use of low-rank approximations (e.g., Nyström, random features) and how they affect spectral accuracy.
- Stability and consistency: Clarify conditions under which the kernel-based Koopman estimates are consistent or stable (e.g., assumptions on ergodicity, mixing, or sampling). Any theoretical guarantees or empirical diagnostics for assessing convergence would be valuable.
- Noise robustness: Empirically assess sensitivity to observation and process noise, as realistic datasets often violate idealized assumptions. Does regularization in the RKHS help attenuate noise-induced spectral artifacts?
- Model selection criteria: If the method yields multiple plausible components (eigenvalues/modes), consider offering guidance on selecting the number of components or thresholds for spectral truncation (e.g., via scree plots, spectral gaps, or cross-validated reconstruction accuracy).
- Interpretability: For real-data experiments, discuss how the learned spectral objects (e.g., dominant eigenfunctions/modes) relate to domain-specific structures. This would strengthen the substantive conclusions beyond predictive metrics.
- Experimental details: Ensure that experimental setups (train/test splits, time horizons, preprocessing, kernel parameters, regularization coefficients) are fully specified to facilitate reproducibility. Reporting variance across multiple runs would quantify statistical reliability.
- Comparison metrics: In addition to qualitative spectral alignment, include quantitative metrics relevant to Koopman analysis (e.g., prediction error at various horizons, reconstruction error, and stability of identified eigenvalues across bootstraps or resamples).

Minor Comments
- Clarify notation early where operators, kernels, and finite-sample estimates interact, especially when switching between function space and matrix representations.
- Improve figure readability (axis labels, units, font sizes) and, where appropriate, overlay confidence intervals for spectral estimates.
- If available, provide code or pseudocode to aid reproducibility.

Overall Assessment
This is a strong and well-written paper that makes a meaningful contribution by bringing RKHS machinery to Koopman spectral analysis and demonstrating its practical value. The paper would be substantially strengthened by (i) direct comparisons to established non-kernel Koopman methods on the same systems and (ii) a deeper, empirical discussion of kernel choice and hyperparameter effects. Addressing these points would clarify the method’s advantages, provide guidance to practitioners, and bolster the empirical claims. I recommend acceptance pending the suggested additions and clarifications.
