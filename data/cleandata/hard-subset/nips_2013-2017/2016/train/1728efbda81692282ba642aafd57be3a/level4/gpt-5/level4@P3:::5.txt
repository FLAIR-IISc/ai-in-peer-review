Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a kernelized extension of Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) for Koopman spectral analysis. The authors define the Koopman operator in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), construct the RKHS Krylov subspace from time-shifted feature maps, and estimate Koopman eigenvalues/eigenfunctions via eigendecomposition of the operator projected onto that subspace. They give a kernelized algorithm that operates entirely through Gram matrices, and a “robustified” variant that projects onto (truncated) kernel PCA/POD directions to mitigate ill-conditioning. Theoretical statements (Theorems 1–2) connect empirical Ritz pairs to Koopman spectra under assumptions (e.g., distinct eigenvalues; eigenfunctions lying in the closure of the RKHS feature span). The method is illustrated on a toy nonlinear system and the Hénon map; additional applications include short-term prediction via pre-images, recognition of dynamics via kernel principal angles between subspaces, and sequential change-point detection with a CUSUM-like score.

The work is closely related to DMD and its variants (exact/optimized/sparsity-promoting DMD), and to extended DMD (EDMD), which estimates Koopman spectra using pre-specified dictionaries. The present contribution places Koopman spectral estimation explicitly in RKHS, thereby realizing an implicit (potentially infinite) dictionary via the kernel, and connecting DMD to kernel PCA/POD methodologies. The paper also touches on spectral methods in system identification and predictive state representations.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean kernelization of DMD that unifies Koopman spectral estimation with RKHS machinery; the Gram-matrix formulation is appealing and practical.
- The robustified procedure via kernel PCA/POD is sensible and parallels standard practice in DMD.
- Theorems provide a useful bridge between empirical Ritz pairs in RKHS and true Koopman spectra, clarifying when recovery is possible.
- The applications (pre-image-based prediction; subspace-angle embeddings; change-point detection) illustrate broader machine learning relevance beyond fluid dynamics.
- Code availability is valuable for reproducibility.

Weaknesses and needed clarifications
- Clarity and organization: The exposition is overly dense where a higher-level narrative is needed, and verbose in places that could be condensed. Key ideas (intuition for kernelizing DMD; when kernel choice helps; how truncation affects bias/variance) are buried in algebra.
- Assumptions: Theorems rely on strong assumptions (distinct eigenvalues; eigenfunctions in the RKHS span; κj in span(U)). Their practicality, violation modes, and implications should be discussed.
- Numerical stability and scalability: While Section 4 addresses ill-conditioning via POD, the paper lacks a clear complexity and memory analysis (Gram matrix O(τ2) memory, O(τ3) time), guidance for large τ, and discussion of alternatives (Nyström, randomized SVD).
- Relation to EDMD: The connection and differences to EDMD (and to exact/subspace/optimized DMD) deserve a more explicit comparison. Empirically, EDMD with polynomial/RBF dictionaries is a natural baseline for KDMD.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments are limited, mostly qualitative, and lack systematic comparisons, robustness to noise, hyperparameter selection (kernel width/degree, truncation rank), and quantitative metrics (e.g., spectral recovery error, prediction error, classification accuracy, detection delay/false alarms).
- Pre-image and change-point sections: The pre-image approach is sketched but lacks algorithmic details, complexity, and quantitative evaluation. The change-point derivation assumes a nonparametric exponential family; the approximation that drops the second term should be justified empirically, and comparisons to kernel CUSUM/MMD baselines reported.
- Notation and typos: Some notation is overloaded (H as centering matrix and as the Hilbert space), φ/ϕ usage varies, and there appear to be typos (e.g., the “nonlinear toy” system may read as linear; “0.92” likely denotes 0.9^2).

Quality
Technically, the core idea is sound and aligns with established kernel methods and Koopman/DMD theory. However, the current presentation does not fully justify assumptions, numerical choices, or the approximation steps in the applications. The experimental evidence is not yet sufficient to support strong claims about performance or robustness.

Clarity
Needs significant revision. Suggestions:
- Start with an intuitive overview of Koopman/DMD, then motivate kernelization with examples where linear dictionaries fail.
- Provide a concise algorithm box for KDMD and for the POD-truncated variant, including complexity and memory.
- Move lengthy derivations/proofs to an appendix; keep the main text focused on intuition and practicalities.
- Unify notation (H, φ/ϕ, adjoint vs. transpose) and fix typos.
- Condense related work to emphasize positioning vs. exhaustive listing.

Originality
Moderately original. Kernelizing DMD to operate in an implicit feature space is a natural and valuable generalization, with connections to EDMD. The formal RKHS framing and the POD-truncated variant are well-motivated, though conceptually close to prior art; the novelty is in the integration and analysis.

Significance
Potentially significant. A clear, well-validated KDMD could influence both the Koopman/operator-learning community and applications in ML where nonlinear dynamics matter. The current empirical evidence is insufficient to demonstrate consistent advantages over EDMD/DMD across tasks.

Specific suggestions for improvement
- Provide head-to-head comparisons against DMD, exact DMD, subspace DMD, and EDMD (with polynomial and RBF dictionaries) on:
  - Spectral recovery on systems with known eigenpairs (including noise).
  - Short-term prediction accuracy and robustness to noise/misspecification.
  - Dynamics recognition (report classification accuracy; compare to PCA subspace methods and DTW).
  - Change-point detection (detection delay/false alarm rates versus kernel CUSUM/MMD).
- Discuss kernel and truncation selection (median heuristic vs. cross-validation), sensitivity analyses, and practical guidance.
- Elaborate on scalability (Nyström or random features; streaming variants) and numerical conditioning.
- Clarify the data requirements (consecutive sequences versus paired snapshots), centering, and handling nonstationarity.
- Tighten Sections 3–4 by merging overlapping content; shorten Section 5 (related work) or move some to an appendix; expand Section 6–7 with quantitative results.
- Fix the toy example (if nonlinear, ensure the equation reflects that) and standardize notation.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually compelling kernelized framework for Koopman spectral analysis with clear links to DMD and RKHS.
- Theoretical statements clarify when kernel methods can recover Koopman spectra.
- Broad potential impact across modeling, prediction, and representation of nonlinear dynamics; code provided.

Arguments against acceptance
- Exposition is too cryptic and verbose for NIPS; key ideas are hard to extract.
- Empirical evaluation is limited, mostly qualitative, and lacks comparisons to strong baselines.
- Practical aspects (assumption checks, hyperparameters, scalability, stability) are underdeveloped.

Overall assessment
In its current form, I cannot recommend acceptance primarily due to clarity and insufficient empirical validation. I encourage the authors to substantially revise the presentation, strengthen the experiments with rigorous baselines and metrics, and clarify assumptions and numerical aspects. With these improvements, the work could be a valuable and highly cited contribution.
