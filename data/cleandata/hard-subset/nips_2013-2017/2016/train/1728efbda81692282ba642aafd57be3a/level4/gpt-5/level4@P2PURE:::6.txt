This paper derives a spectral analysis of the Koopman operator in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and proposes a data-driven algorithm to carry out this analysis for dynamical systems. The Koopman operator originates in fluid dynamics, and the authors suggest it has relevance for machine learning. They demonstrate the method on two simple systems—a damped 1D linear system and a chaotic map (the Hénon map)—and apply it to real-world locomotion data, comparing against standard dynamic mode decomposition (DMD), PCA, and kernel PCA, with results that are somewhat unclear.

The work presents an interesting extension of DMD, broadly in line with prior efforts to “kernelize” techniques formulated via linear operators (e.g., kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, GP bandits, etc.). The experiments are mainly on artificial problems. The paper is clearly written and has pedagogical value for readers unfamiliar with tools originating in fluid dynamics. I am generally supportive of efforts that introduce machine learning audiences to techniques from other fields.

Conceptually, this appears to be a decomposition technique for deterministic dynamical systems defined by functions f: M → M (without stochastic components). I am not aware of such setups being widely used in machine learning, where random effects (innovations and noise) typically play a role, though I may be missing relevant work. In its current form, the paper needs to do more to convince ML readers of the practical utility of these techniques. What are concrete use cases? Can the method compete, at least to some extent, with the state of the art in those areas? Robotics is one ML domain where learning and inference in dynamical systems are central and where substantial prior work exists—what would Koopman analysis offer practitioners in robotics? Alternatively, please motivate and evaluate in another suitable domain.

While the theoretical derivations are carefully executed, they are not particularly surprising to readers familiar with kernel PCA, kernel ICA, kernel independence tests, or “kernel Bayes rule”: once you have a linear operator and functions living in an RKHS, finite-dimensional eigendecompositions naturally arise.

Specific points:
- It is unclear whether the modal decomposition algorithm requires an explicit representation in feature space. If so, this would be a limitation relative to kernel PCA, and it should be stated explicitly.
- Please include results for standard DMD on the toy example and on the Hénon map to highlight the differences in these simple settings.
