- Paper proposes an online structured dictionary-learning algorithm for neuroimaging that extends [14] by adding a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of atoms.  
- Model definition and algorithms are clear and follow a well-established framework, but empirical benefits of the Sobolev prior are not clearly demonstrated.  
- Figure 2 and related results do not quantitatively show in what ways SSMF outperforms alternatives (e.g., smoother boundaries or detection of connected regions); need neuroscience-justified criteria and measurements.  
- After reading the rebuttal the reviewer raised the technical-quality rating from ~2 to 3 and requests that the new quantitative results and neuroscience interpretations be included in the revision.  
- Novelty: method is a notable original contribution by incorporating Sobolev priors into the [14] framework.  
- Potential impact: an efficient online Sobolev-prior dictionary learner could be a valuable tool for neuroscience/medical imaging and may extend to tasks like image segmentation or regional sensing.  
- Presentation is rough with confusing descriptions, typos, and unresolved co-author comments that hinder readability.  
- Specific technical/paper errors: ambiguity about transpose on V^j in the decomposition equation and missing HCP dataset reference.  
- Multiple grammatical errors noted (e.g., “can imposes,” “a single, a single,” “th how”) that should be fixed.  
- Mathematical inconsistencies: sign discrepancy between Equations (6) and (8), unclear meaning of V in Eq. (6), and request to provide the explicit algorithmic/mathematical solution to Eq. (6) (e.g., in appendix).  
- Figure problems: Figure 2 uses different slices making direct comparison difficult and does not substantiate “more structured” claims.  
- Figure 3 is unclear about the prediction task, whether regression is on U_i’s, missing legend explanation and PCA baseline, and the R^2>0.1 claim is not clearly supported by labeled curves.
