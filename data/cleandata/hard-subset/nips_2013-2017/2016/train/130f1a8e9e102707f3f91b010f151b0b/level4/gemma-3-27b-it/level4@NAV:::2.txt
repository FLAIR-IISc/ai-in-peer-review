This paper presents an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across subjects, building upon the established framework of [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior to promote spatial contiguity within the dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate their approach on empirical data, investigate the impact of parameter tuning, and compare its performance against two existing methods. 

Regarding technical quality, I initially assessed this work as marginally below a 3, but have revised my rating to a 3 following review of the rebuttal. The model formulation is clear, and the algorithm is sound, closely following the principles of [14]. However, the presentation somewhat obscures the empirical benefits of the Sobolev prior. Specifically, the results section lacks a clear demonstration of how the proposed method improves upon alternatives. In Figure 2, for instance, it’s unclear *how* the SSMF method’s results differ from those of the comparison methods. Quantifying the smoothness of spatial boundaries or identifying regions uniquely delineated by SSMF – regions known to be connected based on neuroscientific principles – would strengthen the analysis. Beyond simply stating that the SSMF results are “more structured,” the authors should articulate specific, neuroscience-informed criteria and provide quantitative measurements demonstrating improvement against those criteria. The rebuttal provides informative results, and I encourage the authors to incorporate these into their revision, along with a more detailed, quantitative analysis of the structure and neurobiological relevance of the learned atoms.

The novelty of this work lies in its extension of [14] through the application of Sobolev priors to encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms, representing a valuable contribution. The potential impact is also significant. Dictionary learning is a useful technique for analyzing neuroimaging data across large subject groups, and the theoretical benefits of incorporating spatial priors, such as the Sobolev prior, are compelling. This paper offers an efficient online algorithm for achieving this, potentially providing a useful new tool for the neuroscience and medical imaging communities. Exploring applications beyond the current scope, such as image segmentation and regional sensing, could further broaden its impact.

The paper is generally well-organized, but the manuscript would benefit from careful editing. Several instances of unclear phrasing and typographical errors impede comprehension. Specific points requiring attention include:
