This paper presents an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals, extending the established framework of Mairal et al. (2010) [14] with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply their method to empirical data, report the effect of varying tuning parameters, and compare its performance with two alternative methods commonly used in the field. 

**Summary and Related Work:** The core idea is to leverage cross-subject variability to define brain regions, moving beyond traditional ROI-based approaches. This builds on clustering methods [21, 14] but frames the problem as a linear decomposition with sparsity and smoothness constraints, akin to PCA or ICA [5] but with spatially regularized components. The authors propose a multivariate online dictionary learning method incorporating both an L1 penalty for sparsity and a Laplacian penalty for structural smoothness, aiming to delineate brain structures from functional images. This extends the online dictionary learning work of Mairal et al. (2010) [16] with a relatively small computational overhead. The paper correctly positions its contribution within the neuroimaging literature, acknowledging limitations of univariate models and the challenges of ROI definition. Related work, including structured sparsity models [1, 3] and multi-subject ICA [22], is appropriately cited.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear model definition and a well-established algorithmic foundation based on block coordinate descent [16]. The extension to incorporate the Laplacian penalty appears reasonable and logically presented. However, the initial assessment of empirical advantage felt somewhat weak. While the rebuttal provides significantly more compelling results (detailed below), the original submission lacked sufficient quantitative evidence demonstrating the benefits of the Sobolev prior. The experiments appear reasonably designed, utilizing a large, publicly available dataset (HCP) and comparing against relevant baselines. The discussion of convergence and hyperparameter tuning is also valuable. My initial rating was marginally below a 3, but has been revised upwards to a solid 3 based on the rebuttal.

**Clarity:** While the overall organization is acceptable, the paper suffered from several clarity issues. Numerous minor typos and awkward phrasing detracted from readability. Specifically: (1) The transpose symbol in the matrix decomposition equation (lines 67-68) should apply to each individual Vj. (2) The reference to the HCP dataset (line 73) was missing. (3) "The Graph-Net penalty can imposes…" (line 83) is grammatically incorrect. (4) A comment ("bold sentence") remained in the text (line 146). (5) There was an inconsistency in the signs in Equations (6) and (8). (6) Repeated words ("a single, a single" - line 157) and awkward phrasing ("We did an extensive study of th how" - line 169) were present. (7) Explicitly showing the mathematical/algorithmic solution to Equation (6) would enhance understanding. (8) Figure 2 presented results at different slices, hindering direct comparison. The claim of "more structured" results (line 222) lacked clear quantitative support. (9) Figure 3 lacked clear explanations of the predicted variables and the PCA baseline was missing. The description of R^2 values was also unclear. The authors have addressed many of these points in the rebuttal, and further refinement in the revision will significantly improve clarity.

**Originality:** The paper's key contribution lies in the novel extension of online dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior to enforce spatial structure. While the individual components (online dictionary learning, Laplacian regularization) are not entirely new, their combination addresses a specific challenge in neuroimaging – extracting interpretable, spatially coherent brain regions – and represents a valuable contribution.

**Significance:** Dictionary learning is a useful technique for analyzing neuroimaging data and uncovering underlying patterns. Introducing a spatial prior like the Laplacian penalty has the potential to improve the interpretability and accuracy of these decompositions. The online learning framework allows the method to scale to large datasets, making it practical for analyzing population-level neuroimaging studies. This work could provide a valuable tool for the neuroscience and medical imaging communities and open avenues for applications in image segmentation and regional sensing.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of established techniques (online dictionary learning and Laplacian regularization) addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging.
*   Scalable online learning framework allows application to large datasets.
*   Preliminary results demonstrate the potential for improved interpretability and capture of inter-subject variability.
*   The rebuttal provides compelling additional results which bolster the claims of the paper.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Initial presentation of empirical results lacked strong quantitative evidence supporting the benefits of the proposed method. (Addressed in the rebuttal).
*   The paper contained numerous clarity and presentation issues that hindered understanding. (Partially addressed in the rebuttal).
*   The connection to neuroscience literature could be strengthened with more explicit discussion of how the extracted structures relate to known brain organization.

**Rebuttal Summary:** The authors provided a detailed and informative rebuttal, addressing many of the concerns raised in the review. Crucially, they included additional results showing quantitative improvements in spatial coherence and reproducibility with the Sobolev prior. They also clarified several points regarding the experimental setup and interpretation of results. The additional figures and explanations significantly strengthen the paper's claims. 

**Overall Recommendation:** I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors have addressed the major concerns regarding the empirical validation of their method and clarity of presentation. They should focus on polishing the manuscript to eliminate the remaining typos and awkward phrasing, providing more explicit connections to neuroscience literature, and incorporating the new results from the rebuttal into the main paper.
