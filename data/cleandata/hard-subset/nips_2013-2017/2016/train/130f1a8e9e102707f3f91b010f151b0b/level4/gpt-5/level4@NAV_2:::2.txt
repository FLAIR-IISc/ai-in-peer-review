This paper presents an online structured dictionary-learning approach for modeling inter-individual variability in neuroimaging, augmenting a well-known framework [14] with a Sobolev/Laplacian prior to promote spatially contiguous, piecewise-smooth atoms. The method is evaluated on empirical fMRI data, explores sensitivity to hyperparameters, and is compared against two established baselines. The formulation is clearly specified, and the algorithm builds on a mature literature; however, the empirical section does not convincingly and quantitatively substantiate the claimed benefits of the Sobolev prior. In particular, Figure 2 does not make clear in what respects SSMF improves over the alternatives. Beyond stating that the outputs are “more structured,” it would help to define neuroscience-grounded criteria (e.g., spatial compactness, boundary smoothness, known network contiguity) and supply quantitative evidence for gains along those axes.

Technical quality: I would rate slightly below 3 if that were an option. The model is well defined and the algorithmic development—rooted in established methods [14]—appears technically sound. That said, the current presentation undercuts the empirical case for the Sobolev prior. For instance, in Figure 2, it is not apparent which measurable properties are improved by SSMF: Are boundaries quantitatively smoother? Does SSMF uniquely recover regions known to be spatially contiguous? Clear, quantitatively justified evaluation criteria tied to the neuroscience literature would strengthen the claims. Added after reading the rebuttal: The additional results in the rebuttal are very useful. I am raising my technical quality rating from 2 to 3. I encourage the authors to integrate these results in the revision and, moreover, to demonstrate (with quantitative metrics) that SSMF learns more structured atoms that are more neurobiologically meaningful, with appropriate citations.

Originality: The main contribution—extending [14] with a Sobolev prior to enforce spatial structure on dictionary atoms—is nontrivial and original.

Significance/usefulness: Dictionary learning is a valuable tool for modeling group neuroimaging data, and spatial priors such as Sobolev/Laplacian are, in principle, beneficial. This work offers an efficient online algorithm that could become a useful addition for the neuroscience/medical imaging community. Beyond the current application, the approach could be explored for tasks like image segmentation and regional sensing.
