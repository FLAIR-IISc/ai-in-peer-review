

This paper proposes a novel regularization term for matrix factorization that simultaneously enforces sparsity and smoothness constraints. The core contribution—introducing this new regularizer into the optimization framework—is theoretically sound and represents a significant advancement over existing approaches. The experimental results demonstrate consistent and substantial improvements over state-of-the-art methods across multiple benchmark datasets, providing strong empirical validation of the approach's effectiveness. The authors' experimental design is thorough, and the reported performance gains are compelling, highlighting the practical utility of their method.

However, the paper exhibits two critical shortcomings that warrant concern. First, the authors explicitly acknowledge the non-convex nature of the proposed optimization problem. Despite this, the paper fails to establish the existence of a solution (or solutions) to the regularized problem. This omission is significant because, without such a foundation, the algorithmic procedure described in Section 3 lacks a theoretical anchor. While the experiments confirm the algorithm's practical efficacy, the absence of a proof regarding solution existence leaves a fundamental gap in the paper's theoretical justification. The authors should address this by either providing a proof of existence (e.g., via compactness arguments or convergence guarantees for the iterative algorithm) or explicitly acknowledging this limitation as a direction for future work.

Second, the manuscript suffers from suboptimal clarity and presentation in several sections. For instance, the description of the regularization term on line 146 is ambiguous and could be misinterpreted; a more precise mathematical formulation or illustrative example would greatly enhance understanding. Additionally, the connection between the proposed regularizer and its impact on the factorization structure (beyond empirical results) is not sufficiently elaborated, making it difficult for readers to intuitively grasp the mechanism driving the performance gains. The writing in Sections 2 and 3 contains minor grammatical errors and awkward phrasing that distract from the technical content. While these issues do not invalidate the contribution, they hinder the paper's accessibility and impact.

Following the authors' rebuttal, I am satisfied with their explanation regarding the algorithm's convergence properties and the practical stability observed in experiments. Consequently, I am willing to upgrade my assessment of Clarity and Presentation from a 2 to a 3 (on a scale where 1=poor, 5=excellent), as the authors provided a convincing response to the specific concerns raised about the algorithm's implementation and robustness. The rebuttal also clarified the relationship between the proposed regularizer and existing sparsity/smoothness techniques, strengthening the paper's position.

In summary, this work presents a valuable and empirically compelling contribution to matrix factorization. The novel regularization term is well-motivated, and the experimental results are robust and convincing. The primary weaknesses—lack of theoretical grounding for solution existence and subpar exposition—could be addressed with relatively minor revisions. Given the strong empirical results and the authors' responsive rebuttal, I recommend acceptance. The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding a brief theoretical discussion on solution existence (even if only acknowledging it as an open problem) and refining the presentation of the regularization term and its implications. The contribution is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant publication in NIPS, provided these minor clarifications are incorporated.
