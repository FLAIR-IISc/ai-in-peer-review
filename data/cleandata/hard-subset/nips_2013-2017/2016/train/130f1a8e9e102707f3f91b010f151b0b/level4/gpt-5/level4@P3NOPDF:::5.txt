Summary
The paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging applications, augmenting the standard objective with a Sobolev regularization on the dictionary atoms to encourage structured (presumably spatially smooth) bases for brain-region discovery. The goal is to obtain interpretable, stable atoms while maintaining scalability.

Strengths
- Addressing large-scale neuroimaging with online learning is timely and relevant.
- Incorporating spatial structure into the dictionary is a reasonable idea for brain data, where smoothness and contiguity are often desirable.

Major concerns
1) Novelty and positioning
- The method appears to be a direct extension of Online Dictionary Learning (Mairal et al., JMLR 2010) with an added Sobolev penalty on the atoms. As presented, the conceptual and algorithmic novelty is limited. To meet the bar, the paper should clearly articulate what is fundamentally new (e.g., theory for online updates with Sobolev penalties, nontrivial proximal steps, guarantees, or demonstrable new empirical phenomena). A thorough comparison to related structured/graph/TV-regularized dictionary learning and convolutional dictionary learning is needed.

2) Definition and motivation for “structure”
- “Structure” is insufficiently defined. If the data live on a spatial grid (voxels), specify the domain, the order of the Sobolev norm (e.g., H1), anisotropy, and boundary conditions. Clarify how the Sobolev norm is discretized (finite differences, graph Laplacian, etc.).
- Explain why penalizing derivatives of the dictionary atoms is the right inductive bias for your tasks. How does this compare to total variation, Laplacian/graph regularization, or convolutional models? What neurobiological prior (e.g., spatial smoothness, contiguity, parcellation-like supports) motivates this choice? Please provide intuition and references.

3) Objective details and equations
- Eq. (1): I suspect a notational error; the stacking should be [V^1, V^2, …, V^r]^T.
- Eq. (2): You penalize the codes with an ℓ2 norm rather than the ℓ1 norm used in Mairal et al. Please justify this design choice. ℓ2 generally yields dense codes, which may harm interpretability and identifiability. If dense codes are intended (e.g., to reduce variance), discuss the consequences and provide ablations comparing ℓ1, elastic net, and ℓ2. If ℓ2 is chosen for algorithmic simplicity, address performance trade-offs.
- Clearly define all symbols, normalization constraints (e.g., unit-norm atoms), and regularization weights.

4) Optimization and theory
- Provide the derivation of the dictionary update with the Sobolev term. Is the update a proximal step with respect to the Sobolev norm? How is the proximal operator computed in practice on the voxel grid? How are boundary effects handled?
- Do the convergence results for online learning (e.g., to a stationary point) extend under the Sobolev-regularized objective? State assumptions and cite or adapt proofs where possible.
- Complexity and scalability: give per-iteration time, memory footprint, and wall-clock scaling with data size and dictionary size. “Large-scale” claims should be supported with empirical scaling plots.

5) Experiments and evaluation
- Baselines: Include strong structured baselines, such as graph/Laplacian-regularized dictionary learning, TV-regularized atoms, group-structured sparsity, and convolutional dictionary learning, not only the unregularized online method. Compare against non-DL baselines common in neuroimaging (e.g., ICA, NMF with spatial priors).
- Ablations: Vary the Sobolev order and regularization strength; compare to TV and Laplacian penalties; test ℓ1 vs ℓ2 for codes; and include “no Sobolev” as a baseline to isolate its effect.
- Metrics: Beyond reconstruction error, evaluate interpretability and stability relevant to brain regions: spatial smoothness/contiguity, overlap with known atlases, test–retest reliability, cross-subject reproducibility, and downstream task performance (e.g., classification/regression). Provide statistical significance.
- Datasets: Use multiple datasets and report preprocessing steps. Demonstrate generalization across cohorts and scanners.

6) Clarity and presentation
- Several passages are unclear and there are numerous typos. The presentation needs careful editing for grammar and precision.
- The reference to “line 146” is confusing; please clarify or correct the numbering.
- Ensure all equations are self-contained, with consistent notation and dimensions.

Minor comments
- Provide pseudocode for the online updates with the Sobolev-regularized dictionary step, including step sizes and stopping criteria.
- Discuss initialization strategies and sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- Relate the Sobolev penalty to common discretizations: for H1 on a grid, the penalty is equivalent to a quadratic form with a (block) Laplacian; make this explicit and connect to graph-regularized methods.
- Cite additional related work on spatial/structured dictionary learning and spatial priors in neuroimaging, and clarify how your approach differs.
- If possible, release code to support reproducibility.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important problem and the proposed inductive bias is plausible for neuroimaging. However, in its current form the contribution appears incremental relative to standard online dictionary learning, and the motivation, theoretical grounding, and empirical evaluation of the Sobolev regularization are insufficiently developed. I cannot recommend acceptance at this time. Strengthening the definition and motivation of “structure,” clarifying the objective and updates, providing theoretical support, and conducting rigorous comparative experiments with appropriate baselines and ablations would substantially improve the work.
