- Proposes an online dictionary-learning method for neuroimaging enforcing sparsity (L1-type) and spatial smoothness via a Laplacian penalty to produce piecewise-smooth, blob-like atoms.  
- Codes are computed by ridge regression and the dictionary is updated with block-coordinate descent, using FISTA for the Laplacian-regularized subproblem.  
- Extends Mairal et al. (2010) online dictionary learning to structured atoms with ~2–3x computational overhead and retains scalability to large n and p.  
- Experiments on HCP fMRI (n=500) show more interpretable, stable components than SODL and ICA baselines, with competitive explained variance and behavioral prediction.  
- Builds on online sparse dictionary learning and connects to structured sparsity/GraphNet; contrasts with TV-L1 and group ICA approaches.  
- Clear motivation: learning data-driven, spatially structured ROIs capturing inter-subject variability.  
- Simple, elegant formulation combining L1 constraint with Laplacian penalty and a practical BCD+FISTA algorithm.  
- Scalable implementation with minibatches and modest overhead; running time competitive with CanICA.  
- Empirical strengths: cleaner, more interpretable, more stable components and favorable early learning curves.  
- Implementation planned in Nilearn, boosting reproducibility and impact.  
- Main weakness: non-convex problem with no formal existence/uniqueness or statistical recovery guarantees beyond local convergence arguments.  
- Hyperparameter selection (γ for Laplacian, τ for sparsity, α for codes) is critical and guidance is largely heuristic.  
- Practical pitfalls for large γ are noted, but concrete tuning rules are only sketched.  
- Writing and presentation need polishing (typos, grammar, notation), which sometimes hinders readability.  
- Quality assessment: sound algorithmic contribution and thorough empirical evaluation, but limited theoretical analysis and sensitivity quantification.  
- Clarity assessment: understandable overall but needs clearer derivations and explicit guidance on hyperparameters and per-iteration complexity.  
- Originality: non-trivial, practical extension of online DL to spatially regularized atoms and novel scalable realization relative to prior structured decompositions.  
- Significance: addresses an important neuroimaging need and is likely to be adopted; relevance extends to structured matrix factorization communities.  
- Pros for acceptance: practical, scalable extension with clear empirical benefits, improved interpretability and stability, and high relevance.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited theoretical guarantees, heuristic hyperparameter tuning, and presentation issues.  
- Suggested improvements: provide existence/minimizer arguments, clearer convergence guarantees, prescriptive hyperparameter guidelines and sensitivity plots, ablation studies (FISTA steps, γ/τ effects), and better proofreading.  
- After rebuttal: reviewer satisfied with convergence clarifications and raised Clarity/Presentation score from 2 to 3.
