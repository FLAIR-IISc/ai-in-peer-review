- Proposes Smooth-SODL: online dictionary learning with a Laplacian spatial-smoothness prior on atoms, ℓ1/simplex sparsity, ridge-coded samples, and FISTA-based atom updates using online sufficient statistics.

- Novelty is pragmatic: not a new penalty but an efficient, scalable way to incorporate spatial smoothness into online dictionary learning.

- Empirical evaluation on HCP task fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6×10^5, k=40) shows visually more compact and stable atoms, modest EV gains over SODL, inferior EV to CanICA, better early-sample learning, and competitive but mixed behavioral-prediction performance.

- Strength: simple, scalable integration with small runtime overhead and sensible design choices (non-negativity/simplex option, ridge codes, adaptive αt).

- Strength: uses a large public dataset and presents learning curves, split-half stability, and behavioral benchmarks.

- Weakness: validation relies heavily on qualitative visualizations and lacks spatially explicit quantitative interpretability/reproducibility metrics (e.g., connected components, spatial entropy, Dice/Jaccard, NPAIRS/ICC).

- Weakness: possible unfair preprocessing comparisons (CanICA pre-smoothed while SODL variants are not); needs controlled experiments to isolate Laplacian effects.

- Weakness: limited baselines—missing related structured methods (TV-ℓ1/GraphNet dictionary learning, spatially-regularized NMF).

- Weakness: insufficient ablations—unclear effects of Laplacian (γ=0 vs >0), sparsity choices (ℓ1/simplex/no sparsity), code penalties (ridge vs ℓ1), non-negativity, and k.

- Weakness: inter-subject variability claims are under-supported; little analysis showing population-level generalization or alignment with known atlases.

- Weakness: downstream prediction is mixed and often worse than CanICA; the interpretability vs predictive-accuracy trade-off needs discussion and evidence.

- Weakness: limited hyperparameter guidance and CV details; γ/τ selection, ranges, sensitivity analyses, and cross-validation protocols are thin.

- Quality: optimization approach is sound and leverages established techniques; convergence behavior follows prior assumptions.

- Quality caveat: need clearer description of γ schedule and its interaction with At[j,j]/t to ensure stable FISTA step sizes and report sensitivity ranges.

- Clarity: method largely clear but would benefit from a schematic of the online loop, clearer preprocessing parity statements, precise hyperparameter-selection details, consistent terminology, and minor typo fixes.

- Originality: moderate—spatial Laplacian and sparse atoms are established; contribution is in efficient online implementation.

- Significance: potentially useful for scalable, interpretable decompositions in large neuroimaging datasets but impact limited until stronger quantitative validation and broader comparisons are provided.

- Suggested improvements: add quantitative reproducibility metrics, ensure preprocessing parity, include structured baselines, perform systematic ablations, report hyperparameter selection and sensitivity, detail FISTA/γ settings and iterations, and expand inter-subject analyses.

- Arguments for acceptance: simple, scalable extension of a widely used framework with modest computational overhead and promising qualitative/stability gains, especially in low-data regimes.

- Arguments against acceptance: empirical validation lacks quantitative spatial metrics and broader baselines, comparisons may be confounded by preprocessing, prediction results are mixed, and ablations/hyperparameter guidance are limited.
