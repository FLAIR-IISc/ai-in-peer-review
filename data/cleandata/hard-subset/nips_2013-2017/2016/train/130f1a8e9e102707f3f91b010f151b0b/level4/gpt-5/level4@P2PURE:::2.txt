This paper presents an online algorithm for learning a structured dictionary to model neuroimaging data across individuals, extending the established framework in [14] with a Sobolev prior to promote spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply the method to empirical data, study the effects of tuning parameters, and compare performance with two alternatives.

Technical quality: I would rate this marginally below 3 if that option were available. The model is clearly defined, and the algorithms build on a well-established framework [14] and appear technically sound. However, possibly due to presentation issues, the results do not clearly demonstrate the empirical benefit of introducing the Sobolev prior. In particular, in Figure 2 it is unclear in what respect SSMF outperforms the two alternatives. For example: Is the spatial boundary quantitatively smoother? Are there known connected regions that are identified only by SSMF? Beyond the vague statement that SSMF yields “more structured” results, the authors should articulate explicit criteria grounded in the neuroscience literature and provide quantitative measurements demonstrating improvements under those criteria. Added after reading the rebuttal: The additional results in the rebuttal are very informative. I am raising my technical quality rating from 2 to 3. I encourage the authors to include these new results in the revision and, in addition, to demonstrate quantitatively that the atoms learned by SSMF are more structured and more meaningful, with references to the neuroscience literature.

Novelty/originality: The paper makes a notable original contribution by extending [14] with Sobolev priors to encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms.

Potential impact or usefulness: Dictionary learning is a useful approach for modeling neuroimaging data across large subject cohorts, and incorporating Sobolev or similar spatial priors should, in theory, improve results. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm to do so and could contribute a valuable tool to the neuroscience/medical imaging community. The method could also be explored for applications such as image segmentation and regional sensing.
