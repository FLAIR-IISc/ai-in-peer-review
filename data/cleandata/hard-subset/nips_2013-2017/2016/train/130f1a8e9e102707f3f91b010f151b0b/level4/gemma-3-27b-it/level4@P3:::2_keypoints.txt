- Presents an online dictionary learning algorithm with an L1 sparsity term and a Sobolev/Laplacian prior to enforce spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms.  
- Extends Mairal et al. (2010) online dictionary learning to multivariate neuroimaging data with modest computational overhead.  
- Applies the method to a large public dataset (HCP), varies tuning parameters, and compares against two common baselines.  
- Positions contribution within neuroimaging literature, contrasting cluster/ROI approaches and linking to PCA/ICA and structured sparsity work.  
- Technically sound with a clear model and algorithmic foundation (block coordinate descent); extension to include Laplacian penalty is reasonable.  
- Original submission lacked strong quantitative evidence for empirical benefits of the Sobolev prior, but rebuttal added compelling quantitative results.  
- Clarity issues: multiple typos, awkward phrasing, missing dataset citation, grammatical errors, erroneous comment left in text, sign inconsistencies in equations.  
- Specific technical clarity requests: transpose notation fix, explicit solution to Equation (6), clearer figure comparisons (Figure 2 slices) and more explanation for Figure 3 (predicted variables, PCA baseline, R^2).  
- Originality: novel and useful combination of online dictionary learning with spatial regularization tailored to neuroimaging.  
- Significance: scalable to large datasets, improves interpretability and spatial coherence, with potential impact for neuroscience and medical imaging.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel combination, scalability, preliminary improved interpretability, rebuttal strengthens empirical claims.  
- Arguments against acceptance: initially weak empirical presentation, remaining clarity/presentation issues, need for stronger neuroscience contextualization.  
- Rebuttal effectively addressed many concerns, providing additional quantitative improvements in spatial coherence and reproducibility.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions—fix typos/phrasing, incorporate rebuttal results into the main paper, and strengthen neuroscience connections.
