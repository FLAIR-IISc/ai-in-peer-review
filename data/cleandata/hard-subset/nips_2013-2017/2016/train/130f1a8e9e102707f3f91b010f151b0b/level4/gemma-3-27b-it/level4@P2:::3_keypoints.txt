- Proposes multivariate online dictionary learning with sparsity plus a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty to produce smooth, compact dictionary atoms for brain images.  
- Motivation: capture inter-subject variability in neuroimaging that standard analyses often discard.  
- Builds on Mairal et al. (2010) online dictionary learning and prior neuroimaging DL work, extending online framework with spatial regularization.  
- Key novelty is incorporating a Laplacian penalty into an online, scalable algorithm suitable for large datasets (e.g., HCP).  
- Use of spatial priors to improve interpretability is not novel, but scalability to big neuroimaging cohorts is an important contribution.  
- Algorithm is clearly formulated, implementation details provided, and computational cost/parameter selection are discussed.  
- Experiments use a large, public HCP dataset, which strengthens practical relevance.  
- Main results rely heavily on subjective visual inspection; interpretability claims lack rigorous quantitative support.  
- Quantitative improvements (explained variance and prediction) are often marginal and inconsistent across metrics and behavioral variables.  
- Comparison to CanICA is weakened by post-processing (thresholding) applied to CanICA, making fairness of comparison questionable.  
- Discussion of how data size affects the usefulness of spatial regularization is incomplete and lacks exploration of small/noisy dataset scenarios.  
- Paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study systematically varying the Laplacian penalty and other parameters.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a relevant problem, provides a scalable structured prior extension, and shows modest empirical gains.  
- Arguments against acceptance: subjective primary evidence, marginal quantitative benefits, unclear impact on large datasets, and insufficient parameter exploration.  
- Overall recommendation: technically sound but empirical evidence is not strong enough—recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and strengthen quantitative evaluations and ablation studies.
