

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for extracting structured and sparse brain regions from functional neuroimaging data. The authors address a significant challenge in neuroimaging: defining spatially meaningful regions of interest (ROIs) without relying on manual segmentation or cross-modality correspondences. The key innovation is combining L1-norm sparsity with a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure on dictionary atoms, resulting in blob-like patterns that align with known brain organization. This work extends Mairal et al.'s online dictionary learning framework (2010) by incorporating spatial regularization while maintaining computational efficiency, with only a 2-3x increase in runtime compared to standard online methods.

The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on Human Connectome Project (HCP) task fMRI data, showing that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable dictionaries than both standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and CanICA, while better capturing inter-subject variability. The authors provide thorough experimental validation across multiple metrics: visual interpretability, explained variance, stability across data splits, and prediction of behavioral variables. Notably, the method shows particular advantage in small-data regimes where spatial regularization provides significant gains over unstructured approaches.

In terms of quality, the technical framework is sound and well-motivated. The algorithmic adaptation of Laplacian regularization into the online dictionary learning framework is carefully executed, and the convergence guarantees are properly established. However, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of the algorithm's convergence properties or optimality guarantees under the proposed regularization. The authors do not provide any formal bounds on the reconstruction error or analysis of how the Laplacian penalty affects the learning dynamics, which would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

The clarity of the paper is somewhat hampered by inconsistent notation (e.g., switching between V and v) and some technical details that would benefit from better organization. For instance, the definition of the Laplacian penalty (equation 4) could be more clearly introduced, and the relationship between the Laplacian regularization and the resulting spatial structure could be explained more thoroughly. The presence of an unremoved reviewer comment ("146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v.") in the manuscript is concerning and suggests insufficient proofreading.

Regarding originality, the paper builds on existing structured dictionary learning approaches (e.g., [1, 25]) but makes a meaningful contribution by adapting these methods to the large-scale online setting appropriate for modern neuroimaging. The specific application to brain imaging with spatially structured components represents a valuable extension of the online dictionary learning framework, though the core algorithmic innovations are incremental. The authors appropriately cite related work in structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging, though they could better distinguish their contribution from Varoquaux's work on multi-subject dictionary learning [25].

The significance of this work is strong, as it addresses a critical bottleneck in neuroimaging analysis: the lack of principled methods for defining spatially meaningful ROIs. The ability to automatically extract structured brain regions that capture inter-subject variability has clear implications for both research and clinical applications. The demonstration that Smooth-SODL performs particularly well in small-data settings (where traditional methods struggle) adds substantial practical value.

Strengths:
1. Addresses a significant problem in neuroimaging with a well-motivated solution
2. Provides thorough experimental validation across multiple relevant metrics
3. Maintains computational efficiency while adding spatial structure
4. Demonstrates clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods in small-data regimes
5. Makes the implementation available through the Nilearn library

Weaknesses:
1. Lacks theoretical analysis of the algorithm's convergence or optimality
2. Contains inconsistent notation and unproofread content (including a reviewer comment)
3. The relationship between the Laplacian penalty and resulting spatial structure could be explained more clearly
4. The comparison to related work in structured dictionary learning could be more precise
5. The discussion of hyperparameter selection, while thorough, could better explain why certain parameter ranges work best

The paper's contribution is valuable for the neuroimaging community, though it represents an incremental advancement rather than a paradigm shift. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the application is highly relevant. The lack of theoretical analysis is a notable weakness, but this is common in applied machine learning work. The clarity issues and unproofread content are concerning but could be addressed in a camera-ready version.

Pro arguments for acceptance: Strong application to a significant problem in neuroimaging, thorough experimental validation, practical scalability to large datasets, clear advantages over existing methods in small-data settings, and the potential to become a standard tool for brain region extraction.

Con arguments for acceptance: Theoretical contribution is incremental, clarity issues need to be resolved, and the paper could better distinguish itself from related work on structured dictionary learning.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field that addresses a real problem with a practical solution. While not groundbreaking, it represents a valuable methodological advance that should be of interest to the NIPS community, particularly those working at the intersection of machine learning and neuroimaging. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity issues and provide more precise theoretical context.
