This paper proposes an online algorithm for learning a structured dictionary to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals. It extends a well-established framework [14] with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply the method to empirical data, explore the effect of tuning parameters, and compare against two alternative methods.

Technical quality: I would rate this marginally below 3 if given the option. The model definition is clear, and the algorithms follow a solid, well-established framework [14]. However, the empirical section does not clearly demonstrate the advantage of introducing the Sobolev prior. In particular, Figure 2 does not make it clear in what ways SSMF outperforms the alternatives. For example: Are spatial boundaries quantitatively smoother? Are there regions known to be connected that are only identified by SSMF? Beyond the qualitative claim that SSMF yields “more structured” results, the paper should define explicit criteria grounded in the neuroscience literature and report quantitative improvements according to those criteria.

Added after reading the rebuttal: The additional results in the rebuttal are very informative. I have revised my technical quality rating from 2 to 3. I encourage the authors to include these new results in the revision and, in addition, to demonstrate quantitatively that SSMF’s atoms are more structured and more meaningful, with explicit references to neuroscientific findings.

Novelty/originality: The extension of [14] with Sobolev priors to encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms is a notable and original contribution.

Potential impact/usefulness: Dictionary learning is a useful approach for modeling neuroimaging data across large cohorts, and incorporating Sobolev or related spatial priors should, in principle, improve results. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm that could be a valuable tool for the neuroscience/medical imaging community. The authors might also explore other applications, such as image segmentation and regional sensing.

Presentation: The overall organization is reasonable, but the manuscript appears insufficiently edited; several confusing descriptions and typos hinder comprehension. Specific issues include:
- Between Lines 67–68 (matrix decomposition): Should the transpose applied to the group of V^j (j = 1, …, r) be applied to each individual V^j instead?
- Line 73: The reference for the HCP dataset is missing.
- Line 83: “The Graph-Net penalty can imposes …” (grammar).
- Line 146: A bolded sentence appears to be an unresolved co-author comment.
- Equations (6) and (8): The last lines disagree in sign. You write “V^j + At[j,j]^{-1}(V At^j − Bt^j)” versus “At[j,j] V^j − (V At^j − Bt^j).” Also, in the last line of Equation (6), I assume V denotes the dictionary from the previous iteration (i.e., the jth column is not the variable v being optimized).
- Line 157: “a single, a single” (duplicated phrase).
- Line 169: “We did an extensive study of th how” (typo/grammar).
- Section 3.3 (paragraph near Line 147, just below Algorithm 2): Please provide the explicit mathematical/algorithmic solution to Equation (6), at least in an appendix.
- Figure 2: Results for different methods are shown on different horizontal and sagittal slices, which prevents direct visual comparison. In light of this, the statement in Line 222 that SSMF results are more “structured” is not convincing from the figure as presented.
- Figure 3: It is unclear what is being predicted. Is this a regression of brain data onto behavioral metrics? If so, is it based on the U_i’s? The legend is not explained in the text or caption. The figure also does not seem to include the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption. The text in Lines 224–227 referring to R^2 > 0.1 is hard to follow—please indicate explicitly which curves demonstrate this.

Overall: The paper presents a promising and original extension of a well-known dictionary learning framework with a Sobolev prior and an efficient online algorithm. The methods appear technically sound, but the empirical evidence for the added value of the Sobolev prior is not yet compelling in the current draft. The rebuttal addressed some of these concerns with helpful results; incorporating them and adding clear, quantitative measures of spatial structure and neuroscientific relevance would substantially strengthen the paper. Additionally, careful editing to resolve the presentation issues listed above is needed.
