

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, a novel extension of online dictionary learning that incorporates spatial smoothness through a Laplacian penalty, specifically designed for brain imaging applications. The authors address the problem of learning structured, sparse dictionary atoms that can better capture inter-subject variability in functional brain imaging data compared to existing approaches. Their method builds upon Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework while introducing a spatially structured constraint that better aligns with known functional brain organization.

The paper makes a strong contribution in terms of originality by introducing a Laplacian-based regularization that effectively imposes spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms without significantly increasing computational complexity. This represents a meaningful extension of the online dictionary learning framework to handle structured, spatially meaningful decompositions in neuroimaging. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms both traditional sparse dictionary learning (SODL) and canonical ICA (CanICA) methods across multiple evaluation metrics, including visual interpretability, explained variance, and prediction of behavioral variables.

Regarding quality, the paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation on a large-scale fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project. The results consistently show that Smooth-SODL produces more structured, interpretable brain maps that better capture inter-subject variability across small, medium, and large data regimes. The computational efficiency claim (3x slower than SODL, while being 1.6x faster than CanICA) is well-supported by the timing results. The authors also conduct a careful study of hyperparameter selection, which is particularly valuable for practitioners.

The technical presentation is generally clear, though some sections could benefit from more precise mathematical notation. The algorithmic description is sound, and the authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The convergence guarantees are appropriately established by referencing Mairal et al. (2010) and Bertsekas (1999). The experimental setup is well-justified, and the comparison against relevant baselines (SODL and CanICA) is comprehensive.

The significance of this work is substantial for neuroimaging analysis. By enabling the extraction of spatially structured, interpretable brain regions directly from functional data without requiring prior anatomical knowledge or segmentation, the authors address a fundamental challenge in the field. The potential impact extends beyond neuroscience, as the approach could be applied to other spatially structured data where structured sparse representations are beneficial.

The main concerns I have are related to the theoretical guarantees. While the paper presents a well-motivated algorithm and strong empirical results, it does not establish the existence of an optimal solution to the non-convex optimization problem (equation 2). The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not provide theoretical justification for why the algorithm converges to a meaningful solution. The experimental validation is strong, but the lack of theoretical guarantees for the non-convex problem is a notable weakness.

I also note that the writing would benefit from some refinement for clarity. For instance, the explanation of the Laplacian penalty (equation 4) could be more precise, and some technical terms could be better defined for readers unfamiliar with neuroimaging. However, these are minor issues that could be addressed in revision.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical validation across multiple metrics
- Practical computational efficiency (only 3x slower than SODL)
- Clear demonstration of the value of spatial regularization
- Careful hyperparameter analysis
- Good alignment with the problem domain (neuroimaging)

Weaknesses:
- Lack of theoretical guarantees for the non-convex optimization problem
- Limited discussion of the limitations of the approach
- Some technical explanations could be clearer

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neuroimaging analysis by providing a method that naturally extracts spatially structured brain regions from functional data. The proposed Smooth-SODL framework successfully balances interpretability with statistical performance, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches. While the theoretical foundation could be strengthened, the empirical evidence is compelling and the method is well-suited for large-scale neuroimaging applications.

I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS conference, as it addresses an important problem in a novel and effective way. The combination of technical innovation and strong empirical results makes it particularly suitable for the conference. After the authors' rebuttal clarified the algorithmic details, I am satisfied with the theoretical underpinnings of the approach and have raised my clarity rating from 2 to 3.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of neuroimaging and dictionary learning. I recommend acceptance.
