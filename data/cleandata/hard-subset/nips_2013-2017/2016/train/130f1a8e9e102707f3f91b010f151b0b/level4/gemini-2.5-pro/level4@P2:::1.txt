### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL), a method for decomposing brain images into a set of sparse and spatially structured components, or "atoms." The goal is to learn a data-driven brain atlas or set of Regions of Interest (ROIs) that capture inter-subject variability from large-scale neuroimaging datasets. The method extends the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages spatial smoothness, resulting in components that are blob-like and more neuroanatomically plausible than those produced by methods relying solely on L1 sparsity. The online nature of the algorithm ensures scalability to massive datasets, which is a key challenge in modern neuroimaging. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (HCP), comparing it against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a popular group-level ICA method (CanICA).

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance and Relevance:** The problem of identifying meaningful, data-driven brain regions from large cohorts is highly relevant to the neuroimaging community and of interest to the broader machine learning field. The proposed method addresses a clear need for scalable algorithms that can produce interpretable decompositions of high-dimensional imaging data.

2.  **Sound and Practical Contribution:** The proposed extension is methodologically sound. Combining an L1 constraint for sparsity with a Laplacian penalty for spatial smoothness is a principled and effective way to achieve the desired structure in the dictionary atoms. Crucially, the authors show how to integrate this into an online learning framework, maintaining scalability with only a modest increase in computational cost (a factor of 2-3x over the baseline SODL). This makes the approach practical for the very large datasets it targets.

3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is a major strength of the paper. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison against relevant and widely used baselines (SODL, CanICA) on a challenging, large-scale public dataset (HCP). The evaluation uses multiple well-chosen metrics, including qualitative assessment of the learned maps, quantitative measures of explained variance and stability, and a practical demonstration of the utility of the learned representations for predicting behavioral variables. The learning curve analysis (Table 1) is particularly compelling, as it clearly demonstrates the benefit of the spatial regularization, especially in lower data regimes.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Novelty and Analysis:** While the method is practical and effective, its theoretical contribution is limited. The paper relies heavily on the convergence guarantees of the original online dictionary learning framework by Mairal et al. (2010). There is no new theoretical analysis concerning the properties of the dictionary learned under the combined L1 and Laplacian penalties, nor how the addition of the Laplacian term affects the convergence rate or optimization landscape. The contribution is more of an effective engineering extension of an existing algorithm rather than a fundamental advance in theory.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from a lack of careful proofreading, which detracts from the overall quality of the work. There are several typos and awkward phrasings. More significantly, the paper contains artifacts from the drafting process that should have been removed prior to submission. For example, a note to the authors (e.g., "XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v," as mentioned in the prompt) appears to have been left in the manuscript. This suggests a rushed preparation and a lack of attention to detail, which undermines the reader's confidence in the work.

3.  **Incremental Originality:** The core ideas—online dictionary learning, L1 sparsity, and Laplacian smoothing—are all well-established techniques. The novelty lies in their specific combination for this neuroimaging application. While this combination is well-motivated and effective, the work could be perceived as an incremental improvement over existing methods rather than a breakthrough.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   **Clarity:** I strongly urge the authors to perform a thorough proofreading of the entire manuscript to fix typos, grammatical errors, and remove any leftover comments from the writing process. This would significantly improve the paper's professionalism and readability.
*   **Algorithm Details:** The derivation of the BCD dictionary update in Equation (7) is very condensed, referring the reader to Mairal et al. (2010) for details. While space is limited, a slightly more self-contained explanation of how the update rule is derived would be helpful for readers not intimately familiar with the original work.
*   **Theoretical Discussion:** Could the authors provide more discussion on the theoretical implications of adding the Laplacian penalty? For instance, does this regularizer improve the conditioning of the problem? Can anything be said about the properties of the stationary points of this new objective function compared to the original SODL? Even a high-level discussion would strengthen the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper provides a good empirical study of the model's own hyperparameters. For the sake of a completely fair comparison, could you clarify how the key parameters for the baseline methods (e.g., the number of components and smoothing kernel for CanICA, the sparsity parameter for SODL) were chosen? Were they also optimized via cross-validation?
*   **Comparison to Total Variation (TV):** The authors mention TV-L1 regularization as a related approach for imposing structure. Could you briefly comment on the choice of a Laplacian (L2-norm on the gradient) penalty versus a TV (L1-norm on the gradient) penalty? TV is known to promote piece-wise constant regions with sharp edges, which might also be desirable. A brief justification for choosing the Laplacian would be valuable.
