The authors extend dictionary learning models to incorporate a Sobolev prior, aiming to improve the delineation of brain regions from imaging data. This is a relevant problem, and the combination of sparsity and smoothness constraints is a sensible approach. However, assessing the impact and improvement of the proposed algorithm is challenging. The primary results are presented visually, comparing the clarity and consistency of identified brain regions. While the visual comparison is helpful, it may not be readily interpretable for readers unfamiliar with neuroimaging data. 

A crucial strength of the proposed method is its scalability, building upon the online dictionary learning work of Mairal et al. (2010). The authors correctly point out that incorporating spatial regularization is not necessarily advantageous when large datasets, such as those from the Human Connectome Project, are available. Nonetheless, the experiments demonstrate a notable initial advantage in explained variance when data is limited, highlighting a potential benefit in scenarios with fewer subjects.

The paper’s connection to prior work is well-established, referencing relevant publications in both machine learning and neuroimaging. The authors position their contribution effectively within the context of existing methods, such as ICA and PCA, and accurately identify the limitations of those approaches. They acknowledge the challenges in hyperparameter tuning, providing practical guidelines based on their experiments.

However, the validation presented feels somewhat subjective. While the authors compare their results to CanICA and standard SODL, the metrics used (explained variance and prediction of behavioral variables) could be strengthened. A more rigorous validation scheme involving quantitative measures of inter-subject consistency, or comparison to established brain atlases, would bolster the claims. The authors mention utilizing cross-validation for parameter selection, but more details on the specific folds and evaluation metrics would be valuable. It would also be beneficial to explore the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the Laplacian penalty parameter.

Furthermore, the discussion of computational cost, while informative, could be more precise. Stating that the method is “slightly less than 3 times slower” than SODL lacks detail. Providing wall-clock times for different dataset sizes or dictionary complexities would offer a better understanding of the algorithm’s practical limitations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The method extends existing online dictionary learning techniques with a principled approach to incorporating spatial structure.
*   The proposed algorithm scales well to large datasets.
*   The experiments demonstrate improved performance in low-data regimes compared to standard dictionary learning.
*   The paper is well-written and thoroughly references relevant prior work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The validation is somewhat subjective and lacks rigorous quantitative measures.
*   The benefits of the method in the context of large, readily available datasets like the HCP are not fully demonstrated.
*   The discussion of computational cost could be more detailed and precise.



**Overall:** This paper presents a reasonable extension to existing dictionary learning techniques with a focus on neuroimaging applications. While the validation could be strengthened, the work is technically sound and the proposed method has the potential to be useful for analyzing brain imaging data, particularly when the number of subjects is limited. I would lean towards acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
