This paper presents an online dictionary learning algorithm, named Smooth-SODL, designed to decompose large-scale brain imaging data into a set of sparse and spatially structured components. The core idea is to extend the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness, in addition to a standard l1-norm constraint for sparsity. The motivation is to generate components, or "atoms," that are not only sparse but also form compact, blob-like regions, which are more neuroscientifically interpretable as functional brain areas. The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to massive datasets, such as the Human Connectome Project (HCP), which is a key challenge in modern neuroimaging. The authors demonstrate their method on task-fMRI data, comparing it against sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA (CanICA) to show improvements in interpretability, stability, and predictive power for behavioral variables.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of defining meaningful brain regions of interest (ROIs) from population data in a principled, data-driven manner. The development of scalable methods for this task is highly relevant to the neuroimaging community.
*   **Originality:** The primary contribution is the successful integration of a spatial smoothness prior (a Laplacian penalty) into a scalable, online dictionary learning framework. While building upon existing work, this is a non-trivial extension that bridges the gap between computationally efficient online methods and slower batch methods that incorporate structured penalties.
*   **Quality:** The empirical evaluation is sound. The authors compare their method against relevant baselines on a large, modern dataset (HCP). The experiments rightly highlight the trade-offs between explained variance, component interpretability, and stability, showing that the proposed method finds a favorable balance. The learning curve analysis effectively demonstrates the value of the spatial regularizer, especially in low-data regimes.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript requires significant proofreading and polish. There are several typographical errors and awkward phrasings that detract from the overall quality and make the paper feel rushed. For a submission to a top-tier conference, a higher standard of presentation is expected.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents the algorithm and its empirical results but lacks a deeper theoretical analysis. While convergence is justified by citing the work of Mairal et al. (2010), the contribution would be stronger with a more self-contained discussion of the algorithm's properties, particularly how the introduction of the Laplacian penalty affects the optimization landscape or convergence rates.
*   **Novelty:** The approach is an incremental, albeit useful, extension of a well-established algorithm. The core ideas of using sparsity and spatial smoothness priors for brain map decomposition are not new; the main novelty lies in making the combination work within an efficient online optimization scheme.

In summary, the paper introduces a well-motivated and potentially impactful method for a significant problem in neuroimaging. The core idea is sound, and the experiments demonstrate its utility. However, the paper is let down by a lack of careful presentation and a limited theoretical contribution. The technical idea is interesting, but the execution of the manuscript is not yet at the level required for publication.
