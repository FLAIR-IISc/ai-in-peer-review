

This paper proposes an online algorithm for structured dictionary learning in neuroimaging, extending the framework of [14] with a Sobolev prior to enforce spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply the method to empirical data, analyze parameter sensitivity, and compare against two baselines. The core contribution—integrating Sobolev priors into dictionary learning for spatially structured neuroimaging representations—is novel and addresses a meaningful gap in the field.  

**Technical Quality (3/5):**  
The model formulation is clear, and the algorithm builds solidly on established work [14]. However, the initial submission failed to convincingly demonstrate the *empirical advantage* of the Sobolev prior. Figure 2, which compares methods, lacks quantitative justification for claims that SSMF yields "more structured" atoms. For instance:  
- Were spatial boundaries smoother (e.g., via gradient magnitude metrics)?  
- Did SSMF uniquely identify neuroanatomically connected regions absent in baselines?  
- What neuroscience-motivated metrics (e.g., spatial coherence in cortical layers) quantify the improvement?  
*Added after rebuttal:* The rebuttal’s additional results (e.g., quantitative spatial smoothness metrics) significantly strengthen the case. I revise the technical quality rating from 2 to 3. The authors must integrate these results in revision, explicitly linking atom structure to neuroscience criteria (e.g., cortical folding patterns) and providing quantitative validation.  

**Novelty/Originality (4/5):**  
The extension of [14] with Sobolev priors for spatially structured atoms is a notable contribution. This approach directly addresses a key limitation in existing neuroimaging dictionary learning (e.g., fragmented atom representations), offering a theoretically grounded solution.  

**Potential Impact (4/5):**  
The method provides a valuable tool for large-scale neuroimaging analysis, with clear utility for group-level data interpretation. The online algorithm enhances scalability for longitudinal or multi-subject studies. The approach could further benefit image segmentation and regional sensing tasks, though the paper does not explore these.  

**Presentation (2/5):**  
The paper’s organization is functional but suffers from poor editing, hindering comprehension. Critical issues include:  
1. **Mathematical inconsistencies:**  
   - Equation (6): The final line’s sign mismatch ($V^j + A_t[j,j]^{-1}(V A_t^j - B_t^j)$ vs. $A_t[j,j] V^j - (V A_t^j - B_t^j)$).  
   - Equation (8): Ambiguous $V$ notation (likely refers to the *final* dictionary, not the $j$-th column).  
   - Line 83: "can imposes" → "can impose."  
2. **Figure and caption flaws:**  
   - Figure 2: Results shown at inconsistent slices (e.g., different axial planes), preventing direct comparison. The claim of "more structured" atoms lacks visual or quantitative support.  
   - Figure 3: Unclear what is predicted (e.g., behavioral metrics from $U_i$?), and the PCA baseline is absent. Legends and axes are unexplained.  
3. **Minor errors:**  
   - Missing HCP dataset citation (Line 73).  
   - Typo: "a single, a single" (Line 157).  
   - Grammar: "We did an extensive study of th how" (Line 169).  
   - Line 146: Unremoved bolded text (likely a co-author comment).  
4. **Clarity gaps:**  
   - Matrix decomposition (Lines 67–68): Transpose on $V^j$ (for $j=1,\dots,r$) should apply to each $V^j$, not the group.  
   - Section 3.3: Equation (6) requires explicit solution details (e.g., in an appendix).  

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept with major revision.** The method’s novelty and potential impact are strong, but the current submission fails to validate its core innovation. The rebuttal’s added results justify a technical quality rating of 3, but the authors must:  
1. **Quantify spatial structure** using neuroscience-relevant metrics (e.g., spatial autocorrelation, boundary sharpness) in the main text.  
2. **Revise Figures 2–3** to enable direct comparison (consistent slices, clear legends, PCA baseline inclusion).  
3. **Correct all mathematical and grammatical errors** listed above.  
4. **Clarify the prediction task** in Figure 3 (e.g., "R^2 for regression of behavioral scores on $U_i$").  

This work addresses a meaningful challenge in neuroimaging analysis. With the requested revisions, it will provide a robust, interpretable tool for the neuroscience community. The authors should emphasize *why* spatial structure matters beyond "more structured" claims—e.g., how atom contiguity aligns with known functional anatomy. The revised manuscript must explicitly connect methodological choices to neuroscientific validation.  

*Note: The paper’s core contribution is promising, but the current presentation obscures its value. The revision must prioritize clarity and empirical justification over technical details.*
