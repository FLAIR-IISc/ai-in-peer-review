

This paper addresses dictionary learning for learning brain regions, proposing a large-scale online framework enhanced with Sobolev regularization to impose structural constraints. While the application to neuroscience is relevant, the contribution faces significant concerns regarding novelty and technical clarity.  

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **Limited Novelty:** The core approach appears to extend Mairal et al.'s online dictionary learning (JMLR 2010) by adding Sobolev regularization. The novelty is incremental rather than transformative, falling below NIPS' standard for substantial theoretical or methodological advances. The authors should explicitly clarify how Sobolev regularization fundamentally differs from prior work and why this specific addition is non-trivial for brain region learning.  

2. **Unclear Definition of "Structure":** The paper uses Sobolev regularization to enforce "structure" on the dictionary but fails to define this term operationally. If "structure" refers to spatial smoothness (e.g., neighboring brain regions having similar dictionary atoms), this must be explicitly stated. Crucially, the rationale for why Sobolev regularization—typically used for smoothness constraints—improves performance in this context is missing. The claim that "sparseness of the dictionary itself" drives improvements is misleading; Sobolev regularization does not directly enforce sparsity. The motivation for this choice remains unclear and requires rigorous justification.  

3. **Equation Errors:**  
   - Equation (1) contains a notational error: the matrix concatenation should be $[V^1, V^2, \dots, V^r]^\top$, not $[V^1, V^2, \dots, V^r]$ (without the transpose).  
   - Equation (2) lacks justification for using the $\ell_2$-norm on codes instead of $\ell_1$ (as in Mairal et al., 2010). The authors must explain why $\ell_2$ is more appropriate for their problem.  

4. **Ambiguous Line 146:** Line 146 references an unspecified element (e.g., a figure, table, or parameter). This must be clarified to ensure reproducibility.  

5. **Language and Presentation:** The paper contains multiple grammatical errors, typos, and unclear phrasing (e.g., "brain regions learning" should be "learning brain regions"). These undermine readability and professionalism. A thorough language edit is required.  

**Minor Suggestions:**  
- The introduction should better contextualize the gap between existing online dictionary learning and the proposed Sobolev-enhanced framework.  
- A brief discussion of how Sobolev regularization specifically aligns with neuroanatomical constraints (e.g., spatial continuity of brain regions) would strengthen the motivation.  
- The experiments should include ablation studies to isolate the impact of Sobolev regularization versus the baseline online method.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The technical approach is derivative, and critical aspects of the methodology (definition of "structure," equation choices) lack justification. The paper does not demonstrate sufficient novelty or clarity to meet NIPS’ standards. While the neuroscience application is promising, the current work fails to advance the underlying methodology meaningfully. To be acceptable, the authors must substantially revise the paper to:  
(i) Define "structure" precisely and justify Sobolev regularization’s role,  
(ii) Correct equations and clarify line 146,  
(iii) Address all language issues, and  
(iv) Demonstrate a clear, non-trivial contribution beyond extending Mairal et al. (2010).  

Without these revisions, the paper’s contribution is insufficient for publication at NIPS.
