This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for decomposing brain images, aiming to identify brain regions with structured and sparse components. The core idea is to combine sparsity – enforced via an L1-norm constraint – with structural regularization, achieved through a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage piecewise smooth and compact dictionary atoms (blobs). This extends prior work on online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) and is demonstrated on brain imaging data, showing improved interpretability and capture of inter-subject variability.

The paper builds upon a well-established area of research in neuroimaging, where defining regions of interest (ROIs) is crucial for statistical analysis and understanding inter-subject variability. Traditional approaches rely on segmentation or cross-modality correspondence, which have limitations. The proposed method aims to learn these ROIs directly from the data, drawing inspiration from clustering and dimensionality reduction techniques like PCA and ICA, but incorporating spatial priors. The online learning framework is a key contribution, enabling scalability to large datasets, a common challenge in neuroimaging.

**Strengths:**

*   **Scalability:** The online learning approach is well-suited for the large-scale brain imaging datasets encountered in practice, addressing a significant limitation of batch methods.
*   **Integration of Prior Knowledge:** The combination of sparsity and structural regularization through the Laplacian penalty is a principled way to incorporate prior knowledge about the expected characteristics of brain regions (smooth, localized activity).
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset demonstrate the method's effectiveness in extracting interpretable dictionaries and capturing inter-subject variability. The comparison with ICA and standard online dictionary learning provides a baseline for evaluating the benefits of the proposed approach.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** The paper connects the proposed model to existing theoretical frameworks in optimization (Block-Coordinate Descent) and regularization (GraphNet), providing a solid foundation for the method.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper addresses important practical aspects, such as hyperparameter tuning and initialization, offering guidance for applying the method in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Novelty:** While the combination of L1 and Sobolev regularization is a valid extension, the core contribution feels incremental. The paper would benefit from a more compelling argument for why this specific combination offers significant advantages over existing methods, beyond the empirical results.
*   **Clarity of "Structure":** The definition of “structure” could be more precise. While the authors specify piecewise smoothness and compactness, a more formal characterization or connection to relevant neuroimaging literature on anatomical or functional connectivity would strengthen the motivation. The benefit of structural sparsity *specifically* over purely L1 regularization needs more thorough justification.
*   **Typographical Errors & Language:** The manuscript contains a number of typographical errors and instances of awkward phrasing, which detract from readability and clarity. Careful proofreading is recommended.
*   **Equation (1) Typo:** A minor error exists in Equation (1) regarding the transpose operation.
*   **Justification for L2 Norm:** The choice of an L2 norm on the codes (Ui) instead of the L1 norm used in Mairal et al. (2010) requires further explanation. The rationale provided is insufficient.
*   **Line 146 ambiguity:** The reference to line 146 is unclear and requires clarification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The method addresses a relevant and challenging problem in neuroimaging: automated region of interest (ROI) definition.
*   The online learning approach enables scalability to large datasets, making it practical for real-world applications.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness and provide evidence for the benefits of combining sparsity and structural regularization.
*   The theoretical grounding and discussion of practical considerations enhance the rigor and usability of the work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The novelty of the contribution is relatively limited.
*   The paper lacks a clear and compelling motivation for the specific combination of regularization techniques.
*   The writing quality could be significantly improved through careful editing and proofreading.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid and well-executed extension of existing online dictionary learning techniques. However, the incremental nature of the contribution and the lack of a compelling argument for the specific regularization strategy raise concerns. While the empirical results are promising, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential areas for future work. I lean towards a **weak accept** contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the clarity of the motivation, justification of design choices (like the L2 norm), and a careful revision for typographical errors.
