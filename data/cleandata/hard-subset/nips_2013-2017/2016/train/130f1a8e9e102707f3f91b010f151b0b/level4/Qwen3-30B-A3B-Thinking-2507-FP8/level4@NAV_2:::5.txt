

This paper proposes a structured online dictionary learning approach (Smooth-SODL) for brain imaging data, extending Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial structure on dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate that this approach produces more interpretable, denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability across small, medium, and large-scale brain imaging datasets compared to state-of-the-art methods like SODL and CanICA. The work builds upon previous neuroimaging applications of dictionary learning (e.g., [1, 16, 25]) while maintaining the computational efficiency of online methods.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging—defining brain regions in a principled way based on inter-subject variability rather than relying on arbitrary ROIs or segmentation.
2. The method maintains the scalability of online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010), which is crucial for handling massive neuroimaging datasets like HCP.
3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing Smooth-SODL against multiple baselines (SODL, CanICA) across multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, reproducibility, predictive performance).
4. The authors provide valuable insights into hyperparameter selection and demonstrate the method's robustness across different data regimes.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty appears limited as the primary contribution is extending Mairal et al. (2010) with a spatial regularization term. While the Laplacian penalty is appropriate for enforcing spatial structure, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how this specific approach differs from previous spatially-structured dictionary learning methods (e.g., [1, 25]) in terms of both theoretical guarantees and computational efficiency.
2. The definition of "structure" in the context of brain regions could be more precise. The paper states that "structured" means "piece-wise smooth and compact," but it would be helpful to clarify how this specific definition relates to known brain organization principles and why it's preferable to other possible spatial regularization approaches.
3. Equation (1) appears to have a notational inconsistency; it should be written as Xi ≈ V Ui where V = [V₁|...|Vₖ] ∈ ℝᵖˣᵏ rather than using [V¹,V²,…,Vʳ]ᵀ as suggested in the draft review. This notation should be clarified in the paper.
4. The paper does not adequately explain why a ridge penalty (‖Ui‖₂²) is used for the codes instead of the ℓ₁ penalty used in Mairal et al. (2010), which would be more appropriate for sparse coding. This choice has significant implications for the model's behavior and should be justified.
5. Line 146 (in the original draft) appears to reference a section or equation that is not clearly identified in the paper, creating confusion for readers.
6. The paper contains multiple grammatical errors and typos that detract from its clarity (e.g., "intepretable" instead of "interpretable," "denoised" instead of "denoised," "ceteris paribus" used incorrectly).

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of neuroimaging analysis, particularly for large-scale brain imaging datasets. While the core algorithmic extension is incremental, the thorough experimental validation on real brain imaging data demonstrates practical utility. The work would be strengthened by a clearer articulation of the novelty, a more precise definition of "structure," and a more rigorous justification for key modeling choices. The paper meets NIPS standards for technical quality and significance, though the novelty could be more clearly articulated.
