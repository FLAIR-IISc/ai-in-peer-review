This paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework to find interesting brain regions. The topic itself is very interesting, as it addresses a crucial challenge in neuro-imaging by leveraging a multivariate online dictionary-learning method to extract structured and sparse components from brain images.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly the online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010), which it extends by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to impose structured and sparse components. The authors also discuss the limitations of existing approaches, such as Canonical ICA (CanICA) and sparse online dictionary-learning (SODL), which do not account for spatial variability across subjects or lead to dense and noisy dictionary atoms.

The paper's main contribution is the development of the Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL) algorithm, which combines the benefits of sparse and structured dictionary atoms. The authors provide a detailed description of the algorithm, including the optimization problem, the Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) scheme, and the dictionary update sub-routine.

The experimental evaluation of the proposed method is thorough, with comparisons to state-of-the-art methods like CanICA and SODL on a large public dataset (HCP). The results demonstrate the effectiveness of Smooth-SODL in extracting structured and denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability in small, medium, and large-scale regimes.

However, there are some areas for improvement. Firstly, the paper lacks theoretical analysis to show the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. While the authors provide some insights into the optimization problem and the convergence of the algorithm, a more rigorous theoretical analysis would strengthen the paper.

Secondly, the writing style could be improved. The paper contains some informal comments and typos, which detract from the overall quality of the submission. For example, the comment "146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v." should be removed.

Lastly, the paper could benefit from more discussion on the significance of the results and the potential impact of the proposed method on the field of neuro-imaging. While the authors mention some potential applications, a more detailed discussion would be helpful.

In conclusion, this paper proposes an interesting and relevant contribution to the field of neuro-imaging. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed Smooth-SODL algorithm in extracting structured and sparse components from brain images.
