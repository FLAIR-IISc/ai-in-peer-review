This paper introduces Smooth-SODL, a scalable online dictionary learning algorithm designed to extract structured and sparse components from large-scale brain imaging data. The core idea is to extend the well-known online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a spatial regularization term. Specifically, the authors add a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to the dictionary learning objective function. This penalty encourages the learned dictionary atoms (representing brain regions) to be spatially smooth and compact, forming "blobs" rather than scattered, noisy patterns. Sparsity is enforced via a separate l1-norm constraint on the atoms. The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to massive datasets, a crucial feature for modern neuroimaging projects like the Human Connectome Project (HCP). The authors provide a comprehensive experimental evaluation on fMRI data from the HCP dataset, comparing their method to the original sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a standard neuroimaging decomposition method, Canonical ICA (CanICA).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Practical Significance:** The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in neuroimaging: the data-driven discovery of meaningful functional brain regions (ROIs) from large population cohorts. The proposed online algorithm is well-suited for the scale of modern datasets, which is a major advantage over batch methods that are computationally expensive or memory-intensive.

2.  **Sound Methodology:** The proposed extension is technically sound. Incorporating a Laplacian penalty is a principled way to encourage spatial smoothness, and the authors correctly derive the updates within the block-coordinate descent framework. The analysis of the algorithm's complexity and convergence is valuable, showing that the addition of the structural prior does not drastically increase the computational cost.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and compelling. The comparison against both the direct predecessor (SODL) and a widely used domain-specific method (CanICA) is appropriate. The use of multiple evaluation criteria—qualitative visual assessment, quantitative metrics like explained variance and stability, and a downstream task of predicting behavioral scores—provides a comprehensive view of the method's performance. The learning curve analysis (Table 1) effectively demonstrates the benefit of the spatial regularizer, especially in low-data regimes.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Originality:** The primary contribution is the addition of a Laplacian penalty to the existing online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010). While this is a useful and well-executed extension for neuroimaging, the conceptual novelty is limited. The paper would be stronger if the authors could better contextualize their contribution. For instance, a discussion on why the Laplacian penalty was chosen over other spatial regularizers like Total Variation (TV), which the authors cite in related work [1, 3], would be beneficial. TV penalties are known for preserving sharp edges, which might be desirable for delineating boundaries between brain regions. A brief discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the justification for the chosen approach.

2.  **Clarity and Motivation of Modeling Choices:** The motivation behind some modeling choices could be further elaborated for clarity.
    *   The paper aims to impose both sparsity and structure. The introduction could more clearly delineate the distinct roles of the l1-norm constraint (promoting sparsity, i.e., many zero-valued voxels) and the Laplacian penalty (promoting smoothness/compactness of non-zero voxels). The current draft led to some initial confusion about whether "structure" was synonymous with "sparsity."
    *   The model in Eq. (2) uses an l2-norm (Ridge) penalty on the codes `U`, whereas the original work by Mairal et al. often focused on an l1-norm penalty for sparse coding. The authors briefly state that Ridge leads to "stable codes." This justification is important and could be expanded. A more detailed explanation of why stable (dense) codes are preferable to sparse codes in this specific neuroimaging application would be helpful. Is it because each subject's brain activity map is expected to be a combination of many functional networks, rather than just a few?

3.  **Minor Issues:**
    *   There appears to be a dimensional inconsistency in Equation (1). The paper states that `Xi` is a p-dimensional row vector (1 x p), while `V` is (p x k) and `Ui` is (k x 1). The product `VUi` yields a (p x 1) column vector, which does not match the dimensions of `Xi`. This should be corrected for clarity.
    *   The manuscript would benefit from another round of careful proofreading to correct minor typos and grammatical errors, which would improve its overall polish.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper presents a technically sound, scalable, and practically significant method for a challenging problem in a high-impact domain. The algorithm is a clear improvement over its predecessor in this application, as demonstrated by a strong set of experiments on a large, real-world dataset.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The core technical novelty is arguably incremental, consisting of the integration of a standard regularizer into an existing, well-known algorithm. The justification for certain modeling choices could be clearer and more detailed.

Overall, this is a solid piece of work with clear practical value. The strengths, particularly the scalability and the thorough empirical validation, outweigh the concerns about limited novelty. The paper makes a useful contribution to the application of machine learning in neuroimaging. I believe it would be of interest to the NIPS community, especially those working on large-scale matrix factorization and applications in scientific domains. I recommend acceptance, provided the authors can address the points on clarity and motivation in the final version.
