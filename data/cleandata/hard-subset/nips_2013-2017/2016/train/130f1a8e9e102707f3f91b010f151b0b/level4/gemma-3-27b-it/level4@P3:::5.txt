## Review of “Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning”

This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, aiming to identify spatially localized brain regions. The core contribution lies in extending existing online dictionary learning techniques (Mairal et al., 2010) by incorporating both sparsity (L1 regularization) and structural constraints (Sobolev penalty) on the learned dictionary atoms. The authors claim this combination yields decompositions that better delineate brain structures and capture inter-subject variability, scaling to large datasets due to the online learning approach.

The work builds upon a significant body of research in neuroimaging and machine learning. Traditional neuroimaging analyses often rely on defining Regions of Interest (ROIs) either manually or through segmentation, a process prone to noise and subjectivity.  The paper correctly positions itself within the context of approaches seeking to learn these ROIs directly from data, referencing clustering methods (e.g., [21, 14]) and dimensionality reduction techniques like PCA and ICA ([5]), commonly used for functional unit extraction.  The extension of online dictionary learning ([16]) is a logical step towards scalable, data-driven ROI identification.

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability:** The online learning framework is a significant strength, enabling application to large-scale neuroimaging datasets like the Human Connectome Project (HCP).
* **Integration of Structure:** Combining sparsity and structural regularization via the Sobolev penalty is a potentially valuable approach for enforcing biologically plausible constraints on the learned components. The idea of encouraging spatially coherent “blobs” is intuitive for brain region identification.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on the HCP dataset demonstrate improved performance compared to standard SODL [16] and CanICA [22] in terms of explained variance and predictive power for behavioral variables.  The ablation study comparing different regularization parameters (Fig. 1) is helpful.
* **Clear Presentation of Algorithm:** The algorithm is presented clearly, with details on the BCD optimization procedure and convergence considerations.  The authors also acknowledge and address the computational challenges associated with the Laplacian penalty.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of L1 and Sobolev regularization is a reasonable extension, the core innovation feels incremental.  The paper would benefit from a stronger articulation of why this specific combination is particularly well-suited to the problem of brain region learning. The claim of non-trivial extension seems overstated.
* **Motivation for Sobolev Penalty:** The motivation for using the Sobolev penalty (and the definition of "structure") remains somewhat unclear. The authors state it encourages "piece-wise smooth and compact" atoms, but a more concrete explanation of *why* this structural prior is beneficial for brain imaging would strengthen the paper.  Is there prior knowledge suggesting brain regions should be inherently smooth?  The relationship between smoothness and sparsity needs further elaboration.
* **Technical Details & Clarity:**  Several minor technical points and areas of clarity need attention. The equation (1) typo should be corrected. More justification for choosing the L2 norm on the codes (instead of L1 as in Mairal et al., 2010) is necessary. Line 146 is unclear and requires clarification. The writing contains numerous minor typos and grammatical errors which impact readability.
* **Comparison to Related Work:** The related work section could be more comprehensive. While relevant works are cited, a deeper discussion of alternatives like graph-based methods or other structured sparsity techniques in the context of neuroimaging would be valuable.  Recent work employing similar spatial priors ([1, 25]) warrants a more detailed comparison.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging: automated identification of brain regions.
* The proposed method demonstrates empirical improvements over existing techniques on a large, publicly available dataset.
* The online learning framework offers scalability, a crucial aspect for handling large-scale neuroimaging data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the contribution is limited.
* The motivation for the specific choice of the Sobolev penalty is not fully convincing.
* The paper suffers from minor technical issues and lacks polish in its writing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a reasonable extension of existing online dictionary learning methods for brain region identification.  However, the limited novelty and lack of a compelling justification for the specific structural prior raise concerns. While the empirical results are promising, the paper needs further refinement in terms of clarity, motivation, and technical detail.  

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject.  I believe the paper, in its current form, does not meet the high standards of NIPS.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly strengthening the motivation for the Sobolev penalty and improving the clarity of the writing, could significantly improve the paper's potential for acceptance.
