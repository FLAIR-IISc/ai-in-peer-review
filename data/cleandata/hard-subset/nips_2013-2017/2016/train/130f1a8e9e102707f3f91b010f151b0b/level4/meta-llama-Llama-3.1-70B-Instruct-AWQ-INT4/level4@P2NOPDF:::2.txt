This paper proposes an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals. The authors extend a well-established framework [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior to promote spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. They apply their method to empirical data, investigate the effects of varying tuning parameters, and compare its performance with two alternative methods in the field.

Technical quality: I would rate this aspect marginally below 3 if given the option. The model definition is clear, and the algorithms follow a well-established framework [14], appearing solid. However, the paper's presentation may undermine its technical quality. The results section does not clearly demonstrate the empirical advantage of introducing the Sobolev prior. Specifically, Figure 2 does not clearly show in what aspect the SSMF method outperforms the two alternatives. The authors could explicitly describe criteria justified by neuroscience literature and provide quantitative measurements of the improvement according to these criteria. For instance, they could discuss whether the spatial boundary is more smooth quantitatively or if certain regions, known to be connected, are only identified by SSMF. After reading the rebuttal, I found the additional results to be very informative, which prompted me to change my rating on technical quality from 2 to 3. I hope the authors will include these new results in their revision and demonstrate that the atoms learned by SSMF are more structured (with quantitative measurements) and more meaningful (with reference to neuroscience literature).

Novelty/originality: This paper makes a notable original contribution by extending [14] to incorporate Sobolev priors, encouraging spatially structured dictionary atoms.

Potential impact or usefulness: Dictionary learning is a useful approach for explaining neuroimaging data across a large group of subjects. Introducing Sobolev priors or similar spatial priors should theoretically improve the results. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm for doing so, which could be a valuable tool in the neuroscience/medical imaging community. The authors could also explore other applications of this method, such as image segmentation and regional sensing.

Presentation: The paper's organization is acceptable, but it appears to be a draft that requires editing. There are confusing descriptions and typos that make it harder to understand the content. I have listed the confusing parts below, some of which seem to be easy-to-fix typos, while others may require more detailed explanations.

* In the matrix decomposition equation between lines 67 and 68, should the transpose symbol be applied to each individual $V^j$ (j = 1, ..., r)?
* A reference to the HCP dataset is missing on line 73.
* The sentence "The Graph-Net penalty can imposes ..." on line 83 contains a grammatical error.
* The bold sentence on line 146 appears to be a co-author's comment that was not fixed.
* The last line of Equation (6) and Equation (8) do not agree on the sign.
* In the last line of Equation (6), I assume $V$ refers to the dictionary in the last iteration.
* There is a typo on line 157: "a single, a single."
* Another typo is found on line 169: "We did an extensive study of th how."
* In Section 3.3, it would be helpful if the authors explicitly provided the mathematical/algorithmic solution to Equation (6), even in the appendix.
* Figure 2 presents results by different methods at different horizontal and sagittal slices, making it hard to directly compare the results. The description on line 222 claims that the results by SSMF model are more "structured," but this is not clear from the figure.
* Figure 3 is unclear, and it is not obvious what is being predicted. The legends are not explained in the main text or the figure caption. The figure does not appear to show the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption. I did not follow the description on lines 224-227, which claims that R^2 is greater than 0.1.

Overall, the paper has potential, but it requires revisions to address the mentioned issues and improve its clarity and technical quality.
