This paper proposes an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals. The method extends a well-established framework with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply their method to empirical data, report the effect of varying tuning parameters, and compare the performance with two alternative methods in the field.

The main idea of the paper is to use a multivariate online dictionary-learning method to obtain decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The authors propose a penalized dictionary-learning model that combines `1-norm and Sobolev (Laplacian) penalties to impose sparsity and smoothness on the dictionary atoms. The model is an extension of the online dictionary-learning work of Mairal et al. (2010), and the authors claim that it can scale to arbitrarily sized datasets.

The paper is well-organized, and the model definition is clear. The algorithms follow a well-established framework, and the authors provide a detailed description of the optimization procedure. However, the presentation of the paper could be improved. There are several typos and confusing descriptions that make it harder to understand the contents.

The technical quality of the paper is good, but it could be improved by providing more quantitative measurements of the improvement in the results section. The authors claim that their method extracts structured and denoised dictionaries that are more interpretable and better capture inter-subject variability, but they do not provide explicit criteria justified by neuroscience literature to support this claim.

The novelty and originality of the paper are notable, as the extension of the online dictionary-learning framework with a Sobolev prior is a new contribution. The potential impact or usefulness of the paper is also high, as dictionary learning is a useful approach for explaining neuroimaging data across a large group of subjects, and introducing Sobolev prior or similar spatial priors should improve the results in theory.

To improve the paper, the authors could provide more quantitative measurements of the improvement in the results section, explicitly describe some criteria justified by neuroscience literature, and give more detailed explanations of the optimization procedure. Additionally, the authors could explore other applications of this method, such as image segmentation and regional sensing.

Overall, I would rate the technical quality of the paper as 3, novelty/originality as 4, potential impact or usefulness as 4, and presentation as 3. With some revisions to address the issues mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper proposes a new and original extension of the online dictionary-learning framework with a Sobolev prior.
* The method has the potential to improve the results of dictionary learning for neuroimaging data.
* The authors provide a detailed description of the optimization procedure and apply their method to empirical data.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* The authors do not provide explicit criteria justified by neuroscience literature to support their claims.
* The paper could benefit from more quantitative measurements of the improvement in the results section.
