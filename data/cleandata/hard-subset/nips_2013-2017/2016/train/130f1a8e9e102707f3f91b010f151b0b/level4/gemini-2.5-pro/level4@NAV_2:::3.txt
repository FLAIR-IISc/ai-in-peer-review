This paper proposes an extension to the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. [16], which the authors term Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL). The key contribution is the incorporation of a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty is designed to enforce spatial smoothness and compactness, encouraging the learned components (or "atoms") to form blob-like structures that are more representative of functional brain regions. The method combines this Laplacian penalty with a standard l1-norm constraint to ensure sparsity. The authors claim this extension maintains the scalability of the original online algorithm, with only a minor increase in computational cost, while producing more interpretable and robust decompositions of brain images, particularly when data is limited. The method is evaluated on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project, where it is compared against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a PCA/ICA-based method (CanICA).

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses an important and relevant problem: the scalable extraction of structured, interpretable components from massive neuroimaging datasets. Standard dictionary learning methods can produce noisy, scattered components, and the proposed Laplacian penalty is a principled approach to imposing a more biologically plausible spatial structure.
*   **Originality/Clarity:** The extension of the online dictionary learning framework to include a Sobolev prior is a clear and well-motivated contribution. The authors provide a detailed description of the algorithm and the optimization procedure, successfully adapting the block-coordinate descent scheme from [16]. The work is technically sound and builds logically upon existing literature.
*   **Scalability:** A major strength is that the method retains the online nature of the original algorithm, making it applicable to datasets that are too large for batch methods. The empirical comparison of runtimes demonstrates that the added computational cost of the Laplacian term is modest.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Quality of Evaluation:** The validation of the proposed method's primary benefit—improved structure of the learned components—is not entirely convincing.
    *   The main qualitative result (Figure 2a) relies on a visual comparison of brain maps. For a non-expert, it is difficult to objectively determine that the Smooth-SODL components are qualitatively superior to those from the standard SODL baseline. Both appear to identify similar large-scale networks.
    *   A central motivation is to better capture inter-subject variability. However, the evaluation does not directly quantify this. A more compelling validation could involve demonstrating that the learned components are more stable or consistent across subject sub-samples, or that they better explain individual differences in brain organization. The current stability metric (mean correlation) is mentioned but could be explored in more detail.
*   **Significance of Contribution in High-Data Regimes:** The addition of a spatial smoothness prior is expected to produce smoother results. The crucial scientific question is whether this prior leads to a *better* or more *accurate* model of the underlying data. The paper shows a clear advantage in the low-data regime (Table 1), which is a valuable finding. However, for the large-scale HCP dataset used in the main experiments, the benefit is less clear. With abundant data, even a standard SODL model may be capable of learning spatially coherent components, potentially diminishing the necessity of the explicit smoothness prior.
*   **Subjectivity of Validation:** The argument for the model's superiority rests heavily on the "interpretability" of the resulting maps. This is an inherently subjective criterion. The paper would be significantly strengthened by the inclusion of more objective, quantitative metrics that demonstrate the advantages of the structured prior. For instance, do these components provide a better low-dimensional representation for a downstream classification or regression task beyond the behavioral prediction shown? While the prediction results are interesting, Smooth-SODL does not outperform all baselines, making the overall benefit ambiguous.
