- Proposes Smooth-SODL: online dictionary learning with an L1 sparsity term plus a Laplacian spatial-smoothness penalty for fMRI analysis.
- Technical contribution is sound and scales to large datasets (designed for HCP), with modest computational overhead (2–3× slower than standard online dictionary learning).
- Implementation will be released in Nilearn, aiding reproducibility and community use.
- Experiments compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA, using explained variance, behavioral prediction, stability, and qualitative component assessments.
- Results claim more spatially structured and interpretable components than SODL and better stability/reproducibility than thresholded CanICA.
- Quantitative gains are modest: test explained variance improvement is marginal (55% vs 50% for SODL).
- Benefit of spatial regularization diminishes with large data; large advantage at small samples shrinks to near equivalence with abundant data (Table 1).
- Paper lacks clear guidance on when to prefer Smooth-SODL over simpler methods, especially in data-rich settings.
- Manuscript clarity is poor: confusing writing, inconsistent notation (ΩLap vs Laplacian), leftover draft comments, and dense algorithmic exposition.
- Theoretical advantages over other spatial regularizers (e.g., total variation) are not articulated or compared.
- Significance is overstated: claimed improvements (e.g., capturing inter-subject variability) are not convincingly translated into meaningful downstream gains.
- Insufficient discussion of limitations and failure modes, including sensitivity to the Laplacian parameter γ and lack of practical selection guidance.
- Claim that the method works well with random initialization is not adequately supported by experiments.
- Recommendation: promising but not yet a substantial enough advance for acceptance; strengthen experiments for data-scarce scenarios and provide more rigorous theoretical and practical analysis.
