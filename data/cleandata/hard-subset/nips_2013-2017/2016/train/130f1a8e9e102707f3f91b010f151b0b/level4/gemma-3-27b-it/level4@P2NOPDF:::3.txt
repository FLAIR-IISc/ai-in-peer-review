This paper proposes an extension to dictionary learning models by incorporating a Sobolev prior. While the problem addressed is interesting, the significance of this algorithmic contribution and the magnitude of its improvement are difficult to assess based on the presented results. The primary evaluation relies on visual comparison of the clarity and consistency of identified brain regions, which lacks objective measures and may not be readily interpretable for reviewers without specific expertise in neuroimaging. 

Furthermore, experience with sparse decomposition techniques suggests that data quality and preprocessing are often as crucial – or even more so – than the algorithm itself. The observed smoother results, achieved through penalties enforcing smoothness and spatial consistency, are somewhat expected. The potential utility of this approach appears limited to scenarios with sparse data, but given the rich datasets now available through initiatives like the Human Connectome Project, this limitation raises concerns about the broad applicability of the method. 

Ideally, the work would address the consolidation of differences in brain regions *across* subjects. Incorporating inter-subject variability and consistency as a form of validation would strengthen the paper considerably. Currently, the validation presented is largely subjective, and the practical benefits of the proposed method remain unclear. A more rigorous and quantitative evaluation is needed to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of this Sobolev-prior-based dictionary learning approach over existing techniques.
