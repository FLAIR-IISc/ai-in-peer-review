- Proposes Smooth‑SODL: online dictionary learning with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial structure in brain imaging dictionary atoms.
- Builds on Mairal et al. (2010) online dictionary learning and scales to large datasets (500 subjects, ~260k voxels).
- Demonstrates more interpretable, spatially structured brain maps compared to standard sparse dictionary learning and ICA on the Human Connectome Project.
- Leverages inter-subject variability to define brain regions rather than anatomical segmentation.
- Theoretical justification for the Laplacian penalty is well grounded.
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive: ablation studies, learning curves, hyperparameter analysis, and behavioral prediction tasks.
- Method maintains scalability and will be implemented in Nilearn, aiding reproducibility.
- Addresses an important neuroimaging problem with potential clinical and brain‑mapping applications.
- Novelty is incremental: similar structured dictionary learning ideas exist and prior works (e.g., [1,25]) should be compared more thoroughly.
- “Structured” is not precisely defined; the link between the Sobolev penalty and producing compact/blob-like brain regions needs clearer explanation.
- The claimed importance of spatial regularization weakens because differences between Smooth‑SODL and SODL shrink as dataset size increases.
- Comparison to CanICA is misleading since CanICA lacks sparsity; comparisons should contrast structured sparse methods with unstructured ones.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled approach, scalability, improved interpretability, predictive utility, and open-source implementation.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty, insufficient explanation of penalty‑structure connection, unfair baseline comparisons, and diminishing benefits with more data.
- Overall assessment: well‑written and technically sound but the incremental contribution and unresolved clarity/ comparison issues make acceptance hard to justify for a top venue.
