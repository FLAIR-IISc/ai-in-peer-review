- Summary: Proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning method adding an ℓ1 sparsity constraint and Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty to produce sparse, piecewise-smooth spatial atoms for population neuroimaging.

- Algorithm: Builds on Mairal et al. (2010) with block coordinate descent, ridge-coded updates, and projected first-order (FISTA-style) atom updates; claims 2–3x overhead vs SODL and scalability to large n, p.

- Empirical claims: On HCP task-fMRI, reports improved interpretability and stability vs SODL/CanICA, competitive explained variance (especially in low-data regimes), and decent behavioral prediction.

- Relation to prior work: Integrates Laplacian smoothness into online dictionary learning, sitting between unstructured online DL and structured spatial decompositions (TV-ℓ1, GraphNet).

- Novelty: Moderate — mainly algorithmic integration of Laplacian regularization into an online loop rather than a fundamentally new theory.

- Strength: Addresses an important practical problem of scalable, interpretable ROI discovery capturing inter-subject variability.

- Strength: Sound extension of established online DL with practical algorithmic choices and inherited convergence arguments from SODL.

- Strength: Empirical evidence that spatial regularization improves interpretability/stability with good early-data performance.

- Strength: Runtime seems reasonable and planned code release (Nilearn) boosts practical impact.

- Weakness: Limited theoretical contribution — no new convergence rates, identifiability, or approximation guarantees for the structured setting.

- Weakness: Key algorithmic details under-specified (exact projected/FISTA updates, combining ℓ1 constraint with Laplacian, conditions for FFT updates).

- Weakness: Hyperparameter guidance is heuristic with no principled selection strategy or thorough sensitivity analysis.

- Weakness: Evaluation is narrow — missing structured baselines (TV-ℓ1 DL, structured sparse PCA, NMF, constrained ICA), only one dataset/task, and sparse quantitative spatial-coherence metrics.

- Weakness: Presentation quality poor — typos, duplicated lines, inconsistent notation, leftover internal comments, and incomplete references.

- Quality: Optimization formulation and convex subproblems are reasonable; empirical results support claims but methodological ambiguities remain.

- Clarity: Major issues — needs proofreading, consistent notation, clearer figures/captions, and precise solver descriptions.

- Originality: Moderate; idea fits naturally within existing frameworks (SODL, GraphNet, TV-ℓ1) with incremental algorithmic contribution.

- Significance: Potentially high for neuroimaging practice if clarity, stronger baselines, and methodological details are improved.

- Pros for acceptance: Scalable, practical method improving interpretability/stability; solid empirical evidence on a large public dataset; planned open-source code.

- Cons for acceptance: Poor presentation, limited theoretical novelty, under-specified algorithmic details, and incomplete empirical comparisons.

- Suggested fixes — algorithm: Provide exact projected/FISTA updates, step sizes, projection onto C, stopping criteria, and FFT applicability conditions.

- Suggested fixes — hyperparameters: Specify ranges/selection procedures for α, γ, τ; include sensitivity/ablation plots and justify αt schedule.

- Suggested fixes — evaluation: Add structured baselines, quantify spatial structure (compactness, components, smoothness), and report reproducibility metrics and leakage-safe prediction setups.

- Suggested fixes — claims & writing: Temper global-optimum claims, clarify Laplacian vs TV, remove internal comments, fix notation/references/figures, and release code+configs for reproducibility.
