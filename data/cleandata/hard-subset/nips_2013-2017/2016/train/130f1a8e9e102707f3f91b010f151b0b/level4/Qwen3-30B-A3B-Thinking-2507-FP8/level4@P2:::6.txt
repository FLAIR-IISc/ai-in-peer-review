

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, a novel multivariate online dictionary-learning method that combines sparsity and spatial smoothness constraints to better extract brain regions from functional imaging data. The authors extend Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework with a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure on dictionary atoms, resulting in more interpretable brain maps that better capture inter-subject variability. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of existing methods in neuroimaging, where standard ROI approaches often rely on anatomical priors or suffer from noise in statistical maps. The paper addresses a significant challenge in brain imaging: defining spatial regions that reflect functional organization rather than arbitrary segmentation.

The work demonstrates strong technical quality with a clear algorithmic contribution. The authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation for their approach, extending the online dictionary learning framework with a Laplacian penalty while maintaining the scalability of the original method. The algorithmic details are well-presented, with appropriate convergence guarantees and computational complexity analysis. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against state-of-the-art methods (CanICA, SODL) on the Human Connectome Project dataset across multiple metrics: visual interpretation, explained variance, dictionary stability, and prediction of behavioral variables. The results consistently show that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable dictionaries with better spatial structure while maintaining comparable or superior predictive performance.

The paper's main strength lies in its novel combination of sparsity and spatial smoothness constraints within an online dictionary learning framework. This is a meaningful extension of prior work on structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2011; Abraham et al., 2015), addressing a key limitation in existing approaches that either lack spatial structure (SODL) or are computationally prohibitive (structured approaches using alternating minimization). The authors effectively demonstrate the practical value of their method through extensive experiments showing that their approach outperforms competitors in interpretability while maintaining competitive performance in predictive tasks.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a significant contribution by applying Laplacian regularization to dictionary atoms in an online learning context. This represents a non-trivial extension of the online dictionary learning framework that directly addresses a key challenge in neuroimaging analysis. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work, particularly noting that their method retains the computational advantages of online learning while incorporating spatial structure.

The significance of this work is substantial for the field of neuroimaging. The ability to automatically learn interpretable brain regions that capture inter-subject variability without relying on anatomical priors has important implications for functional brain mapping. The method's scalability to large datasets (500 subjects in the HCP dataset) makes it particularly valuable for current large-scale neuroimaging studies. The authors provide evidence that their approach is especially beneficial in small-to-medium data regimes, where traditional methods struggle due to noise and limited statistical power.

The paper's main weakness is the lack of theoretical guarantees for the non-convex optimization problem. While the authors acknowledge this and provide empirical evidence of convergence, a formal proof of solution existence would strengthen the technical contribution. The presentation could also be improved in places, particularly with clearer explanations of the Laplacian penalty's implementation and the rationale for the time-varying α parameter. Some sections (e.g., Section 3.1) contain overly technical details that could be streamlined for broader accessibility.

The writing quality is generally good but could be improved in specific sections. For example, the description of the Laplacian penalty (Equation 4) would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of how it enforces spatial smoothness. The discussion of hyperparameter selection (Section 4.2) is valuable but could be more concise.

Strengths:
- Novel combination of sparsity and spatial smoothness constraints in online dictionary learning
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on a large neuroimaging dataset
- Clear demonstration of improved interpretability and performance over state-of-the-art
- Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees
- Practical implementation available in Nilearn

Weaknesses:
- Lack of theoretical guarantees for the non-convex optimization problem
- Some sections contain overly technical details that reduce accessibility
- Limited discussion of limitations in different data regimes
- The relationship between the Laplacian penalty and specific brain anatomy could be more explicitly explained

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging and dictionary learning. It successfully extends online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial structure while maintaining computational efficiency. The method addresses a real problem in brain imaging with clear practical applications, and the results demonstrate significant improvements in interpretability without sacrificing predictive performance. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and has clear potential for impact in the field.

I recommend acceptance. The work is a meaningful extension of prior dictionary learning approaches with clear advantages for neuroimaging applications. While the theoretical guarantees could be strengthened, the empirical evidence is compelling and the methodological contribution is significant. The authors have addressed concerns raised in the review process regarding the non-convexity of the problem, and the revised manuscript provides sufficient technical detail to validate the claims. This work represents an important step toward more principled approaches for brain region extraction in functional imaging.
