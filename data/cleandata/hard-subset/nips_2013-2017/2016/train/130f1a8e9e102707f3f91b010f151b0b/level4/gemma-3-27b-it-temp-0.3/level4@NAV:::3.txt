The authors present an extension to dictionary learning using a Sobolev prior, addressing a potentially valuable problem. However, the significance and quantitative improvement offered by the proposed algorithm are difficult to assess. The primary results are demonstrated through visual comparison of identified brain regions, and the clarity/consistency improvements may not be readily apparent without specialized expertise. Furthermore, the quality of the input data is often a critical factor in decomposition tasks, potentially overshadowing algorithmic advancements. Given the imposed penalties for smoothness and spatial consistency, the resulting smoother segmentations are somewhat expected. While this approach might prove beneficial when dealing with limited data, its utility appears less compelling in the context of large datasets like those from the Human Connectome Project. The paper would have been strengthened by a focus on consolidating differences *between* brain regions across subjects, and leveraging inter-subject variability as a means of validating performance. The current validation relies heavily on subjective visual assessment, and the practical benefits of the method remain unclear.
