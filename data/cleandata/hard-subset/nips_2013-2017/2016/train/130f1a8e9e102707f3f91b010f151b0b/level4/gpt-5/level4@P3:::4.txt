Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), an online matrix factorization method for neuroimaging that learns under-complete dictionaries whose atoms are both sparse and spatially smooth. Sparsity is enforced by constraining atoms within an ℓ1 ball or simplex, while spatial structure is encouraged via a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty on each atom. Codes are computed by ridge regression against the current dictionary, and atoms are updated by block coordinate descent using a proximal step solved with a few iterations of FISTA. The method extends the online sparse dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) [16] by adding a smoothness prior, with reported computational overhead of only 2–3×. Connections to GraphNet-style penalties are made. Experiments on task fMRI from the HCP (500 subjects) compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and multi-subject ICA/PCA (CanICA), evaluating qualitative interpretability, explained variance, stability across splits, and predictive performance on behavioral measures.

The work is related to (i) online dictionary learning and large-scale matrix factorization [16, 17]; (ii) structured sparsity and spatial regularization in neuroimaging (TV-ℓ1, GraphNet) [1, 3, 11, 14]; and (iii) multi-subject functional decomposition (CanICA, MELODIC) [19, 22]. Prior structured decompositions for neuroimaging have typically relied on slow batch/alternating solvers [1, 23, 25]; this paper aims to bring spatial structure into the efficient online setting.

Strengths
- Problem relevance and scope: Learning reproducible, spatially coherent components from large neuroimaging cohorts is important and fits NIPS topics (large-scale optimization, sparse models, neuroimaging).
- Algorithmic contribution: A simple, practical integration of a Laplacian penalty into online BCD updates, with clear identification of per-atom proximal subproblems and use of FISTA. The discussion of Lipschitz constants and the scaling of γ with At is useful for stability.
- Scalability: The method retains the footprint of [16], with modest overhead. Mini-batches and closed-form ridge codes are appropriate for large n, p.
- Empirical study on a large public dataset: Using HCP data (n=500) is commendable. The comparisons include both unstructured SODL and a widely used ICA/PCA baseline. The learning-curve analysis is insightful: spatial regularization helps markedly in low-data regimes and the gap narrows with more data.
- Interpretability and stability: Qualitative results show cleaner, blob-like atoms. Stability across splits improves over SODL.

Weaknesses
Quality and experimental support:
- The quantitative gains are mixed. CanICA achieves higher explained variance (both train and test) and often better behavioral prediction; Smooth-SODL improves stability and interpretability but the paper does not provide strong quantitative metrics for spatial quality (e.g., spatial compactness, smoothness measures, reproducibility at matched support).
- Claims such as “1–2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient to reach 1e−6 accuracy” are implausible for generic Laplacian-regularized quadratics; precision and stopping criteria should be clarified. More generally, the effect of the proximal solver tolerance on overall convergence/quality is not studied.
- Ablations are limited. It would help to isolate the contributions of (a) Laplacian without sparsity constraint, (b) sparsity constraint without Laplacian, (c) ℓ1 codes vs ridge codes, and (d) simplex vs ℓ1-ball constraints, to substantiate design choices.

Clarity and presentation:
- The manuscript reads as a draft in places (typos, leftover comments, formatting issues, inconsistent notation). Examples: “xperiments,” “intepretable,” “comput,” dangling footnote markers, unexplained symbols in Eq. (7), missing spaces and punctuation, “th Conference...”. Algorithm boxes are hard to follow due to notation mismatches (e.g., At[j,j] vs Ajt, r vs k in loops).
- The constraint set alternates between ℓ1 ball and simplex; the choice (and whether non-negativity is essential) should be justified and kept consistent.
- The connection to GraphNet and why Laplacian (Tikhonov) rather than TV is chosen needs more justification beyond speed; a brief discussion of the trade-offs (edge preservation vs smoothness, blob propensity) would help.

Originality:
- The methodological novelty is incremental: adding a quadratic smoothness penalty to an online dictionary learning framework and solving the per-atom proximal step is a natural extension. Prior work has explored spatially structured penalties for neuroimaging decompositions; the main new aspect is the efficient online integration and empirical demonstration at scale.

Significance:
- The practical message—that one can get cleaner, more reproducible spatial components at modest extra cost in an online solver—is valuable. However, without stronger quantitative evidence (and given that CanICA still dominates on EV and often on prediction), the impact feels limited. Showing broader generalization (resting-state data, other tasks, different k and resolutions) would strengthen significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative spatial metrics: e.g., spatial dispersion (radius of gyration), proportion of mass in largest connected component, total variation, Laplacian energy, and inter-split Dice/ICC on thresholded supports. Report statistical tests across splits.
- Add ablations: Smooth-only (γ>0, no sparsity), Sparse-only (γ=0), ℓ1 vs ridge codes, and simplex vs ℓ1 ball. Include a small-scale comparison to TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning [1, 8] to contextualize the Laplacian choice.
- Clarify and justify hyperparameter strategies: the time-varying αt∼t−1/2 and γt scaling (Eq. 9) versus cross-validation. Provide ranges and selection protocol, and report sensitivity curves.
- Detail preprocessing: normalization, masking, centering, whether atoms are normalized between updates, and whether non-negativity is enforced.
- Tighten the algorithms section: define all symbols in Eq. (7)–(8), specify stopping criteria and tolerances for Alg. 2, and correct notation inconsistencies. Temper claims about FISTA iterations; instead, report empirical runtimes vs tolerance.
- Improve writing and fix editorial issues; remove draft comments; ensure consistent terminology (“atoms/components”), and provide readable figures (higher resolution, clearer legends).

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method that enhances interpretability and stability with modest overhead.
- Solid integration of structured regularization into online dictionary learning with reasonable theory and implementation details.
- Large, public dataset; code release planned; potentially useful in practice.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to known ingredients (online DL + quadratic smoothness).
- Limited quantitative evidence of superiority on core tasks; predictive performance sometimes worse than strong baselines.
- Clarity and polish need significant improvement; several claims require more careful justification.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and the scalability is attractive, but the manuscript needs stronger, more rigorous empirical evidence, clearer ablations and justifications, and substantial editing to meet NIPS standards. With these revisions, the paper could become a solid contribution.
