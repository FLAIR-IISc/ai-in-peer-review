Summary:
The paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework to identify interesting brain regions from neuroimaging data. The topic is timely and potentially impactful, given the growth of large neuroimaging datasets and the need for scalable, interpretable models.

Strengths:
- Relevance: The problem of discovering meaningful brain regions from high-dimensional data is important for both machine learning and neuroscience.
- Scalability focus: An online, large-scale approach is well aligned with practical constraints of modern neuroimaging.
- Potential impact: If effective, the method could yield interpretable structures that facilitate scientific insight and downstream prediction tasks.

Weaknesses:
- Lack of theoretical grounding: The paper does not provide any theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm. In particular, there are no guarantees or discussion of convergence of the online updates, conditions for identifiability or recovery of meaningful components, robustness to noise, or bounds on approximation error. Even a local convergence result to a stationary point or complexity analysis (per-iteration cost and memory) would strengthen the contribution.
- Insufficient clarity and polish: The manuscript contains clear signs of insufficient proofreading and unresolved comments (e.g., “146 XXX: I don’t understand the switch between V and v.”). There are also issues with duplicate or repeated sentences and unclear notation (e.g., inconsistent use of V vs. v). These issues make the paper difficult to follow and undermine confidence in the results.
- Methodological clarity: The paper does not clearly define the objective function, constraints, or regularization used in the dictionary learning formulation, nor the precise online update rules. It is also unclear how “interesting brain regions” are defined operationally (e.g., via spatial sparsity, thresholds, or post-processing) and how they are validated.
- Empirical validation: The current draft does not present sufficient empirical evidence to establish effectiveness. It is unclear which datasets are used, what baselines are compared, and which metrics are reported. There is no discussion of statistical significance, robustness to hyperparameters, or sensitivity analyses. The paper also lacks a systematic evaluation of scalability (e.g., runtime and memory versus sample size, dimensionality, or number of atoms).

Requests for clarification:
- Provide a precise problem statement, objective function, regularizers (e.g., sparsity, spatial smoothness), and the full online update scheme. Include pseudocode and hyperparameter settings.
- Present theoretical analysis (even limited), such as conditions under which the updates converge, complexity per iteration, and memory requirements. If new guarantees are not feasible, relate the algorithm to known results in online dictionary learning and discuss applicability.
- Explain how dictionary atoms are mapped to brain regions and how “interestingness” is quantified. Are there anatomical constraints, spatial contiguity priors, or post hoc thresholding/cluster formation steps?
- Detail datasets, preprocessing, and evaluation metrics. Include comparisons to standard baselines (e.g., ICA, NMF, sparse PCA, K-SVD, Mairal’s online DL, group-sparse models), and report qualitative and quantitative results (e.g., overlap with known atlases, test-retest reliability, prediction performance).
- Clarify and unify notation throughout. Resolve the “V vs. v” inconsistency and remove all placeholder comments before resubmission.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add theoretical support: Provide at least local convergence arguments or reference applicable results; discuss identifiability and stability assumptions; analyze computational complexity and memory footprint to justify the “large-scale” claim.
- Strengthen experiments: Use multiple public neuroimaging datasets; include rigorous baselines; evaluate interpretability (overlap with atlases, neuroscientific plausibility), reproducibility (seed stability, cross-validation across subjects/sites), and scalability (wall-clock time and memory vs. data size). Include ablations (e.g., effect of regularizers, number of atoms) and sensitivity analyses.
- Improve exposition and organization: Define notation early, maintain consistent symbols, and present the algorithm step-by-step. Tighten the writing, remove redundancies, and proofread carefully. Provide implementation details sufficient for replication and consider releasing code.

Overall assessment:
The paper addresses an important and timely problem with a potentially useful approach. However, the current version lacks theoretical analysis, rigorous empirical validation, and sufficient clarity of presentation. These issues prevent a reliable assessment of the method’s novelty and effectiveness. With substantial revisions—adding theoretical grounding or clear links to existing guarantees, comprehensive experiments against strong baselines, and careful proofreading—the work could become a solid contribution. In its current form, I recommend rejection, with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the above concerns.
