This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, incorporating both L1 sparsity and a Sobolev penalty to encourage smooth, compact components (blobs). The core idea is to leverage inter-subject variability to define brain regions, extending previous online dictionary learning work (Mairal et al., 2010) by adding a spatial regularization term. The authors demonstrate the method on fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project, comparing it to ICA-based methods (CanICA) and standard online dictionary learning (SODL).

This work builds upon a growing body of research applying dictionary learning to neuroimaging data. Existing approaches often focus on sparse representations for signal denoising and feature extraction (e.g., Abraham, 2016; Jenatton et al., 2010). The novelty here lies in the combination of sparsity and smoothness constraints, aiming for more interpretable and spatially localized components. Related work in structured sparsity, such as Total-Variation regularization (Baldassarre et al., 2013), has been explored in brain imaging, but often suffers from high computational cost. This paper attempts to bridge that gap by integrating a Laplacian penalty within an efficient online learning framework.  The use of online learning is also a significant consideration, enabling scalability to large datasets as demonstrated by Mairal et al. (2010).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Combination:** The combination of L1 and Sobolev regularization is a reasonable approach to achieve both sparsity and spatial coherence in the learned components, potentially leading to more interpretable results.
*   **Scalability:** The online learning framework allows the method to scale to large datasets, a critical advantage for neuroimaging applications like the HCP.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on a large, publicly available dataset (HCP) provide a concrete evaluation of the proposed method. The comparison to CanICA and SODL provides a baseline for assessing performance.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation and outlines the algorithm for solving the optimization problem.
*   **Implementation Plan:** The authors state intention to release their implementation in Nilearn, increasing potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Modest Improvements:** While the authors demonstrate improvements over baseline methods, the gains are not always substantial. The results section could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the specific conditions under which the proposed method outperforms alternatives. The improvement on predicting behavioral variables is noticeable, but the differences in explained variance seem smaller.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from readability issues. The writing is dense and sometimes confusing. The organization could be improved. Some sections feel rushed, and the presence of draft comments detracts from the professionalism of the submission. The explanation of hyperparameter selection and the derivation of equations could be clarified.
*   **Insufficient Detail in Comparisons:** The comparison to CanICA is somewhat limited. More details on the preprocessing steps used for CanICA (e.g., smoothing) and a discussion of the trade-offs involved would be helpful.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper acknowledges the importance of hyperparameter tuning, the discussion of the optimal regime feels somewhat superficial. More detailed analysis of the impact of each parameter (α, γ, τ) and guidance on how to choose appropriate values would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Discussion of Limitations:** The authors do not fully address the limitations of their approach. For instance, the computational cost increase compared to standard SODL is mentioned, but not thoroughly discussed in relation to the benefits.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to dictionary learning for fMRI data.
*   The online learning framework addresses the scalability challenges of large-scale neuroimaging datasets.
*   The empirical results, while not dramatic, demonstrate the potential of the proposed method to improve the interpretability and performance of brain image decomposition.
*   The authors commit to releasing their implementation, which will facilitate reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The improvements over existing methods are modest and may not justify the added complexity.
*   The manuscript suffers from clarity and presentation issues that hinder understanding.
*   The discussion of hyperparameter selection and limitations is insufficient.
*   The paper feels somewhat rushed and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. The core idea is sound, and the method shows promise, but the presentation and the modest nature of the improvements raise concerns. Addressing the clarity and presentation issues, providing a more thorough analysis of the results, and discussing the limitations in greater detail would significantly strengthen the paper. I believe that with revisions, this paper could make a valuable contribution to the field.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions, contingent on the authors addressing the clarity and presentation issues, providing a more in-depth analysis of the results, and expanding the discussion of limitations and hyperparameter sensitivity.
