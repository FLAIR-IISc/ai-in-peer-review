This paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework for identifying brain regions, extending the work of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially smooth and compact dictionary atoms – effectively “blobs” – alongside sparsity. The core idea of learning structured representations from population-level brain imaging data, rather than relying on predefined regions of interest, is a valuable one, building on related work in clustering (e.g., [21, 14]) and independent/principal component analysis for functional brain mapping [5]. The online learning approach is particularly appealing given the high dimensionality of neuroimaging datasets.

The strengths of this paper lie in its attempt to bridge the gap between scalable online learning methods and the need for spatially structured representations in brain imaging. The authors address a challenging problem – namely, leveraging inter-subject variability to define brain regions – and demonstrate preliminary results on a large HCP dataset. The proposed method appears to offer a good trade-off between sparsity and smoothness, leading to more interpretable dictionaries than traditional sparse dictionary learning. The comparison to ICA-based methods (CanICA) highlights the benefits of the structured sparsity approach. The reported running times and comparisons to existing methods (SODL, CanICA) are useful.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. A significant limitation is the lack of rigorous theoretical analysis supporting the effectiveness of the proposed Laplacian penalty. While the authors mention convergence guarantees inherited from the online dictionary learning framework [16], they do not provide any theoretical justification for *why* this specific regularization scheme improves performance or how the regularization parameter γ should be optimally chosen. The empirical hyperparameter study (Fig. 1) is helpful but does not substitute for a theoretical understanding. Additionally, the writing quality could be improved. While most of the paper is clear, the presence of internal comments ("146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v.") suggests insufficient attention to detail during preparation. Finally, while the authors acknowledge related work, a more thorough discussion of existing spatially regularized dictionary learning methods outside of neuroimaging (e.g., total variation regularization [3]) would strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in neuroimaging: principled ROI definition.
*   Proposes a novel extension to online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for spatial structure.
*   Demonstrates promising results on a large, publicly available dataset (HCP).
*   Offers a scalable solution for high-dimensional brain imaging data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks theoretical analysis justifying the proposed method and parameter selection.
*   Writing quality needs improvement (presence of internal comments).
*   The novelty is somewhat incremental, and a more comprehensive review of related spatially regularized methods would be beneficial.
*   The improvements over existing methods, while demonstrated empirically, are not always substantial.
