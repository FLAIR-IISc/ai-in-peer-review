Summary
The paper proposes an online dictionary learning framework for multivariate neuroimaging that enforces both sparsity and spatial structure in the learned atoms. Building on the online dictionary learning algorithm of Mairal et al. (JMLR 2010), the authors add a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty to encourage piecewise-smooth, spatially compact “blob-like” atoms, while maintaining scalability through an online block-coordinate scheme. Codes are obtained via ridge regression; dictionary updates solve a constrained quadratic denoising problem with Laplacian regularization using a few FISTA steps. Experiments on HCP task-fMRI maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5) compare the proposed Smooth-SODL to vanilla SODL and CanICA, reporting improved spatial interpretability and stability with moderate computational overhead (2–3× SODL), competitive explained variance, and reasonable predictive performance on behavioral variables.

Relation to prior work
The approach extends online sparse dictionary learning (Mairal et al., JMLR 2010) by integrating a spatial regularizer akin to GraphNet and related structured sparsity priors used in neuroimaging (e.g., TV-ℓ1 approaches and structured sparse PCA). Prior structured decompositions have typically relied on slow alternating minimization; the contribution here is to incorporate a Laplacian term within an online solver with convergence guarantees and practical speed. The paper compares to CanICA and SODL but not to strong structured baselines such as TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant neuroimaging problem: learning reproducible, spatially localized regions across subjects with scalability to large n and p.
- Clear algorithmic integration of Laplacian regularization into online BCD; practical details (FISTA with few iterations, mini-batching) are well motivated.
- Empirical results suggest improved spatial coherence and stability over SODL, with modest runtime overhead and reasonable generalization in explained variance and behavior prediction.
- Discussion of hyperparameter effects (τ, γ, αt) and stability–fidelity trade-offs is practical and useful.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty is incremental: the main technical change is adding a Laplacian penalty to the established online dictionary-learning framework. The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical or empirical differentiation from prior structured methods (e.g., TV-ℓ1, GraphNet) and from more comprehensive baselines.
- “Structure” is informally defined. A Laplacian term enforces smoothness, not compactness per se; compactness appears to rely on the ℓ1/simplex constraint. A clearer formulation and quantitative metrics of “blobbiness” or spatial compactness are needed.
- Choice of ridge codes versus ℓ1 codes is not fully justified. While stability is cited, a direct empirical comparison (ridge vs ℓ1 codes) would clarify trade-offs in sparsity, stability, and variance explained.
- Some notation and equations require correction/clarity (see specific comments). The presentation contains typos and duplicated sentences that hinder readability.
- The gamma scheduling and Lipschitz-based discussion (Eq. (9)) is interesting but remains abstract; how is γ chosen in practice across datasets/epochs? The current cross-validation guidance could be made more precise.

Quality
The optimization approach is sound and leverages standard tools with reasonable convergence guarantees (local optimum). The experimental protocol is broadly appropriate, though missing comparisons to strong structured baselines limits conclusions about the specific benefits of the Laplacian versus other spatial priors. Reported metrics (explained variance, stability, behavior prediction) are relevant, yet clearer definitions and additional quantitative measures of spatial compactness would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
Overall understandable, but the manuscript needs careful editing for typos, repeated sentences, and notation consistency. Key modeling choices (definition of “structured,” rationale for ridge codes, practical selection of γ and τ) should be more precisely articulated.

Originality
Moderate. The incorporation of Laplacian regularization into an online dictionary-learning framework is useful but incremental relative to existing SODL and structured regularization literatures.

Significance
Potentially useful for large-scale neuroimaging where spatially coherent and interpretable components are desirable, especially under limited data regimes. Broader impact would be stronger with comparisons to TV-ℓ1/GraphNet-based decompositions and with quantitative interpretability metrics.

Specific comments and requests
1) Definition of structure: Please formalize “structured” beyond qualitative “blobs.” For instance, define and report metrics such as spatial dispersion, cluster extent, or compactness indices, and ablation results showing the separate effects of ℓ1/ simplex constraint and Laplacian smoothing.

2) Codes (ridge vs ℓ1): Provide an empirical comparison between ridge-coded and ℓ1-coded variants (e.g., reconstruction error, stability, explained variance, prediction). Clarify when ridge is preferred and how it affects interpretability.

3) Equation (1) and shapes: Check consistency of dimensions for V, Ui, and Xi. If V=[V1,…,Vk]∈Rp×k with Ui∈Rk and Xi∈Rp, then Xi≈VUi. Some notation in the text appears inconsistent; please correct.

4) Equation (2) construction: Expand the motivation for the constraint set C (ℓ1-ball vs simplex) and its interaction with Laplacian regularization. Why choose a simplex (nonnegativity) versus signed ℓ1-ball in these data?

5) γ selection and Eq. (9): The Lipschitz discussion suggests γ must scale with At[j,j]/t. How is γ set in practice during training? If you use fixed γ with K-fold CV, reconcile this with the time-varying guidance, or report results with a scheduled γt.

6) Complexity claims: Provide more detailed complexity accounting for the dictionary update step (cost per FISTA iteration, number of iterations used, dependence on p and k), and clarify when FFT-based acceleration applies.

7) Baselines: Consider adding a structured baseline such as TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning or GraphNet-based decomposition (e.g., Abraham et al., Grosenick et al.), using recent fast solvers, to contextualize the benefits of Laplacian vs TV.

8) Notation/typos: Numerous minor issues (e.g., duplicated sentences; “xperiments,” “intepretable,” “unintepretable,” “there is not step size,” “we typically use we use,” “volumic”). Also, “line 146” appears to contain a stray fragment; please clarify or remove. A thorough proofreading pass is needed.

Pros
- Scalable, practical extension of online dictionary learning with spatial regularization.
- Produces more interpretable, smoother, and more stable components than SODL at modest extra cost.
- Solid application to a large, relevant dataset; code release planned.

Cons
- Incremental novelty; limited comparison to strong structured baselines.
- Ambiguity around the definition and measurement of “structure/compactness.”
- Insufficient justification for ridge-coded choice and for hyperparameter schedules.
- Presentation/notation issues and typographical errors.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The paper addresses an important problem with a practical method and promising results, but the contribution feels incremental and the evaluation lacks key structured baselines and clearer quantification of “structure.” Addressing the points above would substantially strengthen the case for acceptance.
