- Paper introduces SSMF: an online structured dictionary learning method that adds a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity and smoothness in neuroimaging dictionary atoms.  
- Technical approach is sound and is a logical extension of the framework in [14].  
- Main weakness: insufficient empirical evidence that the Sobolev prior improves dictionary quality.  
- Results (e.g., Figure 2) do not visually or quantitatively substantiate the claim that SSMF produces more “structured” atoms.  
- Reviewer requests explicit quantitative metrics and neuroscience-grounded criteria to define “better” or “more structured” atoms.  
- Author rebuttal added convincing quantitative analyses; reviewer recommends incorporating these into the revised manuscript.  
- Novelty: applying a Sobolev prior within the online dictionary learning framework is a valuable and original contribution.  
- Potential impact: method could improve interpretability of large-scale neuroimaging analyses and be useful in other spatial-structure domains (e.g., image segmentation).  
- Presentation needs substantial editing: multiple typos, unclear descriptions, and formatting/presentation issues hinder readability.  
- Specific technical/notation issues: matrix transpose placement (line 67–68) and ambiguity about whether V in Equation (6) is the previous dictionary.  
- Equations (6) and (8) appear to have conflicting signs in the V^j updates; derivation/explicit solution should be provided (appendix suggested).  
- Missing or incorrect items: HCP dataset reference (line 73), grammatical errors (line 83), stray co-author comment (line 146), repeated words (line 157), and incomplete sentence (line 169).  
- Figure issues: Figure 2 uses different anatomical slices across methods making comparison difficult; Figure 3’s caption/legend are unclear, PCA baseline seems missing, and the R^2 claim is not verifiable.  
- Strong recommendation: add quantitative analyses, neuroscience-relevant validations, and improved visualizations to demonstrate that SSMF atoms are both more structured and more neuroscientifically meaningful.
