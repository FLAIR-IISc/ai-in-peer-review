The paper extends online dictionary learning by adding a Sobolev (Laplacian) prior to encourage spatially smooth, compact, and sparse atoms. The goal is to obtain more interpretable, blob-like components for neuroimaging data while retaining the scalability of prior online methods (e.g., Mairal et al., 2010). The authors apply the method to Human Connectome Project (HCP) task fMRI maps and report that it yields cleaner, more structured components than standard sparse online dictionary learning and PCA/ICA baselines, at a modest computational cost.

While the problem is interesting, it is difficult to assess the impact of the proposed algorithm from the presented evidence. The main results rely heavily on visual inspection of components (blobs) on the brain, which is subjective and not necessarily convincing to an untrained eye. Given that the method explicitly penalizes non-smoothness and enforces spatial consistency, it is not surprising that the resulting components are smoother. In my experience with such decompositions, the characteristics of the data can be at least as important as the algorithm. With the large sample sizes available in HCP, the purported advantage of imposing strong spatial structure may be less compelling than in low-data regimes. I was hoping to see more about consolidating inter-subject differences in a principled way, and to use inter-subject variability/consistency as objective validation. As presented, the validation feels largely qualitative, and the practical benefit of the new prior remains unclear.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper:
- Provide quantitative, objective metrics of interpretability and stability: e.g., split-half reproducibility across subject splits, atom matching with correlation scores, and confidence intervals.
- Evaluate inter-subject consistency explicitly: demonstrate that atoms align better across subjects (or across resampled cohorts) compared with baselines, and quantify improvements.
- Assess the trade-off between reconstruction fidelity and interpretability with ablations over the Laplacian weight (γ) and sparsity level (τ), and report how these choices affect generalization.
- Clarify whether the method delivers gains beyond “smoother blobs”: for example, improved prediction of behavioral measures or more reliable ROI definitions, with appropriate statistical tests.
- If the main advantage is in limited-data settings, include controlled experiments varying sample size to show clear benefits there; with HCP-scale data, justify why spatial regularization is needed and what it adds beyond standard approaches.

Overall, the idea of integrating a Sobolev/Laplacian prior into online dictionary learning is appealing, and the application is timely. However, stronger, quantitative validation—especially around inter-subject reproducibility and objective gains over existing methods—is needed to make the contribution and its impact clear.
