- Proposal: extend dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior to enforce smoothness and spatial consistency in brain imaging.
- Technical soundness: formulation is correct but practical impact and superiority versus existing methods are not demonstrated.
- Weak validation: relies on a single qualitative figure comparing visual clarity of “blobs,” which is subjective and inaccessible to non-experts.
- No quantitative/statistical evaluation: claimed improvements remain anecdotal without metrics or significance testing.
- Expected effect: smoother outputs are unsurprising given the explicit smoothness penalty and not inherently novel.
- Unclear contribution: paper fails to separate algorithmic innovation from the expected effects of adding the prior.
- Misaligned motivation: abundant high-quality neuroimaging data reduces the need for specialized regularization to isolate regions with limited data.
- Missed validation opportunity: no use of inter-subject variability or cross-subject reproducibility, which are standard and crucial in neuroimaging.
- Lack of anatomical relevance checks: authors do not correlate learned patterns with known anatomical or functional boundaries.
- Insufficient benchmarking: no comparison to state-of-the-art neuroimaging methods that enforce spatial coherence or use anatomical/multi-subject priors.
- Potential limitations ignored: risks like over-smoothing and lack of discussion on hyperparameter selection and region-specific effects are not addressed.
- Recommendation: replace subjective visuals with quantitative metrics, benchmark against relevant methods, and analyze cross-subject/functional-anatomical alignment.
