The paper extends dictionary learning by introducing a Sobolev prior to encourage smoothness and spatial coherence in the learned components. This is an interesting direction, but it is difficult to judge the impact and improvement of the proposed algorithm from the current presentation. The main results rely on visual comparisons of the clarity and consistency of brain “blobs,” which is subjective and, to an untrained eye, not clearly distinguishable.

In my experience with this class of decompositions, the data are at least as important as the algorithm. Because the method explicitly penalizes roughness and promotes spatial consistency, it is not surprising that the resulting components look smoother. This may be most useful in low-data regimes to help isolate regions, but given the wealth of data available from the Human Connectome Project, data scarcity does not appear to be the limiting factor here.

I had hoped the paper would do more to consolidate differences in brain regions across subjects, and to use inter-subject differences or consistency as a key form of validation. As it stands, the validation feels largely subjective, and the practical benefit of the approach is unclear. Quantitative, objective measures—particularly of inter-subject consistency—would greatly strengthen the evaluation and clarify the contribution.
