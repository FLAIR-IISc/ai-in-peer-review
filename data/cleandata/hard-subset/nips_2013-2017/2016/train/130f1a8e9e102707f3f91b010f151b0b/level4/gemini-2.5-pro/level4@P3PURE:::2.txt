This paper proposes an online algorithm for learning a structured dictionary to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals. The method extends the well-established framework of [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of the dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate their method on empirical data, evaluate the effect of varying tuning parameters, and compare its performance against two alternative methods.

**Technical Quality**
The model definition is clear, and the algorithm, which follows the established framework of [14], appears solid. However, the primary weakness of the initial submission, perhaps exacerbated by presentation issues, is the failure to clearly demonstrate the empirical advantage of the Sobolev prior in the results section. It is unclear in what specific aspect the proposed SSMF method is superior to the alternatives. For instance, are the spatial boundaries quantitatively smoother? Does SSMF uniquely identify neuroanatomically known connected regions that other methods miss? Instead of vaguely stating that the SSMF results are "more structured," the authors should define explicit, neuroscientifically-justified criteria and provide quantitative measurements of the improvement based on these criteria.

*UPDATE AFTER REBUTTAL:* The rebuttal provided by the authors is very informative and addresses some of the concerns above. Consequently, I have raised my score for technical quality. I strongly recommend that the authors incorporate these new results into the revised manuscript. To further strengthen the paper, they should also include the suggested quantitative measurements to demonstrate that the learned atoms are indeed more structured and neuroscientifically meaningful.

**Novelty/Originality**
The paper's primary novel contribution is the extension of the framework in [14] to include Sobolev priors, which encourage the learning of spatially structured dictionary atoms.

**Potential Impact or Usefulness**
Dictionary learning is a valuable approach for analyzing neuroimaging data from large cohorts, and the inclusion of spatial priors, such as the Sobolev prior, is theoretically well-motivated to improve the quality of the learned components. This paper offers an efficient online algorithm to this end, potentially providing a useful tool for the neuroscience and medical imaging communities. The authors might also consider exploring other potential applications for their method, such as image segmentation or regional sensing.

*   **Line 73:** The reference for the HCP dataset is missing.
*   **Line 83:** Grammatical error: "The Graph-Net penalty can imposes..."
*   **Line 146:** The bolded sentence appears to be an unaddressed co-author comment.
*   **Equation (6) and Equation (8):** The update rules for $V^j$ have conflicting signs. Specifically, the last line of Eq. (6) is $V^j +At[j,j]^{-1}(VAt^j -Bt^j)$ while Eq. (8) has $At[j,j] V^j -(VAt^j - Bt^j)$. This needs clarification. Additionally, in the last line of Eq. (6), it should be clarified that $V$ refers to the dictionary from the previous iteration, not the optimization variable.
*   **Line 157:** Redundant text: "a single, a single".
*   **Line 169:** Typo: "We did an extensive study of th how".
*   **Section 3.3 (paragraph below Algorithm 2):** For clarity, the authors should explicitly provide the mathematical or algorithmic solution to Equation (6), either in the main text or an appendix.
*   **Figure 2:** The comparison is difficult because the results from different methods are shown on different horizontal and sagittal slices. Based on the provided images, it is hard to verify the claim in Line 222 that the SSMF results are more "structured."
*   **Figure 3:** This figure is very unclear. It is not explained what is being predicted (e.g., are brain data being regressed on behavioral metrics using the $U_i$ components?). The legends are not explained in the text or caption. The PCA baseline mentioned in the caption does not appear to be plotted. Furthermore, the claim in Lines 224-227 regarding an $R^2$ greater than 0.1 is difficult to verify as it is unclear which curves to examine.
