This paper proposes an online algorithm for learning a spatially structured dictionary to analyze neuroimaging data across individuals. The core contribution is the extension of the well-established framework from [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior, which is designed to encourage spatial contiguity in the learned dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate their method on empirical fMRI data, evaluate the impact of tuning parameters, and compare its performance against two alternative approaches.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty:** The primary contribution, the incorporation of a Sobolev prior into an online dictionary learning framework to enforce spatial structure, is a notable and valuable extension of existing methods [14].
2.  **Potential Impact:** The method has significant potential to become a useful tool for the neuroscience and medical imaging communities. Dictionary learning is a powerful technique for analyzing large-scale group data, and introducing spatial priors is a theoretically sound approach to improving the interpretability of results. The proposed online algorithm is also efficient, which is a key advantage for large datasets. The approach could also be adapted for other applications, such as image segmentation or regional sensing.
3.  **Technical Foundation:** The model definition is clear, and the algorithms build upon a solid and well-established mathematical framework.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

The central weakness of the manuscript is the insufficient empirical validation of the proposed Sobolev prior. While the motivation is sound, the results section does not clearly or quantitatively demonstrate the advantages of the proposed SSMF method over the baseline approaches.

1.  **Lack of Quantitative Evaluation:** The paper claims that the results from the SSMF model are "more structured," but this is a qualitative statement that is not substantiated with quantitative evidence. The authors should provide metrics to support this claim. For example, are the spatial boundaries of the learned atoms smoother? Does SSMF uniquely identify anatomically or functionally connected regions that are known to exist based on neuroscience literature? The manuscript would be significantly strengthened by defining clear, neuroscientifically-justified criteria for what constitutes a "better" dictionary atom and then measuring the improvement against these criteria.

2.  **Unclear Figures:** The figures, particularly Figure 2 and Figure 3, are not presented in a way that effectively supports the paper's claims.
    *   **Figure 2:** The comparison between methods is hampered by showing results on different anatomical slices. Presenting the results for all methods on the same slice would allow for a more direct and effective comparison. As it stands, it is difficult to visually confirm the claim that SSMF produces more structured results.
    *   **Figure 3:** This figure is difficult to interpret. The caption and main text should clarify what is being predicted (e.g., are subject coefficients U_i being used to regress on behavioral metrics?). The legends are not explained, and the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption does not appear to be included in the plot. It is also unclear how to interpret the claim (Lines 224-227) that R² is greater than 0.1 from the provided curves.

3.  **Presentation and Clarity:** The paper appears to be an unpolished draft, with numerous typos, grammatical errors, and confusing descriptions that detract from the quality of the work and make it difficult to understand. A thorough proofread is required.

**Conclusion and Recommendation (Post-Rebuttal)**

The additional results provided in the author rebuttal were very informative and have substantively addressed my main concerns regarding empirical validation. Consequently, I have raised my score on technical quality from 2 to 3.

I recommend acceptance on the condition that the authors thoroughly revise the manuscript to incorporate these new results and address the presentation issues listed above. The revision should focus on clearly demonstrating, with quantitative metrics and references to neuroscience literature, that the dictionary atoms learned by SSMF are indeed more structured and neuroscientifically meaningful than those produced by the compared methods. With these changes, this paper could be a valuable contribution to the field.
