Summary
This paper proposes an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals. It extends a well-established framework [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply the method to empirical datasets, explore tuning parameters, and compare against two alternatives.

Technical Quality: 3 (after rebuttal)
- The model is clearly defined and the algorithms build on a solid, well-established framework [14].
- Prior to the rebuttal, the empirical evidence for the benefit of the Sobolev prior was not convincingly demonstrated. The additional results provided in the rebuttal were informative and improved my assessment from 2 to 3.
- To fully support the claim that the Sobolev prior yields more meaningful and spatially coherent atoms, the paper should provide quantitative evaluations of spatial structure and neuroscientific plausibility.

Novelty/Originality
- The main contribution—integrating a Sobolev prior into online dictionary learning for multi-subject neuroimaging—is original and well motivated. While the algorithmic framework follows prior work, the structured prior and its efficient online implementation constitute a notable extension.

Potential Impact/Usefulness
- Dictionary learning is a valuable approach for modeling large-scale neuroimaging cohorts. In theory, adding a Sobolev or related spatial prior should improve spatial coherence and interpretability of learned atoms.
- The proposed online algorithm can be a practical addition to the neuroscience/medical imaging toolbox. Beyond group modeling, potential applications include image segmentation and regional sensing.

Empirical Evaluation
- The current presentation does not clearly demonstrate the empirical advantage of the Sobolev prior. For example, in Figure 2 it is unclear in what respects SSMF outperforms the baselines. The paper should:
  - Specify quantitative measures of spatial structure (e.g., boundary smoothness/total variation, number/size of connected components, surface-based smoothness, cluster contiguity) and report improvements with statistical tests where feasible.
  - Connect observed patterns to known neuroanatomical or functional networks, with citations to show that identified regions are plausible and meaningful.
- The rebuttal results were helpful. Please integrate them into the paper and add the requested quantitative demonstrations of improved structure and neuroscientific validity.

Presentation and Clarity
- The paper is generally organized but contains several confusing descriptions, typos, and notational inconsistencies that hinder comprehension. A careful editing pass is needed.

Specific Comments and Questions
- Matrix decomposition (between Lines 67–68): Should the transpose symbol apply to each V^j individually, or to the block [V^1, …, V^r]? Please clarify the notation precisely.
- Line 73: The reference for the HCP dataset is missing; please add the canonical citation.
- Line 83: Grammar: “The Graph-Net penalty can imposes …” → “The Graph-Net penalty can impose …” or “The Graph-Net penalty imposes …”
- Line 146: A bold sentence appears to be a co-author comment that was inadvertently left in; please remove.
- Equations (6) and (8): The last lines do not agree on the sign and scaling. One instance has V^j + A_t[j,j]^{-1}(V A_t^j − B_t^j), while another shows A_t[j,j] V^j − (V A_t^j − B_t^j). Please reconcile these, and clarify whether V denotes the previous-iteration dictionary (i.e., that the jth column is not the optimization variable v at that step).
- Line 157: Typo “a single, a single” → remove duplication.
- Line 169: Typo “We did an extensive study of th how” → correct phrasing.
- Section 3.3 (paragraph near Line 147, below Algorithm 2): Please provide the explicit mathematical/algorithmic solution to Equation (6), at least in the appendix, including the role of the Sobolev prior in the proximal or coordinate update.
- Figure 2: The methods are displayed on different horizontal and sagittal slices, which prevents direct comparison. Please align slices across methods and subjects, and annotate regions of interest. The current figure does not substantiate the claim (Line 222) that SSMF yields more “structured” atoms.
- Figure 3: It is unclear what is being predicted. If this is regression from learned representations to behavioral metrics, specify the predictors (e.g., U_i or subject loadings), the target variables, cross-validation protocol, and evaluation metric. The legend and curves need to be explained in the text/caption. The PCA baseline mentioned in the caption does not appear in the figure. The statement about R^2 > 0.1 (Lines 224–227) needs to indicate which curves/conditions achieve this and under what validation scheme.
- Parameter sensitivity: The exploration of tuning parameters is appreciated. Please make clear how sensitive performance and spatial structure are to the Sobolev prior weight relative to sparsity and other regularizers, ideally with plots and quantitative metrics.
- Computational performance: Since the approach is online, a brief analysis of time/memory complexity and wall-clock comparisons to baselines (for comparable accuracy) would strengthen the case for practicality.
- Reproducibility: Consider providing code or pseudocode with default hyperparameters and data preprocessing steps to facilitate adoption.

Overall Assessment
- Strengths: Clear motivation; principled incorporation of a Sobolev prior; solid algorithmic foundation; promising empirical direction; useful tool for large neuroimaging datasets.
- Weaknesses: The empirical advantage of the Sobolev prior is not convincingly quantified in the current draft; figures and text lack clarity; several notational and editorial issues.

Recommendation
- Borderline positive, contingent on revisions. Please incorporate the rebuttal results, add quantitative evaluations of spatial structure and neuroscientific plausibility, correct the noted inconsistencies and typos, align figures for fair visual comparison, and clarify the predictive analyses. With these improvements, the paper would present a more compelling and rigorous contribution.
