- Proposal: Introduces Smooth-SODL, adding a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty to online dictionary learning to produce spatially structured, piecewise-smooth dictionary atoms for neuroimaging.

- Methodology: Extends Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning by combining ℓ₁ sparsity with Laplacian-based spatial regularization.

- Implementation: Algorithms follow block-coordinate descent with minimal modifications to accommodate the Laplacian penalty.

- Theory: Provides convergence guarantees, computational complexity analysis, and relates to GraphNet and sparse dictionary learning literature.

- Computational cost: Runs ≈3× slower than SODL but is significantly faster than CanICA, retaining scalability for large datasets.

- Experimental datasets: Evaluated on HCP task fMRI and compared against SODL and CanICA.

- Empirical results — interpretability: Produces more spatially structured atoms than SODL (which has ring-like small values) and CanICA (dense maps requiring thresholding).

- Empirical results — stability & prediction: Achieves higher stability (mean correlation 0.4 vs 0.1 for SODL and 0.2 for thresholded CanICA) while maintaining competitive predictive performance.

- Small-data advantage: Learning curves show Smooth-SODL reaches 31% explained variance with 17 subjects versus 2% for SODL, highlighting benefits in low-sample regimes.

- Novelty: Provides a scalable integration of spatial structure into online dictionary learning, unlike prior structured methods that rely on slow alternating optimization.

- Impact: Significant for neuroscience — principled, scalable region definitions and an implementation in Nilearn enhance practical utility.

- Presentation issues: Manuscript has grammatical errors, inconsistent notation (e.g., eq.1 transpose), and unclear figure descriptions that hinder comprehension.

- Evaluation clarity: Needs explicit quantitative spatial-structure metrics (compactness/smoothness) and consistent anatomical slices for fair visual comparisons.

- Predictive-task clarity: Figure 3 and the behavioral prediction task require clearer explanations of setup and the role of learned dictionaries.

- Neuroscience validation: Should include validation that atoms correspond to known functional networks or regions to substantiate interpretability claims.

- Overall verdict: Technically strong, novel, and significant; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve presentation and provide concrete neuroscientific validation.
