- Proposes multivariate online dictionary learning for brain image decomposition, adding a Sobolev penalty to encourage spatially-localized, smooth dictionary atoms.
- Builds on prior online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) and structured decomposition work to extract ROIs from functional images using sparsity + smoothness.
- Addresses importance of ROI definition in neuroimaging and the limits of univariate, a priori ROI approaches.
- Positioned between clustering and signal-based models (PCA/ICA) with added structural constraints for interpretable components.
- Strength — Scalability: online framework enables application to large neuroimaging datasets with many subjects/voxels.
- Strength — Combined regularization: L1 sparsity plus Sobolev smoothness yields spatially coherent, interpretable "blob"-like atoms.
- Strength — Empirical validation: HCP experiments show improvements in explained variance and predictive performance over L1-only dictionary learning.
- Strength — Detailed algorithm: comprehensive description including BCD optimization, hyperparameter tuning, and initialization guidance.
- Weakness — Incremental novelty: extension feels incremental over Mairal et al. (2010) and other structured sparsity methods; novelty not strongly argued.
- Weakness — Vague definition of “structure”: motivation for imposing piecewise smoothness/compactness beyond sparsity is insufficiently justified.
- Weakness — Technical issues and presentation: equations (e.g., transposition error), unclear design choices (L2 on codes vs L1), awkward phrasing and typos reduce clarity.
- Weakness — Limited comparisons: evaluation lacks thorough benchmarking against other state-of-the-art region-identification or segmentation methods.
- Accept argument — Addresses a relevant neuroimaging problem with a scalable method for noisy data.
- Accept argument — Scalability via online learning makes the method practical for large datasets.
- Accept argument — Promising empirical gains on a widely used dataset (HCP) compared to baselines.
- Accept argument — Algorithmic detail could be valuable to practitioners and researchers.
- Reject argument — Limited novelty and weak justification for the chosen regularization reduce impact.
- Reject argument — Clarity and technical inaccuracies undermine confidence in the work.
- Reject argument — Insufficient comparisons prevent compelling demonstration of superiority.
- Overall assessment — Technically sound with promising results but incremental novelty and clarity issues lead reviewer to lean toward rejection, open to reconsideration after a major revision.
- Requested revisions — Better articulate novelty/motivation, clarify mathematical design choices, fix technical/clarity issues, and perform more comprehensive comparisons.
