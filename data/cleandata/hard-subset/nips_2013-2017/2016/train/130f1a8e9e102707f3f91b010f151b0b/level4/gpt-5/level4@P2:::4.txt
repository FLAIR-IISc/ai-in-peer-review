Summary
The paper proposes an online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging that enforces both sparsity and spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. Sparsity is encouraged via an L1-type constraint on the atoms, while spatial contiguity and smoothness are promoted with a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty. The optimization is carried out with block coordinate descent: codes are obtained by ridge regression, and atoms are updated using a few iterations of a fast proximal method (e.g., FISTA) with projections onto a simple convex set (e.g., the L1 ball or simplex). The method extends the online sparse dictionary learning of Mairal et al. (2010) to include a spatial prior, with reported runtime overhead of roughly 2–3x while retaining scalability to large n and p. Experiments on HCP fMRI task maps compare the proposed Smooth-SODL against standard online dictionary learning (SODL) and a PCA/ICA baseline (CanICA), assessing interpretability, explained variance, reproducibility, and behavioral prediction.

Relation to prior work
The approach builds directly on online dictionary learning for large-scale matrix factorization (Mairal et al., 2010; Mensch et al., 2016) and connects to structured sparsity for neuroimaging, including TV-L1 dictionary learning and GraphNet-style penalties. Prior spatially structured decompositions have often relied on slow batch or alternating minimization; here the contribution is an online variant that incorporates a Laplacian prior with minimal algorithmic overhead and standard convergence guarantees for block coordinate updates.

Strengths
- Addresses an important need in neuroimaging: scalable, data-driven extraction of spatially localized, interpretable components that capture inter-subject variability.
- Conceptually simple extension of SODL with a well-motivated spatial prior; practical algorithm with few tuning knobs during optimization (no step sizes).
- Large-scale experiments on HCP data; clear qualitative improvement in spatial coherence and denoising of atoms relative to unstructured SODL.
- Reasonable computational cost relative to baselines; code release is planned.

Weaknesses
- Quantitative gains over simpler methods are modest and not consistently demonstrated. CanICA attains higher explained variance, and Smooth-SODL’s advantages on reproducibility and prediction are not always substantial or statistically assessed.
- Evaluation breadth is limited: few baselines with spatial structure (e.g., TV-L1/GraphNet-based dictionary learning) are included, and measures of interpretability are not formalized beyond qualitative inspection.
- Hyperparameter selection (γ, τ, α) appears delicate; the paper suggests rules of thumb but lacks systematic guidance and sensitivity analyses with statistical support.
- The manuscript is difficult to read in places: residual draft comments (e.g., around line 146), typos, and dense notation impede clarity. Some algorithmic details (stopping criteria, exact projection operators, parameter schedules) are scattered or underspecified.

Quality
The method is technically sound and grounded in established optimization tools. The block coordinate scheme and proximal updates are appropriate, and convergence to a local optimum follows standard assumptions. However, some claims would benefit from stronger empirical backing: (i) ablations isolating the effect of the Laplacian penalty versus L1-only; (ii) statistical tests for reproducibility and prediction gains; (iii) clearer complexity analysis for the dictionary update as a function of γ and k.

Clarity
Overall presentation needs polishing. The narrative is sometimes hard to follow; figures and captions should more explicitly define metrics, thresholds, and matching procedures; and notation could be streamlined. The presence of draft notes and minor errors suggests the paper was rushed and should be carefully edited for clarity and consistency.

Originality
Moderate. Incorporating a Laplacian prior into online dictionary learning for neuroimaging is a useful, nontrivial practical extension, but the underlying components (online DL, L1 constraints, Laplacian/GraphNet penalties) are well known. The main novelty lies in the scalable integration and empirical demonstration.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners seeking interpretable, scalable decompositions of brain maps. However, the current empirical evidence does not yet show clear, consistent superiority over simpler or alternative structured methods, which tempers the broader impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method addressing a real need in large-scale neuroimaging.
- Clear qualitative improvement in spatial coherence and denoising of components.
- Solid optimization framework with standard convergence properties.
- Extensive real-data experiment and reasonable runtime.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty; limited and sometimes inconclusive quantitative gains over baselines.
- Missing comparisons to strong structured alternatives (e.g., TV-L1).
- Clarity and organization issues, including leftover draft text and ambiguous methodological details.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity not rigorously characterized; limited statistical analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: SODL vs Smooth-SODL with identical settings; vary γ and τ to quantify the stability–fidelity trade-off; report metrics such as spatial compactness, smoothness, and sparsity in a standardized way.
- Include structured baselines (e.g., TV-L1 dictionary learning, GraphNet-based decompositions) and, if possible, a group ICA variant with spatial regularization or post hoc mixture modeling, with matched preprocessing.
- Provide statistical tests and confidence intervals for reproducibility and prediction; report split-to-split variability and effect sizes.
- Clarify algorithmic details: stopping criteria and number of FISTA iterations; projection operators and their complexity; parameter schedules (αt, γt) and practical tuning guidance.
- Improve writing and organization: remove draft comments, fix typos, tighten notation, and make figures self-contained with explicit scales and thresholds.

Overall assessment
A promising, practically relevant extension of online dictionary learning that improves spatial structure and interpretability with manageable computational cost. With stronger empirical validation, clearer presentation, and broader comparisons, this work could make a solid contribution. In its current form, the paper is borderline due to modest demonstrated gains and clarity issues.
