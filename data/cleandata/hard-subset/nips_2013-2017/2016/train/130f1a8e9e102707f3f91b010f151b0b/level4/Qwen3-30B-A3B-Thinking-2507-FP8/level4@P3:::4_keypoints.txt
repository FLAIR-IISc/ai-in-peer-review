- Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online multivariate dictionary-learning method adding a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty for spatial smoothness while keeping L1 sparsity.  
- Addresses neuroimaging need for principled brain-region definitions that capture inter-subject variability rather than treating it as noise.  
- Extends Mairal et al.'s online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial structure with minimal computational overhead.  
- Principled mathematical formulation combining L1 sparsity and Laplacian regularization.  
- Successfully adapts online dictionary learning to maintain scalability to large datasets.  
- Theoretical analysis includes convergence guarantees, adding technical soundness.  
- Experiments on HCP data show more interpretable, spatially structured (blob-like) components than SODL and CanICA.  
- Shows particular benefit in small-data regimes where spatial regularization helps, with only a 2–3x runtime increase vs SODL.  
- Quantitative gains are modest: explained variance improved from ~50% to ~55% and reproducibility from 0.1 to 0.4 vs SODL.  
- Paper does not convincingly demonstrate that these modest quantitative gains yield meaningful downstream neuroimaging improvements.  
- Manuscript suffers from poor organization, draft comments, confusing math, and inconsistent notation (e.g., "Laplacian" vs "Sobolev").  
- Explanations (e.g., how Laplacian enforces spatial structure) are unclear and need more intuitive description.  
- Experimental details (preprocessing, hyperparameter selection) are insufficient for reproducibility.  
- Evaluation scope is narrow: single dataset (HCP task fMRI) and limited baselines (SODL, CanICA, PCA).  
- Lacks comparison to other spatial methods (e.g., TV-ℓ1) and broader datasets to strengthen claims.  
- Contribution is original but somewhat incremental; related-work comparisons need clarification on differences and improvements.  
- Pro-acceptance: clear problem statement, theoretical extension, scalability, strong experiments on a public dataset, and practical potential.  
- Con-acceptance: modest quantitative improvements, poor presentation, insufficient justification of advantages, limited comparisons, and some technical inconsistencies.  
- Overall: technically sound with potential, but requires stronger quantitative evidence, clearer writing, and broader evaluation before acceptance.
