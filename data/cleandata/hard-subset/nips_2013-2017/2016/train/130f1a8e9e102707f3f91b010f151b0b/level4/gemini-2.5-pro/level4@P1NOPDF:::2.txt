This paper presents an online algorithm for learning a structured dictionary to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals. The proposed method extends the well-established framework of [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity in the dictionary atoms. The authors apply their method to empirical data, evaluate the effect of varying tuning parameters, and compare its performance against two alternative methods.

**Technical Quality**
The model definition is clear, and the algorithm, which follows the established framework of [14], appears solid. However, the paper fails to clearly demonstrate the empirical advantage of introducing the Sobolev prior, a weakness that is exacerbated by the presentation of the results.

Specifically, it is unclear from Figure 2 in what respect the proposed SSMF method outperforms the alternatives. For instance, are the spatial boundaries quantitatively smoother? Does SSMF uniquely identify regions known to be functionally connected? Rather than vaguely stating that the SSMF results are "more structured," the authors should define explicit, neuroscientifically-justified criteria and provide quantitative measurements to demonstrate this improvement.

*(Update after rebuttal): The results provided in the rebuttal are very informative and address some of my initial concerns. I have raised my score for technical quality accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to include these new results in the final version. It would further strengthen the paper to also include quantitative measurements demonstrating that the learned atoms are more structured and to show they are more meaningful by referencing the neuroscience literature.*

**Novelty/Originality**
The paper's primary novel contribution is the extension of the framework in [14] to include a Sobolev prior for encouraging spatially structured dictionary atoms.

**Potential Impact or Usefulness**
Dictionary learning is a valuable approach for analyzing neuroimaging data from large cohorts. In principle, introducing a Sobolev prior or similar spatial regularizers should improve the interpretability and quality of the learned components. This paper presents an efficient online algorithm for this purpose, which could become a useful tool for the neuroscience and medical imaging communities. The authors might also consider exploring other potential applications, such as image segmentation or regional sensing.
