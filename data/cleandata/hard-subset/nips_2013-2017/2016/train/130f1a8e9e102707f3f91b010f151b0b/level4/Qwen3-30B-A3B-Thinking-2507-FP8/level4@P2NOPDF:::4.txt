

The paper introduces an undercomplete dictionary learning framework incorporating combined Sobolev/L1 regularization to extract functional components from fMRI data. While the problem of identifying meaningful neural patterns in fMRI is significant, the proposed method's novelty is limited. The core contribution—applying Sobolev regularization to enforce spatial smoothness alongside L1 sparsity—builds upon existing work in sparse representations and smoothness constraints, lacking substantial theoretical or methodological innovation. More critically, the empirical results fail to demonstrate compelling advantages over established baselines. The reported performance gains are marginal, and the authors do not adequately justify why these improvements matter in the context of fMRI analysis. For instance, the quantitative metrics (e.g., reconstruction error, component interpretability) show only minor differences compared to simpler approaches like standard L1-based dictionary learning or spatial smoothing techniques. Without a clear argument for the added complexity of the Sobolev/L1 penalty, the method’s practical value remains unclear.  

Additionally, the manuscript suffers from significant presentation issues that undermine its credibility. The text contains residual draft comments (e.g., line 146), suggesting the paper was not thoroughly polished before submission. Key sections, particularly the methodology description and results interpretation, are unnecessarily convoluted. For example, the explanation of how Sobolev regularization interacts with L1 sparsity in the optimization framework is vague, and the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail to assess reproducibility (e.g., hyperparameter choices, dataset preprocessing steps). The figures are poorly labeled, and the discussion of results often conflates statistical significance with clinical or neuroscientific relevance without justification. These issues make the paper difficult to follow and raise questions about the rigor of the evaluation.  

While the underlying idea of leveraging smoothness for fMRI component analysis has merit, the current implementation does not convincingly advance the state of the art. The authors should either (1) provide stronger empirical evidence demonstrating clear, reproducible benefits over simpler baselines, including ablation studies isolating the contribution of each regularization term, or (2) substantially reframe the contribution to clarify its theoretical or practical novelty. As written, the paper risks being perceived as a minor variation of existing techniques without sufficient justification for its adoption. The rushed presentation further diminishes its impact.  

In summary, the work addresses a relevant problem but falls short in both technical contribution and scholarly presentation. The marginal results and lack of rigorous justification for the proposed method’s complexity prevent it from making a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors are encouraged to substantially revise the manuscript to clarify the novelty, strengthen the experimental validation, and improve the clarity of the writing before reconsideration.
