## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission – [Paper ID]

This paper investigates dictionary learning for the specific application of brain region analysis. The authors propose a large-scale online dictionary learning framework incorporating a Sobolev regularization term to enforce structure on the learned dictionary. While the approach is technically sound, I have concerns regarding the novelty and clarity of motivation that prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time.

My primary concern lies with the incremental nature of the contribution. The proposed method largely extends existing online dictionary learning techniques (e.g., Mairal et al., JMLR 2010) by adding a Sobolev regularization term. While this addition is not incorrect, the novelty appears limited and, in my assessment, does not fully meet the standards for publication at NeurIPS. A more substantial theoretical or empirical advancement would be needed to justify acceptance.

The paper’s motivation for employing Sobolev regularization is also unclear. The authors aim to impose “structure” on the learned dictionary, but this concept remains vaguely defined. If the intended structure is sparsity, the benefit of imposing sparsity *on the dictionary itself* within the context of brain region learning isn't adequately explained. A clearer articulation of *why* a structured dictionary, specifically one encouraged by Sobolev regularization, improves performance on this task is critical. What specific characteristics of brain region data necessitate this type of regularization?

I identified several specific points requiring attention:

* **Equation (1):** There appears to be a typographical error. The matrix should likely be transposed, and the correct form is [V<sup>1</sup>, V<sup>2</sup>,…, V<sup>r</sup>]<sup>T</sup>.
* **Equation (2):** The derivation and justification for this equation require further elaboration. Specifically, the choice of an l2-norm on the codes, in contrast to the l1-norm commonly used in online dictionary learning (e.g., Mairal et al., JMLR 2010), needs a clear explanation. What are the benefits of using an l2-norm in this particular context, and how does it affect the learned dictionary and code representations?
* **Line 146:** This line reference is unclear and appears to be incomplete. Please verify the line number and ensure the sentence is properly formed.

Beyond these specific points, the paper suffers from a lack of polish. The language requires refinement, and I encountered numerous typos throughout the manuscript. A careful proofreading pass is strongly recommended. 

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** The contribution is largely an extension of existing work with incremental improvement.
* **Unclear Motivation:** The rationale for using Sobolev regularization and its connection to brain region learning is not convincingly established.
* **Technical Inaccuracies:** Typographical errors in equations and unclear line references.
* **Language & Presentation:** The paper requires substantial editing for clarity and grammatical accuracy.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Can you provide a more compelling argument for the novelty of your approach, highlighting its key distinctions from existing online dictionary learning methods?
* What specific properties of brain region data benefit from the use of Sobolev regularization? How does this regularization translate to improved performance in your application?
* Can you provide a more detailed explanation for the choice of an l2-norm in Equation (2), compared to the more common l1-norm?



I rate this paper as **Weak Reject**. While the technical foundation appears sound, the limited novelty, lack of clear motivation, and presentation issues prevent me from recommending it for acceptance in its current form. Addressing the concerns raised above, particularly a stronger justification for the Sobolev regularization and a more thorough explanation of the methodological choices, could potentially improve the paper's standing.
