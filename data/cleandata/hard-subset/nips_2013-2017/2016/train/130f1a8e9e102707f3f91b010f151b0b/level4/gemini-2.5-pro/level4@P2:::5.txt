This review provides a summary of the paper, followed by a discussion of its strengths and weaknesses, and concludes with specific suggestions for improvement.

### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a large-scale online dictionary learning algorithm, named Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), designed to discover spatially structured regions from brain imaging data. The proposed method extends the well-known online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages the learned components (i.e., brain maps) to be spatially smooth and form contiguous "blobs," which aligns with the known functional organization of the brain. The model also enforces sparsity via an `l1`-ball constraint on the atoms. The authors demonstrate that their online algorithm is scalable to massive datasets and that the addition of the spatial regularizer introduces only a minor computational overhead. Through experiments on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (HCP), the paper shows that Smooth-SODL produces dictionaries that are more interpretable, stable, and better capture inter-subject variability, particularly in low-data regimes, compared to standard sparse online dictionary learning and ICA-based methods.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Practicality and Scalability:** A key strength of this work is its scalability. By building upon an online learning framework, the method can handle massive neuroimaging datasets that are becoming increasingly common. The authors convincingly show that their extension to enforce spatial structure is computationally efficient, increasing the runtime by only a factor of 2-3 compared to the unstructured baseline. This makes the approach practical for real-world applications.

2.  **Improved Interpretability of Results:** The experimental results provide strong qualitative evidence that the Laplacian penalty is effective. The resulting dictionary atoms are visibly smoother and less noisy than those from competing methods, leading to brain maps that are more interpretable for neuroscientists. This is a significant advantage, as a primary goal of such decomposition methods is to extract scientifically meaningful components.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments on a large, challenging dataset. The comparison against relevant baselines like standard SODL and CanICA is well-executed across multiple quantitative and qualitative metrics, including explained variance, stability, and predictive performance on behavioral tasks. The learning curve analysis in Table 1 is particularly effective, clearly demonstrating the benefit of the spatial regularizer in scenarios with limited data.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The main criticism of this work is that its technical novelty is somewhat limited. The core contribution is the integration of a standard Laplacian regularizer into an existing online dictionary learning framework. While the application to neuroimaging is valuable and the engineering is non-trivial, the fundamental algorithmic advance is incremental. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly articulated its conceptual novelty beyond this combination of known techniques.

2.  **Clarity of Motivation and Model Formulation:** The motivation for certain modeling choices could be better justified.
    *   The paper defines "structure" as "piece-wise smooth and compact" and uses a Laplacian penalty to enforce it. While this is a reasonable choice, a more detailed discussion on why this specific regularizer was chosen over alternatives like Total Variation (which is also used for structured sparsity in neuroimaging) would be beneficial.
    *   The model uses an `l2` (Ridge) penalty on the codes, whereas the original framework by Mairal et al. also prominently features the `l1` penalty. The authors note that the `l2` penalty leads to "stable codes," but this point deserves elaboration. A clearer explanation of why this assumption is appropriate for modeling the composition of brain activity maps would strengthen the paper's rationale.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript could be improved with careful proofreading and clarification of certain technical points.
    *   The paper contains several typos and grammatical errors that detract from its readability.
    *   There appears to be a notational inconsistency in the problem formulation. In Section 2, `Xi` is described as a "p-dimensional row vector," but the objective function uses the term `‖Xi −VUi‖22`. Given that `V` is `p x k` and `Ui` is `k x 1`, their product `VUi` is a `p x 1` column vector. This suggests `Xi` should also be a column vector for the subtraction to be valid. Please clarify this notation.
    *   In Equation (7), the reference to "[16] for the details" regarding the underbraced term is a bit opaque. While space is limited, explicitly naming this term (e.g., "the unconstrained atom update before projection") or briefly describing its origin would enhance clarity for a broader audience.
    *   The meaning of "line 146" in the draft review is unclear, but likely points to the underbraced expression in Equation (7) as mentioned above. Improving the clarity of this step is recommended.

In summary, this paper presents a well-engineered and practically useful extension of online dictionary learning for a significant application in neuroimaging. The empirical results are strong and demonstrate clear benefits over existing methods. However, the contribution's novelty is incremental, and the paper's clarity could be improved with better justification of modeling choices and careful proofreading.
