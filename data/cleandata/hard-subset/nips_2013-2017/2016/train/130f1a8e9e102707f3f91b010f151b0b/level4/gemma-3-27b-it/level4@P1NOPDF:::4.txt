The paper proposes an under-complete dictionary learning approach, incorporating combined Sobolev and L1 regularization, for identifying functional components in fMRI data. This approach appears interesting. The primary contribution lies in the use of dictionary learning with this combined regularization strategy to simultaneously encourage both smoothness and sparsity of the learned components. However, the results do not demonstrate a substantial improvement over simpler methods, or at least, this improvement is not convincingly demonstrated. Furthermore, the manuscript feels somewhat rushed; for example, draft comments remain in the text (line 146), and the overall presentation is tedious and, at times, confusing. 

The motivation for combining Sobolev and L1 regularization is clearly stated and intuitively appealing. The idea of leveraging Sobolev regularization to capture the inherent smoothness of fMRI signals while simultaneously promoting sparsity via L1 regularization is a reasonable one. However, the experimental evaluation does not fully justify the added complexity of the proposed method.

Specifically, the performance gains observed on the synthetic data are modest and could potentially be attributed to the parameter tuning process. A more thorough sensitivity analysis regarding the regularization parameters (lambda and the Sobolev parameter) would be beneficial. It is unclear how these parameters were selected and whether the reported results are robust to different choices. 

On the real fMRI data, the improvement over competing methods, such as PCA and standard sparse coding, is minimal. The authors claim improved functional connectivity analysis, but the supporting evidence – primarily visualized in Figures 3 and 4 – is not particularly compelling. A quantitative comparison of functional connectivity metrics (e.g., correlation coefficients, graph theoretical measures) would provide a stronger basis for this claim. The visual comparisons alone are insufficient to demonstrate a clear advantage.

The writing quality could be significantly improved. The manuscript suffers from a lack of polish, making it difficult to follow the technical details. Several sentences are awkwardly phrased, and the overall flow is disrupted by inconsistencies in notation and terminology. For instance, the definition of the Sobolev norm is somewhat unclear, and its relation to the discrete implementation used in the algorithm is not fully explained. 

The description of the algorithm itself is also rather dense and could benefit from a more step-by-step explanation, perhaps incorporating pseudocode. The connection to existing dictionary learning methods with other regularization techniques could also be discussed in more detail to better position the contribution.

Finally, the inclusion of draft comments within the text (e.g., line 146) suggests a lack of careful review prior to submission. This detracts from the professionalism of the manuscript.

In summary, while the idea of combining Sobolev and L1 regularization for fMRI analysis is interesting, the paper falls short of demonstrating a significant practical advantage over existing methods. The results are not sufficiently convincing, and the manuscript requires substantial revision to improve its clarity, readability, and overall presentation. I would recommend rejection in its current form, but would be open to reconsidering a significantly revised version that addresses the concerns outlined above.
