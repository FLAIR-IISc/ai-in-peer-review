This paper presents an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals. The authors extend a well-established framework [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior, which encourages spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. They apply their method to empirical data, report the effects of varying tuning parameters, and compare its performance with two alternative methods in the field.

Technical quality: I would rate this aspect marginally below 3 if given the option. The model definition is clear, and the algorithms follow a well-established framework [14], appearing solid. However, the presentation undermines the paper's technical quality, as it does not clearly demonstrate the empirical advantage of introducing the Sobolev prior in the results section. Specifically, in Figure 2, it is unclear in what aspects the SSMF method outperforms the two alternatives. Is the spatial boundary more smooth quantitatively? Are there certain regions, known to be connected, only identified by SSMF? Instead of vaguely stating that the results by SSMF are "more structured," the authors could explicitly describe some criteria justified by neuroscience literature and provide quantitative measurements of the improvement according to these criteria.

Added after reading the rebuttal: The results provided in the rebuttal are very informative, prompting me to change my rating on technical quality from 2 to 3. I hope the authors include these new results in their revision and demonstrate that the atoms learned by SSMF are more structured (with quantitative measurements) and more meaningful (with reference to neuroscience literature).

Novelty/originality: This paper makes a notable original contribution by extending [14] to apply Sobolev priors and encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms.

Potential impact or usefulness: Dictionary learning is a useful approach for explaining neuroimaging data across a large group of subjects. Introducing Sobolev priors or similar spatial priors should improve the results in theory. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm for doing so, which could add a valuable tool to the neuroscience/medical imaging community's toolbox. The authors could also explore other applications of this method, such as image segmentation and regional sensing.

Presentation: The general organization of the paper is acceptable. However, the paper appears to be a rough manuscript, with confusing descriptions and typos that make it harder to understand the contents.
