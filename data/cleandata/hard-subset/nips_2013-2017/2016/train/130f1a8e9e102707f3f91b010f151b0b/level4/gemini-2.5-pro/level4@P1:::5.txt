This paper presents a method for learning representations of brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data. The authors extend the well-known online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by introducing a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty on the dictionary atoms. The goal is to produce atoms that are not only sparse (via an L1-norm constraint) but also spatially structured—meaning they form smooth, compact blobs rather than scattered patterns. This approach, termed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), is designed to be scalable to massive datasets and aims to extract more interpretable and functionally relevant brain networks compared to existing methods. The authors demonstrate its effectiveness on a large fMRI dataset, showing improvements in component interpretability and stability over standard sparse online dictionary learning and ICA-based methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Scalability:** The primary strength of this work is its scalability. By building on an online learning framework, the proposed method can handle the massive datasets that are becoming common in neuroimaging, such as the HCP dataset used in the experiments.
*   **Significance of the Problem:** The problem addressed—learning data-driven, interpretable brain regions (ROIs)—is highly significant for the neuroimaging field. Well-defined ROIs can boost statistical power and improve the signal-to-noise ratio in subsequent analyses.
*   **Empirical Results:** The paper provides a compelling empirical case, demonstrating that the proposed spatial regularization leads to more structured and reproducible dictionary atoms. The learning curve analysis (Table 1) effectively shows the advantage of the method in low-data regimes.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The authors show that their extension adds only a modest computational overhead (a factor of 2-3) compared to the highly optimized baseline method, making it a practical tool.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The main technical contribution is the addition of a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to the existing online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010). While this is a useful and well-motivated extension for the target application, the conceptual novelty from a machine learning perspective is limited. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of why this specific combination is non-trivial and what challenges were overcome in integrating the Laplacian term into the online Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) update scheme.

2.  **Motivation for Regularizer Choice:** The motivation for using a Sobolev penalty could be clarified further. The paper defines "structure" as being "piece-wise smooth and compact," which is imposed by the Laplacian penalty, while sparsity is handled separately by the L1-norm constraint. However, the introduction could better articulate why this specific form of smoothness is the most appropriate prior for this problem compared to other spatial regularizers, such as Total Variation (TV), which is also known to promote piece-wise constant regions. A clearer justification for this modeling choice would improve the paper.

3.  **Justification for Model Formulation:** The construction of the objective function in Equation (2) warrants more discussion. In particular, the choice of an L2 (Ridge) penalty on the codes `U_i` is a departure from the L1 penalty often used in sparse coding and in the original Mairal et al. paper. The authors briefly state that the L2 norm leads to more "stable" codes. It would be beneficial to elaborate on this point. What are the practical implications of this stability in the context of neuroimaging data? An empirical comparison showing the effect of using an L1 versus an L2 penalty on the codes would make the justification for this design choice more convincing.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript would benefit from careful proofreading to improve clarity and fix minor errors. For example:
    *   In Equation (1), the dimensions appear inconsistent. `X_i` is defined as a p-dimensional row vector, but the product `VU_i` (where `V` is p x k and `U_i` is k x 1) results in a p x 1 column vector. Please clarify this notation.
    *   There are several typos and grammatical errors throughout the text that detract from the paper's readability. A thorough pass by a native English speaker would significantly improve the presentation.

In summary, the paper presents a practical and well-engineered solution to an important problem in neuroimaging. However, the limited technical novelty and the need for clearer justification of certain modeling choices are notable weaknesses.
