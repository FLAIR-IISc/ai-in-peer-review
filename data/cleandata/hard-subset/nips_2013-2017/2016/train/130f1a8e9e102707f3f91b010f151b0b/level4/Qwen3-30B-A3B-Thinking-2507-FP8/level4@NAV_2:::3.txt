

The authors extend the online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial structure in brain image decompositions. This work builds upon recent advances in structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging, particularly addressing the need for spatially coherent brain regions rather than scattered activation patterns. The proposed Smooth-SODL method maintains the online scalability of the original framework while adding spatial smoothness constraints, with only a modest increase in computational complexity (approximately 2-3 times slower than standard SODL).

The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on Human Connectome Project (HCP) data, showing that the structured dictionaries better capture inter-subject variability across different dataset sizes. The authors provide thorough experimental validation across multiple metrics, including visual interpretation of dictionary atoms, explained variance, stability measures, and predictive performance for behavioral variables.

Strengths: The technical approach is sound, with appropriate theoretical grounding in the Laplacian regularization and clear experimental validation. The method's scalability to massive datasets is well-motivated and demonstrated. The comparison against relevant baselines (SODL, CanICA) is comprehensive, and the analysis of hyper-parameter effects provides valuable practical guidance. The implementation as part of Nilearn enhances reproducibility.

Weaknesses: The primary concern is the difficulty in objectively assessing the claimed improvements in interpretability. While the visual comparisons in Figure 2(a) show smoother spatial patterns, the justification for why these patterns represent "better" brain regions is not sufficiently compelling. The paper does not adequately address how these structured patterns directly improve scientific understanding of brain organization beyond visual appeal. The validation metrics (explained variance, stability) do not clearly demonstrate that the structured approach leads to more biologically meaningful regions compared to alternatives.

Quality: The method is technically sound with appropriate theoretical foundation and experimental validation. The computational complexity analysis is appropriate, and the convergence guarantees are well-established.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, though the interpretation of results would benefit from more specific biological validation. The relationship between the proposed method and previous work on structured dictionary learning (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2012) could be more explicitly articulated.

Originality: The integration of Sobolev regularization into online dictionary learning for neuroimaging is novel and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. The approach extends rather than merely replicates previous work.

Significance: While the method is technically sound, the significance is somewhat limited by the lack of clear demonstration that the structured patterns provide new scientific insights about brain organization beyond what can be achieved with standard dictionary learning. The paper shows improved visual coherence and modest gains in predictive performance, but does not convincingly demonstrate that these improvements translate to more meaningful neuroscientific findings, particularly given the large HCP dataset used.

The most significant gap is the lack of objective validation that the structured patterns correspond to known functional brain organization. The paper would be strengthened by incorporating additional validation metrics such as comparison with known neuroanatomical atlases or examining whether the structured components better align with known cognitive processes. The claim that the method "better captures inter-subject variability" would be more convincing with statistical validation of the variability patterns rather than just visual comparisons.

Overall, this is a technically solid contribution that extends existing methods in a meaningful way, but the significance would be substantially enhanced by more concrete evidence of how the structured patterns improve scientific understanding of brain organization.
