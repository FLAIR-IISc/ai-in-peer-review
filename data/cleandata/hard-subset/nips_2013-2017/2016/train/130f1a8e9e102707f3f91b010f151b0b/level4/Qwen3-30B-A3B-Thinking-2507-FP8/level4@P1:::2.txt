

This paper proposes an extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging data analysis, incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms. The authors extend Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework to learn brain regions that are both sparse and spatially contiguous, addressing inter-subject variability in functional brain imaging. The method is applied to HCP task fMRI data, with comparisons to SODL (sparse online dictionary learning) and CanICA.

Technical quality: The model is clearly defined and builds logically on established work. The algorithmic approach is sound and follows a well-established framework. However, the empirical demonstration of the Sobolev prior's advantages was initially unclear. The authors claimed "more structured" dictionaries without providing specific quantitative metrics or neuroscience-justified criteria to support this claim. Figure 2 shows visual differences but lacks clear quantitative evidence of improved spatial contiguity (e.g., boundary smoothness measurements, connectivity metrics for known brain networks). After reviewing the authors' rebuttal, which provided additional quantitative evidence, I have revised my technical quality rating from 2 to 3. I encourage the authors to include these quantitative metrics in the final manuscript, explicitly demonstrating how their method improves spatial structure and interpretability according to neuroscience criteria.

Novelty/originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by extending online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial structure via a Laplacian penalty. While sparse dictionary learning has been applied to neuroimaging, this is one of the first attempts to integrate spatial structure with the online learning framework. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from prior work that used slow alternating optimization (e.g., [1, 25]) or lacked spatial constraints (e.g., CanICA).

Potential impact: This work provides an efficient computational tool for brain region extraction that could become valuable in neuroimaging analysis pipelines. The online nature of the algorithm makes it suitable for large-scale datasets like HCP. The method could potentially be extended to other medical imaging applications beyond neuroimaging, such as image segmentation or regional analysis in other anatomical structures.

Presentation: The paper is generally well-organized but contains several presentation issues that hinder clarity. I've compiled specific concerns below:

- In the matrix decomposition equation (lines 67-68), the transpose symbol on V^j (j=1,...,k) should apply to each individual V^j, not the group.
- Line 73 references the HCP dataset without proper citation.
- Line 83 states "The Graph-Net penalty can imposes" (should be "impose").
- Line 146 contains an unedited bolded comment that appears to be a co-author's note.
- Equations (6) and (8) have inconsistent signs in the expression V^j + At[j,j]^{-1}(VAt^j - Bt^j) vs. At[j,j]V^j - (VAt^j - Bt^j). In Equation (6), V refers to the dictionary from the previous iteration.
- Line 157 contains the redundant phrase "a single, a single".
- Line 169 has a typo: "We did an extensive study of th how".
- Section 3.3 (below Algorithm 2) should explicitly provide the mathematical solution to Equation (6), even if in the appendix.
- Figure 2 presents results at different anatomical slices (horizontal vs. sagittal), making direct visual comparison difficult. The claim that Smooth-SODL produces "more structured" atoms lacks quantitative support in the figure.
- Figure 3 is unclear about what was predicted (brain data on behavioral metrics? using U_i?).
- The figure caption mentions a PCA baseline, but this is not visible in the figure itself.

The paper demonstrates the method's computational efficiency (3× slower than SODL, 1.6× faster than CanICA) and shows improved interpretability of brain regions. The authors provide a useful analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and demonstrate the method's effectiveness across small, medium, and large datasets. The claim that their method better captures inter-subject variability is supported by stability metrics (mean correlation between atoms across data splits: Smooth-SODL=0.4 vs. SODL=0.1, CanICA=0.1).

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with clear potential impact for neuroimaging analysis. The method extends existing work in a meaningful way and provides an efficient solution for large-scale brain imaging data. While the initial submission had presentation issues and insufficient quantitative demonstration of the spatial structure advantage, the authors' rebuttal provided valuable additional evidence. I recommend acceptance with the authors addressing the presentation issues and providing more explicit quantitative metrics demonstrating the spatial structure improvement. The paper would benefit from clearer explanations of the algorithmic details, consistent mathematical notation, and more precise figures that facilitate direct comparison of methods.
