- Summary: Proposes an online dictionary learning method (Smooth-SODL) adding a Laplacian penalty to enforce piecewise-smooth, spatially compact atoms with scalable online BCD updates and FISTA-based dictionary solves.  
- Relation to prior work: Extends Mairal et al. (2010) SODL by integrating Laplacian/GraphNet-like spatial regularization, but lacks comparison to strong structured baselines (e.g., TV-ℓ1).  
- Strength: Addresses scalable, reproducible learning of spatially localized neuroimaging components for large n and p.  
- Strength: Clear algorithmic integration of Laplacian regularization with practical implementation details (mini-batching, few FISTA steps).  
- Strength: Empirical gains in spatial coherence and stability over SODL with modest runtime overhead and reasonable predictive performance.  
- Strength: Useful discussion of hyperparameter effects and stability–fidelity trade-offs.  
- Weakness: Novelty is incremental—main change is adding a Laplacian penalty to an existing online framework.  
- Weakness: “Structure” is informally defined; Laplacian enforces smoothness but not necessarily compactness—need quantitative metrics for “blobbiness.”  
- Weakness: Choice of ridge (ℓ2) codes over sparse (ℓ1) codes is under-justified; lacks direct empirical comparison.  
- Weakness: Notation errors, typos, and duplicated sentences reduce readability and require correction.  
- Weakness: Gamma scheduling and Lipschitz-based guidance (Eq. (9)) are abstract; practical selection of γ across training is unclear.  
- Quality: Optimization approach is sound with reasonable guarantees, but missing structured baselines limits claims about Laplacian benefits.  
- Clarity: Generally understandable but needs clearer articulation of “structured,” code choice, and hyperparameter selection, plus proofreading.  
- Originality: Moderate — useful but incremental relative to SODL and structured regularization literature.  
- Significance: Potentially useful for large-scale neuroimaging, but broader impact depends on comparisons to TV-ℓ1/GraphNet and quantitative interpretability metrics.  
- Request 1: Formalize “structure” and report metrics (spatial dispersion, cluster extent, compactness) and ablations separating ℓ1/simplex and Laplacian effects.  
- Request 2: Provide empirical comparison between ridge-coded and ℓ1-coded variants (reconstruction error, stability, explained variance, prediction).  
- Request 3: Fix notation and dimension consistency for V, Ui, and Xi (clarify Xi ≈ VUi).  
- Request 4: Expand motivation for constraint set C (ℓ1-ball vs simplex) and justify nonnegativity versus signed representations.  
- Request 5: Clarify how γ is chosen in practice (fixed vs scheduled) and reconcile with the Lipschitz/time-varying discussion.  
- Request 6: Provide detailed complexity accounting for dictionary updates (cost per FISTA iteration, iterations used, dependence on p and k) and when FFT acceleration applies.  
- Request 7: Add structured baselines (e.g., TV-ℓ1 or GraphNet decompositions using fast solvers) to contextualize benefits.  
- Request 8: Thoroughly proofread manuscript to fix typos, duplicated fragments, and notation issues.  
- Pros: Scalable, practical extension producing smoother, more stable components with application to large datasets and planned code release.  
- Cons: Incremental novelty, unclear measurement of compactness, insufficient justification for ridge codes and hyperparameter schedules, and presentation issues.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak reject — promising and practical but needs stronger baselines, clearer quantification of structure, and improved presentation to justify acceptance.
