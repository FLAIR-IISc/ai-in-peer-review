This paper introduces a novel multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, aiming to identify structured and sparse components – effectively, brain regions. The core idea is to combine L1 regularization for sparsity with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce smoothness and compactness of the learned components (“blobs”). This extends prior work on online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) and offers a potentially scalable approach for analyzing large-scale neuroimaging datasets. The authors motivate the work by highlighting the limitations of univariate models and ROI-based analyses in fMRI, arguing that inter-subject variability contains valuable information often discarded.

The paper positions itself within a growing body of work leveraging dictionary learning for brain image analysis, differentiating itself by explicitly incorporating spatial regularization. It draws parallels to clustering approaches, but advocates for a more signal-focused model akin to PCA or ICA, constrained by sparsity and smoothness. The authors demonstrate the method on a large HCP dataset, comparing its performance against ICA (CanICA) and standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Combination of Penalties:** The combination of L1 and Sobolev penalties is a reasonable approach to simultaneously promote sparsity and spatial coherence in the learned components, potentially leading to more interpretable and biologically plausible results.
*   **Scalability:** Leveraging the online learning framework (building on Mairal et al., 2010) allows the method to scale to large datasets, a significant advantage in neuroimaging.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors evaluate the method on a publicly available, large-scale dataset (HCP), providing a basis for comparison with existing approaches.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem – handling inter-subject variability and defining ROIs – and positions the proposed method as a solution.
*   **Implementation plan:** The intention to release the implementation as part of Nilearn is a positive step toward reproducibility and broader impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Improvement:** While the method demonstrates improvements over baselines, these improvements are not always substantial, and the quantitative gains are not always convincingly demonstrated (e.g., the advantage over CanICA in behavioral prediction appears small). The authors acknowledge a limited advantage and the results need more thorough evaluation.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from issues of clarity and organization. The writing is dense, and at times confusing. The presence of draft comments (e.g., line 146) detracts from the professionalism of the presentation. Some sections feel rushed and lack sufficient detail.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper dedicates a section to hyperparameter tuning, but the discussion could be more nuanced. The optimal parameter regime remains somewhat unclear.
*   **Limited Theoretical Novelty:** While the combination of penalties is a valuable contribution, the theoretical underpinnings remain relatively standard, building directly on existing work. The theoretical analysis could be strengthened.
*    **Comparison to related works**: The related works section mentions a number of papers, but lacks a clear discussion of the key differences.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a relevant problem in neuroimaging – extracting meaningful components from large-scale brain data.
*   The proposed method offers a reasonable and potentially scalable solution.
*   The empirical results, while not dramatic, demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
*   The code release will facilitate further research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The improvements over existing methods are incremental and may not justify publication in a highly competitive venue like NIPS.
*   The clarity and presentation of the manuscript require significant improvement.
*   The theoretical contribution is limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a reasonable contribution to the field of neuroimaging, offering a potentially useful method for extracting structured components from brain data. However, the incremental nature of the improvements, combined with the clarity issues in the manuscript, raise concerns. I recommend **weak acceptance with major revisions**.

The authors should focus on:

1.  **Improving Clarity and Organization:** Rewrite the manuscript for better readability, addressing the density and confusing sections. Remove all draft comments.
2.  **Strengthening Quantitative Analysis:** Provide more rigorous statistical comparisons with baseline methods. Investigate the statistical significance of the observed improvements.
3.  **Deepening Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Provide a more nuanced discussion of hyperparameter selection and its impact on the results.
4. **Expanding related works**: Discuss the differences between this method and other related works.

Addressing these issues would significantly enhance the quality of the paper and increase its potential impact.
