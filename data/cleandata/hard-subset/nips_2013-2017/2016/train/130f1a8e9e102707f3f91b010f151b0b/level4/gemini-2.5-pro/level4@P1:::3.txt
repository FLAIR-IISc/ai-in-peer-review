This paper proposes an extension to the online sparse dictionary learning framework by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) prior. The goal is to learn dictionary atoms from fMRI data that are not only sparse but also spatially structured, forming smooth, compact regions. This method, termed Smooth-SODL, builds upon the work of Mairal et al. (2010) with the aim of producing more interpretable brain maps. The authors demonstrate their approach on a large-scale task fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project, comparing it to standard sparse dictionary learning and ICA-based methods.

The paper addresses the relevant problem of extracting meaningful spatial components from complex neuroimaging data. The proposed technical extension to include a spatial smoothness penalty within an efficient online learning framework is a notable contribution, especially given its scalability and modest computational overhead. However, the paper's main weaknesses lie in its evaluation and the justification for the method's application in a high-data context.

The central issue is that it is difficult to judge the practical impact and improvement offered by the proposed algorithm. The primary evidence for the model's superiority comes from a visual comparison of the identified brain regions in Figure 2. For a reader without deep expertise in functional neuroanatomy, the visual differences in the "clarity" and "consistency" of these blobs are subtle, making the qualitative evaluation subjective and not entirely convincing.

Furthermore, the core result—that incorporating penalties for smoothness yields smoother components—is somewhat expected. The critical question is whether this induced structure reflects a more faithful representation of the underlying neurobiology or is merely a consequence of the regularization. The paper could be strengthened by providing more objective evidence that these smoother components are more meaningful, rather than simply demonstrating that the model produces what it is designed to.

The motivation for applying this method to the Human Connectome Project dataset is also unclear. The authors' own analysis in Table 1 shows that the benefit of spatial regularization is most pronounced in low-data regimes. With a large and rich dataset like the HCP, one might expect that sufficient structure could be learned from the data alone, diminishing the need for a strong spatial prior. The manuscript would be more compelling if it demonstrated its utility on a dataset where data is scarce or noisy, making the contribution of the prior more significant.

Finally, the validation could be more rigorous. I was hoping the paper would do more to analyze how the learned model consolidates representations of brain regions across subjects. A more direct assessment of the learned regions' quality is needed, perhaps by using inter-subject consistency as a validation metric. Quantifying the reproducibility of the identified regions or their correspondence with established functional brain atlases would provide a more objective measure of the algorithm's benefit. As it stands, the validation presented seems subjective, and the overall benefit of the proposed method remains unclear.
