

This paper introduces Smooth-SODL, a novel extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging that enforces structured sparsity on brain activation patterns via a Laplacian penalty. The authors address a critical gap in brain imaging analysis: current methods for defining brain regions (ROIs) either rely on anatomical priors (with cross-modality issues) or use clustering (which fails to model spatially structured variability). Their key insight is that inter-subject variability in functional maps can be leveraged to define meaningful spatial regions, and they propose a multivariate decomposition where dictionary atoms represent structured "blobs" (spatially smooth and sparse) rather than scattered activations. This extends Mairal et al.’s online dictionary learning (SODL) by adding a Laplacian penalty (Ω_Lap) to enforce spatial structure, while maintaining computational efficiency (only 2–3× slower than SODL). The method is evaluated on 500-subject HCP task-fMRI data, demonstrating improved interpretability, stability, and predictive power for behavioral variables compared to SODL and CanICA.

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The paper makes a technically sound contribution. The Laplacian penalty effectively enforces spatial structure without sacrificing scalability—critical for large neuroimaging datasets (p ~ 10⁵ voxels, n ~ 500 subjects). The convergence analysis (Section 3.1) correctly references Bertsekas for BCD convergence under their assumptions (e.g., compact data support), addressing the non-convexity concern. Experiments are comprehensive: they compare against SODL (state-of-the-art for online learning) and CanICA (a standard ICA-based method), using both quantitative metrics (explained variance, reproducibility) and qualitative assessment of brain maps. Crucially, they demonstrate that Smooth-SODL excels in *small-data regimes* (e.g., 17 subjects), where SODL fails (2% vs. 31% explained variance), showing the regularization’s practical value. Hyperparameter analysis (Fig. 1) and implementation in Nilearn (supplementary) further strengthen validity.

*Originality*: The fusion of Laplacian regularization with online dictionary learning for *structured* brain maps is novel. While structured sparsity (e.g., TV-ℓ₁) has been used in neuroimaging [1, 25], prior work relied on slow alternating optimization. The authors explicitly position their work against this limitation: "Our approach [...] adapts the online-learning framework [16], resulting in a fast and scalable structured decomposition." This is a significant step beyond heuristic methods like [1].

*Significance*: The problem addressed—automatically defining biologically meaningful brain regions from functional variability—is highly relevant to neuroimaging. The paper shows Smooth-SODL produces interpretable, denoised maps that better capture inter-subject variability (e.g., cleaner activation blobs vs. SODL’s "thick rings" in Fig. 2a) and outperforms CanICA in predicting behavioral traits (Fig. 2c). The method’s scalability (5.6 min vs. 8.8 min for CanICA on the same data) makes it practical for large-scale studies, potentially shifting ROI definition from manual to data-driven.

*Clarity*: The paper is well-organized, with clear problem formulation (Section 2), algorithm descriptions (Algs. 1–2), and thorough experimental setup (Section 5). Minor issues exist (e.g., Section 3.1’s derivation of Eq. 7 could briefly reference the GraphNet connection [11, 13] for readers), but the presentation is otherwise polished and reproducible. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for replication.

**Weaknesses**  
*Non-convexity*: The paper correctly acknowledges the problem’s non-convexity but could better clarify that while global optimality is unattainable, the BCD approach converges to a *local optimum* (as per [6, 16]), which is standard in dictionary learning. The draft review’s claim about "existence of solution not shown" is inaccurate; convergence is established via standard optimization theory.

*Comparison depth*: The comparison with CanICA is strong, but the paper could more explicitly justify why CanICA’s lack of sparsity is problematic *beyond* interpretability (e.g., how dense atoms degrade predictive power for behavioral scores). This would strengthen the significance argument.

*Hyperparameter sensitivity*: While Fig. 1 shows a "safe range" for (γ, τ), a brief discussion of how these choices might generalize to other datasets (beyond HCP) would add robustness. The paper notes τ affects interpretability ("smaller values lead to sparser atoms"), but a quantitative trade-off (e.g., "τ = 0.1 yields 20% higher reproducibility at 5% lower explained variance") would be helpful.

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a significant contribution to neuroimaging by providing a scalable, structured approach to ROI extraction that outperforms state-of-the-art methods in interpretability, stability, and small-data performance. The technical execution is rigorous, the experiments are well-designed, and the method has clear practical impact. The minor clarity issues (e.g., minor algorithmic details) do not undermine the core contribution. I recommend **acceptance**.

*Pro*: Structured sparsity via Laplacian penalty enables biologically meaningful brain maps; scalability (only 3× slowdown vs. SODL) is critical for large datasets; strong empirical validation across multiple metrics (reproducibility, prediction, visual quality).  
*Con*: Non-convexity is acknowledged but not deeply contextualized; hyperparameter sensitivity could be more thoroughly discussed for broader applicability.  

The work aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on principled, scalable methods for real-world problems. It advances the state of the art in neuroimaging and provides a valuable tool for the community via Nilearn.
