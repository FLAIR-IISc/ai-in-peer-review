This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The method extends the online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to impose structure on the dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a large-scale brain imaging dataset, showing that it extracts structured and denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability.

The paper relates to previous work on dictionary learning and brain imaging analysis. The authors cite relevant papers on online dictionary learning, sparse coding, and brain imaging analysis, and position their work as a contribution to these areas. The paper also discusses the limitations of existing methods, such as the need for pre-smoothing in PCA/ICA-based methods and the lack of spatial structure in standard online dictionary learning.

The strengths of the paper include its clear presentation of the proposed method, its thorough evaluation on a large-scale dataset, and its comparison to state-of-the-art methods. The authors provide a detailed description of their algorithm, including the optimization procedure and the choice of hyperparameters. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in extracting structured and denoised dictionaries that capture inter-subject variability.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. One major concern is the novelty of the proposed method. The authors' extension of the online dictionary-learning framework with a Sobolev regularization term may be seen as a relatively minor contribution, and it is unclear whether this is sufficient to meet the NIPS standard. Additionally, the definition of "structure" in the context of the dictionary learning problem is not clearly explained, and it is unclear why the Sobolev regularization term is necessary or effective.

Furthermore, there are some minor errors and typos in the paper, such as the incorrect equation (1) and the unclear line 146. The language of the paper could also be refined to improve clarity and readability.

In conclusion, while the paper proposes a useful extension to the online dictionary-learning framework and demonstrates its effectiveness on a large-scale brain imaging dataset, its novelty and impact may be limited. The authors should address the concerns raised above and provide a clearer explanation of the motivation and contribution of their work.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a useful extension to the online dictionary-learning framework that incorporates spatial structure.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a large-scale brain imaging dataset.
* The paper provides a thorough evaluation and comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
