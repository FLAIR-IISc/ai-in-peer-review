

Summary: The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) that incorporates a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness in the learned dictionary atoms. This approach is motivated by the need to extract interpretable functional brain regions from fMRI data, building on previous work in structured dictionary learning for brain imaging (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2014; Abraham et al., MICCAI, 2015).

Strengths:
- The method extends the online dictionary learning framework to incorporate spatial structure with minimal computational overhead (3x slower than standard SODL), making it suitable for large-scale brain imaging applications.
- The approach is well-motivated for brain imaging, where spatial smoothness is a critical property of functional brain regions.
- The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method can extract structured and denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability across different data scales compared to state-of-the-art models.

Weaknesses:
- The improvement over simpler methods (SODL, CanICA) is not convincingly demonstrated. While the paper claims Smooth-SODL better captures inter-subject variability, the evidence provided (e.g., Figure 2) does not clearly show a significant advantage over existing methods.
- The manuscript contains several clarity issues, including draft comments (e.g., line 146) and confusing explanations of technical details, making it difficult to follow the algorithm and results.
- The connection between the proposed method and previous work on structured dictionary learning is not sufficiently clarified. The authors should more explicitly state how Smooth-SODL differs from Varoquaux et al. (2014) and other related work.
- The significance of the proposed method is not fully justified. While the authors claim the method "better captures inter-subject variability," they don't provide strong evidence that this leads to improved performance on downstream tasks beyond the basic metrics presented.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, but the experimental results don't fully support the claimed advantages. The paper would benefit from more rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods and clearer explanation of the benefits of the proposed approach.

Clarity: The manuscript is difficult to follow in several places, with unclear explanations of the algorithm and results. The presence of draft comments indicates the paper is not thoroughly polished.

Originality: The combination of online dictionary learning with spatial regularization is somewhat novel, but the paper doesn't sufficiently clarify how this differs from prior work. The authors should better articulate the novelty relative to existing structured dictionary learning approaches.

Significance: While the problem of extracting interpretable brain regions is important, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method provides significant advantages over existing approaches. The claimed improvements in interpretability and capturing inter-subject variability need stronger evidence.

Pro: Extends online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial structure with minimal computational overhead; well-motivated for brain imaging applications.

Con: Claims of superiority over simpler methods are not sufficiently demonstrated; manuscript contains clarity issues; significance of the method is not fully justified.
