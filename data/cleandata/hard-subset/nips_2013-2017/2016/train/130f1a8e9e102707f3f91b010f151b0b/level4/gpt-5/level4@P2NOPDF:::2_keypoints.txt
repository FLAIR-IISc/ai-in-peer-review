- Paper proposes an online structured dictionary-learning algorithm for neuroimaging, extending [14] with a Sobolev prior to promote spatially contiguous atoms.  
- Model and algorithms are well defined and grounded in [14], but empirical evidence for the Sobolev prior’s added value is weak.  
- Reviewer initially rated technical quality below 3, raised to 3 after rebuttal results; urges including those additional results in revision.  
- Requests explicit, literature-justified quantitative metrics (e.g., spatial contiguity, smoothness, atlas alignment, gradient/Laplacian measures, connected-component analysis) to validate “more structured” atoms.  
- Novelty: meaningful and nontrivial extension of [14] by incorporating Sobolev priors for spatial structure.  
- Potential impact: useful, theoretically motivated, and efficient online algorithm with value for neuroscience/medical imaging and related tasks (e.g., segmentation).  
- Presentation needs careful editing: several confusing passages, typos, and unresolved co-author comments reduce readability.  
- Specific clarifications requested in math: transpose in matrix decomposition (Lines 67–68), sign/scaling inconsistencies in Equations (6) and (8), and whether V denotes previous-iteration dictionary.  
- Algorithmic detail requested: explicit solution strategy for Eq. (6) (closed-form update, proximal operator, solver details, convergence criteria), at least in appendix.  
- Dataset and references: missing HCP reference (Line 73) and other grammatical/typo fixes (Lines 83, 146, 157, 169).  
- Figure 2 issues: misaligned slices across methods prevent direct comparison; align slices and support claims with quantitative spatial metrics.  
- Figure 3 issues: unclear what is being predicted, unexplained legend, missing PCA baseline in plot, and ambiguous text about which curves achieve R^2 > 0.1.  
- Revision recommendations: (1) include rebuttal results, (2) substantiate Sobolev prior benefits with quantitative, literature-aligned metrics and neuroscientific references, and (3) substantially improve clarity and correctness.
