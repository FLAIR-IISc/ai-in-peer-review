

This paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework for identifying functionally meaningful brain regions. While the topic addresses a compelling problem in neuroscience, the manuscript suffers from critical deficiencies that significantly undermine its contribution. Primarily, the work lacks theoretical analysis to establish the algorithm's effectiveness—such as convergence guarantees or performance bounds—which is essential for an algorithmic contribution. Without such analysis, it remains unclear whether the observed empirical results are attributable to the proposed method or other factors, weakening the paper's scientific rigor.  

Additionally, the manuscript exhibits severe issues in presentation and proofreading. Notably, it retains unedited reviewer comments from prior drafts (e.g., "146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v."), which should have been removed before submission. This reflects a lack of diligence in finalizing the work, a particularly concerning oversight for a venue like NIPS, where meticulous scholarship is expected. The presence of such errors suggests the authors did not conduct a thorough review of the final manuscript, further diminishing confidence in the work's quality.  

The paper's technical claims and results are also difficult to evaluate due to insufficient detail in methodological descriptions. For instance, the handling of large-scale data and the criteria for "interesting" brain regions are vaguely defined, making reproducibility and validation challenging. While the empirical results on neuroimaging datasets are presented, they are not contextualized against relevant baselines or ablation studies, leaving key questions unanswered about the method's advantages over existing approaches.  

In summary, the paper tackles an important problem but fails to deliver a rigorous, well-executed contribution. The absence of theoretical grounding, coupled with poor editorial quality, prevents the work from meeting NIPS' standards for algorithmic novelty and scholarly precision. The authors must address these fundamental issues—providing theoretical analysis, clarifying methodological details, and eliminating all proofreading errors—before the manuscript can be reconsidered for publication. As currently written, it does not advance the field sufficiently to warrant acceptance.
