This paper proposes an online algorithm for learning a structured dictionary to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals. The method extends the well-established framework of [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity in the learned dictionary atoms. The authors applied their method to empirical data, reported the effect of varying tuning parameters, and compared its performance against two alternative approaches.

**Technical Quality**
My initial assessment of the technical quality was below the acceptance threshold. However, the additional results provided in the author rebuttal were very informative, and I have raised my score accordingly.

The model is clearly defined, and the optimization algorithm, which builds upon the framework in [14], appears solid. However, the initial manuscript, perhaps due to its presentation, did not convincingly demonstrate the empirical advantages of incorporating the Sobolev prior. Specifically, in Figure 2, it was difficult to discern in what respects the proposed SSMF method outperformed the alternatives. For instance, are the spatial boundaries quantitatively smoother? Does SSMF uniquely identify certain neuroanatomically connected regions? The paper vaguely describes the SSMF results as "more structured," but it would be significantly strengthened by defining explicit, neuroscientifically justified criteria and then presenting quantitative measurements of the improvement based on these criteria.

*Added after reading the rebuttal:* The results provided in the rebuttal are very informative. I have changed my rating on technical quality from 2 to 3. I strongly encourage the authors to include these new results in their revision. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more rigorous demonstration—with both quantitative metrics and references to neuroscience literature—that the dictionary atoms learned by SSMF are indeed more structured and scientifically meaningful.

**Novelty/Originality**
The paper's primary original contribution is the extension of the framework in [14] to include Sobolev priors for encouraging spatially structured dictionary atoms. This is a notable contribution.

**Potential Impact or Usefulness**
Dictionary learning is a valuable approach for analyzing large-scale, multi-subject neuroimaging data, and the inclusion of spatial priors, such as the Sobolev prior, is theoretically well-motivated to improve results. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm to achieve this, which could be a useful addition to the toolbox for the neuroscience and medical imaging communities. The proposed method may also have broader applications, which the authors could explore, such as in image segmentation or regional sensing.

**Presentation**
The overall organization of the paper is adequate. However, the manuscript appears to have been hastily prepared, containing several confusing descriptions and typographical errors that hinder comprehension. I have listed the most significant issues below; some are minor typos, while others require more detailed explanation.

*   In the matrix decomposition equation (between lines 67 and 68), should the transpose be applied to each individual matrix $V^j$, rather than the entire group of matrices?
*   Line 73: The reference for the HCP dataset is missing.
*   Line 83: Grammatical error in "The Graph-Net penalty can imposes..."
*   Line 146: The bolded sentence appears to be a leftover co-author comment that was not removed during editing.
*   There is a sign inconsistency between the update rules in Equation (6) and Equation (8). Additionally, in the last line of Equation (6), it is unclear if $V$ refers to the dictionary from the previous iteration, as the $j$-th column is also the variable being optimized.
*   Line 157: Repetition of "a single".
*   Line 169: Typo in "th how".
*   In Section 3.3 (paragraph below Algorithm 2), it would be very helpful if the authors provided the explicit mathematical or algorithmic solution to Equation (6), even if placed in an appendix.
*   Figure 2: The results from different methods are shown on different horizontal and sagittal slices, making direct visual comparison difficult. Based on the figure as presented, I could not verify the claim in Line 222 that the SSMF results are more "structured."
*   Figure 3: This figure is difficult to interpret. It is unclear what is being predicted (e.g., is brain data, perhaps derived from the $U_i$ components, being regressed on behavioral metrics?). The legends are not explained in the text or caption. Furthermore, the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption does not appear to be plotted. I was also unable to follow the claim in Lines 224-227 regarding an R^2 value greater than 0.1, as it is not clear which curves this refers to.
