This paper studies dictionary learning applied to brain region learning. The authors propose a large-scale online dictionary learning framework augmented with a Sobolev regularization term to encourage structure in the learned dictionary.

Major comments:
1) Novelty: The method appears to be a straightforward extension of online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., JMLR 2010) with an added Sobolev regularizer. As presented, the contribution seems incremental and does not meet the NIPS standard for novelty. Please clarify what fundamentally new insights or capabilities arise from incorporating the Sobolev term beyond prior work, both theoretically and empirically.

2) Definition and motivation of “structure”: The paper uses Sobolev regularization to impose a structural constraint on the dictionary, but the notion of “structure” is not clearly defined. If by “structure” you mean sparsity, please explain why sparsity in the dictionary atoms (as opposed to the codes) should yield significant improvements for your tasks. More generally, the motivation for choosing a Sobolev penalty—what specific spatial smoothness or continuity properties it enforces, how this aligns with the expected organization of brain regions, and why this is preferable to alternative priors—should be made explicit and supported by analysis or ablations.

3) Possible error in Eq. (1): I suspect a typographical error. Should the expression read [V^1, V^2, …, V^r]^T?

4) Construction of Eq. (2): The choice of an ℓ2 norm on the codes requires justification, especially since Mairal et al. (JMLR 2010) use an ℓ1 penalty to promote sparse codes. Please explain the rationale for switching to ℓ2 (e.g., stability, scalability, domain-specific considerations), and analyze its impact on representation quality and interpretability. Comparative experiments or theoretical arguments would strengthen this choice.

5) Line 146: This line is unclear. Please clarify what is meant there and, if it refers to a definition or step in the algorithm, provide sufficient detail to make it unambiguous.

6) Writing quality: The paper’s language needs significant polishing. I found many typos and instances of awkward phrasing. A careful proofreading and, ideally, editing by a fluent English speaker are recommended.

Overall, while the application domain is interesting and large-scale training is relevant, the current submission lacks a clear and compelling justification for the proposed regularization, provides limited novelty over established online dictionary learning methods, and needs clearer definitions and explanations (especially around Eqs. (1) and (2)). Improving the clarity of the technical presentation and strengthening the motivation and empirical validation of the Sobolev term would substantially enhance the paper.
