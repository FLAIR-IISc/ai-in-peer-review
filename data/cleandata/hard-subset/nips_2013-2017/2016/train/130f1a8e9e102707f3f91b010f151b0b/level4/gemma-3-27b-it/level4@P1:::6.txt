A new regularization technique for matrix factorization is introduced, aiming to impose both sparsity and smoothness on the learned components. While a proof of solution existence is not provided, the authors present an algorithm for obtaining a solution and demonstrate experimentally that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods. The introduction of this novel regularizer within the optimization problem is a significant strength of the paper, and the experimental results highlight the potential of the proposed approach. However, as the authors acknowledge, the problem is non-convex, and the algorithm’s validity is supported solely by empirical evidence. The manuscript would also benefit from improved writing clarity; for example, the phrasing on line 146 could be more concise. Following rebuttal, I am satisfied with the authors’ response regarding the algorithm’s convergence properties, and I am willing to revise my score for Clarity and presentation from 2 to 3.

**Summary:**

The paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method to decompose brain images into structured and sparse components. Sparsity is enforced using an `l1`-norm constraint, while structure, representing piecewise smooth and compact “blobs,” is imposed via a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty. This extends previous online dictionary-learning work (Mairal et al., 2010) with a reported modest increase in runtime. The authors demonstrate on brain imaging data that their method extracts dictionaries that are more interpretable and better capture inter-subject variability compared to existing approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Regularization:** The combination of `l1`-sparsity and Laplacian regularization for dictionary learning is a valuable contribution. This approach addresses a key challenge in neuroimaging – identifying meaningful brain regions while managing variability across subjects.
*   **Scalability:** Leveraging the online learning framework from Mairal et al. (2010) ensures the method can handle large datasets, crucial for modern neuroimaging applications.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in terms of dictionary interpretability, variance explained, and predictive power. The comparison with established techniques like ICA and standard online dictionary learning provides compelling evidence of improvement.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The authors address important practical aspects, including hyperparameter tuning and initialization, providing guidance for successful application of the method.
*   **Clear Problem Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of handling inter-subject variability in neuroimaging and the limitations of existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** The non-convexity of the problem and the absence of proof of solution existence are limitations. While empirical results are strong, theoretical justification would further strengthen the work.
*   **Writing Clarity:** While generally understandable, the writing occasionally suffers from awkward phrasing and could be more concise. (e.g., line 146).
*    **Computational Cost:** Although scalable, the proposed method introduces a computational overhead compared to the baseline online dictionary learning method (a 3x increase in runtime), which might be a concern for some applications. A more detailed analysis of the computational trade-offs could be beneficial.

**Originality:**

The approach of combining `l1`-sparsity with a Laplacian penalty for dictionary learning in the context of neuroimaging appears novel. While dictionary learning and sparse coding have been widely used in this field, the specific regularization strategy presented here offers a unique way to enforce both sparsity and spatial coherence. The authors appropriately reference related work, including previous dictionary learning methods and approaches to ROI definition.

**Significance:**

The results suggest that this method could significantly improve the analysis of neuroimaging data by providing a more principled and interpretable way to define brain regions. The ability to capture inter-subject variability and predict behavioral scores indicates the potential for broader applications beyond simply denoising or dimensionality reduction. The method advances the state-of-the-art by offering a computationally feasible way to incorporate spatial structure into dictionary learning, which has been a long-standing challenge.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to a challenging problem in neuroimaging.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
*   The method is scalable and addresses a practical need in the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of theoretical guarantees regarding solution existence is a weakness.
*   The writing clarity could be improved.
*   The increased computational cost compared to the baseline method may be a limitation for some applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution and promising results. While the lack of theoretical guarantees is a concern, the compelling empirical evidence and practical relevance of the work outweigh this limitation. The authors have addressed a significant challenge in neuroimaging, and their approach has the potential to impact the field. I recommend acceptance.
