This paper investigates dictionary learning applied to the problem of brain region analysis. Specifically, the authors propose a large-scale online dictionary learning framework incorporating a Sobolev regularization term to encourage structure in the learned dictionary. 

My primary concern revolves around the novelty of this work. The approach appears to be a direct extension of existing online dictionary learning methods (e.g., Mairal et al., JMLR 2010) with the addition of Sobolev regularization. While technically sound, this extension, in my assessment, lacks the significant innovation typically expected at NIPS. The contribution feels incremental and may not fully justify publication in this venue.

A key element of the proposed method is the use of Sobolev regularization to impose structural constraints on the dictionary. However, the definition of this “structure” remains vague. If the intention is to enforce sparsity, the rationale for achieving performance gains through dictionary sparsity in this particular application is not clearly articulated. The motivation for employing Sobolev regularization, and how it specifically benefits brain region learning, needs to be more convincingly explained. A stronger connection between the regularization and the inherent properties of brain region data would significantly strengthen the paper.

I identified a potential error in Equation (1); it appears the transpose should be applied to the vector concatenation: [V<sup>1</sup>, V<sup>2</sup>,…, V<sup>r</sup>]<sup>T</sup>. 

Further clarification and justification are required for the formulation of Equation (2). Specifically, the authors should explain the choice of an l2 norm on the codes, differing from the l1 norm used in the foundational work by Mairal et al. (2010). A detailed discussion of the trade-offs involved in this design decision is necessary.

Additionally, line 146 is unclear and requires attention. 

Finally, the paper would benefit significantly from careful editing. I encountered numerous typos and instances of awkward phrasing that hinder clarity. Refining the language throughout the manuscript is crucial for effective communication of the research.
