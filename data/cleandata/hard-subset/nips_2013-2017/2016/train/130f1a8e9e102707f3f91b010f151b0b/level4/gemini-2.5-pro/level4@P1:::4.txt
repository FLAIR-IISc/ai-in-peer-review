This paper proposes a method called Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL) to identify functional components in fMRI data. The approach extends the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage spatial smoothness, in addition to an L1-norm constraint for sparsity. The goal is to learn a dictionary of components that are not only sparse but also spatially coherent, forming smooth "blobs" that are more neuroscientifically interpretable. The online algorithm is designed to scale to the massive datasets increasingly common in neuroimaging.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The primary strength of this paper is its methodological contribution. The integration of a spatial smoothness prior into a scalable, online dictionary learning algorithm is an interesting and valuable idea. It addresses a clear need in the neuroimaging community for methods that can discover structured functional patterns from very large datasets, where traditional batch optimization methods would be computationally infeasible. The proposed algorithm appears technically sound, building upon a well-established framework and providing a clear path to optimization.

However, the paper suffers from two major weaknesses that currently limit its impact.

First, the manuscript's clarity and presentation are poor. The writing is often dense and difficult to follow, making the paper a tedious and sometimes confusing read. It appears to have been prepared in a hurry, which is strongly suggested by the presence of leftover draft comments in the text (e.g., the note on line 146 to "refer to [16] for the details"). This lack of polish significantly detracts from the paper's overall quality and hinders the reader's ability to fully appreciate the technical contributions.

Second, the empirical evaluation does not sufficiently demonstrate a significant advantage over simpler methods. While the components learned by Smooth-SODL are qualitatively smoother (Figure 2a), the quantitative improvements are not compelling. For instance, in the behavioral prediction task (Figure 2c), the proposed model is outperformed by the standard CanICA baseline. The authors highlight that the method's main benefit is in the small-to-medium data regime (Table 1), where it substantially outperforms standard SODL in explained variance. This argument is somewhat at odds with the primary motivation for an *online* algorithm, which is its scalability to *large-scale* data. In the large-data regime, where the online framework is most relevant, the performance gap between Smooth-SODL and SODL narrows considerably, making the justification for the added model complexity less convincing.

In summary, while the proposed approach is interesting, the paper in its current form is not ready for publication. The weaknesses in presentation and the limited empirical justification for the method's superiority undermine its contributions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses the important and relevant problem of scalable, data-driven discovery of structured brain regions.
*   The method is a technically sound and non-trivial extension of a widely-used online learning algorithm, adapting it for structured neuroimaging data.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The manuscript is poorly written and appears rushed, which severely impacts its clarity and readability.
*   The experimental results fail to provide strong evidence that the proposed method offers a substantial improvement over existing baselines, especially in the large-scale settings for which it is designed. The justification for the additional modeling complexity is therefore weak.
