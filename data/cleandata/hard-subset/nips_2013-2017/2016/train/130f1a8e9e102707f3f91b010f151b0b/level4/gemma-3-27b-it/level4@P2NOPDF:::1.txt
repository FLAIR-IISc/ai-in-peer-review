This paper introduces a large-scale online dictionary learning framework applied to the problem of identifying significant brain regions. The research topic is compelling and potentially impactful. However, the manuscript suffers from a lack of theoretical justification for the proposed approach, leaving the effectiveness of the algorithm unproven. 

Furthermore, the presentation and writing quality raise concerns. The paper appears to have undergone insufficient revision prior to submission, containing internal notes and unresolved questions – for instance, the comment “146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v” remains present in the text. This detracts from the clarity and professionalism of the work.

To strengthen this submission, the authors should prioritize the following:

* **Theoretical Analysis:** A formal analysis demonstrating the properties and benefits of the online dictionary learning approach within the context of brain region identification is crucial. This could include convergence guarantees, bounds on reconstruction error, or a discussion of the method's statistical power. Without such analysis, it is difficult to assess the validity and generalizability of the findings.
* **Clarity and Polish:** A thorough revision is necessary to eliminate all internal comments, clarify ambiguous statements, and ensure consistent notation. The presence of unresolved questions within the submitted manuscript suggests a lack of careful attention to detail. Specific issues, like the noted confusion regarding ‘V’ and ‘v’, must be addressed.
* **Justification of Design Choices:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of key design choices within the online dictionary learning framework. Why was this specific online learning strategy chosen? How do the parameters influence performance? Addressing these questions will help readers understand the rationale behind the approach.
* **Comparison to Baselines:** While the paper demonstrates application to brain imaging data, a more rigorous comparison to established methods for identifying brain regions would strengthen the claims.  This comparison should include both traditional techniques and relevant dictionary learning approaches, providing a clear benchmark for assessing the proposed method’s performance.
* **Discussion of Limitations:**  A frank discussion of the limitations of the proposed method is valuable. This could include considerations regarding the computational cost, sensitivity to parameter settings, or assumptions about the underlying data distribution.



In its current form, the paper presents an interesting idea but lacks the theoretical grounding and polish necessary for publication at a competitive venue like NeurIPS. Addressing the aforementioned concerns would significantly improve the manuscript's quality and impact.
