**Summary:** The paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method that incorporates both sparsity and structural constraints (smoothness) for decomposing brain images. Sparsity is enforced via an L1-norm penalty, while smoothness is achieved through a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty, resulting in dictionary atoms resembling spatially localized "blobs." This extends the online dictionary learning work of Mairal et al. (2010) with a modest increase in runtime. The authors demonstrate improved interpretability and capture of inter-subject variability on brain imaging data compared to existing methods.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach, building upon established online dictionary learning techniques. The theoretical motivation for combining L1 and Laplacian penalties is clear, and the algorithm appears logically structured. Claims are supported by experimental results, though a theoretical analysis of convergence properties in the non-convex setting would strengthen the work. The authors acknowledge the limitations of relying solely on empirical validation. The inclusion of a comparison to CanICA is valuable, as it represents a strong baseline.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized, but the writing could be more concise. The notation is standard, but some sections are dense and require careful reading. Specific instances like line 146 could be improved for clarity. While the paper provides sufficient information for an expert to understand the approach, reproducing the results might require further clarification regarding implementation details.

**Originality:** The combination of L1 and Laplacian penalties within an online dictionary learning framework for brain image decomposition appears novel. While both sparsity and smoothness have been individually explored in related work (e.g., Baldassarre et al., 2013; Varol & Davatzikos, 2014), their simultaneous application within this online learning setting constitutes a significant contribution. The paper adequately references relevant work, including Mairal et al. (2010) and other pertinent publications in the field.

**Significance:** The proposed method addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging – handling inter-subject variability and defining meaningful brain regions. The ability to extract structured and interpretable dictionary atoms has the potential to improve the analysis of functional brain imaging data. The scalability of the online learning approach is a significant advantage for large datasets. The demonstration of improved prediction of behavioral variables further supports the significance of the work.
