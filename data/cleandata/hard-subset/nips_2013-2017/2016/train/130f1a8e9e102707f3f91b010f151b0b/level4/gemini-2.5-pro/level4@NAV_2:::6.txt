This paper proposes an online dictionary learning method designed to decompose brain images into components that are both sparse and spatially structured. The work extends the well-known online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages the atoms to be spatially smooth and form contiguous "blobs," which is a desirable property for identifying neuro-anatomically plausible brain regions. This approach combines the scalability of online learning with the structured priors previously seen in slower, batch-optimization methods, aiming to provide an efficient tool for discovering data-driven brain atlases from large-scale neuroimaging datasets.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of deriving interpretable functional brain regions from large cohorts. The proposed method cleverly bridges a gap between highly scalable but unstructured decomposition methods (like standard online dictionary learning) and structured methods that are often too computationally intensive for massive datasets. Integrating the spatial smoothness prior into the online framework with a minimal computational overhead is a valuable and novel contribution.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed model, Smooth-SODL, is well-formulated. The optimization procedure, which adapts the Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) scheme from prior work, is technically sound. The authors correctly analyze the dictionary update sub-problem, identify an efficient solver (FISTA), and provide a reasonable analysis of its computational complexity. The justification for the overall algorithm's convergence, by verifying the assumptions of the foundational work by Mairal et al., is appropriate.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against relevant and widely-used baselines (SODL and CanICA) on a large, public fMRI dataset (HCP). The use of multiple quantitative (explained variance, stability, predictive performance) and qualitative (visual inspection of components) metrics provides strong evidence for the benefits of the proposed approach. The learning curve analysis (Table 1), showing the method's superior performance in low-to-medium data regimes, is particularly compelling.

**Weaknesses (Areas for improvement):**

*   **Clarity of Derivations:** While the paper is generally clear, the derivation of the dictionary update step in Equation (7) is quite compressed and relies heavily on referencing the original Mairal et al. paper. A slightly more detailed walkthrough of this step would improve the paper's self-containedness and make it easier for readers to follow the technical details of the algorithm.
*   **Discussion of Non-Convexity:** The authors correctly note that the joint optimization problem is non-convex. They provide good empirical evidence that random initializations produce stable results. However, the discussion could be strengthened by explicitly stating that the theoretical convergence guarantees are for a stationary point, which is standard for this class of algorithms. This is a minor point of clarification rather than a fundamental weakness.
*   **Hyper-parameter Heuristics:** The paper presents a data-adaptive choice for the code regularization parameter α (αt ∼ t−1/2), which is reported to work well. While pragmatic, this choice is presented without theoretical motivation. A brief justification, perhaps by relating it to schedules used in stochastic approximation, would add to the technical depth of this practical consideration.
