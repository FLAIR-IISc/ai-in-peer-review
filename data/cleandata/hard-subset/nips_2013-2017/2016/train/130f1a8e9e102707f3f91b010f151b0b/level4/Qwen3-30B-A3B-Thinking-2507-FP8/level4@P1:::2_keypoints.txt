- Proposes online dictionary learning extended with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to learn sparse, spatially contiguous brain regions; evaluated on HCP task fMRI vs SODL and CanICA.

- Model is clearly defined and builds logically on Mairal et al. (2010); algorithmic approach is sound.

- Initial empirical demonstration of the Sobolev prior’s advantages was weak, lacking quantitative metrics for spatial contiguity.

- After the authors’ rebuttal provided additional quantitative evidence, the reviewer raised the technical quality rating from 2 to 3.

- Reviewer urges inclusion of concrete quantitative metrics (e.g., boundary smoothness, connectivity measures) tied to neuroscience criteria in the final paper.

- Novelty: meaningful extension of online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial structure, distinct from prior slow alternating-optimization or spatially unconstrained methods.

- Potential impact: an efficient, scalable tool for brain region extraction on large datasets (e.g., HCP) and extensible to other medical imaging tasks.

- Computational performance reported: 3× slower than SODL but 1.6× faster than CanICA, with hyperparameter sensitivity analysis and validation across dataset sizes.

- Results claim better capture of inter-subject variability supported by stability metrics (mean atom correlation: Smooth-SODL=0.4 vs SODL=0.1, CanICA=0.1).

- Multiple presentation issues: typos, incorrect transpose placement on V^j, inconsistent equation signs, an unedited co-author comment, and redundant phrasing.

- Figures need revision: Figure 2 mixes anatomical slices preventing direct comparison and lacks quantitative support for “more structured” atoms.

- Figure 3 is unclear about prediction targets and the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption is not shown.

- Section 3.3 should explicitly provide the mathematical solution to Equation (6) (at least in the appendix); ensure consistent mathematical notation throughout.

- Overall recommendation: accept with revisions to fix presentation, clarify algorithmic/mathematical details, improve figures, and add explicit quantitative demonstrations of spatial-structure improvement.
