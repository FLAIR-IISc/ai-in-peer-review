This paper proposes a method for learning sparse and spatially structured components from brain imaging data, framing the problem as an online dictionary learning task. The primary contribution is the extension of the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) to include a Laplacian penalty (a Sobolev-type prior) on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages spatial smoothness, resulting in components that are blob-like and more interpretable as functional brain regions. This is combined with a standard l1-norm constraint on the atoms to enforce sparsity. The authors present an online algorithm based on Block Coordinate Descent (BCD) to solve this optimization problem, demonstrating that the additional smoothness penalty only adds a minor computational overhead compared to the unstructured baseline. The method, termed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), is evaluated on a large-scale fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project.

### Strengths

1.  **Relevance and Significance:** The problem of identifying meaningful functional regions of interest (ROIs) from neuroimaging data in a scalable, data-driven manner is highly significant. The proposed online approach is well-suited for the massive datasets that are now common in the field, such as the HCP dataset used in the experiments.

2.  **Novelty:** While sparse dictionary learning and structured penalties are not new to neuroimaging, the integration of a spatial smoothness prior into a fast *online* learning framework is a novel and practical contribution. It effectively bridges the gap between computationally efficient but unstructured methods like SODL [16] and more structured but slower batch methods that rely on penalties like Total Variation [1, 25].

3.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithmic solution is a well-reasoned extension of the BCD framework from Mairal et al. [16]. The derivation of the dictionary update step as a GraphNet-like denoising problem is correct, and the authors provide sound reasoning for the convergence of the overall algorithm by showing it satisfies the necessary assumptions of the original framework.

4.  **Strong Performance in Low-Data Regimes:** A key strength, demonstrated in Table 1, is the method's ability to capture meaningful variance in low-data regimes. The reported 14-fold improvement in explained variance over the unstructured SODL when using only a small number of subjects highlights the value of the spatial regularization as an effective prior when data is scarce.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript's clarity is a major concern and significantly detracts from the quality of the work. It appears to have been written in a rush, with several signs of incomplete editing.
    *   Equation (7) contains a placeholder comment, "refer to [16] for the details," which is inappropriate for a final submission and makes the derivation difficult to follow.
    *   The writing is often dense and convoluted, making the paper a tedious read.
    *   The reference list is incompletely formatted (e.g., missing years, venues), which suggests a lack of care in the preparation of the manuscript.

2.  **Insufficiently Supported Claims of Superiority:** The experimental results present a mixed picture, and the claims that the proposed method is "better" than state-of-the-art models are not consistently supported across all evaluated metrics.
    *   **Predictive Performance:** In the behavioral prediction task (Figure 2c), the proposed Smooth-SODL is outperformed by the simpler CanICA baseline on test data. While it improves upon SODL, its failure to surpass a standard, dense decomposition method on a key downstream task is a significant weakness that is not discussed. Why does a model that supposedly captures inter-subject variability better lead to worse predictive accuracy?
    *   **Explained Variance:** While Smooth-SODL improves explained variance over SODL, it is still substantially lower than CanICA (Figure 2b). The paper emphasizes the improved interpretability of its components (Figure 2a), which is valid, but it should more explicitly frame this as a trade-off against raw explanatory power rather than an unambiguous improvement.
    *   **Qualitative Comparison:** The qualitative improvement in atom structure over SODL is clear. However, the visual difference compared to the hard-thresholded CanICA (tCanICA) is less pronounced. The argument for the superiority of Smooth-SODL would be stronger if it demonstrated a clear advantage that thresholding a dense map cannot achieve.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Major Revisions for Clarity:** The paper requires a thorough revision to improve readability. The authors should strive for clearer, more direct language, and ensure that all parts of the paper, including equations and references, are complete and professionally presented.
*   **Nuance the Contributions:** The authors should tone down the claims of general superiority. The contribution is more accurately framed as a new method that offers a favorable trade-off between computational scalability, component interpretability, and model performance, particularly in data-limited settings. The discussion should honestly address the experiments where the method does not outperform baselines (e.g., the prediction task) and speculate on the reasons.
*   **Strengthen the Experimental Analysis:** The analysis of the learning curves in Table 1 is a strong point and should perhaps be given more prominence in the main text. The discussion of Figure 2c is currently insufficient; a deeper analysis of why smoother components might hurt prediction could add significant value (e.g., does smoothing remove fine-grained but predictive spatial details?).
*   **Justification for Parameter Choices:** While the paper provides a study on hyper-parameter selection (Figure 1), the final choices for the experiments (e.g., `γ = 10^3` or `10^4`) and the justification for these specific values could be made clearer.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and practical problem in neuroimaging with a novel approach.
*   The integration of a smoothness prior into an online dictionary learning framework is a valuable contribution.
*   The method is technically sound and scalable.
*   Demonstrates a clear and substantial benefit in low-data regimes, which is an important use case.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is poorly written and appears rushed, with incomplete sections and references, hindering understanding and reflecting a lack of polish.
*   The experimental evidence for the method's superiority is mixed. The method is outperformed by a simpler baseline in a key prediction task, a weakness that the authors do not address.
*   The claims in the abstract and conclusion are not fully supported by the full range of experimental results.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting idea with a sound technical basis. However, its current state, particularly regarding clarity and the nuanced interpretation of the results, is below the standard expected for NIPS. A major revision addressing the clarity and providing a more balanced discussion of the experimental outcomes is necessary.
