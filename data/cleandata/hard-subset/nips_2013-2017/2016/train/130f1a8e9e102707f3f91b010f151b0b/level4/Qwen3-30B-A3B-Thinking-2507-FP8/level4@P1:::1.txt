

This paper introduces Smooth-SODL, a structured sparse online dictionary learning framework for decomposing brain imaging data into spatially coherent, interpretable components. The method extends Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms, thereby extracting brain regions that better capture inter-subject variability than existing approaches (e.g., SODL, CanICA). The authors demonstrate on Human Connectome Project (HCP) data that their method produces more structured, denoised dictionaries with comparable runtime (3× SODL) while significantly improving interpretability and stability across small-to-large datasets. Key strengths include a well-motivated problem formulation addressing the limitations of ROI-based neuroimaging analysis, thorough empirical validation on a large-scale public dataset, and clear demonstration of how spatial regularization improves both qualitative (e.g., blob-like structures) and quantitative metrics (e.g., reproducibility, behavioral prediction). The implementation is planned for release via Nilearn, enhancing reproducibility.

However, the paper has two critical weaknesses. First, it provides no theoretical analysis to justify the algorithm’s convergence or statistical properties. While the authors reference prior work (e.g., Mairal et al., 2010) and state the algorithm converges, they omit key theoretical guarantees (e.g., convergence rates, error bounds) expected for a NIPS submission. This omission is particularly concerning given the paper’s focus on a novel optimization framework. Second, the manuscript contains unresolved editorial issues that undermine professionalism. For instance, a placeholder comment ("146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v.") remains in the text, indicating insufficient proofreading. Such errors suggest the authors did not carefully revise the draft before submission, which is inconsistent with NIPS standards for technical rigor.

The paper’s empirical contributions are valuable, but the absence of theoretical grounding and poor editorial quality prevent it from meeting NIPS’s bar for a complete scientific contribution. The authors should address these gaps before reconsideration. I recommend rejection due to insufficient theoretical analysis and unresolved writing issues.
