This paper provides an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals by extending a well-established framework with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms.

The main idea of the paper is to propose a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The authors impose sparsity via an `1-norm constraint and structure via a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty. The proposed method is an extension of the online dictionary-learning work of Mairal et al. (2010) and is shown to scale to arbitrarily sized datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of defining regions of interest (ROIs) in neuro-imaging is clearly explained. The authors provide a thorough review of related work and clearly explain the limitations of existing methods. The proposed method is described in detail, and the optimization problem is clearly formulated.

The experimental results are promising, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on a large public dataset. The results show that the proposed method extracts structured and denoised dictionaries that are more interpretable and better capture inter-subject variability in small, medium, and large-scale regimes.

However, the paper could be improved in several ways. The presentation is sometimes confusing, and there are several typos and grammatical errors throughout the paper. The authors could also provide more quantitative measurements of the improvement of their method over existing methods. Additionally, the results could be more clearly presented, and the figures could be improved for better comparison.

In terms of technical quality, I would rate the paper as a 3. The model definition is clear, and the algorithms are solid. However, the presentation could be improved, and the results could be more clearly presented.

In terms of novelty/originality, I would rate the paper as a 4. The extension of the online dictionary-learning framework with a Sobolev prior is a notable original contribution.

In terms of potential impact or usefulness, I would rate the paper as a 4. Dictionary learning is a useful approach for explaining neuroimaging data across a large group of subjects, and introducing Sobolev prior or similar spatial priors should improve the results in theory. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm for doing so and could add a good tool to the toolbox of the neuroscience/medical imaging community.

In terms of presentation, I would rate the paper as a 2. The general organization of the paper is okay, but the paper appears not to be a well-edited manuscript. There are confusing descriptions and typos that make it harder to understand the contents.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper provides a novel extension of the online dictionary-learning framework with a Sobolev prior.
* The proposed method is shown to be effective on a large public dataset.
* The results are promising, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method in capturing inter-subject variability.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The presentation could be improved, and the results could be more clearly presented.
* The paper could benefit from more quantitative measurements of the improvement of the proposed method over existing methods.
* There are several typos and grammatical errors throughout the paper.
