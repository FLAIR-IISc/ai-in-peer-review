This paper introduces an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals. The approach extends the established framework of [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior, designed to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate their method on empirical neuroimaging data, analyze the impact of tuning parameters, and compare its performance against two existing techniques.

**Technical Quality:** I initially assessed the technical quality as marginally below a 3, but have revised my rating to a 3 following the rebuttal. The model definition is clear, and the algorithmic framework builds logically upon [14], appearing sound in its core implementation. However, the presentation initially obscured a clear demonstration of the empirical benefits of the Sobolev prior. Specifically, Figure 2 lacked sufficient detail to convincingly illustrate the superiority of the SSMF method. It was unclear *how* SSMF outperformed the alternatives – were spatial boundaries smoother quantitatively? Did SSMF uniquely identify known connected regions?  Simply stating the results are “more structured” is insufficient.  The authors should explicitly define criteria, grounded in neuroscience literature, for assessing structure, and provide quantitative measurements demonstrating improvement according to these criteria. The results included in the rebuttal were very informative, and I strongly encourage their inclusion in the revised manuscript. Furthermore, the revision should provide more detailed quantitative analysis demonstrating the structural properties of the learned atoms and connect these properties to neuroscientific interpretations.

**Novelty/Originality:** The paper presents a noteworthy contribution through its extension of [14] via the application of Sobolev priors to encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms. This is a sensible and potentially impactful modification.

**Potential Impact or Usefulness:** Dictionary learning is a valuable tool for analyzing neuroimaging data across large subject groups, and the theoretical benefits of incorporating spatial priors like the Sobolev prior are evident. This paper offers an efficient online algorithm for achieving this, potentially adding a useful method to the toolkit of the neuroscience and medical imaging communities.  Exploring applications beyond the current scope, such as image segmentation or regional sensing, could further broaden the impact of this work.

**Presentation:** The overall organization of the paper is adequate, but the manuscript requires significant editing. Several confusing descriptions and typos hindered understanding. I detail specific issues below:

*   **Equation (6) & (8) Discrepancy:** The final lines of Equations (6) and (8) present conflicting signs:  $V^j +At[j,j]^{-1}(VAt^j -Bt^j)$ versus $At[j,j] V^j -(VAt^j - Bt^j)$. Clarification is needed.
*   **Equation (6) Variable Definition:** In the last line of Equation (6), it is assumed that *V* refers to the dictionary from the previous iteration; therefore, the jth column is *not* the variable *v* being optimized. This assumption should be explicitly stated.
*   **Typographical Errors:** Several typographical errors exist, including "a single, a single" (Line 157) and "We did an extensive study of th how" (Line 169).
*   **Matrix Decomposition (Lines 67-68):**  It’s unclear whether the transpose symbol applies to each individual $V^j$ (j = 1,..., r) or only to the entire group.
*   **HCP Dataset Reference (Line 73):** The reference for the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset is missing.
*   **Grammatical Error (Line 83):** “The Graph-Net penalty can imposes…” should be corrected to “The Graph-Net penalty can impose…”
*   **Unclear Sentence (Line 146):** The bold sentence appears to be a co-author’s comment and should be removed or integrated properly.
*   **Algorithm 2 Solution (Section 3.3):** In the paragraph following Algorithm 2 (near Line 147), providing the mathematical or algorithmic solution to Equation (6), even in the appendix, would significantly improve clarity.
*   **Figure 2 Comparison:** The presentation of results in Figure 2, across different horizontal and sagittal slices, made direct comparison between methods difficult.  The claim in Line 222 that SSMF results are “more structured” is not supported by the figure as presented.
*   **Figure 3 Clarity:** Figure 3 lacks clarity. It is unclear what is being predicted (e.g., regression of brain data onto behavioral metrics). If so, is this based on the $U_i$'s? The legends are missing from both the figure itself and the caption. The PCA baseline mentioned in the caption is also absent. The description in Lines 224-227, claiming R<sup>2</sup> values greater than 0.1, is difficult to follow without knowing which curves are being referenced.



In summary, this paper presents a solid technical contribution with potential for significant impact. However, substantial improvements to the presentation and a more rigorous demonstration of the benefits of the proposed approach, particularly with quantitative measurements tied to neuroscientific principles, are needed. The inclusion of the results presented in the rebuttal is essential for a successful revision.
