The authors extend dictionary learning models to incorporate a Sobolev prior. This is an interesting problem, but it is hard to judge the impact or improvement of this newly proposed algorithm. 

The main results figure relies on visually comparing the clarity/consistency of identified blobs on the brain, but this is not clear to an untrained eye. A more detailed explanation of the visual differences between the methods and their significance would be beneficial. 

Also, in my experience with this type of decomposition, just as important as the algorithm (if not more) is the data. By incorporating penalties for smoothness and spatial consistency, it isn't that surprising that you get smoother blobs. Perhaps this is more useful to isolate a region with little data, but with the vast amount of data accessible with the Human Connectome Project, this doesn't seem to be the issue.

I was hoping that this paper would do more to consolidate the differences of brain regions across subjects, and perhaps to use inter-subject differences/consistency as validation of the performance. The authors do provide some quantitative metrics, such as explained variance and stability of the dictionary atoms, but these metrics are not thoroughly discussed. 

Validation presented here seems very subjective and it is not clear the benefit. To strengthen the paper, the authors could provide more objective and quantitative comparisons between their method and existing approaches, and further discuss the implications of their results.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their proposed algorithm. However, the evaluation of the method is somewhat limited, and the authors could benefit from more thorough comparisons with existing approaches.

Clarity: The paper is well-written, but some parts could be improved for clarity. For example, the authors could provide more details on the visual differences between the methods and their significance.

Originality: The incorporation of a Sobolev prior into dictionary learning is an interesting and novel approach. However, the impact and improvement of this approach are not thoroughly demonstrated.

Significance: The paper presents a new method for dictionary learning, but the significance of the results is not clearly demonstrated. The authors could benefit from more thorough discussions of the implications of their results and more objective comparisons with existing approaches.
