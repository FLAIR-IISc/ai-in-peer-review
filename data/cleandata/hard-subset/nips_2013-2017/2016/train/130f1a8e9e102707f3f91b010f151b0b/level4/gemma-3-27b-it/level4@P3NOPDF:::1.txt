## Review of NIPS_2016 Submission: [Paper Title - *Please Insert Here*]

This paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework for identifying potentially interesting brain regions from neuroimaging data. The topic is inherently appealing, given the potential for advancing our understanding of brain function and the challenges associated with analyzing high-dimensional neural datasets. However, despite the promise of the approach, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in both theoretical justification and presentation.

A primary concern is the complete lack of theoretical analysis supporting the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. While the empirical results demonstrate some interesting findings, the authors do not provide any guarantees or insights into *why* the method should work. What properties of the data or the algorithm itself lead to the identification of meaningful brain regions? Without a theoretical grounding, it is difficult to assess the generalizability of the results or to understand the limitations of the approach. Establishing theoretical properties, even under simplifying assumptions, would significantly strengthen the contribution.

Beyond the theoretical shortcomings, the paper exhibits a concerning lack of polish in its writing and presentation. Numerous instances of unclear phrasing and internal inconsistencies detract from the reader’s understanding. Most notably, comments clearly intended for internal communication – such as “146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v.” – remain within the submitted manuscript. This suggests a lack of careful review prior to submission and raises questions about the rigor with which the work was conducted. 

Further specific points detract from the overall quality:

* **Clarity of Methodology:** The description of the online dictionary learning process, while presented, could benefit from greater clarity. A more detailed explanation of the update rules and the handling of large datasets would be helpful. What specific optimizations are employed to make the online learning scalable, and what are their computational costs?
* **Baseline Comparisons:** The empirical evaluation would be considerably strengthened by comparison to more established and widely-used methods for feature selection or dimensionality reduction in neuroimaging. Simply demonstrating performance on a specific dataset is insufficient; the authors need to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing techniques.
* **Evaluation Metrics:** The choice of evaluation metrics could be better justified. Are these metrics appropriate for the specific task of identifying “interesting” brain regions? Are there alternative metrics that might provide a more comprehensive assessment of the method’s performance? A discussion of the limitations of the chosen metrics is also warranted.
* **Biological Interpretability:** While the authors identify regions of interest, the biological interpretation of these findings is limited. A more detailed discussion connecting the identified regions to known brain function would increase the impact of the work. It's not enough to *find* regions; demonstrating *why* those regions are interesting from a neuroscientific perspective is crucial.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The paper does not address the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters. How robust are the identified brain regions to variations in these parameters? A sensitivity analysis would provide valuable insights into the stability and reliability of the method.

In summary, while the core idea of applying large-scale online dictionary learning to brain imaging data is promising, this paper falls short of making a compelling contribution. The absence of theoretical analysis, combined with the numerous issues in presentation and a lack of thorough empirical evaluation, significantly weaken the work. 

**Recommendation:** Reject. Substantial revisions, including a thorough theoretical analysis, improved writing clarity, and more robust empirical validation, would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors should address the specific points raised above and demonstrate a commitment to producing a rigorously evaluated and well-presented manuscript.
