- Proposes online dictionary learning with a Sobolev (Laplacian) prior to produce spatially smooth, compact, sparse atoms.  
- Applied to HCP task fMRI and claims cleaner, more structured components than sparse online dictionary learning and PCA/ICA at modest computational cost.  
- Impact is hard to assess because results rely heavily on subjective visual inspection of components.  
- Smoother components are unsurprising given the explicit smoothness penalty; data characteristics may drive outcomes.  
- With large HCP sample sizes, the benefit of strong spatial priors is unclear compared to low-data regimes.  
- Lacks principled treatment and validation of inter-subject differences and consolidation across subjects.  
- Validation is largely qualitative; practical benefits of the new prior remain unclear.  
- Suggests adding quantitative interpretability/stability metrics (e.g., split-half reproducibility, atom matching correlations, CIs).  
- Recommends explicitly evaluating inter-subject consistency and alignment across subjects or resampled cohorts.  
- Advises ablations over Laplacian weight (γ) and sparsity (τ) to assess reconstruction vs interpretability trade-offs and generalization.  
- Proposes demonstrating benefits beyond smoother blobs (e.g., improved behavioral prediction or more reliable ROI definitions with statistical tests).  
- If advantage is for limited-data settings, run controlled experiments varying sample size and justify need for spatial regularization at HCP scale.  
- Overall: idea is appealing and timely but requires stronger quantitative validation—especially on inter-subject reproducibility—to clarify contribution and impact.
