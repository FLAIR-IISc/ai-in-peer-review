Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning framework for neuroimaging that augments the sparse online dictionary learning of Mairal et al. (2010) with a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty on the dictionary atoms to encourage spatially smooth, compact “blob-like” components. At training time, codes are obtained via ridge regression, and dictionary atoms are updated by block-coordinate descent with an inner proximal step (e.g., FISTA) to handle the Laplacian term under an l1/simplex constraint. The method is scalable (mini-batches) and aims to learn interpretable, spatially localized population components. Experiments on HCP task fMRI Z-maps compare Smooth-SODL to vanilla SODL and CanICA, reporting explained variance, qualitative structure, stability/reproducibility, and prediction of behavioral scores.

Relative to prior work at NIPS and elsewhere, the contribution is an efficient online formulation that integrates spatial structure, bridging unstructured online dictionary learning [Mairal et al., 2010] and structured penalties used in slower batch methods (e.g., TV-l1/GraphNet variants used in neuroimaging).

Strengths
- Clear modeling idea: combine sparsity constraints on atoms with a Laplacian penalty to promote spatially coherent, compact regions.
- Sound optimization scheme: block-coordinate descent with a simple inner proximal solver; convergence follows the SODL framework.
- Practical scalability: mini-batch coding, small additional computational overhead (≈2–3× SODL), and reasonable wall times on a single CPU.
- Empirical scope: large HCP dataset; comparisons to two widely used baselines (SODL and CanICA); discussion of hyperparameter effects; preliminary stability analysis; learning curves (in rebuttal).
- Potential impact: learning data-driven, spatially coherent ROIs that capture inter-subject variability is valuable for atlas building, dimensionality reduction, and downstream prediction.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Empirical advantage of the Laplacian prior is not quantified rigorously. Beyond “more structured” visuals, please add quantitative structure metrics for atoms: e.g., Laplacian energy vTΔv, total variation, number of connected components, compactness (perimeter^2/area), spatial autocorrelation (Moran’s I), blob size distribution, and proportion of energy within top connected component. Report these measures for Smooth-SODL vs SODL/CanICA (with/without thresholding), ideally with split-half CIs.
- Reproducibility/stability: you report split-half atom correlations (e.g., ~0.4 for Smooth-SODL vs ~0.1–0.2 for SODL/CanICA). Please define precisely the matching criterion (Hungarian algorithm on absolute correlations?), the stability metric, and provide confidence intervals across splits.
- Behavioral prediction: clarify the predictive pipeline. Are features the codes U_i inferred by ridge from held-out X? What regressors were used (ridge/elastic net), how were hyperparameters tuned, and were confounds controlled? Include PCA baseline and RAW consistently in plots and text; ensure train/test separation and report variability.
- Baselines: consider adding a structured baseline such as TV-l1 or GraphNet decompositions (even on a subset) to isolate the value of Laplacian vs no spatial prior; alternatively, cite strong evidence from prior work and explain trade-offs.
- Hyperparameters: you recommend α_t ∝ t^(-1/2) and discuss γ scaling. Please report the chosen γ, τ ranges and selection protocol (e.g., K-fold CV on explained variance), and show sensitivity plots or learning curves (as in rebuttal) in the main text.

Clarity
- Improve figure comparability: show the same anatomical slices and thresholds across methods. If thresholding is used (e.g., tCanICA), show both unthresholded and thresholded maps with aligned thresholds.
- Provide the explicit proximal update used in Alg. 2 (closed-form or FISTA steps), even in the appendix, and specify the projection onto C (simplex or l1-ball) used in experiments.
- Notation/consistency: ensure the code penalty (ridge vs l1) matches the algorithm used in experiments; clarify whether the constraint set is the simplex Sp(τ) throughout.

Originality
- The integration of a Laplacian penalty within an online dictionary-learning framework with efficient updates is a meaningful incremental contribution. The connection to GraphNet-style penalties is appropriate; positioning relative to TV-l1 and structured sparse PCA/ICA is clear.

Significance
- If the method yields more reproducible, compact, and interpretable atoms at modest computational cost, it can be impactful for population neuroimaging, atlas learning, and as a preprocessing step for prediction. Broader applications (segmentation, regional sensing) are plausible and worth a brief discussion.

Pros
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated extension of online dictionary learning with spatial structure.
- Scalable algorithm with modest overhead and practical convergence.
- Promising qualitative structure and improved stability; useful discussion of hyperparameters.
- Rebuttal adds informative learning curves and additional empirical evidence.

Cons
- Limited quantitative evidence that atoms are “more structured” beyond visuals; needs concrete structure metrics.
- Some clarity/notation issues and figure comparability hinder interpretation.
- Baselines could be broadened to include a structured (TV/GraphNet) decomposition.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. With the additional quantitative analyses shown in the rebuttal and clearer evidence that the Laplacian prior yields more compact, reproducible, and interpretable atoms (supported by explicit metrics and clearer figures), this would be a solid contribution. As it stands, I lean toward weak accept contingent on incorporating these analyses and clarifications. Releasing code (e.g., in Nilearn) will further strengthen the paper’s impact and reproducibility.
