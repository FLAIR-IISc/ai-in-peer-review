Summary
The paper proposes an online multivariate dictionary-learning framework for brain imaging that augments sparse dictionary learning (Mairal et al., JMLR 2010) with a Sobolev/Laplacian regularization on the dictionary atoms. The goal is to obtain atoms that are both sparse and spatially structured (piecewise smooth, compact “blobs”) while scaling to large datasets. The authors claim only a 2–3× runtime overhead compared with vanilla online dictionary learning and present experiments on fMRI data suggesting improved interpretability and better capture of inter-subject variability compared with SODL and ICA-based baselines.

Major comments
1) Novelty: The method appears to be a straightforward extension of online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) by adding a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty on the atoms. As such, the conceptual contribution seems limited relative to the NIPS standard.

2) Definition and motivation of “structure”: The paper states that “structured” atoms are piecewise smooth and compact, but this notion is not defined precisely, nor is it distinguished clearly from sparsity. If “structure” is intended to mean spatial smoothness and locality, please formalize this and justify why a Laplacian penalty is the right choice for the application. If, instead, “structure” is (partly) conflated with sparsity, please clarify the role of each regularization (Laplacian vs. sparsity constraint) and explain why sparsity of the atoms, specifically, leads to substantial gains in this setting. Overall, the motivation for using the Sobolev/Laplacian penalty needs to be sharpened.

3) Equation (1): I believe there is a notational or dimensional error. With Xi ≈ VUi, the dictionary should stack atoms as V = [V1, V2, …, Vr] ∈ Rp×r, and the code Ui ∈ Rr. The current presentation suggests a transpose is missing (e.g., [V^1, V^2, …, V^r]^T). Please correct to ensure dimensions are consistent.

4) Construction of Eq. (2): More explanation is needed for the modeling choices. In particular, why is an ℓ2 (ridge) penalty imposed on the codes instead of an ℓ1 penalty as in Mairal et al. (2010)? What is the empirical or theoretical rationale for preferring ℓ2 codes here, and how does it affect stability, sparsity of representations, and performance in this application? A clearer discussion of trade-offs and, ideally, a comparison would strengthen the paper.

5) Ambiguity at line 146: “What is the line 146?” This part is unclear in the current draft. Please clarify or correct the text around line 146.

6) Writing and typos: The manuscript requires significant language editing. There are many typographical and grammatical errors, and some sentences are hard to parse. Refining the writing will help convey the technical content and improve clarity.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: The algorithmic framework builds on a solid baseline (online dictionary learning), but the technical presentation has issues (Eq. (1) inconsistency, insufficient justification for key modeling choices in Eq. (2)). Without clearer definitions and motivation for the Sobolev/Laplacian term and the code penalty, it is hard to fully assess soundness.
- Clarity: The paper needs substantial editing for grammar and style. The notion of “structured” atoms should be defined precisely; the role of each regularizer should be explained; and ambiguous parts (e.g., line 146) should be fixed.
- Originality: The main idea is to add a Laplacian/Sobolev penalty to online dictionary learning. This extension, while practical, seems incremental.
- Significance: The application—learning spatially meaningful brain regions at scale—is important. However, the impact depends on a clearer and more rigorous justification of the modeling choices and cleaner presentation. As written, the limited novelty and unclear definitions weaken the contribution.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant large-scale neuroimaging problem with a practical online method.
- Aims to combine sparsity with spatial structure to improve interpretability.
- Claims modest additional computational cost relative to standard online dictionary learning.

Cons
- Novelty is limited to adding a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty to an established framework.
- “Structure” is not rigorously defined or clearly separated from sparsity; motivation for the specific regularization is unclear.
- Notational inconsistency in Eq. (1) and insufficient explanation of Eq. (2), especially the choice of ℓ2 codes.
- Writing quality and typos hinder comprehension.
