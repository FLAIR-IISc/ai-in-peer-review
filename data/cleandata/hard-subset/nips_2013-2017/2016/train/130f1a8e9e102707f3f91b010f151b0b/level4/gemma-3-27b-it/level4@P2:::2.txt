This paper presents an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals, extending the established framework of Mairal et al. (2010) [14] with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply their method to empirical data, investigate the effect of tuning parameters, and compare performance with two alternative methods commonly used in the field.

**Technical Quality:** The model definition is clear and the algorithmic approach builds upon a solid foundation [14]. However, the presentation initially lacked a compelling demonstration of the empirical advantage conferred by the Sobolev prior. Figure 2, in particular, did not clearly illustrate *how* the proposed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL) method performed better than the alternatives. Quantitative measures assessing spatial smoothness or identification of known, connected brain regions would have strengthened the analysis. The authors’ claims of improved “structure” lacked concrete justification tied to neuroscientific literature. *However, based on the information provided in the rebuttal, I have revised my assessment and now rate the technical quality as a 3*. The rebuttal’s results are highly informative and significantly bolster the technical claims. I recommend the authors incorporate these findings into their revision, and further demonstrate the structured nature of learned atoms with quantitative metrics and references to existing neuroscientific knowledge.

**Novelty/Originality:** The paper exhibits significant originality in extending [14] by incorporating Sobolev priors to encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms. This is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Potential Impact or Usefulness:** Dictionary learning is a useful technique for explaining neuroimaging data across large subject groups, and introducing spatial priors—like the Sobolev prior presented here—theoretically improves results. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm for achieving this, offering a potentially valuable tool for the neuroscience and medical imaging communities. Future work could explore applications to image segmentation and regional sensing.

**Presentation:** The paper’s overall organization is adequate, but the manuscript requires significant editing for clarity and precision. Numerous inconsistencies and potential typos hinder comprehension. Specific issues include:

*   In the matrix decomposition equation (Lines 67-68), the transpose symbol appears misplaced on the group of $V^j$ (j = 1,..., r). It should likely apply to each individual $V^j$.
*   Line 73 is missing a reference for the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset.
*   Line 83 contains a grammatical error: “The Graph-Net penalty can imposes…”.
*   Line 146 includes a bolded sentence that appears to be a co-author’s comment that should be removed.
*   There is a sign discrepancy between the last line of Equation (6) and Equation (8): $ V^j +At[j,j]^{-1}(VAt^j -Bt^j)$ versus $At[j,j] V^j -(VAt^j - Bt^j)$. The meaning of 'V' in the last line of Equation (6) should be clarified—is it the dictionary from the previous iteration?
*   Line 157 contains a repetition: “a single, a single”.
*   Line 169 contains a grammatical error: “We did an extensive study of th how”.
*   Section 3.3 lacks a clear presentation of the mathematical/algorithmic solution to Equation (6), even in the appendix.
*   Figure 2 presents results at different horizontal and sagittal slices, making direct comparison difficult. The claim in Line 222 that Smooth-SODL results are “more structured” is not readily apparent from the figure.
*   Figure 3’s legends are unclear. It is unclear what is being predicted (regression of brain data onto behavioral metrics?), and whether it is based on the $U_i$'s. The PCA baseline mentioned in the caption is also absent. The description in Lines 224-227, regarding R<sup>2</sup> values greater than 0.1, is difficult to follow.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel extension of a well-established dictionary learning framework with a theoretically sound approach to incorporating spatial structure.
*   The algorithm is efficient, scalable, and addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging – handling inter-subject variability.
*   The rebuttal demonstrates significant empirical gains and justifies the technical claims.
*   The potential impact on the field is positive, providing a new tool for researchers.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The initial presentation lacked a convincing demonstration of the benefits of the proposed method, relying on qualitative statements without sufficient quantitative support. *This has been largely addressed in the rebuttal.*
*   The manuscript contains numerous presentation issues (typos, unclear phrasing, inconsistencies) that detract from its overall quality.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound and novel approach. While the initial presentation was somewhat lacking, the information provided in the rebuttal significantly strengthens the work. I believe that with careful revision addressing the presentation issues and incorporating the rebuttal’s results, this paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
