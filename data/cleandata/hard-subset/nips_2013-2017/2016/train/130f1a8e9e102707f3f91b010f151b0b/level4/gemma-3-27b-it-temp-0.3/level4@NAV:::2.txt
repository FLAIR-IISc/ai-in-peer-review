This paper introduces an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to analyze neuroimaging data across subjects, building upon the established framework of [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior to promote spatial coherence in the learned dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate their method on empirical data, investigate the impact of parameter tuning, and compare its performance against two existing approaches. 

Regarding technical quality, I initially assessed this work as marginally below a 3. The model formulation is clear, and the algorithm logically extends a well-known method [14], appearing sound in its construction. However, the presentation somewhat obscures the empirical benefits of the Sobolev prior. Specifically, the results section lacks a clear demonstration of *how* the proposed SSMF method improves upon the alternatives. For instance, Figure 2 does not readily convey the advantages of SSMF. It would be beneficial to quantify the smoothness of spatial boundaries or highlight regions uniquely identified by SSMF that align with known neuroanatomical connectivity. Beyond stating that SSMF results are “more structured,” the authors should articulate neuroscience-driven criteria for structure and provide quantitative measurements demonstrating improvement according to these criteria. *Following review of the rebuttal, which provides informative additional results, I have revised my technical quality assessment to a 3. I encourage the authors to incorporate these new findings into their revision and to further substantiate the claim of improved structure in the learned atoms with quantitative metrics and connections to relevant neuroscience literature.*

The novelty of this work lies in the extension of [14] through the application of Sobolev priors to encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms, representing a valuable contribution. The potential impact is also significant. Dictionary learning is a powerful tool for analyzing neuroimaging data across large cohorts, and the theoretical benefits of incorporating spatial priors like the Sobolev prior are clear. This paper offers an efficient online algorithm for achieving this, potentially adding a useful technique to the toolkit of researchers in neuroscience and medical imaging. Exploring applications beyond the current scope, such as image segmentation and regional sensing, could further broaden the impact of this work.
