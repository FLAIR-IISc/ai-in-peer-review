This paper presents an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals. The approach extends a well-established framework from [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors validate their method on empirical data, analyzing the impact of tuning parameters and comparing performance against two existing methods. Technically, I initially assessed the quality as marginally below a 3. The model definition is clear, and the algorithm builds upon a solid foundation [14]. However, the presentation somewhat obscures the empirical benefits of the Sobolev prior, and the results section does not clearly demonstrate its advantages. Specifically, Figure 2 lacks sufficient detail to explain why the SSMF method outperforms the alternatives. It is unclear whether the spatial boundaries are demonstrably smoother quantitatively, or if SSMF identifies connections between regions known *a priori* to be related. Beyond stating that SSMF results are “more structured”, the authors should explicitly define criteria grounded in neuroscience literature and provide quantitative measurements of improvement according to these criteria. *Following review of the rebuttal, which included informative additional results, I have increased my technical quality rating from a 2 to a 3. I encourage the authors to incorporate these new results into their revision and, furthermore, to quantitatively demonstrate the improved structure and neuroscience-driven interpretability of the learned atoms.*

Regarding novelty, this work makes a notable contribution by extending [14] through the application of Sobolev priors to promote spatially structured dictionary atoms. The potential impact is also significant. Dictionary learning is a valuable technique for analyzing neuroimaging data across large subject groups, and introducing spatial priors, such as the Sobolev prior presented here, should theoretically improve results. This paper offers an efficient online algorithm for achieving this, potentially adding a useful tool for researchers in neuroscience and medical imaging. Future work could explore applications such as image segmentation and regional sensing.
