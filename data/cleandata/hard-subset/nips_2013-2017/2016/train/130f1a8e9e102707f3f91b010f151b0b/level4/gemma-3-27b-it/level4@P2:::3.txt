This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method incorporating both sparsity and structural priors—specifically, a Sobolev penalty to encourage smooth, compact dictionary atoms (blobs)—for decomposing brain images. The motivation stems from the observation that inter-subject variability in neuroimaging data often contains valuable information currently discarded in standard analyses. The authors build upon prior work in online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) and aim to improve the interpretability and capture of inter-subject variability by enforcing structured decompositions.

The core idea is sound and builds upon a relevant body of work in neuroimaging and machine learning. Dictionary learning has been previously used for brain image analysis (e.g., Abraham, 2016; Varol & Davatzikos, 2011), and the use of spatial priors to improve interpretability is not novel (e.g., Baldassarre et al., 2013; Varoquaux et al., 2016). However, the authors’ contribution lies in extending the *online* dictionary learning framework to incorporate a Laplacian penalty, enabling scalability to large datasets – a crucial advantage for neuroimaging applications like the Human Connectome Project (HCP). The authors correctly identify the limitations of purely data-driven approaches (like PCA/ICA – Beckmann & Smith, 2009) which can produce noisy, uninterpretable components, and attempt to address this with the proposed structured regularization. 

The paper’s strengths include its clear problem formulation and connection to existing literature. The proposed algorithm is presented with sufficient detail, and the authors acknowledge the computational cost associated with the Laplacian penalty and provide insights into parameter selection. The experiments on HCP data are also a positive aspect, utilizing a large and publicly available dataset.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence for the benefits of the proposed method. The primary results rely heavily on visual inspection of the learned dictionaries (Fig. 2a), which is subjective and difficult to assess without expertise in neuroanatomy. While the authors state that the Smooth-SODL atoms are "more interpretable," this claim isn’t rigorously substantiated. The quantitative results (Fig. 2b & 2c) show improvements in explained variance and prediction accuracy, but these are often marginal, and the advantages aren’t consistently demonstrated across all metrics and behavioral variables. The comparison to CanICA is somewhat weakened by the post-processing step (thresholding) applied to the CanICA results, making a fair comparison difficult. Furthermore, the discussion regarding the impact of data size on the effectiveness of the spatial regularization could be more nuanced. The authors suggest that the benefit is diminished with large datasets like HCP, but don’t fully explore scenarios where this regularization might be particularly valuable (e.g., smaller sample sizes, noisy data). A more detailed ablation study, systematically varying the strength of the Laplacian penalty, would strengthen the analysis. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging – extracting meaningful and interpretable features from high-dimensional brain data.
*   Extends a scalable online dictionary learning framework with a structural prior.
*   Demonstrates improvements in explained variance and predictive performance, although modest.
*   The method is well-motivated and connected to existing literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The primary results rely on subjective visual assessment.
*   Quantitative improvements are often marginal.
*   The benefit of the spatial regularization is not convincingly demonstrated, particularly in the context of large datasets.
*   The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study exploring the impact of different parameter settings.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a technically sound approach and addresses a relevant problem, the evidence for its significant impact is not strong enough for clear acceptance. I believe the paper would benefit from more rigorous quantitative evaluation, a more thorough exploration of the parameter space, and a more convincing demonstration of the benefits of the proposed method, particularly in scenarios where spatial regularization is expected to be most impactful. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit with a stronger empirical evaluation.
