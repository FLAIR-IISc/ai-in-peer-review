This paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The authors extend the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev regularization term to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. This non-trivial extension allows the algorithm to scale to arbitrarily sized datasets while maintaining a reasonable running time.

The proposed method, dubbed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), is well-motivated by the need to extract interpretable and structured brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, highlighting the limitations of existing methods and the potential benefits of their approach.

The technical contributions of the paper are sound, and the authors provide a thorough description of their algorithm, including the optimization procedure and the construction of the objective function. The use of a Sobolev regularization term to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms is a novel aspect of the paper, and the authors provide a clear explanation of its purpose and effect.

The experimental evaluation of the proposed method is thorough and well-executed, with a clear presentation of the results and a comparison with state-of-the-art methods. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in extracting structured and denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability in small, medium, and large-scale regimes.

However, I have some minor comments and suggestions for improvement:

1. The novelty of the paper could be further emphasized by highlighting the key differences between the proposed method and existing online dictionary learning approaches.
2. The definition of "structure" in the context of the Sobolev regularization term could be clarified, and the authors could provide more intuition about why this type of regularization is beneficial for the problem at hand.
3. The equation numbering is inconsistent, and some equations are not numbered at all. It would be helpful to renumber the equations consistently throughout the paper.
4. The language and grammar of the paper could be refined to improve clarity and readability. I found several typos and minor errors throughout the text.
5. The authors could provide more details about the construction of the objective function, including the choice of the l2 norm for the codes instead of the l1 norm used in Mairal et al. (2010).

Overall, this is a well-written and technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging and dictionary learning. With some minor revisions to address the comments above, I believe the paper could be even stronger.

Strengths:

* Novel extension of online dictionary learning framework with Sobolev regularization
* Clear and concise introduction to the problem and motivation
* Thorough description of the algorithm and optimization procedure
* Well-executed experimental evaluation and comparison with state-of-the-art methods

Weaknesses:

* Minor inconsistencies in equation numbering and formatting
* Some typos and minor errors in language and grammar
* Could benefit from further emphasis on novelty and key differences with existing methods
* Could provide more intuition about the benefits of Sobolev regularization

Recommendation:

I recommend accepting this paper for publication, pending minor revisions to address the comments above.
