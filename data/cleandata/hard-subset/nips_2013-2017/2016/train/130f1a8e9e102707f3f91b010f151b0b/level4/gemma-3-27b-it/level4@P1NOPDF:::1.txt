This paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework for identifying informative brain regions. The topic is inherently interesting. However, the paper lacks theoretical analysis to support the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. Furthermore, the writing quality suggests a lack of careful revision prior to submission. Several issues, such as the comment “146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v,” remain in the manuscript, indicating insufficient attention to detail. 

The core idea is to leverage online dictionary learning to adaptively discover relevant features within large-scale brain imaging datasets. This is a compelling approach, and the scale of the data addressed is commendable. However, without a theoretical foundation, it’s difficult to assess *why* this approach should succeed, or under what conditions it will outperform existing methods. The authors should address this by providing some analysis – even if preliminary – regarding the convergence properties of the online learning algorithm, or the statistical guarantees regarding the learned dictionary elements.

My main concerns lie in the experimental evaluation. The results are presented, but the justification for the chosen parameters and the comparison to baseline methods feels somewhat weak. The choice of dictionary size, for example, isn’t thoroughly motivated. Similarly, while comparisons are made to other techniques, the paper doesn’t clearly articulate *how* the proposed method is expected to differ in performance, or why those specific baselines were selected. A more rigorous ablation study, isolating the contributions of different components of the algorithm, would also be beneficial.

Specific comments:

*   The notation is occasionally inconsistent. Defining all variables clearly at their first use is crucial for readability.
*   Several figures could benefit from clearer labeling and captions. It's not always immediately obvious what is being visualized.
*   The description of the dataset used is adequate, but more detail regarding preprocessing steps would be helpful. Were any specific artifact removal techniques employed?
*   The phrase “interesting brain regions” is used repeatedly. While understandable, it would be stronger to specify *what* makes these regions “interesting” in the context of the experiment and the underlying neuroscientific question. Are they regions with high discriminative power? Regions that correlate with a specific cognitive task?
*   The discussion of the results feels somewhat superficial. The authors state *that* differences are observed, but not *why* these differences might occur. A deeper dive into the biological interpretation of the findings would significantly strengthen the paper.

Overall, this paper presents a promising idea, but it falls short of its potential due to the lack of theoretical grounding and the insufficient rigor in the experimental evaluation and presentation. Addressing the concerns raised above, particularly the theoretical analysis and a more in-depth discussion of the results, would significantly improve the paper. As it stands, the paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a fully developed contribution.
