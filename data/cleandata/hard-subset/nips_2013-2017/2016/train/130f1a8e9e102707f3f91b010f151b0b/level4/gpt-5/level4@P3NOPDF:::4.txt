Summary
The paper proposes an under-complete dictionary learning framework for fMRI that combines a Sobolev regularizer to encourage smoothness with an L1 penalty to promote sparsity of the learned components. The motivation is consistent with known properties of fMRI data (spatial/temporal smoothness and functional parsimony), and the approach is, in principle, broadly applicable. However, the current manuscript leaves key aspects of the method, evaluation, and empirical gains insufficiently explained, and the presentation contains evident drafting artifacts that hinder readability.

Strengths
- Combining a Sobolev (smoothness) term with sparsity within a dictionary-learning objective is a natural and potentially useful design for fMRI decomposition.
- The under-complete setting is reasonable for discovering a compact set of functional modes and may reduce overfitting.
- If properly validated, integrating smoothness into the optimization (rather than relying on pre-smoothing) could be advantageous.

Weaknesses
- Novelty appears incremental. Similar combinations of smoothness and sparsity (e.g., TV/L1, graph-Laplacian/L1, fused lasso with sparse coding) have been explored in imaging. The distinct advantage of the specific Sobolev formulation over these alternatives is not clearly articulated.
- The empirical improvements over simpler baselines are modest and not convincingly analyzed. The paper does not isolate where the gains come from (e.g., the Sobolev term vs. standard preprocessing), nor does it provide statistical significance or effect-size analyses.
- The objective function and optimization details are under-specified. It is unclear which Sobolev norm (e.g., H1) is used, how it is discretized (finite differences, Laplacian, boundary conditions), and whether the regularization is spatial, temporal, or both. Convergence properties, step-size choices, and stopping criteria are not described in sufficient detail.
- The under-complete design choice (dictionary size relative to data dimensionality) is not justified. How does performance vary with the number of components?
- Baselines are limited or not clearly described. Comparisons should include ICA (FastICA/Infomax), sparse PCA/NMF, standard dictionary learning with L1 only, and smoothness-regularized variants (e.g., TV-regularized dictionary learning or Laplacian-regularized sparse coding).
- Reproducibility is unclear: code availability, parameter settings, initialization, data splits, and preprocessing pipelines are not adequately documented.
- The writing is rushed and sometimes confusing. There are leftover draft comments (e.g., at line 146), inconsistent notation, and unclear figure captions, which impede comprehension.

Requested clarifications and questions
1. Precisely define the optimization problem. Specify the Sobolev seminorm used, discretization operator, and whether the smoothness acts on atoms, loadings, or both. Provide the full objective with all terms and constraints.
2. Describe the optimization algorithm (e.g., alternating minimization with proximal steps). Which proximal operators are used for the Sobolev and L1 terms? Any convergence guarantees for the alternating scheme?
3. Explain how regularization parameters were chosen. Was there cross-validation, a stability criterion, or heuristic rules? Include sensitivity analyses over a reasonable range.
4. Detail the data preprocessing (motion correction, detrending, spatial smoothing). If external smoothing is applied, how do you ensure the observed gains are not simply due to double-smoothing?
5. Justify the under-complete regime. Provide results as a function of dictionary size (e.g., 20–200 components) to show robustness and the trade-off with reconstruction error and interpretability.
6. Provide stronger baselines and ablations:
   - L1-only dictionary learning (no Sobolev)
   - Sobolev-only (no L1)
   - Pre-smoothed data with L1-only dictionary learning
   - TV/Laplacian-regularized alternatives
7. Quantify improvements using multiple metrics: reconstruction error, component sparsity/smoothness measures, test–retest or split-half stability, alignment with known atlases or networks, and downstream performance (e.g., task decoding).
8. Report statistical tests and confidence intervals. Are improvements consistent across subjects/sessions?
9. Discuss computational complexity and runtime on whole-brain datasets. What is the memory footprint? Any practical tricks (warm starts, screening rules)?
10. Clarify whether the smoothness is spatial, temporal, or both. If temporal, how does TR and filtering affect the discretization? If spatial, how are boundaries and non-brain voxels handled?

Suggestions for improving the manuscript
- Reorganize the exposition: start with a clear problem statement, provide an intuitive motivation for why a Sobolev/L1 combination is preferable to pre-smoothing plus sparsity, then present the exact objective and algorithm (pseudocode would help), followed by ablations and comprehensive comparisons.
- Include a schematic figure of the model and the flow of the alternating optimization.
- Improve figures: readable fonts, consistent color scales, and side-by-side comparisons with baselines for representative components.
- Add a robustness section: initialization sensitivity, number of iterations, and stability across random seeds.
- Tighten the writing, remove drafting artifacts (e.g., the comment at line 146), fix notation, and streamline the presentation to reduce redundancy.

Minor comments
- Define all symbols at first use and keep notation consistent throughout.
- Clarify whether “components” refer to spatial maps, temporal time courses, or both; use unambiguous terms (atoms vs. loadings).
- State dataset details (subjects, runs, task/rest, voxel resolution) and train/test splits.

Overall assessment
The idea of integrating Sobolev smoothness with sparsity in dictionary learning for fMRI is sensible and has potential. However, in its current form the paper offers limited novelty, lacks crucial methodological and experimental detail, and does not convincingly demonstrate meaningful gains over simpler, well-established baselines. With clearer formulation, comprehensive ablations, stronger comparisons, and improved presentation, the work could become a more compelling contribution. As it stands, I would not recommend acceptance without substantial revisions.
