This paper introduces Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method designed to extract sparse and spatially structured components from large-scale neuroimaging data. The central idea is to extend the widely-used online sparse dictionary learning framework (Mairal et al., 2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages spatial smoothness, resulting in components that are blob-like and compact, while an l1-norm constraint ensures sparsity. The motivation is to provide a scalable, data-driven method for defining functional regions of interest (ROIs) that captures inter-subject variability. The approach is evaluated on a large functional MRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project, comparing it to standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a multi-subject ICA-based method (CanICA).

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Scalability:** The paper addresses a highly relevant problem in neuroimaging: the scalable analysis of massive datasets. The proposed online algorithm is well-suited for this challenge, as batch-based methods for structured decomposition often struggle with the computational and memory demands of modern datasets.
*   **Novelty:** The main contribution is the effective and computationally efficient integration of a spatial smoothness prior into the online dictionary learning framework. The authors demonstrate that this significant structural enhancement comes at a modest computational cost (a 2-3x increase in runtime over the unstructured SODL), which is a valuable practical achievement.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The method is benchmarked against appropriate baselines on a large, contemporary dataset. The comparison uses a variety of metrics, including visual quality, explained variance, component stability, and performance on a downstream prediction task (predicting behavioral scores). The inclusion of a learning curve analysis (Table 1) is particularly effective, clearly demonstrating the benefit of the spatial regularizer in low-to-medium data regimes.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript would benefit significantly from a careful revision for clarity. The writing is dense in several sections, making it challenging for the reader to follow the technical details and the narrative of the contributions. The paper also shows signs of being rushed; for example, a note "refer to [16] for the details" remains as an underbrace in the derivation of Equation 7. Improving the organization and polishing the prose would make the work more accessible and impactful.
*   **Magnitude of Improvement:** While the proposed method yields components that are visually more interpretable and less noisy, the quantitative improvements over baselines are not always substantial. In the large-data setting, the increase in explained variance over the standard SODL is modest (Fig. 2b). Furthermore, in the behavioral prediction task, the proposed method is outperformed by the dense CanICA model. The paper would be stronger if it provided a more in-depth discussion of these trade-offs, better justifying why the gains in interpretability and stability outweigh the sometimes-modest quantitative improvements in other metrics.

In summary, the paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound extension to online dictionary learning for neuroimaging analysis. The method's scalability is a key advantage. However, the significance of the contribution is somewhat diminished by the clarity of the presentation and the modest quantitative gains over simpler methods in some of the reported experiments.
