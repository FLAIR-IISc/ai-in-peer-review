This paper proposes an extension of dictionary learning models by incorporating a Sobolev prior to enforce spatial smoothness in the learned dictionary atoms. While the problem of imposing meaningful structure in these models is interesting, the manuscript in its current form makes it difficult to assess the impact and tangible benefits of the proposed algorithm.

The primary weakness lies in the evaluation of the results. The main validation is qualitative, relying on a visual comparison of the clarity and consistency of identified spatial components ("blobs") on the brain. This form of assessment is inherently subjective and is not easily interpretable by a non-expert reader. Without quantitative metrics, it is difficult to determine if the proposed method offers a genuine improvement over baseline approaches.

Furthermore, the model incorporates penalties designed to enforce smoothness and spatial consistency. Consequently, the fact that the algorithm produces smoother blobs is an expected outcome rather than a surprising or particularly insightful result. The paper needs to demonstrate that this enforced structure provides a concrete advantage beyond simply reflecting the chosen prior. For example, do these smoother components correspond better to known functional areas, or do they exhibit higher predictive power for a separate task?

The motivation for this specific prior could also be clarified, particularly in the context of the chosen application. While such regularization might be valuable for isolating regions from sparse or noisy data, its utility is less apparent for massive, high-quality datasets like the Human Connectome Project, where data scarcity is not the primary challenge. A more compelling contribution would be to show how this method addresses a specific limitation of existing techniques when applied to such data. For instance, a stronger validation could have been achieved by focusing on inter-subject analysis. The paper could have demonstrated an improved ability to identify consistent brain regions across subjects or used inter-subject consistency as a more objective performance metric. This would provide much stronger evidence for the model's utility than the current visual inspection.

In summary, the validation presented is too subjective, making it difficult to ascertain the concrete advantages of the proposed Sobolev prior. The paper would be significantly strengthened by the inclusion of objective, quantitative evaluation and a validation strategy that more clearly demonstrates the scientific or practical benefits of the smoother decompositions.
