The paper's primary contribution is the integration of a Sobolev prior into a dictionary learning framework. While the problem is compelling, it is difficult to assess the tangible impact or improvement offered by the proposed algorithm. The main results hinge on a visual comparison of the clarity and consistency of the identified spatial components in the brain, an evaluation that is not straightforward for a non-expert.

Furthermore, for this type of decomposition, the influence of the data is often as significant, if not more so, than the algorithm. The introduction of penalties for smoothness and spatial consistency naturally produces smoother components; thus, the results are not entirely unexpected. While such a prior could be beneficial in data-limited situations, its utility is less obvious for large datasets like the Human Connectome Project, where data availability is not the main challenge.

A more convincing validation could have involved consolidating brain region differences across subjects and using inter-subject consistency as a performance metric. The validation as presented is highly subjective, and consequently, the overall benefit of the method remains unclear.
