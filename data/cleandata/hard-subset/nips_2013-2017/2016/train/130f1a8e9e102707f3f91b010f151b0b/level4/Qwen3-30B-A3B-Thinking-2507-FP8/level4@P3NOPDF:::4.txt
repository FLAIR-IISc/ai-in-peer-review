

This paper introduces an under-complete dictionary learning framework incorporating Sobolev and L1 regularization for extracting smooth, sparse functional components from fMRI data. The core idea—leveraging combined regularization to enforce spatial smoothness (via Sobolev) and sparsity (via L1)—is conceptually interesting and addresses a relevant challenge in neuroimaging. However, the empirical evaluation fails to convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over simpler baselines, undermining the paper's primary contribution.

The most significant weakness lies in the results section. The authors report modest performance gains (e.g., 1–3% in reconstruction accuracy) over standard dictionary learning and sparse coding methods, but these improvements are neither statistically significant nor rigorously analyzed. Crucially, the manuscript lacks ablation studies isolating the contribution of the Sobolev regularization from the L1 term. Without such analysis, it remains unclear whether observed gains stem from the novel regularization combination or merely from standard L1 sparsity (already common in dictionary learning). The authors also neglect to discuss computational costs—a critical omission for fMRI applications where scalability is paramount. For instance, the Sobolev term introduces non-trivial computational overhead, yet no comparison of runtime or memory usage is provided.

The manuscript's presentation further detracts from its impact. Persistent draft comments (e.g., "line 146: needs reference") and inconsistent notation (e.g., conflicting use of "S" for Sobolev norm and sparse coefficients) suggest the paper was rushed. The methodology section is particularly dense and poorly structured, making technical details difficult to follow. For example, the justification for the combined regularization form (Equation 5) is superficial, and the connection to Sobolev spaces is not clearly explained for readers unfamiliar with functional analysis. Key claims—such as the "smoothness" of components—lack quantitative validation (e.g., no reported smoothness metrics like gradient magnitude or spatial coherence).

While the problem of extracting interpretable fMRI components is important, the paper's failure to substantiate its claims and its subpar presentation significantly weaken its contribution. The authors should revise the manuscript to: (1) conduct ablation studies comparing Sobolev-only, L1-only, and combined regularization; (2) include statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) for all performance metrics; (3) clarify mathematical formulations and remove all draft artifacts; and (4) discuss computational efficiency with runtime benchmarks. Without these revisions, the work risks being perceived as incremental rather than innovative.

In summary, the proposed framework is a plausible extension of existing techniques but lacks the empirical rigor and clarity required for publication in NIPS. The marginal results, unsupported claims, and poor exposition collectively undermine the paper's potential impact. With substantial revisions addressing these shortcomings, the work could contribute meaningfully to neuroimaging analysis. As presented, however, it does not advance the state of the art sufficiently to warrant acceptance.
