This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The authors extend the online dictionary-learning work of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev penalty to impose piece-wise smooth and compact structures on the dictionary atoms. The proposed method, dubbed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), is evaluated on a large public dataset and compared to state-of-the-art models.

The paper relates to previous work on online dictionary learning and sparse coding, and the authors demonstrate the advantages of their approach in extracting structured and denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability. The method is shown to outperform competing methods in terms of explained variance, stability, and reproducibility.

Strengths of the paper include its clear presentation of the proposed method, thorough evaluation, and comparison to state-of-the-art models. The authors also provide a concise study of the impact of hyper-parameter selection on the problem and describe the optimality regime.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement. The novelty of the paper may be considered weak, as it builds upon existing work on online dictionary learning. The definition of "structure" in the context of the Sobolev regularization could be clearer, and the motivation for using this regularization term could be better explained. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more detailed analysis and explanation of the construction of the proposed model, including the choice of l2 norm instead of l1 norm for the codes.

Some minor issues include a potential mistake in Eq.(1) and a unclear statement on line 146. The language of the paper could also be refined to eliminate typos and improve clarity.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel extension to online dictionary learning that incorporates a Sobolev penalty to impose structure on the dictionary atoms.
* The method is thoroughly evaluated and compared to state-of-the-art models, demonstrating its advantages in extracting structured and denoised dictionaries.
* The paper provides a clear presentation of the proposed method and its evaluation.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The novelty of the paper may be considered weak, as it builds upon existing work on online dictionary learning.
* The definition of "structure" in the context of the Sobolev regularization could be clearer, and the motivation for using this regularization term could be better explained.
* The paper could benefit from more detailed analysis and explanation of the construction of the proposed model.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents a clear and thorough evaluation of the proposed method. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and it is a good candidate for acceptance.
