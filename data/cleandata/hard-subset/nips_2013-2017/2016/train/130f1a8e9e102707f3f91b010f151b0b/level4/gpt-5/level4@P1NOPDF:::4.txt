This paper proposes an undercomplete dictionary learning approach for fMRI analysis with a combined Sobolev/L1 regularization to promote smoothness and sparsity in the learned components. The overall idea is interesting, and the use of a Sobolev term alongside an L1 penalty is a somewhat original contribution in this context.

However, the empirical benefits over simpler or more standard methods are not convincingly demonstrated. Either the improvements are marginal, or the manuscript does not explain clearly why and when the proposed regularization yields a substantial advantage. As written, it is difficult to discern whether the gains stem from the Sobolev term, the sparsity term, the undercomplete constraint, or some interaction among them.

The presentation also feels rushed. There are still draft comments in the text (e.g., at line 146), and the manuscript is at times tedious and confusing to read. The exposition would benefit from a clearer problem statement, a precise definition of the optimization objective (with all terms and hyperparameters explicitly introduced), and a transparent description of the algorithm and its convergence or stopping criteria.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:
- Providing a more thorough empirical comparison against simpler baselines, with clear quantitative summaries (e.g., improvements relative to standard dictionary learning, ICA, or sparsity-only/smoothness-only variants).
- Including ablation studies that isolate the impact of the Sobolev term versus the L1 term and the effect of using an undercomplete dictionary.
- Clarifying the rationale for the undercomplete setting in fMRI (e.g., stability, interpretability) and explaining how the chosen dimensionality was selected.
- Reporting sensitivity analyses for key hyperparameters (weights of Sobolev and L1 penalties) and discussing practical guidance for their selection.
- Improving figure/table captions and visualizations to make the qualitative advantages of the learned components more evident.
- Removing draft comments and carefully editing for clarity and flow.

In summary, the method is promising and the combination of smoothness and sparsity is appealing, but the current manuscript does not clearly establish a strong advantage over simpler alternatives, and the presentation needs significant polishing.
