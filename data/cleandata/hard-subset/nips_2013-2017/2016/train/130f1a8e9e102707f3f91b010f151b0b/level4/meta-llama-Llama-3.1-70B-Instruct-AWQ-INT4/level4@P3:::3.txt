The authors propose a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. This work extends the online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The method, dubbed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), is evaluated on a large public dataset from the Human Connectome Project.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method and its relation to previous work. The use of a Sobolev prior to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms is an interesting contribution, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in extracting structured and denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability.

The experimental evaluation of the method is thorough, and the authors compare their proposed Smooth-SODL model with competing state-of-the-art methods like SODL (sparse online dictionary-learning) and CanICA. The results show that Smooth-SODL extracts dictionaries that are more interpretable and better capture inter-subject variability in small, medium, and large-scale regimes alike.

However, the validation of the method's performance is somewhat subjective and relies heavily on visual comparisons of the identified blobs on the brain. While the authors provide some quantitative metrics, such as explained variance and stability of the dictionary atoms, more objective evaluation criteria would strengthen the paper.

Furthermore, the authors could provide more insight into the impact of the Sobolev prior on the performance of the method. For instance, how does the choice of the regularization parameter γ affect the results? Are there any limitations or potential drawbacks to using a Sobolev prior in this context?

Additionally, the authors mention that their method is particularly useful when dealing with limited data, but they do not provide any experiments to support this claim. It would be interesting to see how the method performs on smaller datasets or in scenarios where data is scarce.

In terms of related work, the authors provide a comprehensive overview of existing methods for dictionary learning and brain imaging analysis. However, they could further discuss how their method differs from or improves upon these existing approaches.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting contribution to the field of brain imaging analysis, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed Smooth-SODL model in extracting structured and denoised dictionaries. However, more objective evaluation criteria and further insight into the impact of the Sobolev prior would strengthen the paper.
