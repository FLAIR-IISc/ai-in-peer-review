This paper provides an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals, extending a well-established framework [16] with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors applied their method to empirical data, reported the effect of varying tuning parameters, and compared the performance with two alternative methods in the field.

**Technical Quality:** The model definition is clear, and the algorithms follow a well-established framework [16], appearing solid. However, the paper initially lacked a clear demonstration of the empirical advantage of introducing the Sobolev prior in the results section. Specifically, in Figure 2, it was not immediately apparent in what aspect the Smooth-SODL method was better than the two alternatives. Were the spatial boundaries more smooth quantitatively? Were there certain regions, known to be connected, only identified by Smooth-SODL? The authors stated the results were “more structured,” but could have explicitly described criteria justified by neuroscience literature, and provided quantitative measurements of the improvement according to these criteria. Following the rebuttal, the additional results provided are very informative. The authors should include these results in their revision and demonstrate that the atoms learned by Smooth-SODL are more structured (with quantitative measurements) and more meaningful (with reference to neuroscience literature).

**Novelty/Originality:** There is notable original contribution in extending [16] by applying Sobolev priors to encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms.

**Significance/Impact:** Dictionary learning is a useful approach for explaining neuroimaging data across large groups of subjects, and introducing spatial priors like the Sobolev prior should improve results. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm for doing so and could add a valuable tool to the neuroscience/medical imaging community. The authors could explore further applications of this method, such as image segmentation and regional sensing.

**Clarity/Presentation:** The general organization of the paper is adequate. However, the manuscript requires editing for clarity and conciseness. Several issues were noted:

*   In the matrix decomposition equation (lines 67-68), the transpose symbol on the group of $V^j$ (j = 1,..., k) should be applied to each individual $V^j$.
*   Line 73: The reference to the HCP dataset was missing.
*   Line 83: “The Graph-Net penalty can imposes…” should be corrected to “The Graph-Net penalty can impose…”
*   Line 146: A bold sentence appeared to be a co-author’s comment and should be removed.
*   The last line of Equation (6) and Equation (8) had a sign discrepancy: $V^j + At[j,j]^{-1}(Bjt - VAt^j)$ versus $At[j,j]V^j - (VAt^j - Bt^j)$. In the last line of Equation (6), *V* refers to the dictionary from the previous iteration; the jth column is not the variable *v* being optimized.
*   Line 157: "a single, a single" should be corrected.
*   Line 169: “We did an extensive study of th how” should be corrected.
*   Section 3.3, near line 147, it would be beneficial to include the mathematical/algorithmic solution to Equation (6), even in the appendix.
*   Figure 2: Results were presented at different horizontal and sagittal slices, hindering direct comparison. The claim in line 222 that Smooth-SODL results were more “structured” was not supported by a clear visual comparison.
*   Figure 3: The predicted variable was unclear. If the brain data were regressed onto behavioral metrics, was this based on the *U<sub>i</sub>*’s? The legends were not explained in the main text or figure caption. The PCA baseline mentioned in the caption was not shown. The description in lines 224-227, which claimed R<sup>2</sup> values greater than 0.1, was difficult to follow.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging: handling inter-subject variability and defining meaningful brain regions.
*   The proposed method builds upon a solid foundation (online dictionary learning) and introduces a novel and theoretically sound extension (Sobolev prior).
*   The experiments demonstrate the potential of the method, and the rebuttal provided additional strong evidence of its effectiveness.
*   The online learning aspect makes the method scalable to large datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The initial presentation lacked sufficient quantitative evidence to convincingly demonstrate the advantage of the proposed method.
*   The manuscript contained several minor editing issues that impacted clarity.
*   The connection to existing neuroscience literature regarding the interpretation of the learned structures could be strengthened.
