This paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework for identifying brain regions. The topic is highly relevant, as discovering meaningful structure in neuroimaging data is a significant challenge. The authors aim to extend existing online dictionary learning methods [16] by incorporating both sparsity and structural constraints (smoothness) to better delineate brain structures. The approach combines an `1-norm penalty for sparsity with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage smooth, compact “blobs” representing brain regions. They demonstrate the method on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project, comparing it to ICA-based methods (CanICA) and standard online dictionary learning (SODL).

The core idea – leveraging population variability to define regions – builds on previous work in clustering-based approaches [21, 14] but moves towards a more model-based approach akin to PCA or ICA [5]. The innovation lies in the combination of sparsity and smoothness penalties within an online learning framework, allowing scalability to large datasets. The authors correctly point to the limitations of univariate models and the need for principled region-of-interest (ROI) definition, often relying on manual segmentation or cross-modality constraints.

**Strengths:**

*   **Scalability:** The online learning aspect allows the method to handle large datasets (both in terms of number of voxels and subjects), a crucial requirement for modern neuroimaging studies.
*   **Structured Sparsity:** The combination of `1-norm and Laplacian penalties seems well-motivated for identifying spatially localized and coherent brain regions, and preliminary results suggest it improves interpretability.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset provide a reasonable starting point for evaluating the method, comparing it against established techniques.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning and initialization provides some guidance for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** A significant drawback is the absence of a more rigorous theoretical analysis. While the authors mention convergence guarantees based on [16], a deeper understanding of the properties of the proposed regularization (particularly the interplay between the `1-norm and Laplacian penalties) would strengthen the paper. Is the proposed penalty well-conditioned? What are the theoretical implications of choosing different values for α and γ?
*   **Limited Novelty Beyond Combination:** While the combination of existing techniques (online dictionary learning, `1-norm, Laplacian regularization) is reasonable, the paper doesn’t fully articulate *why* this particular combination is expected to be superior. A more thorough discussion of the potential limitations of each individual component and how the combination addresses those limitations would be beneficial.
*   **Presentation and Clarity:** The writing, while technically sound, could be improved for clarity and conciseness. There are instances of verbose phrasing and a lack of flow. Although the authors have since removed the internal comment (“146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v.”), the writing still contains minor inconsistencies and areas where explanations could be more precise. For example, the relationship between the parameters α and γ, and their impact on the resulting dictionaries, could be discussed more clearly.
*   **Comparison to Existing Spatially Regularized Methods:** The related work section acknowledges spatially-structured decompositions [25, 1] but doesn’t adequately contrast the proposed approach with these existing methods in terms of computational complexity or theoretical properties. A more detailed comparison would contextualize the contribution.
* **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: While discussed in section 3.2, the reported sensitivity to hyperparameters raises concerns about robustness and generalizability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in neuroimaging (ROI definition and dimensionality reduction).
*   Presents a scalable algorithm that can handle large datasets.
*   Demonstrates promising empirical results on a publicly available dataset.
*   Offers practical considerations for implementation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   Incremental novelty – primarily a combination of existing techniques.
*   Writing could be more clear and concise.
*   Limited comparison to closely related work.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging, offering a scalable method for identifying brain regions. However, the lack of theoretical analysis and the limited discussion of novelty relative to existing work are significant weaknesses. The clarity of the presentation could also be improved. While the empirical results are encouraging, they are not sufficient to fully justify acceptance in their current form.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. The authors should consider addressing the weaknesses identified above – particularly adding theoretical justification and a more detailed comparison to related work – before resubmitting. A revision addressing the clarity and conciseness of the writing would also be beneficial.
