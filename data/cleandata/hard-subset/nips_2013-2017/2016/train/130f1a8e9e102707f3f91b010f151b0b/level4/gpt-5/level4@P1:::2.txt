Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an online multivariate dictionary-learning method for decomposing population-level brain images into sparse, spatially structured components (“atoms”). The method extends the well-established online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. [14] by adding a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty on the atoms to encourage piecewise smooth, compact regions (“blobs”) rather than scattered activations. The codes are estimated via ridge regression, and the dictionary is updated with block-coordinate descent using FISTA steps to handle the Laplacian term. The authors evaluate the method on large-scale task fMRI data, study the effect of hyperparameters, and compare against Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (SODL) [14] and CanICA [22]. The work relates to structured sparse decompositions in neuroimaging (e.g., TV-ℓ1 approaches [1, 3, 11, 24]) but targets scalability by staying within the online framework and using a Laplacian regularization that is cheaper than TV.

Technical quality
I would rate this marginally below a 3 if given the option. The model is clearly defined. The algorithms follow a solid, known framework [14], and the proposed modifications are sensible. However, in its current presentation, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate the empirical advantage of adding the Sobolev prior. In particular, Figure 2 does not make it clear in what respects the proposed method (Smooth-SODL; called SSMF in my earlier notes) outperforms the baselines. For example:
- Are spatial boundaries measurably smoother or more compact?
- Are there known contiguous regions that only Smooth-SODL successfully recovers?
- Beyond stating that results are “more structured,” can the authors provide quantitative criteria (e.g., connectedness, compactness, Laplacian energy, number/size of connected components, or sparsity metrics) and justify them with references from the neuroscience literature?

Added after reading the rebuttal: The additional results in the rebuttal are very informative. I raise my technical quality rating from 2 to 3. I encourage the authors to include these results in the camera-ready version and, in addition, to quantify that the learned atoms are more structured (using the types of measures suggested above) and more meaningful (with neurobiological references).

Originality
The main novelty is the extension of online dictionary learning [14] with a Sobolev/Laplacian prior to enforce spatial structure on the atoms, while maintaining scalability. This is a nontrivial and useful contribution given the computational limits of prior structured methods (e.g., TV-ℓ1 with slow alternating optimization).

Significance and potential impact
Dictionary learning is a powerful approach for modeling inter-subject variability in neuroimaging, and spatial priors should, in principle, improve interpretability and robustness. Providing an efficient online algorithm with structured atoms is valuable and could become a practical tool for large datasets. The method may also be useful for related tasks such as image segmentation or region sensing; exploring these applications would broaden impact.

Clarity and presentation
The overall organization is acceptable, but the manuscript contains confusing passages and typos that impede understanding. Below are specific issues to fix or clarify:
- Matrix decomposition equation (between Lines 67–68): Should the transpose be applied to each Vj individually?
- Line 73: The HCP dataset reference is missing.
- Line 83: “The Graph-Net penalty can imposes ...” should be corrected.
- Line 146: A bold sentence appears to be an unresolved co-author comment.
- Equations (6) and (8): The final expressions do not agree in sign, e.g., Vj + A t[j,j]^{-1}(V A t^j − B t^j) versus A t[j,j] Vj − (V A t^j − B t^j). Also, in the last line of Equation (6), please clarify that V refers to the dictionary from the previous iteration (i.e., the jth column is not the optimization variable v).
- Line 157: “a single, a single” should be corrected.
- Line 169: “We did an extensive study of th how” should be corrected.
- Section 3.3 (paragraph near Line 147, just below Algorithm 2): Please provide the explicit solution method for Equation (6) (even if in the appendix), including the SVD-based computation and complexity.
- Figure 2: The methods are shown on different axial/sagittal slices, which makes comparison difficult. Please use identical slices across methods. As presented, I cannot follow the claim (Line 222) that the proposed method is more “structured.”
- Figure 3: It is unclear what exactly is predicted and how. Is the prediction regressing behavioral metrics on Ui (codes)? The legend is not explained in the main text or caption. The PCA baseline mentioned in the caption does not appear to be shown. The text (Lines 224–227) refers to R^2 > 0.1, but it is not clear which curves or bars to inspect.

Strengths
- Clear formulation that extends a widely used online framework with a spatial prior.
- Sensible algorithmic choices (ridge-coded updates; FISTA steps for Laplacian-regularized atom updates) with attention to scalability.
- Empirical evaluation on large-scale HCP task fMRI.
- The rebuttal adds informative results; including them would strengthen the paper.

Weaknesses
- The empirical advantage of the Laplacian prior is not clearly demonstrated in the current figures and metrics.
- Several presentation issues and inconsistencies (equations, line references, figure clarity) hinder reproducibility and interpretability.
- Claims of “more structured” atoms should be supported with quantitative measures and neurobiological justification.

Suggestions for improvement
- Incorporate the new results from the rebuttal.
- Add quantitative measures of spatial structure (e.g., Laplacian energy, compactness/connectedness metrics, sparsity ratios) and use consistent slices/thresholding across methods for fair visual comparison.
- Clarify the behavioral prediction setup (targets, features—raw X vs Ui, training/testing protocol) and ensure all baselines shown in captions appear in figures.
- Fix the noted typos, equation sign inconsistencies, and missing references (e.g., HCP).

Overall assessment
Promising and potentially useful contribution with solid algorithmic grounding and scalability. With clearer empirical evidence of the benefits of the spatial prior and improved presentation, this work could be a valuable addition to the toolbox for large-scale neuroimaging analysis.
