Summary
The paper proposes an online dictionary-learning framework for neuroimaging that enforces both sparsity and spatial structure on dictionary atoms. Sparsity is imposed via an L1-type constraint (e.g., simplex or L1-ball), and spatial smoothness/compactness is encouraged by a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty on the atoms, leading to piecewise-smooth “blob-like” components. Codes are obtained via ridge regression, and the dictionary is updated with block-coordinate descent (BCD), using a small number of FISTA iterations for the Laplacian-regularized subproblem. The approach extends the online dictionary learning of Mairal et al. (2010) to structured atoms with a reported 2–3x computational overhead, while retaining scalability to large n and p. Experiments on HCP fMRI (n=500) show that the method yields more interpretable and stable components than standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and ICA-based baselines, with competitive explained variance and behavioral prediction performance.

Relation to prior work
This work builds directly on online sparse dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) and connects to structured sparsity and GraphNet-style smoothness (e.g., Grosenick et al.). It contrasts with TV-L1-based decompositions for brain atlases (e.g., Abraham et al.), which are effective but computationally heavy in alternating schemes. It also situates itself relative to group ICA approaches (Varoquaux et al.), arguing for spatially localized, sparse components rather than dense ICA maps.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for learning data-driven ROIs that capture inter-subject variability with spatial structure.
- A simple, elegant formulation combining an L1-type constraint with a Laplacian penalty on atoms; practical algorithm based on BCD with FISTA inner loops.
- Scalability: online updates, minibatches, and modest overhead vs. SODL; competitive running time compared to CanICA.
- Empirical gains: cleaner, more interpretable, and more stable components than SODL; favorable early learning curves; competitive predictive performance.
- Implementation planned in a widely used library (Nilearn), enhancing reproducibility and impact.

Weaknesses
- The problem is non-convex. The paper does not provide a formal existence/uniqueness result for global solutions or statistical guarantees (e.g., recovery/consistency). Convergence arguments focus on local convergence of the BCD scheme under standard assumptions.
- Hyperparameter selection (γ for Laplacian, τ for sparsity, α for codes) is critical; guidance is partly heuristic. The analysis of safe ranges and time-varying schedules could be made more explicit and principled.
- Some claims on step-size/Lipschitz behavior suggest pitfalls for large γ, but practical tuning rules are only sketched.
- Writing and presentation need polishing (typos, grammar, minor notation issues), which occasionally hinder readability.

Evaluation by criteria
Quality: The algorithmic contribution is sound: the Laplacian-regularized update reduces to a strongly convex denoising problem solved efficiently with FISTA inside a BCD loop. Convergence to a stationary point is plausible under cited conditions. Empirical evaluation is thorough for one large dataset, with meaningful baselines (SODL, CanICA) and multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, prediction). However, theoretical analysis is limited (no existence or statistical guarantees), and sensitivity to hyperparameters could be better quantified.

Clarity: The overall story is understandable, and the algorithmic steps are outlined. Still, several typos/phrasing issues and some dense derivations could be clarified. More explicit guidance on choosing γ, τ, α and on computational complexity per iteration would help practitioners.

Originality: Using a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatially compact, smooth atoms within an online dictionary-learning framework is a non-trivial and practical extension of Mairal et al. Compared with prior structured decompositions (e.g., TV-L1), the online, scalable realization is novel and of interest to NIPS.

Significance: The method addresses a common need in neuroimaging—learning structured, interpretable population-level components at scale—and is likely to be adopted in practice. Beyond neuroimaging, it may interest communities working on structured matrix factorization and large-scale representation learning.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, scalable extension of SODL to structured atoms with clear empirical benefits.
- Improved interpretability and stability without prohibitive cost.
- Relevance and impact for neuroimaging and structured factorization.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited theory: no existence/optimality or statistical recovery guarantees beyond local convergence.
- Hyperparameter tuning guidance is heuristic; performance may depend on careful selection.
- Writing/presentation issues that reduce clarity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a formal existence argument for minimizers under the compact constraint set and continuity (even if only per block), and clarify convergence guarantees (e.g., to critical points).
- Offer more prescriptive hyperparameter guidelines (e.g., scaling of γ with At, practical ranges, sensitivity plots), and discuss trade-offs between ridge vs. L1 codes.
- Add ablations (number of FISTA steps; effect of γ and τ on EV, stability, prediction) and report variability across random initializations/seeds.
- Improve proofreading and fix typos; clarify notation and the derivation of the dictionary update.

After rebuttal
I am satisfied with the authors’ clarification regarding the convergence behavior of the algorithm. I am willing to raise my Clarity/Presentation score from 2 to 3.
