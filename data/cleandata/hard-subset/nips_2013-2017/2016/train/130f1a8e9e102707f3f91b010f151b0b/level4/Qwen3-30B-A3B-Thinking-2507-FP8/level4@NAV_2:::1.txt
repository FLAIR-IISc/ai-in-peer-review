

This paper introduces Smooth-SODL, a novel extension of online dictionary learning for brain imaging applications that incorporates spatial structure through Laplacian regularization. The authors propose a method that simultaneously enforces sparsity (via ℓ1-norm constraints) and spatial smoothness (via Laplacian penalty) on dictionary atoms to better delineate brain structures from functional images. The work builds upon Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework while maintaining scalability to large datasets, with experiments demonstrating improved interpretability and better capture of inter-subject variability compared to state-of-the-art methods like SODL and CanICA on HCP task fMRI data.

The paper's primary strengths lie in its well-motivated application to neuroimaging, where the authors effectively address the important challenge of defining brain regions in a principled way using inter-subject variability. The proposed method provides a computationally efficient solution (only 2-3x slower than standard SODL) that scales to large datasets, making it particularly valuable for modern neuroimaging studies. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including visual assessment, explained variance, stability metrics, and behavioral prediction tasks, all demonstrating clear advantages of the spatial regularization approach.

However, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings in theoretical analysis and presentation. The authors provide no theoretical guarantees for their proposed algorithm, including convergence properties, error bounds, or optimality conditions, which is particularly concerning given the algorithmic novelty and the importance of such guarantees in machine learning papers. The lack of theoretical foundation makes it difficult to assess the fundamental properties of the method. Additionally, the manuscript contains numerous writing errors and unprofessional elements, such as the persistent comment "146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v" in the final version, suggesting insufficient proofreading and attention to detail. These issues undermine the paper's credibility and scientific rigor.

While the empirical results are promising, the absence of theoretical analysis represents a critical gap that needs to be addressed for this work to be considered a strong scientific contribution to the field. The authors should provide a thorough theoretical justification for their approach, including convergence guarantees and analysis of the impact of regularization parameters, before the paper can be recommended for acceptance.
