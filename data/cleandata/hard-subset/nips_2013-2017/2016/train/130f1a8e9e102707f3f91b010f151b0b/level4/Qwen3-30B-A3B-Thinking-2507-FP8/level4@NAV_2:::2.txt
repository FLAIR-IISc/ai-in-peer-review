

This paper presents an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals, extending the established framework of [14] with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply their method to empirical data, report the effects of varying tuning parameters, and compare performance with two alternative methods.

Technical quality: The model definition is clear and the algorithms follow a well-established framework [14], appearing technically sound. However, the paper does not clearly demonstrate the empirical advantages of the Sobolev prior in the results section. Specifically, Figure 2 does not quantify how the proposed method (SSMF) improves upon alternatives in terms of spatial contiguity or neuroscientific interpretability. The authors should provide specific, quantifiable metrics related to neuroscience to support their claim that SSMF produces "more structured" results. After reviewing the authors' rebuttal, which provides additional informative results, I have raised my technical quality rating from 2 to 3. I recommend the authors include these results in their final version and provide more explicit quantitative measures of the structural improvements.

Novelty/originality: The paper makes a notable contribution by extending [14] to incorporate Sobolev priors for encouraging spatially structured dictionary atoms, which represents a meaningful advance in the field.

Potential impact: This work provides an efficient online algorithm for structured dictionary learning that could become a valuable tool in neuroscience and medical imaging. While the current application is to neuroimaging, the method could potentially be extended to other domains like image segmentation and regional sensing.

Presentation: While the paper's organization is generally acceptable, it suffers from several editing issues that hinder comprehension. There are numerous typos and unclear descriptions that need correction. Specific issues include: (1) Inconsistency in the matrix decomposition equation (Lines 67-68) regarding transpose symbols; (2) Missing reference to HCP dataset (Line 73); (3) Grammatical error in Line 83 ("can imposes" should be "can impose"); (4) Unnecessary repetition of "a single" (Line 157); (5) Typo in "th how" (Line 169); (6) Inconsistency in the sign notation between Equations (6) and (8); (7) Unclear description of Figure 2 (different slices for different methods make comparison difficult); (8) Ambiguous description of Figure 3 (unclear what is being predicted and how the results relate to the caption). The authors should clarify these issues in their revision.
