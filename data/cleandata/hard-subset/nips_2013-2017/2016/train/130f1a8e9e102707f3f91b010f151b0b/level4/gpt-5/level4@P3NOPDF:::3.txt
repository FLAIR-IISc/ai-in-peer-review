This paper extends dictionary learning with a Sobolev-type prior to encourage spatial smoothness and consistency in learned components for brain imaging data. The problem is important and the approach is intuitively appealing: introducing spatial regularization should yield more anatomically coherent components and potentially improve interpretability. The manuscript is generally well motivated and the figures suggest visually smoother, blob-like components.

However, in its current form, it is difficult to assess the impact and substantive benefit of the proposed method. The main empirical support relies on qualitative visual comparisons of spatial maps. While these figures are illustrative, qualitative assessment alone is subjective and makes it hard to judge whether the method captures meaningful neurobiological structure or simply imposes smoothness. In particular, if the prior directly penalizes roughness and rewards spatial coherence, smoother components are an expected outcome; demonstrating that this smoothness corresponds to improved accuracy, stability, or cross-subject consistency is crucial.

Key concerns:

- Validation is largely qualitative. The paper would benefit from quantitative metrics that link the imposed smoothness to improvements in reliability or biological plausibility. For instance:
  - Held-out reconstruction error or explained variance to show that regularization does not overly degrade fit.
  - Test-retest or split-half reliability of components within subjects.
  - Cross-subject consistency after alignment (e.g., Dice overlap, spatial correlation, clustering stability), to evaluate whether the method consolidates homologous regions across individuals.
  - Stability under resampling or perturbations (e.g., bootstrap stability, perturbation of input data or seeds).
  - Downstream task performance (e.g., predicting behavioral or clinical variables), to demonstrate utility beyond visualization.

- Positioning with respect to prior work. There is an existing literature on spatially regularized dictionary learning and related models (e.g., Laplacian or graph-regularized sparse coding, total variation penalties, spatial ICA/NMF). The paper should more clearly articulate what is novel about the Sobolev prior relative to these approaches, both theoretically and empirically. Does the Sobolev term offer advantages over Laplacian/Tikhonov/TV regularization in terms of resolution, edge preservation, or bias-variance trade-offs?

- Baselines and ablations. To isolate the contribution of the Sobolev prior, comparisons should include:
  - Standard dictionary learning without spatial regularization.
  - Alternatives such as Laplacian regularization, total variation, and graph-structured penalties.
  - Non-dictionary methods commonly used in neuroimaging (e.g., ICA, sparse NMF) as sanity checks.
  - An ablation varying the order and weight of the Sobolev term to examine sensitivity and the smoothness–accuracy trade-off.

- Data regime and interpretability. The motivation that smoothness helps when data are scarce is reasonable, but with large public datasets (e.g., HCP), it is less clear that smoothing addresses the main bottlenecks. It would help to:
  - Demonstrate benefits in both low-data and high-data regimes (e.g., subsampling studies).
  - Show that smoothing does not blur or merge distinct functional parcels, perhaps by comparing to anatomical or functional atlases and quantifying boundary fidelity.

- Practical aspects. The paper would be stronger with details on:
  - Hyperparameter selection (how the regularization strength and Sobolev order are chosen); ideally via cross-validation or an evidence criterion.
  - Computational complexity, runtime, and scalability to high-resolution datasets.
  - Robustness to preprocessing choices, registration quality, and whether analyses are performed in volume or on cortical surfaces.

- Presentation. Some figures are difficult to interpret without guidance, and it is not clear which differences are statistically significant or practically important. Adding quantitative summaries alongside visuals (e.g., distributions of smoothness measures, overlap scores) would increase clarity. A more explicit discussion of limitations (e.g., risk of oversmoothing, dependence on alignment) would also be valuable.

Overall assessment: The paper tackles a meaningful problem and proposes a principled modification to dictionary learning that yields visually smoother spatial maps. However, the current evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate that the method improves neuroscientific validity, reliability, or utility beyond producing smoother-looking components. Without stronger quantitative validation, clearer comparisons to established spatial regularizers, and analyses of cross-subject consistency, the contribution is difficult to assess. I encourage the authors to add the suggested experiments and clarifications. With more rigorous validation and positioning relative to prior work, the method could be a useful addition to the neuroimaging toolbox; in its current state, I am not convinced of its significance. My recommendation is borderline to weak reject.
