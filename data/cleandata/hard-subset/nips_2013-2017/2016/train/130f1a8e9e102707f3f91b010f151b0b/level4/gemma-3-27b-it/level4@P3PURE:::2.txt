This paper presents an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals, extending the established framework of [14] with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply their method to empirical data, report the effect of varying tuning parameters, and compare performance against two alternative methods. 

Technical quality: I initially assessed the technical quality as marginally below a 3, and have revised it to a 3 following the rebuttal. The model definition is clear, and the algorithm follows a well-established framework [14], appearing generally sound. However, the presentation somewhat obscures the empirical advantages of introducing the Sobolev prior. Specifically, Figure 2 does not clearly demonstrate how the SSMF method outperforms the alternatives. Is the spatial boundary smoothness improved quantitatively? Does SSMF identify connections between regions known *a priori* to be connected, while the other methods do not? Beyond stating that SSMF results are “more structured,” the authors should explicitly define criteria – justified by neuroscience literature – and provide quantitative measurements demonstrating improvement according to those criteria. The results provided in the rebuttal are very informative and address this concern; I strongly encourage the authors to include these new results in the revision, and to further demonstrate the structured nature of the learned atoms (with quantitative measurements) and their meaningfulness with reference to established neuroscience findings.

Novelty/originality: The paper makes a notable original contribution by extending [14] through the application of Sobolev priors to encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms.

Potential impact or usefulness: Dictionary learning is a valuable approach for explaining neuroimaging data across large subject groups, and the theoretical benefits of introducing spatial priors—such as the Sobolev prior—are clear. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm for doing so, potentially adding a useful tool to the neuroscience and medical imaging community. Exploring further applications, such as image segmentation and regional sensing, could broaden its impact.

Presentation: The general organization of the paper is adequate, but the manuscript would benefit significantly from careful editing. Numerous confusing descriptions and typos hinder understanding. I detail specific concerns below:

*   Between lines 67 and 68, the transpose symbol in the matrix decomposition equation appears to be misplaced. Should it apply to each individual $V^j$ (j = 1,..., r)?
*   Line 73 lacks a reference for the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset.
*   Line 83 contains a grammatical error: “The Graph-Net penalty can imposes…” should read “The Graph-Net penalty can impose…”
*   Line 146 includes a bolded sentence that appears to be a co-author’s comment and should be removed or integrated into the text.
*   The last lines of Equations (6) and (8) are inconsistent: $V^j +At[j,j]^{-1}(VAt^j -Bt^j)$ versus $At[j,j] V^j -(VAt^j - Bt^j)$. Also, in the last line of Equation (6), I assume $V$ refers to the dictionary from the previous iteration (i.e., the jth column is not the variable $v$ being optimized).
*   Line 157 contains a repetition: “a single, a single.”
*   Line 169 contains a typo: “We did an extensive study of th how…” should read “We did an extensive study of how…”
*   Section 3.3, near line 147 below Algorithm 2, would benefit from an explicit mathematical/algorithmic solution to Equation (6), potentially in the appendix.
*   Figure 2 presents results from different methods at different horizontal and sagittal slices, making direct comparison difficult. Based on the figure, I do not follow the claim on line 222 that SSMF results are more “structured.”
*   Figure 3 lacks clarity regarding what is being predicted. If regressing brain data onto behavioral metrics, is this based on $U_i$'s? The legends are not explained in the main text or the figure caption. Furthermore, the figure does not appear to show the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption. The description on lines 224-227, claiming R^2 values greater than 0.1, is difficult to interpret; which curves are being referenced?
