- Proposed method: multivariate online dictionary learning for brain images combining sparsity with a Sobolev penalty to produce piecewise-smooth, compact “blobs.”
- Builds on Mairal et al. (2010) and targets automated ROI definition from multi-subject functional imaging by leveraging inter-subject variability.
- Claimed novelty: unique combination of online learning, sparsity, and Sobolev spatial prior applied at large neuroimaging scale versus prior batch methods.
- Strength — scalability: online algorithm can handle large datasets (e.g., HCP).
- Strength — principled spatial prior: Sobolev penalty enforces structured smoothness and compactness of components.
- Strength — clear motivation: tackles high dimensionality and the need for principled ROI definition across subjects.
- Strength — experiments: comprehensive evaluation on a large public dataset with comparisons to CanICA and SODL.
- Strength — theoretical grounding: BCD algorithm well-described with convergence guarantees.
- Weakness — subjective evaluation: visual atom comparisons are hard to interpret and lack quantitative measures of quality.
- Weakness — limited novelty in spatial regularization: spatial smoothness is established in the field and benefit over existing methods is not convincingly shown.
- Weakness — hyperparameter sensitivity: tuning discussion is incomplete; lacks systematic sensitivity analysis.
- Weakness — insufficient validation of inter-subject consistency: behavioral prediction experiments do not directly demonstrate cross-subject component consistency.
- Weakness — limited computational-cost analysis: reports a 2–3× runtime increase vs SODL but lacks detailed complexity breakdown and broader comparisons.
- Arguments for acceptance: relevant problem, technically sound, scalable online approach, and promising large-scale results.
- Arguments against acceptance: evaluation is largely subjective, spatial-regularization novelty is limited, and analyses of hyperparameters and comparative benefits are insufficient.
- Overall assessment: technically solid and potentially valuable but requires more rigorous quantitative validation and deeper analysis of limitations.
- Recommendation: weak accept with major revisions.
- Requested revisions: provide quantitative atom evaluations (spatial coherence/consistency/anatomical alignment), compare to more spatially-regularized methods, run systematic hyperparameter sensitivity analyses, include metrics for inter-subject consistency, and give a detailed computational-cost breakdown.
