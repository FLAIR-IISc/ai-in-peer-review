### **Summary**

This paper proposes an extension to the online sparse dictionary learning framework (Mairal et al., 2010) for discovering brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data. The key contribution is the introduction of a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty on the dictionary atoms, in addition to the standard L1 sparsity constraint. The authors term this model "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL). The L1 penalty promotes sparse components, while the novel Laplacian penalty encourages these components to be spatially smooth and compact, forming contiguous "blobs" rather than scattered patterns. The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to massive datasets. The authors apply their method to fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project (HCP) and compare it against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA (CanICA), evaluating the results on qualitative interpretability, explained variance, stability, and predictive performance on behavioral scores.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Important Problem:** The task of defining meaningful functional regions of interest (ROIs) from population data in a principled, data-driven manner is a significant and challenging problem in neuroimaging. This work addresses a clear need in the field.
2.  **Technically Sound and Scalable:** The proposed method is a non-trivial and well-motivated extension of a state-of-the-art online dictionary learning algorithm. The online optimization framework is a major strength, making the approach computationally feasible for very large datasets, which are increasingly common in neuroimaging. The authors demonstrate that the additional computational cost of the smoothness penalty is modest.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation on a large, modern, and publicly available dataset (HCP). The comparison against relevant baselines like SODL and CanICA is appropriate and provides useful context for the results.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection):**

1.  **Subjective and Potentially Circular Evaluation:** The primary evidence for the superiority of the proposed method rests on the visual quality of the resulting brain maps (Figure 2a). However, assessing the "interpretability" of these maps is highly subjective and difficult for a non-expert to verify. A more significant concern is that the main result—producing smoother, more compact regions—is a direct and expected consequence of adding a smoothness penalty. The paper does not sufficiently demonstrate that this enforced smoothness provides a benefit beyond aesthetics, such as revealing novel neuroscientific insights or leading to significantly better performance on objective, downstream tasks.
2.  **Mismatch Between Method's Justification and Experimental Setting:** The authors rightly argue (and show in Table 1) that spatial regularization is most beneficial in low-data regimes. However, the main experiments are conducted on the HCP dataset, which is data-rich. In such a setting, one might expect that sufficient data would allow an algorithm like standard SODL to learn smooth, structured components without an explicit spatial prior. The use of a strong prior on a massive dataset raises the question of whether the method is simply finding what it is constrained to find, rather than uncovering structure inherent to the data.
3.  **Validation Does Not Fully Support Claims of Superiority:** The paper claims that Smooth-SODL "better captures inter-subject variability." The prediction of behavioral variables (Figure 2c) is a good way to test this claim objectively. However, the results show that the baseline CanICA model generally performs best on this task, while Smooth-SODL does not offer a clear advantage over the thresholded version of CanICA or even SODL in many cases. This finding undermines the central claim that the proposed model provides a superior representation of meaningful subject-level differences. The stability analysis is more favorable, but the predictive utility remains unconvincing.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Quantitative Metrics for "Structure":** To move beyond subjective visual assessment, the authors could report quantitative metrics that capture the notion of "structure" or "interpretability." For instance, they could measure the spatial sparsity, average size, or contiguity of the resulting components. This would provide an objective basis for the claim that the atoms are more "blob-like."
*   **Strengthening the Validation of Inter-Subject Variability:** The paper's motivation hinges on capturing inter-subject variability. A more direct way to validate this would be to use the learned components to show better separation between known subject groups or stronger correlation with subject traits than baselines. The current prediction task is a good start, but the results need to be stronger to be compelling. Perhaps another downstream task could better highlight the advantages of the structured components.
*   **Clarification on the Role of Smoothing:** The authors should further discuss the trade-off between pre-smoothing the data (as required for CanICA) and incorporating a smoothing penalty into the model itself. Is the proposed method simply a more principled way to achieve what is commonly done via pre-processing? A more direct comparison exploring this relationship would strengthen the paper.
*   **Minor Typos and Formatting:** The paper is generally well-written, but a final proofread would be beneficial. For example, in the abstract, "preliminary xperiments" should be "preliminary experiments." In Figure 2, the labels are small and difficult to read.

### **Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a technically sound and scalable algorithm for an important problem in computational neuroimaging. The idea of incorporating a spatial smoothness prior into an online dictionary learning framework is elegant. However, the manuscript's primary weakness lies in its evaluation. The evidence for the significance of the contribution is largely based on the visual appeal of the resulting maps, which is both subjective and an expected outcome of the regularization applied. The objective quantitative analyses, particularly the behavioral prediction task, do not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method offers a significant advantage over existing, simpler baselines in this data-rich setting. While the work is interesting, its impact is unclear without a stronger demonstration of its practical benefits on objective, scientifically relevant tasks.
