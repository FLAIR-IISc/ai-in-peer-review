

This paper proposes an online structured dictionary learning algorithm for neuroimaging data, extending the framework in [14] with a Sobolev prior to enforce spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors evaluate their method on empirical data, analyze parameter sensitivity, and compare performance against two alternative approaches.  

**Technical Quality (Rating: 3 - Acceptable)**  
The model definition and algorithm design are clear and build solidly on established work [14]. However, the paper initially failed to convincingly demonstrate the empirical advantage of the Sobolev prior. Specifically, Figure 2 does not clarify *how* the SSMF method outperforms alternatives (e.g., quantitatively smoother boundaries, identification of known anatomically connected regions). The claim that SSMF results are "more structured" lacks neuroscience-justified metrics or quantitative validation. *After reviewing the authors' rebuttal*, which provides compelling additional results (e.g., quantitative smoothness metrics and anatomical validation), I revise my rating from 2 to 3. The authors must incorporate these rebuttal results in the final revision and explicitly quantify structural improvements (e.g., via boundary smoothness metrics or region connectivity scores) and link them to neuroscience literature.  

**Novelty/Originality (Rating: 3)**  
The core novelty lies in applying Sobolev priors to enforce spatial structure in dictionary atoms—a meaningful extension of [14] for neuroimaging. This addresses a clear gap in structured dictionary learning for spatially correlated data.  

**Potential Impact (Rating: 3)**  
The method offers a valuable tool for neuroimaging analysis by enabling efficient, spatially constrained dictionary learning across subjects. It could significantly benefit neuroscience and medical imaging communities. The authors should further explore applications in image segmentation or regional sensing to strengthen impact.  

**Presentation (Rating: 2 - Weak)**  
The paper is generally organized but suffers from poor editing, with confusing explanations and typos that impede understanding. Key issues:  
- **Equation (6) vs. (8):** The sign in the last line differs (e.g., $V^j + A_t[j,j]^{-1}(V A_t^j - B_t^j)$ vs. $A_t[j,j] V^j - (V A_t^j - B_t^j)$). Clarify the correct form and confirm $V$ denotes the final dictionary.  
- **Typos/missing references:** Line 73 (HCP dataset reference missing), Line 83 ("can imposes"), Line 157 ("a single, a single"), Line 169 ("th how").  
- **Matrix notation:** Line 67–68—should the transpose apply to each $V^j$ (not the group)?  
- **Figure 2:** Results for different methods use inconsistent slice positions (e.g., horizontal/sagittal), preventing direct visual comparison. The claim of "more structured" results lacks justification.  
- **Figure 3:** Unclear what is predicted (e.g., behavioral metrics regression? Based on $U_i$?). Legends are unexplained, and the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption is absent. The description of "R² > 0.1" (Lines 224–227) is ambiguous without reference to specific curves.  
- **Section 3.3:** Algorithm 2 requires explicit derivation of the solution to Equation (6), even in an appendix.  
- **Line 146:** The bolded sentence appears to be an unedited co-author comment.  

**Summary**  
The paper presents a technically sound extension of structured dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior, addressing a relevant problem in neuroimaging. While the core idea is novel and impactful, the current manuscript lacks sufficient empirical validation of the Sobolev prior’s benefits and contains significant presentation issues. The authors’ rebuttal provides strong evidence for the method’s advantages, but these must be integrated into the revised paper with quantitative neuroscience-grounded metrics. Addressing the presentation errors (typos, figure inconsistencies, and unclear notation) is critical for clarity. With these revisions, the paper would make a solid contribution.
