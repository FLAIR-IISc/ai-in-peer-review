The authors propose extending dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior, aiming to improve the clarity and consistency of identified brain regions. While the problem is interesting, evaluating the impact of this approach proves challenging based on the presented results. 

The primary evaluation relies on visual comparison of identified “blobs” within brain images, assessing clarity and consistency. However, this visual assessment is largely inaccessible to reviewers without specialized neuroimaging expertise. The perceived improvement in blob definition may simply reflect the expected outcome of incorporating smoothness and spatial consistency penalties – it's unsurprising that regularization encouraging smoother solutions yields smoother results. While such regularization *could* be beneficial in scenarios with limited data, the context of the Human Connectome Project, with its abundance of data, diminishes the potential significance of this effect.

My primary concern is the lack of quantitative evaluation and a somewhat subjective validation process. The paper would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating concrete advantages beyond visual inspection. I anticipated this work might address the crucial challenge of consolidating differences in brain regions *across* subjects – a central goal in connectomics. Leveraging inter-subject variability and consistency as a means of validating the performance of the proposed algorithm would be a compelling contribution. For example, demonstrating that the learned dictionary captures features consistently observed across a large cohort, or that discrepancies correlate with known subject characteristics, would provide strong evidence of efficacy. 

Currently, the presented validation feels insufficiently rigorous to establish a clear benefit of the proposed method over existing dictionary learning approaches, or standard smoothing/regularization techniques. The authors need to more convincingly demonstrate that the Sobolev prior leads to *scientifically meaningful* improvements in brain region identification, beyond simply producing visually smoother outputs. Further investigation into the influence of parameter settings, and comparison with established methods using quantitative metrics, are crucial.
