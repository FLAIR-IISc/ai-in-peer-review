This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, aiming to identify brain regions. The authors extend previous online dictionary learning work (Mairal et al., 2010) by incorporating a Sobolev penalty to encourage structured, spatially-localized dictionary atoms (i.e., “blobs”). The goal is to achieve decompositions that delineate brain structures from functional images, leveraging both sparsity and smoothness.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in neuroimaging where defining regions of interest (ROIs) is crucial for statistical power and signal-to-noise ratio. It acknowledges the limitations of univariate models and the difficulties in defining ROIs *a priori*. The approach presented draws inspiration from clustering techniques but opts for a signal-based model reminiscent of Principal Component Analysis (PCA) or Independent Component Analysis (ICA), with added sparsity and structural constraints.  Related work includes earlier attempts at structured decompositions (e.g., Baldassarre et al.) and online dictionary learning methods (e.g., Mensch et al.), although this work aims to bridge the gap between scalability and spatial structure.

**Strengths:**

*   **Scalability:** The online learning framework, inherited from Mairal et al. (2010), allows the method to scale to large datasets, a critical requirement for neuroimaging applications with many subjects and voxels.
*   **Combined Regularization:** The simultaneous use of sparsity (L1 norm) and smoothness (Sobolev penalty) is a potentially effective strategy for extracting interpretable and spatially coherent brain regions. The authors argue this provides decompositions closer to known functional brain organization.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset demonstrate improvements over traditional L1-regularized dictionary learning, showing gains in explained variance and predictive performance.
*   **Detailed Algorithm:** The paper provides a comprehensive description of the algorithm, including BCD optimization steps and considerations for practical implementation, such as hyperparameter tuning and initialization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Novelty:** While the combination of online learning and Sobolev regularization is a reasonable extension, the core contribution feels incremental. The novelty relative to Mairal et al. (2010) and the substantial existing work in structured sparsity (e.g., TV regularization) could be more clearly articulated. The paper needs to better justify why this specific combination is particularly advantageous.
*   **Clarity of "Structure":** The definition of “structure” remains somewhat vague. While the authors explain it as piecewise smoothness and compactness, the motivation for explicitly imposing this structure beyond simply inducing sparsity is not fully compelling.  A clearer explanation of *why* this particular structure is expected or beneficial for brain image analysis is needed.
*   **Technical Details & Presentation:** The paper contains some technical inaccuracies and readability issues. Equation (1) appears to have a transposition error (should be V<sup>T</sup>U). More thorough explanation of design choices, such as the use of an L2 norm on the codes instead of an L1 norm as in Mairal et al. (2010) is needed. Several instances of awkward phrasing and potential typos detract from the clarity.
*   **Limited Comparison:**  While comparisons are made to CanICA and SODL, a more thorough evaluation against other state-of-the-art methods for brain region identification, such as spatial clustering algorithms or more sophisticated segmentation techniques, would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The method addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging – identifying meaningful brain regions from noisy data.
*   The online learning framework provides scalability, making it applicable to large datasets.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a clear improvement over baseline methods on a widely used dataset (HCP).
*   The paper provides a detailed algorithmic description that could be valuable to researchers in the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The novelty is relatively limited, and the paper lacks a compelling justification for the specific choice of regularization.
*   The clarity of the paper is hindered by technical inaccuracies, awkward phrasing, and a lack of detailed explanations for key design choices.
*   The comparison to existing methods is not exhaustive enough to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound approach to brain region learning, building upon existing work in online dictionary learning and incorporating a spatial regularization term. However, the incremental novelty and clarity issues raise concerns. While the empirical results are promising, further justification of the design choices and a more thorough comparison to alternative methods are needed to elevate this work to the standard expected at NIPS.  I currently lean towards **reject**, but would be open to reconsidering with a major revision addressing the identified weaknesses. Specifically, the authors should: 1) More clearly articulate the novelty and motivation for their approach. 2) Provide a more detailed explanation of the mathematical rationale behind the model design choices. 3) Improve the clarity and technical accuracy of the paper. 4) Conduct a more comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art methods.
