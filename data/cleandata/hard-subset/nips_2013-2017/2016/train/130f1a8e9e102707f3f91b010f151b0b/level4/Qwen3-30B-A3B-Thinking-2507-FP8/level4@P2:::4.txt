

This paper proposes an extension of online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) to incorporate spatial structure through a Laplacian penalty, combined with L1 sparsity, for brain imaging analysis. The authors aim to learn structured, sparse components that better capture functional brain organization from fMRI data. This builds on prior work in structured dictionary learning (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2014; Abraham et al., 2013) but adapts it to an online setting for scalability to massive datasets like the Human Connectome Project (HCP).

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field. The proposed Smooth-SODL model integrates a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness while maintaining the L1 sparsity constraint, resulting in a method that scales efficiently to large datasets. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation and demonstrate that the computational overhead is modest (only 2-3x slower than standard online dictionary learning), which is a significant advantage for large-scale brain imaging applications. The implementation will be made available in the Nilearn library, enhancing reproducibility.

The paper demonstrates thorough experimental evaluation using HCP data. The authors compare their method against SODL (the base online dictionary learning approach) and CanICA (a standard ICA-based method), evaluating both quantitative metrics (explained variance, prediction accuracy for behavioral variables) and qualitative assessments of the learned components. The results show that Smooth-SODL produces more spatially structured and interpretable components than SODL, while maintaining comparable explained variance on test data (55% vs 50% for SODL). The method also outperforms the thresholded CanICA (tCanICA) in stability and reproducibility metrics.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that limit its contribution. First, the claimed advantages of the proposed method are not convincingly demonstrated. While the authors show that Smooth-SODL produces more spatially structured components, the improvement in explained variance on test data is marginal (55% vs 50% for SODL), and the qualitative differences are subjective. The paper fails to provide a clear justification for when this approach should be preferred over simpler methods, particularly since the authors themselves acknowledge that with abundant data (like HCP), the benefit of spatial regularization diminishes as more data is incorporated (see Table 1). The learning curves in Table 1 show that the 14-fold advantage of Smooth-SODL over SODL at small sample sizes diminishes to near-equivalence with sufficient data, suggesting that the spatial regularization may be most beneficial only in data-scarce scenarios.

Second, the manuscript suffers from poor clarity and presentation. The writing is sometimes confusing, with inconsistent notation (e.g., inconsistent use of ΩLap and Laplacian penalty) and draft comments remaining in the text (line 146). The explanation of the algorithmic details (Section 3.1) is dense and difficult to follow without additional guidance. The authors also fail to clearly articulate the theoretical advantages of their approach over existing methods, particularly how their Laplacian-based regularization compares to other spatial regularization techniques like total variation.

Third, the significance claim is overstated. While the method is technically sound, the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate how this approach advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way for brain imaging applications. The authors claim that their method "better captures inter-subject variability," but the evidence provided (Fig. 2) doesn't convincingly show that this translates to meaningful improvements in downstream applications beyond marginal gains in stability and interpretability.

The paper also lacks sufficient discussion of limitations and potential failure modes. For instance, the authors acknowledge that the method is sensitive to the choice of γ (the Laplacian regularization parameter), but don't provide clear guidance on how to select it in practice. The claim that "the method works well even with random initialization" is not adequately supported by the experiments.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound extension to online dictionary learning with a modest computational cost, and the implementation will be valuable for the community. However, the claimed advantages over existing methods are not sufficiently demonstrated, the significance of the contribution is overstated, and the manuscript requires substantial improvement in clarity and presentation. While the work has potential, it doesn't yet meet the bar for acceptance at NIPS as a significant contribution to the field. The authors should strengthen their experimental evaluation to clearly demonstrate when and why their method is superior to existing approaches, particularly in scenarios where data is limited, and provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the benefits of their spatial regularization.
