- Summary: Proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning method with a Laplacian penalty to produce spatially smooth, sparse brain components scalable to large fMRI datasets.

- Motivation: Aims to discover neuroanatomically plausible ROIs that capture inter-subject variability in massive cohorts.

- Novelty: Integrates spatial smoothness prior into an online dictionary-learning framework, bridging efficient online methods and structured sparsity.

- Scalability & efficiency: Builds on Mairal et al. (2010) to handle datasets that don't fit in memory; Laplacian penalty adds only modest (~3x) overhead.

- Empirical evaluation: Thorough experiments vs SODL and CanICA using visual assessment, explained variance, stability, and behavioral prediction.

- Benefit of Laplacian regularization: Learning curves show Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL in low-data regimes, capturing variance more efficiently.

- Presentation issues: Manuscript has many grammatical errors, typos, and leftover internal comments, suggesting it is an unfinished draft.

- Theoretical analysis lacking: No formal convergence proof tailored to the proposed modifications and no justification for choosing Laplacian over alternatives like TV.

- Hyperparameter tuning: Empirical study provided, but practical details for selecting α, γ, τ at large scale are insufficient for reproducibility.

- Proofreading requested: Authors should thoroughly fix language, typos, and remove internal notes for clarity.

- Laplacian vs TV question: Reviewer asks authors to justify L2-gradient (Laplacian) choice versus TV (L1-gradient), considering neuroscientific and computational trade-offs.

- Convergence assumptions: Reviewer requests explicit mapping of [16]’s assumptions (e.g., compact data support, unique coding solution) to this method and dataset.

- Prediction results concern: CanICA beats Smooth-SODL on test prediction—reviewer asks for explanation why structural interpretability didn’t improve predictive performance.

- Overall recommendation: Promising, novel, and well-evaluated work but requires major revision to fix presentation and strengthen theoretical and methodological justification before publication.
