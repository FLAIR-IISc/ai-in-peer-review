

This paper proposes an extension of online dictionary learning for brain image analysis by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial structure in dictionary atoms. The authors introduce "Smooth-SODL," which builds upon Mairal et al.'s (2010) Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (SODL) framework, adding a Laplacian penalty to promote piece-wise smooth, compact atoms rather than scattered patterns. The method is designed to handle large-scale neuroimaging data (e.g., HCP dataset with 500 subjects), maintaining computational efficiency (only 2-3x slower than SODL) while improving interpretability of brain regions. The authors demonstrate their approach on fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project, comparing against SODL and CanICA, with claimed improvements in spatial structure, interpretability, and capturing inter-subject variability.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing a gap in the literature: previous structured dictionary learning methods for brain imaging either required prohibitively slow optimization (e.g., alternating minimization) or lacked computational efficiency for large-scale datasets. The authors' adaptation of the online learning framework with Laplacian regularization is a significant technical advancement that aligns with the growing trend of scalable methods in neuroimaging. While related work has explored structured penalties (e.g., [1, 25]), these approaches were computationally intractable for large datasets, making this work particularly relevant for modern neuroimaging.

**Strengths:** The paper excels in technical quality and computational efficiency. The theoretical foundation is sound, with appropriate convergence guarantees and careful analysis of the optimization challenges introduced by the Laplacian penalty. The experimental design is comprehensive, including multiple evaluation metrics (visual quality, explained variance, reproducibility, prediction performance), and the implementation details are sufficiently thorough for reproducibility. The comparison with SODL and CanICA provides a meaningful context for evaluating the method's advantages. The authors also provide valuable insights on hyperparameter selection, noting the delicate balance between sparsity, spatial structure, and data fidelity.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's most significant weakness lies in the subjective nature of its primary validation method. While the authors claim improved interpretability through visual comparison of dictionary atoms, the evidence is largely qualitative and not convincing to a non-specialist. The visual comparisons in Figure 2(a) are difficult to interpret without domain expertise, and the paper fails to provide objective metrics for evaluating the "structuredness" of the atoms. The claim that the method better captures inter-subject variability is not rigorously supported—while the authors show improved reproducibility metrics (0.4 for Smooth-SODL vs. 0.1 for SODL), they do not demonstrate how this translates to better scientific insights about brain organization.

The significance of the method is questionable given the scale of modern neuroimaging datasets. The authors acknowledge that with abundant data (like HCP), spatial regularization may not be necessary, yet they do not provide compelling evidence that their method is superior in large-scale settings. The learning curve analysis in Table 1 shows a 14-fold advantage for Smooth-SODL over SODL with minimal data (17 subjects), but this advantage diminishes as more data is added. For most practical applications with large datasets, the marginal improvement in interpretability may not justify the added computational cost.

**Originality:** The technical approach of incorporating Laplacian regularization into online dictionary learning is novel and well-motivated. The authors clearly distinguish their work from related approaches that use spatial regularization (e.g., [1, 25]) by emphasizing computational efficiency. However, the core idea of combining sparsity with spatial structure is not entirely new, as evidenced by the related work section.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear mathematical formulations. The algorithms are presented with sufficient detail for reproducibility. However, the experimental section could be strengthened by providing more objective metrics for evaluating spatial structure (e.g., blob compactness, smoothness scores) rather than relying solely on visual assessment.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a technically sound extension to online dictionary learning with clear computational advantages. However, the significance of the approach is limited by the lack of objective validation for the claimed improvements in interpretability and inter-subject variability capture. The visual evidence is insufficient to convince a general machine learning audience of the method's value, and the claimed advantages diminish with large datasets. The authors should address these concerns by providing more objective metrics for spatial structure and demonstrating the method's utility in specific neuroscientific contexts beyond visual appeal.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**
- *Pro:* Technical contribution is sound and well-executed; computational efficiency is maintained; implementation is open-source (Nilearn); provides valuable insights into hyperparameter selection.
- *Con:* Validation is primarily visual and subjective; significance is questionable for large-scale neuroimaging data; claimed advantages over existing methods are marginal in most practical settings.

Overall, the paper makes a modest technical contribution to the field of structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging. While the method is well-designed and efficient, the lack of compelling evidence for its scientific value—particularly given the abundance of large-scale neuroimaging data—limits its significance. I recommend rejection, though the authors may strengthen their case by providing more objective validation metrics and demonstrating clear neuroscientific insights enabled by their approach.
