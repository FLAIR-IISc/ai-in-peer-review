This paper proposes a novel multivariate online dictionary-learning method, dubbed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), for extracting structured and sparse components from brain images. The method extends the standard online dictionary-learning framework by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to impose piece-wise smooth and compact dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on a large public dataset, showcasing improved performance compared to state-of-the-art models.

The paper's main contribution lies in the introduction of a new regularizer that combines sparsity and smoothness constraints. This innovation enables the extraction of structured and denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability in brain data. The authors provide a thorough analysis of their method, including a detailed description of the algorithm and its convergence properties.

The experiments presented in the paper are well-designed and comprehensive, covering various aspects of the proposed method. The results demonstrate the superiority of Smooth-SODL over competing methods, including Canonical ICA (CanICA) and Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (SODL). The authors also investigate the impact of hyper-parameter selection on the performance of their method, providing valuable insights for practitioners.

One of the strengths of the paper is its clarity and presentation. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, followed by a detailed description of their method and its theoretical underpinnings. The experimental results are well-organized and easy to follow, making it simple for readers to understand the benefits and limitations of the proposed approach.

However, there are some areas for improvement. One issue is that the problem is non-convex, and the existence of the solution is not shown. While the authors provide an algorithm for obtaining a solution, its convergence properties are not rigorously established. Furthermore, the writing could be improved in some places, such as line 146.

Overall, I am satisfied with the paper's content and presentation. The introduction of a new regularizer is a significant contribution, and the experimental results demonstrate the potential of the approach. While there are some areas for improvement, I believe that the paper is well-suited for publication in its current form.
