This paper presents a method for learning sparse and spatially structured components from large-scale brain imaging data. The proposed model, named Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), extends the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by introducing a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatial smoothness in the dictionary atoms. This is combined with a standard L1-norm constraint to enforce sparsity. The goal is to produce decompositions that are more interpretable and better representative of functional brain regions than those produced by methods that enforce only sparsity or no structure at all. The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to massive datasets, which is a key challenge in modern neuroimaging. The method is evaluated on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (HCP) and compared against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA (CanICA).

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution of this work is the successful integration of a spatial structure penalty (the Laplacian term) into a scalable, online dictionary learning framework. While structured penalties have been used before for brain image decomposition, they have typically relied on slower, batch-optimization methods. Making this type of structured decomposition computationally feasible for massive datasets is a significant and timely contribution to the field of neuroimaging analysis.

*   **Improved Interpretability:** The qualitative results presented in Figure 2(a) convincingly demonstrate that the proposed Smooth-SODL method produces dictionary atoms that are visually cleaner, less noisy, and more spatially coherent (i.e., blob-like) than those from the unstructured SODL model. This directly addresses the paper's main motivation of learning more interpretable functional regions.

*   **Strong Performance in Low-Data Regimes:** A key strength, highlighted in Table 1, is the method's superior performance when data is limited. The spatially regularized model achieves a dramatically higher explained variance compared to the unstructured SODL model when trained on a small number of subjects. This demonstrates that the spatial prior provides substantial value, especially when data is not abundant enough to learn the structure on its own. This is a very relevant use case in many clinical and research settings.

*   **Computational Efficiency:** The authors report that their method is only about 2-3 times slower than the highly optimized vanilla SODL algorithm. This is a very reasonable computational trade-off for the significant improvements in component structure and interpretability.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection):**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript appears to have been written in haste and would benefit from careful proofreading and revision. The writing is often dense and difficult to follow. For instance, the derivation of the dictionary update step in equation (7) is not self-contained and refers the reader to another paper for details. More critically, the manuscript contains what appears to be a draft-stage comment ("refer to [16] for the details") in the main text of equation (7), which undermines confidence in the paper's readiness for publication.

*   **Insufficiently Demonstrated Superiority in the Large-Data Regime:** While the method's motivation is strong, the quantitative results on the full dataset do not consistently show a clear advantage over existing methods.
    *   In terms of explained variance (Fig. 2b), the non-sparse CanICA model performs best on both training and test data.
    *   In the behavioral prediction task (Fig. 2c), CanICA again appears to be the strongest performer on the test set. Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL, but the improvement is modest and does not establish it as the state-of-the-art.
    *   The paper should provide a more nuanced discussion of the trade-off between raw predictive power/explained variance (where ICA excels) and interpretability (where Smooth-SODL excels). The current presentation overstates the method's superiority without fully acknowledging the strengths of the baselines in certain metrics.

*   **Limited Novelty of the Regularization Technique:** While the integration into an online framework is novel, the core components of the model—the L1 penalty for sparsity and the Laplacian penalty for smoothness—are both well-established techniques in signal processing and machine learning. The contribution lies in the engineering and algorithmic combination rather than a fundamentally new modeling concept.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a valuable and computationally efficient method for learning structured brain networks from large-scale fMRI data. The core idea of adding a Laplacian penalty to an online dictionary learning algorithm is well-motivated and addresses a clear need in the field. The experiments effectively demonstrate that the method produces more interpretable results than its unstructured counterpart, particularly in low-data scenarios.

However, the paper is hampered by significant issues with clarity and presentation, including leftover draft comments. Furthermore, the empirical evaluation, while promising, does not fully support the claim that the proposed model is superior to existing state-of-the-art methods across all relevant metrics, especially in the large-data regime where its scalability is most relevant. The simpler CanICA baseline often achieves better quantitative performance, a trade-off that is not sufficiently discussed.

Overall, the paper presents a solid contribution that is relevant to the NIPS community. The scalability of the structured decomposition is a clear strength. I believe the weaknesses, primarily related to the clarity of the manuscript and the need for a more balanced discussion of the results, can be addressed in a revision. Therefore, I lean towards acceptance, conditioned on a thorough revision to improve the writing and contextualize the empirical results more carefully.
