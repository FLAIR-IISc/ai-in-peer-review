

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning for brain imaging that incorporates a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial structure in the dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate that this approach extracts more interpretable, structured brain regions compared to standard sparse dictionary learning and ICA-based methods on the Human Connectome Project dataset.

The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the problem of brain region definition by leveraging inter-subject variability rather than relying on anatomical segmentation. The method builds upon Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework, adding a Laplacian penalty to produce spatially structured dictionary atoms. The authors show that their approach maintains scalability to large datasets (500 subjects, 260k voxels) while producing more interpretable brain maps.

Strengths:
1. Quality: The paper provides a technically sound extension of existing online dictionary learning. The theoretical foundation for using the Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure is well-grounded, and the experimental results are comprehensive, including ablation studies on hyperparameters, learning curves, and prediction tasks on behavioral variables. The method scales well to large datasets as claimed.

2. Clarity: The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the figures effectively illustrate the qualitative differences between methods. The implementation will be made available in Nilearn, enhancing reproducibility.

3. Significance: The method addresses a real challenge in neuroimaging - defining brain regions in a principled way that captures inter-subject variability. The results show improved interpretability and performance across small, medium, and large-scale datasets, with potential applications in brain mapping and clinical neuroimaging.

Weaknesses:
1. Originality: While the paper extends online dictionary learning with spatial regularization, the core idea is not entirely novel. Similar approaches have been used in structured dictionary learning (e.g., [1, 25]) but with different optimization methods. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with these prior works to clarify the novelty.

2. Clarity: The paper would be stronger with a more precise definition of "structured" in the context of brain imaging. The connection between the Sobolev penalty and the desired brain structures (blobs, compact regions) could be better explained. The motivation for using the Laplacian penalty specifically to produce these structures needs more elaboration.

3. Significance: The paper claims that spatial regularization is crucial for capturing inter-subject variability, but the learning curves in Table 1 show that the gap between Smooth-SODL and SODL closes as more data is added. This suggests that with sufficient data, the spatial regularization may not be necessary, which weakens the significance claim.

4. Comparison: The comparison with CanICA is somewhat misleading. CanICA is an ICA-based method that doesn't enforce sparsity, while the authors' method does. The comparison should be framed as comparing structured sparse dictionary learning with unstructured methods, not specifically with CanICA.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The method provides a principled approach to brain region learning that leverages inter-subject variability
- The online framework maintains scalability to large datasets
- The proposed Sobolev regularization effectively produces more interpretable brain maps
- The results on behavioral prediction tasks demonstrate practical utility
- The implementation will be available in Nilearn, enhancing reproducibility

Arguments against acceptance:
- The novelty is somewhat incremental (extending existing online dictionary learning with spatial regularization)
- The connection between the Sobolev penalty and desired brain structures is not sufficiently explained
- The comparison with CanICA is not entirely fair, as CanICA doesn't enforce sparsity
- The claimed advantages diminish with larger datasets, as shown in the learning curves

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but the novelty is not strong enough to meet NIPS standards. While the method provides a useful tool for brain imaging, the incremental nature of the contribution, combined with the diminishing significance claim as data size increases, makes it difficult to justify acceptance over other more novel contributions. The paper would benefit from a more precise definition of "structured" and a clearer articulation of how this work differs from prior structured dictionary learning approaches.
