Summary
The paper proposes an online dictionary-learning framework, Smooth-SODL, for decomposing brain images into sparse, spatially structured components (“atoms”). Sparsity is enforced via an L1-type constraint on the atoms, while spatial structure (piecewise smooth, compact “blobs”) is encouraged with a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty. The method extends the online dictionary learning of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian regularizer into the dictionary update step, solved efficiently via block coordinate descent and a small number of FISTA iterations. Codes are obtained with ridge regression (mini-batches), and the approach scales to large n and p. Empirically, on HCP task fMRI data, Smooth-SODL is reported to produce more interpretable, less noisy components than standard SODL and CanICA, with a 2–3× runtime overhead over SODL.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) and related scalable approaches, positioning itself against PCA/ICA (e.g., CanICA) and slower structured sparse models (e.g., TV-L1-based dictionary learning). The Laplacian penalty connects to GraphNet-style regularization. The contribution is a practical, scalable integration of spatial regularization into an online framework.

Strengths
- Timely and relevant problem: data-driven, interpretable functional parcellations that account for inter-subject variability at scale.
- Methodologically simple and potentially impactful: an online solver with a lightweight Laplacian-regularized dictionary update.
- Empirical evidence suggests improved interpretability and stability of atoms over baselines, with modest additional runtime.
- Implementation plans in Nilearn increase potential impact and reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- No theoretical analysis is provided to substantiate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm. While the paper references convergence properties of the underlying online framework, the specific effect of the Laplacian penalty on convergence rates, stability, or statistical properties is not analyzed.
- The paper suffers from significant writing and presentation issues, which undermine clarity. There are numerous typos and inconsistencies, and an internal comment remains in the text: “146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v.” This suggests the draft was not carefully proofread.
- The experimental evidence is described as preliminary; the evaluation, while suggestive, would benefit from more rigorous quantitative analysis and clearer reporting.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The optimization approach (BCD with ridge coding and FISTA-based atom updates under Laplacian regularization) is reasonable and grounded in existing techniques. However, the paper does not provide theoretical analysis of convergence behavior specific to the modified updates or guarantees on the quality of the learned representations, which weakens the scientific rigor.
- Empirical support: Results indicate improved interpretability and competitive explained variance relative to baselines. Still, the lack of deeper statistical analysis (e.g., sensitivity to hyperparameters beyond heuristic guidance, robustness across tasks/datasets) limits confidence.
- Carefulness: The presence of editing artifacts and inconsistent notation detracts from the technical presentation.

Clarity
- The manuscript is difficult to read in places due to typos, inconsistent use of notation (e.g., V vs. v), and occasional formula and indexing issues. The inclusion of an unresolved inline comment is a serious oversight.
- Organization is broadly acceptable, but algorithmic descriptions could be streamlined and notation standardized.

Originality
- The idea of combining L1 constraints with Laplacian regularization for spatially structured atoms is known, but integrating it into a scalable online dictionary-learning framework is a meaningful and useful extension of prior work.
- The contribution is primarily algorithmic and practical rather than theoretical.

Significance
- If robustly validated, the method could become a practical tool for large-scale, interpretable brain mapping, especially given its planned open-source release.
- The current lack of theoretical guarantees and presentation issues reduce the immediate scientific impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a scalable, practical method.
- Empirical results suggest improved interpretability and stability over standard baselines with manageable computational cost.
- Potential for broad adoption via open-source integration.

Cons for acceptance
- No theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm’s properties beyond inherited guarantees from prior work.
- Serious clarity and presentation problems, including a leftover internal comment in the main text.
- Evaluation is preliminary and could be strengthened.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide at least a minimal theoretical treatment (e.g., conditions under which the modified BCD retains convergence properties, bounds on Lipschitz constants under the Laplacian term, or stability analysis).
- Thoroughly proofread the manuscript, fix typos, standardize notation (e.g., consistent use of V vs. v, L1 notation), and remove internal comments.
- Clarify the interplay between the sparsity constraint and Laplacian regularization, justify the time-varying schedules (e.g., αt ∼ t−1/2; γt scaling), and report sensitivity analyses more systematically.
- Strengthen the empirical section with clearer metrics, confidence intervals, and additional datasets or tasks.

Overall recommendation
Interesting and potentially useful contribution, but the absence of theoretical analysis and the poor state of the writing significantly detract from the paper. I encourage the authors to address these issues; with improved rigor and presentation, the work could be impactful.
