This paper proposes an online algorithm for learning a structured dictionary to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals. It extends a well-established framework [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior to promote spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply the method to empirical data, examine the effect of tuning parameters, and compare performance with two alternative approaches.

Technical quality: I would rate this marginally below 3 if given the option. The model is clearly defined, and the algorithms follow a solid, established framework [14]. However, the presentation weakens the empirical case for the Sobolev prior. In particular, the results do not clearly demonstrate the added value of this prior. In Figure 2, it is unclear in what respect SSMF outperforms the alternatives. Are spatial boundaries quantitatively smoother? Are there known anatomically connected regions that only SSMF recovers? Rather than stating that SSMF yields “more structured” results, the paper should define explicit, literature-grounded criteria (e.g., measures of spatial contiguity, alignment with known atlases, cluster compactness), and report quantitative improvements according to these criteria.

Added after reading the rebuttal: The additional results in the rebuttal are very informative. I am raising my technical quality rating from 2 to 3. I encourage the authors to include these new results in the revision and, in addition, to provide quantitative evidence that SSMF learns more structured atoms and that these atoms are more neuroscientifically meaningful, with appropriate references.

Novelty/originality: The key novel contribution is the extension of [14] with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms. This is a substantive and relevant innovation for group-level neuroimaging analysis.

Potential impact/usefulness: Dictionary learning is a useful approach for modeling neuroimaging data across large cohorts, and incorporating Sobolev or related spatial priors should, in principle, improve interpretability and performance. The proposed online algorithm appears efficient and could become a valuable tool for the neuroscience/medical imaging community. The method may also be applicable to problems such as image segmentation and regional sensing.

Presentation: The overall organization is acceptable, but the manuscript appears insufficiently edited. Several confusing descriptions and typographical errors impede comprehension. Specific issues include:

- Matrix decomposition (between lines 67–68): Should the transpose applied to the group of V^j (j = 1, …, r) be applied to each individual V^j instead?
- Line 73: The reference for the HCP dataset is missing.
- Line 83: “The Graph-Net penalty can imposes …” (grammar).
- Line 146: A bold sentence appears to be an unresolved co-author comment.
- Equations (6) and (8): The last lines are inconsistent. One shows V^j + At[j,j]^-1 (V At^j − Bt^j), while the other shows At[j,j] V^j − (V At^j − Bt^j). Please resolve the sign/scaling discrepancy. Also, in the last line of Eq. (6), I assume V denotes the dictionary from the previous iteration (i.e., the jth column is not the optimization variable v); please clarify.
- Line 157: “a single, a single” (duplicate phrase).
- Line 169: “We did an extensive study of th how” (typo/grammar).
- Section 3.3 (paragraph near line 147, just below Algorithm 2): Please provide the explicit mathematical/algorithmic solution to Eq. (6), at least in the appendix.
- Figure 2: Results from different methods are shown on different horizontal and sagittal slices, making direct visual comparison difficult. As presented, I cannot reconcile the claim on line 222 that SSMF results are more “structured.”
- Figure 3: It is unclear what is being predicted. Is this a regression of brain data on behavioral metrics, and if so, is it based on the U_i’s? The legends are not explained in the text or caption. The figure also does not appear to include the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption. I also cannot follow the claim on lines 224–227 about R^2 > 0.1; please indicate which curves support this.

In summary, the paper presents a promising and technically sound extension of dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior, implemented in an efficient online algorithm. The empirical validation and presentation need strengthening—particularly clear, quantitative demonstrations of the benefits of the Sobolev prior and improved clarity in figures, equations, and text. The rebuttal addresses some concerns; incorporating those results and the suggested clarifications would substantially improve the paper.
