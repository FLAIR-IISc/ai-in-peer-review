

This paper proposes an extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging data analysis, introducing a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage spatial structure in dictionary atoms. The authors extend Mairal et al.'s (2010) sparse online dictionary learning framework to produce spatially coherent brain regions from functional imaging data. The method addresses the challenge of defining meaningful brain regions without relying on predefined anatomical regions or clustering, instead leveraging inter-subject variability to identify functional units. This approach differs from standard methods like PCA/ICA (e.g., CanICA) that produce dense, less interpretable components, and from traditional dictionary learning that lacks spatial structure constraints.

The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on Human Connectome Project (HCP) data, comparing it against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and CanICA. The authors show that their Smooth-SODL method produces more spatially structured dictionary atoms that better capture inter-subject variability, with only a modest (2-3x) increase in computational cost compared to SODL.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-motivated, building logically on previous work. The theoretical foundation is solid, with appropriate use of Laplacian regularization to enforce spatial smoothness. The empirical results generally support the claims, though the quantitative demonstration of spatial structure improvement could be strengthened. The authors demonstrate that their method captures more meaningful brain regions across varying data sizes, with the advantage being most pronounced with smaller datasets (e.g., 14x improvement in explained variance with 17 subjects). The added computational cost (3x SODL) is justified by the improved interpretability and performance in small-data regimes. The paper would benefit from more specific quantitative measures of spatial structure (e.g., boundary smoothness metrics, spatial coherence scores) to complement the qualitative comparisons in Figure 2.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized, but contains several presentation issues that hinder comprehension. There are inconsistent mathematical notations (e.g., Equation 6 vs. Equation 8), missing references (Line 73), and typos ("can imposes" in Line 83). The description of the algorithmic details (Section 3.1) is somewhat difficult to follow due to inconsistent notation and missing intermediate steps. The figures (particularly Figure 2) present results from different anatomical slices, making direct comparison challenging. The authors should clarify in the main text how Figure 2's results correspond to the description in Lines 222-227. The prediction results in Figure 3 lack clear explanation of what is being predicted (behavioral scores from U_i or X) and the significance of the R² values.

**Originality:** The core contribution—applying a Sobolev penalty to online dictionary learning for spatial structure in brain imaging—is novel. While structured dictionary learning has been explored in other domains, this is the first application to neuroimaging that integrates spatial structure with online learning for large-scale data. The paper clearly differentiates from related work (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2012; Abraham et al., 2014) by providing an efficient online solution rather than slow alternating optimization. This represents a meaningful extension of the Mairal et al. framework.

**Significance:** The method addresses a significant challenge in neuroimaging analysis: the need for interpretable, data-driven brain regions that capture meaningful inter-subject variability. By producing structured, sparse dictionary atoms that align with known brain organization, the approach provides a more principled alternative to ROI-based methods that rely on anatomical priors or ad hoc thresholding. The online nature of the algorithm makes it suitable for increasingly large neuroimaging datasets. While the authors claim potential for broader applications (e.g., image segmentation), the paper could better articulate how this method advances the field beyond existing approaches.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a clear technical contribution that addresses a real problem in neuroimaging analysis. The implementation as part of the Nilearn library (mentioned in the acknowledgments) will facilitate adoption by the neuroscience community. The experiments comprehensively evaluate the method across multiple metrics (reproducibility, explained variance, predictive performance), with particularly compelling results for small-to-medium datasets where spatial regularization provides the most significant benefit.

**Weaknesses:** The paper would be strengthened by:
1. More specific quantitative measures of spatial structure (beyond qualitative "more structured" claims)
2. Consistent mathematical notation throughout
3. Clearer figure captions and presentation of results
4. Better integration of neuroscience literature to explain why the observed brain regions are meaningful
5. Clarification of the prediction task in Figure 3

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a technically sound, novel approach that addresses an important problem in neuroimaging analysis with clear potential impact. The method extends existing work in a meaningful way, provides empirical evidence of its benefits, and offers an efficient implementation. While the presentation could be improved, the core contribution is significant and well-executed. With minor revisions to address the clarity issues and strengthen the quantitative demonstration of spatial structure, this paper would make a strong contribution to the NIPS community.
