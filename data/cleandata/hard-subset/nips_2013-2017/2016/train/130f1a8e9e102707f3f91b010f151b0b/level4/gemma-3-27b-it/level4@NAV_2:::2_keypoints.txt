- Paper proposes an online structured dictionary learning algorithm with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatially contiguous atoms for neuroimaging data.  
- Technical approach is clear and builds on a solid, well-established framework [16], with algorithms appearing sound.  
- Initial results did not clearly demonstrate the empirical advantage of the Sobolev prior; quantitative measures and neuroscience-justified criteria were missing.  
- Rebuttal supplied informative additional results; those should be incorporated into the revision to quantify and demonstrate improved structure and neuroscientific relevance.  
- Novel contribution: extending [16] by applying Sobolev priors to promote spatially structured dictionary atoms.  
- Significance: method is useful for explaining inter-subject neuroimaging variability, is scalable (online), and could impact neuroimaging/medical imaging applications (e.g., segmentation, regional sensing).  
- Manuscript needs editing for clarity and conciseness; multiple typographical and wording errors noted.  
- Specific equation issues: transpose placement in matrix decomposition, sign/discrepancy in the last lines of Eq.(6) and Eq.(8), and clarity that V refers to previous-iteration dictionary in Eq.(6).  
- Request to include the mathematical/algorithmic solution to Eq.(6) (at least in the appendix).  
- Figure 2: comparisons use different slices, preventing direct visual comparison and failing to support the “more structured” claim.  
- Figure 3: unclear what the predicted variable is, whether regression used U_i, unexplained legends, and missing PCA baseline described in the caption.  
- Missing dataset reference: HCP dataset not cited where mentioned.  
- List of minor corrections: wording fixes (e.g., “can imposes” → “can impose”), stray co-author comment to remove, repeated words (“a single, a single”), and other copyediting issues.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important neuroimaging problem, theoretically sound Sobolev prior, scalable online algorithm, and experiments (including rebuttal) show potential.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lacked initial quantitative evidence for advantage, several editorial issues, and connection to neuroscience literature on interpreting learned structures could be stronger.
