### **Summary**

This paper proposes "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL), an algorithm designed to learn structured and sparse dictionary atoms from large-scale neuroimaging data. The method extends the well-established online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty. This additional regularization term encourages the resulting dictionary atoms to be spatially smooth and contiguous, forming "blobs" that are more consistent with the expected structure of brain regions. The authors demonstrate the method on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project, comparing its performance against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA (CanICA) on metrics such as explained variance, stability, and predictive power for behavioral scores.

### **Evaluation**

This paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging: the scalable, data-driven identification of meaningful brain regions from large population datasets. The proposed method is a logical and valuable extension of existing work. However, the manuscript in its current form requires significant revision to strengthen its empirical claims and improve its clarity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—integrating a spatial smoothness prior into a scalable online dictionary learning framework—is novel and significant. Standard sparse methods can produce scattered, noisy components, while methods that enforce structure are often computationally prohibitive for large datasets. This work provides an efficient algorithm to bridge that gap, which could be highly impactful for the neuroscience and medical imaging communities.
*   **Scalability:** The online nature of the algorithm is a key advantage, making it well-suited for the increasingly large datasets being generated in the field. The paper demonstrates that this scalability is preserved, with only a modest increase in runtime compared to the unstructured baseline.
*   **Potential Impact:** If validated effectively, this method could become a valuable tool for data-driven ROI definition, dimensionality reduction, and biomarker discovery in neuroimaging. The authors' plan to release the implementation in a widely used library (Nilearn) further increases its potential for broad adoption.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Insufficient Empirical Justification:** The paper's primary weakness is that the experimental results do not sufficiently demonstrate the claimed advantages of the Laplacian regularization. While the text asserts that the Smooth-SODL components are "more structured" and "less noisy," the visual evidence in Figure 2a is not conclusive. To strengthen the paper, the authors should provide quantitative metrics to support these claims. For example, metrics for spatial smoothness, compactness, or contiguity of the learned atoms would offer objective evidence. Furthermore, demonstrating that the identified regions better correspond to known neuroanatomical or functional parcels would provide crucial validation.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript appears to be an unpolished draft and contains numerous typos, inconsistencies, and unclear descriptions that detract from its quality and make it difficult to follow. The figures, in particular, are not presented in a way that facilitates clear comparison between methods. (Specific examples are detailed below).

### **Detailed Comments**

The following points should be addressed to improve the manuscript:

1.  **Quantitative Evaluation of "Structure":** The central claim of the paper is that the proposed method produces "more structured" dictionaries. This needs to be supported by more than visual inspection. The authors should consider reporting quantitative measures of smoothness or compactness for the atoms produced by each method.

2.  **Clarity of Figures:**
    *   **Figure 2a:** Displaying corresponding atoms from different methods on different anatomical slices makes direct comparison nearly impossible. All atoms in a given column should be displayed on the same canonical slices to highlight differences in spatial structure.
    *   **Figure 2c:** The legend for this figure is missing from the caption and the main text, making it difficult to interpret. The caption also mentions a "PCA" baseline that does not appear in the plot. The claim that R² scores are greater than 0.1 is hard to verify without knowing which curves correspond to which behavioral variables. It is also unclear what features were used for prediction (e.g., were the subject-specific codes `U_i` used?).

3.  **Mathematical and Algorithmic Clarity:**
    *   **Equation (1):** The decomposition is written as `Xi ≈ VUi`. Given the dimensions `V ∈ R^(p×k)` and `Ui ∈ R^(k×1)`, this is correct. However, the text refers to `V` as `[V1|...|Vk]`, which is standard. A common convention in this field is `X ≈ UV^T`. Please confirm the formulation is standard and clearly state the dimensions of all matrices.
    *   **Equations (7) and (8):** There is a sign inconsistency in the update rule. The argument of the `argmin` in Eq. (7) is `v, Vj + At[j, j]^-1 (Bjt - VAjt)`, while in Eq. (8) it appears to be `v, Vj + At[j, j]^-1 (Bjt - VAj)`. Please verify and correct the expressions. Also, it should be clarified that in this update step, `V` refers to the dictionary from the previous iteration.
    *   **Solving Equation (7):** The paper states that this subproblem can be solved efficiently with FISTA. It would be beneficial for reproducibility to provide the explicit gradient and algorithmic steps for this update, perhaps in the supplementary material.

4.  **Typos and Editorial Issues:** The paper requires careful proofreading. Examples include:
    *   Abstract: "preliminary xperiments"
    *   Section 2: The definition of the Ridge penalty on codes is `1/2 α||Ui||2^2`, but in Algorithm 1 (line 4), the `1/2` is missing in the `argmin`. Please ensure consistency.
    *   Section 3.1: "a single iteration is sufficient" (appears to be a copy-paste error from an earlier draft).
    *   Section 3.2: "We did an extensive study of th how"
    *   The reference to the HCP dataset [20] is provided in the main text but missing from the reference list at the time of writing this draft review (the list ends at [26] but the in-text citation is [20]). Please ensure all references are correct. *Note: The paper itself has the reference, but the draft review pointed this out as a potential issue.*

### **Post-Rebuttal Comments**

The authors provided additional results during the rebuttal period that more clearly demonstrated the benefit of spatial regularization, particularly in low-data regimes (as shown in Table 1). These learning curves are very informative and compelling. Including these results in a revised manuscript would significantly strengthen the paper's main claims. My recommendation is contingent on the authors incorporating this analysis and thoroughly addressing the clarity and presentation issues outlined above.

### **Final Assessment**

The paper introduces a promising and well-motivated method for a significant problem. The core idea is strong, but the execution in the current manuscript is flawed by a lack of rigorous empirical validation and poor presentation. If the authors revise the paper to include quantitative evidence of the method's benefits (as was partially done in the rebuttal) and carefully edit the manuscript for clarity and correctness, it would represent a solid contribution to the NIPS community.
