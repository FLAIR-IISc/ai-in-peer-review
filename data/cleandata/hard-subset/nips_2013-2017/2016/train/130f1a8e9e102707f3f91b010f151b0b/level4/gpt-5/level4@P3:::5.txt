Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), an online matrix factorization model tailored to neuroimaging. Each subject image Xi ∈ Rp is approximated as Xi ≈ VUi, with a dictionary V ∈ Rp×k whose atoms are constrained to be sparse (via an ℓ1 ball or simplex constraint) and spatially structured (via a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty ΩLap(v) = ||∇v||2). Codes Ui are estimated by ridge regression; dictionary atoms are updated by block coordinate descent (BCD) using a FISTA step to handle the Laplacian regularizer and projection onto the sparsity-inducing constraint set. The method is positioned as an extension of online dictionary learning in Mairal et al. (JMLR 2010) to incorporate spatial structure at modest extra cost, enabling large-scale analysis of brain images.

Experiments on 500 HCP subjects (task fMRI Z-maps; p ≈ 2.6×105; k = 40) compare Smooth-SODL with (i) standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL; Mairal et al. 2010) and (ii) multi-subject PCA/ICA (CanICA; Varoquaux et al. 2010). Smooth-SODL yields more spatially coherent, less noisy atoms than SODL, higher stability/reproducibility across splits, and competitive explained variance and predictive performance. Runtime is ~3× SODL and faster than CanICA.

Strengths
- Clear practical motivation: deriving data-driven, spatially coherent regions (blobs) that better capture inter-subject variability than dense ICA or unstructured sparse DL.
- Algorithmic design is sensible: BCD for online updates; ridge-coded mini-batches; FISTA for atom updates with Laplacian regularization; convergence inherits from Mairal et al.
- Scalability demonstrated on a large, high-dimensional dataset with reasonable wall-clock times on CPU.
- Empirical evaluation covers multiple criteria (visual quality, explained variance, stability, behavior prediction), which is valuable for neuroimaging applications.
- The Laplacian regularizer is a principled and efficient way to encode smoothness compared to TV-based formulations that are harder to scale.

Weaknesses and questions
Originality and positioning
- The methodological novelty is incremental. The contribution is essentially adding a quadratic smoothness penalty (GraphNet-style) to the atoms within the online DL framework of Mairal et al., with standard first-order optimization. Related structured sparse models (e.g., GraphNet, structured sparse PCA, TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning) should be discussed more thoroughly, with a clearer statement of what is new beyond scalability.

Clarity and technical details
- “Structure” is described informally as “piece-wise smooth and compact blobs.” A precise definition is needed. The Laplacian penalty enforces smoothness but not compact support per se; compactness stems from the sparsity constraint. Please clarify how the combination of the constraint set C (ℓ1-ball vs simplex/nonnegativity) and ΩLap jointly yields blob-like atoms, and contrast with alternatives such as TV.
- Dimensions around Eq. (1) are inconsistent: Xi is defined as a row vector (1×p), V ∈ Rp×k, Ui ∈ Rk×1. Then VUi is p×1. Please fix to XiT ≈ VUi or Xi ≈ U⊤i V⊤ and use consistent orientation throughout (also in Eq. (6)).
- The paper alternates between calling the penalty “Sobolev” and “Laplacian.” If ∆ = ∇⊤∇, then v⊤∆v = ||∇v||2 ≥ 0. Please define boundary conditions on the brain mask and confirm positivity. The 1/2 factor in ΩLap is cosmetic but should be consistent.
- The claim that BCD is “parameter free” is misleading: the FISTA step relies on a Lipschitz constant that scales with γ and At[j,j]/t. The proposed schedule γt = O(‖At‖∞,∞/t) is important—please state the exact schedule used in experiments and its sensitivity.
- Codes are ridge-regularized, whereas Mairal et al. use ℓ1 coding. You note ℓ1 is possible but do not evaluate it. Please justify the choice (e.g., stability, speed) and compare ridge vs ℓ1-coded variants on at least a subset of the data (stability, EV, interpretability).
- Fairness of baselines: CanICA is run on smoothed data and often visualized with hard-thresholding, whereas Smooth-SODL/SODL are not smoothed and are inherently sparse. Please justify this mismatch and, if possible, include a small set of results with comparable pre-processing and thresholding choices.
- The constraint set C is sometimes an ℓ1-ball and sometimes the simplex. The simplex imposes nonnegativity and sum constraints, which affect interpretability and scaling of atoms. Please report which option is used in each experiment and why.
- More detail is needed on computational and memory complexity per iteration, stopping criteria for inner FISTA steps (you mention 1–2 iterations suffice), number of passes over data, and reproducibility across random seeds.

Empirical evaluation
- While Smooth-SODL improves interpretability and stability, CanICA retains the best explained variance and often the best predictive scores on test data. Please discuss this stability–fidelity trade-off and whether Smooth-SODL can be tuned to close the gap in EV without losing interpretability.
- An ablation isolating the impact of ΩLap (γ = 0 vs γ > 0), and possibly comparing to a TV-regularized atom update on a smaller subset, would strengthen the case for the chosen regularizer.
- Cross-validation: provide details on folds, metrics (explained variance definition), and how γ and τ were selected. The adaptive schedules αt ∼ t−1/2 and γt deserve empirical justification.

Minor issues and typos
- There are many typographical and grammatical errors (e.g., “xperiments,” “intepretable,” “There is not step size”). Please proofread thoroughly.
- Spurious text appears mid-paragraph (“th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.”). If this corresponds to “line 146” in the submitted PDF, please remove.
- In Algorithm 2, clarify the notation Aj vs A[:,j] and ensure consistency in At[j, j] and vector subscripts.
- Cite closely related structured DL works more fully and distinguish from your approach (e.g., Jenatton et al. 2010; Grosenick et al. 2013; Abraham et al. 2013; Varoquaux et al. 2011/2012).

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Algorithmic choices are sound and scalable; math presentation needs correction and additional detail; some comparisons and ablations are missing.
- Clarity: The paper is generally readable but requires significant copy-editing and clearer definitions.
- Originality: Moderate; the main contribution is an incremental but useful extension enabling scalable, structured DL.
- Significance: Practical for neuroimaging; improved interpretability and stability are valuable, though gains in prediction/EV are modest.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important practical need: scalable, structured region discovery in brain imaging.
- Demonstrates clear improvements in interpretability and stability at scale with reasonable compute.
- Method is simple, implementable, and likely to be adopted (planned Nilearn release).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond known smoothness priors integrated into an existing online DL framework.
- Clarity issues, dimensional inconsistencies, and numerous typos.
- Incomplete empirical comparisons (ridge vs ℓ1 codes; fairness with ICA baselines; ablations).

Overall assessment
This is a valuable engineering contribution for large-scale neuroimaging with convincing qualitative and some quantitative benefits, but with limited methodological novelty and several presentation gaps. With corrections, clearer positioning, and stronger ablations/baselines, it would be a solid application paper. As it stands, I lean borderline.
