

This paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework with Sobolev regularization for learning brain regions. While the application to neuroscience is timely, the core contribution lacks sufficient novelty and clarity to meet NeurIPS standards. Below are my major concerns:

1. **Limited Novelty**: The work appears to be a straightforward extension of Mairal et al.'s online dictionary learning (JMLR 2010) by adding Sobolev regularization. The primary innovation—incorporating Sobolev regularization for structural constraints—does not substantially advance the state of the art. At NeurIPS, we expect contributions that offer significant theoretical or methodological breakthroughs, not merely incremental modifications. The authors provide no compelling evidence that Sobolev regularization fundamentally alters the learning dynamics or outcomes beyond what existing methods achieve. This weak novelty fails to align with NeurIPS' expectations for transformative work.

2. **Ambiguous Definition of "Structure"**: The paper repeatedly references "structure" as a constraint for the dictionary but never precisely defines it. Is the structure related to spatial relationships between brain regions, temporal coherence, or another property? Crucially, the authors claim Sobolev regularization improves performance due to "structure," yet they do not clarify *why* sparsity in the dictionary (a common Sobolev effect) would be beneficial for their specific brain-region learning task. For instance, if the goal is to learn spatially smooth dictionaries (e.g., for fMRI data), the connection to Sobolev regularization should be explicitly justified. Without this, the motivation for the regularization term remains opaque and unconvincing.

3. **Technical Errors and Ambiguities**: 
   - Equation (1) likely contains a typographical error; the matrix dimensions suggest the correct form should be $[V^1, V^2, \ldots, V^r]^\top$ rather than as written.
   - Equation (2) requires deeper justification. The authors use an $\ell_2$-norm on the codes (as in Mairal 2010), but omit discussion of why $\ell_2$ is preferred over $\ell_1$ for this task. Mairal's work used $\ell_2$ for computational efficiency, but the paper should explicitly address whether $\ell_1$ would yield better sparsity or performance in the brain-region context, and why it was discarded.
   - Line 146 is unclear—without context, it is impossible to assess the claim made here. This undermines the paper's technical rigor.

4. **Insufficient Analysis**: The paper lacks critical analysis of how Sobolev regularization interacts with the learning problem. For example:
   - How does the regularization strength ($\lambda$) affect the dictionary's interpretability for brain regions?
   - What empirical evidence supports that Sobolev regularization *specifically* enhances performance beyond standard online dictionary learning? The results section does not isolate the impact of the Sobolev term (e.g., by ablation studies).
   - The theoretical justification for Sobolev regularization in this setting is absent. Why does it enforce meaningful structure for brain data, rather than acting as a generic smoothness penalty?

5. **Language and Presentation**: The paper contains numerous grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "brain regions learning" instead of "learning brain regions," inconsistent notation). This undermines readability and professionalism. A thorough language edit is required before reconsideration.

**Recommendation**: The paper's core idea is insufficiently novel and poorly motivated. The technical execution lacks clarity, with unresolved ambiguities in definitions, equations, and analysis. While the application domain is relevant, the contribution does not advance the field sufficiently for NeurIPS. I recommend rejection unless the authors comprehensively address all concerns above, particularly by:
- Defining "structure" precisely and justifying Sobolev regularization's role in the brain-region context.
- Providing ablation studies to isolate the impact of Sobolev regularization.
- Correcting technical errors and clarifying all equations.
- Revising the language to eliminate grammatical issues.

Without these revisions, the paper does not meet NeurIPS' bar for novelty, technical depth, or clarity. The current version reads as a minor adaptation of existing methods without meaningful innovation.
