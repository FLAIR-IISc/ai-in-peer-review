This paper introduces an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to model neuroimaging data across individuals. It builds upon the established framework of [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior, aiming to encourage spatial contiguity in the learned dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate their method on empirical neuroimaging data, explore the influence of parameter tuning, and compare its performance against two existing approaches.

**Technical Quality:** Initially, I assessed the technical quality as marginally below a 3. The model is clearly defined, and the algorithm logically extends a well-known method [14], appearing sound in its construction. However, the presentation somewhat obscures the empirical benefits of the Sobolev prior. Specifically, the results section lacks a convincing demonstration of its advantages. Figure 2, in particular, does not clearly illustrate *how* the proposed SSMF method outperforms the alternatives. It would be significantly strengthened by quantifying improvements in spatial smoothness, or by highlighting regions demonstrably identified by SSMF but missed by other methods, especially with justification from neuroscience literature. Simply stating results are “more structured” is insufficient; explicit criteria, grounded in neuroscientific principles, coupled with quantitative measurements, are needed. 

The rebuttal provided valuable additional results which have led me to revise my rating to a 3. I strongly encourage the authors to incorporate these results into the revised manuscript. Further, the revision should include quantitative measurements demonstrating the learned atoms’ increased structure and a discussion of their neuroscientific relevance.

**Novelty/Originality:** The paper presents a notable contribution by extending [14] through the application of Sobolev priors to promote spatially structured dictionary atoms. This is a logical and potentially impactful extension of existing work.

**Potential Impact or Usefulness:** Dictionary learning is a valuable technique for analyzing neuroimaging data across large subject groups. Introducing spatial priors, like the Sobolev prior used here, has the potential to improve results. This paper delivers an efficient online algorithm for achieving this, offering a potentially useful addition to the toolkit of researchers in neuroscience and medical imaging. Future work could explore applications beyond the current scope, such as image segmentation or regional brain sensing.

**Presentation:** The paper's overall organization is adequate, but the manuscript would benefit significantly from careful editing. Numerous instances of unclear phrasing and typographical errors hinder understanding. Specific issues include:

*   Between lines 67-68, the transpose symbol in the matrix decomposition equation applied to $V^j$ (j = 1,..., r) should be applied to each individual $V^j$.
*   Line 73 lacks a citation for the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset.
*   Line 83 contains a grammatical error: "The Graph-Net penalty can *imposes*…" should be "impose".
*   Line 146 includes a bold sentence that appears to be a remnant of a collaborative editing process and should be removed.
*   There is an inconsistency in the sign between the last line of Equation (6) and Equation (8): $V^j + At[j,j]^{-1}(VAt^j - Bt^j)$ versus $At[j,j] V^j -(VAt^j - Bt^j)$. In the last line of equation (6), it's assumed that *V* represents the dictionary from the previous iteration, and thus the jth column is not the optimization variable *v*.
*   Line 157 contains a repetition: "a single, a single".
*   Line 169 includes a typo: "We did an extensive study of th how".
*   Section 3.3, near line 147 below Algorithm 2, should include the mathematical/algorithmic solution to Equation (6), potentially in the appendix.
*   Figure 2 presents results at different horizontal and sagittal slices for each method, making direct visual comparison difficult. The claim in Line 222 that SSMF results are “more structured” is not readily apparent from the figure.
*   Figure 3’s predicted values are unclear. If the figure represents regression of brain data onto behavioral metrics, clarify whether this is based on $U_i$'s. The legends are absent from both the main text and the figure caption, and the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption is not shown. The description in Lines 224-227, claiming R^2 values greater than 0.1, is difficult to follow without clear indication of which curves are being referenced.
