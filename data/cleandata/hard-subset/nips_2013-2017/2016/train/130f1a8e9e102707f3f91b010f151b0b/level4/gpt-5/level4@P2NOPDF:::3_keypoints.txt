- Paper adds a Sobolev prior to dictionary learning to enforce spatial smoothness and consistency.
- Evaluation relies mainly on visual inspection of spatial “blobs,” which is subjective and hard to interpret for non-experts.
- Smoother components are expected given the explicit roughness penalty, so improvements may merely reflect the prior rather than better representations.
- Data quality and quantity can be as important as algorithm choice; benefits may be limited with large datasets like the Human Connectome Project.
- The manuscript lacks a clear statement of scenarios (e.g., low-data regimes) where the Sobolev prior provides material gains.
- No analysis of inter-subject consistency or variability to assess whether components are biologically meaningful and robust across subjects.
- Validation is currently subjective and does not convincingly demonstrate practical advantages over standard dictionary learning.
- Needs quantitative metrics beyond visuals (reproducibility across splits/subjects, component stability, atlas overlap, downstream task performance).
- Should compare against strong spatial-regularization baselines and include ablation studies isolating the Sobolev term’s effect.
- Recommend evaluating performance across different data regimes (limited vs. abundant) to test the low-data hypothesis.
- Overall: approach is promising but requires more rigorous, objective validation to establish clear advantage and impact.
