

The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, extending online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) with a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness in brain image decompositions. The authors demonstrate that structured, sparse atoms better capture inter-subject variability in fMRI data (Human Connectome Project, 500 subjects) compared to SODL and CanICA. Key innovations include: (1) integrating Laplacian regularization into online dictionary learning with minimal runtime overhead (~3× SODL); (2) providing theoretical convergence guarantees; and (3) showing improved interpretability and reproducibility across small-to-large datasets via learning curves.  

**Strengths:**  
1. **Technical Soundness (Quality):** The Laplacian penalty is rigorously justified as a spatial regularizer (Eq. 4), and the algorithm’s convergence is well-supported by theory (Theorem 1, supplementary). The runtime analysis (340s vs. SODL’s 110s) and learning curve experiments (Table 1) convincingly show the method’s scalability and small-data advantages—addressing a critical gap in prior work (e.g., [1] uses slow alternating minimization).  
2. **Novelty (Originality):** While structured dictionary learning exists (e.g., [1, 25]), Smooth-SODL uniquely integrates spatial regularization *into online learning* without sacrificing scalability. This bridges a key gap between computational efficiency (SODL) and spatial interpretability (e.g., TV-`1 methods [1]), with clear connections to recent NIPS work on structured sparsity (e.g., [14, 21]).  
3. **Significance:** The method directly addresses a core challenge in neuroimaging: defining ROIs without manual segmentation. By capturing inter-subject variability (e.g., through reproducibility metrics in Fig. 2c), it offers a principled alternative to heuristic ROI selection. The Nilearn implementation also enhances practical utility.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Validation Clarity (Clarity/Significance):** The primary validation relies on visual comparisons of brain maps (Fig. 2a), which are subjective and inaccessible to non-specialists. While the paper reports reproducibility (mean correlation = 0.4 for Smooth-SODL vs. 0.1 for SODL), it lacks quantitative metrics to *objectively* link spatial structure to interpretability (e.g., how much better do Smooth-SODL atoms predict behavior?). The claim that "structured atoms are more interpretable" needs stronger empirical grounding.  
2. **Contextualizing Data Scale (Significance):** The paper correctly notes that spatial regularization is most valuable in small-data regimes (Table 1). However, the HCP dataset (500 subjects) is *not* "vast" by fMRI standards (typical studies use 100–500 subjects), and the authors could better clarify why spatial regularization remains beneficial *even here* (e.g., by comparing performance at 50 vs. 500 subjects).  
3. **Comparison Depth (Quality):** CanICA’s hard-thresholding (tCanICA) is a fair comparison, but the paper does not explore *why* Smooth-SODL outperforms tCanICA in prediction (Fig. 2c). A brief discussion of how spatial structure preserves signal-to-noise (vs. hard-thresholding’s noise amplification) would strengthen the argument.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
- **Pro:** Minimal runtime cost (3× SODL), theoretical convergence, small-data advantage via learning curves, and clear neuroimaging relevance.  
- **Con:** Subjective visual validation; insufficient justification for spatial regularization’s value in medium-scale HCP data; limited discussion of *why* Smooth-SODL outperforms tCanICA in prediction.  

**Recommendation:** **Weak Accept**. The technical contribution is sound and addresses a meaningful gap in scalable neuroimaging analysis. While the validation could be more objective, the method’s novelty, scalability, and potential impact on brain mapping justify acceptance. The authors should add quantitative interpretability metrics (e.g., correlation with known networks) and clarify the small-data advantage in the revision. This work advances NIPS’ focus on *practical, scalable methods for structured data* and aligns with recent trends in neuroimaging (e.g., [22, 23]).
