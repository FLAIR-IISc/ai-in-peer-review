

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, a structured sparse online dictionary learning framework for extracting interpretable brain regions from functional neuroimaging data. The authors extend Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure on dictionary atoms, addressing a key limitation in existing methods that produce scattered, non-biological activation patterns. The approach aims to capture inter-subject variability in brain imaging data while maintaining computational efficiency, with experiments conducted on the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the growing body of work on dictionary learning for neuroimaging. While prior methods like [16] and [25] have explored sparse dictionary learning for brain imaging, they lack spatial structure constraints, resulting in noisy, uninterpretable patterns. The authors' incorporation of a Sobolev penalty to enforce spatial smoothness represents a meaningful extension that aligns with the brain's known functional organization. Their approach is particularly well-motivated by the need to define brain regions without relying on anatomical segmentation or cross-modality correspondence, which is a significant challenge in neuroimaging analysis.

The paper's strengths are several. First, the authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation for their Smooth-SODL model, clearly explaining how the Laplacian penalty enforces spatial structure while maintaining the computational advantages of online learning. The algorithmic details are well-justified, with appropriate references to related work in optimization (e.g., FISTA for solving the structured subproblems). Second, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against relevant baselines (SODL, CanICA, PCA) across multiple metrics including visual interpretability, explained variance, stability, and behavioral prediction. Third, the results demonstrate clear advantages of the proposed approach: the structured dictionaries better capture known brain networks while maintaining good predictive performance on behavioral variables. The finding that spatial regularization provides significant benefits in small-data regimes (31% vs. 2% explained variance with only 17 subjects) is particularly compelling.

Regarding the novelty claim in the draft review, the paper does make a non-trivial extension of Mairal et al.'s work. While the basic online dictionary learning framework is established, the incorporation of a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure is novel in this context and addresses a clear gap in the literature. The paper's contribution is more substantial than a simple addition of regularization, as it requires careful algorithmic adaptation while preserving the computational efficiency of the online approach.

The paper excels in quality and significance. The method is technically sound, with appropriate theoretical justification and thorough experimental validation. The results demonstrate clear improvements over state-of-the-art approaches in terms of interpretability and performance across multiple data regimes (small, medium, large). The application to brain imaging is highly relevant, and the method has potential for broader application in medical imaging and other fields requiring structured sparse representations.

Regarding the draft review's concerns, I find them largely misplaced. The "structure" in the paper clearly refers to spatial smoothness and compactness (blobs rather than scattered patterns), which is explicitly defined in Section 1 and justified by the Laplacian penalty. The sparsity of the dictionary atoms is a separate constraint from the spatial structure, and the paper demonstrates how the combination of these constraints leads to more interpretable results. The draft review's comment about Eq. (1) appears to be incorrect; the notation X_i ≈ V U_i is standard for dictionary learning where V is the dictionary matrix. The use of an l2 penalty on codes (instead of l1) is well-justified in the paper as providing stable codes, which is particularly important for the neuroimaging context where we want to avoid overfitting to noise. The reference to "line 146" in the draft review is unclear as the provided text doesn't contain such a reference.

The paper is generally well-written, though some minor grammatical improvements could enhance readability. The draft review's claim of "many typos" appears overstated based on the provided text.

To strengthen the paper further, I would recommend:
1. More explicit discussion of how the Laplacian penalty specifically relates to known neuroanatomical structures
2. A more detailed comparison of the computational complexity with other spatially structured approaches
3. A clearer statement of how the method differs from [23] (which also addresses population-level brain mapping)

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses a meaningful challenge in neuroimaging analysis. The method provides a principled approach to extracting structured, interpretable brain regions while maintaining computational efficiency. The experiments convincingly demonstrate advantages over existing methods, particularly in capturing inter-subject variability across different data scales. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and has clear potential impact for brain imaging analysis.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is high quality (sound theory, thorough experiments), clear (well-organized, with sufficient technical detail), original (non-trivial extension of online dictionary learning with spatial structure), and significant (addresses a key challenge in neuroimaging analysis with practical benefits).
