

The paper proposes an extension of online dictionary learning (SODL) for fMRI data analysis, introducing Sobolev (Laplacian) and L1 regularization to enforce spatial smoothness and sparsity in dictionary atoms. The core contribution is the "Smooth-SODL" framework, which aims to extract interpretable, structured brain regions by jointly optimizing for spatial compactness (via Laplacian penalty) and sparsity (via L1 norm). The authors claim this approach better captures inter-subject variability compared to SODL and CanICA, with only a 2–3x runtime overhead. Experiments on HCP task-fMRI data (500 subjects, 260k voxels) demonstrate improved visual interpretability, stability (mean correlation 0.4 vs. CanICA’s 0.1), and behavioral prediction performance over baselines. The work extends Mairal et al.’s SODL (2010) by integrating spatial structure into the online learning framework, addressing a gap in prior methods that either used slow alternating optimization (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2013) or sacrificed interpretability (e.g., CanICA).

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The technical formulation is sound. The Laplacian penalty (Eq. 4) effectively enforces spatial smoothness, and the BCD algorithm (Alg. 1–2) properly handles the non-convex problem while retaining convergence guarantees from [16]. The runtime analysis (340s vs. SODL’s 110s) is well-justified, and the ablation study (Fig. 1) provides useful insights into hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., γ’s role in balancing sparsity and data fidelity).  
*Originality*: The integration of Sobolev regularization into online dictionary learning is novel for neuroimaging. While spatial priors exist (e.g., [1, 25]), prior work relied on slow alternating minimization; this paper adapts the scalable SODL framework to include spatial structure, which is a significant methodological step.  
*Significance*: The method addresses a real need in fMRI analysis—interpretable region-of-interest (ROI) definition without manual segmentation. The demonstration of improved stability (0.4 vs. CanICA’s 0.1) and behavioral prediction (Fig. 2c) suggests practical utility, particularly for small-to-medium datasets where data scarcity limits standard methods. The open-source implementation via Nilearn ([2]) further enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**  
*Clarity*: The manuscript suffers from inconsistent terminology and unclear exposition. For instance:  
- "Structured" is defined as "piece-wise smooth and compact" but conflated with "blobs" (p. 1), which lacks precise mathematical grounding.  
- The Laplacian penalty derivation (Eq. 4) omits context on why ∇x, ∇y, ∇z operators are defined as p×p matrices (p = voxels), causing confusion.  
- Draft comments (e.g., "line 146") and repetitive phrasing (e.g., "more interpretable" used 5x in abstract) suggest rushed writing.  
*Significance Gap*: The claimed advantages are overstated relative to the evidence. While Smooth-SODL shows better stability (0.4 vs. 0.1) and visual interpretability (Fig. 2a), its explained variance on test data (55% vs. CanICA’s 60%) is lower (Fig. 2b), and the performance gap with SODL narrows significantly as data grows (Table 1). The 3x runtime cost is substantial for large-scale applications, yet the paper does not convincingly argue why this trade-off is justified *beyond* small-data regimes. The assertion that "spatial regularization is essential" contradicts the learning curve analysis (Table 1), where SODL catches up to Smooth-SODL with more data.  
*Evaluation Rigor*: The comparison with CanICA is flawed—CanICA was run on *pre-smoothed* data (6mm FWHM), while Smooth-SODL used raw data. This violates the "fair comparison" principle, as smoothing is a standard preprocessing step for ICA but not for dictionary learning. The paper should have applied identical preprocessing.

**Recommendations**  
1. **Clarify technical contributions**: Explicitly define "structured" as "spatially compact with minimal boundary artifacts" and clarify the Laplacian implementation (e.g., "∇x denotes discrete gradient along x-axis, implemented as a sparse p×p matrix").  
2. **Reframe significance claims**: Acknowledge that spatial regularization is most valuable for *small datasets* (Table 1), not universally. Highlight where Smooth-SODL *outperforms* SODL (stability, interpretability) without overstating explained variance gains.  
3. **Standardize experiments**: Run all baselines on *identically preprocessed data* (no smoothing for CanICA). Report runtime on larger datasets (e.g., 1,000+ subjects) to assess scalability beyond the HCP sample.  
4. **Address hyperparameter sensitivity**: The "safe range" for (γ, τ) (Fig. 1) should be quantified (e.g., "γ ∈ [10³, 10⁴] for τ = 0.1") rather than described qualitatively.

**Overall Assessment**  
This is a technically solid extension of online dictionary learning with clear neuroimaging relevance. The method is well-motivated, rigorously derived, and implemented in an accessible library. However, the significance is undermined by overstated claims and insufficiently justified trade-offs. The improvement over SODL is meaningful *only* for small-to-medium datasets, and the runtime penalty (3x) is nontrivial for large-scale applications. The paper would be stronger with: (1) a clearer distinction between small-data vs. large-data regimes, (2) fairer comparisons, and (3) tighter alignment between claims and evidence. As written, it is a *modest* contribution that advances the state of the art in structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging but falls short of the transformative impact implied.  

**Recommendation**: **Weak Accept**. The method is novel and well-executed, but the significance argument requires strengthening to justify the computational cost. With revisions addressing the clarity and evaluation gaps, this could be a strong contribution.
