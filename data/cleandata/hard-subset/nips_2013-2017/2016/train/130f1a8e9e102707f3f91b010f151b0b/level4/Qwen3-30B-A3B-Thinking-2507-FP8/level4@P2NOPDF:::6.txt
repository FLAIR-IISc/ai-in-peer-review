

This paper introduces a novel regularization technique for matrix factorization that enforces both sparsity and smoothness. The core contribution lies in the proposed regularizer, which represents a significant methodological advancement and is well-motivated. Experimental results demonstrate consistent superiority over state-of-the-art approaches across multiple benchmarks, highlighting the practical potential of the method.  

However, the paper has notable shortcomings that require attention. The authors explicitly acknowledge the non-convex nature of the optimization problem, yet fail to establish the existence of a solution, a critical theoretical gap for a method relying on algorithmic convergence. While the experimental validation is thorough and convincing, the algorithmic approach lacks theoretical grounding beyond empirical performance. The absence of a solution existence proof or convergence guarantees weakens the foundation of the proposed method.  

Additionally, the writing requires substantial revision. For instance, line 146 contains ambiguous phrasing that impedes clarity, and the presentation of technical details (e.g., the optimization procedure) would benefit from greater rigor and precision. The manuscript would be significantly strengthened by addressing these issues.  

Following the rebuttal, the authors provided a clear and satisfactory explanation of the algorithmic implementation and its convergence behavior in the context of the non-convex problem. This resolved my initial concern about the theoretical justification. Consequently, I am prepared to revise my evaluation of clarity and presentation from a 2 to a 3.  

In summary, the paper offers a valuable new regularizer with compelling empirical results. Its primary limitations—lack of theoretical guarantees for the solution existence and suboptimal writing—have been partially addressed through the rebuttal. While the work is promising, the theoretical gaps and presentation flaws prevent a stronger endorsement. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the writing issues in the camera-ready version. The contribution is novel, the experiments are robust, and the method shows clear promise for applications requiring sparse and smooth factorizations.
