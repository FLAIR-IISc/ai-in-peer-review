The paper proposes an online dictionary learning method incorporating both L1 and Sobolev regularization to facilitate the discovery of structured, sparse components – representing functional brain regions – from fMRI data. The core idea of combining these penalties to encourage both sparsity and smoothness in the learned dictionary atoms is interesting and potentially valuable for neuroimaging applications. The approach builds upon prior work in online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) and extends it by non-trivially incorporating spatial regularization.

The paper’s strengths lie in its attempt to address a relevant problem in neuroimaging – defining regions of interest (ROIs) in a data-driven manner, circumventing the limitations of traditional, often heuristic, segmentation approaches. The authors correctly identify the challenges of inter-subject variability and the computational demands of analyzing high-dimensional fMRI data. The proposed method addresses both of these concerns by leveraging an online learning framework suitable for large datasets, and by encouraging the extraction of spatially coherent, interpretable features. The extensive experimental evaluation using the HCP dataset is also a positive aspect, allowing for comparisons with established methods like CanICA and standard SODL. 

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the authors demonstrate that their method achieves comparable or slightly better performance than existing techniques, the magnitude of the improvement is not always clearly articulated, and in some cases (e.g., explained variance) appears marginal. The results presented in Figure 2(b) suggest only a modest gain over SODL, particularly as the amount of data increases. The authors claim improvements in capturing inter-subject variability, but this is not always convincingly demonstrated through the provided metrics. The manuscript suffers from a lack of polish; remnants of draft comments (e.g., line 146) detract from the overall presentation. The writing style can be dense and occasionally confusing, hindering readability. The discussion of the hyperparameter tuning process, while acknowledging its difficulty, lacks specific guidance or practical recommendations for researchers. Finally, while related work is cited, the connection to more recent advances in spatially regularized dictionary learning, beyond the references provided, could be strengthened.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging with a novel methodological approach.
*   Extends existing online dictionary learning methods with a meaningful regularization scheme.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance on a large, publicly available dataset.
*   Provides an implementation that will be made available through Nilearn.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The magnitude of improvement over existing methods is not always substantial or clearly demonstrated.
*   The manuscript’s presentation is somewhat rough and could benefit from significant polishing.
*   The hyperparameter tuning discussion lacks specific practical guidance.
*   The writing style is dense and sometimes difficult to follow.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a technically sound approach to a challenging problem. While the improvements over existing methods are not dramatic, the combination of online learning and structured sparsity regularization is a valuable contribution. However, the paper's clarity and presentation need improvement. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, focusing on clarifying the key findings, addressing the writing quality, and providing more detailed guidance on hyperparameter selection. The authors should also more explicitly discuss the limitations of their approach and potential avenues for future research.
