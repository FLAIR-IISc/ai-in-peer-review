This paper introduces an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries from multi-subject neuroimaging data. The primary contribution is the extension of the framework from [14] to incorporate a Sobolev prior, which is designed to encourage spatial contiguity and smoothness in the learned dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate their method, termed Structured Sparse Matrix Factorization (SSMF), on empirical fMRI data, comparing its performance against two existing methods and analyzing the effect of tuning parameters.

**Technical Quality**

The paper's technical approach is sound. The model is clearly defined, and the proposed algorithm is a logical extension of the well-established framework presented in [14].

However, the primary weakness of the manuscript in its current form is the lack of compelling empirical evidence to support the central claim—that the Sobolev prior improves the quality of the learned dictionaries. The results section does not sufficiently demonstrate the advantages of the SSMF method over the baselines. For instance, in Figure 2, it is difficult to discern why the SSMF results are superior. The paper claims the results are "more structured," but this assertion is not substantiated with quantitative metrics. To strengthen the paper, the authors should explicitly define criteria for what constitutes a "better" or "more structured" dictionary atom, ideally grounded in neuroscience literature. For example, do the SSMF atoms better delineate known functional brain networks? Do they exhibit smoother spatial boundaries (which could be quantified)? Are anatomically or functionally connected regions more consistently grouped within single atoms?

*(Added after reading author rebuttal):* The new results provided in the author response are much more convincing and directly address many of the concerns above. I have raised my score accordingly. I strongly recommend that the authors incorporate these new quantitative analyses and visualizations into the revised manuscript. This should include a clear demonstration that the atoms learned by SSMF are not only more structured (supported by quantitative metrics) but also more neuroscientifically meaningful (supported by references to established findings).

**Novelty and Originality**

The paper's novelty lies in the application of a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial structure within the online dictionary learning framework of [14]. This is a valuable contribution that addresses a practical need in the analysis of neuroimaging data.

**Potential Impact or Usefulness**

Given the importance of dictionary learning for analyzing large-scale neuroimaging datasets, a method that produces more interpretable and spatially coherent results has significant potential impact. The proposed online algorithm is efficient and could become a useful tool for the neuroscience and medical imaging communities. The authors might also consider discussing potential applications beyond neuroimaging, such as in image segmentation or other domains where spatial structure is key.

**Presentation and Clarity**

The paper's presentation could be significantly improved with careful editing. Several typos, unclear descriptions, and presentation choices make the manuscript difficult to follow. I have listed specific examples below:

*   **Matrix Decomposition Equation (line 67-68):** Should the transpose be applied to each individual matrix $V^j$, rather than the entire group of matrices?
*   **Line 73:** The reference for the HCP dataset is missing.
*   **Line 83:** The phrase "The Graph-Net penalty can imposes..." contains a grammatical error.
*   **Line 146:** The bolded sentence appears to be an unaddressed comment from a co-author.
*   **Equations (6) and (8):** The update rules for $V^j$ appear to have conflicting signs: one has $V^j + \dots$ while the other has $A_t[j,j] V^j - \dots$. Please clarify the correct form. Additionally, in Equation (6), it should be made explicit that $V$ refers to the dictionary from the previous iteration, not the variable being optimized in the current step.
*   **Line 157:** Repetition of "a single, a single".
*   **Line 169:** The sentence "We did an extensive study of th how" is incomplete.
*   **Section 3.3 (near line 147):** The derivation of the solution to Equation (6) is not provided. It would be beneficial to include the explicit mathematical solution, perhaps in an appendix, to improve reproducibility and clarity.
*   **Figure 2:** The different methods are visualized on different anatomical slices, which makes a direct comparison difficult. Please present all results on the same slices. As mentioned earlier, the claim that SSMF results are more "structured" is not visually apparent from this figure.
*   **Figure 3:** This figure is very unclear. The caption and text do not sufficiently explain what is being predicted or what the different curves and legends represent. For example, is brain data being regressed on behavioral metrics using the subject-specific loadings ($U_i$)? The PCA baseline mentioned in the caption also appears to be missing from the plot. The claim (lines 224-227) that $R^2$ is greater than 0.1 is not easily verifiable from the provided figure.
