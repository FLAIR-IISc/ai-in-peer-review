This paper presents an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals, extending the well-established framework of [14] with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply their method to empirical data, report the effect of varying tuning parameters, and compare performance with two alternative methods in the field.

**Technical Quality:** I would rate this marginally below a 3 if given the option. The model definition is clear, and the algorithms follow a well-established framework [14], appearing solid. However, the paper’s presentation somewhat undermines its strengths, and the empirical advantage of introducing the Sobolev prior isn’t clearly demonstrated in the results section. Specifically, Figure 2 doesn’t clearly illustrate *how* the SSMF method outperforms the alternatives. Is the spatial boundary smoother quantitatively? Does SSMF identify connections between regions known to be connected, while the other methods do not? Beyond vaguely stating that SSMF results are “more structured,” the authors could explicitly describe criteria justified by neuroscience literature and provide quantitative measurements of improvement based on these criteria.

*(Added after reading the rebuttal: The results provided in the rebuttal are very informative. I have increased my rating on technical quality from 2 to 3. I strongly encourage the authors to include these new results in their revision. Furthermore, they should demonstrate that the atoms learned by SSMF are more structured – with quantitative measurements – and more meaningful, with reference to relevant neuroscience literature.)*

**Novelty/Originality:** The paper makes a notable original contribution by extending [14] to incorporate Sobolev priors, encouraging the learning of spatially structured dictionary atoms.

**Potential Impact or Usefulness:** Dictionary learning is a valuable approach for explaining neuroimaging data across large subject groups, and introducing a Sobolev prior, or similar spatial prior, should theoretically improve results. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm for doing so and could become a useful tool for the neuroscience/medical imaging community. The authors could also explore other applications of this method, such as image segmentation and regional sensing.

**Presentation:** The general organization of the paper is adequate, but the manuscript could benefit from careful editing. Confusing descriptions and typos make understanding the content more difficult. I list specific issues below; some are likely simple typos, while others may require more detailed explanations.
