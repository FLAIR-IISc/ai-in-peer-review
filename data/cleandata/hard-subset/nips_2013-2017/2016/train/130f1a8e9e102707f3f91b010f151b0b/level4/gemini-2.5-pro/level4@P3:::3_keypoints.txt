- Proposes Smooth-SODL: online dictionary learning with an l1 sparsity penalty plus a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty to enforce spatial smoothness in brain image components.  
- Derives an efficient Block-Coordinate Descent online algorithm; structural penalty adds only ~2-3x computational overhead.  
- Evaluated on HCP task-fMRI against SODL and CanICA using visual quality, explained variance, stability, and behavioral prediction.  

Strengths:
- Addresses a well-motivated problem: scalable extraction of interpretable, spatially coherent ROIs from large neuroimaging datasets.  
- Technically sound extension of Mairal et al.'s online dictionary learning with clear derivations and online applicability.  
- Thorough experiments, with learning-curve analysis showing clear benefits of spatial regularization in low-data regimes.  

Weaknesses:
- Improvement largely justified by visual quality, which is subjective and hard for non-experts to verify; gains over SODL look marginal in images.  
- Adding smoothness yields the expected "smoother blobs," so novelty/impact feels predictable unless tied to stronger downstream benefits.  
- Limited quantitative gains on full large-scale data; CanICA often matches or outperforms Smooth-SODL in explained variance and behavioral prediction.  
- Insufficient direct validation of inter-subject variability despite being a stated motivation; stability measures don't fully address subject-level differences.  

Evaluation notes:
- Quality: technically correct and experiments well-designed; authors honestly report trade-offs.  
- Clarity: well-written and organized with informative figures and tables.  
- Originality: incremental but useful—combining spatial smoothness with online scalability is novel in practice.  
- Significance: moderate—valuable for small-to-medium datasets, but benefits for "massive" data remain unclear.
