

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for decomposing brain images into structured, sparse components. The authors extend the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by adding a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms while maintaining L1 sparsity. The work addresses a significant challenge in neuroimaging: the need for principled definition of brain regions that capture inter-subject variability rather than treating it as noise. Previous approaches either rely on predefined ROIs that may not match functional organization or use clustering that doesn't account for signal structure. The authors position their work as a meaningful extension of online dictionary learning to handle spatial structure with minimal computational overhead.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it provides a principled mathematical formulation with both L1 sparsity and Laplacian regularization. Second, the authors successfully adapt online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial structure while maintaining scalability to large datasets. Third, thorough experiments on HCP data demonstrate that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable dictionaries with better spatial structure compared to standard SODL and CanICA approaches. The theoretical analysis of the algorithm, including convergence guarantees, adds to the paper's technical soundness.

The primary strength lies in the clear problem formulation and comprehensive experimental validation. The authors demonstrate that their method extracts more spatially structured components (less noisy, more blob-like) while maintaining good data explanation. Crucially, they show that Smooth-SODL performs particularly well in small-data regimes, where spatial regularization provides significant benefits. The comparison with SODL shows that Smooth-SODL maintains online learning scalability with only a 2-3x runtime increase.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. The most significant issue is that claimed improvements over existing methods are not sufficiently justified. While Figure 2(a) visually shows more structured components, quantitative results (Table 1, Figure 2b) reveal only modest gains in explained variance (55% vs 50% for SODL on test data) and reproducibility (0.4 vs 0.1 for SODL). The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that these modest gains translate to meaningful improvements in downstream neuroimaging analysis.

The manuscript also suffers from poor organization and presentation. It contains several draft comments (e.g., "line 146"), confusing mathematical formulations, and unclear explanations. For instance, the explanation of how the Laplacian penalty enforces spatial structure would benefit from a more intuitive description. The experimental section lacks sufficient detail about data preprocessing and hyperparameter selection, making results difficult to reproduce.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the narrow scope of evaluation. The authors test only on one dataset (HCP task fMRI) and compare against only three methods (SODL, CanICA, PCA). While results are promising, the paper would be stronger with additional comparisons against other spatially structured methods (e.g., Abraham et al.'s TV-ℓ1 approach) and more diverse datasets.

In terms of clarity, the paper is dense and sometimes difficult to follow. Mathematical notation is inconsistent (e.g., using both "Laplacian" and "Sobolev" interchangeably), and key concepts like the interpretation of the Laplacian regularization are not adequately explained. The authors should consider restructuring the paper to provide a clearer narrative, perhaps with a more detailed introduction to the neuroimaging problem before technical details.

The contribution is original in combining online dictionary learning with spatial structure, but the novelty is somewhat incremental. The authors cite relevant prior work, but the comparison with related methods could be more comprehensive. For instance, they should more clearly explain how their approach differs from Abraham et al.'s TV-ℓ1 method and how it improves upon it.

To summarize, the paper presents a technically sound extension of online dictionary learning with spatial structure, with potential value for neuroimaging. However, the claimed improvements over existing methods are not sufficiently demonstrated, the manuscript needs substantial editing for clarity, and the evaluation is limited in scope.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Clear problem statement addressing an important neuroimaging challenge
- Technical extension of online dictionary learning with theoretical justification
- Demonstrated scalability to large datasets
- Good experimental validation on a large public dataset
- Potential for practical impact in neuroimaging

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Modest quantitative improvements over existing methods
- Poor manuscript organization and presentation
- Insufficient justification of claimed advantages
- Limited experimental scope and comparison
- Some technical inconsistencies and unclear explanations

Overall, the paper has potential but requires significant revision to address the concerns outlined above. The authors should strengthen the quantitative evidence for their method's advantages, improve the manuscript's clarity and organization, and broaden the experimental evaluation before it can be accepted for NIPS.
