### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL), a method for decomposing large-scale brain imaging data into a set of sparse and spatially structured components, or "atoms." The primary motivation is to discover meaningful, data-driven brain regions (ROIs) that capture inter-subject variability. The proposed method extends the well-known online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages spatial smoothness, resulting in components that are blob-like and more neuroanatomically plausible than the scattered patterns often produced by standard sparse methods. The online nature of the algorithm ensures scalability to massive datasets, a key challenge in modern neuroimaging. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project, comparing it to standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA (CanICA). The evaluation is based on qualitative assessment, explained variance, stability, and the ability of the learned components to predict behavioral scores.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and challenging problem in neuroimaging: the scalable, data-driven discovery of functional brain regions from large population cohorts. The proposed solution—integrating a spatial smoothness prior directly into an online dictionary learning framework—is a novel and pragmatic contribution. It successfully bridges the gap between computationally efficient online methods, which typically lack spatial priors, and slower batch methods that incorporate structured sparsity.

2.  **Scalability and Efficiency:** A major strength is the algorithm's scalability. By building upon the online framework of [16], the method can handle datasets that are too large to fit in memory. The experiments convincingly show that the addition of the Laplacian penalty incurs only a modest computational overhead (a factor of ~3), making it a practical tool for large-scale analysis.

3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare their method against appropriate and widely used baselines (SODL and CanICA). The evaluation uses a comprehensive set of metrics, including visual inspection, quantitative measures of model fit (explained variance on train and test sets), model stability, and, importantly, relevance to an external task (predicting behavioral variables).

4.  **Clear Justification for Spatial Regularization:** The learning curve analysis presented in Table 1 provides compelling evidence for the value of the Laplacian regularization. It clearly demonstrates that in low-data regimes, Smooth-SODL significantly outperforms the standard SODL, capturing meaningful variance much more efficiently. This finding is particularly important, as many studies do not have access to datasets as large as the HCP.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The primary weakness of the manuscript is its lack of polish, which significantly detracts from the quality of the technical contribution. The paper contains numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasings. More concerningly, it appears to be an unfinished draft, as evidenced by a leftover internal comment on line 146 ("XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v."). This suggests a rushed preparation and a lack of careful proofreading, which makes it difficult for a reviewer to confidently assess the technical details.

2.  **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the algorithm is empirically well-motivated and performs well, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical discussion. The authors state that convergence is guaranteed by citing [16], but they do not provide a formal proof or analysis in the context of their specific modifications. Furthermore, there is no theoretical justification for the choice of the Laplacian penalty over other spatial regularizers, such as a Total Variation (TV) norm, which is also commonly used for promoting piecewise-smooth regions. A discussion of the trade-offs between these choices would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Discussion of Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper acknowledges that hyperparameter tuning is a difficult problem and provides an empirical study in Figure 1. While this is helpful, the final strategy for setting parameters (`α`, `γ`, `τ`) could be described more concretely. The paper mentions that k-fold cross-validation was a good strategy for `γ` and `τ`, but more details on the practical implementation of this for such a large-scale problem would increase the work's reproducibility.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   Please perform a thorough proofreading of the entire manuscript to correct grammatical errors, typos, and remove any internal comments. Improving the presentation quality is crucial for publication.
*   Could you elaborate on the choice of the Laplacian penalty (`L2` norm of the gradient) versus a Total Variation penalty (`L1` norm of the gradient)? TV penalties are known to promote sharp boundaries, which might be desirable for defining ROIs. What are the computational and neuroscientific arguments for your choice?
*   The convergence argument relies entirely on the work of [16]. While plausible, it would be beneficial to explicitly state how each of the assumptions from [16] (e.g., compact support of data, unique solution to the coding step) holds for your specific problem formulation and dataset.
*   In the prediction task (Figure 2c), the performance of all decomposition-based methods is substantially lower than the RAW model on the training set, which is expected due to overfitting in the RAW model. However, on the test set, CanICA seems to perform best. Could you comment on why this might be the case, and why the interpretability/structure of Smooth-SODL does not translate to better predictive performance in this specific task?

**Overall Recommendation**

This paper presents a valuable and well-motivated contribution to the field of large-scale neuroimaging analysis. The proposed method is novel, scalable, and supported by a strong set of experiments. However, the manuscript's poor state of presentation severely undermines its quality and suggests it was not ready for submission. While the technical ideas are promising, the paper requires a major revision to address the clarity and presentation issues before it can be considered for publication.
