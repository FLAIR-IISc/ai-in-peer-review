This paper presents an online algorithm for learning a structured dictionary to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals. The method extends the well-established framework of Mairal et al. (2010) [16] by incorporating a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity in the dictionary atoms. The authors apply their method to empirical fMRI data, investigate the effect of hyperparameter tuning, and compare its performance against two alternative methods in the field.

**Technical Quality**
The model is clearly defined, and the proposed algorithm follows a well-established framework [16], appearing technically sound. However, the empirical advantage of introducing the Sobolev prior is not clearly demonstrated in the results section. Specifically, in Figure 2, it is unclear in what respect the proposed Smooth-SODL method is superior to the alternatives. For instance, are the spatial boundaries quantitatively smoother? Are there certain known connected regions that are only identified by Smooth-SODL? Rather than vaguely stating that the results are "more structured," the authors should define explicit, neuroscientifically-justified criteria and provide quantitative measurements of the improvement according to these criteria.

*Update after reading the rebuttal:* The results provided in the rebuttal are very informative and have prompted me to raise my score for this criterion. I strongly encourage the authors to include these new results in their revision. In addition, they should still aim to quantitatively demonstrate that the learned atoms are more structured and provide justification for their neuroscientific meaning.

**Originality**
The paper's primary original contribution is the extension of the online dictionary learning framework of [16] to include a Sobolev prior, which encourages the learning of spatially structured dictionary atoms. This is a notable contribution.

**Significance**
Dictionary learning is a powerful approach for analyzing neuroimaging data from large cohorts. In theory, introducing a Sobolev prior or similar spatial regularizers should improve the interpretability and quality of the results. This paper contributes an efficient online algorithm for this purpose, which could become a valuable tool for the neuroscience and medical imaging communities. The authors might also consider exploring other potential applications, such as image segmentation.

**Clarity and Presentation**
The overall organization of the paper is reasonable. However, the manuscript appears to have been hastily prepared, with several confusing descriptions and typographical errors that hinder comprehension. Below is a list of specific points that were unclear. Some are likely simple typos, while others may require more detailed explanation.

*   In Equation (1), the definition of the dictionary V as a concatenation of column vectors V_j is standard, but the text could be slightly clearer. A reviewer noted a potential ambiguity with a transpose, which should be double-checked for clarity.
*   The reference for the HCP dataset was noted as missing in an earlier version. Please ensure it is present and correct (e.g., [20]).
*   Please proofread for typos, such as in the phrases "The Graph-Net penalty can imposes...", "a single, a single", and "of th how".
*   There appears to be an unaddressed co-author comment left in bold text in the manuscript.
*   There seems to be an inconsistency in the dictionary update rule. Please clarify the correct form of the expression in the `argmin` of Equations (7) and (8).
*   In Equation (7), it should be explicitly stated that in the term `VAjt`, `V` refers to the dictionary from the previous iteration, where the j-th column is not the variable `v` being optimized.
*   In the paragraph below Algorithm 2, the paper states that Equation (7) can be solved with methods like FISTA. For completeness and reproducibility, it would be helpful to provide the explicit algorithm for this subproblem, perhaps in an appendix.
*   In Figure 2a, the visualizations for different methods are shown on different anatomical slices, which makes direct visual comparison difficult. Presenting all results on the same slices would be more effective. Based on this figure, I could not verify the claim that the Smooth-SODL results are more "structured."
*   Figure 2c is difficult to interpret. It is unclear what behavioral variables are being predicted and what features are used (e.g., are the codes U_i used for prediction?). The legend is not explained in the text or caption. Furthermore, the PCA baseline mentioned in the text does not appear to be included in the plot. Finally, the claim regarding an R2 score greater than 0.1 is hard to verify from the figure.
