### **Summary**

This paper introduces "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL), a novel method for decomposing brain images into sparse and spatially structured components. The core contribution is the extension of the well-established online dictionary learning framework by Mairal et al. (2010) to include a Laplacian penalty. This penalty enforces spatial smoothness on the dictionary atoms, encouraging them to form contiguous, blob-like regions, which is neuroscientifically desirable. This is combined with a standard l1-norm constraint to ensure sparsity. The resulting algorithm remains scalable to massive datasets due to its online nature, with only a modest increase in computational cost compared to the unstructured baseline. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on a large-scale fMRI dataset (HCP), showing that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable and stable brain maps that better capture inter-subject variability, especially in low-data regimes, when compared to standard sparse dictionary learning (SODL) and Independent Component Analysis (ICA) based methods like CanICA.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significant and Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper addresses the important challenge of identifying meaningful functional regions from population-level neuroimaging data in a data-driven way. Combining spatial structure with sparsity in a scalable decomposition framework is a significant goal for the field.

2.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** The primary contribution—integrating a Laplacian spatial regularizer into the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al.—is a novel and valuable extension. This successfully bridges the gap between computationally efficient online methods and slower batch methods that incorporate spatial priors. The derivation of the BCD updates for the new objective function is technically sound.

3.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed.
    *   The use of a large, modern dataset (HCP) demonstrates the method's real-world applicability and scalability.
    *   Comparisons are made against relevant and strong baselines (SODL, CanICA).
    *   The evaluation is multi-faceted, covering qualitative assessment of the learned maps, quantitative metrics like explained variance and stability, and a practical application in predicting behavioral scores.
    *   The learning curve analysis in Table 1 provides compelling evidence for the utility of the spatial regularizer, showing a dramatic improvement in performance when training data is limited.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The authors effectively motivate the problem, present their model formally, detail the optimization algorithm, and systematically report their results.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Convergence Guarantees for a Non-Convex Problem:** As the authors correctly note, the joint dictionary learning problem is non-convex. The review draft raises a concern about the "existence of the solution." A more precise way to frame this is to discuss the convergence properties of the proposed algorithm. While the authors state that convergence to a local optimum is guaranteed by satisfying the hypotheses of Mairal et al. (2010), this point could be made more explicit in the main text. For instance, clarifying that the BCD algorithm converges to a stationary point of the non-convex objective would reassure readers and preempt potential confusion.

2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model introduces several hyperparameters (α, γ, τ), and the paper shows that performance is sensitive to their selection (Fig. 1). While the authors provide a helpful empirical study and suggest cross-validation, the practical utility of the method would be enhanced by more concrete guidance or a more robust method for setting these parameters, as extensive cross-validation can be computationally demanding.

3.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The manuscript is generally of high quality but could benefit from a final proofreading pass to correct minor grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "intepretable" instead of "interpretable," inconsistent capitalization).

### **Post-Rebuttal Comments**

The authors have satisfactorily addressed the concern regarding the algorithm's convergence in their rebuttal. By clarifying that their method inherits the convergence guarantees to a stationary point from the framework of Mairal et al. (2010), they have resolved the main ambiguity about the theoretical underpinnings. My confidence in the paper's technical soundness has increased, and I have updated my scores accordingly.
