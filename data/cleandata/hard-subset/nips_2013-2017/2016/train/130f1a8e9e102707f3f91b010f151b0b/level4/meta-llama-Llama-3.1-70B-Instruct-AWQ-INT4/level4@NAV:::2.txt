This paper proposes an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals, building upon the established framework in [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior to promote spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply their method to empirical data, investigate the effect of varying tuning parameters, and compare its performance with two alternative methods in the field. 

Technical quality: I would rate this marginally below 3 if given the option. The model definition is clear, and the algorithms follow a well-established framework [14], appearing solid. However, the paper's presentation may undermine its technical quality, as it does not clearly demonstrate the empirical advantage of introducing the Sobolev prior in the results section. Specifically, in Figure 2, it is unclear in what aspects the SSMF method outperforms the two alternatives. For instance, is the spatial boundary smoother quantitatively, or are there certain regions known to be connected only identified by SSMF? Instead of vaguely stating that the SSMF results are "more structured," the authors could provide explicit criteria justified by neuroscience literature and quantitative measurements of the improvement according to these criteria. 

Added after reading the rebuttal: The results provided in the rebuttal are informative, prompting me to change my rating on technical quality from 2 to 3. I hope the authors include these new results in their revision and demonstrate that the atoms learned by SSMF are more structured (with quantitative measurements) and more meaningful (with reference to neuroscience literature).

Novelty/originality: This paper makes a notable original contribution by extending [14] to apply Sobolev priors, encouraging spatially structured dictionary atoms.

Potential impact or usefulness: Dictionary learning is a useful approach for explaining neuroimaging data across a large group of subjects. Introducing Sobolev priors or similar spatial priors should theoretically improve the results. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm for this purpose, potentially adding a valuable tool to the neuroscience/medical imaging community's toolbox. The authors could also explore other applications of this method, such as image segmentation and regional sensing.

Presentation: The paper's general organization is acceptable, but it appears to be a poorly edited manuscript, with confusing descriptions and typos that hinder understanding. Some of these issues seem to be easy-to-fix typos, while others may require more detailed explanations.
