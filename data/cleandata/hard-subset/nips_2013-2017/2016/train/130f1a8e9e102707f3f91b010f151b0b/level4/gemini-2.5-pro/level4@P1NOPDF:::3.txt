The authors extend dictionary learning models by incorporating a Sobolev prior. While this is an interesting direction, the paper makes it difficult to assess the impact or improvement offered by the proposed algorithm.

The primary empirical validation relies on a visual comparison of the clarity and consistency of identified spatial maps of the brain. However, this assessment is highly subjective and the purported improvements are not readily apparent to a non-expert. Furthermore, in my experience with this type of decomposition, the quality of the data is often as important, if not more so, than the specifics of the algorithm. By incorporating penalties for smoothness and spatial consistency, it is not surprising that the method produces smoother resulting components; this outcome is an expected consequence of the chosen prior.

The authors suggest the method could be useful for isolating regions with sparse data. However, given the use of the vast Human Connectome Project dataset where data scarcity is not a primary concern, this particular motivation seems less compelling.

The paper could have been significantly strengthened by applying the model to a more challenging and impactful problem, such as identifying consistent brain regions across subjects. Using inter-subject consistency as a quantitative validation metric, for instance, would have provided more objective evidence of the method's performance than the current visual inspection.

In summary, the validation presented is largely subjective, which makes it difficult to ascertain the practical benefits of the proposed method.
