Summary
The paper proposes an online multivariate dictionary-learning method that enforces both sparsity and spatial structure in learned components (“atoms”) for neuroimaging data. Building on the online sparse coding framework of Mairal et al. (2010), the authors add a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty on the atoms to encourage piecewise-smooth, compact “blob-like” regions, while using an ℓ1-type constraint to promote sparsity. The algorithm retains the scalability of online learning and employs block-coordinate updates with efficient proximal steps for the Laplacian-regularized atom updates. Experiments on HCP task fMRI maps compare the proposed method (Smooth-SODL) to standard online dictionary learning (SODL) and a multi-subject PCA/ICA baseline (CanICA), and explore hyperparameter effects.

Relation to prior work: The approach extends the SODL framework of Mairal et al. to structured atoms via a Sobolev prior, aiming for the benefits of structured sparsity seen in related work (e.g., TV-ℓ1 and GraphNet penalties) while preserving online scalability. It addresses the need for spatially coherent components in population-level fMRI analysis, going beyond ICA/PCA methods that often produce dense, noisy maps.

Quality
- The modeling framework and optimization scheme are clearly defined and grounded in established methods. The block-coordinate strategy, ridge coding, and Laplacian-regularized atom updates are sound.
- However, in the main submission, the empirical evidence for the added value of the Laplacian prior was not sufficiently demonstrated. Figure 2 did not make it clear in what quantitative sense Smooth-SODL improved over SODL and CanICA. Beyond qualitative statements such as “more structured,” the paper should define and report quantitative spatial-structure metrics (e.g., Laplacian energy, number/size of connected components, boundary smoothness/compactness, Dice overlap with known atlases, reproducibility across splits) and show where Smooth-SODL identifies coherent regions missed by SODL/CanICA.
- After reading the rebuttal, the additional results were informative and addressed part of this gap. I would raise my technical assessment accordingly. Please incorporate those results into the revision and, if possible, add quantitative measures of “structure” and neurobiological plausibility with appropriate citations.

Originality
- The main contribution is the integration of a Sobolev (Laplacian) prior into online dictionary learning for large-scale brain imaging. While structured penalties are not new per se, the online formulation and efficient atom updates that retain scalability over very large n and p provide a notable methodological advance.

Significance
- A scalable approach to learn sparse, spatially coherent components across subjects is valuable for neuroimaging. If the structural prior indeed yields more interpretable, reproducible, and behaviorally predictive components without sacrificing much explained variance, the method could have practical impact for atlas construction, dimensionality reduction, and downstream prediction. Exploring further applications (e.g., segmentation or regional sensing) would broaden its relevance.

Clarity and presentation
- The paper is generally well organized but contains typos, unclear statements, and notational glitches that impede understanding. Please address the following:
  - Matrix decomposition (between Lines 67–68): should the transpose apply to each V^j individually rather than to the group?
  - Line 73: add the missing HCP reference.
  - Line 83: grammar (“The GraphNet penalty can impose …”).
  - Line 146: remove the bold sentence that appears to be an internal comment.
  - Equations (6) and (8): the signs and factors do not match. Clarify whether V in the update refers to the previous iterate (with the j-th atom fixed) and ensure consistent notation for Bt and Aj terms.
  - Line 157: duplicate phrase (“a single, a single”).
  - Line 169: typo (“We did an extensive study of th how”).
  - Section 3.3 (around Line 147): provide the explicit algorithmic solution for the atom update (e.g., FISTA steps, projection onto C) in an appendix, including the Lipschitz constant and stopping criteria.
  - Figures:
    - Figure 2: use identical slices across methods to enable visual comparison. If arguing “more structured,” accompany with quantitative metrics in the caption or main text.
    - Figure 3: clarify what is being predicted (behavioral scores), what features are used (codes U_i vs raw maps), how models are trained and evaluated (train/test split, cross-validation, confounds), define all legend items, and include the PCA baseline if mentioned. Specify which curves correspond to R^2 > 0.1 and on which split (train vs test).

Pros for acceptance
- Scalable, principled extension of online dictionary learning with a spatial smoothness prior.
- Potentially more interpretable and reproducible components than unstructured SODL or dense PCA/ICA.
- Relevant to population-level neuroimaging and ROI discovery; promising preliminary results and sensible hyperparameter guidance.

Cons for acceptance
- In the main submission, limited quantitative evidence that the Laplacian prior improves structure or neuroscientific validity beyond qualitative visuals. The rebuttal partially addresses this; the paper should include those results and additional structure metrics.
- Some notational and editorial issues that obscure algorithmic details and make reproduction harder.
- Comparative baselines and figures need tighter, more standardized presentation to support claims.

Recommendation
This is a meaningful and timely extension of online dictionary learning to structured atom estimation in neuroimaging. With the additional results from the rebuttal integrated, and with clearer quantitative evaluation of spatial structure, reproducibility, and neuroscientific plausibility, the paper would make a solid contribution. I encourage the authors to tighten the presentation, correct the noted issues, standardize the comparisons, and include quantitative structure metrics alongside explained variance and prediction performance.
