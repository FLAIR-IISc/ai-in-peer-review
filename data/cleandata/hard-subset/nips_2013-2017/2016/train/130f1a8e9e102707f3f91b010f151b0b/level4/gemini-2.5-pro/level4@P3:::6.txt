This paper introduces Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), a method for decomposing large-scale neuroimaging data into a set of sparse and spatially structured component maps. The work extends the well-known online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty promotes spatial smoothness, encouraging the learned components to form compact, blob-like regions, which are more neuroscientifically interpretable than the scattered patterns often produced by standard L1-regularized methods. The authors propose a Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) algorithm that preserves the scalability of the online framework, allowing it to be applied to massive datasets. The method is validated on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (HCP), where it is compared against standard Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (SODL) and Canonical Independent Component Analysis (CanICA), a widely used technique in the field.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution is the elegant and effective integration of a spatial smoothness prior (Laplacian penalty) into a scalable online dictionary learning framework. This directly addresses a significant problem in neuroimaging and other fields dealing with spatial data: how to learn parts-based representations that are not only sparse but also spatially coherent. While the individual components (online learning, L1 and Laplacian penalties) are known, their combination in this computationally efficient manner is novel and provides a powerful tool for discovering data-driven brain atlases or regions of interest.

2.  **Technical Quality and Scalability:** The proposed method is technically sound. The authors provide a clear derivation of the BCD algorithm and correctly situate its convergence guarantees within the theoretical framework established by Mairal et al. (2010). A crucial strength is the method's scalability. The experiments demonstrate that this added structural prior comes at a modest computational cost (a reported 2-3x increase in runtime over the highly optimized SODL), making it practical for the very large datasets for which it is designed.

3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper is supported by a strong and convincing set of experiments. The choice of the HCP dataset is appropriate, and the comparison to both a sparse-only baseline (SODL) and a standard decomposition method (CanICA) is well-chosen. The evaluation is thorough, using a combination of qualitative visual inspection and quantitative metrics, including explained variance, stability across data splits, and the predictive power of the learned representations for behavioral scores. The learning curve analysis in Table 1 is particularly compelling, as it clearly demonstrates the advantage of the spatial regularization in low- and medium-data regimes, where unstructured methods struggle with noise.

### **Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection)**

1.  **Non-Convexity and Initialization Sensitivity:** The authors correctly note that the overall optimization problem is non-convex, meaning the algorithm is guaranteed only to find a local minimum. This is a common and accepted limitation in dictionary learning. However, the quality of the solution can therefore depend on initialization. While the paper mentions that random initializations produced stable results, a more quantitative analysis of this stability (e.g., by reporting the variance in performance across different random seeds) would strengthen the work and provide users with greater confidence in the method's robustness.

2.  **Practical Challenge of Hyper-parameter Tuning:** The model's performance relies on the careful selection of at least three key hyper-parameters: the code regularization (α), the sparsity level (τ), and the spatial smoothness weight (γ). The authors acknowledge this is a difficult problem and provide a useful empirical study (Fig. 1), suggesting cross-validation as a general strategy. However, this multi-parameter search can be computationally expensive and may present a barrier to adoption for practitioners. Providing more robust heuristics or an adaptive scheme for setting these parameters would significantly enhance the practical utility of the method.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** While the paper is generally well-organized, some of the technical sections are dense and could be made more accessible to a broader audience. For example, the presentation of the dictionary update step in Section 3.1 (e.g., Eq. 7 and the surrounding text) is mathematically compact. Expanding on the derivation and providing more intuition behind the steps would improve readability and help readers better appreciate the technical details of the algorithm.

### **Summary and Recommendation**

This paper presents a valuable and well-executed contribution to the field of large-scale machine learning for neuroimaging. The proposed Smooth-SODL method is a principled and scalable solution to the important problem of learning interpretable, structured components from complex spatial data. The strengths—in particular, the novel formulation, computational efficiency, and thorough empirical validation—convincingly demonstrate the method's utility and superiority over existing approaches in key aspects like interpretability and performance in data-limited settings. The weaknesses concerning initialization sensitivity and hyper-parameter tuning are common to many such models and do not detract significantly from the core contribution. I recommend acceptance.
