Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for multivariate decomposition of brain images that enforces both sparsity and spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. Sparsity is imposed via an l1-type constraint (implemented as an l1 ball or simplex), while spatial smoothness and compact support are encouraged by a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty on each atom. The algorithm extends Mairal et al.’s online dictionary learning by adding a structured update for atoms solved via a few iterations of FISTA within a block-coordinate descent loop, and retains scalability with only a 2–3× runtime overhead. The authors relate their approach to structured decompositions such as TV–l1/GraphNet, structured sparse PCA, and multi-subject ICA (e.g., CanICA), arguing that prior structured methods relied on slow alternating minimization and do not scale to large neuroimaging datasets.

Experiments on HCP task fMRI (n=500 subjects; k=40 components) compare Smooth-SODL to standard SODL and CanICA on interpretability, explained variance, stability/reproducibility across splits, and behavioral prediction. Smooth-SODL yields sparser, smoother, and visually more localized “blob-like” components than SODL, and far more interpretable maps than dense PCA/ICA components. It improves stability over SODL and thresholded ICA, with moderate explained variance (lower than CanICA, as expected), and competitive behavioral prediction relative to SODL.

Strengths
- Problem relevance and scope: Learning spatially interpretable, population-level components from massive neuroimaging data is an important task with broad interest at NIPS (matrix factorization, online optimization, structured sparsity).
- Methodological contribution: Incorporates a Laplacian prior directly into an online dictionary learning framework and shows how to update atoms efficiently with few FISTA iterations. The connection to GraphNet-style denoising is clear and useful.
- Scalability: The online nature and modest overhead relative to SODL are compelling for large datasets; mini-batching and sufficient statistics (A, B) are standard but well leveraged.
- Empirical evaluation: Uses a large public dataset (HCP), compares to appropriate baselines (SODL, CanICA), and evaluates interpretability, stability, explained variance, and behavioral prediction. The learning-curve analysis showing benefits of spatial regularization in low-data regimes is informative.
- Practical guidance: Discusses hyperparameter effects and proposes data-adaptive schedules (e.g., αt ∝ t−1/2), as well as safe ranges for (γ, τ), with cross-validation recommendations.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and presentation: The paper contains numerous typos, duplicated sentences, and unresolved editor notes (e.g., “XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v.”). These issues hinder readability and should be fixed. Algorithmic details (stopping criteria, exact projection sets used, choice between l1 ball vs simplex and implications for non-negativity) need clearer, consolidated exposition.
- Limited theoretical analysis: Beyond referencing convergence guarantees of online dictionary learning, there is no new theoretical analysis tailored to the added Laplacian penalty (e.g., conditions for identifiability, atom recovery, or stability). The “1–2 FISTA iterations suffice” claim is empirical and should be supported by sensitivity analyses or systematic stopping criteria.
- Fairness and breadth of comparisons: CanICA is evaluated with and without hard thresholding and with pre-smoothing, whereas SODL/Smooth-SODL are not pre-smoothed. It would be helpful to report results with consistent preprocessing across methods and include additional structured baselines (e.g., TV–l1 dictionary learning, structured sparse PCA) where feasible.
- Evaluation details: The stability/reproducibility metric (mean correlation of matched atoms) needs clearer definition (matching protocol, variability across runs) and confidence intervals. Behavioral prediction pipelines (featureization, model family, regularization, and cross-validation) should be described precisely to ensure reproducibility and fairness.
- Hyperparameter selection: While practical tips are given, the methodology for selecting γ and τ (and batch size, k) is not fully specified. An ablation showing sensitivity to these choices would strengthen the empirical claims.

Quality
- The algorithm is technically sound as an extension of online BCD with proximal updates for structured atoms. However, the lack of deeper analysis tailored to the Laplacian penalty and the sparse constraints limits theoretical support. Empirical results are promising but would benefit from more rigorous and balanced evaluations and clearer reporting.

Clarity
- The high-level ideas are understandable, but clarity is undermined by typographical errors, duplicated text, inconsistent notation (V vs v), and missing details for the atom update and projections. The paper needs careful proofreading and tighter organization of algorithms and hyperparameter guidance.

Originality
- Integrating a Laplacian prior into an online dictionary learning framework for large-scale neuroimaging appears novel relative to prior structured but offline methods (e.g., TV–l1) and unstructured online methods (SODL). The FFT-based variant (in supplement) and practical guidance add to the contribution.

Significance
- The approach is likely to be useful for scalable, interpretable decompositions in neuroimaging and potentially other imaging domains. The demonstrated gains in interpretability and stability, at modest computational cost, are valuable. Broader impact depends on stronger empirical validation and clearer, reproducible methodology.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Fix all editorial issues (e.g., remove “XXX” comments, duplicated sentences, consistent notation for V/v, and correct typos like “intepretable,” “xperiments”).
- Clearly define the constraint set C used in experiments (l1 ball vs simplex). If a simplex is used, discuss the implications for non-negativity and whether negative loadings are meaningful in the application.
- Provide explicit stopping criteria and tolerances for the FISTA inner loop; include a sensitivity analysis showing the effect of the number of inner iterations on accuracy and runtime.
- Report memory usage and complexity per iteration; include wall-clock timings across different k and batch sizes.
- Ensure fair preprocessing across baselines; consider adding a structured baseline (e.g., TV–l1 dictionary learning) on a smaller subset for completeness.
- Add confidence intervals or variability bands for explained variance, stability, and prediction metrics; detail the matching strategy for stability (e.g., Hungarian algorithm).
- Release code and scripts to reproduce the experiments (or provide an anonymized link if possible).

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a scalable, principled method.
- Improves interpretability and stability of learned components with modest overhead.
- Solid empirical results on a large public dataset; practical insights on hyperparameters.

Arguments against acceptance
- Presentation issues and missing details impede reproducibility.
- Limited theoretical contribution beyond adapting proximal updates.
- Comparisons could be more balanced and comprehensive; evaluation lacks statistical rigor in places.

Overall assessment
This is a promising and timely contribution that extends online dictionary learning with spatial structure for neuroimaging, with clear practical benefits. However, the paper requires significant polishing and stronger, more rigorous empirical validation. I lean toward a weak accept if the authors can substantially improve clarity, ensure fair and reproducible comparisons, and address the evaluation gaps; otherwise, it is borderline.
