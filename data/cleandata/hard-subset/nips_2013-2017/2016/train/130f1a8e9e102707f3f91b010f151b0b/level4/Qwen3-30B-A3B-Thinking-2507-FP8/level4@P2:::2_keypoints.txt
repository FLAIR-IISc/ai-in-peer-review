- Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online dictionary learning extension with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatially coherent dictionary atoms for neuroimaging.

- Extends Mairal et al.'s sparse online dictionary learning to leverage inter-subject variability and identify functional brain units without anatomical priors or clustering.

- Claims advantages over PCA/ICA (dense, less interpretable components) and standard dictionary learning (no spatial constraints).

- Empirical evaluation on HCP data shows more spatially structured atoms and better capture of inter-subject variability compared to SODL and CanICA.

- Demonstrates largest benefits in small-data regimes (e.g., ~14× improvement in explained variance with 17 subjects).

- Computational cost increases modestly (2–3× or noted as 3×) relative to SODL, considered justified by gains in interpretability and performance.

- Technical approach is sound with a solid theoretical foundation using Laplacian regularization to enforce spatial smoothness.

- Empirical support is good but quantitative evidence of spatial-structure improvement is lacking and could be strengthened.

- Clarity issues: inconsistent mathematical notation, missing references, typos, and hard-to-follow algorithmic descriptions in Section 3.1.

- Figures need improvement: Figure 2 uses different anatomical slices hindering direct comparison and lacks correspondence to textual description; Figure 3 lacks clarity on prediction targets and R² interpretation.

- Originality: novel application of Sobolev-regularized online dictionary learning to neuroimaging and differentiates from prior structured-dictionary or offline methods.

- Significance: addresses an important neuroimaging need for interpretable, data-driven brain regions and scales to large datasets due to online formulation.

- Strengths include clear technical contribution, Nilearn implementation for accessibility, and comprehensive experiment suite (reproducibility, explained variance, predictive performance).

- Weaknesses to address: add quantitative spatial-structure metrics, fix notation and presentation issues, improve figure captions and alignment with text, better relate results to neuroscience literature, and clarify the prediction task in Figure 3.

- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve clarity and strengthen quantitative validation of spatial structure.
