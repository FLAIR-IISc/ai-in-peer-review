The authors extend dictionary learning models to incorporate a Sobolev prior. This is an interesting problem, but the impact and improvement offered by the proposed algorithm are difficult to assess. The main results are presented through visual comparison of the clarity and consistency of identified brain regions, which may not be readily apparent to readers unfamiliar with the field. Furthermore, based on my experience with these types of decomposition methods, data quality is often as important as, if not more important than, the algorithm itself. Given that the method incorporates penalties for smoothness and spatial consistency, it’s not particularly surprising that the resulting regions appear smoother. This approach might be more valuable when dealing with limited data, but with the extensive datasets available through the Human Connectome Project, this doesn’t appear to be a significant challenge. I had hoped this paper would focus more on consolidating differences in brain regions across subjects, and potentially leverage inter-subject differences or consistency as a form of performance validation. The validation presented is largely subjective, and the concrete benefits of the approach remain unclear.
