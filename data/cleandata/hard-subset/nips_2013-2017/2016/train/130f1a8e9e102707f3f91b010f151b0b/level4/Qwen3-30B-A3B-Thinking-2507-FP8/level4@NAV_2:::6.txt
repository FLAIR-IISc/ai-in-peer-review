

This paper introduces a novel extension to online dictionary learning for brain imaging applications, incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness in the dictionary atoms while maintaining sparsity. The authors build upon the foundational work of Mairal et al. (2010) and related structured dictionary learning approaches [1, 25], addressing the critical need for interpretable spatially-structured representations in neuroimaging data analysis. The proposed Smooth-SODL framework effectively combines sparsity (via ℓ1-norm) and spatial structure (via Laplacian penalty) to produce more interpretable brain region decompositions that better capture inter-subject variability across different dataset sizes.

Strengths:
- The paper makes a significant technical contribution by extending online dictionary learning with spatial structure through a Laplacian penalty, which is theoretically well-motivated and computationally efficient (only 2-3x slower than standard SODL).
- The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against relevant baselines (SODL, CanICA) across multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, behavioral prediction) on the HCP dataset, demonstrating clear advantages of the proposed method.
- The method addresses an important practical problem in neuroimaging: principled extraction of brain regions without relying on anatomical priors or cross-modality correspondences.
- The paper provides valuable insights into hyperparameter selection and the trade-offs between sparsity, structure, and explanatory power.

Weaknesses:
- The paper acknowledges the non-convex nature of the problem but does not provide a theoretical analysis of solution existence or convergence guarantees beyond noting that the algorithm converges to a local optimum.
- The experimental validation is strong, but the theoretical foundation could be strengthened with more rigorous analysis of the optimization landscape.
- The writing requires improvement in places (e.g., line 146), though the authors have addressed these concerns in their rebuttal.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, with thorough experimental validation demonstrating clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods. The paper adequately addresses both strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and generally clear, though some sections (notably line 146) require improvement. The authors' rebuttal effectively addressed the clarity concerns, making the paper substantially clearer upon revision.

Originality: The combination of online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization for spatial structure in brain imaging represents a meaningful advancement over previous work [1, 25], with the authors clearly articulating how their approach differs from existing methods.

Significance: The work addresses a critical challenge in neuroimaging - principled extraction of brain regions that capture inter-subject variability - with potential impact on brain mapping and functional connectivity analysis. The method's scalability to large datasets makes it particularly relevant for modern neuroimaging studies.

Pro: Novel regularization approach that combines sparsity and spatial structure; strong experimental validation across multiple metrics; clear demonstration of advantages over state-of-the-art methods.

Con: Limited theoretical analysis of solution existence for the non-convex problem; some writing requires improvement (addressed in rebuttal).

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging analysis, with a well-motivated approach that effectively balances theoretical grounding with practical utility. The authors have successfully extended the online dictionary learning framework to address a key challenge in brain imaging, demonstrating both technical merit and practical significance.
