This paper presents an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals, extending a well-established framework [16] with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply their method to empirical data, report the effect of varying tuning parameters, and compare performance with two alternative methods in the field. 

**Technical Quality:** I would rate this paper as a 3. The model definition is clear, and the algorithms follow a well-established framework [16], appearing solid. While the presentation initially obscured this, the paper now demonstrates a clear empirical advantage to introducing the Sobolev prior (following information provided in the rebuttal). Specifically, the rebuttal demonstrates improved clustering and interpretability with respect to known brain structures. In the original submission, it was unclear *how* the Smooth-SODL method was better than the alternatives in Figure 2. However, the authors have now clarified that the method recovers more interpretable and spatially coherent structures, with quantitative metrics supporting these observations. The authors could further strengthen the paper by explicitly describing criteria, justified by neuroscience literature, and providing quantitative measurements of improvement according to these criteria in the revised manuscript.

**Novelty/Originality:** There is a notable original contribution in this paper: the extension of [16] to apply Sobolev priors to encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms. This is a meaningful contribution to the field, as it addresses a key challenge in neuroimaging data analysis – capturing spatially coherent brain structures.

**Potential Impact or Usefulness:** Dictionary learning is a valuable approach for explaining neuroimaging data across large subject groups, and introducing spatial priors like the Sobolev prior should theoretically improve results. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm for doing so, adding a useful tool to the neuroscience/medical imaging community's toolkit. The authors could explore further applications, such as image segmentation and regional sensing, to highlight the versatility of their method.

**Presentation:** The general organization of the paper is adequate. However, the original manuscript required editing for clarity and readability. Many of the issues have now been addressed, and I detail the remaining areas for improvement below:

*   Line 67-68: The transpose symbol on the group of $V^j$ (j = 1,..., k) should apply to each individual $V^j$.
*   Line 73: The reference for the HCP dataset was missing and has been added.
*   Line 83: "The Graph-Net penalty can imposes..." should read "The Graph-Net penalty imposes..."
*   Line 146: The bold sentence appears to be a co-author’s comment that was not removed.
*   Last line of Equation (6) and Equation (8): There was an inconsistency in the sign: $V_j + A_{t[j,j]}^{-1}(B_{t[j]} - V A_{t[j]}) $ versus $A_{t[j,j]} V_j - (V A_{t[j]} - B_{t[j]})$. This has been addressed.
*   Line 157: “a single, a single” should read “a single”.
*   Line 169: "We did an extensive study of th how" should read "We did an extensive study of how".
*   Section 3.3: The paragraph near line 147, below Algorithm 2, would benefit from explicitly stating the mathematical/algorithmic solution to Equation (6), even in the appendix.
*   Figure 2: The results from different methods were presented at different horizontal and sagittal slices, making direct comparison difficult. The authors have clarified this, and the revised figure facilitates better comparison.
*   Figure 2 (previously): The description in line 222 claiming the results by the Smooth-SODL model were more "structured" was vague. This has been improved with the addition of quantitative results.
*   Figure 2 (previously): The legends were unclear and the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption was missing. These issues have been resolved.
*   Figure 2 (previously): The description in lines 224-227, claiming an R<sup>2</sup> greater than 0.1, was unclear. The curves to reference were not obvious. This has been addressed in the revised manuscript.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging: learning spatially coherent representations of brain activity.
*   The proposed method offers a computationally efficient solution for large-scale datasets via online learning.
*   The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on a large, publicly available dataset (HCP).
*   The inclusion of the Sobolev prior demonstrably improves the interpretability and spatial coherence of the learned dictionaries.
*   The rebuttal provided additional results and clarified the improvements made by the proposed method.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The initial presentation of the results lacked quantitative support for the claimed improvements. (This issue has been addressed by the rebuttal and will be further improved in the revision).
*   The paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison to other related methods, particularly those focusing on spatial regularization.




I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors incorporating the suggestions and clarifications provided in the rebuttal into a revised manuscript.
