This paper presents an online algorithm for learning structured sparse dictionaries from multi-subject neuroimaging data. The proposed method, named Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), extends a well-established online dictionary learning framework by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to promote spatial smoothness in the learned dictionary components, or "atoms". The authors apply their method to a large fMRI dataset, analyze the effects of hyper-parameters, and compare its performance with standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a multi-subject Independent Component Analysis (ICA) approach.

**Summary of Contributions**
The core contribution is an efficient, scalable algorithm for structured dictionary learning that imposes a spatial smoothness prior on the dictionary atoms. This is achieved by adding a Laplacian regularization term to the objective function of the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010), resulting in an algorithm that is only marginally slower than the unstructured version. The authors test this method on task fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project, evaluating the interpretability, stability, and predictive power of the learned dictionaries.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**
The paper's technical approach is sound. The model is clearly defined, and the algorithm is a well-motivated extension of a proven framework. The primary novelty—the integration of a Laplacian penalty into a scalable online learning algorithm—is a valuable contribution, as such priors are theoretically beneficial for improving the interpretability of neuroimaging decompositions. The online nature of the algorithm makes it applicable to the large datasets that are now common in the field.

The main weakness of the paper is in the empirical demonstration of the proposed method's benefits. The results section does not provide compelling evidence that the addition of the Laplacian penalty leads to superior outcomes compared to the baseline methods. While the authors claim the results are "more structured" and "less noisy," these assertions are not well-supported by quantitative analysis.
*   The qualitative comparison in Figure 2a is difficult to assess, as different anatomical slices are shown for different methods, preventing a direct comparison. It is not obvious from the figure that the Smooth-SODL components are qualitatively better.
*   The paper would be significantly improved by including quantitative metrics that directly assess the "structuredness" of the learned atoms (e.g., measures of spatial compactness, smoothness, or contiguity).
*   To substantiate the neuroscientific value, the authors could demonstrate that their method is better at recovering known functional networks or that the resulting brain regions show higher correspondence with established atlases.

*Update after rebuttal:* The additional results provided in the author rebuttal are very informative and begin to address the concerns about empirical validation. I have raised my score accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to incorporate these new findings into the revised manuscript and to further strengthen the claims about the learned atoms being more structured and neuroscientifically meaningful.

**Clarity and Presentation**
The overall organization is logical, but the manuscript suffers from a lack of polish, including several typos and unclear descriptions that detract from its readability. The paper would benefit significantly from a thorough round of proofreading. Specific points for improvement are listed below:
*   **Mathematical Formalism:**
    *   There appears to be an inconsistency in the dictionary update rule as described in Equation (7) and Algorithm 2. The authors should carefully check the signs and terms in these equations to ensure they are correct and consistent. It should also be clarified that in the update step, `V` refers to the dictionary from the previous iteration.
    *   For reproducibility, it would be helpful to provide the explicit algorithm or pseudo-code used to solve the dictionary update sub-problem (Equation 8), even if it is a standard method like FISTA. This could be included in an appendix.
*   **Figures:**
    *   **Figure 2a:** As mentioned above, using consistent anatomical views for all methods is crucial for effective qualitative comparison.
    *   **Figure 2c:** This figure is difficult to understand. The caption and text should clearly state what is being predicted, what features are used (e.g., the subject codes `Ui`), what the different colored bars/legends represent, and why the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption is not shown. The textual claim about R² scores is also difficult to verify from the plot.
*   **Minor Errors and Typos:** A number of typographical errors were noted, including (but not limited to) those on lines 146, 157, and 169 of the original draft. A sentence in bold on line 146 suggests a leftover co-author comment. These should be corrected.

In summary, this paper presents a promising and well-motivated method for a relevant problem in neuroimaging. However, its acceptance is conditional on a substantial revision that strengthens the empirical results and improves the overall clarity and quality of the presentation.
