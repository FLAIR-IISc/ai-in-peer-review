The authors propose a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. This approach extends previous work by incorporating a Sobolev penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The paper provides a thorough description of the algorithm and its implementation, as well as experiments on a large public dataset.

The main contribution of this work is the incorporation of a Sobolev prior into the dictionary learning framework. This is an interesting problem, and the authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their approach. The use of a Laplacian penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms is a novel aspect of this work.

The experiments presented in the paper demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in extracting structured and denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability. The comparison with state-of-the-art methods, such as SODL and CanICA, shows that the proposed method outperforms these approaches in terms of explained variance and stability of the dictionary atoms.

However, the validation presented in the paper seems subjective and not entirely clear. The main results figure relies on visually comparing the clarity/consistency of identified blobs on the brain, but this is not clear to an untrained eye. Furthermore, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed method consolidates the differences of brain regions across subjects.

One potential limitation of this work is that the incorporation of penalties for smoothness and spatial consistency may not be surprising, given the vast amount of data accessible with the Human Connectome Project. The authors could have done more to demonstrate the benefits of their approach in isolating regions with little data.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper addresses the quality, clarity, originality, and significance of the work. The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their approach, and the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. However, the originality of the work could be further emphasized, and the significance of the results could be more clearly explained.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to dictionary learning that incorporates a Sobolev prior.
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in extracting structured and denoised dictionaries.
* The comparison with state-of-the-art methods shows that the proposed method outperforms these approaches in terms of explained variance and stability of the dictionary atoms.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The validation presented in the paper seems subjective and not entirely clear.
* The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed method consolidates the differences of brain regions across subjects.
* The incorporation of penalties for smoothness and spatial consistency may not be surprising, given the vast amount of data accessible with the Human Connectome Project.

Overall, the paper presents a well-structured and clear approach to dictionary learning that incorporates a Sobolev prior. However, the validation and significance of the results could be further improved.
