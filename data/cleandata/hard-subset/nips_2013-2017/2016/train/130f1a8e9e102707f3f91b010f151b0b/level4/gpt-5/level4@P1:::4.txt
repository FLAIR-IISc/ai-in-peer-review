Summary
The paper proposes an online, under-complete dictionary learning approach for neuroimaging that combines an L1 constraint on atoms to encourage sparsity with a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty to promote smooth, blob-like spatial structure. The goal is to obtain interpretable functional components from fMRI and related brain imaging data while scaling to large datasets. The method is presented as a nontrivial extension of the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010), with a reported 2–3× runtime overhead, and is evaluated on Human Connectome Project data. The authors claim improved interpretability and better capture of inter-subject variability relative to simpler baselines.

Strengths
- The problem addressed is important: discovering interpretable, localized functional components from large neuroimaging datasets.
- The core idea—sparsity plus spatial smoothness via an L1 constraint and Laplacian penalty—is reasonable and likely to yield blob-like atoms that align with neuroanatomical regions.
- Building on the online framework of Mairal et al. is practical for scaling; the claimed runtime overhead appears modest.
- The approach is potentially useful for ROI definition and dimensionality reduction in population studies.

Weaknesses
- The empirical improvements over simpler methods are unclear. The reported gains are either small or not convincingly demonstrated, and the manuscript does not sufficiently explain why the proposed regularization produces better results in practice.
- The presentation feels rushed. There are editing artifacts (e.g., draft comments remain, including at line 146), and the text is at times tedious and confusing to follow.
- The experimental section does not convincingly establish the advantage of the proposed method over standard baselines; the connection between the method’s design and the observed outcomes is not clearly articulated.

Quality
The approach is interesting and grounded in established techniques (online dictionary learning; sparsity; Laplacian regularization). However, the empirical evidence is not strong enough to support the main claims. The paper should more clearly quantify the benefits over simpler baselines (e.g., standard sparse online dictionary learning without Laplacian and PCA/ICA), with robust metrics and analysis. At present, the improvement appears modest or insufficiently explained.

Clarity
The manuscript is difficult to read in places. Draft comments remain (e.g., line 146), and the exposition is sometimes confusing. The paper would benefit from tighter organization, clearer algorithmic descriptions, and improved narrative flow linking the method to the empirical results. As written, it is tedious to parse, which hinders understanding and evaluation.

Originality
The contribution is somewhat original: the combination of an L1 constraint on atoms with a Laplacian penalty within an online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging is a reasonable, incremental extension of prior work. The relationship to Mairal et al. (2010) is straightforward, and the novelty lies in the specific structured regularization and its application.

Significance
Potential significance is limited by the current empirical support. If the method consistently yields more interpretable components without sacrificing reconstruction quality or generalization, it could be valuable for large-scale neuroimaging. However, the present results do not convincingly establish a substantive advantage over simpler approaches.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and strengthen the empirical evaluation:
  - Provide clear, quantitative comparisons to standard sparse online dictionary learning (γ = 0) and PCA/ICA baselines on the same data, with consistent preprocessing.
  - Report effect sizes, confidence intervals, and statistical tests where appropriate.
  - Include interpretability and stability metrics that directly support the claim of improved, blob-like, reproducible atoms.
  - Provide ablations (vary γ and sparsity constraint) to show how the Laplacian term drives observed improvements.
- Improve the exposition:
  - Remove all draft comments and proofreading artifacts; streamline the text to improve readability.
  - Clearly explain the motivation for the Laplacian penalty (why this form of smoothness), and provide intuition for how it interacts with the L1 constraint on atoms.
  - Describe the computational overhead and convergence behavior more transparently, and specify practical ranges for key hyperparameters.
- Help the reader reproduce results:
  - Summarize key implementation details and hyperparameter settings used in the main experiments.
  - Ensure that comparisons to baselines are fair (e.g., consistent smoothing/thresholding choices).

Pros
- Addresses an important problem in large-scale neuroimaging.
- Sensible combination of sparsity and spatial smoothness; likely to enhance interpretability.
- Scalable online algorithm with modest reported overhead.

Cons
- Gains over simpler baselines appear small or are not sufficiently demonstrated.
- Writing is rushed and sometimes confusing; draft comments remain.
- Limited clarity on why and when the proposed regularization yields practical benefits.
