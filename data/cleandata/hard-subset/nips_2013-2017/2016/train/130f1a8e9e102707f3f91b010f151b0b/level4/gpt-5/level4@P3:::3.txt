Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning method for brain imaging that augments the Mairal et al. (2010) framework with a spatial smoothness prior on dictionary atoms. Atoms are constrained for sparsity via an ℓ1-type constraint (or simplex) and for spatial structure via a Laplacian (Sobolev/Tikhonov) penalty, while sample codes are computed with ridge regression. The optimization combines the standard online sufficient statistics (A, B) with block-coordinate updates of atoms, each solved as a constrained quadratic denoising subproblem using FISTA. The authors argue this yields piece-wise smooth, compact “blobs” that are more interpretable than standard SODL, with only a 2–3× runtime increase.

The work sits between unstructured online dictionary learning (Mairal 2010; Mensch 2016) and structured decompositions used in neuroimaging (e.g., TV-ℓ1/GraphNet, Abraham 2013; Grosenick 2013; Jenatton et al. 2010), and group ICA methods (Varoquaux 2010). The novelty is not a new penalty per se (Laplacian-type smoothness is well established), but an efficient way to incorporate it in an online dictionary-learning scheme with practical convergence and scalability.

Empirical evaluation on HCP task fMRI (n=500; p≈2.6×10^5; k=40) compares Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA. Results show improved visual compactness and stability of learned atoms, modest EV improvements over SODL (and below CanICA), better early-sample learning curves relative to SODL, and competitive behavioral prediction (though CanICA often performs best).

Strengths
- Methodological contribution: a simple, scalable integration of spatial smoothness into online dictionary learning, with a clear algorithmic path (online sufficient statistics; convex atom subproblems solved efficiently by FISTA).
- Practical benefits: small runtime overhead relative to SODL and faster than the batch CanICA implementation; suitable for large n, large p regimes.
- Sensible design choices: non-negativity/simplex option for atoms, ridge-coded samples for stability, and an adaptive αt ∝ t^−1/2 schedule.
- Empirical scope: use of a large, public dataset; learning curves illustrating the “small-data” advantage; basic stability analysis via split-half correlations; behavior prediction benchmarking.

Weaknesses
- Validation is dominated by qualitative visualization. The paper would benefit from stronger quantitative, spatially explicit metrics of interpretability and reproducibility (e.g., number of connected components, spatial entropy, Dice/Jaccard overlap across splits, NPAIRS/ICC).
- Comparisons and preprocessing may not be fully fair. CanICA is smoothed a priori while SODL/Smooth-SODL are not; a smoothed-SODL baseline or a no-smoothing CanICA variant would help disentangle the effect of the Laplacian from generic spatial smoothing.
- Baselines are limited. Established structured baselines (e.g., TV-ℓ1/GraphNet dictionary learning, nonnegative matrix factorization with spatial regularization) are not compared, despite being closely related conceptually and likely to affect interpretability.
- Limited ablations. It is unclear how much each design choice contributes: Laplacian vs no Laplacian (γ=0), ℓ1/simplex vs no sparsity, ridge vs ℓ1 codes, and the effect of non-negativity. A systematic ablation would clarify the source of improvements.
- Inter-subject variability claims are not deeply substantiated. Beyond atom stability measures, there is little analysis of whether the learned components capture population-level variability in a way that improves generalization across splits or aligns with known functional atlases.
- Downstream prediction mixed. CanICA generally achieves higher R^2 on behavioral prediction than Smooth-SODL; the trade-off between interpretability and predictive accuracy deserves discussion and stronger evidence that Smooth-SODL confers practical advantages.
- Hyperparameter guidance is limited. While the paper suggests αt and gives a caution on γ via Lipschitz constants, concrete selection procedures, ranges, and sensitivity analyses are thin. The cross-validation strategy for γ and τ needs more detail.

Quality
- The optimization is sound and leverages standard ingredients (online sufficient statistics, convex subproblems, FISTA). Convergence to a local minimum follows prior work’s assumptions.
- Practical caveat: the γ schedule and its interaction with At[j, j]/t should be detailed to guarantee stable step sizes; report the actual γ ranges used and sensitivity curves.
- Empirical claims would be stronger with objective spatial reproducibility metrics and additional structured baselines.

Clarity
- The method description is largely clear, but the exposition would benefit from:
  - A concise schematic of the online loop and the atom update subproblem.
  - Clearer statements on preprocessing parity across methods.
  - More details on hyperparameter selection (grids, CV folds, selection criteria).
  - Consistent terminology: “Sobolev/Laplacian penalty,” “atoms/components,” “structured” (define via metrics).
  - Minor typos: “xperiments,” “intepretable,” “there is not step size,” etc.

Originality
- Moderate. Laplacian/GraphNet-type regularization and sparse atoms are known; the main contribution is bringing this spatial prior into an efficient online dictionary-learning framework with minimal overhead.

Significance
- Potentially useful for scalable, more interpretable decompositions in large neuroimaging datasets. However, the paper would have higher impact with stronger quantitative validation, broader baselines, and clearer evidence that the method better captures inter-subject structure in ways that benefit downstream analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative interpretability/reproducibility metrics: connected components per atom, spatial entropy, smoothness (∑‖∇v‖^2), overlap with known atlases, split-half Dice/Jaccard/ICC of thresholded maps across data splits, and stability across random seeds.
- Ensure preprocessing parity: compare (i) CanICA with/without smoothing, (ii) SODL/Smooth-SODL with/without modest smoothing, and (iii) SODL + post hoc Gaussian smoothing vs Smooth-SODL.
- Include structured baselines: TV-ℓ1/GraphNet-based dictionary learning (e.g., as in Abraham et al.; Grosenick et al.), and nonnegative matrix factorization with Laplacian smoothing.
- Provide ablations: γ=0 vs γ>0; simplex vs ℓ1 vs no sparsity; ridge vs ℓ1 codes; with/without non-negativity; effect of k.
- Report hyperparameter selection: ranges, CV protocol, selection criterion (e.g., test EV, stability), and sensitivity plots for γ and τ.
- Clarify the γ schedule and robustness of FISTA iterations (step sizes, stopping criteria); report the actual number of FISTA steps used per atom update and its impact on speed/quality.
- Expand inter-subject analyses: demonstrate that learned atoms yield higher between-split reproducibility and align better with population variability than baselines; consider subject-embedding analyses and between-subject generalization tasks.
- Discuss the trade-off between interpretability and predictive performance, and when practitioners should prefer Smooth-SODL.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, scalable extension of a widely used online framework with practical benefits.
- Clear algorithmic contribution with modest computational overhead.
- Promising qualitative improvements and stability gains; advantage in low-sample regimes.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical validation lacks strong quantitative spatial metrics and broader structured baselines.
- Some comparisons may be confounded by preprocessing differences.
- Mixed downstream prediction results, with CanICA often superior.
- Limited ablations and hyperparameter guidance.

Overall, the idea is promising and practically relevant, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by more rigorous, quantitative evaluation and broader comparative analyses.
