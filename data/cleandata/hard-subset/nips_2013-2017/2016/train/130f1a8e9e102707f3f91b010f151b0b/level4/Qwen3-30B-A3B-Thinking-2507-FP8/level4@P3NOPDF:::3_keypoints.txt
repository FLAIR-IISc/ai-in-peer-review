- Paper proposes Sobolev-prior dictionary learning for brain imaging, but claimed contributions and impact are unclear due to methodological and validation weaknesses.  
- Main evaluation relies on qualitative visual "blob" comparisons, which are insufficient and subjective evidence of improvement.  
- No quantitative metrics provided (e.g., reconstruction error, spatial consistency, statistical tests) to support claims.  
- Sobolev prior predictably yields smoother maps, so smoother activations are expected and not necessarily novel.  
- Similar smoothness can be achieved with simpler regularizers (e.g., Tikhonov smoothing) without dictionary learning.  
- Authors ignore the role of data quality and scale, failing to show how their method addresses limitations of large datasets.  
- Paper does not quantify data sparsity regimes where the Sobolev prior offers an advantage.  
- No experiments contrasting low- vs high-data regimes to isolate algorithm utility.  
- Unclear whether observed smoothness is biologically plausible or merely an artifact of regularization.  
- Crucial omission of inter-subject validation; no measures of spatial consistency across subjects.  
- Authors do not leverage anatomical/functional landmarks or compare recovery of known brain networks (e.g., default mode).  
- Validation confined to single-subject visual inspection, failing to establish biological meaningfulness.  
- Claim of "better isolation of brain regions" is unsubstantiated and may reflect data structure rather than method superiority.  
- No comparison against existing spatially constrained methods (e.g., spatial K-SVD, total-variation approaches) on high-resolution data.  
- Technical exposition is shallow: no derivation of benefits over alternatives (e.g., Laplacian smoothing), no computational efficiency analysis, and no ablation studies isolating the Sobolev term.  
- Overall: method fails to demonstrate meaningful improvement over existing approaches and risks conflating regularization effects with genuine neuroscientific insight.  
- Recommended remedies: use quantitative neuroimaging metrics, compare to spatial-prior baselines, and demonstrate utility in data-scarce or noisy scenarios.
