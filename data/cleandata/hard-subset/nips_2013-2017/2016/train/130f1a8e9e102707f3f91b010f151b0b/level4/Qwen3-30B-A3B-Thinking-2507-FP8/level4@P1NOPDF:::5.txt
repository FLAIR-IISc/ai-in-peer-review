

This paper addresses dictionary learning for brain region modeling, proposing a large-scale online dictionary learning framework augmented with Sobolev regularization to enforce structural properties. While the application context is relevant, the core contributions fall short of NIPS standards. My major concerns are as follows.  

First, the technical novelty appears limited. The approach primarily extends Mairal et al.'s online dictionary learning (JMLR 2010) by adding a Sobolev regularization term. This incremental modification does not sufficiently advance the state of the art to meet NIPS' typical bar for originality.  

Second, the motivation for Sobolev regularization requires clarification. The paper claims this term enforces "structure" in the dictionary, yet the precise meaning of "structure" remains ambiguous. If "structure" refers to sparsity (a common dictionary property), the justification for why Sobolev regularization—typically promoting smoothness rather than sparsity—improves performance is unclear. The relationship between the regularization term and the claimed structural benefit is not adequately explained, weakening the core rationale.  

Third, Equation (1) likely contains a notational error. The current form appears to reference a vector of matrices; it should instead be expressed as $[V^1, V^2, \dots, V^r]^\top$ (with transposed block vectors) to align with standard matrix notation for concatenated features.  

Fourth, the design choice in Equation (2) needs stronger justification. Specifically, the use of an $\ell_2$-norm penalty on the code vectors (as opposed to the $\ell_1$-norm used in Mairal et al.) is not explained. Why does the $\ell_2$-norm better suit this structured learning task? A comparative analysis or theoretical argument would be necessary to support this deviation from the foundational work.  

Fifth, Line 146 references an undefined element ("the matrix $\mathbf{B}$" is mentioned without prior introduction). This creates confusion for readers; the notation should be clearly defined at first use.  

Finally, the manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors and typos that undermine readability. For example, inconsistent use of "dictionary learning" vs. "dictionary" (e.g., "the sparseness of dictionary itself" should be "sparsity in the dictionary"), and awkward phrasing like "fails to cohere with the NIPS standard" (better: "falls short of NIPS' typical standards"). These issues require thorough editing before publication.  

In summary, while the problem domain is pertinent, the paper's incremental contribution, unclear technical motivations, and presentation flaws significantly diminish its impact. The Sobolev regularization's purpose and implementation require substantial clarification, and the manuscript demands careful revision to address grammatical issues and strengthen its theoretical foundation. Without these improvements, the work does not justify acceptance at NIPS.
