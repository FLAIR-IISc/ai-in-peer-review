Summary:
The paper introduces a new regularization term for matrix factorization designed to promote both sparsity and smoothness. An algorithm is proposed to optimize the resulting objective, and experiments indicate superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

Strengths:
- The introduction of a novel regularizer that simultaneously enforces sparsity and smoothness is a clear contribution. This is a meaningful addition to the toolbox for structured matrix factorization and appears well-motivated by practical needs.
- Empirical results show promising improvements over competitive baselines, suggesting the approach has strong potential.
- The algorithm is clearly described at a high level, and the experimental evidence supports its practical utility.

Weaknesses:
- The optimization problem is non-convex, and the paper does not establish the existence of a solution (e.g., by showing coercivity, compactness of feasible sets, or other sufficient conditions). This omission weakens the theoretical foundation.
- No convergence guarantees are provided for the proposed algorithm (e.g., convergence to a stationary point). The method is primarily justified empirically.
- Some parts of the writing require significant polishing for clarity and precision. For example, there are phrasing/typo issues (e.g., around line 146) that hinder readability.

Comments and suggestions:
- Provide theoretical support for the existence of a minimizer, even under mild assumptions (e.g., boundedness and lower semicontinuity of the objective, coercivity via the new regularizer). If existence cannot be guaranteed in full generality, state conditions under which it holds.
- Include at least a basic convergence analysis of the algorithm, such as monotonic decrease of the objective and convergence to a critical point under standard assumptions. If formal proofs are infeasible, discuss known results for related algorithms and how they may apply here.
- Clarify the intuition behind how the regularizer balances sparsity and smoothness, and discuss its effect on the factor matrices. A short ablation study isolating the contribution of each component of the regularizer would strengthen the empirical case.
- Report sensitivity to key hyperparameters and initialization. Non-convex problems can be sensitive to starting points; discussing practical strategies (e.g., multiple restarts, warm starts) would be helpful.
- Improve presentation: tighten notation, fix typographical errors, and ensure that all symbols are defined upon first use. Specific unclear sentences (e.g., around line 146) should be revised.
- If possible, include computational complexity per iteration and practical runtime comparisons to baselines.

Clarity and presentation:
While the paper is generally understandable, it would benefit from careful editing to improve grammar, remove typos, and streamline explanations. The technical exposition around the new regularizer and its properties should be made more precise.

Overall assessment:
This work presents a novel and practically useful regularizer for matrix factorization, with encouraging empirical results. However, the lack of theoretical guarantees (existence of solutions and algorithmic convergence) and the current presentation issues detract from the overall strength. Addressing these points would significantly improve the paper’s impact and credibility.
