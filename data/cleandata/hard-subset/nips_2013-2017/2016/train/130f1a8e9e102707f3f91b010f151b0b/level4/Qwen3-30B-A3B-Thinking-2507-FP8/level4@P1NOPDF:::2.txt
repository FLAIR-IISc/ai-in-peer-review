

This paper introduces an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals, extending a well-established framework [14] with a Sobolev prior to enforce spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply their method to empirical data, analyze parameter sensitivity, and benchmark against two alternative approaches.  

**Technical Quality:** I rate this as a 3 (Accept). The model is clearly defined, and the algorithm builds solidly on [14]. However, the paper initially failed to convincingly demonstrate the empirical advantage of the Sobolev prior. Specifically, Figure 2 does not clarify *how* SSMF outperforms the alternatives—e.g., whether spatial boundaries are quantitatively smoother, or if SSMF identifies known anatomically connected regions missed by other methods. Merely stating results were "more structured" is insufficient. The authors should explicitly quantify improvements using neuroscience-justified criteria (e.g., boundary smoothness metrics, regional connectivity coherence). *After reviewing the rebuttal*, the added results are informative and justify the technical contribution. I have revised my rating from 2 to 3. The authors must include these results in the final version and provide quantitative evidence of increased structural coherence and neuroscientific meaningfulness.  

**Novelty/Originality:** The core novelty lies in extending [14] with Sobolev priors to enforce spatial structure in dictionary atoms—a significant contribution to the field.  

**Potential Impact:** Dictionary learning is valuable for group-level neuroimaging analysis, and spatial priors like the Sobolev constraint should theoretically improve interpretability. This efficient online algorithm offers a useful tool for neuroscience and medical imaging. The authors could further enhance impact by exploring applications in image segmentation or regional sensing.  

**Presentation:** The paper’s organization is acceptable but suffers from poor editing. Below are specific issues requiring correction:  
- **Equation (1) (Lines 67–68):** The transpose symbol on $V^j$ (for $j=1,\dots,r$) likely applies to each $V^j$, not the group.  
- **Line 73:** Missing reference to the HCP dataset.  
- **Line 83:** "can imposes" → "can impose."  
- **Line 146:** The bolded sentence appears to be an unremoved co-author comment.  
- **Equations (6) & (8):** The sign in the last term conflicts: $V^j + A_t[j,j]^{-1}(V A_t^j - B_t^j)$ (Eq. 6) vs. $A_t[j,j] V^j - (V A_t^j - B_t^j)$ (Eq. 8). Additionally, in Eq. (6), $V$ likely denotes the dictionary from the *final* iteration (not the $j$-th column variable).  
- **Line 157:** "a single, a single" → "a single."  
- **Line 169:** "We did an extensive study of th how" → "We conducted an extensive study of the..."  
- **Section 3.3 (near Line 147):** Explicitly provide the solution to Equation (6), even in the appendix.  
- **Figure 2:** Results for different methods are shown at inconsistent slices (horizontal/sagittal), hindering direct comparison. The claim that SSMF yields "more structured" results (Line 222) lacks visual or quantitative support.  
- **Figure 3:** The prediction target is ambiguous—was brain data regressed against behavioral metrics using $U_i$? Legends are unexplained in the text or caption. The figure does not display the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption, and it is unclear which curves correspond to $R^2 > 0.1$ (Lines 224–227).  

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound extension with clear potential impact. Addressing the presentation issues and providing the requested quantitative comparisons will significantly strengthen the contribution. The rebuttal’s additional results are promising, but the final version must integrate them and clarify all ambiguities.
