### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries from neuroimaging data. The method, named Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), extends the well-established online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty. This penalty encourages the learned dictionary atoms to be spatially smooth and contiguous, making them more suitable for identifying structured brain regions of interest. The authors apply their method to a large-scale task fMRI dataset, evaluate the effect of hyper-parameters, and compare its performance against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical Independent Component Analysis (CanICA), a widely used technique in the field.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution is the novel and well-motivated extension of the online dictionary learning framework to include spatial structure. Standard sparse methods often produce scattered, less interpretable components from noisy neuroimaging data. Enforcing smoothness with a Laplacian prior is a theoretically sound approach to address this limitation. Given the growing scale of neuroimaging datasets, an efficient online algorithm for learning structured brain atlases could become a valuable tool for the neuroscience and medical imaging communities.

2.  **Technical Approach:** The algorithmic formulation is clear and builds upon a solid, established foundation. Crucially, the paper demonstrates that this additional structural constraint can be integrated into the online optimization scheme with only a modest increase in computational cost (a factor of 2-3), thereby preserving the scalability that makes the original algorithm so powerful for large datasets.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the method is technically sound, the manuscript's empirical validation and clarity could be significantly improved.

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Demonstration of the Core Contribution:** The central claim is that the Sobolev prior leads to more "structured" and interpretable atoms. However, the results presented in the initial submission do not sufficiently substantiate this claim.
    *   The qualitative comparison in Figure 2a is difficult to assess, as the improvements are subtle and not immediately obvious. The claim that Smooth-SODL results are "more spatially structured and less noisy" needs stronger, more objective support.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by including quantitative metrics to support this claim. For instance, the authors could report metrics of spatial compactness, contiguity, or smoothness for the resulting dictionary atoms and show a measurable improvement over the baselines. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to connect the identified regions to established neuroscientific knowledge (e.g., known functional networks), demonstrating that the learned components are not only more structured but also more neuroscientifically meaningful.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper appears to be a draft in need of careful editing. Several confusing descriptions, typos, and inconsistencies detract from its readability and reproducibility.
    *   **Equations:** There appears to be an inconsistency in the dictionary update rule. The expression in Equation (7) does not seem to match the one described in Algorithm 2 (line 4). Please clarify the correct formulation and ensure all equations are consistent.
    *   **Algorithmic Details:** While the paper mentions that the subproblem in Equation (8) can be solved efficiently using methods like FISTA, providing the explicit solution or a more detailed algorithmic description (perhaps in an appendix) would improve the paper's self-containedness for readers seeking to implement the method.
    *   **Figures:** The visualization in Figure 2a makes a direct comparison between methods difficult because different anatomical slices are shown for each method. Presenting the results on the same slices would allow for a more effective visual comparison. The caption for Figure 2c (behavioral prediction) is unclear; it should explicitly state what variables are being used as predictors (presumably the subject-specific codes $U_i$) and what is being predicted. The legends are not explained, and the PCA baseline mentioned is not shown.
    *   **Typos:** The manuscript contains several typos that should be corrected through careful proofreading (e.g., "The Graph-Net penalty can imposes...", "a single, a single", "We did an extensive study of th how"). The reference for the HCP dataset is missing on line 73.

**Update Following Author Rebuttal:**

The authors provided additional results in their rebuttal (now included as Table 1) that compellingly demonstrate the advantage of spatial regularization, particularly in low-data regimes. The learning curves show a 14-fold gain in explained variance for Smooth-SODL over SODL when trained on a small number of subjects. This is a very strong result that effectively addresses my main concern about the lack of empirical validation for the proposed regularization.

Based on this new information, I have raised my rating. I strongly encourage the authors to incorporate these findings into the main paper and address the presentation issues listed above in their revision. Doing so would turn this into a solid contribution to the field.
