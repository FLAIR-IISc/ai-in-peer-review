- Proposes Smooth-SODL: adds a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to online sparse dictionary learning to produce spatially smooth, compact dictionary atoms.  
- Uses L1 for sparsity and Laplacian for contiguous "blob-like" components; online algorithm scales to very large neuroimaging datasets.  
- Evaluated on HCP fMRI against SODL and CanICA on interpretability, explained variance, stability, and behavioral prediction.  

Strengths:
- Tackles an important problem: principled, data-driven ROI discovery in population neuroimaging.  
- Technically sound and scalable: non-trivial extension with modest extra computational cost.  
- Thorough experimental setup using a large public dataset and appropriate baselines.  

Weaknesses:
- Evaluation is subjective and potentially circular: smoother maps are an expected outcome of the penalty and may be only aesthetic.  
- Experimental setting mismatch: spatial regularization argued to help in low-data regimes but tested mainly on the data-rich HCP, risking enforced rather than discovered structure.  
- Validation does not clearly show superiority: behavioral prediction often favors CanICA and Smooth-SODL rarely outperforms baselines on predictive utility.  

Suggestions:
- Report quantitative "structure" metrics (e.g., spatial sparsity, component size, contiguity) to move beyond visual assessment.  
- Validate inter-subject variability more directly (group separation, stronger trait correlations, alternative downstream tasks).  
- Clarify and compare model-based smoothing versus pre-smoothing (as done for CanICA).  
- Fix minor typos and improve figure label readability.  

Overall:
- An elegant, scalable approach with unclear practical impact due to subjective evaluation and insufficient objective evidence of benefits over simpler baselines.
