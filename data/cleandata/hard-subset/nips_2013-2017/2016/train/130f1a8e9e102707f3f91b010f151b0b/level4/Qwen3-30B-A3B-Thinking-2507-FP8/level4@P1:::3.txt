

The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning that incorporates a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial smoothness in dictionary atoms. This builds on Mairal et al.'s (2010) Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (SODL) framework, adding structured sparsity to produce more interpretable brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors claim their method maintains the scalability of online learning while producing spatially structured components that better capture inter-subject variability across small, medium, and large datasets.

The paper's strengths include a clear technical contribution that extends an important line of work in dictionary learning, a well-motivated problem statement regarding the limitations of standard region-of-interest (ROI) approaches in neuroimaging, and thorough experimental evaluation across multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, prediction performance). The authors demonstrate their method's scalability by showing it runs in approximately three times the time of SODL (340s vs. 110s for the same dataset), which is a reasonable trade-off for improved spatial structure. The paper also provides a detailed analysis of hyperparameter selection and convergence properties.

However, the paper's main weakness lies in its evaluation methodology. The primary claim that Smooth-SODL better captures inter-subject variability is largely supported by visual inspection of brain maps (Figure 2), which is highly subjective and difficult to assess for non-experts. While the authors include quantitative metrics like explained variance and prediction performance for behavioral variables, these do not convincingly establish the claimed advantage in capturing inter-subject variability.

The authors acknowledge that with abundant data (like the HCP dataset), standard dictionary learning might perform well asymptotically, but they fail to convincingly demonstrate when their spatial regularization provides a meaningful advantage. The learning curve analysis in Table 1 shows Smooth-SODL has a 14-fold performance gain over SODL at very small sample sizes (17 subjects), but this is not the typical use case for the Human Connectome Project data (500 subjects). For the full dataset, the performance gap between Smooth-SODL and SODL narrows substantially, as shown in Figure 2(b), which undermines the paper's main claim that their method is particularly beneficial "across small, medium, and large-scale regimes alike."

The paper also does not sufficiently address how their method improves upon previous structured dictionary learning approaches for neuroimaging, such as those by Varoquaux et al. (2013) or Abraham et al. (MICCAI 2014), which also use spatial regularization. The comparison with CanICA is somewhat superficial, as CanICA is an ICA-based method that doesn't enforce sparsity, making it less comparable to the authors' sparse dictionary learning approach.

The significance of this work is limited by the lack of clear evidence that the proposed spatial regularization provides meaningful benefits for the specific neuroimaging problem they're addressing. While the visual quality of the maps is improved, the paper doesn't convincingly show that these more structured maps lead to better scientific insights or more accurate interpretations of brain function.

In summary, the paper makes a technically sound extension to dictionary learning that is well-motivated and scalable, but the evidence supporting its primary claim is weak. The evaluation is overly reliant on subjective visual inspection without sufficient quantitative validation of the claimed advantages for the specific application to neuroimaging data.

Pros:
- Clear extension of online dictionary learning with meaningful spatial regularization
- Good scalability demonstration (3x runtime increase for structured model)
- Thorough experimental comparison with multiple baselines
- Well-organized presentation of the technical approach
- Comprehensive analysis of hyperparameter selection

Cons:
- Evaluation primarily based on subjective visual inspection of brain maps
- Insufficient evidence that spatial regularization provides meaningful benefits with large datasets (HCP)
- Lack of clear demonstration that the method better captures inter-subject variability as claimed
- Superficial comparison with relevant prior work in structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging
- Inconsistent emphasis on the claimed benefits across different data regimes

While the technical contribution is sound, the paper fails to provide convincing evidence that the proposed method offers significant advantages over existing approaches for the specific problem context. The evaluation methodology does not adequately support the main claims of the paper, particularly regarding the benefits for large-scale neuroimaging data. I recommend rejection.
