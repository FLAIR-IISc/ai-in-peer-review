The authors propose a multivariate online dictionary-learning method incorporating a Sobolev prior to obtain decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. This work builds upon the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by adding a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially smooth and compact dictionary atoms, effectively representing “blobs.” The motivation stems from the observation that inter-subject variability in neuroimaging data, often discarded as noise, can contain valuable structural information. The proposed method aims to delineate brain structures from functional images and, crucially, scales to large datasets due to its online nature.

The paper is well-structured and clearly introduces the problem of defining regions of interest (ROIs) in neuroimaging, relating it to existing challenges in statistical analysis and the limitations of univariate models and traditional segmentation techniques. The authors appropriately position their contribution in relation to clustering approaches (e.g., [21, 14]) and dimensionality reduction techniques like PCA and ICA [5], highlighting the novelty of combining sparsity and spatial smoothness constraints. The mathematical formulation of the Smooth-SODL model is clearly presented, and the algorithmic details, including the BCD scheme and FISTA implementation, are adequately described. 

A key strength of this work is its scalability, inheriting the online learning capability of Mairal et al. (2010). The authors demonstrate this through experiments on the large-scale HCP dataset. However, assessing the significance of the improvements is challenging. The primary results rely heavily on visual comparison of dictionary atoms (Fig. 2a), which lacks quantitative rigor and may be difficult to interpret for those unfamiliar with neuroimaging data. While the authors present quantitative metrics (explained variance, reproducibility, prediction of behavioral variables), the benefits of the spatial regularization are not always clear-cut, especially with abundant data like the HCP dataset. The ablation study showing improved performance with limited data (Table 1) is compelling, demonstrating the value of the spatial prior when data is scarce.

Several weaknesses should be addressed. The comparison to CanICA [22] seems somewhat unfair, as the latter requires pre-smoothing and does not explicitly enforce sparsity. A more direct comparison, perhaps with a spatially regularized ICA variant, would be more informative. The discussion of hyperparameter tuning could be expanded, providing more practical guidance for users. While the authors mention a data-adaptive choice for α (Eq. 10), the rationale and limitations are not fully explored. Furthermore, the connection between the learned dictionary atoms and known neuroanatomical structures could be strengthened through more detailed anatomical labeling and comparison to existing atlases. The writing quality is generally good, but could be improved by more concise phrasing and reducing redundancy.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging: principled ROI definition and leveraging inter-subject variability.
*   Presents a technically sound extension of existing online dictionary learning methods.
*   Demonstrates scalability on a large, publicly available dataset (HCP).
*   Shows improved performance in low-data regimes (Table 1), suggesting a benefit in scenarios where data is limited.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The primary results rely on subjective visual assessment.
*   The quantitative benefits of spatial regularization are not always compelling, particularly with abundant data.
*   The comparison to existing methods could be more thorough and fair.
*   The connection to known neuroanatomical structures could be further strengthened.



Overall, this paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field. While the significance of the improvements is not definitively established, the proposed method is technically sound, scalable, and addresses an important problem. With revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, this paper could be a valuable addition to NIPS.
