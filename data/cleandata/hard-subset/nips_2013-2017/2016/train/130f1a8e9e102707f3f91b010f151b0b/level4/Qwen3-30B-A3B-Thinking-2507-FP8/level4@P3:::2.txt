

This paper proposes an extension to online dictionary learning for neuroimaging data analysis, introducing a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage spatially structured, piece-wise smooth dictionary atoms. The method, termed Smooth-SODL, builds upon Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework [16] while adding spatial structure constraints to better capture brain organization. The authors demonstrate their approach on Human Connectome Project (HCP) task fMRI data, comparing against SODL [16] and CanICA [22].

The paper makes a technically sound contribution that extends the online dictionary learning framework to incorporate spatial structure. The formulation (2) properly combines sparsity-inducing ℓ₁-norm constraints with Laplacian-based spatial regularization, resulting in a model that produces more interpretable brain maps. The algorithmic implementation (Algorithms 1-2) follows established BCD approaches with minimal modifications to accommodate the Laplacian penalty. The authors provide appropriate convergence guarantees and computational complexity analysis, noting that their method runs approximately 3× slower than SODL while being significantly faster than CanICA. The theoretical grounding in the GraphNet framework [11,13] and the connection to sparse dictionary learning literature is well-established.

The experimental evaluation demonstrates that Smooth-SODL produces more spatially structured dictionary atoms compared to SODL (which produces atoms with "thick rings of very small nonzero values") and CanICA (which produces dense, uninterpretable maps requiring post-hoc hard-thresholding). The authors provide several quantitative metrics: explained variance, stability (measured by mean correlation between corresponding atoms across data splits), and predictive performance for behavioral variables. The key finding is that Smooth-SODL achieves better stability (0.4 vs. 0.1 for SODL and 0.2 for thresholded CanICA) while maintaining competitive predictive performance. The learning curve analysis (Table 1) is particularly compelling, showing that Smooth-SODL achieves 31% explained variance on test data with only 17 subjects, while SODL achieves only 2%, demonstrating the advantage of spatial regularization in small-data regimes.

The paper's novelty lies in successfully integrating spatial structure into the online dictionary learning framework without sacrificing scalability. While structured dictionary learning has been explored in [1,25], these approaches rely on slow alternating optimization. Smooth-SODL adapts the efficient online framework [16] to handle spatial structure with only a modest computational cost. The authors properly acknowledge related work in [1,25] and clarify their contribution as a scalable implementation of structured dictionary learning.

The significance of this work is substantial for the neuroscience community, as it provides a principled approach to defining brain regions that better capture inter-subject variability. The method's efficiency makes it suitable for large-scale datasets like HCP, where traditional methods become computationally prohibitive. The authors' implementation as part of Nilearn [2] further enhances the potential impact of their contribution.

The presentation could be improved in several ways. The manuscript contains several grammatical errors and unclear descriptions that hinder comprehension. For example, the matrix decomposition equation (1) has inconsistent transpose notation, and the description of Figure 2 lacks clear comparative metrics for "structured" atoms. The authors should explicitly define quantitative measures of spatial structure (e.g., compactness, smoothness metrics) that would be meaningful to neuroscientists. The comparison between methods in Figure 2 would benefit from consistent anatomical slices for direct visual comparison. The results in Figure 3 (predicting behavioral variables) require clearer explanation of the prediction task and the role of the learned dictionary.

The paper would be strengthened by including specific neuroscience-based validation of the learned regions. For instance, the authors could demonstrate that Smooth-SODL identifies regions known to be functionally connected in the literature, or that the atoms correspond to established brain networks (e.g., default mode network, executive control network). The authors' claim that their method produces "more structured" atoms needs to be substantiated with quantitative metrics that neuroscience researchers would find meaningful.

The technical quality of the paper is strong, with a well-motivated problem, sound theoretical foundation, and thorough experimental evaluation. The main weaknesses are presentation issues and insufficient justification for the claimed interpretability advantages. The authors have provided some additional results in the rebuttal that address these concerns, suggesting that the paper could be significantly improved with careful revision.

In summary, this is a technically sound contribution with clear novelty and significant potential impact for neuroimaging analysis. The method provides a scalable solution to a problem of growing importance in large-scale brain imaging studies. With improved presentation and more specific validation against neuroscience knowledge, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the presentation issues and provide more concrete evidence for the claimed advantages of the structured dictionary atoms.
