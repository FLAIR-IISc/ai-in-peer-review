The paper extends dictionary learning by introducing a Sobolev prior to promote spatial smoothness and consistency. While the problem is interesting, it is difficult to assess the impact and practical improvement of the proposed method based on the current evaluation.

The central results hinge on visual inspection of spatial “blobs” on brain maps. These comparisons are not easily interpretable to readers without specific domain expertise, and even for trained readers they remain largely subjective. Moreover, given that the method explicitly penalizes roughness and enforces spatial coherence, it is unsurprising that the resulting components appear smoother. This raises the question of whether the observed improvements reflect genuine advances in representation quality or simply mirror the prior.

In my experience with this class of decomposition methods, data quality and quantity are at least as critical as the choice of algorithm. The proposed approach may be particularly valuable in low-data regimes by stabilizing estimates, but with the extensive datasets available from the Human Connectome Project, data scarcity is less of a concern. The manuscript would benefit from a clearer articulation of the settings in which the Sobolev prior materially improves outcomes over existing approaches when data are plentiful.

I was also hoping to see a more thorough analysis of how the method consolidates or distinguishes brain regions across subjects. Using inter-subject consistency (and differences) as a validation axis could provide a more objective assessment of the method’s utility. As presented, the validation feels subjective, and the practical benefits over standard dictionary learning or other spatially regularized baselines are not clearly demonstrated.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:
- Providing quantitative metrics beyond visual inspection (e.g., reproducibility across splits/subjects, stability of components, overlap with known anatomical/functional atlases, or performance on downstream tasks).
- Comparing against strong baselines with explicit spatial regularization, including ablations isolating the contribution of the Sobolev term.
- Evaluating performance across data regimes (limited vs. abundant data) to test the hypothesis that the prior is most helpful when data are scarce.
- Reporting inter-subject variability and consistency to assess whether the method yields more robust, biologically meaningful components.

Overall, the approach is promising and the problem is worthwhile, but the current evidence does not convincingly establish a clear advantage or impact. More rigorous and objective validation would make the contribution significantly stronger.
