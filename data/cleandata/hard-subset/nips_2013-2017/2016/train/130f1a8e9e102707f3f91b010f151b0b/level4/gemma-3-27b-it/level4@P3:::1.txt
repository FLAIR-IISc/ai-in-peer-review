This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images into structured and sparse components, aiming to delineate brain regions. The core idea is to combine sparsity constraints (using an L1 norm) with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce smoothness and compactness in the learned dictionary atoms, effectively representing “blobs” corresponding to brain structures. This builds upon prior work in online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) and addresses the challenge of inter-subject variability in neuroimaging data. The authors demonstrate the method on a large-scale fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project.

The motivation for this work is well-articulated, stemming from the limitations of traditional univariate models and the challenges of defining Regions of Interest (ROIs) in neuroimaging. The proposed approach offers a data-driven alternative to manual ROI definition, potentially leveraging information contained in inter-subject variability that is often discarded. The connection to existing techniques like clustering, PCA, and ICA is clearly explained, and the paper positions the contribution as a model closer to signal recovery with built-in spatial constraints.  Related work, including methods for structured sparse decomposition (e.g., Baldassarre et al., 2014) and online learning (e.g., Mairal et al., 2010; Mensch, 2014) is appropriately referenced. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Combination:** The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for enforcing structural priors is a novel approach, addressing a limitation of existing methods.
* **Scalability:** Leveraging the online learning framework allows the method to scale to large datasets, crucial for neuroimaging applications.
* **Promising Results:** The preliminary experiments on HCP data suggest the method can extract interpretable and informative dictionaries, capturing inter-subject variability effectively. The reported gains in explained variance and prediction performance compared to standard SODL are encouraging.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and the motivation for the proposed solution within the context of neuroimaging.
* **Thorough Experimentation:** The comparison against CanICA and SODL, with analysis of running time, qualitative assessment, and predictive power, provides a relatively comprehensive evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed method. While the authors mention convergence guarantees based on existing work on online dictionary learning, a deeper analysis of the impact of the Laplacian penalty on the convergence properties or the quality of the learned dictionary would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning highlights the sensitivity of the method to parameters like α, γ, and τ. While the authors provide some guidance, a more systematic analysis of the parameter space and its impact on performance is needed.  A more principled way to select parameters beyond cross-validation would strengthen the work.
* **Clarity and Polish:** While generally well-written, the paper suffers from some instances of awkward phrasing and minor inconsistencies. The mention of comments remaining in the draft ("146 XXX: I don't understand...") is concerning and indicates a lack of careful revision.  The mathematical notation, while largely correct, could be improved for readability (e.g., consistently defining variables).
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to other structured sparse decomposition methods (e.g., those utilizing Total Variation regularization, as referenced) is missing. Including such comparisons would further strengthen the claims of novelty and improvement.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The method presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to brain region extraction.
* The scalability of the algorithm is a significant advantage for large neuroimaging datasets.
* The preliminary experimental results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the method.
* The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging with a well-motivated and clearly explained approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of theoretical analysis weakens the scientific rigor of the work.
* The paper's clarity and polish need improvement.
* The limited comparison to other relevant methods leaves open the question of whether the proposed approach represents a significant advance over existing techniques.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field, but currently falls short of acceptance in its present form. The core idea is strong, and the experimental results are encouraging, but the lack of theoretical analysis and some issues with clarity and polish are significant concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

**Weak Reject**. I recommend rejection with encouragement for the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the inclusion of theoretical analysis and a more thorough comparison to related methods.  A careful revision addressing clarity and consistency is also essential. If these issues are addressed, I would be happy to reconsider the paper.
