- Proposes Smooth-SODL: online dictionary learning for neuroimaging with sparse atoms and a Laplacian/Sobolev smoothness penalty.
- Codes estimated by ridge regression; atoms updated by block coordinate descent with FISTA for the Laplacian and projection onto a sparsity constraint.
- Shown on 500 HCP subjects (p ≈ 2.6×10^5, k=40) vs SODL and CanICA: yields smoother, less noisy, more reproducible atoms; competitive EV/prediction; runtime ~3× SODL and faster than CanICA.

Strengths
- Clear practical motivation: produce spatially coherent, interpretable regions (blobs) capturing inter-subject variability better than dense ICA.
- Sound algorithmic design: BCD for online updates, ridge-coded minibatches, FISTA for Laplacian regularization; inherits convergence properties from Mairal et al.
- Demonstrates scalability on large, high-dimensional data with reasonable CPU times.
- Empirical evaluation covers visual quality, explained variance, stability, and behavior prediction.
- Laplacian regularizer is a principled, scalable smoothness prior compared to TV-based approaches.

Weaknesses / Questions — Originality & Positioning
- Methodological novelty is incremental: essentially adds a quadratic smoothness (GraphNet-style) to Mairal et al.'s online DL; related structured-sparse work is not fully discussed.
- Need clearer statement of what is new beyond scalability and prior work comparisons.

Weaknesses / Questions — Clarity & Technical Details
- “Structure” is described informally; must precisely explain how Laplacian + sparsity produce compact, blob-like atoms and contrast with TV.
- Inconsistent dimensions/orientations around Eq.(1) and Eq.(6); notation must be fixed globally.
- Inconsistent naming “Sobolev” vs “Laplacian”; define ∆ = ∇⊤∇, boundary conditions on brain mask, positivity, and consistent scaling factors.
- BCD is not parameter-free: FISTA requires a Lipschitz constant and γ schedule; report the exact γt schedule used and sensitivity analysis.
- Choice of ridge coding (vs ℓ1 coding used by Mairal et al.) not justified or empirically compared; request experiments comparing ridge vs ℓ1 codes for stability, EV, interpretability.
- Baseline fairness concern: CanICA is run on smoothed/thresholded results while SODL variants are not—justify mismatch or rerun with matched preprocessing/thresholding.
- Ambiguity in constraint set C (ℓ1-ball vs simplex/nonnegativity); report which option is used per experiment and rationale.
- Provide computational/memory complexity per iteration, FISTA stopping criteria (you mention 1–2 iterations), number of data passes, and reproducibility across seeds.

Weaknesses / Questions — Empirical Evaluation
- CanICA often retains best EV and predictive performance; discuss the stability–fidelity trade-off and whether Smooth-SODL can be tuned to improve EV without losing interpretability.
- Ablation needed to isolate impact of ΩLap (γ=0 vs γ>0) and, if possible, a small TV-regularized comparison.
- Missing cross-validation details: fold definitions, EV metric definition, selection of γ and τ, and empirical justification for adaptive schedules (αt ∼ t−1/2, γt).

Minor issues & typos
- Numerous typographical and grammatical errors; paper needs careful proofreading.
- Spurious stray text appears in the PDF and should be removed.
- Algorithm notation inconsistent (Aj vs A[:,j], At[j,j], subscripts); clarify symbols in pseudocode.
- Missing/insufficient citations to related structured DL works (e.g., Jenatton et al. 2010; Grosenick et al. 2013; Abraham et al. 2013; Varoquaux et al. 2011/2012).

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: algorithmic choices are sound and scalable, but math presentation needs correction and additional ablations/comparisons are missing.
- Clarity: generally readable but requires copy-editing and clearer definitions.
- Originality: moderate; an incremental but useful extension enabling scalable, structured DL.
- Significance: practically useful for neuroimaging—improves interpretability and stability though EV/prediction gains are modest.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important practical need for scalable, structured region discovery in brain imaging.
- Demonstrates improvements in interpretability and stability at scale with reasonable compute.
- Simple and implementable method likely to be adopted (planned Nilearn release).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond integrating known smoothness priors into an existing online DL framework.
- Clarity, notation, and typographical issues need fixing.
- Incomplete empirical comparisons and missing ablations (ridge vs ℓ1, baseline fairness, γ ablation).

Overall assessment
- Valuable engineering/application contribution with convincing qualitative and some quantitative benefits but limited methodological novelty and presentation/experimental gaps; reviewer leans borderline.
