This paper introduces a large-scale online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images into structured, sparse components, aiming to identify meaningful brain regions. The core idea is to combine sparsity-inducing penalties (L1-norm) with a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially smooth and compact dictionary atoms – effectively “blobs” representing brain structures. This extends prior work by Mairal et al. (2010) with a modest increase in computational cost. The authors demonstrate the method on a large fMRI dataset, showing improvements in interpretability and capturing inter-subject variability compared to state-of-the-art models.

The problem addressed – defining regions of interest (ROIs) in neuroimaging data – is important. Traditional ROI definition often relies on segmentation or cross-modality correspondence, both of which present challenges. The proposed approach offers a data-driven alternative, leveraging population variability to define regions and potentially improving statistical power. The motivation for utilizing structured sparsity is also sound, as it aligns with the expectation that brain regions are spatially localized. This work builds upon existing approaches like clustering (e.g., [21, 14]) and PCA/ICA [5] but differentiates itself by combining a dictionary learning framework with explicit spatial regularization. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Combination:** The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for spatial structure is a novel contribution.
*   **Scalability:** Leveraging the online learning approach from Mairal et al. (2010) allows the method to scale to large datasets.
*   **Promising Results:** Empirical results on the HCP dataset demonstrate improved interpretability and performance compared to alternatives, particularly regarding capturing inter-subject variability.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper includes a discussion of hyperparameter tuning and implementation details, addressing practical aspects of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough theoretical analysis justifying the effectiveness of the proposed method or comparing it to existing approaches. While the authors mention convergence guarantees based on prior work [16], a more detailed analysis of the impact of the Laplacian penalty would be beneficial.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from issues in clarity and presentation. The presence of internal comments (“146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v.”) suggests a lack of careful review before submission. While the method is explained, the notation can be dense, and some explanations could be more accessible.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to other methods, while present, is not exhaustive. A more comprehensive evaluation against related work in structured sparsity for neuroimaging (e.g., [1, 3, 25]) would strengthen the claims.
*   **Overstated Claims:** Some statements, such as the claim of “14-fold gain” in explained variance, require more careful justification and context. The timing analysis shows some improvement in initial variance explained, but doesn’t necessarily translate to a substantial overall benefit.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially valuable approach to ROI definition in neuroimaging.
*   The empirical results demonstrate promising performance on a large, publicly available dataset.
*   The online learning framework addresses a key challenge in analyzing large-scale neuroimaging data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of theoretical analysis weakens the justification for the proposed method.
*   The clarity and presentation issues detract from the overall quality of the paper.
*   The comparison to related work could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper has the potential to contribute meaningfully to the field. However, the current manuscript requires significant revisions to address the weaknesses in clarity, theoretical justification, and comparison to related work. I recommend a **weak reject** with the suggestion that the authors address these issues and resubmit. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis:** Justify the choice of the Laplacian penalty and its impact on the resulting decomposition.
2.  **Improve clarity and presentation:** Carefully review the manuscript for clarity, removing internal comments and ensuring consistent notation.
3.  **Expand the related work section:** Discuss and compare the proposed method to more recent and relevant approaches in structured sparsity for neuroimaging.
4.  **Refine claims:** Provide more nuanced interpretations of the empirical results and avoid overstated claims.
