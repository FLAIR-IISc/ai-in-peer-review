Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) to learn spatially structured, sparse components from large-scale neuroimaging data. The authors constrain dictionary atoms to be sparse via an ℓ1-type constraint (e.g., ℓ1-ball or simplex) and promote spatial coherence via a 3D Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty, encouraging piecewise-smooth, blob-like maps. Codes are estimated per-sample with a ridge (or optionally ℓ1) penalty. The optimization is cast as an online block-coordinate descent (BCD): for each mini-batch, codes are updated by ridge regression and atoms are updated sequentially by solving a compactly constrained GraphNet-like denoising problem with FISTA steps. The method scales similarly to standard SODL, with ~2–3× overhead from the Laplacian term.

The work is positioned against: (i) unstructured online sparse coding (Mairal et al., 2010; Mensch et al., 2016), which scales well but yields scattered, less interpretable atoms; and (ii) spatially structured decompositions using total-variation/structured sparsity (e.g., TV–ℓ1, GraphNet; Abraham et al., 2013; Jenatton et al., 2010; Grosenick et al., 2013), which are typically solved by slow alternating minimization and do not readily scale online. The paper also compares to multi-subject PCA/ICA approaches (e.g., CanICA; Varoquaux et al., 2010), which optimize explained variance but lack explicit spatial sparsity and typically require smoothing and thresholding for interpretability.

On 500-subject HCP task fMRI maps (p≈2.6×10^5 voxels, k=40), the method produces spatially coherent, denoised, and more reproducible atoms than standard SODL, achieves competitive explained variance, improves early-sample predictive performance, and remains computationally practical. Hyper-parameter behavior and selection are discussed; code will be released in Nilearn.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The objective is clearly specified, with compact convex atom constraints and a smooth quadratic Laplacian penalty. The online BCD with sufficient statistics mirrors [16]; per-atom updates reduce to a strongly convex quadratic plus constraint, solvable efficiently by FISTA. The paper discusses Lipschitz constants, step sizes, and a practical schedule for γ to avoid vanishing steps, as well as αt ∝ t^−1/2 for codes. Convergence arguments are adapted from [16] (local convergence under standard assumptions), though a formal theorem specialized to the added Laplacian term and compact constraints would strengthen the paper. Empirical scaling and stability are convincing. Experimental comparisons are comprehensive (qualitative maps, explained variance on train/test, dictionary stability with Hungarian matching, behavioral prediction), though confidence intervals would help.
- Experimental support: The HCP evaluation is appropriate and large. Comparisons with SODL and CanICA are fair and align with known trade-offs (EV vs. interpretability). The early-sample learning curve is a nice addition, demonstrating the benefit of spatial structure in low-data regimes. Runtime comparisons indicate reasonable overhead.
- Reproducibility: Implementation is to be released. The paper lists hyper-parameter choices and tuning strategy (cross-validation for γ, τ), mini-batch size, and dictionary size.

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable and well organized, with useful algorithmic details. Several typos and minor issues hinder readability (e.g., “xperiments,” “intepretable,” “There is not step size,” “conference” instead of convergence; inconsistent factors in the Laplacian definition). It would help to:
- Clearly state the finite-sample surrogate minimized online (using At, Bt) before introducing the asymptotic objective.
- Summarize the exact assumptions under which convergence from [16] applies with the Laplacian term.
- Clarify the role and choice of code penalties (ridge vs. ℓ1) in experiments.
- Make explicit the projection mechanics for the chosen constraint (simplex vs. ℓ1-ball) and how nonnegativity interacts with the Laplacian.
- Provide guidance on the γ schedule and its interaction with At[j,j]/t (Eq. 9), and specify practical ranges.

Originality
Moderate to strong. While structured sparsity and GraphNet/TV regularization have been explored for neuroimaging decompositions, integrating a Laplacian prior into an online dictionary-learning framework with efficient per-atom updates appears novel and practically impactful. The work usefully bridges scalable online learning and spatially structured priors.

Significance
High practical significance for large-scale neuroimaging: the method yields more interpretable and stable atoms while maintaining scalability. It is likely to see use in data-driven ROI discovery and dimensionality reduction. Methodologically, the approach is a relevant addition to the online matrix factorization literature with structured atoms. Some outcomes (e.g., CanICA outperforming in behavioral prediction) temper claims, but the interpretability/stability gains are valuable.

Strengths
- Scalable algorithm that integrates spatial smoothness and sparsity with modest overhead.
- Clear per-atom update as a constrained GraphNet denoising step; practical FISTA implementation.
- Strong empirical evidence: improved interpretability and stability; competitive EV; early-sample advantage.
- Comprehensive comparisons and useful practical guidance on hyper-parameters.
- Relevance and likely impact for neuroimaging; planned open-source release.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Theory: Provide a succinct theorem (and assumptions) ensuring existence of a minimizer under compact constraints and convergence of the online BCD to a stationary point with the Laplacian term. Clarify how assumptions in [16] are satisfied here.
- Parameterization: Further justify the γ and α schedules; report sensitivity analyses with confidence intervals. Consider reporting results with ℓ1-coded variants for completeness.
- Evaluation: Include variability estimates (e.g., across splits/seeds) for EV, stability, and prediction. Discuss the effect of smoothing choices on CanICA and, if possible, test an unsmoothed variant or matched pre-processing across methods.
- Presentation: Fix typos and improve notation consistency (e.g., factors in ΩLap, constraints, indices). Briefly elaborate on the optional FFT-based update mentioned in the supplement.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and practically important integration of spatial structure into online dictionary learning.
- Solid empirical gains in interpretability/stability with competitive accuracy and good scaling.
- Addresses a real need in large-scale neuroimaging; likely to be adopted (Nilearn integration).

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited formal analysis specific to the new regularizer beyond references to [16].
- Some experimental metrics lack uncertainty estimates; behavioral prediction gains are mixed.
- Minor clarity issues and typos in the current draft.

After rebuttal
The authors’ clarification on algorithmic convergence addressed my main concern; I encourage including an explicit statement of assumptions and a brief theorem in the final version. I revise my Clarity/Presentation assessment from 2 to 3.
