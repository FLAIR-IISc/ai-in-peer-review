This paper presents a method for learning sparse and spatially structured components from large-scale brain imaging data. The core idea is to extend the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages the learned components, or "atoms," to be piece-wise smooth and compact, resembling functional regions of interest (ROIs). The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to massive datasets, such as the Human Connectome Project (HCP) data used in the experiments. The authors demonstrate that their proposed method, "Smooth-SODL," produces more interpretable and stable results than standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and canonical ICA, particularly in low-data regimes, while incurring a modest computational overhead.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The problem of defining functional ROIs from neuroimaging data in a principled, data-driven manner is highly relevant. The proposed approach offers a scalable solution to a difficult and important problem in the field.
*   **Scalability:** A key strength is the algorithm's scalability. By building on an online learning framework, the method can handle datasets that are too large for batch methods to process, which is crucial for modern large-scale neuroimaging initiatives.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the proposed method against relevant baselines (SODL, CanICA) on real-world fMRI data. The results convincingly show that the addition of the Laplacian penalty leads to dictionary atoms that are more interpretable (spatially structured), more stable across data splits, and better at capturing inter-subject variability, especially when limited data is available. The analysis of the performance trade-offs (e.g., explained variance vs. stability) is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Originality:** The primary technical contribution is the integration of a Laplacian penalty into the established online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010). While the application is novel and the engineering is non-trivial, the conceptual advance over prior work on structured sparse coding could be further clarified. The authors should more explicitly position their contribution with respect to other structured penalties used in neuroimaging, such as the Total Variation penalty in [1].
*   **Clarity of Motivation:** The motivation for specific modeling choices could be strengthened.
    *   The paper states that the Laplacian penalty imposes "structure," defined as piece-wise smooth and compact blobs. A more detailed justification for why this specific form of regularization is neuro-anatomically preferable to others would be beneficial.
    *   The authors opt for an L2 (Ridge) penalty on the codes, departing from the L1 penalty in the reference work by Mairal et al. (2010), with the brief justification that it leads to "stable codes." This choice warrants further discussion. An elaboration on the implications of using L2 versus L1 regularization in the context of this neuroimaging problem would significantly improve the paper.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The manuscript would benefit from a thorough proofreading. I noticed several typographical errors and awkward phrasings that detract from the overall clarity and professionalism of the work. For example, the description of the BCD update in Algorithm 2 and Equation (8) is dense and could be clarified for the reader.

In summary, this paper presents a well-motivated and scalable algorithm for an important problem in computational neuroimaging. The experimental results are solid and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed spatial regularization. The main weaknesses relate to the perceived incremental nature of the technical contribution and the need for clearer justification of certain modeling choices. Addressing these points and improving the overall writing quality would strengthen the paper considerably.
