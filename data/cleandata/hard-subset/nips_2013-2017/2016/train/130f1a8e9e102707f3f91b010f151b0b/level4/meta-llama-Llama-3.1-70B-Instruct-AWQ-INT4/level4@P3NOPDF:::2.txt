This paper proposes an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals, building upon a well-established framework [14] with the incorporation of a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. The authors apply their method to empirical data, investigate the effect of varying tuning parameters, and compare the performance with two alternative methods in the field.

Technical quality: I would rate this aspect marginally below 3 if given the option. The model definition is clear, and the algorithms follow a well-established framework [14], appearing solid. However, the presentation may undermine this solidity. The paper does not clearly demonstrate the empirical advantage of introducing the Sobolev prior in the results section. Specifically, in Figure 2, it is unclear in what aspects the SSMF method outperforms the two alternatives. The authors could explicitly describe criteria justified by neuroscience literature and provide quantitative measurements of the improvement according to these criteria. For instance, they could discuss whether the spatial boundary is more smooth quantitatively or if certain regions, known to be connected, are only identified by SSMF. The current description of the results as "more structured" is vague and requires further clarification.

After reading the rebuttal, I found the additional results to be informative and have changed my rating on technical quality from 2 to 3. I hope the authors include these new results in their revision and demonstrate that the atoms learned by SSMF are more structured (with quantitative measurements) and more meaningful (with reference to neuroscience literature).

Novelty/originality: This paper makes a notable original contribution by extending [14] to incorporate Sobolev priors, encouraging spatially structured dictionary atoms.

Potential impact or usefulness: Dictionary learning is a useful approach for explaining neuroimaging data across a large group of subjects. Introducing Sobolev priors or similar spatial priors should theoretically improve the results. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm for doing so, potentially adding a valuable tool to the neuroscience/medical imaging community's toolbox. The authors could also explore other applications of this method, such as image segmentation and regional sensing.

Presentation: The general organization of the paper is acceptable, but it appears to be a poorly edited manuscript. There are confusing descriptions and typos that make it harder to understand the contents. I have listed the confusing parts below, some of which seem to be easy-to-fix typos, while others may require more detailed explanations.

- In the matrix decomposition equation between lines 67 and 68, should the transpose symbol be applied to each individual $V^j$ (j = 1,..., r)?
- A reference for the HCP dataset is missing on line 73.
- Line 83 contains a grammatical error: "The Graph-Net penalty can imposes" should be corrected.
- The bold sentence on line 146 appears to be a co-author's comment that was not addressed.
- The last line of Equation (6) and Equation (8) disagree on the sign: $ V^j +At[j,j]^{-1}(VAt^j -Bt^j)$ versus $At[j,j] V^j -(VAt^j - Bt^j)$. Furthermore, in the last line of Equation (6), I assume $V$ refers to the dictionary in the last iteration, i.e., the jth column is not the variable $v$ to optimize.
- Line 157 contains a repetition: "a single, a single."
- Line 169 contains a grammatical error: "We did an extensive study of th how" should be corrected.
- In Section 3.3, it would be beneficial to explicitly provide the mathematical/algorithmic solution to Equation (6), even in the appendix.
- Figure 2 presents the results by different methods at different horizontal and sagittal slices, making it challenging to directly compare the results. Based on the figure, I do not follow the description in line 222, which claims that the results by the SSMF model are more "structured."
- Figure 3 is unclear, as it is uncertain what is being predicted. Is it regressing brain data on behavioral metrics, and if so, is it based on $U_i$'s? The legends are not explained in the main text nor the figure caption. The figure does not appear to show the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption. I do not follow the description in lines 224-227, which claims that R^2 is greater than 0.1, as it is unclear which curves to examine.

Overall, the paper has potential, but it requires significant revisions to address the technical, novelty, and presentation issues mentioned above.
