This paper presents an online algorithm for learning a structured dictionary to characterize neuroimaging data across multiple individuals. The proposed method extends the well-established framework of [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior to promote spatial contiguity in the learned dictionary atoms. The authors apply their approach to empirical data, investigate the effects of key tuning parameters, and compare its performance against two alternative methods in the field.

**Technical quality:**
My initial rating for technical quality was a 2, but after reviewing the authors' rebuttal, I have raised it to a 3. The model is defined clearly, and the algorithm, which follows the established framework of [14], seems methodologically sound. However, the paper's primary weakness, perhaps exacerbated by its presentation, was its failure to convincingly demonstrate the empirical benefits of the Sobolev prior. Specifically, in Figure 2, it was not apparent how the proposed SSMF method outperformed the alternatives. For instance, is the spatial boundary quantitatively smoother? Does SSMF uniquely identify anatomically connected regions known from neuroscientific literature? The claim that the SSMF results were "more structured" is too vague. The authors should instead define explicit, neuroscientifically-justified criteria and provide quantitative measurements to substantiate this claim.

*Added after reading the rebuttal:* The additional results provided in the rebuttal are highly informative and address some of these concerns. I strongly encourage the authors to incorporate this new analysis into the revised manuscript. This should include quantitative metrics demonstrating that the learned atoms are indeed more structured and validation from neuroscience literature confirming that they are more meaningful.

**Novelty/originality:**
The paper's primary novel contribution is significant: the extension of the framework in [14] to include Sobolev priors for enforcing spatial structure in dictionary atoms.

**Potential impact or usefulness:**
Dictionary learning is a valuable technique for analyzing large-scale neuroimaging datasets, and the inclusion of spatial priors like the Sobolev prior is theoretically well-motivated. By providing an efficient online algorithm for this purpose, this work could become a valuable tool for the neuroscience and medical imaging communities. Furthermore, the proposed method holds promise for other applications, such as image segmentation or regional sensing.

**Presentation:**
While the overall structure of the paper is acceptable, the manuscript suffers from a lack of careful editing. Numerous typos and confusing descriptions detract from its clarity and make the content difficult to follow. I have listed specific issues below, some of which are simple typos while others may require more substantial clarification.
*   In the matrix decomposition equation between Lines 67 and 68, it is unclear if the transpose symbol should apply to the entire group of $V^j$ (j = 1,..., r) or to each individual matrix $V^j$.
*   Line 73: The reference for the HCP dataset is missing.
*   Line 83: Grammatical error: "The Graph-Net penalty can imposes..."
*   Line 146: The bolded sentence appears to be an unaddressed comment from a co-author.
*   There is a sign inconsistency between the update rules in Equation (6) and Equation (8): compare $ V^j +At[j,j]^{-1}(VAt^j -Bt^j)$ with $At[j,j] V^j -(VAt^j - Bt^j)$. Additionally, in the last line of Equation (6), it should be clarified that $V$ refers to the dictionary from the previous iteration, not the optimization variable $v$.
*   Line 157: Typo: "a single, a single".
*   Line 169: Typo: "of th how".
*   In Section 3.3, in the paragraph below Algorithm 2, it would be beneficial for the authors to provide the explicit mathematical or algorithmic solution to Equation (6), perhaps in an appendix.
*   Figure 2: The use of different horizontal and sagittal slices for each method makes a direct comparison of the results difficult. The visual evidence presented does not sufficiently support the claim on Line 222 that the SSMF model's results are more "structured".
*   Figure 3: This figure is unclear. The authors should specify what is being predicted (e.g., is brain data being regressed on behavioral metrics using the coefficients $U_i$?). The figure legends are not explained in the text or caption. The PCA baseline mentioned in the caption is not visible in the plot. Finally, the claim in Lines 224-227 regarding an R^2 value greater than 0.1 is not evident from the presented curves.
