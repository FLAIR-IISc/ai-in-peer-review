The paper proposes an under-complete dictionary learning method with combined Sobolev and L1 regularization to identify functional components in fMRI data. The proposed approach is interesting, with the key novelty being the use of combined Sobolev/L1 regularization to encourage both smoothness and sparsity in the learned components. However, the reported improvements over simpler methods are not substantial, and the explanation of these improvements could be clearer. Furthermore, the manuscript feels somewhat rushed; it contains draft comments (e.g., line 146) and can be a tedious and occasionally confusing read.

The authors aim to address inter-subject variability in neuroimaging data, a challenge often treated as statistical residual. They propose using a multivariate online dictionary learning method to decompose brain images into structured and sparse components. Sparsity is enforced via an L1 norm, while structure—defined as piecewise smooth and compact “blobs”—is imposed using a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty. The method extends previous online dictionary learning work (Mairal et al., 2010) with a modest increase in running time (a factor of 2 or 3). The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to large datasets. Preliminary experiments on brain data demonstrate that the proposed method extracts more interpretable and structured dictionaries, and better captures inter-subject variability, compared to state-of-the-art models.

This work builds on a line of research employing dictionary learning for fMRI analysis.  Approaches like ICA and PCA (e.g., Beckmann & Smith, 2009) are frequently used to extract functional units, but often lack explicit sparsity or spatial constraints. The authors correctly position their work within this context, noting the limitations of purely statistical approaches and the benefits of incorporating prior knowledge about brain structure.  Related work employing spatial regularization, such as TV-L1 minimization (Baldassarre et al., 2013), is also acknowledged. The key distinction here lies in the integration of both sparsity and smoothness within an *online* dictionary learning framework, allowing for scalability to large datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Scalability:** The online learning approach is a significant advantage, enabling the method to handle large-scale neuroimaging datasets.
*   **Combined Regularization:** The combination of L1 and Sobolev penalties is a sensible approach for encouraging both sparsity and spatial coherence in the learned components.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper articulates a clear motivation for addressing inter-subject variability and the limitations of existing approaches.
*   **Comparison to Established Methods:** The authors compare their method to relevant state-of-the-art techniques, including CanICA and standard SODL.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Modest Improvements:** The reported performance gains, particularly in the experiments, are not overwhelmingly convincing. The difference between Smooth-SODL and SODL appears marginal in some metrics.
*   **Clarity and Polish:** The manuscript suffers from issues with clarity and polish. The writing is dense in places, and the presence of draft comments detracts from the professional presentation. The explanation of certain choices (e.g. parameter setting) feels incomplete.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper discusses hyperparameter tuning, the impact of different parameter settings is not fully explored, and the recommended choices feel somewhat ad hoc.
*  **Justification of Laplacian Penalty:** The connection between the Laplacian penalty and the desired "blob" structure could be explained more intuitively. While mathematically sound, the reader might benefit from a more visual or conceptual explanation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel combination of techniques (online dictionary learning with combined L1 and Sobolev regularization) addressing a relevant problem in neuroimaging.
*   The method demonstrates scalability to large datasets, a crucial requirement for neuroimaging applications.
*   The authors provide a thorough comparison to existing methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The performance improvements are not substantial enough to warrant strong acceptance.
*   The manuscript requires significant revision to improve clarity, polish, and completeness.
*   The lack of a more thorough exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity weakens the conclusions.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting contribution with potential for advancing the field of neuroimaging data analysis. However, the current manuscript requires substantial revision before it would be suitable for publication at NIPS. Specifically, the authors should focus on clarifying the writing, strengthening the justification for their methodological choices, and providing a more compelling demonstration of the method’s advantages through more rigorous experimentation and analysis.  I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form, but encourage the authors to revise and resubmit.
