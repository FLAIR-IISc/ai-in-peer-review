This paper presents a large-scale online dictionary learning framework for the application of learning brain regions from functional imaging data. The primary contribution is the incorporation of a Sobolev regularization term into the online learning framework to enforce spatial structure on the learned dictionary atoms.

While the application is interesting and the approach is plausible, there are several major concerns regarding the novelty, methodological justification, and clarity of the paper.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The core technical contribution appears to be the addition of a Sobolev regularization term to the well-established online dictionary learning algorithm of Mairal et al. (JMLR 2010). While this is a reasonable extension for the target application, the conceptual novelty is somewhat incremental for a NIPS publication. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could better articulate how this specific combination provides fundamentally new insights or overcomes a major challenge that previous methods could not address.

2.  **Unclear Motivation for Sobolev Regularization:** The rationale for using Sobolev regularization is not sufficiently justified. The authors state that its purpose is to impose a "structure" on the learned dictionary, but this term is not clearly defined. It is crucial for the authors to elaborate on the specific properties (e.g., spatial smoothness, piece-wise continuity, contiguity) that this regularizer is intended to promote. Furthermore, a more compelling argument is needed for why these particular structural properties are beneficial for the task of learning brain regions. Why is this specific regularizer the most appropriate choice over other possible structural priors (e.g., Total Variation, graph-based regularizers)? A clearer connection between the mathematical properties of the regularizer and the neuroscientific goals is needed.

3.  **Justification of the Objective Function:** The objective function in Equation (2) warrants further explanation. Specifically, the choice to use an L2 norm on the codes deviates from the standard L1 sparsity-inducing norm commonly used in dictionary learning, including the foundational work by Mairal et al. This is a significant methodological choice that requires justification. The authors should explain the rationale for preferring an L2 norm and discuss its impact on the resulting codes and dictionary atoms, especially since sparsity is often a desired property in such representations.

**Minor Comments and Questions:**

*   **Equation (1):** There may be a typographical error in Equation (1). Please clarify the formulation of V. As written, it appears to be a row concatenation of matrices. A column vector format, i.e., [V^1, V^2, ..., V^r]^T, might be more consistent with standard notation for the subsequent matrix operations.

*   **Clarity on Line 146:** The statement on line 146 is unclear. Please revise this for clarity or correct what appears to be a typo.

*   **Writing and Presentation:** The manuscript would benefit significantly from a thorough proofreading to improve overall clarity and correct numerous grammatical errors and typos. Refining the language would make the paper's contributions easier to understand and evaluate.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses an important problem, its limited novelty, coupled with insufficient justification for key methodological choices, prevents a strong recommendation for acceptance at this time. The authors need to provide a clearer motivation for their approach and a more rigorous discussion of their contributions relative to existing work.
