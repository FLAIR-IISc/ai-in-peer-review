Summary
The paper extends online dictionary learning by adding a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty on dictionary atoms to encourage spatially smooth, compact “blob-like” components, alongside sparsity constraints on the atoms and ridge-penalized (or optionally ℓ1) codes. The resulting Smooth-SODL algorithm uses block-coordinate descent: ridge coding via SVD and atom updates solved with a few FISTA steps, with convergence guarantees similar to Mairal et al. (2010). Experiments on HCP task fMRI compare Smooth-SODL to sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and CanICA, reporting improved visual interpretability, competitive explained variance, higher stability, and modest runtime overhead.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant need in neuroimaging: obtaining sparse, spatially coherent components that may better reflect functional regions.
- Scalable online algorithm with modest overhead (≈2–3× SODL), practical for large n and p.
- Clear connection to prior work (SODL, GraphNet/TV-like penalties), with convergence arguments and implementable updates.
- Empirical evaluation on a large public dataset; some quantitative metrics (explained variance, stability, downstream prediction) are reported.
- Practical guidance on hyperparameters and initialization; plan for open-source release.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Validation relies heavily on qualitative visual inspection of “blobs.” Interpretability claims are subjective and hard to assess for non-experts.
- The core benefit—spatial regularization yields smoother/cleaner components—is unsurprising given the penalty; it is unclear how much this translates to objectively better or more reproducible brain regions.
- With abundant data (HCP), the necessity of strong spatial regularization is questionable; the learning-curve evidence is suggestive but limited. Please quantify gains with confidence intervals and additional sample regimes.
- Comparisons to structured baselines are incomplete. TV-ℓ1/GraphNet-based atlas methods (e.g., Abraham et al., related TV-constrained DL) are not directly benchmarked, making it hard to judge novelty and impact beyond incremental adaptation to the online setting.
- Fairness of comparisons: CanICA is smoothed and often thresholded; SODL/Smooth-SODL are not. This can bias both interpretability and explained-variance comparisons. Consider consistent preprocessing and both thresholded and unthresholded reporting across methods.
- Several methodological details could be clearer: stopping criteria and tolerances for FISTA; how γ is tuned and whether the adaptive γt schedule is used; sensitivity to τ, positivity/simplex constraints; boundary conditions in the Laplacian; and the practical effects of using ridge vs ℓ1 coding.

Quality
The algorithmic extension is technically sound and consistent with prior theory on online DL and convex denoising with Laplacian penalties. However, some claims warrant more rigorous support:
- “1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e−6 accuracy” seems optimistic; please report actual tolerances, wall-clock per-update costs, and sensitivity to stopping.
- Provide a principled schedule or cross-validation for γ and τ, and quantify stability of results to these choices.
- Include run-time and memory profiles across a range of k, p, and batch sizes.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but contains typos and formatting issues; figures could be more self-contained. Suggested improvements:
- Define all constraints (ℓ1-ball vs simplex/positivity) and justify their choice; clarify projection algorithms used.
- Detail Laplacian discretization, boundary handling, and scaling.
- Provide exact training protocols: number of passes, batch size, masks, preprocessing, hyperparameter grids, and seeds.
- Report matching procedure for atom stability (e.g., Hungarian matching) and the exact stability metric.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of imposing spatial smoothness/structure with sparsity is well-established (e.g., TV-ℓ1, GraphNet), and prior work has explored structured DL for brain maps. The main novelty is adapting these ideas to an online DL framework with modest overhead and demonstrating scalability on large neuroimaging datasets.

Significance
Potentially useful as a practical tool if it reliably improves reproducibility and downstream performance, especially in low- to medium-data regimes. As presented, the evidence is not yet decisive: improvements are mostly qualitative, and advantages shrink with large sample sizes.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper
- Add objective spatial metrics: total variation, compactness, number/size of connected components, overlap/Dice with anatomical or functional atlases.
- Provide split-half/test-retest reproducibility with confidence intervals, including stability across hyperparameters and initializations.
- Include ablations isolating the effect of γ (and τ) on EV, stability, prediction performance, and spatial metrics.
- Benchmark against strong structured baselines (TV-ℓ1/GraphNet-based atlas learning), ideally with comparable runtimes.
- Harmonize preprocessing and thresholding across methods; report both raw and thresholded maps for all.
- Release code and scripts to reproduce all experiments.

Arguments for acceptance
- Scalable, simple extension of a widely used online DL framework with convergence guarantees.
- Empirical evidence of improved spatial structure and some gains in stability/prediction.
- Likely to be useful in practice for interpretable decompositions and ROI definition.

Arguments against acceptance
- Validation is predominantly qualitative; objective gains over baselines are limited and sometimes diminish with large n.
- Missing comparisons to the most relevant structured methods.
- Some methodological details and experimental controls are insufficiently documented.

Overall assessment
Promising and practical, but the current evidence does not clearly demonstrate a substantial advance over established structured approaches or convincingly quantify when spatial regularization materially improves scientific conclusions. I would lean toward rejection unless the authors can provide stronger, quantitative validation and more comprehensive comparisons.
