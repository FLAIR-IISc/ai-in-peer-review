This paper proposes an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries across individuals in neuroimaging, extending the well-established framework in [14] with a Sobolev prior to promote spatially contiguous atoms. The method is applied to empirical datasets, explores tuning parameters, and is compared against two field alternatives.

Technical quality: I would rate this marginally below 3 if that option existed. The model is clearly defined, and the algorithms follow a solid framework based on [14]. However, the empirical section does not convincingly demonstrate the added value of the Sobolev prior. In particular, Figure 2 does not make it clear in what respect SSMF outperforms the baselines. For example: Are spatial boundaries quantitatively smoother? Are known connected regions uniquely or more accurately recovered by SSMF? Rather than stating that SSMF results are “more structured,” the paper should define explicit, literature-justified criteria (e.g., spatial contiguity, smoothness, alignment with known functional/structural atlases) and report quantitative improvements on these metrics. After reading the rebuttal: the additional results are informative, and I raise my technical-quality rating from 2 to 3. I encourage the authors to include those results in the revision and to further demonstrate, with quantitative measures and neuroscientific references, that SSMF atoms are both more structured and more interpretable.

Novelty/originality: The main original contribution is the extension of [14] to incorporate Sobolev priors that encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms. This is a meaningful and nontrivial development.

Potential impact/usefulness: Dictionary learning is a useful tool for modeling neuroimaging data across large cohorts, and incorporating Sobolev or related spatial priors is theoretically well-motivated and likely to improve results. The proposed online algorithm appears efficient and could become a valuable addition to the neuroscience/medical imaging toolbox. The authors might also explore related applications such as image segmentation and regional sensing.

Presentation: The paper is generally well organized but needs careful editing. Several confusing passages and typographical errors impede readability. Specific issues include:
- In the matrix decomposition between Lines 67–68, should the transpose on the block of V^j (j = 1,…, r) apply to each individual V^j instead?
- Line 73: missing reference for the HCP dataset.
- Line 83: grammar issue (“The Graph-Net penalty can imposes ...”).
- Line 146: a bolded sentence appears to be an unresolved co-author comment.
- Equations (6) and (8): the last lines disagree in sign and scaling: V^j + At[j,j]^{-1](V At^j − Bt^j) versus At[j,j] V^j − (V At^j − Bt^j). Also, in the last line of Eq. (6), please clarify whether V denotes the dictionary from the previous iteration (i.e., the j-th column is not the optimization variable v).
- Line 157: duplicated phrase (“a single, a single”).
- Line 169: typo (“We did an extensive study of th how”).
- Section 3.3 (paragraph near line 147, just below Algorithm 2): please provide the explicit solution strategy for Eq. (6), at least in the appendix (e.g., closed-form update, proximal operator, or solver details and convergence criteria).
- Figure 2: methods are shown on different horizontal and sagittal slices, preventing direct visual comparison. Please align slices across methods and support the “more structured” claim (line 222) with quantitative spatial metrics (e.g., gradient norm, Laplacian energy, connected-component analysis, overlap with known atlases).
- Figure 3: it is unclear what is being predicted. If this is brain–behavior regression, is it built on the U_i’s? The legend is not explained in the text or caption, the PCA baseline mentioned in the caption does not appear in the plot, and lines 224–227 are hard to follow (e.g., which curves achieve R^2 > 0.1?).

Overall, this is a promising extension of a strong framework, with clear methodological grounding and potential impact. To maximize its contribution, the revision should (1) include the additional results from the rebuttal, (2) substantiate the benefits of the Sobolev prior with quantitative, literature-aligned metrics of spatial structure and interpretability, and (3) substantially improve clarity and correctness in presentation.
