This paper proposes a method for identifying functional components in fMRI data using under-complete dictionary learning. The primary contribution is the introduction of a novel regularization scheme that combines a Sobolev (H1) norm to encourage spatial smoothness with an L1 norm to promote sparsity in the learned dictionary atoms (the functional components). The approach is evaluated on both synthetic and real fMRI data.

**Strengths:**

The core idea of enforcing specific, neuroscientifically relevant structural priors on the learned components is well-motivated. Combining a smoothness-inducing penalty like the Sobolev norm with a sparsity-inducing L1 penalty is a sound and interesting approach for fMRI analysis, where functional networks are expected to be both spatially coherent and sparse. The formulation that integrates these two priors directly into the dictionary learning objective is a novel contribution to this problem domain.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the interesting formulation, the paper has several significant weaknesses in its current form.

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Justification:** The primary weakness is that the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate a meaningful advantage of the proposed method over simpler baselines. The performance gains reported for the Sobolev/L1 approach are marginal, and their significance is not adequately explained or statistically validated. The paper fails to provide a clear answer to the crucial question: what tangible benefit does the added complexity of the Sobolev regularizer offer over using, for instance, only L1 regularization or other standard techniques? A more in-depth qualitative and quantitative analysis is required to justify the novel formulation. For example, a visual comparison of the learned components from different methods could have helped illustrate whether the proposed approach yields more interpretable or neuroscientifically plausible brain networks.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript is difficult to follow and appears to have been written in a rush. The exposition is often dense, and the mathematical formulations lack sufficient intuition and clear motivation, making the paper a tedious and sometimes confusing read. Key concepts and the overall narrative could be presented with much greater clarity. This lack of clarity significantly hinders the reader's ability to appreciate the paper's potential contributions.

3.  **Lack of Professionalism in Preparation:** The manuscript shows clear signs of being unfinished. For instance, there are draft comments from the authors still present in the text (e.g., line 146). This lack of careful proofreading detracts from the overall quality and professionalism of the submission.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Strengthen the Empirical Evaluation:** The authors should provide a more compelling case for their method. This could include:
    *   Statistical significance testing to validate any performance claims.
    *   Ablation studies that compare the combined regularization against models with only L1 or only Sobolev regularization to isolate the specific benefits of the combined approach.
    *   A strong qualitative analysis. Show the learned components from the proposed method and the baselines side-by-side. Do the components learned with the Sobolev regularizer better resemble known functional networks or exhibit fewer noise artifacts? This would be a powerful argument in the method's favor.

*   **Improve Clarity and Readability:** The paper requires a thorough revision for clarity. I strongly recommend restructuring the methods section to provide a high-level overview and intuition for the approach before delving into the mathematical details. A table summarizing the different models, their objective functions, and key assumptions could also greatly aid comprehension.

*   **Thorough Proofreading:** The manuscript must be meticulously proofread to correct all grammatical errors, typos, and to remove any remaining editorial comments.

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting idea, its execution in terms of empirical validation and manuscript preparation is currently below the standard for a top-tier conference. The lack of convincing results and poor presentation makes it difficult to recommend for acceptance in its present form.
