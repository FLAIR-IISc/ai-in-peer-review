This paper presents an online algorithm for learning structured dictionaries to characterize neuroimaging data across individuals. The authors extend a well-established framework [14] by incorporating a Sobolev prior to encourage spatial contiguity of dictionary atoms. They apply their method to empirical data, investigate the effect of varying tuning parameters, and compare its performance to two alternative methods in the field.

Technical quality: I would rate this aspect marginally below 3 if given the option. The model definition is clear, and the algorithms follow a well-established framework [14], appearing solid. However, the presentation may undermine this solidity, as the paper does not clearly demonstrate the empirical advantage of introducing the Sobolev prior in the results section. Specifically, in Figure 2, it is unclear in what aspects the SSMF method outperforms the two alternatives. For instance, are the spatial boundaries more smooth quantitatively? Are there certain regions, known to be connected, identified only by SSMF? Instead of vaguely stating that the SSMF results are "more structured," the authors could explicitly describe criteria justified by neuroscience literature and provide quantitative measurements of the improvement according to these criteria.

After reading the rebuttal, I found the additional results to be very informative, prompting me to revise my rating on technical quality from 2 to 3. I hope the authors will include these new results in their revision and demonstrate that the atoms learned by SSMF are more structured (with quantitative measurements) and more meaningful (with reference to neuroscience literature).

Novelty/originality: This paper makes a notable original contribution by extending [14] to incorporate Sobolev priors and encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms.

Potential impact or usefulness: Dictionary learning is a useful approach for explaining neuroimaging data across a large group of subjects. Introducing Sobolev priors or similar spatial priors should, in theory, improve the results. This paper provides an efficient online algorithm for doing so, which could become a valuable tool in the neuroscience/medical imaging community. The authors may also explore other applications of this method, such as image segmentation and regional sensing.

Presentation: The general organization of the paper is acceptable, but it appears to be a draft that requires further editing. There are confusing descriptions and typos that hinder understanding.
