This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, incorporating both sparsity and structural priors – specifically, a Sobolev penalty to encourage piecewise smooth, compact “blobs” representing brain regions. The approach builds upon the online dictionary learning work of Mairal et al. (2010) and aims to delineate brain structures from functional images, leveraging inter-subject variability. The motivation is well-articulated: traditional methods struggle with high dimensionality and effectively utilizing inter-subject variability, while ROI-based approaches require principled definition of those regions. The proposed method seeks to automatically define such regions.

This work relates to a substantial body of research in neuroimaging and machine learning. Dictionary learning has been previously applied to fMRI data (e.g., Baldassarre et al., 2013; Varoquaux et al., 2013), often with sparsity constraints. The use of spatial priors to improve interpretability is also not entirely novel (e.g., Abraham et al., 2015; Varol & Davatzikos, 2012). However, the combination of online learning, sparsity, and a Sobolev penalty for structured smoothness, applied to large-scale brain imaging data, appears to be a unique contribution. Several recent works (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2013, 2016) have explored related ideas for multi-subject analysis, but often rely on batch processing which limits scalability.

**Strengths:**

*   **Scalability:** The online learning aspect of the method is a significant strength, allowing it to handle the large datasets common in neuroimaging (e.g., HCP).
*   **Principled Approach:** The use of a Sobolev penalty offers a mathematically grounded way to impose structural constraints on the learned components.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges in neuroimaging and positions the proposed method as a solution to these problems.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** Experiments are conducted on a large, publicly available dataset (HCP), and the results are compared against established methods (CanICA and SODL).
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The BCD algorithm is well-described and convergence guarantees are mentioned.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Subjective Evaluation:** The primary visual comparison of dictionary atoms (Fig. 2a) is difficult to interpret for readers unfamiliar with neuroanatomy. While the authors state the blobs are more interpretable, a more quantitative evaluation of the “quality” of these blobs is needed.
*   **Limited Novelty in Spatial Regularization:** While the combination is novel, the core idea of incorporating spatial smoothness is well-established in image processing and neuroimaging. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate a substantial advantage over existing spatially regularized methods.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning, while present, feels somewhat incomplete. The optimal regime is described, but a more systematic analysis of the sensitivity of the results to different parameter settings would be valuable.
*   **Insufficient Validation of Inter-Subject Consistency:** The paper motivates the work around leveraging inter-subject variability, but the validation presented (predicting behavioral variables) doesn’t directly assess whether the learned components capture consistent patterns *across* subjects.
*   **Limited discussion of computational cost:** The authors mention a factor of 2-3 increase in runtime compared to SODL, but a more detailed breakdown of computational complexity, and a comparison to alternative spatially regularized methods, would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The method addresses a relevant and challenging problem in neuroimaging.
*   The proposed approach is technically sound and leverages existing algorithms in a novel way.
*   The experiments demonstrate the potential of the method on a large, publicly available dataset.
*   The online learning aspect is a significant advantage for scalability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The evaluation is largely subjective and lacks strong quantitative validation.
*   The novelty of the spatial regularization component is limited.
*   The paper does not fully explore the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter settings.
*   The benefits of the method over simpler approaches (e.g. SODL with careful data preprocessing) are not convincingly demonstrated.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging. However, the subjective evaluation and limited demonstration of substantial improvement over existing methods raise concerns. While the scalability and principled approach are commendable, the paper would benefit from more rigorous quantitative validation and a more thorough analysis of its limitations.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with major revisions. The authors should:

1.  **Provide a more quantitative evaluation** of the dictionary atoms. This could involve measures of spatial coherence, consistency across subjects, or alignment with known anatomical structures.
2.  **Compare against a wider range of spatially regularized dictionary learning methods.**
3.  **Conduct a more systematic hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.**
4.  **Include validation metrics that specifically assess inter-subject consistency** of the learned components.
5.  **Provide a detailed breakdown of the computational cost** of the algorithm and compare it to alternative approaches.
