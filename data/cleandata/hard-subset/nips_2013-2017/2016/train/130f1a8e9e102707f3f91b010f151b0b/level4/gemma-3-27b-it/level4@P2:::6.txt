This paper introduces a novel regularization technique for online dictionary learning, aiming to achieve both sparsity and spatial smoothness in learned components. The core idea is to combine an L1 penalty for sparsity with a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially contiguous, blob-like structures, particularly relevant for neuroimaging applications. While the authors don't provide a proof of solution existence, they present an algorithm and demonstrate experimentally that their approach outperforms existing methods.

The strength of this work lies in the introduction of this new regularizer, offering a promising avenue for improving the interpretability and denoising capabilities of dictionary learning in the context of brain imaging. The experimental results, particularly the comparison to state-of-the-art methods like CanICA and standard online dictionary learning (SODL), showcase the potential benefits of the proposed approach. The authors convincingly demonstrate improvements in capturing inter-subject variability and predicting behavioral variables.  The large-scale experiment on the HCP dataset is particularly commendable.

However, the non-convex nature of the optimization problem and the lack of theoretical guarantees regarding solution existence are weaknesses.  The paper relies heavily on empirical validation, which, while strong, doesn't fully address the theoretical underpinnings. The writing, while generally good, contains areas for improvement in clarity and conciseness. Specific instances, such as line 146, could benefit from rephrasing for improved readability. The description of the hyperparameter selection process, while present, could be more detailed.  It would be beneficial to discuss the computational trade-offs more explicitly – while the authors mention a 2-3x slowdown compared to standard SODL, a more thorough analysis of scalability with increasing dataset size would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Introduces a novel and well-motivated regularizer that combines sparsity and smoothness.
*   **Empirical Performance:** Demonstrates superior performance on a challenging, large-scale neuroimaging dataset compared to established methods.
*   **Relevance:** Addresses a significant problem in neuroimaging – extracting interpretable and robust features from noisy and variable brain data.
*   **Scalability:** Builds upon the scalable online dictionary learning framework, allowing application to large datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:**  The non-convexity of the optimization problem and absence of solution existence proofs are limitations.
*   **Writing Clarity:** While generally clear, certain sections could benefit from improved writing and organization.
*   **Computational Cost:** The increased computational cost (2-3x slowdown) relative to standard SODL should be carefully considered, and a more in-depth scalability analysis would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of dictionary learning and its application to neuroimaging. The proposed regularization technique is innovative and shows promising results. While the lack of theoretical guarantees and some writing clarity issues are concerns, the strength of the empirical validation and the potential impact of the work outweigh these weaknesses. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance, particularly given the author’s responsiveness to feedback (as indicated by the post-rebuttal comment regarding clarity and presentation).
