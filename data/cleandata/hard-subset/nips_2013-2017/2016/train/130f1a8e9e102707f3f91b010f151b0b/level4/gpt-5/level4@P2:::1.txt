Summary
The paper introduces Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning method for decomposing population neuroimaging data into sparse, spatially structured components (“atoms”). The model constrains atoms with an ℓ1-type constraint to encourage sparsity and adds a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty to promote piecewise-smooth, compact “blob-like” maps. The algorithm builds on Mairal et al. (2010), using block coordinate descent with ridge-coded updates and a projected first-order method (FISTA-style) for atom updates. The authors claim a 2–3x computational overhead relative to SODL while scaling to large n and p. Experiments on HCP task-fMRI compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA, reporting improved interpretability and stability, competitive explained variance (especially in low-data regimes), and reasonable prediction of behavioral scores.

Relation to prior work
The approach sits between unstructured online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010; Mensch et al., 2016) and structured spatial decompositions such as TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning and GraphNet-style penalties used in neuroimaging (e.g., Abraham et al.; Grosenick et al.). The main novelty is integrating a Laplacian smoothness prior directly into an online dictionary-learning loop with minimal computational overhead. Compared to prior alternating-minimization approaches for spatially structured decompositions, the online formulation is appealing for large-scale datasets.

Strengths
- Addresses an important problem: data-driven, interpretable ROI discovery that captures inter-subject variability at scale.
- Sound extension of a widely used online dictionary-learning framework with a spatial smoothness prior; practical algorithmic choices (ridge-coded updates, projected gradient) and convergence arguments inherited from SODL.
- Empirical evidence that spatial regularization improves interpretability and stability without excessive loss in explained variance; clear learning-curve advantage in low-data regimes.
- Runtime is reasonable and the promise to release code via Nilearn suggests practical impact.

Weaknesses
- Limited theoretical contribution beyond adapting SODL: no new convergence rates, identifiability, or approximation guarantees for the structured setting.
- Some algorithmic details are under-specified: the exact projected/FISTA update with constraints, the handling of the ℓ1-ball/simplex constraint in combination with the Laplacian term, and conditions under which FFT-based updates apply.
- Hyperparameter guidance is heuristic (e.g., time-varying αt and discussion of γ’s effect on Lipschitz constants) without a principled selection strategy or robust sensitivity analysis.
- Evaluation breadth is limited: comparisons omit TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning, structured sparse PCA, NMF, and other constrained ICA baselines; only one dataset/task is used; quantitative measures of spatial coherence/compactness are sparse.
- The manuscript requires substantial editing: duplicated sentences, numerous typos, inconsistent notation (V vs v), and an unedited internal comment (“146 XXX: I don’t understand the switch between V and v.”) remain. Several references are incomplete.

Quality
The objective and optimization scheme are reasonable, and the subproblems are convex with standard solvers. The empirical results are promising and largely support the claims on interpretability and early data efficiency. However, the methodological presentation leaves ambiguities (e.g., projected FISTA details, γ scheduling) and the theoretical contribution is modest.

Clarity
As written, clarity is a major issue. The paper would benefit from thorough proofreading, consistent notation, complete references, clearer figures/captions, and a more precise description of the dictionary-update solver and projections.

Originality
Moderate. The core idea—Laplacian-regularized atoms within online dictionary learning—fits naturally within existing frameworks (SODL, GraphNet, TV-ℓ1 methods). The contribution is mainly algorithmic integration and scalability for neuroimaging.

Significance
Potentially high for neuroimaging practice: scalable, more interpretable decompositions that capture population variability are valuable. Broader impact is plausible, but stronger baselines and clearer methodology would increase confidence.

Pros for acceptance
- Scalable, practical method that improves interpretability and stability over unstructured SODL.
- Solid empirical evidence on a large, public dataset with multiple evaluation angles (interpretability, reproducibility, prediction).
- Likely to be useful to practitioners; planned open-source release.

Cons for acceptance
- Presentation quality is currently below conference standards (typos, leftover comments, clarity issues).
- Limited theoretical novelty and under-specified algorithmic details.
- Incomplete empirical comparisons and ablations; missing structured baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Algorithmic clarity: Provide the exact projected gradient/FISTA update for Eq. (7)–(8), including step sizes, projection operator onto C (ℓ1-ball/simplex), and stopping criteria. Clarify when FFT-based updates are applicable. Distinguish clearly between constrained vs penalized formulations.
- Hyperparameters: Specify ranges and selection procedures for α, γ, τ; report cross-validation protocol (splits, metrics), and include sensitivity/ablation plots (e.g., varying γ and τ). Justify the αt ∝ t−1/2 schedule and provide a practical recipe for γ to avoid step-size collapse.
- Evaluation: Add structured baselines (TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning, structured sparse PCA, NMF, constrained ICA/MELODIC with standard thresholding protocol). Quantify spatial structure (e.g., cluster compactness, number/size of connected components, smoothness indices) and reproducibility (e.g., split-half stability, Dice/ARI). Report prediction setups to avoid leakage.
- Claims: Temper statements such as “global optimum” to “global optimum of the convex subproblem”; clarify the role of the Laplacian vs TV (and consider a brief comparison or discussion).
- Writing and presentation: Remove duplicated lines and internal comments; fix spelling/grammar; unify notation (V vs v, vector/matrix conventions); complete and format references; ensure figures are legible and self-contained; define all symbols on first use.
- Reproducibility: Provide code and scripts (or a permanent link) with configuration files, preprocessing details, and random seeds.

Overall, this is a promising and practically relevant contribution, but it needs substantial revision for clarity and a stronger empirical/theoretical foundation to meet conference standards.
