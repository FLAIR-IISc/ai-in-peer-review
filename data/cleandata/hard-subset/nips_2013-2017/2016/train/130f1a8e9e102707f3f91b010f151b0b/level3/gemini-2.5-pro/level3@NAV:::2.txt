This paper introduces an online structured sparse dictionary learning algorithm, named Smooth-SODL, designed for analyzing neuroimaging data. The central contribution is the extension of the well-established online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) prior. This prior is intended to encourage the learned dictionary atoms to be spatially contiguous and smooth, making them more interpretable as functional brain regions. This work represents a novel combination of familiar techniques applied to a relevant problem in computational neuroscience.

The primary strength of the paper is its conceptual soundness and potential impact. The proposed method could become a useful tool for researchers in neuroscience and medical imaging, offering a scalable way to extract structured components from large datasets. The core idea of integrating a spatial regularizer into a fast online framework is practical and addresses a clear need in the field. The technical approach follows a well-established optimization framework, and the additional results provided in the rebuttal helped to clarify some initial concerns about its solidity.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in its current form, primarily in empirical validation and presentation clarity. The original manuscript failed to provide compelling quantitative evidence that the Sobolev prior confers a significant advantage. The claim that Smooth-SODL atoms are more "structured" or meaningful was not substantiated with neuroscience-justified criteria or metrics. This is particularly evident in Figure 2, where the use of different slices for different methods hinders direct comparison and the visual evidence for improved structure is weak.

Furthermore, the manuscript's quality is severely hampered by poor presentation. It contains numerous typos, confusing descriptions, and formatting errors that impede understanding. There are critical mathematical inconsistencies, including potential errors in the signs and terms of Equations (6) and (8), unclear variable references, and a missing reference for the HCP dataset. For reproducibility, the authors should include a detailed derivation for the solution to Equation (6). Figure 3 is also unclear regarding the prediction task, the baselines used, and which results support the claims made in the text.

In summary, while the proposed method is promising and the core idea is original, the paper is not yet ready for publication. A major revision is required to strengthen the empirical evidence, clarify all figures and claims, and thoroughly proofread the manuscript to fix the extensive presentation and notational issues. The informative results from the rebuttal should also be integrated into the main paper.
