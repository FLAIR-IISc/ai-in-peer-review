- Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online multivariate dictionary-learning method combining l1 sparsity with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to yield spatially smooth, blob-like brain atoms.  
- Builds on Mairal et al. (2010) online dictionary learning to handle large-scale neuroimaging without manual ROI definition.  
- Novelty: first to combine online dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior for spatial regularization in neuroimaging.  
- Scalability: online algorithm is suitable for large datasets (e.g., Human Connectome Project).  
- Potential impact: could aid automatic discovery of brain regions, denoising, and analysis of inter-subject variability across modalities.  
- Theoretical foundation: uses established optimization tools (block coordinate descent, FISTA) and appears mathematically sound.  
- Empirical evaluation weak: Figure 2 lacked quantitative metrics and clear visual evidence that spatial regularization produced more structured atoms.  
- Comparisons to CanICA are confounded by differing preprocessing (smoothing) and inconsistent visualization choices.  
- Clarity and presentation problems: typographical errors, inconsistent notation (transpose placement), and confusing phrasing hinder comprehension.  
- Key equations unclear: Equations (6) and (8) need better explanation and the algorithm variable references should be clarified.  
- Missing details and references: HCP dataset citation missing and the derivation for solving the Eq.(6) sub-problem is absent.  
- Neuroscience justification insufficient: hyperparameter choices (α, γ, τ) lack neuroimaging rationale and no quantitative measure of “meaningfulness” vs. other methods.  
- Figure problems: different slices shown across methods (Fig.2), unclear predictions and role of Ui in Fig.3, missing PCA baseline, and ambiguous R^2 claims.  
- Rebuttal helped: additional hyperparameter and convergence analyses improved technical quality and partially addressed concerns.  
- Revision required: authors must incorporate rebuttal experiments into the manuscript, add derivations, and improve clarity and presentation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, novel and scalable method, and rebuttal addressed some issues.  
- Arguments against acceptance: empirical validation remains weak, presentation and mathematical detail gaps persist, and neuroanatomical linkage is underdeveloped.  
- Overall assessment: worthwhile contribution with potential impact but requires major revisions; recommend acceptance with major revisions.
