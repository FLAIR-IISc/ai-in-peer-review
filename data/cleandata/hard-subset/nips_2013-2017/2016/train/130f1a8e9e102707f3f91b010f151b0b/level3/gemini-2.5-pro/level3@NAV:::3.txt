This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of the online sparse dictionary learning framework (Mairal et al., 2010) for analyzing large-scale neuroimaging data. The key idea is to augment the standard sparse dictionary learning objective with a Sobolev (Laplacian) prior on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages the learned components, or "atoms," to be spatially smooth and form contiguous "blobs," which the authors argue are more neuroscientifically interpretable than the scattered patterns produced by standard sparse models. The proposed online algorithm is shown to be scalable and computationally efficient, extending existing work by introducing a structured penalty while maintaining comparable convergence guarantees.

The strengths of the paper lie in its technically sound proposal and clear motivation. Extending a highly scalable online learning framework to incorporate spatial priors is a valuable and non-trivial contribution. The problem of learning data-driven, interpretable brain regions is significant in neuroimaging, and the proposed method offers an elegant way to approach it.

However, the paper's primary weakness is its experimental validation, which fails to clearly demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed Sobolev prior. The main results rely heavily on the visual comparison of learned brain "blobs." To an untrained eye, the superiority of the proposed method's outputs is not obvious. More fundamentally, the observation that adding a smoothness penalty yields smoother results is unsurprising; the paper needs to more rigorously demonstrate why this is advantageous. The authors use the large-scale Human Connectome Project dataset, which may not suffer from the data scarcity issues that spatial smoothing is typically meant to address. A more convincing validation would involve quantifying the inter-subject consistency of the learned regions or showing a significant improvement in a downstream predictive task. The current prediction experiment shows the method is competitive but does not outperform all baselines, thus the added complexity of the prior is not fully justified.

In summary, while the proposed method is an interesting technical extension, the current manuscript does not provide sufficient evidence to support its scientific significance. The link between the proposed prior and a tangible improvement in neuroscientific insight or predictive power remains unclear. For the paper to be acceptable, the authors must strengthen their evaluation with quantitative metrics that move beyond subjective visual assessment and more clearly establish the value of their proposed regularization.
