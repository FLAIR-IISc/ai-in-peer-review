

This paper proposes "Smooth-SODL," extending online dictionary learning with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to impose spatial structure on brain imaging data, aiming to extract structured, sparse brain regions ("blobs") from fMRI data. The authors extend Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework by adding a spatial smoothness constraint, claiming their method better captures inter-subject variability while maintaining scalability. The approach is evaluated on Human Connectome Project (HCP) data using qualitative visual comparisons and limited quantitative metrics.

The technical approach is well-motivated, with clear algorithmic details and convergence guarantees. The Laplacian penalty does indeed produce smoother spatial patterns, as expected. However, the paper's claims about the method's significance are difficult to substantiate. The main results rely almost entirely on visual comparisons of "blobs" that lack clear interpretation for untrained readers, and the paper fails to demonstrate how these blobs correspond to known neuroanatomical structures. The authors acknowledge that on large datasets like HCP, data scarcity isn't the primary challenge, yet they claim their method provides significant benefits in "small, medium, and large-scale regimes" without adequately addressing why spatial regularization is necessary when sufficient data is available.

The validation approach is problematic. The paper compares against CanICA and SODL but doesn't properly address inter-subject consistency as a validation metric, which was a key expectation. The quantitative metrics (explained variance, behavioral prediction) don't clearly demonstrate superiority of the spatially regularized approach over baseline methods, especially since CanICA's performance is comparable or better in some cases. The paper also doesn't adequately address why the observed "smoother blobs" represent a meaningful improvement rather than simply a consequence of the smoothing penalty.

While the extension of online dictionary learning is technically sound, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate the method's scientific contribution or practical utility in brain imaging. The claimed benefits are not clearly supported by the evidence presented, and the validation approach is insufficient for a conference paper of this caliber. The work would benefit from more rigorous validation against established neuroanatomical knowledge and a clearer demonstration of when spatial regularization provides tangible advantages over simpler approaches.
