Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method for large-scale neuroimaging that adds a spatial smoothness prior on the dictionary atoms to the standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) framework of Mairal et al. (2010). The “structure” is enforced by a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty on each atom, while sparsity is enforced by constraining atoms to an L1-ball (often, the positive simplex), and the codes are estimated via ridge regression. The dictionary update reduces to a constrained quadratic “denoising” subproblem akin to GraphNet; the authors solve it by one or a few FISTA steps inside a block-coordinate descent scheme adapted from Mairal et al. Experiments on HCP task fMRI maps compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and group PCA/ICA (CanICA), reporting improved visual interpretability and stability at a modest (2–3x) runtime overhead, and competitive explained variance and behavioral prediction.

This work sits between several strands of literature: scalable online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010; Mensch et al., 2016), spatially structured decompositions for fMRI using TV-L1/GraphNet-like penalties (e.g., Abraham et al., MICCAI’13; Varoquaux et al., IPMI’10; Grosenick et al., NeuroImage’13), and group ICA/PCA pipelines (Beckmann & Smith, 2004; Varoquaux et al., 2010). Conceptually, Smooth-SODL is SODL with a Laplacian penalty on atoms; the paper’s main contribution is to show that such spatial structure can be integrated into an online scheme with limited overhead and improved stability/interpretability.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The optimization strategy is reasonable: alternating code updates (ridge) and atom updates (convex quadratic with Laplacian and L1/simplex constraint). However, several aspects need clarification:
  - Equation (2) uses lim_{n→∞} 1/n ∑ … as the objective. This is unusual and confusing in a finite-sample setting. Please rewrite as the empirical risk minimized online and relate it rigorously to the population expectation used in online analysis.
  - Choice of ridge (L2) codes vs L1: the paper allows L1 but defaults to ridge, arguing “stable codes.” This design choice substantially changes sparsity/identifiability properties. A systematic comparison (same atoms, identical constraints) with L1-coded SODL would strengthen the case.
  - Laplacian prior: a graph Laplacian encourages smoothness, but by itself does not enforce compact, blob-like support. The claim of “piece-wise smooth and compact” needs justification. If compactness relies on the L1-ball/simplex constraint, please analyze how the two priors interact and whether non-negativity is essential. A comparison to TV-based penalties (which more directly promote spatially compact regions) would be informative, even on a subset.
  - Convergence and inner accuracy: stating that “1–2 FISTA iterations suffice to reach 1e−6 accuracy” is not credible; please clarify the stopping criterion and empirical tolerance actually used. Also, if γ is large, the Lipschitz constant and step sizes become small; the proposed time-varying γ_t heuristic needs theoretical or empirical justification (beyond Eq. (9)).
  - Notation/derivations: The equality ΩLap(v) = 0.5 v^T Δ v typically uses Δ = ∇^T∇; check constants. The claim “global optimum at each BCD iteration” holds for the convex subproblem given exact solution; with approximate inner solves, a convergence discussion is needed.
- Empirical evaluation: The HCP study is valuable, but the evaluation lacks some controls:
  - No ablation isolating the effect of Laplacian vs sparsity vs non-negativity.
  - Comparisons to TV-L1/GraphNet dictionary-learning baselines (even on fewer subjects) would better position the contribution against prior spatially-structured methods.
  - Behavioral prediction: Report train/test splits, confidence intervals, and how thresholds for tCanICA are chosen. Provide atom stability with error bars and matching methodology (Hungarian matching objective).
  - Overlap with known atlases/functional networks could quantify interpretability claims.

Clarity
The paper is hard to read and contains many typos and organizational issues (e.g., “xperiments,” “intepretable,” “th Conference on…,” inconsistent symbols). “Line 146” is referenced but unclear. Equation (1) mixes vector/matrix notation; please check whether the intended dictionary is V = [V1|…|Vk] with Xi ≈ V Ui (current text seems consistent) or whether a transpose/indexing change is needed (the present notation is acceptable, but double-check). Define “structure” precisely early on. Clearly state boundary conditions for the Laplacian, the voxel grid, and whether masks/brain topology are incorporated.

Originality
The idea of adding spatial smoothness to dictionary atoms is not new; TV- and Laplacian-type priors exist in the neuroimaging literature, and GraphNet-like regularization is well-known. The novelty here lies in integrating a Laplacian penalty into the online SODL framework with minor overhead and demonstrating scalability. This is an incremental contribution; to meet NIPS standards, the paper should sharpen the technical novelty (e.g., analysis of time-varying γ, convergence under inexact BCD, or a principled stochastic approximation view) and/or strengthen empirical comparisons to structured baselines.

Significance
If robust and reproducible, a fast scalable method that yields smoother, sparser, and more stable components on very large fMRI cohorts is useful for practitioners and could be adopted (especially with a Nilearn implementation). However, the current empirical evidence is not fully convincing without ablations and stronger baselines; and the conceptual advance beyond prior structured dictionary learning appears modest.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant problem (scalable, structured decompositions for neuroimaging) with a practical algorithm.
- Reasonable runtime overhead versus SODL, and faster than a batch ICA baseline.
- Large-scale evaluation on HCP; improvements in stability and interpretability are promising.
- Open-source implementation planned in a widely used library.

Cons
- Limited novelty: essentially SODL + Laplacian on atoms; prior structured methods not fully compared.
- Motivation and theory around the choice of Laplacian vs TV and L2 vs L1 codes are underdeveloped.
- Clarity and writing quality issues; ambiguous references (e.g., “line 146”), typos, and notation glitches.
- Evaluation lacks ablations, statistical rigor, and comparison to stronger structured baselines.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Justify the choice of ridge-coded updates vs L1-coded SODL with controlled experiments and discuss identifiability/interpretability trade-offs.
- Clarify Eq. (2) (remove the lim_{n→∞}), the precise form of ΩLap and boundary conditions, and the role/effect of non-negativity (simplex) on atom interpretability.
- Provide ablations varying γ and τ (and sign constraints), and include a TV-L1 or GraphNet-based dictionary-learning baseline (even on fewer subjects) to isolate the benefit of the Laplacian prior.
- Report inner-loop tolerances, number of FISTA iterations, and sensitivity of runtime to γ. If using time-varying γ_t, motivate it theoretically or empirically.
- Quantify interpretability via overlap with known atlases or sparsity/smoothness metrics, and add confidence intervals for stability and prediction metrics.
- Fix typos and improve organization; remove unclear cross-references (e.g., “line 146”).

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem and empirical scale are compelling, and the approach is practical, but the novelty is limited and the paper needs stronger motivation, clearer exposition, and more rigorous empirical comparisons to prior structured methods. Strengthening these aspects could make it a solid contribution.
