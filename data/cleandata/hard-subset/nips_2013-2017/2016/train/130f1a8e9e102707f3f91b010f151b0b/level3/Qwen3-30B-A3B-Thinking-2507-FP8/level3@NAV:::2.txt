

This paper proposes an online structured dictionary learning algorithm with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatially contiguous dictionary atoms for neuroimaging data. The authors extend Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure on dictionary atoms, resulting in what they term "Smooth-SODL." The approach is motivated by the need for more interpretable brain region extraction from fMRI data, where traditional methods either lack spatial structure (SODL) or require post-hoc thresholding (CanICA).

The technical foundation of the work is sound, building upon established online dictionary learning principles with a well-defined Laplacian regularization term. The algorithmic development appears solid, with appropriate convergence guarantees and practical implementation details. The authors provide a thorough comparison against SODL and CanICA on HCP fMRI data, addressing both theoretical and computational aspects.

However, the empirical evaluation suffers from critical weaknesses. The key claim that Sobolev regularization produces more "structured" atoms (Figure 2) is visually unsupported and lacks quantitative metrics to substantiate the assertion. The comparison across methods uses different brain slices for different algorithms, preventing direct visual assessment. The explanation of Figure 3 is unclear, with missing baselines (PCA) and ambiguous R² claims. The authors' rebuttal provided additional results that strengthened the technical quality, but these need incorporation into the final manuscript.

The paper contains numerous presentation issues: inconsistent notation (particularly in Equations 6 and 8), missing HCP dataset reference, and multiple typos. The mathematical derivation of the dictionary update (Equation 6) requires explicit presentation in the appendix. While the method shows promise for neuroscience applications, the weak empirical validation and poor presentation undermine the contribution's significance.

The novelty lies in applying Sobolev priors to online dictionary learning for neuroimaging, but this extension is relatively modest. The potential impact is promising for brain imaging applications, though the paper fails to demonstrate how the structured atoms improve downstream neuroscience analysis beyond superficial comparisons.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound approach, but the empirical support is insufficient to justify the claims, and the manuscript requires significant revision to improve clarity and presentation. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the empirical evaluation weaknesses and presentation issues.
