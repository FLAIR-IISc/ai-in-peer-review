This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The method, called Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), extends the standard online dictionary-learning framework by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on a large public dataset and show that it extracts structured and denoised dictionaries that are more interpretable and better capture inter-subject variability compared to state-of-the-art models.

The main contribution of this paper is the extension of prior work by incorporating Sobolev priors for spatial structure. This is a novel contribution that has the potential to be a useful tool for neuroscience and medical imaging. The method could also be applied to other tasks like image segmentation and regional sensing.

The technical soundness of the paper is solid, and the model definition and algorithms follow a well-established framework. However, the initial technical rating was marginally below average due to some inconsistencies and possible typos in the mathematical notation. The authors have addressed some of these issues in their rebuttal, which has raised the technical quality rating.

The empirical support for the method is weak, and the paper does not clearly demonstrate the empirical advantage of the Sobolev prior. Figure 2 does not quantify or clearly show in what way Smooth-SODL outperforms alternatives. The reviewer requests explicit, neuroscience-justified criteria and quantitative measurements showing that Smooth-SODL atoms are more structured and meaningful.

The presentation quality of the manuscript is poor, with confusing descriptions, typos, and editing issues that impede understanding. The reviewer requests that the authors revise the manuscript to address these issues.

Specific mathematical and notation issues include inconsistencies and possible typos in matrix decomposition transpose placement, signs and terms in Equations (6) and (8), and unclear variable references in updates. The reviewer requests that the authors clarify these issues and provide explicit mathematical and algorithmic solutions to Equation (6) in an appendix.

The reviewer also requests that the authors revise Figure 2 to facilitate direct comparison between different methods. The current figure shows different slices for different methods, which hinders comparison. The claim that Smooth-SODL is more "structured" is not supported visually or quantitatively.

Figure 3 is also unclear, and the reviewer requests that the authors revise the figure to explain what is being predicted, whether regression uses U_i, and provide legends and captions that explain the baselines (PCA is missing). The claims about R^2 > 0.1 are ambiguous as to which curves support this.

Minor typos and stray comments need to be fixed throughout the manuscript.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The method has the potential to be a useful tool for neuroscience and medical imaging.
* The incorporation of Sobolev priors for spatial structure is a novel contribution.
* The authors have addressed some of the technical issues raised by the reviewer in their rebuttal.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The empirical support for the method is weak.
* The presentation quality of the manuscript is poor.
* The reviewer requests that the authors revise the manuscript to address the issues raised above.

Recommendation:

I recommend that the authors revise the manuscript to address the issues raised above. Specifically, the authors should:

* Provide explicit, neuroscience-justified criteria and quantitative measurements showing that Smooth-SODL atoms are more structured and meaningful.
* Revise the manuscript to improve presentation quality and address mathematical and notation issues.
* Revise Figures 2 and 3 to facilitate direct comparison between different methods and provide clear explanations of what is being predicted.
* Fix minor typos and stray comments throughout the manuscript.

Once these issues are addressed, I believe that the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of neuroscience and medical imaging.
