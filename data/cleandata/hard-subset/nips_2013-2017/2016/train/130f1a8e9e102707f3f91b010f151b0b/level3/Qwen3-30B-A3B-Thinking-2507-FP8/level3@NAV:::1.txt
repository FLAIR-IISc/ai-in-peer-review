

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework that integrates Laplacian regularization to enforce spatial smoothness on brain imaging data, extending Mairal et al.'s SODL (2010) for structured, sparse decomposition of fMRI maps. The core idea—using inter-subject variability to define interpretable brain regions via structured sparsity—aligns with recent neuroimaging trends (e.g., Varoquaux, IPMI 2015), but the contribution is primarily algorithmic adaptation rather than conceptual innovation.  

**Strengths:** The topic is highly significant for neuroimaging, addressing a critical need for principled ROI definition beyond heuristic segmentation. The scalability claim (3× slower than SODL, handling massive datasets) is compelling, and experiments on HCP data demonstrate improved interpretability and stability over SODL/CanICA in small-to-large regimes. The practical implementation in Nilearn and clear ablation studies (e.g., learning curves showing 14× gain in sparse data) add tangible value.  

**Weaknesses:** The work suffers from severe shortcomings in *Quality* and *Clarity*. Crucially, it lacks *any* theoretical analysis—no convergence guarantees, error bounds, or justification for the Laplacian penalty’s efficacy beyond empirical results. This undermines the core claim of "non-trivial extension" (Section 2). Manuscript quality is poor: careless writing (e.g., "146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v.") and unresolved comments indicate no proofreading. While the *Significance* of the application is high, the *Originality* is marginal (a straightforward penalty addition to SODL without novel insights), and the *Clarity* deficit impedes reproducibility.  

**Verdict:** The paper tackles an important problem with promising empirical results but fails as a rigorous scientific contribution due to theoretical gaps and poor presentation. It does not advance the state of the art beyond incremental adaptation. The lack of theoretical grounding and manuscript errors prevent it from meeting NIPS standards for technical soundness.  

**Recommendation:** Weak reject. The work is not suitable for publication as-is; substantial theoretical development and meticulous revision are required.
