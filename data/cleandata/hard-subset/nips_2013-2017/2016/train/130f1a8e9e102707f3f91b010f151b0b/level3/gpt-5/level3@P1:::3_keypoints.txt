- Proposal: Smooth-SODL augments online sparse dictionary learning with a Laplacian/Sobolev prior to produce spatially compact, piecewise-smooth atoms for neuroimaging.
- Method: Codes via ridge regression; atoms updated by block coordinate descent with a Laplacian-regularized proximal step solved by a few FISTA iterations.
- Scalability: Preserves online learning scalability with minibatch support and reuse of sufficient statistics.
- Empirical setup: Evaluated on HCP task-fMRI GLM contrast maps, compared to unstructured SODL and PCA/ICA-based CanICA.
- Reported gains: Improved spatial interpretability, better early-sample data efficiency, and improved stability vs. SODL.
- Cost: Runtime overhead ≈2–3× relative to SODL but lower than batch ICA baselines.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on online sparse coding and connects to GraphNet-style smoothing; incremental novelty is integrating Laplacian prior into online DL at scale.
- Strength — algorithmic practicality: BCD + FISTA implementation and sufficient-statistics reuse are well motivated.
- Strength — prior choice: Laplacian penalty is a principled, cheaper alternative to TV for spatial coherence.
- Strength — computational narrative: Clear runtime/memory characterization and moderate overhead.
- Strength — empirical observations: Learning curves, stability analysis, and basic hyperparameter guidance are useful.
- Weakness — subjective evaluation: Heavy reliance on visual inspection of “blobs” without objective spatial-structure metrics.
- Weakness — inter-subject consistency: Lacks rigorous split-half/bootstrapped validation of atom reproducibility across subjects.
- Weakness — downstream utility unclear: CanICA has higher explained variance and better behavioral prediction; Smooth-SODL advantages are mostly qualitative and early-stage.
- Weakness — missing baselines/fairness: No comparisons to TV-ℓ1 DL, GraphNet DL variants, structured PCA, or recent online DL methods; preprocessing/thresholding inconsistencies.
- Weakness — technical gaps: Unclear handling of γ schedule vs fixed γ, insufficient evidence that 1–2 FISTA iterations suffice, and under-specified stopping/projection details.
- Quality: Optimization is coherent and correct but empirical claims need statistical testing and targeted ablations (ridge vs ℓ1, constraint choices, γ schedules).
- Clarity: Generally readable but contains typos, dense algorithmic sections, and overly visual figures that would benefit from quantitative metrics.
- Originality: Moderate — useful engineering advance but not a major conceptual leap beyond existing spatial priors in neuroimaging.
- Significance: Potentially useful for practitioners but empirical benefits over SODL/CanICA are modest and primarily qualitative.
- Suggested additions: Add objective spatial metrics (Moran’s I, connected components, atlas overlap) and report statistics across splits.
- Suggested additions: Strengthen inter-subject validation with split-half/Hungarian matching and Dice/Jaccard overlap analyses.
- Suggested additions: Expand baselines to include TV-ℓ1, GraphNet variants, structured PCA, and recent online DL methods with fair preprocessing.
- Suggested additions: Evaluate downstream behavioral prediction with strict nested CV and statistical significance testing.
- Suggested additions: Provide ablations/sensitivity analyses (ridge vs ℓ1 codes, constraint types, γ schedules, FISTA iterations) and release reproducible code.
- Acceptance arguments: Scalable, easy-to-implement extension that produces cleaner, more stable components and is practically relevant for large neuroimaging datasets.
- Rejection arguments: Evidence is largely visual/qualitative, gains over strong baselines are modest, and novelty is incremental with missing comparisons.
- Recommendation: Weak reject pending stronger objective validation, broader baselines, and more rigorous downstream evaluations.
