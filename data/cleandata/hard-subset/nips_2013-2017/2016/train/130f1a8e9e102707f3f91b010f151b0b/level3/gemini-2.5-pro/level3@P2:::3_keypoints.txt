- Proposal: extends online dictionary learning with a Laplacian (Sobolev) prior to learn sparse, spatially smooth dictionary atoms for neuroimaging.  
- Scalability: retains the scalability of Mairal et al. (2010), enabling application to massive datasets like HCP.  
- Technical soundness: clear algorithmic derivation using block-coordinate descent and FISTA, with only a small constant-factor slowdown.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and motivation for structured sparse components is clearly presented.  
- Unconvincing empirical validation: main evidence is qualitative visual comparison, which is hard to judge and undermined by reproducible major structures across models.  
- Tautological core result: adding a smoothing penalty predictably yields smoother maps but the paper fails to show these maps are better neural representations.  
- Motivation mismatch for large data: HCP is high-quality and abundant, so spatial regularization may be unnecessary; the method’s clear advantage is actually in low-data regimes.  
- Quantitative metrics not decisive: CanICA explains more variance and predicts behavior better on test data; Smooth-SODL only outperforms SODL but not the state of the art.  
- Stability gain: Smooth-SODL improves stability (0.4 vs 0.1 for SODL), which is positive but requires stronger validation (e.g., cross-subject consistency or atlas alignment).  
- Suggested remedies: provide objective biological validation (alignment with atlases or sensitive downstream tasks) or reframe paper to emphasize advantages in small-to-medium datasets.  
- Overall conclusion: an interesting, scalable, and technically solid method, but empirical evidence is insufficient to claim general superiority on large-scale data.
