This paper proposes an extension to the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) for application to neuroimaging data. The goal is to learn a dictionary of component maps, or "atoms," that are both sparse and spatially structured. To achieve this, the authors add a Laplacian penalty (a Sobolev prior) to the dictionary learning objective function, encouraging the learned atoms to be smooth and form compact "blobs" representative of functional brain regions. The proposed method, termed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), retains the scalability of the original online algorithm, which is crucial for handling massive datasets common in modern neuroimaging, such as the Human Connectome Project (HCP) data used in the experiments. The paper presents a block-coordinate descent algorithm to solve the new objective and demonstrates its performance on a large fMRI dataset, comparing it to standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA (CanICA).

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the important problem of extracting meaningful, structured components from large-scale brain imaging data in a scalable manner. The combination of an online dictionary learning framework with a spatial smoothness prior is a novel and principled approach to this problem. Previous methods for learning structured components have often been batch-based and computationally prohibitive for very large datasets, so the scalability of this method is a significant practical contribution.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed model is a well-motivated extension of an established framework. The algorithmic derivation is clear, and the authors correctly identify the subproblems and provide efficient methods (e.g., FISTA) for their solution. The analysis of the computational complexity, demonstrating only a small constant-factor slowdown compared to the unstructured version, is a strong point.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for needing structured, sparse components to define brain regions is clearly articulated, and the proposed mathematical formulation directly addresses this goal.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Unconvincing Empirical Validation:** The main weakness of the paper lies in the evaluation of the proposed method. The primary evidence for the superiority of Smooth-SODL comes from a qualitative visual comparison of the learned dictionary atoms (Fig. 2a). To an untrained eye, it is difficult to judge whether the "smoother blobs" from Smooth-SODL are genuinely more interpretable or scientifically valid than the components from other methods. In fact, the paper notes that "major structures in each atom are reproducible across the different models," which somewhat undermines the claim of significant improvement.

2.  **Tautological Core Result:** Adding a smoothing penalty to an optimization problem will, by design, produce smoother results. The paper demonstrates this expected outcome but fails to convincingly show *why* this added smoothness provides a tangible benefit over other methods. The central question is not whether the method produces smoother maps, but whether these maps are better representations of the underlying neural processes. This connection is not clearly established.

3.  **Motivation for Smoothing on Large-Scale Data:** The experiments are performed on the HCP dataset, which is large and of high quality. Spatial regularization is often motivated by the need to overcome noise or data scarcity. It is not clear that this is the primary problem for this dataset. The paper does include an excellent learning curve analysis (Table 1) that shows a dramatic improvement in the low-data regime, which is a strong result. However, this is presented as a secondary analysis, while the main results focus on the full dataset where the benefits are less pronounced. The motivation would be stronger if the paper centered on the advantages in small-to-medium-scale settings, which remain very common in neuroimaging research.

4.  **Quantitative Metrics Do Not Show a Clear Win:** The quantitative results do not consistently support the superiority of Smooth-SODL.
    *   **Explained Variance (Fig. 2b):** CanICA explains significantly more variance on both training and test data. While a penalty-based method is expected to trade some data fidelity for regularization, the gap is notable.
    *   **Prediction of Behavioral Variables (Fig. 2c):** CanICA again performs best on the test set. While Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL, it does not advance the state of the art in this predictive task.
    *   **Stability:** The stability of Smooth-SODL components (0.4) is a clear improvement over SODL (0.1), which is a good result. However, a more direct form of validation, such as showing better consistency of the identified regions across subjects or relating them more directly to known brain atlases, would be more compelling.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper presents a novel, scalable, and technically sound algorithm for an important problem. The idea of integrating a spatial structure prior into an online dictionary learning framework is elegant. The method shows very strong performance gains in the low-data regime, which is highly relevant for many neuroimaging studies.

*   **Con-acceptance:** The evidence presented for the general superiority of the method is weak. The validation relies heavily on subjective visual inspection, and the quantitative metrics fail to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing state-of-the-art methods like CanICA when using the full large-scale dataset. The core benefit of the added smoothing penalty is not sufficiently justified beyond the fact that it produces smoother outputs.

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces an interesting and potentially useful method. However, in its current form, the empirical validation is not strong enough to support the claims of superiority. The benefit of the proposed Sobolev prior is not clearly demonstrated, especially on the large-scale dataset where it is primarily tested. To strengthen the paper, the authors should either (1) provide more objective and compelling evidence that the smoother components are scientifically more valid (e.g., via better alignment with known neuroanatomy or improved performance on a more sensitive downstream task) or (2) re-frame the paper to highlight the clear advantages in the data-limited regimes, as demonstrated in their learning curve analysis.
