

# Review for "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes an extension to online dictionary learning for brain imaging data by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on the dictionary atoms, resulting in more structured "blobs" that better delineate brain regions. The authors frame their work as an extension of Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning (SODL) framework, adding a Laplacian penalty to create "Smooth-SODL" that maintains the online nature of the algorithm while imposing spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The approach is motivated by the need to define brain regions that capture inter-subject variability without relying on predefined ROIs.

The paper relates to previous work in structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging, particularly the work by Varoquaux et al. on multi-subject dictionary learning for brain activity mapping. It also connects to broader literature on structured sparsity in machine learning (e.g., Jenatton et al. for structured sparse PCA, and Grosenick et al. for GraphNet).

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-motivated, with a clear algorithmic extension to the SODL framework. The authors provide convergence guarantees and demonstrate that the Laplacian penalty can be efficiently incorporated with minimal computational overhead (only 2-3x slower than SODL). However, the experimental validation lacks sufficient rigor. The paper primarily relies on visual comparisons of brain maps, which are subjective and difficult to evaluate for an untrained reader. The metrics used (explained variance, reproducibility, prediction performance) are standard, but the comparisons fail to convincingly demonstrate that the spatial regularization provides meaningful improvements over existing methods, particularly given the large dataset used (HCP with 500 subjects). The claim that the method "better captures inter-subject variability" is not adequately supported by statistical validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly describes the algorithmic extension. The presentation of the Laplacian penalty and its integration into the online dictionary learning framework is technically sound. However, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility, particularly regarding the hyperparameter selection process and the specific implementation details of the spatial regularization. The visual comparisons (Fig. 2) are helpful but do not adequately convey the claimed benefits to readers unfamiliar with neuroimaging.

**Originality:** The core idea of incorporating a Sobolev penalty into online dictionary learning is novel in the context of brain imaging applications. However, the novelty is somewhat limited as similar structured penalties have been used in other domains (e.g., total variation regularization in image processing). The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from related work in structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging (e.g., the work by Varoquaux on multi-subject dictionary learning, and the TV-`1 approach by Abraham et al.). The main contribution appears to be the adaptation of structured dictionary learning to the online setting for large-scale brain imaging data.

**Significance:** The significance of the work is questionable. The paper claims that the method better captures inter-subject variability, but this is not convincingly demonstrated. With large datasets like the Human Connectome Project, the data scarcity problem that structured methods typically address is less relevant, making the added complexity of spatial regularization less compelling. The paper fails to show that the proposed method provides tangible benefits over simpler approaches (like SODL) that are already widely used in the field. The claimed improvements in interpretability are subjective and not validated through quantitative metrics that would be meaningful to the broader machine learning community.

**Strengths:** The algorithmic extension is technically sound and well-executed, with minimal computational overhead. The implementation is made available in the Nilearn library, enhancing reproducibility. The paper demonstrates the method's scalability on large datasets, which is valuable for neuroimaging applications.

**Weaknesses:** The main weakness is the lack of convincing evidence that the proposed method provides meaningful improvements over existing approaches. The validation relies heavily on visual inspection of brain maps, which is subjective and not sufficient for a scientific contribution. The paper does not adequately address whether the spatial regularization is necessary for large datasets where data scarcity is not an issue. The comparison with CanICA is limited as it uses a hard-thresholded version of CanICA (tCanICA), which is not a fair comparison to the original method.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
- *Pro:* The paper presents a technically sound extension of online dictionary learning with spatial structure, which is well-executed and implemented in an open-source library.
- *Con:* The claimed benefits are not convincingly demonstrated, the validation is subjective, and the significance is questionable given the large dataset used.

**Overall assessment:** While the algorithmic extension is technically sound, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method over existing approaches, particularly in the context of large-scale neuroimaging datasets. The validation is insufficiently rigorous, relying on subjective visual comparisons rather than robust statistical validation. Given the conference's emphasis on scientific contributions with clear impact, I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit from additional experiments that quantitatively demonstrate the advantages of the spatial regularization, particularly in scenarios where data scarcity is an issue (e.g., smaller datasets), and a more rigorous validation framework that goes beyond visual inspection of brain maps.
