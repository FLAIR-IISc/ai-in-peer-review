This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images into structured and sparse components, aiming to identify brain regions directly from data without relying on predefined regions of interest. The approach combines sparsity-inducing L1 norms with a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially smooth and compact dictionary atoms (blobs), extending previous online dictionary learning work (Mairal et al., 2010). The authors demonstrate the method on fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project, showing improved interpretability and ability to capture inter-subject variability compared to ICA and standard online dictionary learning.

The idea of learning brain regions directly from data, rather than relying on predefined atlases or ROIs, is a valuable and actively researched area in neuroimaging. Existing approaches often struggle with the high dimensionality of brain imaging data and the need for computationally efficient methods for large-scale datasets. The proposed online dictionary learning framework addresses this challenge by scaling to arbitrarily sized datasets. The combination of sparsity and smoothness constraints is also a sensible approach for identifying localized brain regions. The work builds upon and extends the well-known online dictionary learning method of Mairal et al. (2010), demonstrating a clear connection to prior work. Related approaches like clustering (referenced in the introduction) and ICA are appropriately discussed as points of comparison.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. A significant concern is the lack of robust theoretical analysis. While the authors mention convergence guarantees based on previous work (Bertsekas, 1999; Mairal et al., 2010), there's no discussion of the theoretical properties of *this specific* combination of L1 and Laplacian penalties. What guarantees can be made about the recovery of meaningful structures given this regularization? How does the choice of regularization parameters impact these guarantees? Without theoretical justification, it is difficult to assess the reliability of the proposed method. 

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, feels somewhat superficial. While comparisons to CanICA and standard SODL are valuable, the choice of metrics could be improved. Beyond explained variance and prediction of behavioral variables, it would be helpful to see comparisons based on functional connectivity analyses or the alignment of learned regions with known anatomical structures. The ablation study focusing on the impact of the Laplacian penalty is beneficial, but could be expanded. The paper also states that the algorithm is only “slightly” slower than SODL (a factor of 2 or 3), but this runtime increase is not trivial and a more detailed breakdown of computational cost would be useful. The statement about random initialization producing stable results is reassuring, but a formal sensitivity analysis would strengthen this claim.

Perhaps the most concerning issue is the manuscript's poor quality. The authors acknowledge the importance of careful evaluation, yet the writing is often unclear and contains instances of awkward phrasing. The reference to leftover comments ("146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v.") is particularly alarming and suggests a lack of thorough proofreading. While the ideas themselves are promising, the presentation undermines confidence in the authors' attention to detail.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in neuroimaging – identifying brain regions from data.
*   Proposes a scalable online dictionary learning framework that extends existing work.
*   Demonstrates promising results on a large-scale fMRI dataset.
*   Combines sparsity and smoothness constraints in a theoretically motivated way (although lacking detailed analysis).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed method.
*   Superficial experimental evaluation with limited metrics.
*   Poor manuscript quality and lack of polish, including visible editing remnants.
*   The improvement over existing methods (particularly SODL) is not overwhelmingly significant, given the increased computational cost.



**Overall assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the core idea is interesting and potentially valuable, the lack of theoretical analysis, superficial evaluation, and poor manuscript quality are significant concerns. The paper requires substantial revision to address these issues before it would be suitable for publication in NIPS. Specifically, the authors should focus on providing a theoretical justification for their approach, conducting a more thorough experimental evaluation with a wider range of metrics, and carefully proofreading the manuscript to ensure clarity and correctness.
