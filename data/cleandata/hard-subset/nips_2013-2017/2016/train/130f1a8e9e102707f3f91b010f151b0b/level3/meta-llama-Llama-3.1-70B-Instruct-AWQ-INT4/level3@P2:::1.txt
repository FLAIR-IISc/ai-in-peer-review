This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method, dubbed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The method extends the standard online dictionary-learning framework by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a large public dataset, showing improvements over traditional `1-regularized dictionary learning.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere in the field of neuroimaging and dictionary learning. The authors draw inspiration from the work of Mairal et al. (2010) on online dictionary learning and extend it to incorporate spatial structure. They also compare their approach to other methods, such as Canonical ICA (CanICA) and sparse online dictionary learning (SODL).

The strengths of the paper include its ability to extract structured and denoised dictionaries that are more interpretable and better capture inter-subject variability in small, medium, and large-scale regimes. The authors also provide a comprehensive evaluation of their method, including qualitative and quantitative metrics, such as visual quality of the dictionaries, explained variance, stability of the dictionary atoms, and prediction of behavioral variables.

However, the paper lacks theoretical analysis to demonstrate the proposed algorithm's effectiveness. The manuscript quality is also poor, with careless writing and leftover comments indicating that the authors did not proofread the paper carefully.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on significance, as it addresses a difficult problem in neuroimaging and provides a novel solution. The paper also scores well on originality, as it extends the standard online dictionary-learning framework to incorporate spatial structure. However, the paper scores poorly on quality, as the theoretical analysis is lacking, and the manuscript quality is poor. The paper scores moderately on clarity, as the writing is clear, but the organization could be improved.
