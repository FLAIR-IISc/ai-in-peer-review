- Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online multivariate dictionary learning method with a Sobolev penalty to enforce spatial smoothness and compactness in brain image atoms.  
- Extends online dictionary learning [Mairal et al., 2010] to represent brain regions as smooth “blobs” and retain inter-subject variability for ROI definition.  
- Situates work within dictionary learning, sparse coding, PCA/ICA, clustering, and spatial regularization literature; connects to TV/GraphNet approaches.  
- Technical soundness: method builds on established framework, uses BCD with FISTA, and the Sobolev prior is logically motivated.  
- Novelty: combining online dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior for scalable, spatially structured decomposition is a new contribution.  
- Potential significance: could aid principled ROI definition, capture inter-subject variability, and produce interpretable, localized features for applications like segmentation and disease characterization.  
- Scalability: online algorithm can handle arbitrarily large neuroimaging datasets.  
- Empirical evaluation weak: Figure 2 lacked quantitative comparisons and clear visual evidence that the Sobolev prior improves structure or interpretability.  
- Presentation and clarity issues: numerous typos, notation inconsistencies (e.g., matrix transposes, signs in eqs. 6 and 8), and confusing descriptions impair understanding.  
- Missing algorithmic details: derivation/solution for Equation (6) not provided; needs clearer relation to GraphNet or appendix derivation.  
- Figure 2 limitations: inconsistent slices across methods prevent direct visual comparison; claims of improved structure need quantitative metrics.  
- Figure 3 ambiguities: unclear prediction target, unexplained use of U_i, missing PCA baseline in legend, and vague R^2 claim.  
- Reference omission: missing citation for the Human Connectome Project [Van Essen et al., 2013].  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses ROI problem, technically sound and novel, scalable, and rebuttal added helpful results/clarifications.  
- Arguments against acceptance: initial empirical results unconvincing, manuscript clarity and presentation problems, and incomplete algorithmic details.  
- Overall: promising and potentially valuable contribution but initially hampered by weak experiments and presentation; rebuttal improvements are encouraging.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions, including incorporating rebuttal results, proofreading, providing Eq.(6) derivation, revising Figure 2 for consistent slices and quantitative metrics, clarifying Figure 3, and adding the missing HCP reference.
