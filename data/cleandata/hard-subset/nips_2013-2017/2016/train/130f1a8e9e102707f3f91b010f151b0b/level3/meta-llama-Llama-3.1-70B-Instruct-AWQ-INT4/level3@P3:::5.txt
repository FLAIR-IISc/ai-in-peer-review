This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The method, dubbed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), extends the standard online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) with a Laplacian penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms.

The authors provide a clear motivation for their work, highlighting the limitations of existing methods in handling large-scale brain imaging data and the need for a method that can extract structured and sparse components from such data. They also provide a thorough description of their proposed method, including the formulation of the optimization problem, the algorithm for solving it, and the convergence guarantees.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors do a good job of explaining the technical details of their method. However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. First, the novelty of the method is not entirely clear, as it builds upon existing work in online dictionary learning. While the addition of the Laplacian penalty is a new contribution, it is not entirely obvious how this changes the existing framework. Second, the motivation for the Laplacian penalty could be more clearly explained. The authors mention that it is used to impose "structure" on the dictionary atoms, but it is not entirely clear what this means or why it is necessary.

The paper also contains some errors and areas for improvement. For example, in Equation (1), the notation should likely read [V^1, V^2, …, V^r]^T. Additionally, the reference to "line 146" is unclear and needs clarification. Finally, the paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the optimization problem and the choice of the l2 norm on the codes instead of the l1 norm used in Mairal et al. (2010).

Despite these limitations, the paper presents a solid contribution to the field of brain imaging and dictionary learning. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on a large-scale dataset and provide a thorough evaluation of its performance compared to existing methods. The paper is well-written, and the authors do a good job of explaining the technical details of their method.
