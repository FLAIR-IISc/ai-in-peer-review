## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes extending online dictionary learning with a Sobolev (Laplacian) prior to encourage spatially smooth and compact dictionary atoms, aiming to better delineate brain structures from fMRI data. The motivation, addressing the challenge of defining Regions of Interest (ROIs) in neuroimaging, is well-articulated, building on existing work in clustering and sparse decomposition methods like ICA and PCA. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches and position their work as a scalable alternative that incorporates spatial consistency. 

The core idea of combining sparsity and smoothness constraints is interesting and logically sound – imposing a Laplacian penalty should naturally lead to more interpretable “blobs” representing brain regions. However, the paper struggles to convincingly demonstrate the *benefit* of this approach. The primary results rely heavily on visual comparison of the learned dictionaries, which is subjective and insufficient for a rigorous evaluation. While the authors show examples where the proposed method produces smoother blobs, it’s unclear if this smoothness reflects genuine underlying structure in the data or simply an artifact of the regularization. 

A significant weakness is the lack of consolidation of brain-region differences *across* subjects. The paper focuses on individual decompositions and doesn't leverage inter-subject consistency as a validation metric. Given the large-scale dataset (HCP), one would expect to see evidence of reproducible regions across individuals, and the proposed method’s ability to identify these consistent structures should be a key evaluation point. The quantitative results, while showing comparable running time to existing methods, don’t strongly support the claim of improved performance. The reported gains in explained variance are modest, and the behavioral prediction results, while positive, don’t definitively isolate the contribution of the spatial regularization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging (ROI definition).
*   Presents a technically sound extension to existing online dictionary learning methods.
*   Scalable approach suitable for large datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Evaluation is primarily visual and subjective.
*   Lack of inter-subject consistency analysis.
*   Limited demonstration of significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The impact of the proposed method remains unclear – smoothing the blobs doesn’t necessarily equate to discovering meaningful brain regions.



**Overall:** While the paper presents a technically valid contribution, the lack of robust evaluation and unclear significance prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The authors should focus on incorporating inter-subject analysis and providing more quantitative evidence to support their claims. A more compelling demonstration of the practical benefits of the proposed method is needed to justify its inclusion in a competitive conference like NIPS.
