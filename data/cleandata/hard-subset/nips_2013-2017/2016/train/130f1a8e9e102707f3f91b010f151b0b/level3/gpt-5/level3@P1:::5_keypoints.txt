- Proposal: Smooth-SODL augments online dictionary learning with a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty on atoms to produce spatially smooth, blob-like components, using ridge-coded codes and projected FISTA updates.  
- Relation to prior work: positioned between Mairal et al. (2010) online sparse coding and structured neuroimaging methods (GraphNet/TV-ℓ1); pragmatic integration rather than a large conceptual leap.  
- Strength: scalable, practical algorithm with modest runtime overhead (≈2–3× SODL) and inherited convergence guarantees under convex subproblems.  
- Strength: empirical gains—more localized, visually cleaner components and improved split-half stability, especially in low-data regimes.  
- Strength: sensible discussion of practical issues (regularization scheduling, mini-batching, hyperparameter guidance) and large public-dataset experiments (HCP) including EV, behavioral prediction, and runtime.  
- Strength: open-source implementation planned within Nilearn, aiding reproducibility and adoption.  
- Weakness: novelty is limited—essentially SODL + smoothness penalty and projected first-order updates; lacks deeper theory or substantial conceptual advance.  
- Weakness: “structured” is ill-defined—need a precise definition (e.g., Sobolev seminorm, TV, bounded TV) and justification for Laplacian vs TV/GraphNet/morphological constraints.  
- Weakness: choice of ridge (ℓ2) coding is under-justified; must provide principled rationale and empirical ablation vs ℓ1/elastic net on stability, EV, prediction, runtime.  
- Weakness: notation and presentation errors (row/column inconsistencies, min/max mistakes, unclear line references, multiple typos) reduce clarity and confidence.  
- Algorithmic clarity needed: explicitly state smooth vs nonsmooth terms in atom update, projection/proximal details, and handling of Δ boundary conditions and brain mask/voxel spacing.  
- Convergence concerns: claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e-6 accuracy is not credible—report inner residuals, principled stopping rules, and warm-start behavior.  
- γ scheduling concerns: Eq. (9) heuristic—provide formal argument or empirical evidence of its effect on step sizes and convergence; state whether backtracking is used.  
- Evaluation fairness: mixed preprocessing (CanICA on smoothed data, SODL on unsmoothed) is unfair—run all methods with/without smoothing or justify the choice.  
- Statistical reporting: missing error bars, statistical tests, split details, and Hungarian matching criteria for stability/EV/prediction results.  
- Modeling constraint concern: nonnegativity/simplex constraints may bias representation of negative effects—report results with signed atoms (ℓ1-ball without nonnegativity).  
- Broader validation needed: test on additional tasks/resting-state datasets, robustness to registration variability, and comparisons of Laplacian vs TV regularization.  
- Complexity: lacking per-iteration time/memory complexity in terms of p, k, batch size; clarify FFT variant applicability and conditions.  
- Quality assessment: components are standard and sound if clarified, but key modeling choices and implementation details are under-justified.  
- Clarity assessment: presentation needs major polishing—consistent vector conventions, corrected typos, and explicit separation of optimization terms.  
- Originality assessment: incremental contribution; to strengthen, add theoretical insights (identifiability/stability) or broader/superior empirical evidence.  
- Significance: potentially useful for neuroimaging practice if robustly validated, but current predictive/EV gains are inconsistent and validation limited.  
- Key questions for authors: why ridge codes vs lasso/elastic net; how are Δ boundary conditions and voxel anisotropy handled; precise projected FISTA scheme and stopping criteria; effect of nonnegativity on negative effects; sensitivity analyses for γ and τ and TV vs Laplacian.  
- Pros for acceptance: scalable, modest overhead algorithm; improved interpretability and stability; practical connection to widely used online sparse coding tools.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited novelty, incomplete justification of modeling choices, mixed preprocessing in comparisons, presentation/notation issues, and lack of thorough statistical validation.  
- Overall assessment: borderline to reject—could be a solid neuroimaging contribution with clearer motivation, stronger empirical validation/ablations, and improved exposition.
