This paper proposes a method for learning spatially structured functional brain components from fMRI data. The approach is based on an under-complete, online dictionary learning framework. The primary contribution is the introduction of a combined regularization scheme for the dictionary atoms: an L1-norm constraint is used to enforce sparsity, while a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty is added to encourage spatial smoothness and contiguity of the learned components. This extends the well-known online dictionary learning algorithm of Mairal et al. (2010) to incorporate spatial structure, with the goal of producing more interpretable, region-like components from large-scale neuroimaging datasets. The online nature of the algorithm ensures scalability, which is demonstrated on a large dataset of 500 subjects from the Human Connectome Project.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Motivation:** The combination of Sobolev and L1 regularization within a scalable, online dictionary learning framework is a novel and well-motivated approach for fMRI analysis. The goal of learning components that are simultaneously sparse and spatially coherent aligns well with the need for interpretable, data-driven regions of interest in neuroimaging. Bridging the gap between efficient online matrix factorization methods and structured decomposition techniques, which are often computationally expensive, is a valuable contribution.

2.  **Significance and Scalability:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of modeling inter-subject variability in large neuroimaging cohorts. By building upon an online learning framework, the proposed method is inherently scalable, which is a crucial feature for modern datasets containing thousands of subjects. The authors demonstrate that the additional computational cost of the smoothness penalty is modest (a 2-3x increase in runtime), making the approach practical for large-scale applications.

3.  **Problem Formulation:** The problem is clearly formulated as a principled optimization problem (Eq. 2), and the proposed block-coordinate descent algorithm is a sound and appropriate method for solving it.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Empirical Gains:** While the idea is compelling, the experimental results do not provide strong evidence for the superiority of the proposed method over simpler baselines.
    *   In the explained variance analysis (Fig. 2b), the proposed Smooth-SODL achieves a modest improvement over the standard SODL baseline (55% vs. 49% on test data) but is still outperformed by CanICA (60%).
    *   In the behavioral prediction task (Fig. 2c), the performance gains over SODL are small and inconsistent across different cognitive scores. Again, CanICA appears to be the strongest performer on test data for this downstream task.
    *   The authors argue that the advantage of spatial regularization is most pronounced in low-data regimes, presenting learning curve results in Table 1 (which is referenced but not included in the manuscript). However, the main results presented are for a large-data regime (n=500), where the benefits are marginal. The claim that the method is superior in "small, medium, and large-scale regimes alike" seems overstated based on the figures provided.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis and Discussion:** The paper lacks a deep analysis of its experimental findings. Given the minor performance improvements, a discussion of why the added spatial prior does not lead to more substantial gains in generalization (e.g., on test set variance or prediction) would be crucial. Is the Laplacian penalty the right form of spatial prior? Is the effect simply a cosmetic smoothing of the components without improving their functional relevance? The qualitative comparison in Fig. 2a shows that the Smooth-SODL maps are visually "cleaner" than SODL, but it is unclear if this visual improvement translates to more meaningful neuroscientific insight. The discussion remains superficial on these key points.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript appears to be in a preliminary, rushed state, which significantly hinders readability and confidence in the work.
    *   The reference section is incomplete, with many entries missing publication year, venue, or page numbers. This is a significant oversight for a conference submission.
    *   The writing is often dense and confusing. For instance, the derivation of the dictionary update in Eq. (7) is abbreviated, with key details deferred to another paper.
    *   The overall presentation lacks polish, with informal language (e.g., "choosing γ 'too large' will provably cause the dictionary updates to eventually take forever to run") and awkward formatting (e.g., a footnote used in place of a standard citation for the FISTA solver). These issues make the paper tedious to read and evaluate.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

The paper introduces an interesting and well-motivated extension to online dictionary learning for neuroimaging data analysis. The idea of combining sparsity and spatial smoothness in a scalable algorithm is a valuable research direction. However, the current manuscript is not ready for publication. The primary concerns are the unconvincing experimental results, which show only marginal benefits over a simpler baseline, and the poor quality of the presentation, which suggests the work was rushed. The lack of in-depth analysis of the results further weakens the contribution.

While the core idea has merit, the paper would need a major revision to be acceptable. This would involve a more thorough experimental evaluation, potentially including different datasets or tasks where the benefit of the spatial prior is clearer, a deeper analysis of the results, and a complete overhaul of the manuscript to improve clarity, correctness, and professionalism.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated combination of Sobolev and L1 regularization in a scalable online dictionary learning framework.
*   Addresses an important and relevant problem in large-scale neuroimaging analysis.
*   The algorithmic approach is sound and provides a practical solution.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Experimental results show only minor improvements over the most relevant baseline (SODL).
*   Lack of analysis and discussion regarding the limited performance gains.
*   The manuscript is poorly written and appears rushed, with an incomplete reference list and unclear technical descriptions, harming its scientific quality and reproducibility.
