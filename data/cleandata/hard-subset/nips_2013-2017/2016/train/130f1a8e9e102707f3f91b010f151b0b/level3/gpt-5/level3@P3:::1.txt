Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for multivariate decomposition of brain images into sparse, spatially structured atoms. The key idea is to regularize dictionary atoms with both a sparsity-inducing constraint (e.g., L1-ball or simplex) and a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty to encourage piecewise smooth, blob-like spatial patterns. Codes are computed via ridge regression; the dictionary is updated by block-coordinate descent, solving per-atom convex subproblems with a FISTA inner loop. The method extends Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (SODL) of Mairal et al. (2010) to incorporate spatial structure at modest computational overhead. Experiments on HCP task fMRI compare against CanICA and unstructured SODL, reporting better interpretability and stability with competitive explained variance and reasonable runtime.

This work sits at the intersection of online sparse coding [Mairal 2010; Mensch 2016], structured sparsity and spatial regularization in neuroimaging (TV-L1 and GraphNet-style penalties [Abraham MICCAI 2013; Grosenick NeuroImage]), and multi-subject decompositions (CanICA, MELODIC, group ICA). The move from TV to Laplacian/Tikhonov smoothing trades sharper edges for faster, scalable updates, positioning the contribution as a practical, scalable variant of earlier structured approaches.

Strengths
- Addresses an important need in neuroimaging: scalable learning of interpretable, spatially localized components/ROIs from large cohorts.
- Sound optimization design: per-atom convex subproblems with a simple FISTA solver; retains the online, memory-efficient flavor of SODL.
- Empirical gains in interpretability and stability over unstructured SODL and ICA-based baselines; clear qualitative improvements in spatial structure.
- Sensible large-scale setup on HCP with several evaluation axes (explained variance, stability, runtime, behavioral prediction).
- Runtime overhead is modest (≈2–3× SODL) and far below single-batch baselines.

Weaknesses
- Lack of theoretical analysis specific to the proposed algorithm. Beyond inheriting assumptions from SODL, there is no formal convergence/stationarity guarantee for the alternating scheme with Laplacian-regularized, constrained atoms, no rate, and no analysis of the proposed time-varying schedules for αt and γt (Eq. 9–10). Claims like “1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e-6 accuracy” are not credible without justification and tolerance-sensitivity experiments.
- Manuscript quality is poor. Numerous typos and grammatical errors, leftover internal comments (e.g., “146 XXX: I don’t understand the switch between V and v.”), inconsistent notation (V vs v; Aj vs A[j,j]; “max” where “min” is intended), and copy-paste artifacts (“th Conference …” in the main text) suggest the paper was not proofread.
- Experimental methodology has gaps:
  - Fairness of baselines: CanICA is evaluated both dense and hard-thresholded (tCanICA), while SODL/Smooth-SODL are inherently sparse; pre-smoothing differs across methods. Include PCA/ICA with matched pre-processing and sparse post-processing as a controlled baseline, and compare against TV-L1 dictionary learning [Abraham 2013] or GraphNet-based decompositions on subsets where feasible.
  - Coding penalty: the method uses ridge codes; since SODL commonly uses L1 codes, a direct comparison of ridge vs L1 coding within Smooth-SODL is needed.
  - Reproducibility and statistics: no error bars, confidence intervals, or significance tests; insufficient details on data splits, matching procedure, and stability calculations.
- Missing implementation details: construction of ∆ (grid spacing, boundary conditions, masking), projection onto the simplex vs L1-ball (non-negativity may be inappropriate for signed Z-maps), stopping criteria for inner FISTA, memory/runtime scaling with p and k.
- Some claims are overstated or unclear (e.g., “parameter free” BCD; “global optimum at each iteration” is true only for the per-atom convex subproblem, not the full nonconvex objective).

Quality
- Technical soundness: Reasonable optimization design but insufficient theoretical backing for the new regularization and schedules. Empirical results are promising but need stronger controls and statistical support.
- Completeness: The work is more an engineering extension than a theoretically grounded contribution; ablations and baseline coverage are incomplete.

Clarity
- Organization is acceptable, but the writing and notation issues significantly impede comprehension. Figures and tables referenced (e.g., Fig. 1, Tab. 1) need clearer presentation and captions with precise metrics and variability.

Originality
- The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian prior on atoms is a practical and useful twist, though conceptually incremental relative to SODL and prior structured penalties (TV/GraphNet). The main novelty is computational/practical.

Significance
- If cleaned up and better validated, the approach could see adoption for large-cohort ROI discovery. Current empirical evidence suggests usefulness, but broader impact requires stronger baselines, ablations, and theory.

Specific suggestions for improvement
- Provide a convergence/stationarity analysis for the alternating scheme with Laplacian-regularized, constrained atoms (e.g., show monotonic decrease and convergence to critical points under standard assumptions). Justify or remove the γt and αt schedules; if retained, analyze their effect on convergence and performance.
- Add ablations: ridge vs L1 codes; with/without simplex (non-negativity) constraint; varying γ and τ with quantitative trade-offs; effect of FISTA tolerance/iterations on solution quality and runtime.
- Strengthen baselines: TV-L1 structured dictionary learning on smaller subsets, GraphNet-based decompositions, and matched PCA/ICA pipelines (with/without smoothing and sparsity post-processing).
- Report error bars and statistical tests for EV, stability, prediction R2; include multiple random splits and seeds; release code and exact scripts (not “soon”).
- Clarify implementation: Laplacian discretization and boundary conditions; masking; FFT-based updates; memory scaling; stopping criteria; Hungarian matching details; brain-space normalization.
- Thorough proofreading: fix typos, leftover comments, notation consistency (V vs v; A indices), and correct “max”→“min” typos.

Questions for the authors
- How is ∆ constructed (voxel spacing, boundary, masking)? Is it isotropic? Any effect on learned atoms?
- Why enforce non-negativity via the simplex for Z-maps that can be signed? Have you tried L1-ball without Rp+?
- How sensitive are results to the inner FISTA tolerance? How many iterations are actually used per atom per update?
- Can you provide comparisons with L1-coded SODL and Smooth-SODL?
- What is the memory footprint for p≈10^6, k≈100?

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant, large-scale neuroimaging problem with a practical, scalable extension of a widely used method.
- Clear qualitative gains in interpretability and stability; modest runtime overhead.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient theoretical analysis specific to the proposed algorithm and schedules.
- Poor manuscript quality and numerous presentation errors.
- Incomplete and potentially unfair experimental comparisons; lack of statistical rigor and missing implementation details.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is promising and likely valuable to practitioners, but the current manuscript’s theoretical, experimental, and presentation shortcomings are substantial. With a careful rewrite, stronger baselines, ablations, and at least a basic convergence/stationarity analysis, this work could be competitive in a future submission.
