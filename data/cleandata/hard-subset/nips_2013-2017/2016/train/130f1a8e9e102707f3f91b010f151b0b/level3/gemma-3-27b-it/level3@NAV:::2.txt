## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a novel online dictionary learning algorithm, Smooth-SODL, extending prior work by Mairal et al. (2010) with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatially contiguous dictionary atoms for neuroimaging data. The motivation, leveraging inter-subject variability to define brain regions, is well-grounded in the neuroimaging literature, addressing the challenge of principled ROI definition. The approach builds upon established methods like ICA and PCA, aiming to improve interpretability and capture variability through sparsity and spatial structure. 

The technical foundation appears solid, utilizing a block coordinate descent approach based on existing optimization techniques. However, the initial technical assessment was marginally below average, and this reviewer agrees. While the model definition generally follows a logical framework, the presentation suffers from inconsistencies and typos – specifically regarding matrix transpose placement and terms within Equations (6) and (8), hindering a clear understanding of the algorithmic details. The missing reference to the Human Connectome Project dataset (HCP) is also a notable omission. The rebuttal significantly improved the technical rating through additional results, which *should* be incorporated into the revision. 

The primary weakness remains the lack of compelling empirical evidence demonstrating a clear advantage of the Sobolev prior. Figure 2, intended to showcase the superiority of SSMF, fails to provide quantitative metrics or a direct visual comparison (different slices are displayed). The claim of “more structured” atoms is subjective without concrete measurement. While the reported prediction results (Fig. 2c) are promising, the explanation of the baselines is incomplete (PCA is missing), and the significance of R^2 values > 0.1 is ambiguous. Furthermore, the explanation of what is being predicted is unclear. 

The rebuttal addressed some of these concerns, but the refined results need to be integrated into the manuscript. Explicit, neuroscience-justified criteria for evaluating “structuredness” and quantitative measurements (e.g., spatial autocorrelation, compactness) should be included. The algorithmic solution to Equation (6), as requested, would enhance clarity. Addressing the typographical errors and inconsistencies is crucial. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** The method offers a potentially valuable tool for neuroimaging, with possible applications to image segmentation and regional sensing. The online learning framework ensures scalability.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Weak empirical support for the Sobolev prior, unclear presentation, and mathematical inconsistencies.

**Overall:** This work presents an interesting approach with a promising idea. However, the paper requires substantial revision to address the weaknesses in empirical validation, clarity, and technical correctness before it can be considered for acceptance. The improvements demonstrated in the rebuttal are encouraging and should be fully integrated into a revised version.
