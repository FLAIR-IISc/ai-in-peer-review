- Proposes Smooth-SODL: a multivariate online dictionary learning method for brain images combining sparsity with a Sobolev (Laplacian) spatial-smoothness penalty.  
- Aims to extract meaningful brain regions by leveraging inter-subject variability rather than discarding it.  
- Builds on online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) and demonstrates scalability on the Human Connectome Project fMRI dataset.  
- Conceptually aligns with related neuroimaging work (TV-constrained dictionary learning, structured sparse PCA) and situates itself relative to ICA/PCA.  
- Technical formulation and algorithm (block coordinate descent) are well-defined, with convergence discussion included.  
- Authors provide hyperparameter tuning guidance, a data-adaptive ridge parameter, implementation details, and plan to release code (Nilearn).  
- Online learning aspect is a clear strength, enabling application to large datasets.  
- Primary results rely heavily on visual inspection of dictionary atoms, making objective assessment difficult and subjective for non-experts.  
- Quantitative metrics (explained variance, behavioral prediction) do not convincingly show clear benefits over competing methods.  
- Comparison shows CanICA attains higher explained variance, trading interpretability for variance capture.  
- Predictive performance improvements are modest and not compelling.  
- Claim that spatial regularization is particularly beneficial with limited data is not convincingly demonstrated given the large HCP dataset used.  
- Paper lacks experiments varying dataset size to justify the Laplacian penalty’s added complexity.  
- Validation of extracted regions is weak: little evidence of alignment with anatomical atlases or consistency across subjects.  
- Reproducibility discussion is limited and insufficiently detailed.  
- Acceptance arguments: technically sound extension, useful sparsity+structure combination, scalable online approach, and public code release.  
- Rejection arguments: subjective primary results, unproven benefits of spatial regularization for large datasets, limited biological validation, and modest predictive gains.  
- Overall assessment: weak accept pending stronger validation and clearer demonstration of practical benefits; recommend revisions to address validation, dataset-size experiments, and reproducibility.
