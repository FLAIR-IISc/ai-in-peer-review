This paper introduces Smooth-SODL, a novel online dictionary learning method for analyzing large-scale neuroimaging data. The key contribution is a new regularizer that extends the framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by combining a standard l1 penalty for sparsity with a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness in the dictionary atoms. This approach is designed to extract more interpretable and structured brain regions (or 'atoms') from functional images, addressing a significant challenge in the field of data-driven functional neuroimaging.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its strong and convincing empirical validation. The proposed method demonstrates superior performance over established state-of-the-art techniques like standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA on a large-scale fMRI dataset. The resulting dictionary atoms are qualitatively more interpretable—appearing as coherent, smooth blobs rather than scattered patterns—and show better reproducibility. The authors convincingly show that the spatial regularization is particularly beneficial in low-data regimes, highlighting the method's potential significance for a wide range of studies. The online nature of the algorithm makes it scalable and practical, a crucial advantage over slower, batch-based structured decomposition methods.

The main weakness of the work is its reliance on empirical evidence over theoretical analysis. The objective function is non-convex, and the paper does not provide proofs for the existence or uniqueness of a solution for its proposed algorithm. While the algorithm is well-motivated and leverages established block-coordinate descent techniques, it lacks formal guarantees beyond convergence to a local minimum. The initial manuscript also suffered from some clarity issues; specifically, the derivation of the dictionary update step (around line 146, concerning Algorithm 2) was not fully transparent. However, the authors' rebuttal provided a satisfactory clarification of this point, addressing my primary concern regarding the algorithm's implementation and correctness. As a result, I have raised my score for the paper's clarity from 2 to 3.

In summary, while the paper could be strengthened by a more thorough theoretical treatment, its novel formulation, practical utility, and impressive experimental results represent a significant contribution. The proposed method successfully balances model interpretability with computational scalability, and I recommend acceptance.
