- Proposes an online multivariate dictionary-learning method for brain images combining l1 sparsity and a Sobolev (Laplacian) smoothness penalty.  
- Builds on Mairal et al. (2010) online dictionary learning and targets scalable ROI discovery from fMRI population data.  
- Positions approach as a data-driven, linear-decomposition alternative to clustering/ICA/PCA for defining brain ROIs.  
- Strength: scalable online framework suitable for large neuroimaging datasets.  
- Strength: sparsity plus smoothness aims to yield more biologically plausible, interpretable atoms than standard ICA.  
- Strength: empirical results on HCP data show competitive explained variance and behavioral prediction, outperforming SODL and sometimes CanICA.  
- Strength: implementation planned for release in Nilearn, aiding reproducibility and adoption.  
- Weakness: incremental novelty—adding a Laplacian penalty to existing online dictionary learning is modest.  
- Weakness: weak justification for using Sobolev/Laplacian regularization and insufficiently defined notion of desirable structure.  
- Weakness: unexplained choice of l2 norm on codes (Ui) despite original l1 usage in prior work.  
- Weakness: poor writing, typos, and unclear technical statements (e.g., reference to "line 146", likely notational errors in Eq.(1)).  
- Weakness: hyperparameter tuning discussion is superficial; interactions of α, γ, τ not thoroughly explored.  
- Weakness: related-work comparison is limited; lacks depth on spatially regularized dictionary methods (e.g., TV-l1).  
- Argument for acceptance: offers scalable, interpretable ROI definition with strong empirical results on a large public dataset.  
- Argument against acceptance: limited novelty, weak motivation for regularization choice, poor presentation, and insufficient technical depth.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound but incrementally novel and hindered by presentation and justification issues.  
- Recommendation: Reject.  
- Suggested revisions: better justify Laplacian penalty, fix technical/notation and writing issues, deepen comparisons to spatial regularization methods, and strengthen novelty/significance claims.
