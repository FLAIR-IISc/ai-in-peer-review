This paper proposes a novel regularizer for matrix factorization, specifically tailored for brain imaging data, that combines sparsity and smoothness constraints. The authors extend existing online dictionary learning methods to incorporate a Laplacian penalty, encouraging spatially coherent structures (blobs) in the learned dictionary atoms. While the approach shows promising empirical results, outperforming state-of-the-art methods on brain data, it lacks rigorous theoretical guarantees regarding solution existence, uniqueness, and convergence beyond what is inherited from the base online dictionary learning method.

The problem of defining Regions of Interest (ROIs) in neuroimaging is well-motivated. The authors correctly point out the limitations of univariate models and the difficulties of ROI definition via segmentation or cross-modality approaches. Their approach of learning ROIs from population variability is a reasonable direction. The proposed method builds upon the established work of Mairal et al. (2010) on online dictionary learning, and relevantly cites related work in structured sparsity (e.g., Baldassarre et al., 2013) and brain imaging analysis (e.g., Beckmann & Smith, 2009; Varoquaux et al., 2010, 2011, 2012). The connection to existing methods is clearly articulated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Regularization:** The combination of L1 sparsity and Laplacian smoothness is a conceptually sound approach for extracting interpretable, spatially localized features from brain imaging data.
*   **Scalability:** Leveraging online learning allows the method to scale to large datasets, a crucial requirement for modern neuroimaging studies. The reported runtime increase (factor of 2-3 compared to SODL) is acceptable considering the added structural constraint.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing techniques, including CanICA and SODL, in terms of dictionary interpretability, explained variance, and predictive power on behavioral variables. The learning curves presented in the paper also offer valuable insights.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The proposed method addresses a core challenge in neuroimaging—ROI definition—and offers a data-driven approach to identify meaningful brain structures. The intention to release the implementation within the Nilearn package is a significant positive aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** The non-convex nature of the optimization problem and the absence of theoretical guarantees regarding solution existence, uniqueness, and convergence are major drawbacks. While the authors acknowledge this, it limits the theoretical understanding and potential for further development.
*   **Clarity Issues:** As noted by the reviewer, the manuscript suffers from clarity issues. Specifically, the reviewer pointed out a problem at line 146, which needs to be addressed for better readability.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors acknowledge the sensitivity to hyperparameters (α, γ, τ), and while they provide some guidance on parameter selection, a more systematic analysis or automated parameter tuning strategy could strengthen the results.
*   **Comparison to Alternatives:** While the comparison to CanICA and SODL is relevant, a more comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art methods for spatial regularization (e.g., total variation regularization as in Abraham, 2013) would be beneficial.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its implementation and experimental design. However, the lack of theoretical analysis weakens its overall quality as a scientific contribution. The authors are careful in presenting their results and acknowledge the limitations of their approach.

**Clarity:** The paper's clarity is a concern, as highlighted by the reviewer. Addressing the specific issue at line 146 and potentially revising other sections for improved readability would significantly enhance the manuscript. The increased clarity score after rebuttal (2 to 3) suggests the authors responded to the initial concerns adequately, but further polishing is encouraged.

**Originality:** The combination of sparsity and smoothness regularization within an online dictionary learning framework is a novel contribution. While online dictionary learning is established, the application of a Laplacian penalty for spatial structure in this context is original and well-motivated.

**Significance:** The results are significant, as the proposed method demonstrates the potential to improve ROI definition and capture inter-subject variability in brain imaging data. The demonstrated improvements in predictive power on behavioral variables suggest practical relevance. The method could be adopted by researchers in neuroimaging to extract more interpretable and meaningful features from their data.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The method presents a novel and promising approach to ROI definition in neuroimaging.
*   The empirical results demonstrate superior performance compared to existing methods.
*   The scalability of the online learning approach is a significant advantage.
*   The authors have committed to releasing the implementation as part of a widely used package.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of theoretical guarantees is a major weakness.
*   The manuscript suffers from clarity issues.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity requires careful consideration.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the clarity issues identified by the reviewer, particularly the one at line 146. While the lack of theoretical guarantees remains a concern, the empirical results and the potential significance of the work outweigh this limitation. Furthermore, the authors' willingness to address reviewer feedback and the commitment to releasing the implementation are positive indicators. A stronger discussion of the limitations, particularly concerning the choice of the Laplacian penalty and potential alternatives, would also be beneficial.
