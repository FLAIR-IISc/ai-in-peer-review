- Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online dictionary-learning method with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to induce spatially structured, sparse components in brain images.  
- Main novelty: incorporation of Sobolev priors for spatial structure, potentially useful for neuroscience and medical imaging.  
- Technical framework is sound and builds on established online dictionary-learning, but initial rating was lowered due to notation inconsistencies and typos.  
- Authors addressed some technical comments in rebuttal, improving the technical assessment.  
- Empirical support is weak: the paper does not clearly quantify or prove the advantage of the Sobolev prior over alternatives.  
- Figure 2 fails to enable direct comparison across methods and does not visually/quantitatively support the claim of increased structure.  
- Reviewer requests neuroscience-justified quantitative criteria showing Smooth-SODL atoms are more structured and meaningful.  
- Presentation quality is poor: confusing descriptions, typos, stray comments, and editing issues impede understanding.  
- Specific mathematical issues: inconsistencies/typos in transpose placement, signs/terms in Equations (6) and (8), unclear variable references in updates.  
- Reviewer asks for explicit mathematical/algorithmic solutions to Equation (6) in an appendix and clarification of update steps.  
- Figure 3 is unclear: need to state prediction target, whether regression uses U_i, include legends/captions, and add missing baselines (e.g., PCA); R^2 claims are ambiguous.  
- Minor typos and stray comments must be fixed throughout the manuscript.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel Sobolev prior, potential utility in neuroscience/medical imaging, and partial resolution of technical issues.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak empirical validation and poor presentation that require substantive revision.  
- Recommendation: revise and resubmit after (1) adding neuroscience-justified quantitative evaluations, (2) fixing math/notation and algorithmic details, (3) revising Figures 2–3 for direct comparisons and clearer captions, and (4) correcting typos and editing issues.
