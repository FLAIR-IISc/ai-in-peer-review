This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method, Smooth-SODL, for decomposing brain images into structured and sparse components. The core idea is to combine a sparsity-inducing l1-norm constraint with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage spatially smooth, blob-like dictionary atoms, thereby improving the interpretability of decompositions and capturing inter-subject variability. The method builds upon the online dictionary-learning work of Mairal et al. (2010) and aims to address limitations of existing approaches, such as the need for manual region-of-interest (ROI) definition and the challenges of handling large-scale neuroimaging datasets. The motivation stems from the observation that inter-subject variability in neuroimaging data contains valuable information often discarded in traditional analyses.

The work situates itself within a broader landscape of neuroimaging analysis techniques, contrasting with univariate models and statistical ROI-based approaches. It also acknowledges related work in clustering, PCA, ICA, and sparse component analysis, emphasizing that Smooth-SODL offers a distinct approach by modeling observations as linear mixtures of signals with both sparsity and spatial structure constraints. The authors correctly position their contribution as extending existing online dictionary learning frameworks (Mairal et al., 2010) by incorporating a spatial regularization term, addressing a long-standing challenge in applying such methods to neuroimaging.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior for spatial regularization is a novel contribution, particularly in the context of neuroimaging. The method offers a potentially powerful way to automatically discover meaningful brain regions without relying on predefined ROIs.
*   **Scalability:** The online nature of the algorithm, inherited from Mairal et al. (2010), is a significant strength, allowing it to handle large-scale datasets commonly encountered in neuroimaging, such as those from the Human Connectome Project.
*   **Potential Impact:** If successful, this method could provide a valuable tool for neuroscientists and medical imaging researchers, aiding in the discovery of brain structures, denoising data, and understanding inter-subject variability. It has potential applications beyond fMRI, including PET and other imaging modalities.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The mathematical formulation and algorithmic development appear sound, drawing on established optimization techniques like block coordinate descent and FISTA.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The initial empirical evaluation was unconvincing. Figure 2 lacked quantitative metrics and a clear visual demonstration of the benefits of the spatial regularization. The claim that Smooth-SODL produced more “structured” atoms felt unsupported. The comparisons to CanICA were hampered by differing preprocessing steps (smoothing) and unclear visualization choices.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from clarity issues. Typographical errors, inconsistencies in notation (e.g., transpose placement in equations), and confusing phrasing hindered comprehension. Specifically, Equations (6) and (8) require greater clarity, and the variable references in the algorithm updates could be improved.
*   **Missing Details & References:** The HCP dataset reference was missing, and the mathematical derivation for solving the sub-problem in Equation (6) was not provided.
*   **Lack of Neuroscience Justification:** The choice of hyperparameters (α, γ, τ) wasn’t fully justified from a neuroimaging perspective.  Explicit criteria and quantitative measurements demonstrating the increased “meaningfulness” of Smooth-SODL atoms (compared to other methods) were absent. How does the “structure” imposed by the Laplacian prior relate to known neuroanatomical principles?
*   **Figure 2 & 3 Issues:**  Different slices were shown for different methods in Figure 2, preventing direct visual comparison.  Figure 3's predictions were unclear, the role of *U<sub>i</sub>* wasn't stated, and the lack of PCA in the baseline comparison was puzzling. The ambiguous claims about R<sup>2</sup> values further weakened the analysis.

**Rebuttal & Revision Impact:**

The authors' response to the review and the additional results provided were helpful and improved the overall quality of the work. The additional results regarding the impact of hyperparameters and the convergence analysis addressed some of the initial concerns. Raising the technical quality rating to 3 reflects this improvement. However, incorporating these results into the revised manuscript is crucial.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging: the automated discovery of brain regions.
*   The proposed method combines established techniques (online dictionary learning, Sobolev priors) in a novel and potentially impactful way.
*   The scalability of the algorithm is a significant advantage for large-scale datasets.
*   The rebuttal provided valuable additional information and addressed several reviewer concerns.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation, even with the rebuttal material, remains somewhat weak. More rigorous quantitative analysis and more compelling visual demonstrations are needed.
*   The manuscript suffers from clarity and presentation issues that make it difficult to follow.
*   The lack of a detailed mathematical derivation for the sub-problem in Equation (6) is a weakness.
*   The connection between the imposed spatial structure and neuroanatomical principles could be strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a worthwhile contribution with the potential to advance the field of neuroimaging analysis. However, significant revisions are needed to address the clarity, empirical validation, and presentation issues. The authors should prioritize incorporating the rebuttal material into the manuscript, providing a detailed mathematical derivation, strengthening the neuroscientific justification for their choices, and conducting more rigorous quantitative evaluations. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
