

# Review for "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework for brain imaging applications. The authors address the challenge of extracting interpretable brain regions from large-scale fMRI data by introducing a novel regularizer that enforces both sparsity (via L1-norm) and spatial smoothness (via Laplacian penalty). This combination aims to produce structured, blob-like patterns that better match known functional brain organization compared to traditional sparse dictionary learning approaches.

The work builds upon Mairal et al.'s online dictionary learning framework, extending it to incorporate spatial structure while maintaining computational efficiency. The authors argue that traditional approaches like ICA (CanICA) produce dense, unstructured patterns that require post-hoc thresholding to become interpretable, while sparse dictionary learning (SODL) produces sparse but spatially scattered patterns. By combining L1 sparsity with Laplacian smoothness, Smooth-SODL aims to produce interpretable, spatially structured brain regions that better capture inter-subject variability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel contribution:** The paper successfully integrates spatial structure into online dictionary learning, which is a meaningful extension of prior work. The Laplacian penalty effectively produces structured, blob-like brain regions that match neuroscientific expectations.

2. **Strong empirical results:** The authors demonstrate clear advantages of Smooth-SODL over state-of-the-art methods (SODL and CanICA) across multiple metrics: visual interpretability, explained variance, stability, reproducibility, and predictive performance for behavioral variables. The learning curve analysis (Table 1) particularly convincingly shows Smooth-SODL's advantage in small data regimes.

3. **Computational efficiency:** The method maintains the scalability of online dictionary learning, with only a 2-3x increase in runtime compared to SODL. This is crucial for large-scale brain imaging applications.

4. **Practical significance:** The work directly addresses a real challenge in neuroimaging - defining functionally meaningful brain regions that capture inter-subject variability without relying on predefined anatomical ROIs.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of theoretical guarantees:** The problem is non-convex, and while the authors provide an algorithmic solution, they do not prove existence or uniqueness of solutions, nor do they provide convergence guarantees beyond the general BCD framework. This is a significant limitation for a theoretical conference like NIPS.

2. **Over-reliance on empirical evidence:** The paper's primary contribution is supported mainly through experiments rather than theoretical analysis. While the empirical results are compelling, the lack of theoretical grounding weakens the paper's scientific contribution.

3. **Clarity issues:** The manuscript contains several clarity problems (noted at line 146), including insufficient explanation of the relationship between the proposed model and the Laplacian penalty, and some ambiguous notation. However, the authors addressed these concerns satisfactorily in the rebuttal, leading to an improved clarity score.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The method solves a meaningful problem in neuroimaging with clear empirical advantages
- The integration of spatial structure into online dictionary learning is novel and well-motivated
- The computational efficiency makes the approach practical for large-scale brain imaging
- The experimental validation is thorough and demonstrates clear benefits across multiple metrics

*Con:*
- The lack of theoretical guarantees for the non-convex optimization problem is a significant limitation
- The paper does not sufficiently address the challenges of hyperparameter selection beyond empirical observation
- The comparison to related work (particularly structured dictionary learning methods) could be more thorough

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the application of dictionary learning in neuroimaging. The Smooth-SODL method successfully addresses a practical problem with clear empirical advantages over existing approaches, and the computational efficiency makes it suitable for large-scale brain imaging applications. While the lack of theoretical guarantees for the non-convex optimization problem is a notable weakness, the strong empirical results and clear practical significance make this an important contribution to the field.

The authors have addressed the clarity concerns raised during the review process, improving the manuscript's presentation. Given the paper's strong empirical results, practical significance, and relevance to a growing area of research (large-scale neuroimaging analysis), I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the remaining theoretical limitations, which should be acknowledged in the final version.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the application of structured dictionary learning in neuroscience, with clear advantages demonstrated across multiple metrics. While theoretical guarantees would strengthen the paper, the practical impact and compelling empirical results make this a strong candidate for NIPS 2016.
