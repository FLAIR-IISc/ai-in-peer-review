## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a novel regularizer for online dictionary learning, specifically tailored for brain imaging data. The authors aim to improve the interpretability and accuracy of learned brain representations by combining sparsity-inducing L1 regularization with a Laplacian penalty that encourages smoothness and compactness of dictionary atoms – effectively learning “blobs” corresponding to brain regions. The proposed Smooth-SODL algorithm builds upon the existing online learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010), presenting a computationally efficient approach to this problem.

The core idea of incorporating spatial structure into dictionary learning for neuroimaging is relevant and builds upon existing work utilizing techniques like ICA and PCA for functional unit extraction (Beckmann & Smith, 2004). The motivation to move beyond purely statistical approaches, especially concerning inter-subject variability, is well articulated, acknowledging the limitations of standard ROI-based analyses. The proposed method addresses a significant challenge in the field – automatically defining meaningful regions from brain imaging data.

The strengths of the paper lie in its practical focus and promising experimental results. The authors demonstrate superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods like CanICA and standard SODL on a large-scale HCP dataset, showcasing improvements in both explained variance and predictive power for behavioral variables. The algorithm's scalability, inherited from Mairal et al. (2010), is also a significant advantage. The ablation study and discussion of hyperparameter selection provide useful insights.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of rigorous theoretical analysis. While the algorithm is presented, the existence and uniqueness of solutions are not proven, and the non-convexity of the problem is acknowledged without a detailed exploration of its implications. The theoretical guarantees are limited, and the convergence analysis relies on citing the work of Mairal et al. (2010) without fully extending it to the new regularizer.  Additionally, the manuscript clarity could be improved; specifically, the discussion around the update rules (Algorithm 2) felt dense and could benefit from simplification. Though this was addressed in the rebuttal, and my clarity/presentation score was raised to 3. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in neuroimaging with a novel and practical approach.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance on a large, publicly available dataset.
*   Scalable algorithm suitable for massive datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks theoretical guarantees regarding solution existence, uniqueness, and convergence.
*   The manuscript requires some improvement in clarity and presentation.
