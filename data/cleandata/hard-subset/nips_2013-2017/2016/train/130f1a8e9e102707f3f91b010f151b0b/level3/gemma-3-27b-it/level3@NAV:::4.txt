## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes an online dictionary learning method for fMRI analysis, incorporating both sparsity (L1 regularization) and smoothness (Sobolev regularization) constraints to extract meaningful brain regions. The approach builds upon existing online dictionary learning techniques (Mairal et al., 2010) and aims to address the challenge of inter-subject variability in neuroimaging data. The core idea of combining these two regularization terms – promoting both sparse and spatially coherent dictionary elements – is interesting and potentially valuable for identifying functional brain structures. This aligns with a broader trend in neuroimaging towards methods that leverage population-level data to improve ROI definition (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2016).

The key originality lies in this specific combination of Sobolev and L1 regularization within the online dictionary learning framework. While sparsity-inducing penalties are common in fMRI analysis (e.g., Abraham et al., 2017), and spatial regularization has been explored (e.g., Baldassarre et al., 2013), integrating both within an online scalable method is a novel contribution. The authors correctly position their work concerning related approaches like ICA/PCA (Beckmann & Smith, 2004) and TV-L1 minimization (Dohmatob, 2017), highlighting the advantages of their online approach for large datasets.

However, the experimental results demonstrate only minor or unclear improvements over simpler methods like standard online dictionary learning (SODL). The gains in explained variance and predictive power, while present, are not overwhelmingly convincing, especially considering the increased computational cost (roughly 3x slower than SODL). Critically, the paper lacks a sufficient analysis of *why* the added Sobolev regularization doesn’t yield more substantial benefits. While the authors briefly mention the diminishing returns with abundant data, a more detailed investigation into the interplay between the hyperparameters (α, γ, τ) and their impact on performance is needed. 

The manuscript also suffers from a rushed presentation. Numerous draft comments remain (e.g., line 146), and the overall writing is often dense and difficult to follow. The mathematical derivations are presented without sufficient intuition or explanation, hindering a complete understanding of the algorithm. Clarity is a major concern. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of L1 and Sobolev regularization in online dictionary learning.
*   Potential for improved interpretability of learned brain regions.
*   Scalability of the online learning framework.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Marginal experimental improvements over existing methods.
*   Insufficient analysis of why performance gains are limited.
*   Poor manuscript quality (clarity, organization, draft comments).



**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of delivering a compelling contribution due to limited experimental validation and poor presentation. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and address the outlined weaknesses. A more thorough analysis of the regularization parameters, additional experiments on diverse datasets, and a significant rewrite to improve clarity are necessary before this work would be suitable for NIPS.
