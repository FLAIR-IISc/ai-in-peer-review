This paper proposes an extension of dictionary learning models with a Sobolev prior to obtain decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The main idea is to use a multivariate online dictionary-learning method with a Laplacian penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The paper presents a detailed formulation of the problem, an algorithm to solve it, and experimental results on a large public dataset.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and understand the proposed method. The introduction provides a clear motivation for the work, highlighting the limitations of existing methods and the potential benefits of the proposed approach. The related work section is thorough, covering relevant papers in the field and highlighting the contributions of the proposed method.

The technical part of the paper is sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed algorithm and its components. The use of a Laplacian penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms is an interesting idea, and the authors provide a good justification for this choice. The experimental results are also well-presented, with a clear description of the dataset, the experimental setup, and the evaluation metrics.

However, the impact of the proposed algorithm is unclear, and it is hard to judge its improvement over existing methods from the paper. The main results rely on visual comparison of identified brain "blobs," which are not clear to an untrained eye. The data quality and choice matter greatly, and adding smoothness/spatial-consistency penalties unsurprisingly yields smoother blobs. With large datasets like the Human Connectome Project, smoothing may not address a real data scarcity problem.

Moreover, the reviewer expected consolidation of brain-region differences across subjects and use of inter-subject consistency for validation. However, the presented validation is subjective and does not clearly demonstrate the benefit of the proposed method. The comparison with other methods, such as SODL and CanICA, is limited, and it is unclear how the proposed method performs in different scenarios.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel combination of familiar techniques, but it is unclear how this work differs from previous contributions. The related work section is thorough, but it does not provide a clear comparison with existing methods. The paper does not provide a unique theoretical or pragmatic approach, and the contribution of the paper is mainly empirical.

In terms of significance, the results are not surprising, and the paper does not address a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The paper does not provide unique data, unique conclusions on existing data, or a unique theoretical or pragmatic approach. The proposed method may be useful in certain scenarios, but its impact is unclear, and further evaluation is needed to demonstrate its benefits.

Overall, the paper is well-written and well-organized, but its impact is unclear, and further evaluation is needed to demonstrate its benefits. The proposed method is an interesting idea, but its contribution to the field is mainly empirical, and its significance is limited.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper proposes an interesting idea, using a Laplacian penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms.
* The technical part of the paper is sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed algorithm and its components.
* The experimental results are well-presented, with a clear description of the dataset, the experimental setup, and the evaluation metrics.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The impact of the proposed algorithm is unclear, and it is hard to judge its improvement over existing methods from the paper.
* The main results rely on visual comparison of identified brain "blobs," which are not clear to an untrained eye.
* The data quality and choice matter greatly, and adding smoothness/spatial-consistency penalties unsurprisingly yields smoother blobs.
* The presented validation is subjective and does not clearly demonstrate the benefit of the proposed method.
* The comparison with other methods is limited, and it is unclear how the proposed method performs in different scenarios.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods, including a clear evaluation of the proposed method's performance in different scenarios.
* Use more objective evaluation metrics, such as quantitative measures of brain-region differences across subjects and inter-subject consistency.
* Provide more insight into the contribution of the paper, including a clear explanation of how the proposed method differs from previous contributions.
* Consider adding more experimental results, including a larger dataset and more evaluation metrics, to demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method.
