This paper introduces Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), a method for decomposing large-scale brain imaging datasets into a set of sparse and spatially structured components, or "atoms." The goal is to identify meaningful brain regions in a data-driven manner. The work builds directly upon the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010), extending it by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages spatial smoothness, resulting in components that are blob-like and more neuroscientifically interpretable than the scattered patterns often produced by standard sparse methods. The combination of an L1-norm constraint for sparsity and the Laplacian penalty for structure is optimized using a Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) scheme adapted for the online setting. The authors demonstrate their method on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (HCP), comparing it to standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a popular group ICA method (CanICA).

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a significant and highly relevant problem in neuroimaging: the data-driven discovery of functional brain regions from large population studies. The proposed approach is a novel and practical combination of existing techniques. Adapting the highly scalable online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. to include spatial structure priors is a valuable contribution. It effectively tackles the computational bottleneck that has limited the application of structured decomposition methods on massive datasets, offering a principled alternative to methods that require heavy pre-smoothing or ad-hoc post-processing.

2.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is a key strength of the paper. The authors use a large, modern dataset (HCP) and compare their method against well-chosen and relevant baselines (SODL and CanICA). The evaluation is comprehensive, using a variety of metrics including qualitative visual inspection, explained variance on held-out data, stability of the learned components, and, importantly, the utility of the learned representations for predicting behavioral variables.

3.  **Compelling Results:** The results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed spatial regularization. The qualitative results in Figure 2a clearly show that Smooth-SODL produces cleaner, more spatially coherent brain maps than SODL, without the need for arbitrary thresholding required by CanICA. The quantitative results support this: Smooth-SODL achieves a better trade-off between data fidelity (explained variance) and component stability compared to its competitors. The learning curve analysis in Table 1 provides particularly strong evidence, showing a dramatic performance gain over SODL in the low-data regime, which is a common scenario in many clinical studies.

### Weaknesses (Arguments Against Acceptance)

1.  **Limited Theoretical Contribution:** The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of theoretical analysis. While the method is technically sound from an algorithmic perspective, its novelty lies in adding the Laplacian regularizer. The paper does not provide any analysis of how this addition affects the properties of the learned dictionary, the convergence rate, or the sample complexity of the algorithm. The authors state that convergence is guaranteed by citing the work of Mairal et al., but this is insufficient. A more thorough analysis would investigate the interplay between the sparsity (τ) and smoothness (γ) parameters and their impact on the solution. The discussion around equation (9) about the Lipschitz constant is a good practical observation but should be expanded into a more formal treatment.

2.  **Poor Manuscript Quality and Clarity:** The clarity of the paper is significantly hampered by a lack of careful proofreading. The manuscript is riddled with typographical errors, grammatical mistakes, and awkward phrasing that make it difficult to follow in several places. This suggests a rushed preparation and detracts from the overall quality of the submission. A particularly concerning example is what appears to be a leftover internal comment in the submitted draft (e.g., "XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v," which I noted on line 146 in my reading), which is unacceptable for a formal submission. This lack of polish undermines the credibility of the work and places an unnecessary burden on the reviewer and reader to parse the intended meaning.

### Detailed Comments

*   The derivation of the dictionary update in equation (7) is very condensed and relies heavily on the reader being familiar with [16]. While space is limited, a slightly more self-contained explanation would improve readability.
*   The hyper-parameter tuning section provides useful practical advice, but the discussion could be linked more formally to the optimization problem. For instance, the observation that large γ can cause issues is tied to the conditioning of the subproblem in (7); this connection could be made more explicit.
*   While the empirical comparison is strong, it would be interesting to see a comparison with other structured sparsity methods, such as those using a Total Variation (TV) penalty, even if on a smaller dataset where they are computationally feasible, to better contextualize the trade-offs made by using a Laplacian penalty.

### Conclusion

In summary, the paper presents a valuable and practical approach to a significant problem. The core idea of integrating a spatial smoothness prior into a scalable online dictionary learning framework is compelling, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness for neuroimaging data analysis. However, the contribution is weakened by a near-complete absence of theoretical analysis regarding the proposed extension and is severely undermined by the poor quality of the manuscript. The numerous errors and leftover comments suggest that the paper is not ready for publication. I believe the work has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field, but it requires a major revision to address the clarity issues and, ideally, to strengthen its theoretical foundations. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
