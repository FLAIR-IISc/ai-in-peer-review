This paper proposes "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL), an online algorithm for decomposing large-scale neuroimaging data into a dictionary of sparse and spatially structured components. The core idea is to extend the well-established online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) prior on the dictionary atoms. This prior encourages spatial smoothness and contiguity, aiming to produce blob-like brain regions that are more neuroscientifically interpretable than the scattered patterns often resulting from standard sparse models. The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to massive datasets, which is a key advantage in modern neuroimaging.

### Strengths

*   **Relevance and Potential Impact:** The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in neuroimaging: the data-driven identification of functional brain regions from large population cohorts. A scalable method that learns spatially coherent and interpretable components could be a valuable tool for researchers, potentially advancing the state-of-the-art in creating functional atlases. The approach may also be applicable to other domains like image segmentation or regional sensing.
*   **Algorithmic Scalability:** By building upon an online learning framework, the proposed method can handle datasets that are too large to fit in memory, which is a crucial feature for modern neuroimaging studies like the Human Connectome Project (HCP). The claim that this is achieved with only a minor increase in computational cost (2-3x) is a strong practical advantage.
*   **Novelty:** The primary contribution is the novel combination of a Sobolev prior with the online dictionary learning framework to enforce spatial structure. While an incremental extension of existing work, this combination is well-motivated for the target application and represents a useful contribution to the field.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

The paper, in its current form, has significant weaknesses in its empirical evaluation, clarity, and presentation that need to be addressed.

**1. Empirical Support is Weak and Unconvincing**

The central claim—that the Sobolev prior produces more structured and meaningful atoms—is not adequately supported by the provided experiments.

*   **Figure 2 (Qualitative Comparison):** This figure is intended to be the primary visual evidence for the method's superiority, but it is not convincing.
    *   Different axial slices are shown for different methods, making a direct and fair comparison impossible.
    *   The claim that Smooth-SODL atoms are more "structured" is subjective. The authors should define explicit, neuroscience-justified criteria for what constitutes a "good" or "structured" atom (e.g., spatial compactness, contiguity, smoothness) and provide quantitative measurements to support their claims. Without quantification, the visual difference is not compelling enough.
*   **Figure 3 (Prediction Task):** The predictive modeling experiment is poorly explained and executed.
    *   It is unclear what features are used for prediction—are the subject-specific codes (U_i) used as input to a regression model? This must be clarified.
    *   The legends and caption are incomplete; the PCA baseline is shown in the plot but missing from the legend.
    *   The claim that some variables are predicted with R^2 > 0.1 is ambiguous, as it is not clear from the plot which methods achieve this on the test set.

**2. Clarity, Presentation, and Technical Details**

The manuscript is difficult to follow due to confusing descriptions, notational inconsistencies, and numerous typos. A thorough round of editing is required.

*   **Mathematical/Notational Issues:**
    *   There appear to be inconsistencies in notation. For instance, the text describes `Xi` as row vectors, but the update `Bt ← Bt−1 + XtUTt` suggests `Xt` is a row vector and `Ut` is a column vector, which is non-standard. Please clarify dimensions and ensure consistency.
    *   Equation (6) appears to have a typo or omission in its derivation from the ridge regression problem (5). The solution should be `Ut = (V^T V + αI)^-1 V^T Xt`.
    *   Equation (8) and the surrounding text are confusing. The term `VAj` is ambiguous. A clear, self-contained derivation of this update step is needed, perhaps in an appendix, rather than just referencing prior work.
*   **Missing Details and References:**
    *   The paper is missing a formal citation for the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset.
    *   The authors should provide the explicit mathematical solution or algorithmic steps for solving Equation (7), as this is a key part of their contribution.
*   **Typos and Editing:** The paper contains multiple distracting errors, such as "Graph-Net penalty can imposes" (Sec 3.1), duplicated words ("we typically use we use"), and a stray bolded sentence fragment in Section 2.

### Comments on Rebuttal

The additional results provided during the author rebuttal were informative and helped to address some of the concerns regarding the method's empirical performance. This new information raised the initial technical quality rating from 2 to 3. It is crucial that these additional quantitative analyses and improved visualizations be incorporated directly into the final revised version of the paper to substantially strengthen its claims.

### Summary

The paper introduces a promising and scalable algorithm for an important problem in computational neuroscience. The core idea of integrating a Sobolev prior into online dictionary learning is sound. However, the initial submission was significantly hindered by a weak empirical evaluation that did not convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages, as well as by poor presentation and several technical ambiguities. Based on the improvements shown in the rebuttal, the paper has the potential to be a solid contribution if the authors thoroughly revise the manuscript to include a rigorous quantitative evaluation, clarify all technical and notational details, and perform a comprehensive proofreading.
