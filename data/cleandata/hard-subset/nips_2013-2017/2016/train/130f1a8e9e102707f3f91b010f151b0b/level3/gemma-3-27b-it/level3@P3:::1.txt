This paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework, Smooth-SODL, incorporating a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially smooth and compact dictionary atoms for brain image decomposition. The motivation stems from the desire to automatically define brain regions from inter-subject variability in fMRI data, bypassing the need for manual ROI definition. The approach builds upon existing online dictionary learning techniques (Mairal et al., 2010) and addresses the limitations of purely sparsity-driven methods by promoting structured solutions.

The problem tackled is relevant and potentially valuable. Automatically identifying meaningful brain regions is a longstanding challenge in neuroimaging, and a data-driven approach that leverages inter-subject variability is promising. The idea of combining sparsity and smoothness constraints to achieve more interpretable decompositions is intuitively sound and aligns with the known characteristics of functional brain organization.  Related work in brain imaging frequently relies on ROI definitions (Friston, 2003) or clustering approaches (Hibar et al., 2013), both of which have inherent limitations. The proposed method offers an alternative that attempts to learn these regions directly from the data.  Furthermore, the extension of online learning techniques to incorporate spatial structure addresses a gap in the literature – existing structured decomposition methods (Abraham, 2016; Baldassarre et al., 2013) are often computationally expensive and less scalable.

However, the paper suffers from a significant lack of theoretical analysis. While the authors mention convergence guarantees inherited from Mairal et al. (2010), they do not provide any theoretical justification for the effectiveness of the Laplacian penalty or any analysis of the impact of the regularization parameter γ.  It’s unclear under what conditions this approach is expected to outperform standard online dictionary learning or other existing methods.  The empirical study, while reasonably comprehensive, lacks a rigorous statistical evaluation of the improvements.  Simply demonstrating that the proposed method *can* achieve better results doesn't establish *why* or *when* it will do so.  A theoretical understanding of the trade-offs between sparsity, smoothness, and reconstruction error would significantly strengthen the contribution.

The most striking weakness, however, is the poor manuscript quality. The text is replete with careless writing, grammatical errors, and inconsistencies.  The inclusion of comments such as "146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v." within the submitted manuscript is unacceptable and indicates a complete lack of proper proofreading. This severely detracts from the credibility of the work and makes it difficult to follow the authors' reasoning. The writing style is often convoluted and imprecise, hindering clarity.  For example, the explanation of the objective function (2) could be more concise and accessible.  There are instances of unexplained notation and abbreviations, forcing the reader to infer their meaning from context. The organization could also be improved; the relationship between sections and the flow of ideas aren’t always clear.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in neuroimaging.
*   Offers a novel combination of online dictionary learning with spatial regularization.
*   Demonstrates promising empirical results on a large dataset (HCP).
*   Scalable to large datasets, a key advantage in neuroimaging.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of theoretical analysis to support the proposed method.
*   Poor manuscript quality – careless writing and unprofessional presentation.
*   Insufficiently rigorous statistical evaluation of the results.
*   Limited discussion of the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices beyond the provided figure.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential value of the proposed approach, the significant shortcomings in manuscript quality and the lack of theoretical justification outweigh the strengths. The paper presents an interesting idea, but it is not ready for publication in its current form.  I recommend **rejection** with the strong encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the identified weaknesses.  Specifically, the authors must:

1.  Thoroughly proofread and edit the manuscript to eliminate all grammatical errors, inconsistencies, and unprofessional comments.
2.  Provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed method, including a discussion of the impact of the regularization parameters.
3.  Conduct a more comprehensive statistical evaluation of the results, potentially including significance testing and effect size estimates.
4.  Clarify the writing and improve the organization of the paper to enhance readability.
