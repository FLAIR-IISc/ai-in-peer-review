This paper proposes a novel multivariate online dictionary-learning method for brain imaging data, which combines sparsity and smoothness penalties to extract structured and interpretable dictionaries. The method, dubbed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), extends the standard online dictionary-learning framework by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure.

The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, highlighting the limitations of existing methods and the need for a more principled approach to define regions of interest (ROIs) in brain imaging data. They also provide a thorough review of related work, including online sparse dictionary-learning methods and spatially-structured decompositions.

The proposed method is well-motivated, and the authors provide a detailed derivation of the algorithm, including the optimization problem and the Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) scheme used to solve it. The algorithm is also evaluated on a large public dataset, and the results demonstrate its superiority over state-of-the-art methods.

The paper's strengths include its originality, clarity, and thoroughness. The authors provide a detailed and well-structured presentation of their method, including a clear explanation of the optimization problem and the algorithm used to solve it. The experimental results are also well-presented and demonstrate the potential of the proposed method.

However, there are some weaknesses and limitations to the paper. Firstly, the problem is non-convex, and the algorithm lacks theoretical guarantees. While the authors provide some empirical evidence for the algorithm's convergence, a more rigorous analysis of its theoretical properties would be desirable. Secondly, the manuscript could benefit from some improvement in clarity and presentation, particularly in the introduction and conclusion sections. Finally, the evaluation of the method relies primarily on empirical evidence rather than theoretical analysis, which may limit its generalizability to other datasets and applications.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper meets the criteria for quality, clarity, and originality. The authors provide a clear and concise presentation of their method, and the experimental results demonstrate its potential. However, the paper could benefit from some improvement in terms of significance and impact, particularly in terms of its theoretical contributions and generalizability to other applications.
