

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) for identifying structured brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors introduce a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms, creating interpretable brain regions rather than scattered patterns. This approach extends the SODL framework while maintaining scalability to large datasets, with only a modest (2-3x) increase in computational cost. The method is evaluated on HCP fMRI data, where it demonstrates improved interpretability and better capture of inter-subject variability compared to SODL and CanICA.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of machine learning and neuroimaging. The problem of identifying meaningful brain regions from population data is well-motivated, and the authors effectively demonstrate that their structured approach produces more interpretable dictionaries than existing methods. The empirical results show that Smooth-SODL extracts spatially coherent brain regions that better capture population variability, especially in small-to-medium data regimes where vanilla dictionary learning struggles. The computational efficiency of the online approach is particularly relevant for large-scale neuroimaging datasets, making this a promising method for practical applications.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that undermine its scientific contribution. Most critically, it lacks theoretical analysis to support the proposed algorithm. While the authors reference Mairal et al. (2010) for convergence guarantees, they fail to provide any theoretical justification for how the Laplacian regularization affects the optimization landscape, convergence properties, or generalization bounds. The empirical results are presented descriptively without statistical significance testing, making it difficult to assess whether the observed improvements are meaningful. For instance, the paper claims Smooth-SODL "outperforms" SODL in capturing inter-subject variability, but provides no statistical validation of these claims.

The manuscript quality is poor, with numerous careless errors that suggest inadequate proofreading. The text contains leftover comments like "146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v," which are unprofessional and undermine the authors' credibility. The writing is often unclear, with inconsistent notation (e.g., switching between V and v for dictionary atoms) and insufficient detail about implementation choices. The experimental setup lacks sufficient methodological detail to reproduce the results, such as the exact parameters used for hyperparameter selection beyond the vague statement that "K-fold cross-validation with explained variance metric was retained."

The paper's originality is limited to the specific application of Laplacian regularization to online dictionary learning for neuroimaging. While the combination of spatial structure with online learning is novel for this domain, the underlying algorithmic approach is a straightforward extension of existing work. The authors do not sufficiently differentiate their contribution from related work like Varoquaux et al. (2013) on multi-subject dictionary learning, which also addresses brain region identification but uses different optimization techniques.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the narrow scope of the application to brain imaging. While the method could potentially be applied to other structured data domains, the paper provides no discussion of this broader applicability. The authors claim their method "shall become the de facto way to do dimensionality reduction and ROI extraction," but this assertion is unsupported by evidence beyond the specific neuroimaging experiments presented.

In summary, the paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging with a potentially useful method. However, the lack of theoretical analysis, poor manuscript quality, and insufficient empirical validation prevent it from making a strong scientific contribution. The authors would need to significantly strengthen the theoretical foundation, improve the writing and presentation, and provide more rigorous statistical validation of their claims to meet NIPS standards. The current version reads more like a preliminary technical note than a complete scientific contribution.
