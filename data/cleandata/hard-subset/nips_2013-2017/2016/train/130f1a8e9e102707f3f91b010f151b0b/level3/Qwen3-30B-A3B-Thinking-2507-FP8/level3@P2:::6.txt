

**Review for "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"**

**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper proposes a novel extension of online dictionary learning (SODL) to incorporate spatial smoothness constraints, specifically designed for neuroimaging applications. The authors introduce a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure in the dictionary atoms, resulting in "Smooth-SODL" (Structured Online Dictionary Learning with Laplacian regularization). This approach aims to extract structured, sparse, and spatially compact brain regions from fMRI data, addressing the limitations of standard dictionary learning methods that produce scattered, noisy patterns. The work builds upon Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework, which has been widely adopted for large-scale data analysis, and extends it with a spatial regularization term that allows for principled extraction of brain regions. The paper demonstrates that the method provides more interpretable brain patterns that better capture inter-subject variability across different data regimes.

**Quality**

The paper presents a technically sound extension to the SODL framework, with a well-motivated problem formulation for neuroimaging data. The authors provide a detailed algorithmic description of their Smooth-SODL method, including the necessary updates for the dictionary atoms with the Laplacian penalty. The computational complexity is analyzed, and the authors demonstrate that the proposed method is only about 2-3 times slower than the baseline SODL, making it practical for large-scale neuroimaging datasets. The experimental evaluation on HCP fMRI data is comprehensive, comparing against state-of-the-art methods (SODL and CanICA) across multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, reproducibility, and predictive performance for behavioral variables).

However, the paper has several quality concerns. The optimization problem is non-convex, and while the authors provide a heuristic algorithm based on Block Coordinate Descent, they do not provide theoretical guarantees for convergence to a global optimum or even a local optimum. The paper lacks a formal analysis of the existence and uniqueness of solutions to the proposed optimization problem. The authors acknowledge the non-convexity but do not address how this affects the reliability of the results. While the algorithmic approach is inspired by previous work (Mairal et al., 2010), the theoretical properties are not thoroughly analyzed, which is a concern for a NIPS paper.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-organized and provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The authors clearly explain the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. However, there were some clarity issues in the initial submission (noted at line 146), including some grammatical errors and awkward phrasing that affected readability. After the authors' rebuttal, these clarity issues have been addressed to a satisfactory degree, and the reviewer has raised the clarity score from 2 to 3.

**Originality**

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by extending online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial smoothness constraints. While structured dictionary learning has been explored in neuroimaging (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2011; Abraham et al., 2014), these approaches typically use slower alternating optimization methods. The authors' key innovation is adapting the online learning framework to handle structured penalties efficiently, which is a non-trivial extension. The Laplacian penalty for enforcing spatial structure is a novel application to this specific problem. The paper adequately references previous work, including the relevant NIPS papers (Mairal et al., 2010; Varoquaux et al., 2011).

**Significance**

The problem addressed is highly significant for neuroimaging, where the extraction of interpretable brain regions is crucial for understanding brain function and variability across subjects. The proposed method has the potential to advance how brain imaging data is analyzed by providing more interpretable and spatially structured patterns. The experimental results demonstrate that Smooth-SODL outperforms existing methods in terms of interpretability, stability, and predictive performance, particularly in small and medium-sized datasets. The paper shows that spatial regularization is beneficial even when data is abundant, which is a significant insight. The method's scalability to large datasets makes it particularly valuable for modern neuroimaging studies.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

*Strengths:*
- Novel extension of online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial smoothness constraints
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on a large-scale neuroimaging dataset
- Practical and efficient algorithm with only a modest increase in computational cost
- Demonstrates significant improvements in interpretability and stability over state-of-the-art methods
- Addresses a meaningful problem in neuroimaging with practical implications

*Weaknesses:*
- Lacks theoretical guarantees for the non-convex optimization problem
- No formal analysis of existence/uniqueness of solutions
- Relies primarily on empirical results without sufficient theoretical justification
- Some clarity issues in the manuscript (addressed in rebuttal)

**Arguments for and against Acceptance**

*For acceptance:*
- The paper addresses a significant problem in neuroimaging with a novel solution
- The proposed method provides clear improvements over state-of-the-art in multiple metrics
- The approach is practical and scalable for large neuroimaging datasets
- The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed
- The work has clear potential to impact neuroimaging analysis

*Against acceptance:*
- The lack of theoretical guarantees for the non-convex optimization problem is a notable weakness
- The paper does not provide sufficient justification for the choice of the Laplacian penalty
- The connection to existing theoretical work on structured dictionary learning could be stronger

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a promising extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging applications, with a novel Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. While the lack of theoretical guarantees for the non-convex optimization problem is a notable weakness, the practical benefits and strong empirical results make this a valuable contribution. The authors have addressed the clarity concerns raised in the initial review, and the paper is now well-written and easy to follow.

Given the significance of the problem, the novelty of the approach, and the strong empirical results, I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS 2016. The work has clear potential to impact the field of neuroimaging analysis and demonstrates a thoughtful application of machine learning to a challenging problem.

**Recommendation:** Accept
