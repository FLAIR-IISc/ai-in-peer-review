## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a novel online structured sparse dictionary learning (Smooth-SODL) algorithm for decomposing neuroimaging data, incorporating a Sobolev prior to encourage spatially contiguous dictionary atoms. The work builds upon existing online dictionary learning frameworks (Mairal et al., 2010) by adding a Laplacian penalty to promote smoothness, aiming to better delineate brain structures. The motivation – leveraging inter-subject variability to define brain regions – is well-aligned with current challenges in neuroimaging and represents a potentially valuable contribution. 

The technical approach is sound, extending a well-established framework. The use of a Sobolev prior is a reasonable strategy for inducing spatial structure, and the authors correctly identify the computational benefits of an online learning approach for large datasets. However, the initial technical rating was marginally below average, and this remains a concern. While the rebuttal significantly improved the technical quality by providing additional results and clarifying algorithmic details, these improvements should be incorporated into the revision. Specifically, the requested explicit mathematical solution to Equation (6) (or its derivation in the appendix) is crucial for reproducibility and understanding.

The primary weakness lies in the empirical evaluation. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage of the Sobolev prior. Figure 2, while visually suggestive, lacks quantitative comparison. The claim of “more structured” atoms is subjective without metrics to support it. The rebuttal results were informative, but the revised manuscript needs to include a more rigorous, neuroscience-justified evaluation. This should include quantitative measurements (e.g., spatial coherence, overlap with known brain atlases) demonstrating that SSMF atoms are indeed more structured and meaningful than those obtained by competing methods. 

The presentation suffers from confusing descriptions, typos, and editing issues. Inconsistencies in notation (e.g., matrix transpose placement, signs in Equations 6 & 8) and unclear variable references hinder comprehension. The missing reference to the HCP dataset is a significant oversight. Addressing these issues is critical for clarity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of online dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior for spatial structure.
*   Potential for a useful tool in neuroimaging and related fields.
*   Rebuttal significantly improved the technical quality.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak empirical support for the benefits of the Sobolev prior.
*   Poor presentation quality with numerous errors and inconsistencies.
*   Lack of quantitative evaluation of atom structure.



**Overall:** This paper presents an interesting idea with potential. However, the lack of compelling empirical evidence and the issues with presentation currently limit its impact. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly strengthening the empirical validation and improving clarity, is essential for acceptance. I recommend a revision based on the reviewer comments and the rebuttal material.
