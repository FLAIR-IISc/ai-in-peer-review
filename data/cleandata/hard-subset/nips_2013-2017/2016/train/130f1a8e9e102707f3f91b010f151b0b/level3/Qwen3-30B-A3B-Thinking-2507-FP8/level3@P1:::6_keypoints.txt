- Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online dictionary learning extension with a Laplacian/Sobolev penalty to enforce spatial smoothness while keeping sparsity.
- Targets automatic definition of population-level brain regions (ROIs) instead of relying on anatomical or ad hoc segmentation.
- Builds on Mairal et al.'s online dictionary learning, integrating spatial regularization to scale structured dictionary learning to large neuroimaging datasets.
- Demonstrates more interpretable atoms and better capture of functional brain organization compared to SODL and CanICA.
- Evaluated across visual interpretability, explained variance, reproducibility, and behavioral prediction; shows consistent empirical improvements.
- Key experimental result: Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL in small-data regimes (31% vs 2% explained variance with 17 subjects).
- Scalable and practical: runs only ~2–3× slower than standard SODL and scales to large datasets (HCP, 500 subjects).
- Implementation planned for Nilearn, facilitating reproducibility and community adoption.
- Originality: non-trivial and novel integration of Laplacian spatial regularization into the online dictionary learning framework.
- High significance for neuroimaging applications (clinical, brain mapping, cognitive neuroscience) due to automated, interpretable ROI extraction.
- Major theoretical weakness: non-convex formulation with no proofs of existence, uniqueness, convergence, or optimality guarantees.
- Theoretical analysis is limited to empirical observations (e.g., Lipschitz constants) and lacks formal guarantees expected at NIPS.
- Clarity issues: key formulation (eq.2) lacks intuition on sparsity vs smoothness trade-offs; constraint set C discussion is confusing.
- Authors addressed clarity concerns in rebuttal, and proposed revisions would substantially improve readability.
- Con-acceptance concerns: missing theoretical grounding, insufficient discussion of relation to other spatial regularizers, limited discussion of generalization to other modalities.
- Overall recommendation: valuable and practically significant contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify theoretical aspects.
