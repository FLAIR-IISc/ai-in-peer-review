This paper proposes a novel multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, incorporating both sparsity and structural priors – specifically, a Sobolev penalty to encourage spatially smooth, blob-like dictionary atoms. The authors aim to extract meaningful brain regions from functional images by leveraging inter-subject variability, addressing a common challenge in neuroimaging where such variability is often discarded. The method builds upon existing online dictionary learning work (Mairal et al., 2010) and demonstrates its application to a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project.

The core idea of combining sparsity and structural regularization in dictionary learning is conceptually sound and connects to related work in neuroimaging, such as Total-Variation constrained dictionary learning (Abraham, 2013) and structured sparse PCA (Jenatton et al., 2010). The authors correctly identify the limitations of univariate models and the need for principled region-of-interest (ROI) definition, referencing existing ROI-based approaches in fMRI and PET imaging. The online learning aspect is also a significant contribution, enabling scalability to large datasets. The connection to existing methods like ICA and PCA is also clearly articulated, framing the work within the broader context of dimensionality reduction techniques used in brain imaging (Beckmann & Smith, 2009).

The paper’s strengths lie in its technical soundness and the clear presentation of the proposed Smooth-SODL algorithm. The mathematical formulation is well-defined, and the discussion of the block coordinate descent optimization approach, including the convergence guarantees, is thorough. The authors acknowledge the importance of hyperparameter tuning and provide some guidance based on their experiments, including a data-adaptive approach for the ridge parameter. The inclusion of implementation details and a plan to release the code as part of the Nilearn package is commendable.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. A significant concern is the difficulty in objectively assessing the improvement offered by the proposed method. The primary results rely heavily on visual comparison of the extracted dictionary atoms, which is subjective and not easily interpretable by someone unfamiliar with neuroimaging. While the authors attempt to quantify the results with metrics like explained variance and prediction of behavioral variables, these metrics don't unequivocally demonstrate a clear benefit over competing methods. The comparison to CanICA, while insightful, reveals that CanICA achieves higher explained variance, albeit at the cost of interpretability. The improvement in predictive power is modest.

The authors claim that the spatial regularization is particularly beneficial when data is limited. However, given the scale of the HCP dataset, it’s debatable whether the lack of spatial consistency is a primary limitation in this specific scenario. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the added complexity of the Laplacian penalty is justified given the available data size. A more rigorous comparison, perhaps with varying dataset sizes, would strengthen this claim.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a strong validation of the extracted regions. While the authors show that the resulting dictionaries are interpretable (to those with expertise), there’s little evidence of how well these regions align with known anatomical structures or functional networks. A more compelling validation would involve assessing the consistency of the learned regions across different subjects and comparing them to established brain atlases. The discussion of reproducibility is limited and doesn't provide sufficient detail. The validation relying on predicting behavioral variables is a step in the right direction but the gains over baselines aren’t substantial enough to strongly support the approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The proposed method is technically sound and extends existing online dictionary learning techniques.
*   The combination of sparsity and structural priors is a potentially valuable approach for extracting meaningful brain regions.
*   The online learning aspect enables scalability to large datasets.
*   The code will be made publicly available.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The subjective nature of the primary results (visual comparison of dictionary atoms) makes it difficult to objectively assess the improvement.
*   The benefits of spatial regularization are not convincingly demonstrated, especially given the size of the HCP dataset.
*   The validation of the extracted regions is limited and doesn’t provide strong evidence of their biological relevance.
*   The gains in predictive performance are modest.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the technical contribution is solid and the method is well-presented, the lack of compelling evidence supporting its practical benefits raises concerns. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more rigorous validation of the extracted regions and a more convincing demonstration of the advantages of spatial regularization, particularly in the context of large datasets. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing these concerns in a revised version. A clearer demonstration of the method's utility and impact is crucial for justifying its inclusion in NIPS.
