## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a novel approach to functional brain imaging analysis by extending online dictionary learning with a combined Sobolev (smoothness) and L1 (sparsity) regularization. The core idea – leveraging population variability to define brain regions through structured sparse decomposition – is interesting and builds upon prior work in dictionary learning for fMRI analysis, such as Mairal et al. (2010). The authors correctly identify the limitations of univariate models and the need for principled ROI definition, positioning their work within a relevant and active research area. 

The main originality lies in the combination of Sobolev and L1 regularization within the dictionary learning framework. This aims to extract spatially coherent, interpretable components (blobs) from fMRI data, addressing the issue of noisy or scattered activations often obtained with purely L1-regularized methods. The authors also highlight the scalability of their online approach, crucial for handling large fMRI datasets.

However, while the proposed method is conceptually sound, the experimental results presented do not convincingly demonstrate significant improvements over simpler baselines. The gains over standard online dictionary learning (SODL) and CanICA are described as “minor or unclear,” and the analysis of these results feels insufficient. The authors acknowledge that the performance gap closes with increasing data size, but don’t fully explore the implications of this finding. A more detailed ablation study, isolating the impact of each regularization term, would be beneficial. 

Furthermore, the manuscript suffers from a lack of polish. The reviewer noted draft comments (e.g., line 146) and the writing is often dense and difficult to follow. The extensive mathematical derivations, while rigorous, could be streamlined for clarity. The discussion of hyperparameter tuning, while present, feels somewhat disconnected from the experimental results. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of regularization techniques for fMRI analysis.
*   Scalable online learning approach suitable for large datasets.
*   Potential for improved interpretability of learned brain regions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited and unclear experimental improvements over existing methods.
*   Insufficient analysis of results and lack of ablation studies.
*   Poorly written and organized manuscript, hindering clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting idea, but its contribution is currently limited by the lack of compelling experimental evidence and the manuscript’s overall quality. While the approach has potential, it requires further refinement and a more thorough evaluation to justify acceptance to a competitive conference like NIPS. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the concerns raised above. A clearer presentation of results, a more in-depth analysis, and a significant improvement in writing quality are essential for this work to reach its full potential.
