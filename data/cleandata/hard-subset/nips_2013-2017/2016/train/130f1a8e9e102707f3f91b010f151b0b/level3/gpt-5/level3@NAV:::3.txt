Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning algorithm that augments Mairal et al. (2010) with a Sobolev/Laplacian prior on dictionary atoms, in addition to sparsity constraints. The goal is to obtain spatially compact, smooth “blobs” that delineate brain regions from large-scale fMRI data. This connects to structured sparsity and spatial regularization lines (GraphNet/Tikhonov smoothing, TV-ℓ1, structured sparse PCA) and to group ICA for multi-subject decomposition. The novelty is implementing a Laplacian penalty directly on atoms within an online framework, claiming near-SODL scalability.

Quality: The optimization is plausible (block coordinate descent with ridge coding; FISTA for Laplacian-regularized denoising) and the complexity overhead appears modest. However, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate improvement over strong baselines: most evidence is visual, and the “blobs” are difficult to assess objectively. Quantitative gains (explained variance, stability, prediction) are reported but either favor ICA for EV/prediction or remain small and sensitive to hyperparameters. Crucially, validation does not isolate the contribution of the Sobolev prior from simpler alternatives (e.g., input pre-smoothing, TV penalties, Laplacian on codes rather than atoms).

Clarity: The method is described in reasonable detail, but important choices (data preprocessing, Laplacian construction, boundary conditions, hyperparameter selection) are under-specified. Figures rely on visual inspection without objective readability; an untrained reader cannot judge “better blobs.” The evaluation section would benefit from clearer, reproducible metrics and ablations.

Originality: The idea of spatially regularizing latent maps is well-known (GraphNet, TV-ℓ1, structured PCA). The main contribution is the online implementation and empirical study at HCP scale. This is incremental rather than conceptually novel.

Significance: With abundant data (HCP), smoothing may be unnecessary, and the paper does not show that the proposed prior addresses a real limitation (e.g., low-n regimes) beyond producing smoother maps. Expected neuroimaging validations—split-half reproducibility across subjects, inter-subject consistency, correspondence to known atlases—are missing or only qualitatively addressed.

Pros for acceptance:
- Scalable, simple extension of a widely used online DL framework.
- Practical interest to neuroimaging communities seeking structured components.
- Modest computational overhead and clear algorithmic recipe.

Cons against acceptance:
- Impact unclear; improvements over baselines are hard to judge and largely qualitative.
- Validation is subjective; lacks rigorous inter-subject consistency/reproducibility analyses.
- Limited comparison to established structured penalties (TV/GraphNet) and to input smoothing.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity not convincingly resolved; ablations incomplete.

Suggestions: Quantify split-half/stability (Dice/MI/correlation of binarized atoms), inter-subject consistency, and atlas correspondence; compare to TV and pre-smoothing; show benefits in low/medium-n; report statistical significance for prediction; include thorough ablations (γ, τ, Laplacian vs none).
