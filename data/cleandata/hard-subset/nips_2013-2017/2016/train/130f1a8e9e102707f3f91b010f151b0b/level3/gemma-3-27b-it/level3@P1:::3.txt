This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method incorporating both sparsity and structural priors to decompose brain images, aiming to delineate brain regions. The authors address the problem of inter-subject variability in neuroimaging, arguing that current methods often discard valuable information. They extend existing online dictionary learning work by adding a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce smoothness and compactness in the learned dictionary atoms, effectively encouraging the formation of "blobs" representing brain structures. The method is presented as scalable due to its online nature, and preliminary experiments on HCP data demonstrate improved interpretability and capture of inter-subject variability compared to ICA and standard dictionary learning.

This work builds on a well-established line of research in dictionary learning for signal processing and, more specifically, its application to neuroimaging. The use of online learning, extending the work of Mairal et al. (2010), is a practical consideration for large-scale datasets. The introduction of a spatial prior (Laplacian penalty) is a reasonable approach to enforce structure and address the common problem of noisy or fragmented segmentation in brain imaging, drawing on related work in total variation regularization (e.g., Abraham et al., 2015). The authors correctly identify the benefits of dictionary learning for dimensionality reduction and the potential for ROI extraction.

The strengths of this paper lie in its technical soundness and the clear formulation of the proposed method (Smooth-SODL). The algorithm appears well-defined, and the authors demonstrate awareness of computational considerations, particularly regarding the efficiency of the online learning approach and the cost of the Laplacian penalty. The extension of Mairal et al.’s work is a non-trivial addition, and the authors acknowledge and address the potential challenges related to parameter selection. The experiments using the HCP dataset are also a positive aspect, providing a basis for evaluation on a large, publicly available benchmark.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The primary concern is the difficulty in objectively assessing the improvements claimed by the authors. The evaluation heavily relies on visual comparison of the learned dictionary atoms (the “blobs”), which is subjective and lacks quantitative rigor. While the authors state the resulting atoms are "more interpretable," this is not clearly demonstrated for someone unfamiliar with neuroimaging. The comparison to ICA (CanICA) is hampered by the need for pre-smoothing in the latter, creating an uneven playing field. The claim that the smoothness penalty leads to “better capture of inter-subject variability” is not sufficiently supported by quantitative metrics beyond explained variance. 

The discussion of the impact of data size is also problematic. The authors acknowledge that with abundant data, the benefits of the spatial regularization may diminish.  This raises a fundamental question: under what conditions is this method truly valuable? The paper doesn’t convincingly argue that the addition of the Laplacian penalty addresses a critical limitation of existing methods when applied to datasets like the HCP, where data scarcity isn’t the primary challenge.  The experiments showing a boost in explained variance with limited data are interesting but don't justify the additional complexity for large-scale datasets.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a compelling demonstration of how the learned dictionary atoms translate into meaningful insights about brain organization. Ideally, one would expect to see a consolidation of brain-region differences across subjects, and the inter-subject consistency used as a validation metric. The validation presented with behavioral scores is reasonable but doesn't fully capitalize on the potential of the method to reveal underlying population structure. The paper also lacks comparison to more recent and advanced brain parcellation techniques.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound extension of existing online dictionary learning methods.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging—modeling inter-subject variability.
*   Scalable algorithm suitable for large datasets.
*   Demonstrates reasonable performance on a standard benchmark dataset.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Subjective evaluation based primarily on visual inspection.
*   Lack of strong quantitative evidence to support the claimed improvements.
*   Unclear justification for the added complexity in the context of large-scale datasets.
*   Limited comparison to related work in brain parcellation.
*   Insufficient demonstration of the biological relevance of the learned dictionary atoms.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the technical work is solid, the lack of a convincing validation strategy and the limited demonstration of practical significance raise concerns. The authors need to strengthen the evaluation, providing more quantitative metrics and demonstrating how the learned structures translate into meaningful insights about brain function and organization.  A more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art brain parcellation methods would also be beneficial. I believe the core idea has potential, but the paper, in its current form, does not make a compelling case for its significance.
