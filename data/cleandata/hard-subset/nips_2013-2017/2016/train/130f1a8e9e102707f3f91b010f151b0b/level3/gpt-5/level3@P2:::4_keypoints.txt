- Proposal: Smooth-SODL — online dictionary learning for neuroimaging adding a Laplacian/Sobolev penalty and L1-type atom constraints; codes via ridge regression; atoms updated by convex Laplacian-regularized denoising solved with FISTA.

- Relation to prior work: builds on online dictionary learning (Mairal et al.) and structured sparsity/GraphNet/TV-L1 approaches; aims to bring Laplacian smoothness into scalable online DL for fMRI.

- Technical soundness: overall scheme is standard and convex per-atom; Lipschitz/FISTA analysis and convergence claims follow prior work.

- Concern: claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e-6 accuracy is implausible without warm-start evidence or objective/primal-dual traces.

- Concern: the proposed γ schedule and the bound tying γ to At[j,j]/t are informal and lack empirical validation or practical guidance.

- Ambiguity: atom constraint alternates between L1-ball and simplex (nonnegativity vs signed atoms) without clear justification or ablation of effects.

- Missing comparison: no ablation or analysis of using L1 codes versus ridge codes, despite both being mentioned.

- Empirics summary: HCP experiments show modestly more structured atoms, slightly higher stability, similar explained variance to SODL, and slightly worse EV than CanICA; runtime comparable to SODL and faster than CanICA in reported setup.

- Evaluation weakness: interpretability claims are mainly qualitative; stability lacks statistical rigor; missing structured baselines like TV-L1 DL or structured sparse PCA/NMF.

- Manuscript clarity: readable but rushed, with typos, dangling text, scattered definitions (constraint set C, Δ), and incomplete algorithmic details (projections, stopping criteria, FFT/memory variants).

- Originality: moderate — natural but useful extension of SODL; methodological step from γ=0 to γ>0 is incremental.

- Significance: moderate — practical value for practitioners seeking smoother, localized components, but empirical gains are small and interpretability not rigorously quantified.

- Strengths: clear practical motivation, scalable algorithm with modest overhead, convex atom subproblem enabling first-order methods, early-data advantage and improved stability, and comprehensive comparison to SODL and CanICA.

- Weaknesses: limited novelty, modest/uneven empirical improvements, lack of thorough hyperparameter/stability/interpretability analysis, missing structured baselines, and clarity/overclaim issues.

- Suggested experiments/improvements: ablations (γ=0 vs >0; Laplacian vs TV; simplex vs L1-ball; ridge vs L1 codes); quantify interpretability (contiguity, Laplacian energy, atlas overlap, split-half Dice); detail FISTA inner-loop (stopping, warm-starts, iterations); justify constraints; compare fair baselines and report learning curves with statistical tests; fix typos and standardize notation.

- Arguments for acceptance: scalable, practical method with early-data and stability benefits and potential for adoption (OSS intent).

- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, unclear empirical advantage, and insufficient methodological/experimental rigor and clarity.

- Overall recommendation: Weak reject.

- Reviewer confidence: high on dictionary learning/neuroimaging methods; moderate on HCP task specifics.
