- Proposes Smooth-SODL: extends Mairal et al. (2010) online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness in dictionary atoms.  
- Produces contiguous, blob-like components that are more neuroscientifically interpretable than standard sparse outputs.  
- Uses a Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) scheme designed to scale to massive datasets with many subjects and voxels.  
- Empirical results on the Human Connectome Project show smoother, more stable, and more interpretable dictionaries than SODL and CanICA, especially in low-to-medium data regimes.  
- Technical approach is sound and well-motivated; adding spatial smoothness is appropriate for neuroimaging.  
- Experimental evaluation is a major strength: comprehensive metrics (qualitative, explained variance, stability, behavioral prediction) and comparisons on a large real-world dataset.  
- Demonstrated benefits: visually coherent components, improved stability across splits, and better capture of inter-subject variability.  
- Primary weakness: lacks theoretical analysis of the non-convex objective and the optimization landscape; BCD convergence claims are not fully justified with the added Laplacian term.  
- Relies heavily on empirical validation, leaving open questions about solution existence, uniqueness, and theoretical guarantees.  
- Clarity issue: derivation of the BCD dictionary update (Sec. 3.1) and transition to Eq. (7) / Algorithm 2 is hard to follow and not self-contained.  
- Inadequate explanation: referring to [16] for details on a new Laplacian-involved subproblem is insufficient; needs a step-by-step derivation for reproducibility.  
- Hyperparameter discussion lacks intuition, particularly the relationship between step size (L_t) and γ (Eq. (9)).  
- Originality: novel combination of online dictionary learning and Laplacian regularization for scalable neuroimaging decomposition.  
- Significance: high potential impact for neuroimaging community—scalable, interpretable component learning for large cohorts (e.g., UK Biobank, HCP).  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, presents a useful scalable method, and shows strong empirical advantages likely to be adopted by practitioners.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing theoretical guarantees and insufficient clarity in algorithmic derivation hinder full evaluation and reproducibility.  
- Overall: strong empirical contribution with practical significance; recommend acceptance contingent on improving theoretical discussion and clarity of algorithm derivation.
