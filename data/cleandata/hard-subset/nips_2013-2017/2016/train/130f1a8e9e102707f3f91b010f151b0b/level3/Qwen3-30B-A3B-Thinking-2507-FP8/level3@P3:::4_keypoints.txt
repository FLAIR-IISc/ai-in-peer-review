- Proposes Smooth-SODL: online dictionary learning with Sobolev (Laplacian) spatial smoothness + L1 sparsity to extract structured fMRI components.  
- Implements method via block-coordinate descent with FISTA updates, reporting only a 2–3× runtime increase over standard online dictionary learning.  
- Main claimed benefit: produces spatially coherent "blobs" that better capture inter-subject variability and yield more interpretable brain regions.  
- Demonstrated qualitatively on HCP (500 subjects) with apparent improvements over SODL and CanICA in interpretability.  
- Quantitative results are weak: CanICA outperforms Smooth-SODL on test explained variance (60% vs 55%) and behavioral prediction.  
- Claimed "14-fold gain" of Smooth-SODL over SODL in small-data regimes is interesting but lacks statistical validation and practical significance.  
- Experimental setup lacks critical reproducibility details (e.g., selection of the 40 atoms) and systematic hyperparameter justification or ablations for γ and τ.  
- Manuscript clarity issues: draft comments remain, confusing writing in parts, and insufficient detail in key sections.  
- Originality is limited: combination of Laplacian + L1 is novel for this application but is a straightforward extension of Mairal et al. (2010) and not well differentiated from prior structured dictionary methods.  
- Significance is unconvincing: improvements are marginal, inconsistent across metrics, and diminish with larger datasets.  
- Technical inconsistencies: authors claim a "non-trivial extension" yet report only minimal computational overhead and provide weak theoretical justification for the Laplacian penalty in fMRI.  
- Pro-accept: tackles an important problem, conceptually interesting regularizer combination, practical implementation available in Nilearn.  
- Con-accept: insufficient experimental rigor, marginal gains, poor manuscript clarity, and unsupported claims about advantages over existing methods.  
- Overall: method is technically sound but fails to demonstrate substantial scientific advancement; needs stronger experiments, clearer presentation, and better justification to merit acceptance.
