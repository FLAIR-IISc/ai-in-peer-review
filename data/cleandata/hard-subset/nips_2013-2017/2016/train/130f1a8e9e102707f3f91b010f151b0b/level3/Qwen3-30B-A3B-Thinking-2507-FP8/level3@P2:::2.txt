

# Review for "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper proposes an extension of online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) for neuroimaging data by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage spatially structured dictionary atoms. The authors aim to learn brain regions from functional imaging data by obtaining decompositions with structured and sparse components, which they argue better capture inter-subject variability compared to standard approaches. The method builds upon the structured sparse dictionary learning framework (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2012; Jenatton et al., 2011) but extends it to an online setting suitable for massive datasets. The work relates to prior efforts in dictionary learning for neuroimaging (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2014; Abraham et al., MICCAI), but addresses the computational bottleneck of structured approaches by adapting the online framework.

## Strengths

The paper makes a technically sound contribution by extending the online dictionary learning framework to incorporate spatial structure through a Laplacian penalty. The algorithmic approach is well-motivated and carefully derived, with convergence guarantees for the proposed method. The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation on a large HCP dataset, comparing against state-of-the-art methods (CanICA, SODL) across multiple metrics including visual quality, explained variance, stability, and predictive performance for behavioral variables. The implementation is being made available through the Nilearn library, enhancing reproducibility. The method's computational efficiency (only 2-3x slower than standard SODL) is a significant practical advantage for large-scale neuroimaging applications.

## Weaknesses

**Empirical Support:** The paper's main weakness is the lack of clear, quantitative evidence that the Sobolev prior provides meaningful improvements. Figure 2(a) claims that Smooth-SODL produces "more structured" atoms, but the visual comparison is problematic - different slices are shown for different methods, making direct comparison impossible. The paper should provide quantitative metrics for spatial structure (e.g., blob compactness, smoothness scores) to support the claim that the atoms are more structured.

**Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript contains multiple editing issues that impair understanding. The mathematical notation is inconsistent (e.g., in Equations 6 and 8, transpose placement is confusing), and several equations contain apparent typos (e.g., "Graph-Net penalty can imposes" in the abstract). The description of the algorithm in Section 3.1 is difficult to follow due to inconsistent variable references and unclear presentation of the dictionary update procedure. The authors should provide a complete derivation of the solution to Equation (6) in an appendix.

**Missing Context:** The HCP dataset reference (Van Essen et al., 2013) is missing from the references, which is a significant oversight for a paper using this dataset. The paper should also clarify the specific neuroscientific rationale for preferring spatially structured atoms over sparse ones in the context of brain imaging.

**Figure Interpretation:** Figure 2(c) claims that Smooth-SODL outperforms other methods in predicting behavioral variables, but the caption and legend are unclear about the baselines being compared (PCA is mentioned but not shown). The claim that "R^2 > 0.1" is ambiguous without specifying which curves support this claim.

## Quality and Technical Soundness

The technical approach is sound and well-grounded in existing work on dictionary learning and spatial regularization. The authors correctly identify the computational advantage of online methods for large-scale neuroimaging data. However, the empirical evaluation lacks the rigor needed to validate the main claim that spatial regularization improves interpretability and performance. The learning curves in Table 1 show that spatial regularization provides a significant advantage in small data regimes, but the paper fails to quantify how the structured atoms are better for neuroscience interpretation.

## Originality and Significance

The core novelty lies in extending online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure. While the Laplacian penalty for spatial structure has been used before (e.g., in Varoquaux et al., 2014), the online adaptation is a meaningful contribution. The significance of this work is moderate - it provides a useful tool for neuroimaging analysis that could potentially be applied to other medical imaging tasks. However, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that this method provides substantially better results than existing approaches for the intended application.

## Overall Assessment

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides a computationally efficient method for structured dictionary learning
- Addresses an important problem in neuroimaging analysis
- Implementation will be made publicly available
- Demonstrates clear computational advantage over previous structured approaches

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Fails to provide quantitative evidence for the claimed advantages of spatial regularization
- Contains multiple presentation issues that impair understanding
- Empirical results are not sufficiently analyzed to support the main claims
- Missing references and unclear descriptions undermine the paper's credibility

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes a technically sound contribution with potential value for neuroimaging analysis, but the empirical evaluation is insufficient to support the main claims. The authors have addressed some of these concerns in their rebuttal, raising the technical quality rating from 2 to 3. To strengthen the paper for acceptance, the authors should:

1. Provide quantitative metrics for spatial structure (e.g., blob compactness, smoothness scores)
2. Fix all mathematical notation inconsistencies and typos
3. Include a complete derivation of Equation (6) in an appendix
4. Clarify Figure 2 with consistent slices for all methods
5. Add the missing HCP dataset reference and clarify behavioral prediction methodology

With these revisions, the paper would be a strong contribution to the field of neuroimaging and dictionary learning. As it stands, the current version does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claimed advantages of the Sobolev prior.
