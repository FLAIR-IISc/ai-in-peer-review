## Review of Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning

This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for decomposing brain images into structured and sparse components, aiming to delineate brain structures from functional images. The approach combines sparsity constraints (L1 norm) with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage spatially smooth, compact dictionary atoms – essentially “blobs” representing brain regions. The authors extend the online dictionary learning work of Mairal et al. (2010) and demonstrate its application on large-scale brain imaging data from the Human Connectome Project.

The problem addressed is a significant one in neuroimaging. Inter-subject variability is often discarded in statistical analyses, despite potentially containing valuable information. Univariate models struggle with the high dimensionality of brain imaging data, and defining Regions of Interest (ROIs) is often a manual and subjective process. The proposed method offers a data-driven approach to ROI definition by leveraging population-level variability to discover spatially coherent structures. This builds on existing work attempting to define spatial units for brain data analysis (e.g., clustering approaches cited in the paper – references [21, 14]) but proposes a model closer to PCA/ICA with specific spatial and sparsity penalties ([25, 1]).  The extension of online dictionary learning is a logical step, addressing the scalability issues inherent in processing large neuroimaging datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of L1 sparsity with a Laplacian penalty within an *online* dictionary learning framework appears novel. While structured sparsity has been explored previously (e.g., [1], [3]), applying it within an online learning setting for large-scale brain imaging is a worthwhile contribution.
* **Scalability:** The online learning approach is a key strength, allowing the method to scale to arbitrarily sized datasets, which is crucial for modern neuroimaging studies. This addresses a practical limitation of many existing methods.
* **Empirical Results:**  The preliminary experiments on HCP data demonstrate promising results. The method appears to extract more interpretable dictionaries with better spatial structure compared to standard online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA (CanICA). The results suggesting improved performance in predicting behavioral variables (Fig. 2c) are particularly encouraging. The comparison against CanICA is well-motivated, as it highlights the benefits of the sparsity and structured priors.
* **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning (α, γ, τ) and initialization, while detailed, acknowledges the challenges and offers guidance. The authors' consideration of the trade-offs between explainability, stability, and fidelity is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed method. While the authors mention convergence guarantees based on the work of Bertsekas [6] and Mairal et al. [16], they do not provide any bounds on the error, convergence rate, or conditions for the recovery of meaningful structures.  The justification for the Laplacian penalty and its impact on the resulting dictionary are largely empirical.  A theoretical investigation of the properties of the Smooth-SODL model would significantly strengthen the contribution.
* **Manuscript Quality:** The writing quality is surprisingly poor for a NIPS submission. There are instances of careless phrasing and a lack of polish. The mention of leftover comments within the text (“146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v.”) is particularly concerning and indicates a lack of thorough proofreading. This detracts from the clarity and credibility of the work.
* **Limited Baselines:** While CanICA and SODL are included as baselines, the comparison could be more comprehensive.  Exploring other state-of-the-art methods for brain region segmentation or feature extraction (e.g., spatially regularized ICA, graph-based methods) would provide a more robust assessment of the proposed method’s performance.
* **Overstated Claims:** Some claims are somewhat overstated.  For example, the assertion that the method will “become the de facto way to do dimensionality reduction and ROI extraction” is overly optimistic and not fully supported by the presented results.
* **γ parameter selection:** The discussion of the γ parameter selection is too vague. Saying γ should be chosen such that it is O(max At[j,j] /t) doesn't explain the selection of the constant of proportionality in practice.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a challenging and important problem in neuroimaging.
* The proposed method is novel and offers a practical solution for large-scale data analysis.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the approach.
* The online learning framework makes the method scalable to large datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of theoretical analysis is a significant weakness.
* The manuscript quality is subpar and requires substantial revision.
* The limited comparison to other state-of-the-art methods weakens the claims of superiority.
* Overstated claims.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed method offers a novel and scalable approach to brain region segmentation. However, the lack of theoretical analysis and the poor manuscript quality are significant drawbacks.  I recommend **rejection** in its current form.  I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses by providing a more rigorous theoretical foundation for their method, thoroughly proofreading and polishing the manuscript, and conducting a more comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art techniques.  A revised submission addressing these concerns would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
