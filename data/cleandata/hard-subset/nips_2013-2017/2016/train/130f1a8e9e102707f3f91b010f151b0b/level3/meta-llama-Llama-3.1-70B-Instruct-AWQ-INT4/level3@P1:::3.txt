This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components, referred to as Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL). The method extends the standard online dictionary-learning framework by incorporating a Sobolev prior to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a large public dataset, showcasing improvements in extracting structured and denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its underlying principles. The use of a Sobolev prior to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms is an interesting idea, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in extracting more interpretable and meaningful brain regions.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. Firstly, the impact of the proposed algorithm is not entirely clear, and it is difficult to judge the improvement it brings over existing methods. The main results rely on visual comparisons of identified brain "blobs," which may not be clear to an untrained eye. Furthermore, the data quality and choice of datasets greatly affect the results, and the addition of smoothness/spatial-consistency penalties unsurprisingly yields smoother blobs.

Moreover, the reviewer notes that with large datasets like the Human Connectome Project, smoothing may not address a real data scarcity problem. The presented validation is also subjective and does not clearly demonstrate the benefit of the proposed method. The reviewer expected a more rigorous validation, including the consolidation of brain-region differences across subjects and the use of inter-subject consistency for validation.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel extension to the standard online dictionary-learning framework, which is a significant contribution to the field. However, the idea of using a Sobolev prior to impose spatial structure on dictionary atoms is not entirely new, and the authors could have provided more context and discussion on the novelty of their approach.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging, namely the extraction of meaningful brain regions from large datasets. The proposed method has the potential to improve our understanding of brain function and organization, and its application to other fields, such as computer vision, is also possible.

In conclusion, while the paper is well-written and proposes an interesting idea, its impact and improvement over existing methods are not entirely clear. The reviewer suggests that the authors provide more rigorous validation and discussion on the novelty of their approach to strengthen their contribution.
