This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning framework that augments Mairal et al. (2010) with structured spatial priors on dictionary atoms via a Laplacian penalty, alongside sparsity constraints, to identify interpretable, blob-like brain regions. The optimization is cast as block-coordinate descent with ridge-coded updates and per-atom denoising subproblems solved by FISTA; the authors argue the added structure incurs only a 2–3x runtime over vanilla SODL while preserving scalability. On HCP task-fMRI, they report improved interpretability and stability over SODL and PCA/ICA-based baselines (e.g., CanICA), with modest loss in explained variance. The approach relates to structured sparsity and GraphNet models (Grosenick et al.), TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning for neuroimaging (Abraham et al.), and structured sparse PCA (Jenatton et al.), and is conceptually close to recent large-scale DL work (Mensch et al.).

Strengths
- Interesting and valuable problem: scalable, structured decomposition for ROI discovery addresses a clear need in neuroimaging.
- Methodologically plausible: combining Laplacian smoothness with online DL is natural and likely to improve interpretability.
- Empirical trends are sensible: better stability/interpretability than SODL and CanICA; small-data advantage is well-motivated.
- Potential for practical impact via Nilearn integration and use on public data (HCP).

Weaknesses
- Quality: Theoretical support is thin. There is no convergence-rate analysis under the composite constraint/penalty, no regret/sample-complexity characterization, and no guarantees on the effect of the Laplacian term (e.g., bias–variance or recovery of localized atoms under simple generative models). Claims such as “1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e-6 accuracy” and the γ schedule (Eq. 9) are asserted without proofs or empirical sensitivity analyses.
- Experimental scope: Results are limited to a single task; baselines omit stronger structured methods (e.g., TV-ℓ1 DL, GraphNet-like penalties, elastic-net atoms, pre-smoothing + SODL), and ablations (γ vs τ, ridge vs L1 codes) are partial.
- Clarity: Manuscript quality is poor. Numerous typos and inconsistent notation (e.g., V vs v, U vs u) and leftover comments (e.g., “XXX: I don’t understand the switch between V and v.”) indicate lack of proofreading. Several references are incomplete; figure/table pointers are hard to follow; some claims are not reproducible without code (which is “forthcoming”).

Originality and significance
- Originality is moderate: the contribution is a practical integration of known ingredients; the online treatment for Laplacian-structured atoms is useful but incremental.
- Potential significance is good if theory, baselines, and presentation are strengthened.

Pros for acceptance
- Timely topic; practical, scalable method; improved interpretability/stability; use of large public data; likely community interest.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient theory; limited experimental validation/baselines; manuscript quality and reproducibility concerns (no code yet).

Recommendation: In current form, I lean toward rejection. With thorough proofreading, code release, stronger baselines/ablations, and at least partial theoretical support (e.g., convergence under composite regularization, sensitivity analyses), this could become a solid contribution.
