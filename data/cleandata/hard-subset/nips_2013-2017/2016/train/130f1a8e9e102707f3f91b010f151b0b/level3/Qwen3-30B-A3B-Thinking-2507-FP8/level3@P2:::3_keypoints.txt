- Proposal: Extends online dictionary learning by adding a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial smoothness, yielding structured "blob" atoms for brain regions.  
- Framing: Cast as an extension of Mairal et al.'s SODL to a "Smooth-SODL" that preserves online scalability while imposing spatial structure.  
- Motivation: Aims to define brain regions that capture inter-subject variability without relying on predefined ROIs.  
- Relation to prior work: Connects to structured dictionary learning and structured sparsity literature (Varoquaux, Jenatton, Grosenick) but does not clearly differentiate from similar neuroimaging methods (e.g., Varoquaux, Abraham).  
- Quality (algorithm): Technical approach is sound, well-motivated, with convergence guarantees and modest computational overhead (2–3x SODL).  
- Quality (experiments): Experimental validation is weak—relies mainly on subjective visual comparisons and lacks rigorous statistical support.  
- Metrics: Uses standard metrics (explained variance, reproducibility, prediction), but comparisons do not convincingly show benefits of spatial regularization on large HCP data.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally well-written and explains the algorithm well, but experimental details (hyperparameter selection, implementation specifics) are insufficient for reproducibility.  
- Originality: Novel application of a Sobolev penalty in online dictionary learning for neuroimaging, but novelty is limited given similar structured penalties in other domains.  
- Significance: Questionable — claimed benefits (capturing inter-subject variability, improved interpretability) are not convincingly demonstrated, especially on large datasets where data scarcity is less relevant.  
- Strengths: Technically solid extension, scalable implementation, and open-source release in Nilearn.  
- Weaknesses: Lack of convincing quantitative evidence, heavy reliance on subjective visuals, insufficient comparison fairness (e.g., tCanICA vs. CanICA), and unclear necessity of regularization for large datasets.  
- Acceptance pros: Well-executed, scalable technical contribution with open-source implementation.  
- Acceptance cons: Insufficiently rigorous validation and questionable impact, leading reviewer to recommend rejection.
