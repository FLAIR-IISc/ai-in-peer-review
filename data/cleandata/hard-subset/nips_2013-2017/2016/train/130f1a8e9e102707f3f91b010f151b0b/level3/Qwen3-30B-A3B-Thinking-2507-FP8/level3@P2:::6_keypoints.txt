Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online dictionary learning extension incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness in fMRI dictionary atoms.  
Aims to extract structured, sparse, and spatially compact brain regions, improving interpretability over standard dictionary learning.  
Builds on Mairal et al. (2010) online dictionary learning and adapts it to handle structured spatial penalties efficiently.  
Provides detailed algorithmic description and complexity analysis; method is only ~2–3× slower than baseline SODL.  
Comprehensive experiments on HCP fMRI data comparing SODL and CanICA across explained variance, stability, reproducibility, and predictive performance.  
Empirical results show Smooth-SODL yields more interpretable patterns, better stability, and improved predictive performance, especially for small/medium datasets.  
Method scales to large datasets, making it practical for modern neuroimaging studies.  
Technically sound implementation but the optimization problem is non-convex and solved with a heuristic Block Coordinate Descent.  
No theoretical guarantees provided for convergence, optimality, or existence/uniqueness of solutions—major theoretical weakness.  
Paper relies primarily on empirical validation without sufficient theoretical justification for the non-convex formulation.  
Clarity: generally well-organized and reproducible; initial grammatical/phrasing issues were addressed after rebuttal.  
Originality: meaningful extension adapting online dictionary learning to structured spatial penalties; Laplacian penalty is a novel application here.  
Significance: addresses an important neuroimaging problem and demonstrates that spatial regularization is beneficial even with abundant data.  
Strengths: novel approach, thorough experiments, practical scalability, and clear empirical improvements over state-of-the-art.  
Weaknesses: lack of theoretical analysis/guarantees, insufficient justification for Laplacian choice, and initially minor clarity issues.  
Arguments for acceptance: significant problem, novel scalable solution, strong empirical gains, and well-designed evaluation.  
Arguments against acceptance: absence of theoretical guarantees and weaker connection to existing structured dictionary learning theory.  
Overall assessment: promising and valuable empirical contribution despite theoretical gaps; reviewer recommends acceptance.  
Recommendation: Accept.
