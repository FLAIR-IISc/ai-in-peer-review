

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning for brain imaging that combines sparsity (via L1 regularization) and spatial smoothness (via Laplacian regularization) to extract structured, interpretable brain regions from fMRI data. The authors build upon Mairal et al.'s SODL framework [16], adding a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure while maintaining computational efficiency. The method is demonstrated on HCP fMRI data to extract functional brain maps that better capture inter-subject variability than state-of-the-art approaches.

The paper's strengths are significant: the experimental results are thorough and compelling, showing clear advantages over SODL and CanICA across multiple metrics including explained variance, stability, and behavioral prediction. The method's practical utility is well-demonstrated across small, medium, and large-scale regimes, addressing a genuine need in neuroimaging for interpretable brain region extraction. The approach is novel in combining spatial structure with online dictionary learning, extending previous work on structured dictionary learning [1, 25] while maintaining computational efficiency.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses in theoretical foundation. The non-convex problem lacks proofs of solution existence or uniqueness, and the algorithm provides no theoretical convergence guarantees despite being based on block coordinate descent. The authors acknowledge this limitation but rely primarily on empirical evidence rather than theoretical analysis. While the manuscript clarity issue (line 146) has been addressed in the rebuttal, the theoretical gap remains a concern for scientific rigor.

The work is highly significant for neuroimaging, offering a practical solution to an important problem with demonstrated real-world utility. The experimental validation is strong, though the theoretical shortcomings prevent it from being a fully complete scientific contribution. 

Pro: Strong empirical validation, novel combination of sparsity and smoothness, practical impact for brain imaging.
Con: Theoretical weaknesses, lack of convergence guarantees for non-convex problem, over-reliance on empirical evidence.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to neuroimaging methodology, but the theoretical limitations prevent it from being a definitive scientific advance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical gaps.
