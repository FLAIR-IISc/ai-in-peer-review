- Problem is relevant: identifying meaningful brain regions from functional imaging is important and well-motivated.  
- Method builds on online dictionary learning (Mairal et al. 2010) and frames ROI definition in a dictionary-learning context, but is largely incremental.  
- Main technical contribution is adding a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage spatially smooth dictionary atoms; conceptually sound but not novel.  
- Sobolev regularization feels grafted onto existing work and lacks intrinsic motivation within the framework.  
- Scalability is a strength: leveraging online learning enables application to large neuroimaging datasets.  
- Empirical results on HCP data show improvements over standard online dictionary learning and CanICA in interpretability, stability, and predictive performance.  
- Benefits of spatial regularization are most apparent with limited data (learning curve results).  
- Implementation release in Nilearn is a positive for reproducibility and adoption.  
- Novelty is weak: applying known regularization to a standard framework likely falls short of NIPS novelty expectations.  
- Motivation for imposing “structure” via Sobolev regularization is unclear and not well-justified.  
- Technical errors/omissions: probable notation error in Eq.(1) (transpose placement) and insufficient justification for using L2 codes instead of L1 as in prior work.  
- Clarity and presentation are poor, with numerous typos and convoluted writing that hinder comprehension.  
- Hyperparameter tuning discussion is brief and the relationship between γ and τ is presented without intuitive explanation.  
- Specific clarity issues: the term “compact” in constraint set C is vague and should be defined precisely.  
- Convergence discussion is incomplete; the effect of the Laplacian penalty on convergence guarantees is not analyzed.  
- Figure 1 (hyperparameter selection) lacks sufficient detail in the caption and explanation.  
- Comparison with CanICA is useful but the explanation of why CanICA requires smoothing is minimal.  
- Arguments for acceptance: empirical improvements, scalability, and planned public implementation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak novelty, unclear motivation, technical flaws, poor writing, and insufficient coverage of related work on spatial structure methods.  
- Overall recommendation: reject as currently written; significant revisions needed to strengthen novelty, motivation, technical rigor, and presentation before reconsideration.
