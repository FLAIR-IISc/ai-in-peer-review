This paper proposes a novel multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, aiming to identify structured and sparse components representing brain regions. The key contribution lies in combining an L1-norm constraint for sparsity with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial smoothness, resulting in decompositions that better delineate brain structures compared to existing methods. The proposed approach extends the online dictionary learning work of Mairal et al. (2010) with a manageable increase in computational cost. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on a large fMRI dataset, showcasing improved interpretability and capture of inter-subject variability.

The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging – defining regions of interest (ROIs) in a principled, data-driven manner, moving beyond traditional segmentation or reliance on anatomical priors. Existing methods often struggle with noise or cross-modality correspondence issues. The proposed approach offers a promising alternative by leveraging the variability across subjects to define these ROIs. The connection to previous work in clustering and dimensionality reduction techniques like PCA and ICA is clearly articulated, and the authors position their contribution as building upon these existing frameworks while addressing their limitations. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, citing relevant papers in dictionary learning, sparse representation, and neuroimaging.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of L1-sparsity and Laplacian regularization for dictionary learning in the context of brain imaging appears novel and addresses a practical limitation of existing methods.
*   **Scalability:** Leveraging the online learning framework from Mairal et al. (2010) ensures scalability to large datasets, a crucial requirement for neuroimaging applications.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results on the HCP dataset are compelling, demonstrating improved interpretability, stability, and predictive performance compared to standard SODL and CanICA. The learning curves (Table 1) convincingly demonstrate the benefit of the spatial regularization, particularly when data is limited.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The proposed method has the potential to be a valuable tool for neuroimaging researchers seeking to identify and analyze brain regions in a data-driven manner. The authors’ commitment to releasing the implementation as part of the Nilearn package further enhances its potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks a rigorous theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm. While the authors acknowledge the non-convexity of the problem, they do not provide any guarantees regarding convergence, optimality, or the existence and uniqueness of solutions.
*   **Algorithm Guarantees:**  The algorithm's convergence is mentioned as being inherited from Mairal et al. (2010), but the impact of the added Laplacian penalty on convergence properties isn't explored.  The sensitivity analysis regarding the parameter γ (line 9) indicates a potential instability that could limit practical applicability.
*   **Clarity & Presentation:** While generally well-written, the paper occasionally suffers from dense technical descriptions.  The issue noted by the authors concerning line 146 is indicative of areas where more clarity is needed. Although addressed during rebuttal, some aspects could still benefit from streamlining.
*   **Comparison to Alternatives:** The comparison to CanICA is valuable, but a more detailed comparison to other spatially regularized dictionary learning methods (e.g., those using Total Variation regularization - Abraham et al., 2011) could further strengthen the paper.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and promising approach to a challenging problem in neuroimaging.
*   The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over existing techniques.
*   The scalability of the algorithm makes it applicable to large-scale datasets.
*   The authors' commitment to releasing the implementation will facilitate further research in this area.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of theoretical analysis and algorithm guarantees raises concerns about the robustness and reliability of the method.
*   While the experimental results are strong, they are primarily based on a single dataset.
*   The clarity and presentation of the paper could be improved.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the lack of rigorous theoretical analysis, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed method addresses an important problem, offers a novel solution, and demonstrates strong empirical performance. The scalability and practical relevance of the approach further strengthen its appeal. 

**Rating:**  Strong Accept (post-rebuttal). The authors’ response to the reviewer’s concerns, particularly regarding the algorithm's properties, was satisfactory. While theoretical guarantees remain an open area for future work, the empirical evidence and potential impact of the method warrant acceptance.
