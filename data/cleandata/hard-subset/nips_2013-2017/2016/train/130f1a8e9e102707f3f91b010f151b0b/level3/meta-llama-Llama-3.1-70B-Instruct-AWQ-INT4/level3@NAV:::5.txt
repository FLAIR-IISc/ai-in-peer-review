This paper proposes a novel method for learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning, dubbed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL). The authors extend the online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev regularization term to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The method is applied to task fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project dataset and compared to state-of-the-art methods like Canonical ICA (CanICA) and sparse online dictionary-learning (SODL).

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of the Sobolev regularization term, which enables the method to learn structured and sparse dictionary atoms that better capture inter-subject variability. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through experiments, showing that it outperforms competing methods in terms of explained variance, stability, and interpretability of the learned dictionaries.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The novelty of the method is somewhat limited, as it builds upon existing work by Mairal et al. (2010) and adds a relatively simple regularization term. The motivation for using Sobolev regularization is not thoroughly justified, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of why this particular regularization term is chosen. Additionally, the paper contains some errors, such as the notation in Eq.(1), which should likely read [V^1, V^2, …, V^r]^T.

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from more analysis and justification of the choice of the l2 norm in Eq.(2) instead of the l1 norm used in Mairal et al. (2010). The reference to "line 146" is unclear and needs clarification. Finally, the paper's language and presentation are poor and contain many typos, which detract from the overall quality of the paper.

In terms of the conference guidelines, the paper meets the criteria of quality, clarity, originality, and significance. However, the paper's novelty is somewhat limited, and the authors could benefit from more thorough justification of their method and more detailed analysis of the results.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel method for learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning.
* The method is applied to a real-world dataset and compared to state-of-the-art methods.
* The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the method in terms of explained variance, stability, and interpretability of the learned dictionaries.

Arguments con acceptance:
