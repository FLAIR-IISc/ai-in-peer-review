- Problem: Proposes an online structured sparse dictionary-learning method to extract spatially smooth, sparse “brain region” atoms from large-scale functional imaging data.

- Approach: Combines L1 sparsity with a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty in an online dictionary-learning framework, extending Mairal et al. (2010).

- Motivation: Addresses ROI definition, high dimensionality, and inter-subject variability in neuroimaging with a data-driven, scalable method.

- Novelty: Applying structured sparsity (L1 + Laplacian) within an online dictionary-learning setting for brain imaging is a novel and useful contribution.

- Scalability: Online framework enables processing of arbitrarily large neuroimaging datasets, a major practical advantage.

- Empirical results: Promising HCP experiments show more interpretable, spatially coherent atoms and improved behavioral prediction versus SODL and CanICA.

- Practical guidance: Paper provides useful discussion on hyperparameter tuning (α, γ, τ) and initialization, acknowledging trade-offs in explainability, stability, and fidelity.

- Theoretical gap: Lacks rigorous theoretical analysis—no error bounds, convergence rates, or conditions for recovery of meaningful structures.

- Manuscript quality: Writing is poorly polished, contains careless phrasing and leftover comments, reducing clarity and credibility.

- Limited baselines: Comparisons are restricted to SODL and CanICA; missing other state-of-the-art spatially regularized or graph-based methods.

- Overstated claims: Some assertions (e.g., becoming the de facto method for ROI extraction) are unsupported and overly optimistic.

- γ parameter guidance: Recommendations for γ selection are vague and lack practical specification of constants.

- Acceptance arguments: Addresses an important problem, introduces a novel scalable method, and shows promising empirical results.

- Rejection arguments: Major weaknesses include lack of theory, poor manuscript quality, limited comparisons, and overstated claims.

- Overall recommendation: Recommend rejection in current form; encourage stronger theoretical justification, thorough proofreading, and broader empirical comparisons for a future submission.
