

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of Mairal et al.'s online dictionary learning framework (NIPS 2010) to incorporate spatial structure in brain imaging data. The authors aim to learn structured, sparse brain regions (dictionary atoms) by adding a Laplacian penalty to the standard sparse online dictionary learning objective. The key innovation is using spatial regularization to produce interpretable brain regions that are compact and piece-wise smooth, rather than scattered patterns. The authors demonstrate their method on HCP fMRI data, showing improvements in interpretability and capture of inter-subject variability compared to SODL and CanICA.

**Strengths:**
The topic is highly relevant to neuroimaging, where identifying interpretable brain regions is crucial for understanding brain function. The experimental results are promising, showing that Smooth-SODL produces more spatially structured and interpretable brain regions than competing methods. The computational efficiency claim (only 2-3x slower than standard SODL) is valuable for large-scale neuroimaging applications. The paper also demonstrates that the method works well across small, medium, and large datasets, which is significant for real-world applications where data availability varies.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper suffers from serious quality issues that undermine its scientific contribution. The manuscript contains multiple proofreading errors, including leftover comments like "146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v." The writing is often unclear, with inconsistent notation and terminology that would confuse readers.

Most critically, the paper lacks theoretical analysis to support the proposed algorithm's effectiveness. While the authors claim the method "non-trivially extends" Mairal et al.'s work, they provide no convergence guarantees or theoretical analysis of how the Laplacian penalty affects the optimization landscape. The algorithmic description is technically sound but presented without any discussion of convergence rates, optimality conditions, or how the Laplacian parameter γ interacts with the sparsity constraint. The authors state that "empirically, we found that 1 or 2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient," but provide no justification for this claim.

**Quality:** The experimental evaluation is thorough but lacks theoretical grounding. The paper reports results on a single dataset (HCP fMRI) with limited parameter exploration, and the comparison with CanICA is problematic since CanICA requires smoothing preprocessing while Smooth-SODL does not. The paper does not adequately address whether the observed improvements are statistically significant.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is poorly written with numerous inconsistencies (e.g., switching between "V" and "v" for dictionary atoms) and incomplete explanations. The relationship between the Laplacian penalty and spatial structure is not clearly articulated. The supplementary materials are referenced but not described in the main text.

**Originality:** While the specific combination of online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization for brain imaging is novel, the core technique is a straightforward extension of existing methods. Similar spatial regularization approaches have been used in other domains (e.g., TV-`1 minimization in image processing), and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from prior work in structured dictionary learning.

**Significance:** The problem is important for neuroimaging, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how this method advances the state of the art beyond previous structured dictionary learning approaches. The claimed improvements in interpretability are qualitative and not rigorously validated. The paper would have been stronger with a more detailed analysis of how the learned regions correspond to known neuroanatomy.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging with a technically sound algorithm, but the lack of theoretical analysis and poor manuscript quality significantly diminish the contribution. The experimental results are promising but lack the depth needed to establish the method as a significant advancement. The authors should either provide theoretical guarantees or substantially revise the manuscript to address the numerous clarity issues and provide a more rigorous evaluation.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper has a good idea but fails to meet the standards of a NIPS paper due to the lack of theoretical analysis and poor writing quality. The authors should either conduct a more thorough theoretical analysis or substantially revise the manuscript to address the numerous clarity issues before resubmission. The current version does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claims about the method's effectiveness.
