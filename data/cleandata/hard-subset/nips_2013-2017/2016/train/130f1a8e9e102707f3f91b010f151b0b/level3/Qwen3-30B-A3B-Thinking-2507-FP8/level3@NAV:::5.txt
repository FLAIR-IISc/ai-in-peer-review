

This paper proposes a "Smooth-SODL" method for brain image analysis that extends online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) by adding a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial structure in dictionary atoms. The authors claim this approach better delineates brain structures from functional images compared to standard sparse dictionary learning. While the application to neuroimaging is interesting, the technical contribution appears limited.

The paper's novelty is weak as the core method is a straightforward extension of Mairal's online dictionary learning with added Laplacian regularization. The motivation for the Sobolev regularization is unclear - the paper never precisely defines what "structure" means in this context, nor does it justify why this specific regularization improves the problem over standard sparsity constraints. The authors claim their method "non-trivially extends" Mairal et al. (2010), but this extension appears to be merely adding a Laplacian penalty without significant theoretical or algorithmic innovation.

Several technical issues undermine the paper's quality. Equation (1) contains a likely notational error, where the dictionary should be written as [V^1, V^2, ..., V^k]^T rather than as V^1, V^2, ..., V^k. The choice of l2 norm for codes (Eq. 2) over the l1 norm used in Mairal 2010 lacks justification, which is particularly problematic given the paper's focus on sparsity. The reference to "line 146" is unclear and needs clarification. The paper also contains numerous typos and poor presentation that detract from its scientific merit.

While the experiments on brain imaging data show reasonable results (with Smooth-SODL producing more spatially structured dictionaries than standard SODL), the significance is limited. The method is only 2-3x slower than standard online dictionary learning, but this marginal computational cost does not justify the minimal theoretical contribution. The paper fails to clearly demonstrate how this method advances the state-of-the-art beyond existing structured dictionary learning approaches.

The paper suffers from poor clarity and lacks sufficient justification for key design choices. While the neuroimaging application is potentially useful, the technical contribution does not meet NIPS standards for novelty and theoretical rigor. I recommend rejection due to weak novelty, insufficient justification of key design choices, and poor presentation.
