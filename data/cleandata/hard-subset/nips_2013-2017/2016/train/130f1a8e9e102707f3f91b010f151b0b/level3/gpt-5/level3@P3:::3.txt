Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging in which dictionary atoms are encouraged to be both sparse (via an l1-type constraint on atoms) and spatially smooth/compact (via a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty). The optimization is handled by block-coordinate descent: ridge-coded updates for subject-specific coefficients and FISTA-based updates for atoms under Laplacian regularization and simple convex constraints (e.g., simplex or l1-ball). The approach aims to scale to large n and p and to yield spatially coherent “blob-like” atoms that may better reflect functional regions. The work builds on Mairal et al. (2010) for online sparse coding, relates to GraphNet (Grosenick et al.) in imposing smoothness, and contrasts with TV-l1 approaches used previously in neuroimaging (e.g., Abraham et al., Varoquaux et al.), which are often computationally heavier. Experiments on HCP task-fMRI activation maps compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA, reporting run times, explained variance, visual interpretability, stability across splits, and modest behavioral prediction.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Scalable, structured factorization for high-dimensional neuroimaging is important. Online methods are practical for modern datasets.
- Methodologically sensible: The Sobolev prior is a natural, convex way to favor smooth, compact atoms; integration into the online BCD scheme is straightforward.
- Computational practicality: Reported overhead versus SODL is a modest 2–3×, and the approach remains competitive with single-batch PCA/ICA.
- Some empirical signals: Increased visual smoothness and somewhat improved split-half stability; learning-curve evidence suggests small-n benefits.

Weaknesses
- Evidence of benefit is largely qualitative. The main claim (“better delineation of brain structures”) rests on visual inspection of blobs that are not objective to a non-expert. Many figures show selected “most interpretable” atoms, raising concerns about cherry-picking.
- Quantitative results are mixed. Explained variance is lower than CanICA; behavioral prediction is not clearly improved (CanICA often best); stability gains are modest. It is thus hard to judge actual algorithmic improvement beyond producing smoother maps.
- The core effect may reflect prior-imposition rather than better modeling of the data. With a Laplacian penalty, smoother blobs are expected; this does not, by itself, validate that the atoms capture meaningful inter-subject structure.
- With large datasets like HCP, the purported advantage diminishes. The learning-curve analysis suggests spatial regularization mainly helps in low-data regimes; for HCP-scale n, SODL and Smooth-SODL converge to similar explained variance.
- Missing validation against inter-subject consistency and known organization. The paper does not consolidate region differences across subjects or quantify cross-subject reproducibility of regions/atoms beyond mean correlations; no comparison to standard atlases or cross-dataset generalization is provided.
- Some methodological clarity issues: The claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e-6 accuracy is not credible; the gamma schedule in Eq. (9) is motivated but not clearly implemented; fairness of baselines is questionable (e.g., pre-smoothing only for CanICA; thresholding post hoc for ICA but not for others).

Quality
- Technically sound at a high level; the optimization scheme is standard and likely converges to a stationary point under the stated conditions.
- However, the empirical evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate superior performance beyond cosmetic smoothness. The reliance on subjective visualization and selective display weakens the argument. Hyperparameter selection (αt ~ t^-1/2, γ, τ) lacks rigorous justification; cross-validation criteria are not fully specified.

Clarity
- The paper is readable but contains typos and minor inconsistencies. The description of constraints (simplex vs l1-ball) and their implications (e.g., nonnegativity) should be clearer. Figures overemphasize visuals without quantitative spatial metrics. Reporting full sets of atoms (not only the “most interpretable”) would improve transparency.

Originality
- Moderate. The novelty lies in combining a Laplacian prior with online dictionary updates for large-scale neuroimaging. Related structured penalties (TV, GraphNet, structured sparsity) are well known; the main contribution is adapting them efficiently to an online setting.

Significance
- Unclear. If the method materially improved reproducibility, downstream prediction, or alignment with known organization, impact would be high. As presented, benefits are mostly smoother-looking atoms and small gains in stability at small n, which may not translate into scientific utility at HCP scale.

Requested improvements
- Quantify spatial structure and inter-subject consistency:
  - Split-half reproducibility of atom supports (Dice/Jaccard over thresholded maps), ICC of voxel weights, Moran’s I or other spatial autocorrelation measures, compactness metrics.
  - Consensus maps/regions across subjects; validate region consistency across resamples.
- Fair and stronger baselines:
  - SODL with matched pre-smoothing; TV-l1 or GraphNet-based decompositions; Ward/spectral parcellations; atlas-based ROIs; convolutional dictionary learning if applicable.
- External validation:
  - Overlap with known atlases (e.g., Yeo 7/17, Glasser MMP1.0) with permutation tests.
  - Cross-dataset/task generalization; test-retest reliability if available.
- Synthetic experiments with known ground truth to assess recovery of sparse-smooth atoms and sample complexity advantages.
- Clearer hyperparameter protocol: grids, selection criteria (e.g., split-half stability vs EV trade-off), and ablations (γ=0, varying τ). Justify the α schedule.
- Release code and full atom sets; avoid selective visualization.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, scalable extension of online dictionary learning with a well-motivated spatial prior.
- Modest computational cost and simple implementation.
- Some evidence of improved stability and faster early learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Validation is largely subjective; objective gains are not clearly demonstrated.
- With large n, benefits shrink; significance for HCP-scale analyses is unclear.
- Baselines and evaluation choices may bias in favor of the method; lack of robust inter-subject validation.

Recommendation
- Leaning to reject (borderline). The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the empirical evidence does not yet substantiate a clear scientific or practical advantage beyond smoother visuals. Strengthening the quantitative validation and fairness of comparisons could change this assessment.

Confidence
- High on methodological assessment; medium on domain-specific neuroimaging validation.
