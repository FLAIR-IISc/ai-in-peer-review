

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes an extension of online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) to incorporate spatial structure in brain imaging applications. The authors introduce a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce piece-wise smoothness and compactness ("blobs") in dictionary atoms, claiming this improves interpretability and captures inter-subject variability better than standard methods. The approach extends Mairal's work with a Laplacian penalty while maintaining online scalability.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging: finding meaningful brain regions without relying on predefined ROIs. The online nature of the algorithm makes it suitable for large-scale datasets (e.g., HCP data with 500 subjects), and the authors provide some qualitative evidence that their method produces more interpretable brain maps than standard dictionary learning or ICA-based approaches. The implementation is planned to be released as part of the Nilearn library, which is a positive contribution to the community.

**Weaknesses:**

*Originality (Major concern):* The core contribution is weakly novel. The method is essentially Mairal's online dictionary learning (SODL) with added Sobolev regularization. The authors claim "non-trivial" extension, but this is a straightforward modification of existing work. The paper fails to clearly distinguish this from prior work on structured dictionary learning (e.g., [1, 25] which use TV-ℓ1 regularization for brain imaging). The paper does not adequately justify why this particular regularization approach is necessary or superior to existing methods, and the motivation for "structure" remains vague.

*Quality:* The justification for using ℓ2 norm on codes (Eq. 2) instead of ℓ1 (as in Mairal 2010) is insufficiently explained. The authors claim "Ridge leads to stable codes," but provide no theoretical or empirical evidence to support this claim in their specific context. The paper contains a likely notation error in Eq. (1), where the matrix V should probably be written as [V^1, V^2, ..., V^r]^T. The reference to "line 146" is unclear and needs clarification. The experimental results, while showing some qualitative improvements, do not provide strong evidence of significant quantitative advantages over existing methods.

*Clarity:* The paper suffers from poor language and numerous typos throughout. The explanation of the Sobolev regularization is unclear, and the paper does not define what "structure" means in this context. The motivation for the Laplacian penalty is not adequately justified, and the relationship between spatial structure and the problem's objectives remains ambiguous.

*Significance:* The claimed improvements (better interpretability, better capture of inter-subject variability) are not convincingly demonstrated. The paper shows that the method produces more spatially compact atoms, but does not establish that this leads to meaningful improvements in downstream neuroimaging analysis. The computational cost increase (factor of 2-3) is not convincingly justified as worth the marginal improvement, especially since the paper acknowledges that spatial regularization may be unnecessary when data is abundant (Section 6).

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper does not meet NIPS standards for novelty. While it presents a technically sound extension of existing work with some empirical validation, the contribution is incremental and fails to clearly articulate how it differs from or improves upon prior work. The paper's weak motivation, poor justification for key design choices, and unclear presentation undermine its scientific contribution. The authors should engage more thoroughly with the structured dictionary learning literature (e.g., [3, 8, 11, 13, 23, 25]) to better position their work and justify their approach.

**Recommendation:** Reject

**Pro/Con Summary:**
- Pro: Addresses an important problem in neuroimaging; online algorithm is scalable; some qualitative evidence of improved interpretability.
- Con: Weak novelty (straightforward extension of existing work); insufficient justification for key design choices; unclear motivation for Sobolev regularization; poor writing and presentation; marginal improvement over existing methods.
