This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images into sparse and structured components, aiming to delineate brain structures from functional images. The core idea is to combine the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on the dictionary atoms, creating “blobs” representing brain regions. The motivation stems from the premise that inter-subject variability in neuroimaging data contains meaningful structure that is often discarded in standard statistical analyses. While the authors acknowledge the existing work on clustering and PCA/ICA for extracting functional units (e.g., references [5], [21], [14]), they argue that their approach, combining sparsity and spatial smoothness, offers a more principled way to define regions of interest (ROIs) without explicit segmentation. 

The proposed method, Smooth-SODL, extends Mairal et al. (2010) by adding a Laplacian penalty to the dictionary learning objective. The authors claim this addition only increases running time by a factor of 2-3 while enabling better capture of inter-subject variability. They present experiments on task fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project (HCP) demonstrating improved interpretability and performance compared to standard SODL and Canonical ICA (CanICA).

**Strengths:**

*   **Scalability:** The online nature of the algorithm, inherited from Mairal et al. (2010), allows it to handle large datasets, a crucial aspect in neuroimaging.
*   **Potential for Interpretability:** The spatial regularization via the Laplacian penalty is a reasonable approach to encourage the learning of more interpretable dictionary atoms that correspond to meaningful brain regions.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments on HCP data show promising results, with the proposed method demonstrating competitive performance in terms of explained variance and prediction of behavioral variables. The observed improvement over standard SODL, particularly in the early stages of learning, suggests that the spatial regularization is beneficial.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The authors clearly articulate the problem they are trying to solve – leveraging inter-subject variability to define brain regions without relying on traditional segmentation methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The primary contribution – adding a Laplacian penalty to online dictionary learning – feels incremental. The authors need to more strongly justify the novelty and significance of this extension, particularly considering the established work of Mairal et al. (2010). The method doesn’t appear to represent a paradigm shift.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Sobolev Regularization:** The paper lacks a strong theoretical motivation for using the Sobolev penalty. What precisely is meant by "structure" in the context of dictionary atoms? Why is spatial smoothness crucial for delineating brain structures? A more in-depth discussion of the relationship between the Laplacian penalty and the desired properties of the learned regions is necessary.
*   **Questionable Design Choices:** The choice of using an l2 penalty on the codes (Ui) instead of the l1 penalty used in Mairal et al. (2010) requires further justification. The authors allude to stability but do not provide a detailed explanation.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The writing is often unclear and suffers from numerous typos and grammatical errors. The notation could be improved (e.g., potential error in Eq.(1)). The reference to "line 146" is ambiguous.
*   **Insufficient Comparison to Baselines:** While the comparison to CanICA and SODL is valuable, a more thorough comparison to other state-of-the-art methods for ROI definition or spatial clustering in neuroimaging would strengthen the evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound extension to the online dictionary learning framework. However, the limited novelty, lack of strong theoretical justification for the key design choice (Sobolev penalty), and clarity issues raise concerns. While the empirical results are encouraging, they are not sufficiently compelling to warrant a strong recommendation for acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Scalable method for decomposing large-scale brain imaging data.
*   Promising empirical results demonstrating improved interpretability and performance compared to standard SODL.
*   Clear articulation of the problem and potential benefits.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty.
*   Lack of strong theoretical justification for the core contribution (Sobolev penalty).
*   Clarity and presentation issues.
*   Potentially insufficient comparison to relevant baselines.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject.** The paper requires significant revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. The authors need to provide a stronger justification for the novelty and theoretical underpinnings of their approach, improve the clarity and presentation of the manuscript, and conduct a more comprehensive evaluation. A major revision addressing these points could potentially lead to acceptance.
