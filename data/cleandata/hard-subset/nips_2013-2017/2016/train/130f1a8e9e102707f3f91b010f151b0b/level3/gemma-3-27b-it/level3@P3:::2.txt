This paper proposes an online structured dictionary learning algorithm (Smooth-SODL) incorporating a Sobolev prior (Laplacian penalty) to encourage spatially contiguous dictionary atoms for neuroimaging data. The motivation stems from the desire to automatically delineate brain regions from functional images, moving beyond manual ROI definition. The approach builds upon existing online dictionary learning work (Mairal et al., 2010) and aims to provide a scalable method for extracting interpretable and spatially coherent features from large-scale neuroimaging datasets.

**Quality:** The model definition and algorithms generally follow a well-established framework, building logically on prior work in online dictionary learning and incorporating a reasonable spatial prior. However, the initial technical rating was marginally below average, and justified. The mathematical formulation, while largely sound, suffers from inconsistencies and ambiguities, detailed below. The claim of convergence, referencing Bertsekas (1999), is reasonable given the BCD framework, but could be strengthened with a more specific discussion of assumptions and potential limitations in the context of the Laplacian penalty. The empirical evaluation, unfortunately, is the weakest point, failing to convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage of the proposed method.

**Clarity:** The manuscript suffers from significant clarity issues. Descriptions are often confusing, and numerous typos and editing mistakes impede understanding. The notation is inconsistent, and the explanation of the algorithm, particularly the dictionary update step (Algorithm 2), is difficult to follow. The connection between the mathematical formulation and the intuitive goal of identifying spatially coherent brain regions is not always clear.

**Originality:** The core novelty lies in extending online dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior to promote spatial smoothness in the learned dictionary atoms. While the combination of online learning and spatial regularization is not entirely novel, its application to large-scale neuroimaging data, within this specific framework, represents a worthwhile contribution. The approach differentiates itself from earlier methods that relied on slower, alternating optimization schemes for structured decompositions. However, the paper doesn’t adequately position itself within the broader landscape of spatially regularized dictionary learning methods in image processing.

**Significance:** The proposed method has the potential to be a useful tool for neuroscience and medical imaging. The ability to automatically learn spatially coherent features could aid in tasks such as brain region segmentation, functional network identification, and biomarker discovery. The scalability afforded by the online learning approach is particularly valuable for analyzing large datasets like those from the Human Connectome Project. The method may also be applicable to other domains involving spatially structured data, like general image segmentation or regional sensing.

**Strengths:**

*   Scalability due to online learning.
*   Incorporation of a spatial prior to encourage interpretable dictionary atoms.
*   Logical extension of existing online dictionary learning methods.
*   Potential for broad applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Weak empirical support. Figure 2 doesn’t clearly demonstrate the advantage of SSMF.
*   Lack of quantitative evaluation of the “structuredness” of the learned atoms.
*   Numerous clarity issues, typos, and inconsistencies in notation.
*   Missing reference to the HCP dataset.
*   Ambiguity in the algorithmic detail of solving Equation (6).
*   Issues with Figure 2 and Figure 3 presentation and interpretation.



**Detailed Comments & Requests:**

*   **Equation (6):** The reviewer rightly asks for an explicit mathematical/algorithmic solution to Equation (6), or at least a derivation in the appendix. The current description is insufficient.
*   **Figure 2:** The comparison between methods is hindered by showing different slices for each approach, making direct visual assessment difficult. The claim that SSMF is more "structured" is not supported quantitatively or visually. Explicit metrics of structure (e.g., spatial coherence, smoothness) should be provided.
*   **Figure 3:** The variable being predicted is unclear. It’s unclear whether the regression uses *U<sub>i</sub>*. The figure legends and caption do not adequately explain the baselines (PCA is missing). Claims about R<sup>2</sup> > 0.1 are ambiguous – which curves specifically support this claim?
*   **Mathematical Issues:** Inconsistencies exist in matrix decomposition transpose placement, signs, and terms in Equations (6) and (8). Variable references in updates are unclear. These need to be carefully reviewed.
*   **Missing Reference:** The reference to the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset ([20]) is missing.
*   **Typographical Errors:** Numerous typos and stray comments (e.g., "Graph-Net penalty can imposes", duplicated words, bold sentence) require correction.
*   **Rebuttal Integration:** The additional results provided in the rebuttal significantly improved the technical quality. The reviewer rightly requests these be included in the revision.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Potentially useful method for neuroimaging analysis.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in the field.
*   Scalable approach.

**Con:**

*   Weak empirical validation.
*   Clarity and presentation issues significantly hinder understanding.
*   Lack of quantitative assessment of structural properties.
*   Technical inconsistencies in the manuscript.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the idea is promising and the technical foundation is reasonable, the paper falls short due to the lack of compelling empirical evidence, poor clarity, and technical issues. Addressing the points raised above, particularly strengthening the empirical evaluation and improving the manuscript's clarity, would significantly increase the paper's chances of acceptance in a future revision. The rebuttal content *must* be incorporated into the revised manuscript.
