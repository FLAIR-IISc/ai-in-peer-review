

This paper proposes an extension of online dictionary learning (SODL) by incorporating Sobolev (Laplacian) regularization to enforce spatial smoothness in brain imaging applications, alongside standard L1 sparsity. The core idea—combining smoothness and sparsity penalties to extract structured, interpretable functional brain regions from fMRI data—is technically novel and directly addresses a gap in existing methods that produce noisy, scattered patterns. The authors frame this as a non-trivial extension of Mairal et al.’s work, with only a modest (2–3×) computational overhead.

**Strengths:** The methodological innovation is clear and well-motivated for neuroimaging, where spatially coherent regions are biologically plausible. The algorithmic adaptation (using FISTA for Laplacian regularization within BCD) is sound and leverages established optimization principles. The implementation within Nilearn and empirical validation on HCP data add practical relevance.

**Weaknesses:** The primary weakness is the **lack of convincing evidence for the claimed advantages**. The experimental results show only marginal gains (e.g., 5% higher explained variance on test data in Fig. 2b, with learning curves in Table 1 revealing a rapidly closing gap as data increases). Crucially, the paper fails to explain *why* spatial regularization helps in small-data regimes (as claimed), despite providing a learning curve analysis. The "structured" dictionaries (Fig. 2a) appear visually cleaner, but this is subjective and not quantified against baselines. The manuscript also suffers from **poor clarity**: draft comments (e.g., line 146), inconsistent terminology ("intepretable" vs. "interpretable"), and ambiguous claims (e.g., "better capture inter-subject variability" without statistical validation) make the argument difficult to follow. The significance is overstated—while the method is scalable, the incremental gains over SODL do not justify the added complexity for most neuroimaging applications.

**Verdict:** The technical contribution is novel and well-executed, but the weak experimental justification and unclear presentation undermine the paper’s scientific impact. The results do not demonstrate a *substantial* advance over existing methods, and the manuscript requires significant revision to clarify the value proposition and support claims with rigorous analysis. **Reject.**
