

Summary: This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning (SODL) for brain imaging, incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatially structured, sparse dictionary atoms. The method aims to extract interpretable brain regions from fMRI data by leveraging inter-subject variability, contrasting with traditional ROI definition approaches. It builds on SODL with a Laplacian penalty, claiming only 2-3x slower runtime while improving interpretability.

Strengths: The experimental validation on HCP fMRI data is thorough, demonstrating clear advantages in dictionary interpretability and small-data performance (Fig. 2, Table 1). The learning curve analysis (Table 1) effectively shows Smooth-SODL's superiority in regimes with limited data (241 subjects), where it achieves 31% explained variance versus SODL's 2%. The implementation in Nilearn is a practical contribution. The comparison to CanICA and SODL across multiple metrics (stability, reproducibility, behavioral prediction) is well-structured.

Weaknesses: The core novelty is limited. Extending SODL with a Laplacian penalty is a straightforward modification, not a significant methodological advance. The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish this from prior structured dictionary learning work (e.g., [25, 1] using TV regularization), which also addresses spatial structure but at higher computational cost. The justification for the Laplacian penalty is weak: "structured" is never rigorously defined (beyond vague "blobs" and "piece-wise smooth"), and the paper does not establish *why* spatial regularization is necessary for brain regions beyond the HCP's large dataset (where spatial structure is less critical). The use of an l2-norm penalty on codes (α‖Ui‖₂²) instead of the l1-norm used in SODL (α‖Ui‖₁) is unexplained. While the authors note l2 leads to "stable codes," they provide no evidence that this is preferable for brain imaging or how it impacts the structured sparsity goal. The notation error in Eq. (1) (V should be transposed for standard matrix multiplication) is a critical technical flaw. The reference to "line 146" is ambiguous and unhelpful. The paper's language contains multiple typos ("intepretable," "denoised," "fidelity") and lacks clarity in key sections (e.g., the derivation of Eq. 7, the role of the simplex constraint).

Quality & Significance: The technical approach is sound but incremental. The experimental results support the claim that structured dictionaries improve interpretability and small-data performance, but the significance is modest. The method does not advance the state-of-the-art in dictionary learning or brain imaging beyond a minor extension of existing work. The claimed "scalability" is not new (SODL already handles large data), and the runtime overhead (3x SODL) is non-trivial for large-scale applications. The behavioral prediction results (Fig. 2c) are marginal, with CanICA still outperforming Smooth-SODL.

Originality: The paper's core idea is a direct adaptation of SODL with a spatial penalty. While the Laplacian penalty is novel in this specific online context, it does not constitute a significant methodological contribution for NIPS. The related work section (Section 4) fails to adequately contextualize this as a minor extension rather than a substantial advance over prior structured dictionary learning (e.g., [25, 1, 23]), which is a key requirement for NIPS acceptance.

Clarity: The presentation suffers from poor writing, typos, and ambiguous notation (Eq. 1, "line 146"). The motivation for the Laplacian penalty and the choice of l2 over l1 on codes are inadequately justified, leaving key design decisions unexplained. The connection between the mathematical formulation and the biological interpretation of "structured" brain regions is tenuous.

Overall, while the paper presents a useful tool for brain imaging with practical benefits in interpretability and small-data settings, the novelty is insufficient for NIPS. The method is a straightforward extension of established online dictionary learning, not a significant theoretical or methodological advance. The technical flaws (notation, unexplained choices) and lack of rigorous justification for key components undermine the contribution. The results are convincing *within the context of their specific extension*, but they do not represent a meaningful step forward for the broader machine learning community. The paper would be more appropriate for a neuroscience or medical imaging venue.
