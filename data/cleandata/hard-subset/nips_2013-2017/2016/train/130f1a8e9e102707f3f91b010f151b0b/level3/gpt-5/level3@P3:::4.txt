Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online, under-complete dictionary learning framework for population neuroimaging that imposes both sparsity and spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms. Sparsity is enforced via a constraint on an L1-type set (simplex or L1-ball), and smoothness via a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty (vTΔv). The codes are obtained by ridge regression. Optimization proceeds with block-coordinate descent: codes via closed-form ridge updates, atoms via proximal updates that reduce to a denoising-like quadratic with Laplacian regularization, solved with a few FISTA steps plus projection onto the constraint set.

This extends Mairal et al. (2010) sparse online dictionary learning by adding spatial structure to atoms, akin in spirit to structured decompositions used in neuroimaging such as TV–L1 dictionary learning (Abraham et al., MICCAI 2013), GraphNet (Grosenick et al., 2013), and spatially-constrained ICA/SPCA. Relative to TV, the Laplacian penalty is a Sobolev smoothness prior that is computationally convenient and compatible with online updates. The work targets interpretable, compact, blob-like cortical/subcortical patterns and scalability to large n and p (HCP fMRI GLM maps).

Experiments compare Smooth-SODL to SODL (γ=0) and CanICA on HCP task fMRI Z-maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40), reporting runtime, explained variance, “stability” across splits, and predicting behavioral variables.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: An under-complete online dictionary-learning framework that combines L1 sparsity with a Sobolev/Laplacian smoothness prior on atoms. This is a clean, well-motivated hybrid of sparsity and smoothness within an online DL setting.
- Scalability and practicality: Implementation appears 2–3× slower than SODL but still fast, with simple updates (FFT possibility; first-order proximal update on each atom).
- Potential for interpretability: Qualitative maps appear smoother and more compact than vanilla SODL, with improved atom reproducibility across splits.
- Relevance: Learning structured, population-level functional patterns is a timely problem in neuroimaging.

Weaknesses
- Empirical gains are modest/unclear: Explained variance and behavioral prediction improvements over simpler baselines are small or inconsistent (CanICA often wins on EV and prediction; Smooth-SODL vs SODL differences are limited at scale).
- Insufficient analysis of why gains are small: The paper notes trends (e.g., larger gains early in the learning curve) but does not dig into when/why smoothness helps or hurts, or how γ and τ trade off interpretability vs fidelity.
- Missing ablations and stronger baselines: No systematic ablation of Laplacian vs TV, Laplacian-only vs L1-only, simplex vs L1-ball, ridge vs lasso codes, k sensitivity. Competing structured methods (TV–L1 DL, GraphNet-type penalties, sparse PCA with spatial regularization) are not compared quantitatively.
- Algorithmic clarity and validation: Claims like “1–2 FISTA iterations suffice to reach 1e−6” are not credible without empirical evidence; convergence under time-varying γt and inexact inner solves is not carefully justified. The practical rule for γt to avoid tiny steps merits experiments.
- Clarity/quality of presentation: The manuscript reads rushed, with typos and draft comments (e.g., line 146), and occasional confusion (e.g., limit n→∞ in the objective vs finite-sample practice). This makes it tedious to read and hard to reproduce.

Detailed evaluation
- Quality: The formulation is sensible, optimization is standard, and implementation appears efficient. However, the empirical section does not convincingly support strong claims. Important design choices (γ, τ, constraint set) are under-analyzed. The convergence discussion relies on [Mairal 2010] but the added Laplacian term plus inexact updates and adaptive γ deserve a more careful treatment.
- Clarity: The core idea is understandable, but the paper needs editing for language, structure, and precision. Please remove draft notes, fix typos, and streamline Sections 2–3. Clarify the finite-sample objective replacing the n→∞ limit, and specify exact stopping criteria and tolerances for Alg. 2.
- Originality: Combining Sobolev smoothness with L1 constraints in online DL for fMRI is a novel and interesting combination, distinct from TV–L1 DL and from unstructured SODL. The idea is incremental but meaningful.
- Significance: Potentially useful for neuroimaging practice due to speed and interpretability. However, current evidence shows limited improvements over simpler methods and no SOTA in prediction or EV; significance would be higher with stronger, clearer empirical benefits and ablations.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Provide ablations: γ=0 vs γ>0; Laplacian vs TV penalty; L1-ball vs simplex; ridge vs lasso codes; k sensitivity; mini-batch size. Report quantitative interpretability metrics (e.g., compactness, spatial continuity, overlap with canonical atlases).
- Analyze when smoothness helps: Extend learning curves, stratify by SNR or effect size, show how stability and EV evolve with γ and τ. Explain why Smooth-SODL underperforms CanICA on prediction yet improves stability.
- Validate optimization details: Report actual inner-iteration counts, run time split per component, effect of γ on Lipschitz constant and stepsizes, and the impact of inexact solves on the outer objective.
- Baselines: Include TV–L1 DL (e.g., MICCAI 2013), GraphNet-like structured SPCA, and sparse PCA/ICA with spatial priors where feasible.
- Reproducibility: Release code (beyond “soon in Nilearn”), list hyperparameters, initialization, and preprocessing. Define stability metric precisely, provide distributions not just means.
- Writing: Remove draft comments, correct typos, and improve notation consistency. Move detailed derivations to supplement; keep the main text focused.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting and practically relevant formulation (Sobolev + L1 within online DL).
- Scalable implementation; potentially useful for large neuroimaging cohorts.
- Improves qualitative interpretability and split-half stability over SODL.

Cons for acceptance
- Modest or unclear quantitative improvements; CanICA often stronger on EV/prediction.
- Missing ablations and limited analysis of why/when the method helps.
- Presentation quality issues and some algorithmic claims insufficiently substantiated.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and relevant, but the empirical evidence and analysis are not yet strong enough for NIPS. With cleaner writing, thorough ablations, stronger baselines, and deeper analysis of performance trade-offs, this could become a solid contribution.

Confidence
Medium.
