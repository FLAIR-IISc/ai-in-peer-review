This paper proposes Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), a method for decomposing brain images into a set of sparse and spatially structured components, or "atoms". The core contribution is the extension of the well-known online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) to include a spatial smoothness prior. This is achieved by adding a Laplacian penalty (a Sobolev prior) to the objective function, which encourages the resulting dictionary atoms to be spatially contiguous and form smooth "blobs" rather than scattered, "salt-and-pepper" patterns. The motivation is to generate components that are more neuroscientifically interpretable as functional brain regions. The method's online nature allows it to scale to massive datasets, and it is evaluated on task fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project (HCP).

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging: learning data-driven representations of brain function that are both sparse and spatially coherent. The primary novelty lies in integrating a spatial smoothness prior directly into a scalable online dictionary learning algorithm. While structured penalties have been used in brain imaging before (e.g., [1, 25]), applying them within a fast online framework that can handle massive datasets is a valuable and timely contribution. If the method produces more interpretable components, it could be a highly useful tool for neuroscientists defining functional regions of interest (ROIs) and studying inter-subject variability. The potential application extends beyond neuroimaging to other domains requiring regional sensing or structured image segmentation.

2.  **Technical Approach:** The proposed model is a well-motivated extension of a solid and widely-used framework [16]. The optimization strategy, using Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD), is appropriate for the problem structure. The authors correctly identify that the dictionary update sub-problem is a constrained quadratic minimization that can be solved efficiently with first-order methods like FISTA, preserving the overall efficiency of the online approach. The provided convergence analysis, which relies on the guarantees from the original online dictionary learning paper, appears sound.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Empirical Support and Evaluation:** The primary weakness of the paper in its current form is the lack of convincing empirical evidence to support its central claim—that the Sobolev prior leads to qualitatively and quantitatively better dictionary atoms.
    *   The paper asserts that the atoms from Smooth-SODL are more "structured" and "interpretable," but this is not demonstrated with quantitative metrics. The authors should include neuroscience-justified criteria to explicitly measure spatial smoothness, contiguity, or compactness to prove that the Laplacian penalty is having the desired effect.
    *   Figure 2(a) provides a qualitative comparison, but its effectiveness is limited. First, it appears that different anatomical slices are shown for different methods, which prevents a direct visual comparison. Second, the visual difference in "structuredness" between SODL and Smooth-SODL is subtle at best. Without quantitative backup, the visual evidence is not compelling.
    *   The prediction experiment in Figure 2(c) is confusing. It is unclear what features are used for the regression (are they the subject-specific loading vectors U_i?). The caption and legend are incomplete; the "RAW" baseline is not fully explained, and the PCA method is shown in the plot but missing from the legend. The claim that certain models achieve an R^2 score > 0.1 is ambiguous and does not clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript requires significant editing to improve clarity and readability. There are numerous typos, grammatical errors, and awkward phrases that make the paper difficult to follow.
    *   **Mathematical Notation:** Several equations contain potential inconsistencies or typos.
        *   In Equation (1), the decomposition is written as `Xi ≈ VUi`. Given the dimensions `V ∈ R^(p×k)` and `Xi ∈ R^(n×p)` (as a stack), this seems incorrect. If `Xi` is a single `p`-dimensional row vector, the formulation should be `Xi^T ≈ VUi`, where `Ui` is `k×1`. Please clarify the matrix dimensions and transpositions throughout.
        *   The dictionary update in Equation (8) and the related text contains several terms (`At[j, j]`, `Bjt`, `VAj`) whose derivation from Equation (7) is not shown and difficult to follow. A more detailed derivation, perhaps in an appendix, would greatly improve clarity and reproducibility.
        *   Equation (6) gives the solution to the Ridge-coding sub-problem. This is a standard result, but given its centrality, a brief derivation or clearer connection to the SVD approach mentioned would be helpful.
    *   **Missing References:** The reference for the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset [20] is missing from the bibliography.
    *   **Minor Typos:** The paper is littered with small errors (e.g., "GraphNet penalty can imposes", duplicated words like "the the population", a stray bolded sentence in Section 4). These should be carefully corrected.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments**
The additional results provided during the rebuttal period were informative and have raised my assessment of the paper's technical quality. The new quantitative comparisons begin to address the core weakness regarding empirical validation. I strongly recommend that these results be fully integrated into the revised manuscript, as they are crucial for supporting the paper's claims.

**Summary and Recommendation**
This paper introduces a promising and well-motivated method for learning spatially structured dictionaries from large-scale neuroimaging data. The integration of a Sobolev prior into an online learning framework is a novel and significant contribution. However, the manuscript in its current form is hampered by poor presentation, unclear notation, and, most importantly, a lack of strong, quantitative evidence demonstrating the benefit of the proposed spatial regularization.

Based on the strengths of the core idea and the improvements shown in the rebuttal, I lean towards acceptance, but this is conditional on a major revision. The authors must thoroughly revise the paper to improve clarity, correct all mathematical notation and typos, and—critically—incorporate the quantitative results from the rebuttal to provide clear evidence that Smooth-SODL produces more structured and meaningful components than the alternatives.
