This paper proposes a method for learning spatially structured and sparse components from large-scale brain imaging data. The goal is to extract meaningful brain regions that capture inter-subject variability in a data-driven manner. The method extends the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages the learned components to be spatially smooth and blob-like, which is desirable for neuroscientific interpretability. The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to massive datasets, such as the Human Connectome Project (HCP) data used in the experiments. The authors compare their method, named Smooth-SODL, against standard Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (SODL) and Canonical Independent Component Analysis (CanICA), demonstrating improvements in interpretability, stability, and performance in low-data regimes.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and challenging problem in neuroimaging: the scalable, data-driven discovery of functional brain regions from large population cohorts. The ability to extract meaningful and reproducible regions of interest (ROIs) is crucial for statistical power and interpretability in brain mapping studies.
2.  **Scalability:** A major strength is the algorithm's scalability. By building upon an online learning framework, the proposed method can handle datasets that are too large for traditional batch methods to process in memory. The reported runtimes show that the addition of the spatial regularization term incurs only a modest computational cost (a factor of ~3x) compared to the unstructured baseline.
3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The use of a large, public dataset (HCP) is commendable. The paper evaluates the method on multiple fronts: qualitative assessment of the learned dictionaries, quantitative metrics like explained variance and stability, and a downstream task of predicting behavioral variables.
4.  **Improved Interpretability:** The qualitative results in Figure 2a convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the Laplacian penalty. The dictionary atoms from Smooth-SODL are visibly more structured, compact, and less noisy than those from SODL and much more interpretable than the dense maps from CanICA. This directly supports the primary motivation of the paper.
5.  **Demonstrated Need for Regularization:** The learning curve analysis in Table 1 provides a compelling argument for the utility of spatial regularization, particularly in small-to-medium data regimes. The 14-fold improvement in explained variance when trained on few subjects highlights the value of the spatial prior when data is scarce.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Originality:** The main weakness of the work is its limited methodological novelty. The proposed approach is a combination of two well-established techniques: the online dictionary learning algorithm from Mairal et al. (2010) and a standard Sobolev/Laplacian smoothness penalty. While the application and engineering are sound and the result is useful for the neuroimaging community, the core technical contribution may be viewed as incremental from a machine learning perspective, which could be a concern for a top-tier venue like NIPS.

2.  **Clarity and Justification:** The manuscript could be significantly improved in terms of clarity and the justification of certain modeling choices.
    *   The motivation for using a Sobolev penalty over other spatial regularizers (e.g., Total Variation, as used in related work like Abraham et al., 2014) is not discussed. A brief justification of why piece-wise smoothness is preferred would strengthen the paper.
    *   The model in Equation (2) uses a Ridge (L2) penalty on the codes `Ui`, whereas the original work by Mairal et al. often employs an L1 penalty to encourage sparse codes. The paper states this choice leads to "stable codes" but does not elaborate on this. A deeper discussion is needed on the trade-off: an L1 penalty would imply that each subject’s map is a combination of a few dictionary atoms, while the L2 penalty encourages a dense combination. Justification for why dense codes are more suitable for this specific application is missing.
    *   The presentation of the algorithm and equations could be clearer. For instance, the derivation for the dictionary update in Equation (7) is deferred to [16], but given the added Laplacian term, a more self-contained derivation would be helpful for the reader.

3.  **Presentation Quality:** The paper suffers from numerous typos, grammatical errors, and awkward phrasing that hinder readability and detract from the professional quality of the manuscript. A thorough proofreading and copy-editing pass is strongly recommended.

**Questions and Minor Comments**

*   In Equation (1), the text states that `Xi` are p-dimensional row vectors. If `V` is a `p x k` matrix, the product `VUi` is not well-defined if `Ui` is `k x 1`. Please clarify the notation and matrix dimensions. Perhaps the decomposition should be `X ≈ UV^T`?
*   In Algorithm 2, the text refers to a stopping criterion (line 2) but does not specify what it is. Is it a fixed number of iterations, or based on a convergence tolerance? The text later mentions that a single BCD iteration is sufficient in practice, which seems to answer this, but the algorithm description should be made precise.
*   The discussion on hyper-parameter tuning is honest about the difficulty of the problem. However, the proposed time-varying schedule for `alpha` (Eq. 10) seems ad-hoc. Is there any theoretical or stronger empirical justification for this choice?

In summary, the paper presents a scalable and effective method for an important problem in computational neuroscience. The main strengths are its practical significance, scalability, and strong empirical results that clearly demonstrate the benefit of the proposed structured regularization. The primary weakness is the limited novelty of the underlying methodology. Furthermore, the paper requires substantial revision to improve its clarity, justify modeling choices, and correct numerous presentational errors. Despite the concerns about originality, the practical contribution to the neuroimaging field is significant.
