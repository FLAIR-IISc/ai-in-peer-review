This paper proposes an online, structured sparse dictionary-learning method (Smooth-SODL) for neuroimaging, augmenting the Mairal et al. (2010) online framework with two priors on the dictionary atoms: (i) sparsity via an ℓ1 constraint (or simplex) on atoms, and (ii) spatial structure via a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty, yielding piece-wise smooth, compact “blob-like” atoms. The algorithm alternates ridge-coded updates of sample coefficients with block-coordinate updates of atoms, where each atom update solves a GraphNet-like denoising subproblem with FISTA inside the online BCD loop. The method is designed to scale to large n and p, with a reported 2–3× runtime overhead vs unstructured online DL. Experiments on HCP task fMRI contrasts compare Smooth-SODL to standard online DL and CanICA, reporting qualitative maps, explained variance, split-half stability, runtime, and behavioral prediction.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds directly on sparse online DL (Mairal et al., 2010) and subsequent large-scale matrix factorization (e.g., Mensch et al., 2016). It contrasts with structured decompositions using TV-ℓ1 or GraphNet penalties typically solved by slow alternating minimization (e.g., Abraham et al., MICCAI 2013; Grosenick et al., NeuroImage; Jenatton et al., AISTATS), and with ICA/PCA-based group decompositions (e.g., CanICA; MELODIC). The idea of adding a quadratic smoothness term to enable fast first-order inner loops is sensible and computationally attractive in the online setting.

Quality: The modeling choices are sound; the optimization scheme—online BCD with ridge-coded U and proximal-FISTA updates for V under a Laplacian quadratic—is standard and appropriate, and the convergence claims are plausible given separate convexity and compact constraints. The paper discusses step-size/Lipschitz constants and sensitivity of γ, which is important. However, the empirical evidence that the Laplacian prior improves scientific utility is still insufficiently quantified. Figure 2 shows examples and EV/stability, but “more structured and interpretable” remains largely asserted rather than measured. Several notational inconsistencies and unclear variable references (notably around Eqs. (6) and (8)) hinder verification.

Clarity: The manuscript is readable in parts but hampered by typos, inconsistent notation, and missing or ambiguous definitions in the algorithmic sections. The figure design choices (different slices across methods, unclear legends and baselines) make comparisons harder. The rebuttal addressed some issues and provided additional results; these should be integrated into the revision, with clearer metrics and standardized visualizations.

Originality: The contribution—embedding a spatial Sobolev/Laplacian prior within an online DL framework and showing it scales with modest overhead—is a nontrivial and useful extension. Related structured decompositions exist, but applying a simple quadratic smoothness that enables efficient online updates appears novel and practically relevant to large-scale neuroimaging.

Significance: If convincingly demonstrated, the method could provide an attractive tool for data-driven ROI discovery and dimensionality reduction that balances denoising, spatial localization, and scalability. Its generality suggests applications beyond fMRI (e.g., PET, segmentation, regional sensing). The current empirical section points in this direction but stops short of establishing clear advantages over strong baselines with robust quantitative criteria.

Strengths
- Solid formulation and scalable algorithm with clear computational benefits; modest overhead vs unstructured online DL.
- Sensible use of Laplacian penalty to promote spatially compact atoms without expensive TV.
- Experiments on large public data; split-half stability, EV, and prediction metrics reported; runtime comparisons provided.
- Rebuttal improved technical completeness and raised confidence.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient quantitative evidence for “structured and interpretable” claims; visuals not standardized; no atlas-overlap or spatial-compactness metrics.
- Some algorithmic/notation issues (e.g., Eq. (6) and Eq. (8) details, variable naming) reduce clarity and reproducibility.
- Figure 3 (behavioral prediction) lacks methodological clarity (features used, baselines, CV protocol) and omits key baselines (PCA).
- Typos and editing issues throughout; a few citations are missing or unclear in-text.

Requested revisions and suggestions
- Quantify spatial structure and interpretability:
  - Spatial contiguity: number of connected components per atom; average cluster size; compactness (perimeter/area); Laplacian energy v^TΔv; TV norm; Moran’s I.
  - Neuroanatomical relevance: spatial correlation/Dice with known atlases (e.g., Yeo 7/17 networks, AAL/Harvard–Oxford) over a range of thresholds.
  - Reproducibility: split-half with Hungarian matching; report mean/median correlations and confidence intervals; I2C2 or bootstrap stability.
- Standardize figure comparisons:
  - Same slices, color scales, and thresholds (also show unthresholded maps).
  - Match atoms consistently across methods; report per-atom stability/overlap.
- Behavioral prediction:
  - Clarify if regressors use U_i (codes) or raw maps; standardize k across methods; include PCA baseline; use nested CV; report test R^2 with CIs; specify target variables; ensure fairness of preprocessing across methods.
- Algorithmic details:
  - Provide derivation of Eq. (6) (ridge codes), computational complexity per minibatch, and numerical stability considerations.
  - Precisely define Eq. (8) quantities (A_t[j,j], A_jt, B_jt, V_j) and fix transpose/sign typos; give the exact FISTA update, step size (L = 1 + γ̃λ_max(Δ)), stopping criteria, and projection operator for C (simplex vs ℓ1 ball), with references for O(p) projection.
  - Justify schedules α_t ~ t^-1/2 and γ_t constraints; provide sensitivity analyses and recommended ranges.
- References and presentation:
  - Ensure HCP reference (Van Essen et al., 2013) is cited in-text at first mention; include GraphNet and structured PCA references appropriately.
  - Fix typos (e.g., “Graph-Net penalty can imposes”, duplicated words, stray bold sentence) and clean editing.

Arguments for acceptance
- Scalable, principled extension of online DL that is likely to be useful in practice.
- Conceptually clear and potentially impactful for data-driven ROI discovery at scale.
- Rebuttal indicates responsiveness and technical maturity.

Arguments against acceptance
- Current evidence does not yet convincingly demonstrate neuroscientific advantages over baselines.
- Clarity/notation issues impede reproducibility.
- Figures and metrics do not yet support key interpretability claims.

Recommendation: Weak reject (borderline). With the requested quantitative analyses and clarity improvements (some of which appeared in the rebuttal), this work could become a strong contribution. Confidence: medium.
