Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method for neuroimaging that augments Mairal et al. (2010) with a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty on dictionary atoms to encourage spatially compact, smooth “blob-like” components, in addition to sparsity via an l1-type constraint on atoms. Codes are estimated via ridge regression. The algorithm alternates between coding and block-coordinate dictionary updates, using a projected first-order solver (FISTA-style) for the atom subproblem. The goal is interpretable multivariate decompositions that scale to large n and p. Experiments on HCP task fMRI compare Smooth-SODL to vanilla online sparse dictionary learning and CanICA; results emphasize improved interpretability and stability with modest runtime overhead and competitive explained variance and behavioral prediction.

This falls between prior online sparse coding (Mairal 2010; Mensch 2016) and structured sparsity/GraphNet/TV-ℓ1 approaches used in neuroimaging (e.g., Abraham MICCAI; Jenatton et al.; Grosenick et al.). The contribution is a pragmatic integration of spatial smoothness into an online factorization.

Strengths
- Scalable, practical algorithm with modest overhead (≈2–3× SODL) and clear convergence guarantees inherited from online matrix factorization under convex subproblems.
- Empirical evidence that Laplacian regularization yields more localized, visually cleaner components and higher split-half stability than SODL, particularly in low-data regimes.
- Sensible discussion of regularization scheduling (γ tied to At entries), mini-batching, and hyperparameter effects; preliminary guidance for αt.
- Comparisons on a large public dataset (HCP), including explained variance, prediction of behavioral variables, and runtime.
- Promise of open-source implementation within Nilearn.

Weaknesses and requested clarifications
- Novelty is limited. The method is essentially SODL with a smoothness penalty on atoms and projected first-order updates. Given prior structured dictionary learning and GraphNet/TV-ℓ1 literature in neuroimaging, the conceptual advance may not meet NIPS standards without deeper analysis or new theory.
- Motivation and definition of “structure.” The term is used informally (“blobs”). Please define “structured” precisely (e.g., bounded total variation, Sobolev seminorm) and justify why Laplacian is preferable to TV (edge-preserving), GraphNet (ℓ2 + TV), or morphological constraints for delineating ROIs.
- Codes: ℓ2 vs ℓ1. The choice of ridge coding departs from Mairal (2010) and from many neuroimaging decompositions. Provide a principled rationale (stability? speed? identifiability?) and an empirical ablation (ℓ2 vs ℓ1 vs elastic net) on stability, EV, prediction, and runtime. As written, Eq. (2) needs more justification for φ(U) = (α/2)‖U‖2, especially since sparsity in codes can aid interpretability.
- Notation and minor errors.
  - Eq. (1): there is confusion between row/column conventions; Xi is defined as a p-dimensional row vector but coded as VUi with Ui ∈ Rk×1. Please make Xi a column (or transpose consistently) and define V = [v1 … vk] with vi ∈ Rp.
  - In multiple places “max” appears where “min” is intended (e.g., bullets around Eq. (2)); double-check all optimization signs.
  - The reference to “line 146” is unclear; replace with a section/equation reference.
  - Numerous typos and grammatical errors impede readability (“intepretable,” “xperiments,” “volumic,” “there is not step size”).
- Algorithmic clarity.
  - The atom update solves minv∈C 1/2‖v − a‖2 + (γ̃/2)vTΔv. Since this is smooth in v with Lipschitz gradient, and C enforces sparsity (ℓ1-ball) or nonnegativity (simplex), you are using projected (accelerated) gradient. Please state explicitly: smooth term = quadratic + Laplacian; nonsmooth term = indicator of C; proximal of the latter is projection onto C. Clarify boundary conditions for Δ (Neumann vs Dirichlet) and how the brain mask and voxel spacing enter.
  - The claim that “1–2 iterations of FISTA reach 1e-6 accuracy” is not credible without a convergence diagnostic; report inner residuals and use a principled stopping rule or warm-start justification.
  - The γ schedule (Eq. (9)) is heuristic; provide a formal argument (or empirical plot) showing its effect on step sizes and convergence, and whether backtracking is used.
- Evaluation concerns.
  - Fairness: CanICA is run on smoothed data, SODL/Smooth-SODL on unsmoothed data. Show results with/without smoothing for all methods, or justify why this mixed preprocessing is appropriate.
  - Report error bars and statistical tests for EV, stability, and prediction; provide split details and Hungarian matching criteria.
  - Positivity/simplex constraint: many GLM Z-maps contain positive and negative effects. If atoms are constrained to be nonnegative, explain the modeling implication and whether this biases decompositions; include results with signed atoms (ℓ1-ball without nonnegativity).
  - Broader validation: include at least one additional task or resting-state dataset to test generality; assess robustness to registration variability; compare Laplacian vs TV regularization.
  - Complexity: provide per-iteration cost and memory complexity in p, k, and batch size; describe the FFT variant and when it is applicable.

Quality
The algorithmic components are standard and sound if clarified as projected accelerated gradient within an online BCD scheme. However, important choices (codes ℓ2 vs ℓ1; γ scheduling; boundary conditions) are under-justified, and several notational/presentation issues reduce confidence.

Clarity
Presentation needs significant polishing. Clean up notation (vectors as columns throughout), fix typos, remove ambiguous “line 146,” and more clearly separate smooth vs nonsmooth terms in the atom update. Define “structured,” “blobs,” and “reproducibility” metrics precisely. Provide full implementation details for reproducibility.

Originality
Incremental. The main idea—injecting a Laplacian smoothness term into online dictionary updates—is a modest extension relative to SODL and prior structured sparse decompositions (GraphNet/TV-ℓ1, structured PCA). To strengthen originality, include new theoretical insight (e.g., identifiability or stability benefits from Laplacian priors) or clearly superior empirical results across multiple settings.

Significance
If robustly validated, a scalable structured decomposition yielding interpretable ROIs is useful for neuroimaging practice. Current results show interpretability and early-data gains, but predictive and EV metrics are not consistently superior to baselines, and broader validation is limited.

Questions for the authors
- Why ridge codes over lasso/elastic net? Provide comparative results.
- How are Δ boundary conditions and voxel anisotropy handled?
- What is the exact projected FISTA scheme and stopping criterion? Are inner problems solved to fixed tolerance?
- Does the nonnegativity/simplex constraint harm representation of negative effects?
- Can you show sensitivity to γ, τ with confidence intervals, and include TV vs Laplacian?

Pros for acceptance
- Scalable, practical algorithm with modest overhead.
- Improved interpretability and stability over SODL; early-data advantages.
- Clear connection to widely used online sparse coding; potential impact in neuroimaging tooling.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to prior work.
- Incomplete justification of key modeling choices and mixed preprocessing in comparisons.
- Presentation/notation issues and lack of thorough statistical validation.

Overall assessment
Borderline to reject. With clearer motivation, stronger empirical validation (including fair baselines and ablations), and improved exposition, this could be a solid contribution for neuroimaging-focused venues; as-is, it falls short of NIPS standards on originality and clarity.
