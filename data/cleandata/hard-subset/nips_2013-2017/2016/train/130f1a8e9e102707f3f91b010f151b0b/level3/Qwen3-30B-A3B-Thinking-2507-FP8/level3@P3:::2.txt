

# Review for "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes an online structured dictionary learning algorithm with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatially contiguous dictionary atoms for neuroimaging data. The work extends the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness in the dictionary atoms. The authors claim this approach better captures brain structure organization compared to standard sparse dictionary learning (SODL) and other methods like CanICA. The paper demonstrates results on the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset, arguing that the spatial regularization improves interpretability and captures inter-subject variability more effectively.

## Quality
The paper's technical framework follows a well-established approach to dictionary learning, and the integration of the Sobolev prior appears conceptually sound. The algorithmic details in Section 3 are generally well-described, and the convergence guarantees for the proposed method are appropriately referenced. However, the empirical support for the Sobolev prior's benefits is weak. The main claim that the Sobolev prior leads to "more structured" atoms (Figure 2) lacks quantitative support - the visual comparison shows different slices for different methods, making direct comparison impossible, and no quantitative metrics demonstrate the spatial structure advantage. The paper claims "structured" atoms but provides no neuroscience-justified metrics to validate this claim. The rebuttal provided additional results that improved the technical quality rating, but these should be incorporated into the revised manuscript. The running time comparison (3x slower than SODL) is reasonable given the additional penalty, but the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that this cost is justified by the benefits.

## Clarity
The manuscript contains several presentation issues that impede understanding. The mathematical notation is inconsistent (e.g., Equation 6 has a transpose placement issue, and Equation 8 contains unclear variable references). The description of the Laplacian penalty in Equation 4 is confusing, as it doesn't clearly distinguish between the discrete gradient operators and the Laplacian matrix. The reference to the HCP dataset is missing, which is a critical omission for the neuroimaging community. Figure 2 is particularly problematic - the different slices shown for each method prevent direct comparison, and the claim that SSMF atoms are more "structured" lacks visual or quantitative support. Figure 3's caption and legend are unclear about what is being predicted and which baselines are included (PCA is missing from the legend). Multiple typos and editing issues throughout the manuscript (e.g., "Graph-Net penalty can imposes," duplicated words, stray bold text) further reduce clarity.

## Originality
The main contribution is the extension of online dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior for spatial structure. While the Sobolev prior has been used in other contexts (e.g., total-variation regularization in medical imaging), applying it to online dictionary learning for neuroimaging is novel. The paper appropriately references related work (e.g., [1, 25, 23]), though it could better differentiate its contribution from these prior works. The combination of online learning with spatial structure is the key novelty, though the paper doesn't sufficiently emphasize how this differs from prior structured dictionary learning approaches.

## Significance
The method could be a useful tool for neuroscience and medical imaging, potentially advancing how brain regions are defined from functional imaging data. The ability to handle large-scale datasets with online learning is valuable for modern neuroimaging studies. However, the significance is currently limited by the weak empirical evidence supporting the Sobolev prior's benefits. The paper claims to better capture inter-subject variability, but the quantitative evidence for this claim is insufficient. The potential impact on other domains (image segmentation, regional sensing) is mentioned but not explored.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Well-motivated problem of defining brain regions in a principled way
- Appropriate extension of online dictionary learning to include spatial structure
- Good computational efficiency (only 3x slower than SODL)
- Relevant experiments on a large-scale neuroimaging dataset

**Weaknesses:**
- Weak empirical support for the Sobolev prior's benefits
- Figure 2 lacks quantitative metrics and uses different slices for comparison
- Numerous mathematical and presentation errors
- Missing HCP dataset reference
- Insufficient justification for spatial regularization claims

## Recommendation
The paper has significant potential but requires substantial revision to address the empirical and presentation issues. The technical quality is now acceptable (rating 3) following the rebuttal, but the manuscript must incorporate the requested quantitative metrics and fix the presentation issues. The authors should provide explicit neuroscience-justified criteria (e.g., spatial compactness metrics, comparison to known brain regions) to demonstrate the Sobolev prior's advantage quantitatively. The algorithmic details for Equation (6) should be included in an appendix. The figures should be revised to allow direct comparison of atoms across methods using the same brain slices.

Given the improvements from the rebuttal and the potential value of the method for neuroimaging, I recommend acceptance with major revisions addressing the empirical and presentation issues. The paper would be significantly stronger with the requested quantitative evidence and clearer presentation.
