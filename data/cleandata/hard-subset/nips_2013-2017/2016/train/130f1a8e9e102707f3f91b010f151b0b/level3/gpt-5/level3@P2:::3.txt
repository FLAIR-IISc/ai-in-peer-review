Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning framework for neuroimaging in which dictionary atoms are constrained to be sparse (via an L1-type constraint on atoms) and spatially structured (via a Laplacian/Sobolev penalty on atoms). Codes are obtained with ridge regression. The algorithm builds on the online block-coordinate descent framework of Mairal et al. (JMLR 2010), replacing the simple Euclidean projection step for atom updates with a projected accelerated gradient method (FISTA) on a quadratic with a Laplacian term, keeping the per-iteration overhead modest (reported 2–3×). The method targets interpretable “blob-like” components that can serve as data-driven ROIs and is evaluated on HCP fMRI task maps against SODL and CanICA.

The work relates to several strands: online dictionary learning for large-scale matrix factorization [Mairal 2010; Mensch 2016], structured sparsity and spatial priors for neuroimaging (e.g., TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning for atlas extraction, Abraham et al., MICCAI; GraphNet-type smoothness, Grosenick et al.; structured sparse PCA, Jenatton et al.), and multi-subject ICA (Varoquaux et al., CanICA). The main claimed novelty is to integrate a Sobolev/Laplacian prior on atoms into an online DL algorithm with convergence guarantees and small computational overhead, enabling large-scale spatially structured decompositions.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The optimization is standard: codes via ridge regression; atom updates minimize a strongly convex quadratic with a Laplacian term under a convex constraint, solved with projected FISTA. Using the indicator of C as a proximal term is appropriate; the Lipschitz constant and step-size discussion is sensible, though practical guidance on γ beyond asymptotic scaling would help. Convergence to a stationary point follows prior BCD analyses given their assumptions.
- Empirical support: The evaluation relies heavily on visual inspection of components and general descriptive statements of “blobby” structure. Quantitative metrics are limited to explained variance, a split-half “stability” (mean atom correlation), and downstream prediction. While these are useful, they do not directly test whether the method learns more consistent, anatomically meaningful ROIs across subjects. In particular, the claimed benefit—consolidating inter-subject variability into spatially coherent regions—would be better validated with explicit inter-subject consistency measures (e.g., split-half Dice/Jaccard of supports; overlap with known atlases; spatial reproducibility maps; stability selection).
- Baselines/controls: Because the Laplacian prior enforces smoothness, it is important to control for simple pre-smoothing baselines. The paper smooths inputs for CanICA but not for SODL, leaving open whether pre-smoothing plus SODL would match the proposed method. Similarly, an offline TV-ℓ1 baseline—even on a subset—would situate the contribution relative to established structured decompositions.
- Statistical rigor: Hyperparameter selection is discussed qualitatively; more systematic tuning (nested CV) and sensitivity curves for (γ, τ) would strengthen claims. Reported stability numbers (0.4 vs 0.1) are small; confidence intervals or statistical tests would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized but contains typos and some notational inconsistencies. The algorithmic section could benefit from:
- An explicit composite objective decomposition and proximal operator definitions (projection onto C) for the FISTA update.
- Implementation details: boundary conditions for the Laplacian; normalization of atoms; stopping criteria and tolerances; memory/runtime per iteration; mini-batch handling.
- Clear definitions of stability and reproducibility metrics and how atom matching (Hungarian algorithm) is applied.
Figures should include clearer color scales and quantitative annotations; “selected most interpretable atoms” introduces selection bias.

Originality
Adding a Sobolev/Laplacian prior on atoms is not novel per se in neuroimaging, and spatially structured decompositions (TV, GraphNet, structured sparse PCA) are well studied. The main novelty is engineering this within an online DL framework with small overhead and convergence guarantees. This is an incremental but useful contribution if demonstrated to materially improve reproducibility/interpretability at scale.

Significance
The potential impact for data-driven ROI discovery is high. However, the presented evidence does not yet clearly establish practical benefits beyond smoother-looking components:
- On prediction, CanICA performs best on test R2; Smooth-SODL improves over SODL and tCanICA but does not set SOTA.
- On explained variance, Smooth-SODL lags CanICA (as expected), and the gain over SODL is modest.
- The early-data regime advantage (31% vs 2% EV after 17 subjects) is interesting, but its relevance in HCP-scale settings (where data are abundant) is unclear. Demonstrating benefits on smaller cohorts or single-site datasets would strengthen the case.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant problem (interpretable, spatially structured decompositions) with a scalable online method.
- Algorithmic integration is clean and appears to incur only modest overhead; convergence arguments are in line with prior work.
- Qualitative improvements in spatial coherence are plausible and valuable for neuroscientific interpretation.
- Early-data regime improvements suggest potential utility when data are limited.

Cons for acceptance
- Validation leans on subjective visual assessments; objective inter-subject consistency and anatomical plausibility are under-quantified.
- Missing key baselines/controls (pre-smoothed SODL; TV-ℓ1 on subsets).
- Predictive performance does not improve over standard baselines; explained variance gains are limited.
- Hyperparameter selection and stability analyses lack statistical rigor; small stability gains reported without CIs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative spatial reproducibility: split-half Dice/Jaccard of atom supports, test–retest stability, and overlap with standard atlases (e.g., AAL, Yeo) with permutation tests.
- Include baselines: (i) SODL on pre-smoothed inputs; (ii) a TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning baseline on a subset; (iii) ablations (L1-only, Laplacian-only, combined) and code penalty variants (ridge vs L1).
- Provide sensitivity analyses and principled hyperparameter tuning (nested CV) with confidence intervals for EV, stability, and prediction.
- Evaluate on additional tasks/datasets and smaller N to substantiate the early-data advantage.
- Clarify algorithmic details (prox, Laplacian operator, boundary conditions), and release code to ensure reproducibility.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The algorithmic idea is reasonable and likely useful, but the current validation is too subjective and does not convincingly establish a substantive benefit over simpler baselines or existing structured methods. Strengthening the experimental evidence as suggested could make this a solid contribution.
