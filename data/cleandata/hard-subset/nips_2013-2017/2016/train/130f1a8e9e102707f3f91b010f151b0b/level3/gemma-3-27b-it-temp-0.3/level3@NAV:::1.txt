## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework incorporating a Laplacian penalty to identify structured, sparse brain regions from fMRI data. The motivation – leveraging inter-subject variability to define principled regions of interest (ROIs) – is well-articulated and addresses a significant challenge in neuroimaging. The approach builds upon existing online dictionary learning methods (Mairal et al., 2010) by adding a structural prior, aiming for more interpretable and robust decompositions. The problem is relevant to the NIPS audience, as it combines optimization techniques with a challenging application in a large-scale data setting.

The strengths of the paper lie in its attempt to bridge the gap between scalable online learning and the need for spatially coherent features in brain imaging. The authors correctly identify the limitations of purely data-driven approaches like ICA and propose a method that explicitly encourages localized, smooth dictionary atoms. The experimental results on the HCP dataset demonstrate improvements in explained variance and predictive power compared to standard online dictionary learning and CanICA, suggesting the spatial regularization is beneficial. The inclusion of a discussion on hyperparameter tuning and computational complexity is also valuable.

However, the paper suffers from a significant lack of theoretical analysis. While the authors state the problem is convex and leverage existing convergence results from Mairal et al. (2010), there's no discussion of *how* the addition of the Laplacian penalty affects the convergence rate or the statistical properties of the learned dictionary. A theoretical justification for the choice of the Laplacian penalty, or a comparison to other potential structural priors, is missing. Furthermore, the empirical evaluation, while promising, lacks a more rigorous statistical comparison. The gains over baseline methods are not always substantial, and a more thorough ablation study (e.g., varying the strength of the Laplacian penalty) would strengthen the conclusions.

Critically, the manuscript quality is poor. Numerous instances of careless writing and leftover comments (as noted in the submission itself) detract from the overall impression. This suggests a lack of thorough proofreading and attention to detail. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in neuroimaging.
*   Presents a novel combination of online learning and spatial regularization.
*   Demonstrates empirical improvements over baseline methods on a large dataset.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks theoretical analysis to support the proposed method.
*   Poor manuscript quality with numerous writing errors and artifacts.
*   Empirical evaluation could be more rigorous and include a more detailed ablation study.



**Overall, I recommend rejection.** While the idea is promising, the lack of theoretical grounding and the poor manuscript quality prevent this paper from meeting the standards for NIPS. Addressing these issues – particularly providing a theoretical justification for the method and significantly improving the writing – would substantially increase the paper's potential.
