Originality & motivation: Novel, well-motivated combination of Sobolev (Laplacian) and L1 regularization within an online dictionary learning framework for fMRI.

Significance & scalability: Addresses large-cohort neuroimaging modeling; online algorithm scales to 500 subjects with modest additional runtime (2–3x).

Problem formulation: Clear principled optimization (Eq.2) and appropriate block-coordinate descent solver.

Limited empirical gains — explained variance: Smooth-SODL gives modest test improvement over SODL (55% vs 49%) but is outperformed by CanICA (60%).

Limited empirical gains — behavioral prediction: Small, inconsistent improvements over SODL across cognitive scores; CanICA performs best on test data.

Data-regime claim overstated: Authors claim superiority across regimes, but main results in large-data regime show only marginal benefits.

Insufficient analysis: Paper lacks deep investigation into why spatial prior yields limited generalization gains and whether Laplacian penalty is the right choice.

Questionable neuroscientific relevance: Visual "cleaner" maps may be cosmetic; unclear if they improve functional interpretability.

Clarity and presentation: Manuscript is rushed and hard to read, with dense/confusing writing and informal language.

Incomplete references: Reference list has missing years, venues, or page numbers, undermining credibility.

Technical exposition issues: Key derivations (e.g., Eq.7) are abbreviated and details deferred, hurting reproducibility.

Formatting and citation problems: Awkward use of footnotes for citations and other presentation lapses.

Overall recommendation: Interesting, valuable idea but not ready for publication without major revision.

Required revisions: More thorough experimental evaluation (additional datasets/tasks), deeper analysis of results, and full manuscript overhaul for clarity and completeness.

Arguments for acceptance: Novel regularization combination, tackles an important large-scale neuroimaging problem, and presents a sound/practical algorithmic solution.

Arguments for rejection: Marginal empirical improvements, inadequate analysis of results, and poor writing/presentation that impede scientific assessment.
