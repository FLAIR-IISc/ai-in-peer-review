- Proposes Smooth-SODL: online dictionary learning with l1 sparsity and Sobolev/Laplacian smoothness to produce spatially compact neuroimaging atoms.  
- Optimization via block-coordinate descent: ridge updates for coefficients and FISTA for atoms with convex constraints.  
- Targets scalability to large n,p and aims for spatially coherent “blob-like” components compared to SODL/CanICA.

- Strength: Addresses a relevant scalable, structured factorization problem for high-dimensional neuroimaging.  
- Strength: Methodologically sensible—convex Sobolev prior is a natural way to enforce smoothness and integrates into online BCD.  
- Strength: Computationally practical—reported 2–3× overhead vs SODL and competitive with batch PCA/ICA.  
- Strength: Empirical signals include visually smoother atoms, modest split-half stability gains, and small-n learning benefits.

- Weakness: Evidence of benefit is largely qualitative and relies on subjective visual inspection and selected “most interpretable” atoms (possible cherry-picking).  
- Weakness: Quantitative results are mixed—lower explained variance than CanICA, no clear behavioral prediction improvement, and modest stability gains.  
- Weakness: Improvements may simply reflect the imposed prior rather than capturing meaningful inter-subject structure.  
- Weakness: Advantage diminishes with large datasets (HCP-scale); regularization helps mainly in low-data regimes.  
- Weakness: Missing rigorous validation of inter-subject consistency, alignment with known atlases, or cross-dataset generalization.  
- Weakness: Methodological clarity issues—unconvincing claims about FISTA iterations, unclear gamma schedule implementation, and potentially unfair baseline preprocessing.  
- Weakness: Hyperparameter selection lacks rigorous justification and clear cross-validation criteria.  
- Weakness: Figures overemphasize visual examples and do not report full atom sets or quantitative spatial metrics.

- Quality: Technically sound at a high level; optimization is standard and likely convergent to stationary points.  
- Quality: Empirical evaluation insufficiently convincing to show advantages beyond cosmetic smoothness.

- Clarity: Paper is readable but contains typos, minor inconsistencies, and unclear descriptions of constraint implications.  
- Clarity: Visual emphasis without quantitative spatial metrics reduces transparency; full atom sets should be shown.

- Originality: Moderate—novelty mainly in combining Laplacian prior with online dictionary updates for large-scale neuroimaging.

- Significance: Unclear—would be high if it improved reproducibility, prediction, or alignment with known organization, but current benefits seem limited.

- Requested improvement: Quantify spatial structure and inter-subject consistency (e.g., Dice/Jaccard, ICC, Moran’s I, compactness).  
- Requested improvement: Produce consensus maps/regions across subjects and validate region consistency across resamples.  
- Requested improvement: Use fairer and stronger baselines (SODL with matched smoothing, TV-l1/GraphNet, parcellations, atlas ROIs, convolutional dictionary learning).  
- Requested improvement: External validation against atlases (Yeo/Glasser) with permutation tests and cross-dataset/task generalization or test-retest reliability.  
- Requested improvement: Add synthetic experiments with known ground truth to assess recovery and sample complexity.  
- Requested improvement: Provide clear hyperparameter protocol, justify α schedule, and ablation studies (γ=0, varying τ).  
- Requested improvement: Release code and full atom sets; avoid selective visualization.  
- Requested improvement: Clarify implementation details (FISTA accuracy, gamma schedule) and ensure fairness in baseline preprocessing.

- Pros for acceptance: Practical, scalable extension with a well-motivated spatial prior and modest computational cost.  
- Pros for acceptance: Some evidence of improved early learning and small-n stability.  
- Pros for acceptance: Simple implementation that could be useful if validated further.

- Cons for acceptance: Validation is largely subjective and objective gains are not clearly demonstrated.  
- Cons for acceptance: Benefits shrink with large n, reducing significance for HCP-scale analyses.  
- Cons for acceptance: Baseline and evaluation choices may bias results in favor of the method; inter-subject validation is weak.

- Recommendation: Leaning to reject (borderline); interesting idea but empirical evidence insufficient to claim clear advantage.  
- Confidence: High on methodological assessment; medium on domain-specific neuroimaging validation.
