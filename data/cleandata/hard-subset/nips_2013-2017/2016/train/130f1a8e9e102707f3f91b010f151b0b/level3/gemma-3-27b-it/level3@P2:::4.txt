This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, aiming to extract structured and sparse components representing brain regions. The authors combine L1 regularization to enforce sparsity with a Sobolev penalty to encourage smoothness and compactness of the learned components (blobs). The method extends previous online dictionary learning work (Mairal et al., 2010) and is applied to fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project.

The core idea of learning interpretable brain regions directly from data variability, rather than relying on pre-defined ROIs or purely data-driven clustering, is a valuable contribution. This approach aligns with a growing trend in neuroimaging to move beyond simple statistical testing and towards more biologically plausible models. The combination of L1 and Sobolev penalties is a reasonable strategy to achieve both sparsity and structure in the learned components, and builds upon existing work in sparse signal representation and image processing. Relating this to established methods like ICA and PCA, as the authors do, helps contextualize the contribution. The online learning aspect is crucial for scalability to large datasets, which is often a limiting factor in neuroimaging analyses.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the authors claim improvements over state-of-the-art methods, these improvements are often minor or lack a clear explanation. For instance, the performance gains reported in Fig. 2(c) (predicting behavioral variables) are not substantially better than competing methods, and the rationale for why the proposed method *should* perform better is not fully articulated. The explanation of why CanICA performs well despite lacking sparsity constraints is missing. The authors mention a “stability-fidelity trade-off,” but don’t thoroughly explore this trade-off or its implications for their method. It's unclear why the observed improvements justify the added complexity of the Sobolev penalty.

The writing quality is also a significant concern. The manuscript contains draft-like comments ("N.B."), and the overall readability is hampered by convoluted phrasing and a lack of clarity in some sections. The description of the algorithm, while technically detailed, could be more accessible. For example, the explanation of the BCD update rules (Alg. 1 & 2) is dense and requires a strong background in optimization to fully understand. The mathematical notation, while correct, could benefit from more consistent formatting and better explanation of variables.  The discussion of hyperparameter tuning, while acknowledging the difficulty, feels somewhat incomplete. The justification for the data-adaptive choice of α (Eq. 10) is missing.

The originality, while present in the combination of penalties, feels incremental. Several papers have explored sparse dictionary learning for fMRI analysis (e.g., Baldassarre et al., 2011, cited in related works), and others have incorporated spatial priors (Abraham et al., 2014). The novelty lies in the specific combination of online learning, L1, and Sobolev regularization, but the impact of this combination is not convincingly demonstrated.  The related work section is adequate but could benefit from a more thorough discussion of existing methods for spatially regularized decomposition, particularly those used in neuroimaging.

Regarding significance, while the proposed method has the potential to advance the field, its current impact is limited by the modest improvements and the lack of a clear explanation for those improvements. The potential benefits for ROI extraction and understanding inter-subject variability are mentioned, but further validation is needed to demonstrate the practical utility of the method.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in neuroimaging.
*   Presents a novel combination of existing techniques (online dictionary learning, L1 regularization, Sobolev penalty).
*   Demonstrates some improvements over baseline methods, albeit modest.
*   Scales to large datasets due to the online learning approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The claimed improvements are often minor and lack a clear explanation.
*   The writing quality is poor and hinders readability.
*   The originality is incremental, and the impact of the proposed method is not fully demonstrated.
*   Insufficient analysis of the trade-offs involved in the method.
*   The manuscript reads as rushed and incomplete.



**Overall assessment:** Reject. While the paper tackles an interesting problem and presents a technically sound approach, the modest improvements, poor writing quality, and lack of thorough analysis outweigh the contributions. A major revision, addressing the clarity, justification of results, and providing a more compelling demonstration of the method’s significance, would be required for reconsideration. The authors should focus on explaining *why* their method works and *what* specific advantages it offers over existing approaches.
