This paper proposes an extension to the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) for analyzing large-scale neuroimaging data. The primary contribution is the introduction of a spatial smoothness prior, implemented as a Laplacian penalty (a Sobolev prior), on the dictionary atoms. The resulting model, termed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), aims to learn brain "regions" or components that are not only sparse (via an L1-norm constraint) but also spatially coherent and "blob-like". The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to massive datasets, such as the Human Connectome Project (HCP) data used in the experiments. The authors compare their method against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a multi-subject ICA-based method (CanICA), arguing that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable and stable results, particularly in low-data regimes, while maintaining comparable computational efficiency.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Technical Contribution:** The paper addresses a relevant problem: incorporating structural priors into scalable, online matrix factorization models for neuroimaging. Extending the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. to include a Laplacian penalty is a non-trivial technical contribution. The derivation of the block-coordinate descent update steps and the analysis of its computational complexity are well-executed.

2.  **Scalability:** A key strength, inherited from the underlying online framework, is the algorithm's ability to handle datasets that are large in both the number of subjects (n) and the number of features (p). This is highly relevant for modern neuroimaging consortia like the HCP. The empirical timing results demonstrate that the additional complexity of the smoothness penalty is manageable, adding only a modest computational overhead compared to the baseline SODL.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated, the proposed model is formulated precisely, and the algorithmic details are explained. The connection to and differentiation from related work is also clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses and Detailed Comments:**

1.  **Significance of the Contribution is Unclear:** The central claim is that adding a smoothness penalty improves the resulting dictionary. However, it is an unsurprising result that adding a spatial smoothness penalty produces spatially smoother dictionary atoms. The crucial question is whether this leads to a scientifically or practically significant improvement. The evidence presented in the paper is not fully convincing on this point. The improvement seems incremental rather than a breakthrough.

2.  **Evaluation Relies Heavily on Subjective Visual Inspection:** The primary evidence for the superiority of Smooth-SODL comes from the qualitative assessment of dictionary atoms in Figure 2(a). For a reader not deeply familiar with functional neuroanatomy, it is difficult to judge whether the "blobs" from Smooth-SODL are genuinely more interpretable or scientifically valid than those from SODL. The authors state that SODL maps have "a thick ring of very small nonzero values," but this is hard to discern from the provided figures. A more objective or quantitative measure of interpretability or alignment with known neuroanatomical atlases would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

3.  **Quantitative Results Are Not Conclusive:** The quantitative comparisons do not show a clear and consistent advantage for Smooth-SODL.
    *   In terms of explained variance (Fig 2b), CanICA is superior, which is expected. The gain of Smooth-SODL over SODL is modest (~5%) on the test set.
    *   In the prediction of behavioral variables (Fig 2c), CanICA again performs best overall. Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL, but the margin is small, and for several behavioral variables, the predictive power of all models is quite low.
    *   The most compelling result is in Table 1, which shows a significant advantage in the low-data regime. This is an important finding. However, the main experiments and motivation are centered around the HCP, a massive dataset where this data scarcity problem is less pronounced. This creates a slight disconnect between the paper's strongest result and its primary experimental setting.

4.  **Missed Opportunity for Validation via Inter-subject Consistency:** The paper motivates the work by highlighting the importance of modeling inter-subject variability. A powerful way to validate the model would be to demonstrate that the learned components are more stable and reproducible across different subsets of subjects. While a stability score is briefly mentioned (0.4 for Smooth-SODL vs 0.1 for SODL), this is a crucial point that deserves a more thorough analysis and visualization. If Smooth-SODL truly captures more meaningful biological structure, it should find that structure more consistently across the population than methods without this prior. This would be a much stronger argument than the subjective visual appeal of the components.

**Questions for Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more quantitative assessment of the "interpretability" of the learned atoms? For instance, by measuring the overlap or correspondence of the learned components with canonical functional networks or brain atlases?
*   The stability score reported (0.4 for Smooth-SODL vs 0.1 for SODL) is a key result supporting your method. Could you elaborate on how this was calculated and perhaps provide more detail or visualization on the reproducibility of the components across data splits?
*   Given that the strongest performance gains are in the low-data regime (Table 1), do you see the primary application of this method for smaller-scale studies, or do you maintain it offers a significant advantage for large-scale datasets like HCP beyond producing visually smoother maps?

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound and well-engineered extension to online dictionary learning for neuroimaging. The scalability is a definite plus. However, the central weakness is the unconvincing demonstration of the significance of the contribution. The primary evidence is subjective, and the quantitative results, while showing some benefit, are not strong enough to make a compelling case that this method provides a fundamentally better way to analyze large-scale fMRI data. The work would be much stronger if the benefits of the smoothness prior were demonstrated through more objective validation, particularly concerning the consistency and scientific relevance of the discovered brain regions.
