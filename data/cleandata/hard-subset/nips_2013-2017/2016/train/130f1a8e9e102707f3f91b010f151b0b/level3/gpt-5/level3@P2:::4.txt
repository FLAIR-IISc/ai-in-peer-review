Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning algorithm for neuroimaging that enforces sparsity and spatial structure on dictionary atoms. Each brain image is approximated by a linear combination of k atoms; sparsity is imposed via an L1-type constraint on atoms (L1-ball or simplex), and spatial structure via a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty (vTΔv). Codes are computed via ridge regression, and the dictionary is updated with block-coordinate descent, where each atom update solves a Laplacian-regularized denoising problem with a simple convex constraint using FISTA. The method builds on sparse online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) by adding the Laplacian prior, aiming for blob-like, interpretable, compact components. On HCP fMRI, Smooth-SODL is reported to yield more structured atoms, somewhat higher stability across splits, similar explained variance to SODL, and slightly worse explained variance than CanICA. Runtime is within a small constant factor of SODL and faster than CanICA on the reported setup.

This connects to: (i) online dictionary learning and massive matrix factorization (Mairal et al., 2010; Mensch et al., 2016), (ii) structured sparsity and spatial priors for neuroimaging (e.g., TV-L1 dictionary learning and GraphNet; Abraham 2013; Grosenick 2013), and (iii) multi-subject PCA/ICA baselines (Varoquaux et al., CanICA; Smith et al., MELODIC). Prior structured approaches tended to rely on slow alternating optimization; this work argues for an online, scalable variant with a Laplacian penalty.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The block-coordinate online scheme is standard, the code step is ridge regression, and the atom step reduces to a convex denoising problem with a Laplacian quadratic and a convex constraint. The Lipschitz analysis for FISTA (L = 1+γ̃LΔ) is routine. Convergence claims match prior online DL results under similar assumptions. However, several technical aspects are underspecified or potentially overstated:
  - The claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations achieve 1e-6 accuracy seems implausible without warm-start evidence or stopping criteria; showing primal/dual gaps or objective traces would help.
  - The proposed γ schedule (and the bound relating γ to At[j,j]/t) is stated informally and not validated empirically; practical guidance is thin.
  - The atom constraint is sometimes L1 ball, sometimes simplex; the algorithmic consequences differ (nonnegativity vs signed atoms). Clear justification and ablations are missing.
  - No ablation on L1 codes vs ridge codes (the paper mentions both but experiments fix ridge). This matters for stability and sparsity of representations.
- Empirics: The HCP experiment is well-motivated. Reported improvements are modest: interpretability and stability increase, explained variance is lower than CanICA as expected, predictive performance is competitive but not uniformly superior. The early-data regime advantage is interesting. However, the quantitative evaluation of “interpretability” is largely qualitative; stability is measured but not with strong statistical analysis, and baselines (e.g., TV-regularized DL) are missing.

Clarity
The manuscript is readable but feels rushed. There are typos, dangling comments (“th Conference…”, “xperiments”), grammar issues, and some confusing statements (“provably cause updates to take forever”). Several definitions (constraint set C; positivity) and algorithmic details (projection when γ>0; stopping criteria; memory/FFT variant) are scattered or incomplete. Figures are referenced but not fully described in text (metrics, thresholds, split protocol). Suggestions for improving clarity are below.

Originality
Moderate. Combining a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty with online DL is a natural but useful extension of SODL. Prior structured decompositions for neuroimaging have used TV/L1 (often offline or slow), and GraphNet-style smoothness, but the specific formulation of Laplacian-regularized atom updates within an online DL framework appears novel for large-scale fMRI. That said, the step from γ=0 (SODL) to γ>0 is incremental methodologically.

Significance
Moderate. For practitioners, a scalable, near SODL-speed method producing smoother, more localized components is valuable, particularly for ROI extraction and group maps. However, performance gains over simpler baselines are small/unclear, and interpretability is not quantified rigorously. The contribution is more engineering/practical than conceptual. Integration into nilearn would increase impact.

Strengths
- Clear practical motivation: interpretable, compact regions for population fMRI.
- Scalable online algorithm with modest overhead over SODL.
- Sensible convex atom subproblem; reuse of mature first-order methods.
- Early-data regime advantage and improved stability are promising.
- Comprehensive comparison to SODL and CanICA on a large dataset.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty; Laplacian prior is a standard smoothness device.
- Improvements are modest and not consistently demonstrated; predictive results favor CanICA in several targets.
- Lacks rigorous and thorough empirical analysis of hyperparameters, stability (with statistics), and interpretability metrics.
- Missing baselines with structured penalties (e.g., TV-L1 DL; structured sparse PCA/NMF).
- Clarity/quality issues: typos, ambiguous constraints, over-claims about FISTA iterations, informal γ guidance.

Detailed suggestions
- Provide ablations: (i) γ=0 vs γ>0; (ii) Laplacian vs TV regularization; (iii) simplex vs L1-ball constraints; (iv) ridge vs L1 codes. Report EV, stability (split-half correlations with confidence intervals), and prediction.
- Quantify interpretability: spatial contiguity (number/size of connected components), Laplacian energy, overlap with known atlases, and split-half Dice.
- Detail the FISTA inner loop: stopping criterion, number of iterations, warm starts, computational complexity per atom. Report actual inner iteration counts.
- Justify the constraint choice (nonnegativity and L1 radius) for neuroimaging and its effect on results.
- Strengthen fairness: compare with CanICA with and without smoothing and report thresholds; include TV-L1 DL baseline (e.g., Abraham 2013) and structured sparse PCA.
- Provide learning curves and variance across multiple random seeds; include statistical tests where applicable.
- Fix typos and remove draft boilerplate; standardize notation; clearly define Δ, boundary conditions, and FFT option; include memory usage.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method that many neuroimaging users could adopt for more structured maps.
- Early-data regime advantages and improved stability are useful.
- Solid incremental engineering contribution with open-source intent.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to SODL; Laplacian smoothness is standard.
- Empirical gains are minor/unclear; interpretability claims not rigorously quantified.
- Methodological details and clarity need significant polishing; some claims are not well supported.

Overall recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is reasonable and potentially useful, but the current version does not convincingly demonstrate substantial scientific advance or decisive empirical benefits, and the paper needs clearer exposition and stronger experiments.

Reviewer confidence
High in assessing dictionary learning and neuroimaging methods; moderate on domain-specific HCP task details.
