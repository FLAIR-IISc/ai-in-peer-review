## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, aiming to identify spatially coherent brain regions. The core idea is to extend the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage spatially smooth and compact dictionary atoms – effectively “blobs” representing brain structures. The authors motivate this by arguing that inter-subject variability contains valuable structural information, and that a sparse, structured representation can better capture this than traditional approaches. They demonstrate the method on fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project, comparing it to ICA-based methods (CanICA) and standard online dictionary learning (SODL).

The paper builds on a well-established line of work in dictionary learning and its application to neuroimaging. Online dictionary learning, as highlighted, addresses scalability issues inherent in large neuroimaging datasets. The addition of a spatial regularization term is a reasonable approach to encourage biologically plausible, localized representations. The experimental results on HCP data suggest that the proposed method can achieve competitive performance in terms of explained variance and predictive power, and offers improved interpretability compared to standard dictionary learning and ICA. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The novelty is incremental; the method is essentially a straightforward application of Sobolev regularization to an existing online dictionary learning algorithm. This may not meet the standards for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The motivation for the Sobolev regularization remains unclear. The authors state it promotes “structure,” but fail to define what structure means in this context or provide a compelling justification for why spatially smooth atoms are inherently better for representing brain activity. 

Furthermore, there are technical concerns. Eq.(1) appears to have a notational error (likely a transpose missing). The choice of an l2 norm on the codes in Eq.(2) is not adequately justified, particularly in comparison to the l1 norm used in Mairal et al. (2010). The reference to “line 146” is ambiguous. Finally, the paper is poorly written, with numerous typos and a lack of clarity. The language is often imprecise, and the presentation could be significantly improved.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Scalable method for brain image decomposition.
*   Demonstrated performance on a large, publicly available dataset.
*   Potential for improved interpretability compared to existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak novelty – primarily an application of existing techniques.
*   Unclear motivation for the chosen regularization.
*   Technical issues with equations and notation.
*   Poor writing quality and presentation.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend **rejection**. While the work presents a potentially useful method, the lack of significant novelty, the unclear motivation, and the technical and presentational flaws outweigh the strengths. A major revision addressing these points, with a stronger emphasis on the theoretical justification for the approach and a more rigorous evaluation, would be necessary for reconsideration.
