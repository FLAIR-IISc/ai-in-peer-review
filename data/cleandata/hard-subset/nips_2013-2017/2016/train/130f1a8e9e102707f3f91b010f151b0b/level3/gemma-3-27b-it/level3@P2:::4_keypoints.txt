- Proposes multivariate online dictionary learning to decompose brain images into structured, sparse components representing brain regions.  
- Combines L1 regularization for sparsity with a Sobolev penalty to encourage smoothness and compactness of components.  
- Builds on Mairal et al. (2010) online dictionary learning and is applied to HCP fMRI data.  
- Core contribution: learning interpretable brain regions from data variability rather than predefined ROIs or pure clustering.  
- The L1 + Sobolev penalty combination is a reasonable way to obtain sparse, structured components and connects to ICA/PCA literature.  
- Online learning aspect is important for scalability to large neuroimaging datasets.  
- Reported performance improvements are often minor and lack clear explanation (e.g., Fig. 2(c) predictive gains are small).  
- Paper fails to explain why competing methods (e.g., CanICA) perform well despite lacking sparsity constraints.  
- Mentions a “stability–fidelity trade-off” but does not thoroughly analyze or quantify its implications.  
- Unclear why modest observed improvements justify adding the Sobolev penalty’s complexity.  
- Writing quality is poor, includes draft comments, convoluted phrasing, and hampers readability.  
- Algorithm description (BCD updates, Alg. 1 & 2) is dense and inaccessible without strong optimization background.  
- Mathematical notation is correct but inconsistently formatted and variables are under-explained.  
- Discussion of hyperparameter tuning is incomplete; justification for the data-adaptive choice of α (Eq. 10) is missing.  
- Originality is incremental—similar sparse dictionary and spatial-prior approaches exist in prior fMRI work.  
- Novelty mainly in the specific combination of online learning, L1, and Sobolev regularization, but impact is not convincingly demonstrated.  
- Related work section is adequate but should discuss more spatially regularized decomposition methods used in neuroimaging.  
- Significance is limited by modest empirical gains and lack of clear rationale for those gains; further validation needed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a relevant problem, novel combination of techniques, shows some improvements, and scales to large datasets.  
- Arguments against acceptance: minor unexplained improvements, poor writing, incremental novelty, insufficient trade-off analysis, and a rushed/incomplete manuscript.  
- Overall recommendation: Reject in current form; major revision needed to improve clarity, justify results, and more convincingly demonstrate significance.
