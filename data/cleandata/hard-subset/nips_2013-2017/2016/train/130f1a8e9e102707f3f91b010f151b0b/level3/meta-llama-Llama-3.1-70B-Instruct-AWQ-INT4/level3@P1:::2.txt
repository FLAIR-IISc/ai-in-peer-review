This paper proposes a novel online structured dictionary learning algorithm for neuroimaging data, incorporating a Sobolev prior to encourage spatially contiguous dictionary atoms. The authors extend the standard online dictionary-learning framework by adding a Laplacian penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The algorithm, dubbed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), is designed to learn structured regions representative of brain organization from massive brain data.

The technical soundness of the model and algorithms appears solid, following a well-established framework. However, the initial technical rating was marginally below average due to some inconsistencies and possible typos in the mathematical notation. The authors provide a clear explanation of the model and algorithm, and the use of a Sobolev prior is a novel contribution that extends prior work.

The empirical support for the algorithm is somewhat weak, as the paper does not clearly demonstrate the empirical advantage of the Sobolev prior. Figure 2 does not provide a clear visual comparison of the different methods, and the claim that Smooth-SODL is more "structured" is not supported quantitatively. The reviewer requests explicit, neuroscience-justified criteria and quantitative measurements showing that Smooth-SODL atoms are more structured and meaningful.

The rebuttal provided additional results that were informative and raised the technical quality rating. However, the reviewer requests that these results be included in the revision. The novelty of the paper lies in the extension of prior work by incorporating Sobolev priors for spatial structure, which could be a useful tool for neuroscience and medical imaging.

The presentation quality of the manuscript is somewhat lacking, with confusing descriptions, typos, and editing issues that impede understanding. The reviewer requests that the authors address these issues in the revision. Additionally, the reviewer requests explicit mathematical and algorithmic solutions to certain equations, as well as clarification on some notation and variable references.

The potential impact of the method is significant, as it could be applied to various tasks in neuroscience and medical imaging, such as image segmentation and regional sensing. However, the reviewer requests that the authors provide more explicit comparisons to other methods and more quantitative evaluations of the algorithm's performance.

In terms of the strengths and weaknesses of the paper, the main strengths are:

* Novel extension of prior work by incorporating Sobolev priors for spatial structure
* Potential impact on neuroscience and medical imaging
* Clear explanation of the model and algorithm

The main weaknesses are:

* Empirical support is somewhat weak
* Presentation quality is lacking
* Some inconsistencies and possible typos in mathematical notation
* Missing references and unclear variable references

To address these weaknesses, the authors should:

* Provide more explicit comparisons to other methods
* Include more quantitative evaluations of the algorithm's performance
* Address presentation quality issues, including confusing descriptions, typos, and editing issues
* Clarify notation and variable references
* Include missing references

Overall, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of neuroscience and medical imaging, but requires some revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above.
