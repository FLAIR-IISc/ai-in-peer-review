- Technical approach is sound with a well-formulated optimization and convergence guarantees.  
- Algorithmic details and implementation (online framework, FISTA) are clearly described and reproducible.  
- Main claims lack rigorous evidence; results rely mostly on subjective visual comparisons of brain “blobs.”  
- Visual results (Fig. 2a) are not labeled or compared to standard brain atlases, making neuroanatomical interpretation unclear.  
- Validation is inadequate: inter-subject variability is claimed but not quantitatively assessed or consolidated across subjects.  
- Reported metrics (explained variance, behavioral prediction) do not convincingly show added value of spatial regularization.  
- CanICA attains higher explained variance than Smooth-SODL, questioning the benefit of the proposed method.  
- Originality is limited: Laplacian/Sobolev penalty is standard and relation to prior structured dictionary work (Varoquaux et al. 2012) is not clearly distinguished.  
- Claim of non-trivial extension over SODL is somewhat overstated.  
- Significance is weak: performance gap vs SODL shrinks with more data, and HCP-scale data may not need spatial smoothing.  
- Paper does not convincingly demonstrate a real problem in brain imaging that the method uniquely solves.  
- Strengths: solid theoretical formulation, scalable handling of large datasets, clear algorithms, and reasonable computational efficiency.  
- Weaknesses: subjective results, inadequate interpretability/clinical validation, insufficient comparison to related structured methods, and overstated significance.  
- Recommendation: reject — need quantitative spatial-coherence metrics, atlas comparisons, clearer differentiation from prior work, and stronger evidence that spatial regularization improves interpretability or captures inter-subject variability.
