This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for extracting decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The approach combines a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to impose piece-wise smooth and compact atoms, with an L1-norm constraint to achieve sparsity. The main originality of this work is the combination of these two penalties within the dictionary-learning framework, which extends the online dictionary-learning work of Mairal et al. (2010) with only a minor increase in computational cost.

The authors provide a clear and concise summary of the background and motivation for their work, including the limitations of existing approaches for defining regions of interest (ROIs) in neuroimaging. They also provide a detailed description of their proposed method, including the optimization problem and the algorithm used to solve it. The experiments are conducted on a large public dataset (HCP) and compare the proposed method (Smooth-SODL) with competing state-of-the-art methods (SODL and CanICA).

The strengths of this paper include the originality of the proposed method, which combines two penalties to achieve structured and sparse components. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their method, including qualitative and quantitative metrics, such as visual quality of the dictionaries, explained variance, stability of the dictionary atoms, and prediction of behavioral variables. The results show that the proposed method extracts structured and denoised dictionaries that are more interpretable and better capture inter-subject variability in small, medium, and large-scale regimes.

However, there are some weaknesses and limitations to this paper. The experimental improvements over simpler methods are minor or unclear, and the authors do not provide sufficient explanation or analysis for the observed (lack of) performance gains. Additionally, the manuscript seems rushed and contains draft comments, which makes reading tedious and sometimes confusing.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, the authors could provide more details on the optimization problem and the algorithm used to solve it, as well as more analysis on the results.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized, but the writing could be improved. The authors could provide more explanations and examples to help non-experts understand the proposed method and its significance.

In terms of originality, the paper is original in its combination of Sobolev and L1 regularization within dictionary learning. However, the authors could provide more discussion on how their method differs from previous contributions and how it advances the state of the art in the field.

In terms of significance, the paper is significant in its potential to extract structured and denoised dictionaries that are more interpretable and better capture inter-subject variability in neuroimaging data. However, the authors could provide more discussion on the potential impact of their method on the field and how it could be used in practice.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and original method for extracting structured and sparse components from brain images.
* The method is evaluated thoroughly, including qualitative and quantitative metrics.
* The results show that the proposed method extracts structured and denoised dictionaries that are more interpretable and better capture inter-subject variability.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The experimental improvements over simpler methods are minor or unclear.
* The manuscript seems rushed and contains draft comments, which makes reading tedious and sometimes confusing.
* The authors do not provide sufficient explanation or analysis for the observed (lack of) performance gains.

Recommendation:

* Accept with minor revisions to address the weaknesses and limitations mentioned above.
* Request the authors to provide more details on the optimization problem and the algorithm used to solve it.
* Request the authors to provide more analysis on the results and to clarify the experimental improvements over simpler methods.
* Request the authors to revise the manuscript to make it more readable and free of draft comments.
