This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method, dubbed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The method combines the online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The authors claim that this approach extracts structured and denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability in small, medium, and large-scale regimes.

The paper is well-motivated by the need to define regions of interest (ROIs) in a principled way for neuro-imaging analysis. The authors provide a clear overview of the challenges in defining ROIs and how their proposed method addresses these challenges. However, the novelty of the method is somewhat weak, as it is essentially an extension of the online dictionary-learning framework with added Sobolev regularization.

The motivation for using Sobolev regularization is not entirely clear. The paper does not provide a clear definition of "structure" or justify why dictionary sparsity improves the problem. The authors claim that the Sobolev penalty imposes "blobs" on the dictionary atoms, but it is unclear what this means in practice.

The paper provides a clear and well-organized presentation of the proposed method, including the optimization problem, the algorithm, and the convergence guarantees. However, there are some minor errors and typos throughout the paper. For example, the notation in Eq. (1) should likely read [V^1, V^2, …, V^r]^T. Additionally, the reference to "line 146" is unclear and needs clarification.

The analysis of the method is somewhat limited. The authors provide some justification for the choice of the l2 norm on the codes instead of the l1 norm used in Mairal et al. (2010), but this could be further elaborated. The paper could also benefit from a more detailed analysis of the effects of the Sobolev penalty on the dictionary atoms.

The experimental results are well-presented and provide a clear comparison of the proposed method with state-of-the-art methods. However, the results are somewhat limited to a single dataset, and it would be beneficial to see results on other datasets to demonstrate the generalizability of the method.

Overall, the paper provides a clear and well-organized presentation of a novel method for online dictionary learning with Sobolev regularization. However, the novelty of the method is somewhat weak, and the analysis could be further elaborated. With some refinement in language and presentation, this paper has the potential to make a solid contribution to the field.
