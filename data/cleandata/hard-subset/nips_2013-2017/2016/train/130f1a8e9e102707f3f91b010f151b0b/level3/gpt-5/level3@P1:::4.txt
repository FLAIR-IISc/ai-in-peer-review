This paper proposes an online, under-complete dictionary-learning framework for population neuroimaging in which dictionary atoms are both sparse (via an L1 constraint on each atom) and spatially structured (via a Sobolev/Laplacian quadratic penalty). Codes are estimated with ridge regression; dictionary updates are performed in a block-coordinate-descent (BCD) scheme with per-atom updates solved by a few iterations of FISTA. The method extends the online sparse dictionary learning of Mairal et al. (2010) by adding a Laplacian regularizer on the atoms, aiming to produce compact, blob-like regions rather than scattered patterns. Experiments on HCP task fMRI (n=500; p≈2.6e5; k=40) compare the proposed Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA. Results claim modest gains in interpretability and stability over SODL, with small overhead (≈2–3x runtime), and better interpretability than ICA while retaining competitive explained variance and prediction performance for some behavioral variables.

Relation to prior work: Spatially structured sparsity has been explored in neuroimaging via TV-ℓ1 and GraphNet-type penalties for decoding and component extraction (e.g., Abraham et al., Grosenick et al.). Multi-subject PCA/ICA (e.g., CanICA) and online dictionary learning (Mairal et al.; Mensch et al.) are established. The main novelty here is incorporating a Laplacian penalty on atoms within an online dictionary learning loop and demonstrating that it scales with modest computational overhead. Compared to prior TV-ℓ1 atlas learning approaches that relied on slow alternating minimization, the online formulation is practical for large datasets.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant modeling goal: atom-level sparsity plus spatial smoothness to yield compact, interpretable ROIs.
- Sensible algorithmic design: ridge codes for stability; standard online sufficient statistics; per-atom proximal updates with FISTA; overall complexity close to [Mairal et al.].
- Scalability demonstrated on realistic full-brain data (p>2e5; n=500) with practical runtimes on a single CPU.
- Empirical evaluation includes stability, explained variance, qualitative interpretability, and behavioral prediction; learning-curve analysis is informative.

Weaknesses and points to address

Quality
- Empirical gains appear modest or unclear. Explained variance on test is close between SODL and Smooth-SODL; CanICA (unsurprisingly) dominates EV. Stability is reported to improve (0.4 vs. 0.1) but details of the matching protocol, split variability, and confidence intervals are missing.
- The hard-thresholded ICA baseline (tCanICA) is under-specified (threshold selection, reproducibility across thresholds), which can bias comparisons for interpretability and stability.
- Claims of “1–2 FISTA iterations sufficient for 1e-6 accuracy” are not credible for generic large-scale convex problems; please report actual inner-loop tolerances, iteration counts, and wall-clock distribution across steps.
- The proposed time-varying αt ∼ t−1/2 and γt scaling (to keep the Lipschitz constant bounded) makes the objective change over time; the convergence discussion relies on [16], which assumes a fixed objective and sufficiently accurate inner updates. A more careful statement of convergence (or empirical ablation showing stability under fixed α,γ) is needed.
- The per-atom update is convex and thus solvable to global optimality, but the text suggests “Alg. 2 is guaranteed to converge to a global optimum, at each iteration” without clarifying that this refers only to the subproblem; please avoid ambiguity.

Clarity
- The manuscript reads as rushed: multiple typos (“xperiments,” “intepretable”), duplicated words, and editorial artifacts (e.g., “th Conference …”). Eq. (2) uses “lim n→∞” oddly in an online ERM context; Eq. (5) has a formatting glitch (1/2 missing). At one point “maxUi” appears where “minUi” is intended. Statements like “will … take forever” are informal.
- Important implementation details are missing: boundary conditions for the Laplacian; how ∆ is applied without forming a p×p matrix; handling of brain masks; choice of constraint set (simplex vs ℓ1-ball) and its effect on sign constraints; stopping criteria for inner BCD/FISTA loops; batch size sensitivity.
- Figures/tables referenced (e.g., Fig. 1, Tab. 1) should include precise definitions of metrics, confidence intervals, and axis labels; ensure they are legible and self-contained.

Originality
- Combining atom-level sparsity and Laplacian smoothness within an online DL framework is a useful engineering contribution, but conceptually incremental relative to GraphNet/TV-ℓ1 spatial regularization and online DL. The paper would benefit from a sharper positioning: what is made possible here that prior TV/GraphNet atlas learning could not handle at scale, and how does Laplacian vs TV change the resulting maps (e.g., boundary sharpness)?

Significance
- Practical significance hinges on interpretability and stability at scale with modest overhead. Current results suggest some gains, but they are small; more convincing evidence would include:
  - Cross-dataset generalization (different tasks or resting-state) and multiple contrasts.
  - Quantitative interpretability metrics (e.g., spatial compactness, overlap with known ROIs) beyond visual inspection.
  - Robustness across k and hyperparameters, with CIs.
  - Stronger behavioral prediction benchmarks with all targets reported (not only those with positive R2), and consistent model capacity control across methods.

Specific suggestions
- Clarify and tighten the optimization section: state precise assumptions for convergence with time-varying α,γ and inexact inner solvers; report actual inner-loop accuracy; discuss trade-offs of ridge vs ℓ1 codes.
- Provide ablations: fixed vs adaptive α, effect of γ on EV, stability, and interpretability; simplex vs ℓ1-ball constraints (sign constraints matter in fMRI).
- Justify Laplacian vs TV: show edge preservation/compactness differences; consider a lightweight TV-regularized baseline (e.g., FAASTA) on a subset to contextualize trade-offs.
- Document thresholding protocols for tCanICA; report sensitivity analyses.
- Improve writing and fix numerous typos; remove draft artifacts.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important use case (data-driven ROIs) with a scalable method.
- Simple, well-motivated extension of a widely used online DL algorithm.
- Some evidence of improved stability/interpretability at modest computational cost.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty; limited and sometimes unclear empirical gains.
- Convergence claims not fully justified under changing hyperparameters and approximate updates.
- Clarity issues and missing experimental details hinder reproducibility and assessment.

Overall: interesting and potentially useful practical extension, but the paper would benefit from clearer exposition, stronger and more comprehensive empirical validation, and a more rigorous optimization discussion to justify convergence and parameter schedules.
