This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, a large-scale online dictionary learning framework designed to identify spatially structured and sparse brain regions from neuroimaging data. The core idea is to extend the well-established online dictionary learning algorithm of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages spatial smoothness, resulting in component maps that are more interpretable as coherent regions rather than scattered voxels. The authors position their work as a computationally efficient alternative to slower structured decomposition methods, aiming to make structured dictionary learning practical for massive datasets like the Human Connectome Project.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance & Originality:** The paper addresses a highly relevant problem in neuroimaging: the scalable, data-driven discovery of functional brain regions. The proposed method of integrating a Laplacian penalty into a fast online learning framework is a novel and pragmatic contribution. It effectively bridges the gap between scalable but unstructured methods (like standard SODL) and structured but computationally expensive batch methods.
*   **Quality (Empirical):** The experimental evaluation is a clear strength. The authors compare their method against appropriate baselines (SODL, CanICA) on a large, public dataset. The results convincingly demonstrate that Smooth-SODL produces more spatially coherent and interpretable maps than its counterparts, with a modest increase in runtime. The analysis showing its superior performance in low-data regimes is particularly compelling.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Quality (Theoretical):** The primary weakness is the lack of theoretical analysis. While the paper claims convergence by leaning on the framework of Mairal et al. (2010), it does not provide any formal guarantees or analysis of how the introduction of the Laplacian penalty affects convergence rates or the properties of the learned solution. A more rigorous treatment would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript quality is poor and presents a significant barrier to acceptance. The paper is marred by careless writing and contains leftover internal comments (e.g., "XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v."). This suggests a lack of thorough proofreading and undermines the professionalism and credibility of the research. Such errors make it difficult for the reader to trust the technical details and results presented.

In summary, while the paper introduces an interesting and potentially valuable method with promising empirical results, its acceptance is hindered by major issues in manuscript preparation and a lack of theoretical depth. The authors are strongly encouraged to conduct a thorough revision and proofreading of their work.
