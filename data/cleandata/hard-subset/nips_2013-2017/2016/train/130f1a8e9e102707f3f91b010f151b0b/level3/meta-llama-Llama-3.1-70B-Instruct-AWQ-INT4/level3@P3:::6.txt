This paper proposes a novel multivariate online dictionary-learning method, dubbed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The method is an extension of the standard online dictionary-learning framework, with the addition of a Laplacian penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on a large public dataset, showing improvements over traditional `1-regularized dictionary learning.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem and the proposed solution. The introduction provides a good overview of the challenges in neuro-imaging and the limitations of existing methods. The technical sections are well-organized, and the authors provide a detailed description of the proposed algorithm and its components.

The strengths of the paper include the novelty of the proposed approach, the thorough experimental evaluation, and the clear presentation of the results. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method in extracting structured and denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability in small, medium, and large-scale regimes alike. The paper also provides a good discussion of the related work and the advantages of the proposed approach over existing methods.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. The algorithm for obtaining a solution is provided, but the existence and uniqueness of a solution are not proven. The problem is non-convex, and the algorithm lacks theoretical guarantees. The evaluation relies primarily on empirical evidence rather than theoretical analysis. Additionally, the manuscript clarity needs improvement, particularly in the technical sections.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper scores well on originality, as it proposes a novel approach to dictionary learning. The paper also scores well on significance, as it addresses a difficult problem in neuro-imaging and provides a unique solution. However, the paper scores lower on quality, due to the lack of theoretical guarantees and the reliance on empirical evidence. The paper scores well on clarity, but there is room for improvement, particularly in the technical sections.

Overall, I would recommend accepting the paper, but with some revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above. Specifically, the authors should provide more theoretical analysis and guarantees for the algorithm, and improve the clarity of the technical sections.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to dictionary learning that addresses a difficult problem in neuro-imaging.
