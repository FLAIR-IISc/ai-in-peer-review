- Proposes an online under-complete dictionary learning for fMRI with ℓ1 atom sparsity and a Sobolev (Laplacian) smoothness penalty to yield blob-like, interpretable components.  
- Adapts Mairal et al.’s online sparse coding with block-coordinate updates solved via a few FISTA steps and shows scalability on HCP task fMRI versus CanICA and standard online DL.  
- Novelty is moderate: combining Laplacian smoothness with ℓ1-constrained atoms in an online DL scheme is interesting but conceptually close to prior TV–ℓ1, GraphNet, and structured sparse PCA work.  
- Optimization approach is reasonable and grounded, but several claims lack sufficient empirical support.  
- Reported gains over simpler baselines are modest and sometimes CanICA outperforms on EV and prediction.  
- Missing systematic analyses: no clear ablation (ℓ1-only vs Laplacian-only vs combined) or sensitivity study for hyperparameters γ and τ.  
- Convergence claims (e.g., “1–2 FISTA iterations to 1e−6”) need quantitative evidence, tolerances, and runtime breakdowns, especially as γ varies.  
- Comparisons omit stronger spatial baselines such as TV–ℓ1 DL, GraphNet, and NMF with smoothness priors.  
- Experimental details are insufficiently reported: train/test splits, stability metric definitions, and statistical significance are unclear or missing.  
- Manuscript clarity is poor with typos and draft artifacts, making the paper tedious to follow.  
- Interpretability and stability claims are largely qualitative and not convincingly demonstrated by the current empirical evidence.  
- Potential significance: scalable and relevant for large neuroimaging datasets with a plausible path to more interpretable decompositions.  
- Pros for acceptance: scalable formulation, relevance to large datasets, and promising hints of improved component interpretability and stability.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited quantitative gains, lack of ablations and stronger baselines, unclear convergence/runtime analysis, and presentation issues.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject; revise with thorough ablations, stronger spatial baselines, rigorous stability/EV/prediction analyses with statistics, convergence/runtime breakdowns, and careful editing.
