This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The method, dubbed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), extends the standard online dictionary-learning framework with a Laplacian penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a large public dataset from the Human Connectome Project.

The main idea of the paper is to combine sparsity and spatial smoothness penalties to obtain decompositions that are closer to the known functional organization of the brain. The authors achieve this by imposing a boundedness constraint on the `1-like norms of the dictionary atoms to achieve sparsity and simultaneously imposing smoothness by penalizing their Laplacian. The proposed model is solved using a Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) scheme, which is adapted from the online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010).

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their proposed method and its relationship to previous work. The experimental results are also well-presented, with a thorough evaluation of the proposed method against competing state-of-the-art methods like SODL and CanICA. The authors demonstrate that their proposed method extracts structured and denoised dictionaries that are more interpretable and better capture inter-subject variability in small, medium, and large-scale regimes alike.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. Firstly, the impact of the proposed algorithm is not entirely clear, and it is hard to judge the improvement of the proposed algorithm from the paper. The main results rely on visual comparison of identified brain "blobs," which are not clear to an untrained eye. Additionally, the data quality and choice matter greatly, and adding smoothness/spatial-consistency penalties unsurprisingly yields smoother blobs. With large datasets like the Human Connectome Project, smoothing may not address a real data scarcity problem.

Furthermore, the reviewer expected consolidation of brain-region differences across subjects and use of inter-subject consistency for validation. However, the presented validation is subjective and does not clearly demonstrate the benefit of the proposed method. The authors could have strengthened their validation by providing more quantitative measures of the performance of their method.

In terms of the quality of the paper, the authors have made a good effort to provide a clear and concise explanation of their proposed method. The paper is well-organized, and the authors have provided sufficient background information to make the paper accessible to a wide range of readers. The experimental results are also well-presented, with a thorough evaluation of the proposed method against competing state-of-the-art methods.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. Firstly, the authors could have provided more detail on the implementation of their proposed method, including the choice of hyperparameters and the computational complexity of the algorithm. Additionally, the authors could have provided more discussion on the limitations of their proposed method and potential avenues for future research.

In conclusion, the paper proposes a novel method for dictionary learning with structured and sparse components. While the paper is well-written, and the experimental results are well-presented, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors could have strengthened their validation by providing more quantitative measures of the performance of their method, and they could have provided more detail on the implementation of their proposed method.
