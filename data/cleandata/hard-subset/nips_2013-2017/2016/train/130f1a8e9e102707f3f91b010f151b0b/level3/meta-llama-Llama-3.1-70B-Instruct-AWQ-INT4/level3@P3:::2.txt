This paper proposes an online structured dictionary learning algorithm with a Sobolev prior to encourage spatially contiguous dictionary atoms for neuroimaging data. The main contribution of the paper is extending prior work by incorporating Sobolev priors for spatial structure, which is a novel and original contribution. The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem they are trying to solve and the proposed solution.

The model definition and algorithms follow a well-established framework and appear solid. However, the initial technical rating was marginally below average due to some issues with the presentation and notation. The authors use a combination of `1 and Laplacian penalties to impose sparsity and smoothness on the dictionary atoms. The Laplacian penalty is used to encourage spatial structure, which is a key contribution of the paper.

The paper provides some empirical results on a large public dataset, which show that the proposed method extracts structured and denoised dictionaries that are more interpretable and better capture inter-subject variability. However, the empirical support is weak, and the paper does not clearly demonstrate the empirical advantage of the Sobolev prior. For example, Figure 2 does not quantify or clearly show in what way the proposed method outperforms alternatives.

The reviewer requests explicit, neuroscience-justified criteria and quantitative measurements showing that the proposed method's atoms are more structured and meaningful. The paper could benefit from more detailed analysis and evaluation of the proposed method, including comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods.

The rebuttal provided additional results that were informative and raised the technical quality rating. The reviewer suggests including these results in the revision.

The paper has some issues with presentation quality, including confusing descriptions, typos, and editing issues that impede understanding. The reviewer suggests revising the paper to improve clarity and readability.

The paper also has some specific mathematical and notation issues, including inconsistencies and possible typos in matrix decomposition transpose placement, signs and terms in Equations (6) and (8), and unclear variable references in updates. The reviewer suggests revising the paper to address these issues.

The paper is missing a reference to the HCP dataset, which is a key component of the empirical evaluation.

The reviewer requests an explicit mathematical and algorithmic solution to Equation (6), including a derivation in the appendix.

The paper has some issues with Figure 2, including different slices shown for different methods that hinder direct comparison, and a claim that the proposed method is more "structured" that is not supported visually or quantitatively.

The paper also has some issues with Figure 3, including unclear what is being predicted, whether regression uses U_i, legends and caption that do not explain baselines (PCA is missing), and claims about R^2 > 0.1 that are ambiguous as to which curves support this.

The paper has some minor typos and stray comments that need fixing.

Overall, the paper has some potential, but it needs significant revisions to address the issues mentioned above. The reviewer suggests revising the paper to improve clarity, readability, and technical quality.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and original contribution by incorporating Sobolev priors for spatial structure.
* The paper provides some empirical results on a large public dataset that show the proposed method extracts structured and denoised dictionaries.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The empirical support is weak, and the paper does not clearly demonstrate the empirical advantage of the Sobolev prior.

* The paper is missing a reference to the HCP dataset.
