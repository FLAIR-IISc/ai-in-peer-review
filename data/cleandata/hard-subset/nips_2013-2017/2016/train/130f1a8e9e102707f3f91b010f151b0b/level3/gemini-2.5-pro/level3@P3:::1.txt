This paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework, named Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), for identifying structured brain regions from neuroimaging data. The method extends the well-known online dictionary learning algorithm of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This addition encourages the learned components to be spatially smooth and blob-like, which is a desirable property for neuroscientific interpretability. This is combined with a standard l1-norm constraint to ensure sparsity. The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to massive datasets, which is a key advantage over previous methods for structured sparse decomposition that rely on slower batch optimization. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project, comparing it to standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Independent Component Analysis (ICA).

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The problem of learning meaningful, data-driven brain atlases from large population cohorts is highly significant in neuroimaging. This work presents a novel and practical solution by successfully integrating a spatial structure prior into a scalable online learning framework. It bridges a gap between fast but unstructured methods like SODL and more principled but computationally expensive structured decomposition techniques.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is a clear strength. The method is tested on a large and relevant real-world dataset (HCP). The comparisons to strong baselines (SODL, CanICA) are well-chosen. The paper uses a good range of metrics, including visual quality, explained variance, stability, and predictive power for behavioral scores, to demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach. The learning curve analysis in Table 1 compellingly shows the benefit of the spatial regularizer, particularly in the small-to-medium data regime.
*   **Potential Impact:** The proposed Smooth-SODL method is a valuable contribution with the potential for high impact. Given its scalability and the improved interpretability of its outputs, it could become a widely used tool for exploratory data analysis and feature extraction in large-scale neuroimaging studies. The promise of inclusion in the Nilearn library further increases its potential for adoption by the community.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Theoretical Analysis (Quality):** The primary weakness of this paper is the lack of theoretical analysis regarding the proposed extension. The authors state that convergence to a local optimum is guaranteed because the hypotheses of Mairal et al. (2010) are satisfied. While this may be true, the paper would be significantly stronger with a more detailed analysis. For instance, how does the addition of the Laplacian penalty and the corresponding non-trivial dictionary update step affect the convergence rate? The paper notes that a large `γ` can slow down the updates, but a formal characterization of this trade-off is missing. A more rigorous treatment of the properties of the learned dictionary under this new regularizer would provide deeper insight and increase confidence in the method.
*   **Poor Manuscript Quality (Clarity):** The paper is in need of significant proofreading and editing. The clarity is hampered by numerous typographical errors, awkward phrasing, and at least one leftover author comment ("146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v."). Such mistakes give the impression that the work was rushed and reduce the reader's confidence in its technical correctness. While the overall structure is logical, the presentation of the algorithm and the derivation of the update steps could be clearer. For example, the description of the BCD dictionary update in Eq. (7) is dense and relies heavily on the reader referring to the original work by Mairal et al. This lack of polish is substantially below the expected standard for a top-tier conference like NIPS.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

*   The leftover comment on line 146 is a serious oversight and should be removed. The manuscript should be thoroughly proofread for similar errors, typos, and grammatical mistakes.
*   In the BCD dictionary update (Section 3.1), you state that 1 or 2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient for the subproblem. Could you provide a more quantitative justification for this? For instance, what is the typical relative improvement after the first few iterations?
*   The discussion on setting `γ` in equation (9) is important. Can you elaborate on the practical implications? Does this adaptive choice for `γ` mean that the user does not need to tune it, or is this an upper bound that guides the manual selection of a fixed `γ`?
*   While the empirical study on hyperparameter tuning is helpful, the procedure remains complex. Could the authors provide more concrete guidance or perhaps an automated tuning procedure for practitioners who wish to use the method?
*   The paper's claim that it "non-trivially extends the online dictionary-learning work of Mairal et al." is accurate, but the lack of accompanying theoretical extension is a missed opportunity to make a more foundational contribution.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and potentially very useful method for a significant problem in computational neuroscience. The empirical results are promising and demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches. However, the submission is severely let down by its poor presentation and a lack of theoretical depth. The manuscript requires a major revision to improve its clarity and polish before it can be considered a strong scientific contribution.
