Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning approach where dictionary atoms are encouraged to be both sparse and spatially “structured.” Structure is enforced by a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty on each atom, while sparsity is obtained via an ℓ1-type constraint set (ℓ1-ball or simplex). Codes are estimated via ridge (ℓ2) regression rather than ℓ1 sparse coding; dictionary updates are carried out by block coordinate descent, solving a strongly convex quadratic subproblem with a projected first-order method (FISTA). The method is positioned as a scalable extension of online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., JMLR 2010) to incorporate spatial smoothness. Experiments on HCP fMRI Z-maps compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA, reporting improved interpretability and stability, with a 2–3× runtime overhead compared to SODL and faster than CanICA. The paper also discusses hyper-parameter effects and presents behavioral prediction results.

Relation to prior work
The technical backbone (online dictionary learning with block coordinate descent and sufficient statistics) follows Mairal et al. (2010). Adding spatial regularization on atoms is closely related to GraphNet/structured sparse PCA and TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning used in neuroimaging (e.g., Abraham et al., MICCAI 2012; Grosenick et al., NeuroImage 2013; Jenatton et al., AISTATS 2010; Varoquaux et al., IPMI 2011). The paper frames the dictionary-update subproblem as a denoising GraphNet instance.

Strengths
- Practical relevance: large-scale, multivariate decomposition to define interpretable, localized regions from population neuroimaging data.
- Scalability: reuses efficient online machinery; modest additional cost from Laplacian updates.
- Empirical utility: improved visual coherence of atoms and stability over SODL; competitive predictive performance versus baselines.
- Reproducibility intent: implementation announced in Nilearn; use of HCP data.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Novelty and motivation
- Novelty is limited. The method is essentially Mairal (2010) online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty on dictionary columns. This is a well-known form of structured sparsity/GraphNet; the main technical step (projected gradient on a strongly convex quadratic with Laplacian) is straightforward. For NIPS, a stronger methodological advance or theoretical analysis (e.g., convergence under time-varying regularizers, statistical guarantees with Sobolev priors) would be expected.
- Motivation for the Sobolev regularization is underdeveloped. The paper states that “structured” means “piece-wise smooth and compact blobs,” but does not formalize structure (e.g., in terms of level-set topology, decay, or smoothness class). Why Laplacian rather than TV (which more directly promotes piecewise-constant “blobs”), Laplacian+ℓ2 (GraphNet) vs. TV-ℓ1, or learned graph priors? A principled argument (bias profile, edge preservation, robustness to noise) and empirical ablations comparing Laplacian to TV-ℓ1/GraphNet baselines are needed.

Formulation and notation
- Eq. (1) and surrounding text have dimensional inconsistencies. Xi are described as p-dimensional row vectors, while VUi is p×1, so Xi ≈ VUi mismatches. Please fix by using column vectors Xi ∈ Rp and Xi ≈ VUi, or keep Xi as row and write Xi ≈ U⊤i V⊤. Also standardize the atom notation: V = [v1|…|vk] with vj ∈ Rp. If you use superscripts Vj, avoid mixing with transposes. The suggestion to write [V^1, …, V^r]^T reflects the need to clarify stacking.
- Possible typo in Sec. 2 bullets: you write “Each of the terms maxUi …” but Eq. (2) has min; should be “min.”
- Eq. (2): the choice of ℓ2 (ridge) on codes instead of ℓ1 (as in Mairal 2010) needs justification. You briefly claim ridge yields “stable codes,” but there is no analysis of when dense vs sparse codes are appropriate in this application. Please (i) motivate the modeling assumption (are subjects expected to engage many components simultaneously?), (ii) provide an empirical comparison to ℓ1-coded SODL with the same Laplacian atom prior, including stability, EV, and prediction metrics, and (iii) discuss how ℓ2 coding interacts with simplex/ℓ1 constraints on atoms (identifiability, bias).
- The γ schedule in Eq. (9) is ad hoc and not derived from a convergence/stability analysis. If γ must scale with At[j,j]/t to avoid tiny steps, provide a formal argument or adopt a standard diminishing-step approach with guarantees. Also, the claim that “1–2 FISTA iterations suffice to reach 1e-6 accuracy” is implausible for general instances; please report stopping criteria and actual optimality gaps.
- Constraint set: using the simplex Sp(τ) enforces nonnegativity on atoms. Many statistical parametric maps contain positive and negative effects; justify this modeling choice and discuss whether it biases the learned components.

Experiments and fairness
- Baselines: The most relevant structured baseline, TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning (e.g., Abraham et al., MICCAI 2012) or GraphNet-regularized atoms, is not included. Without these, it is hard to attribute gains specifically to the Sobolev prior versus generic smoothing.
- Preprocessing parity: CanICA is run on smoothed data while SODL/Smooth-SODL are not. This asymmetry can affect both interpretability and explained variance; please either smooth all inputs or report both settings.
- Clarify the data split protocol, the “line 146” reference, and the procedure for matching atoms (Hungarian matching parameters, stability metric definition). Report statistical significance for key metrics.
- Report boundary conditions and discretization for ∆, and whether FFT-based updates were used.

Quality
- Algorithmic correctness appears plausible, but the paper contains several notation errors and ambiguous claims (global optimality of subproblems vs. using a single FISTA iteration). The evaluation is suggestive but lacks critical baselines and ablations.

Clarity
- The manuscript needs substantial language editing: numerous typos (“intepretable,” “there is not step size,” “comput”) and unclear references (e.g., “line 146”). Equations and dimensions should be carefully checked and standardized.

Originality
- Incremental. The key idea—adding a Laplacian penalty to online dictionary learning—is a modest extension of established methods.

Significance
- For neuroimaging practice, a fast, online, structured dictionary learner is useful. For NIPS, without deeper theory or broader methodological novelty, the contribution feels more appropriate for an application-focused venue.

Pros
- Addresses a real need (scalable, interpretable brain decompositions).
- Runs fast; code planned for release.
- Improved interpretability/stability vs. SODL; competitive prediction results.

Cons
- Limited novelty; insufficient justification for modeling choices (Sobolev vs alternatives; ℓ2 codes).
- Notational and clarity issues; potential errors in equations.
- Missing key structured baselines and fairness concerns in experiments.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. With clearer motivation and definitions of “structure,” corrected and consistent notation, stronger baselines (TV-ℓ1/GraphNet), a principled analysis of the ℓ2-vs-ℓ1 coding choice, and improved writing, this work could be impactful for neuroimaging, but it currently falls short of NIPS standards.
