

This paper proposes a novel extension of online dictionary learning for brain imaging data, introducing a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on the dictionary atoms while maintaining sparsity. The authors frame their work as addressing the problem of defining meaningful brain regions (ROIs) from population-level brain imaging data, rather than relying on anatomical segmentation or ad hoc region definition. Their approach, Smooth-SODL, builds upon Mairal et al.'s online dictionary learning framework (NIPS 2010) by adding a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty to obtain spatially structured, sparse atoms that better capture functional brain organization. This extends prior work on structured dictionary learning for brain imaging (e.g., [1, 25]), which typically used slow alternating optimization approaches.

The paper's primary strengths lie in its clear motivation for the problem (inter-subject variability in brain imaging), the practical significance of the contribution (addressing a real challenge in neuroimaging), and strong empirical results. The authors demonstrate that their method extracts more interpretable brain regions compared to state-of-the-art approaches (SODL [16] and CanICA [22]) across multiple evaluation metrics: visual interpretability, explained variance, reproducibility, and predictive performance on behavioral variables. The key experimental finding is that Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL in small-data regimes (31% vs 2% explained variance with 17 subjects), showing that spatial regularization is particularly valuable when data is limited. The algorithmic extension is efficient, with only a 2-3x increase in runtime over standard SODL.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses in theoretical rigor. The problem formulation is non-convex, and while the authors provide an algorithmic solution, they do not establish existence or uniqueness of the solution, nor do they provide theoretical guarantees for convergence or optimality. The paper claims convergence to a local optimum but does not prove it or discuss the conditions under which this holds. The theoretical analysis is limited to empirical observations about the Lipschitz constant of the subproblems. The reliance on empirical validation without theoretical grounding is particularly problematic for a machine learning paper at NIPS, where theoretical contributions are highly valued.

Regarding clarity, the manuscript has some issues that impede understanding. The key mathematical formulation (equation 2) is presented without sufficient intuition about the trade-offs between the sparsity and smoothness terms. The authors' discussion of the constraint set C (line 146) is particularly confusing, as it conflates different regularization approaches without clear distinction. However, the authors' rebuttal adequately addresses these clarity concerns, and the manuscript would be significantly improved with the proposed revisions.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a non-trivial extension of online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial structure. While structured dictionary learning has been explored before (e.g., [25, 1]), the authors' approach to integrating it with online learning is novel and addresses a key bottleneck in scaling such methods to large neuroimaging datasets. The Laplacian penalty is a natural choice for spatial smoothness, but the authors' adaptation of it to the online learning framework is the key contribution.

The significance of this work is high for the neuroimaging community. The ability to automatically extract interpretable brain regions from population data is a major practical contribution, with potential applications in clinical neuroimaging, brain mapping, and cognitive neuroscience. The method's scalability to large datasets (HCP with 500 subjects) demonstrates its practical utility, and the authors' implementation in Nilearn will facilitate adoption.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
1. Solves a significant practical problem in neuroimaging with a well-motivated approach
2. Strong empirical results across multiple evaluation metrics
3. Efficient algorithm with only minor computational overhead
4. Clear demonstration of the value of spatial regularization, especially with limited data
5. Will be made publicly available in Nilearn, ensuring reproducibility and community impact

Con-acceptance arguments:
1. Lacks theoretical guarantees for the non-convex optimization problem
2. Insufficient discussion of how the Laplacian penalty relates to existing spatial regularization techniques
3. Some clarity issues in the manuscript that require revision (addressed in rebuttal)
4. Limited discussion of how the method might generalize to other imaging modalities beyond fMRI

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the application of machine learning in neuroimaging. While the theoretical aspects are weak, the strong empirical results, practical significance, and clear path to real-world impact outweigh this limitation. The authors have addressed the key clarity concerns raised in the initial review, making the paper substantially improved. The method is both novel and useful, with the potential to change how brain regions are defined in large-scale neuroimaging studies. Given the high practical significance and strong empirical validation, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the theoretical aspects.
