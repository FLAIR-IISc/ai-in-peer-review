

# Review for "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes an extension to online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) for brain imaging applications, introducing a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial structure on dictionary atoms. The authors frame their work as a method to extract structured brain regions from fMRI data by combining sparsity constraints with spatial smoothness, addressing the challenge of defining brain regions in a principled way. The method extends Mairal's online dictionary learning framework to incorporate spatial structure while maintaining scalability to large datasets. The paper compares their approach (Smooth-SODL) against SODL (Mairal et al., 2010) and CanICA (a standard ICA-based method) on HCP fMRI data, claiming improved interpretability and better capture of inter-subject variability.

## Quality
The technical approach is a straightforward extension of existing online dictionary learning with an additional spatial regularization term. While the algorithmic implementation appears sound and the convergence guarantees are established, the theoretical foundation for the proposed regularization is weak. The paper does not sufficiently justify why the Laplacian penalty is appropriate for the specific problem of brain region extraction, nor does it provide theoretical analysis of how this regularization improves the learning process. The use of an l2 penalty on codes (rather than the l1 used in Mairal et al.) lacks justification and appears arbitrary. The empirical evaluation shows marginal improvements in interpretability but does not convincingly demonstrate significant improvements in predictive performance or other critical metrics. The running time increase (factor of 3) for the proposed method compared to SODL is substantial for a method that claims to be "non-trivially" extending the existing framework.

## Clarity
The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The mathematical notation contains errors (e.g., possible issue with Eq. 1, where the dictionary should likely be written as [V^1, V^2, ..., V^r]^T rather than as a matrix). The presentation contains numerous typos and grammatical errors, making it difficult to follow at times. The explanation of why the Laplacian penalty is appropriate for brain imaging is vague and lacks concrete justification. The description of the algorithm (Algorithm 1) is confusing, particularly the reference to "line 146" which is unclear without context. The experimental setup and results are presented with some clarity, but the paper would benefit from better organization and more precise language.

## Originality
The core contribution is not sufficiently novel for NIPS. The method is a straightforward extension of existing online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) with the addition of a Laplacian penalty. While the authors claim this "non-trivially extends" the work, the modification is relatively simple and does not introduce new theoretical insights or algorithmic innovations. The paper fails to clearly articulate how their approach differs from prior work on structured dictionary learning in neuroimaging (e.g., the work by Abraham et al. on TV-constrained dictionary learning). The authors cite related work but do not sufficiently distinguish their contribution from existing methods that have already incorporated spatial regularization.

## Significance
The significance of the proposed method is limited. While brain imaging applications are important, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method provides a meaningful advancement over existing approaches. The improvements in interpretability are qualitative and subjective, and the paper does not provide strong evidence that the structured dictionaries lead to better scientific insights or more effective downstream analysis. The marginal improvements in explained variance (Fig. 2b) and predictive performance (Fig. 2c) do not justify the substantially increased computational cost. The paper claims to address "inter-subject variability" in a principled way, but the evidence provided does not clearly demonstrate that the structured dictionaries capture meaningful variability beyond what is already achieved by existing methods.

## Overall Assessment

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The method is well-motivated for brain imaging applications
- The implementation is technically sound and scalable to large datasets
- The empirical results provide some evidence of improved interpretability
- The code will be made available through Nilearn, increasing reproducibility

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The novelty is weak; the method is a simple extension of existing work
- The theoretical justification for the Laplacian penalty is inadequate
- The paper contains numerous clarity issues and presentation errors
- The performance gains are marginal and not accompanied by strong theoretical justification
- The running time increase (factor of 3) is substantial for the modest improvements claimed

## Recommendation
This paper falls short of NIPS standards due to insufficient novelty and weak theoretical justification. While the application to brain imaging is relevant, the methodological contribution is incremental and not sufficiently compelling for a top-tier machine learning conference. The paper would need substantial revision to address the theoretical shortcomings, clarify the presentation, and provide stronger evidence of the method's advantages over existing approaches before being suitable for publication at NIPS.

The authors should reconsider the contribution of their work, provide a more rigorous justification for their regularization approach, and demonstrate more compelling evidence of the method's superiority over existing techniques. The current version does not meet the threshold for acceptance at NIPS.
