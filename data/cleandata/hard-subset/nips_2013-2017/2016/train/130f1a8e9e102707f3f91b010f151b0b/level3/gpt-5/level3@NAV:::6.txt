This paper proposes an online matrix factorization/dictionary-learning framework for neuroimaging that couples a sparsity-inducing constraint on atoms with a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty to promote spatial smoothness and compact “blob-like” support. The algorithm alternates ridge-based code updates with FISTA-based atom updates within a block-coordinate descent loop, scaling to large n and p, and is evaluated on HCP task fMRI against SODL (Mairal et al., 2010) and CanICA. The work is situated between prior online sparse coding (e.g., Mairal JMLR 2010) and structured sparsity/GraphNet/TV-based approaches used in neuroimaging (e.g., Abraham; Grosenick; Varoquaux), offering a pragmatic route to structured atoms at near-SODL cost.

Strengths (Quality/Significance):
- Strong empirical performance and convincing qualitative gains in interpretability over SODL and CanICA on a large, relevant dataset; useful stability/EV/prediction analyses.
- Scalability with modest overhead (≈2–3× SODL); practical algorithmic choices and hyperparameter guidance.
- The smoothness+sparsity regularization is well-motivated for ROI discovery; likely to be adopted by practitioners (promised release in Nilearn).

Weaknesses (Quality/Clarity/Originality):
- The optimization problem is non-convex; the paper provides an algorithm but no proof of existence/uniqueness of solutions, no convergence-rate guarantees, and no guarantee of convergence to a critical point for the overall scheme. The heuristic schedule for γ to control the Lipschitz constant is reasonable but lacks analysis.
- Theoretical framing is limited relative to the breadth of experiments; evaluation relies primarily on empirical results.
- Incremental originality: L1 + Laplacian is known; the novelty lies mainly in making it online and efficient.
- Clarity needs work: notation is heavy, some derivations are sketched, and several typos. Line 146: the definition/use of Fγ and the role of A_t[j,j] in the update are ambiguous—please restate the subproblem with all terms defined and dimensions explicit.

Suggestions:
- Prove existence of minimizers (compact constraint + coercivity) and clarify (non-)uniqueness assumptions; at least establish convergence to stationary points under standard KL conditions for BCD with proximal steps.
- Provide a small synthetic recovery study to complement HCP results; compare with a TV-regularized baseline.
- Improve exposition of Alg. 2 and constants (Lipschitz, step sizes); fix typos.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear practical impact in neuroimaging; strong empirical superiority and interpretability.
- Scalable algorithm that fills a gap between SODL and structured decompositions.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited theoretical guarantees for a new regularizer/algorithmic blend.
- Incremental methodological novelty.

After rebuttal: The authors addressed algorithmic questions satisfactorily and clarified implementation details; I raise my clarity/presentation score from 2 to 3. Overall, I remain mildly positive given the empirical strength and practical relevance.
