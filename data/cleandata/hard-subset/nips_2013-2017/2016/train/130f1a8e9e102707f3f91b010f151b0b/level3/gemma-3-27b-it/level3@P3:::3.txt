This paper proposes extending online dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior (Laplacian penalty) to improve the identification of spatially coherent “blobs” representing brain regions from fMRI data. While the idea of incorporating spatial consistency into dictionary learning for neuroimaging is intuitively appealing, and builds upon existing work like Mairal et al. (2010) and related methods in [1, 25], the presented work’s impact remains unclear due to limitations in the evaluation and demonstration of its benefits.

The core contribution lies in adapting the online dictionary learning framework to incorporate a Laplacian penalty, aiming for smoother, more interpretable dictionary atoms. This extension is presented as computationally efficient, adding only a factor of 2-3 to the running time of the original method. However, the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a significant performance gain justifying this increase. The experiments primarily rely on visual comparison of the learned “blobs,” a subjective assessment method that is difficult to interpret for those unfamiliar with neuroimaging data and brain anatomy. While the authors acknowledge the challenge of hyperparameter selection and provide some guidance, a more rigorous and quantitative evaluation is necessary.

The paper appropriately references relevant work in dictionary learning [16, 17], spatial regularization [3, 1], and functional brain mapping [5, 22, 23]. It correctly positions its contribution within the context of existing methods like ICA/PCA and clustering, highlighting the advantages of a sparse, spatially-structured decomposition. However, the related work section could be expanded to include more recent advances in spatially regularized dictionary learning and their applications in neuroimaging.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Extension:** The integration of a Sobolev prior into online dictionary learning is a novel idea, potentially leading to more interpretable and spatially consistent representations of brain activity.
*   **Scalability:** Maintaining the online learning approach allows the method to scale to large datasets like the Human Connectome Project (HCP), addressing a key challenge in neuroimaging analysis.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The reported modest increase in computational cost (factor of 2-3) compared to the baseline online dictionary learning method is encouraging.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and provides a clear mathematical formulation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Subjective Evaluation:** The primary evaluation relies on visual inspection of the learned dictionary atoms, making it difficult to assess the actual improvement over existing methods objectively.
*   **Limited Quantitative Results:** While some quantitative results are presented (explained variance, prediction of behavioral variables), they are not sufficiently compelling to demonstrate a clear advantage of the proposed method.
*   **Data Dependence:** The authors acknowledge that data quality and choice significantly impact the results. With large, high-quality datasets like the HCP, the benefits of added smoothness may be marginal, and smoothing might not address a fundamental data scarcity issue.
*   **Lack of Inter-Subject Consolidation:** The evaluation focuses on individual decompositions rather than consolidating brain region differences *across* subjects. A stronger validation would involve analyzing the consistency of the learned regions across the cohort, potentially using inter-subject consistency as a regularization term or evaluation metric.
* **Overstated Improvements:** Claims like a "14-fold gain" in explained variance early in training should be viewed cautiously, as the absolute variance explained is low.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The proposed method introduces a novel extension to existing dictionary learning techniques with potential applications in neuroimaging.
*   The scalability of the online learning approach is a significant advantage for analyzing large-scale brain datasets.
*   The paper is clearly written and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a rigorous and objective evaluation makes it difficult to assess the true benefits of the proposed method.
*   The reliance on visual inspection of results limits the reproducibility and generalizability of the findings.
*   The potential for smoothing to mask underlying data characteristics, especially with large datasets, raises concerns about the interpretability of the results.
*   The paper does not adequately address the crucial aspect of inter-subject variability and consistency.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea, but falls short of demonstrating a significant contribution to the field. The primary weakness is the subjective evaluation and lack of compelling quantitative results. While the authors demonstrate the feasibility of incorporating a Sobolev prior into online dictionary learning, they don't convincingly show that it leads to substantial improvements in performance or interpretability.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. I recommend rejecting this paper in its current form. The authors should strengthen the evaluation by incorporating more objective metrics, performing a more thorough analysis of inter-subject variability, and providing a more convincing demonstration of the benefits of their proposed method compared to state-of-the-art techniques. A more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of different hyperparameter settings would also be beneficial.
