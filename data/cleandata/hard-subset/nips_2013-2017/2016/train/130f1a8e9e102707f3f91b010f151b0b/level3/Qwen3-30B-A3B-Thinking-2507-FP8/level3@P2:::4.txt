

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning that incorporates both L1 sparsity and Sobolev (Laplacian) smoothness regularization to extract structured functional brain regions from fMRI data. The authors extend Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework by adding a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure in dictionary atoms, aiming to better capture brain organization. The approach is motivated by the need for interpretable brain regions that can handle inter-subject variability in large-scale neuroimaging datasets.

The work builds on the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) and extends it to incorporate spatial structure, which has been previously attempted in brain imaging using slower methods like alternating minimization (e.g., [1, 25]). The paper positions itself as a computationally efficient solution that maintains the scalability of online methods while adding spatial structure to the learned dictionary atoms. It relates to related work in structured sparse learning (e.g., GraphNet [11, 13]) and previous attempts at brain region extraction through dictionary learning.

**Strengths:**
1. The proposed algorithm is a technically sound extension of existing online dictionary learning methods, with well-justified regularization terms.
2. The computational complexity analysis is reasonable, showing only a 2-3x increase over standard SODL.
3. The experimental setup is relevant, using the Human Connectome Project dataset for evaluation.
4. The authors provide a detailed analysis of hyperparameter selection, which is valuable for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Marginal improvements:** The experimental results show only minor improvements over baseline methods (SODL and CanICA). For instance, the explained variance on test data is 55% for Smooth-SODL versus 50% for SODL (Fig. 2b), which is not statistically significant given the context of neuroimaging where small gains can be meaningful but require careful interpretation.
2. **Insufficient analysis:** The paper fails to adequately explain why the proposed method provides these marginal improvements. The claim that "Smooth-SODL better captures inter-subject variability" is not sufficiently supported by the experiments, particularly since the learning curves (Table 1) show the gap between Smooth-SODL and SODL closes as more data is used.
3. **Clarity issues:** The manuscript appears rushed, containing draft comments (e.g., "line 146" reference) and confusing sections. The presentation of results in Fig. 2(c) is particularly difficult to interpret without more detailed explanation of the behavioral prediction metrics.
4. **Originality concerns:** While the combination of Sobolev and L1 regularization is novel, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from related work like Varoquaux et al. (2012) on "Multi-subject dictionary learning to segment an atlas of brain spontaneous activity" or Abraham et al. (2014) on "Extracting brain regions from rest fMRI with Total-Variation constrained dictionary learning," which also address structured dictionary learning for brain imaging.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with appropriate theoretical justification for the regularization terms. However, the experimental validation is insufficient to fully support the claims. The paper does not adequately address the relationship between the proposed method and prior work on structured dictionary learning in neuroimaging, and the claimed improvements are not convincingly demonstrated.

**Clarity:** The paper would benefit from a more polished presentation. Several sections are confusing, particularly the explanation of the Laplacian regularization implementation and the interpretation of the experimental results. The manuscript contains draft comments and seems to have been written hastily.

**Originality:** The combination of Sobolev and L1 regularization within online dictionary learning is novel, but the paper does not sufficiently establish how this differs from previous structured approaches in the field. The connection to prior work on structured dictionary learning for brain imaging (e.g., [1, 25]) is not clearly articulated.

**Significance:** The problem of extracting interpretable brain regions from fMRI data is important, but the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method advances the state of the art. The marginal improvements over existing methods (SODL and CanICA) do not provide sufficient evidence that Smooth-SODL represents a significant advance for the field.

**Overall assessment:** The paper presents a technically sound extension to online dictionary learning that incorporates spatial structure. However, the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate significant advantages over existing methods, and the manuscript suffers from clarity issues and insufficient analysis of the observed performance gains. The marginal improvements reported (e.g., 5-10% higher explained variance in some settings) do not justify the paper's claims of "better capturing inter-subject variability in small, medium, and large-scale regimes alike" without more rigorous statistical analysis and comparison to relevant baselines.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the method is technically sound and the problem is important, the paper fails to adequately demonstrate its significance or provide sufficient evidence to justify the claimed improvements over existing methods. The marginal gains in performance do not justify the novelty claim, and the manuscript requires substantial revision to address the clarity issues and strengthen the experimental validation.
