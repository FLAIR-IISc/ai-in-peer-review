- Proposal: Smooth-SODL — online dictionary learning with L1 sparsity on atoms, Laplacian/Sobolev spatial smoothness, codes via ridge regression, using projected FISTA within an online block-coordinate descent framework.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on online DL (Mairal 2010) and structured sparsity/graph-smooth priors (TV-ℓ1, GraphNet, structured PCA); novelty is integrating Laplacian prior into an online DL algorithm with convergence guarantees.  
- Technical soundness: Optimization approach is standard and appropriate; projected FISTA and convergence arguments are sensible, though practical guidance on step-size γ is lacking.  
- Empirical support: Evaluation relies heavily on visual assessment; quantitative metrics are limited to explained variance, mean atom correlation, and prediction, which do not directly measure inter-subject anatomical consistency.  
- Baselines/controls missing: Important controls absent — notably SODL on pre-smoothed inputs and an offline TV-ℓ1 baseline to isolate the effect of the Laplacian prior.  
- Statistical rigor: Hyperparameter selection is qualitative; needs nested CV, sensitivity curves for (γ, τ), and confidence intervals/statistical tests for reported stability differences.  
- Clarity issues: Paper has typos and notation inconsistencies; algorithmic section needs explicit composite objective, proximal operator definitions, Laplacian boundary conditions, normalization, stopping criteria, runtimes, and minibatch handling.  
- Metric clarity: Definitions of stability/reproducibility and the atom-matching procedure (Hungarian algorithm) should be clearly specified.  
- Figures and selection bias: Figures need clearer color scales and quantitative annotations; selecting “most interpretable atoms” introduces selection bias.  
- Originality assessment: Using a Sobolev/Laplacian prior is not novel in neuroimaging; the contribution is engineering it into an online DL framework — an incremental but useful advance if well validated.  
- Significance: Potentially high for data-driven ROI discovery, but current results do not convincingly show practical benefits beyond smoother-looking components; CanICA still outperforms in prediction and EV.  
- Early-data regime claim: Early improvement (31% vs 2% EV after 17 subjects) is interesting but its relevance for large HCP-scale datasets is unclear; needs demonstration on small-cohort scenarios.  
- Pros for acceptance: Addresses an important problem, integrates algorithm cleanly with modest overhead, provides convergence reasoning, and shows plausible qualitative spatial coherence.  
- Cons for acceptance: Validation is subjective, key baselines/ablations missing, predictive/EV improvements limited, and stability/hyperparameter analyses lack rigor.  
- Suggested experiments/improvements: Add quantitative spatial reproducibility (split-half Dice/Jaccard, atlas overlap, permutation tests), include baselines (pre-smoothed SODL, TV-ℓ1), perform ablations (L1-only, Laplacian-only), run sensitivity analyses and nested CV with CIs, evaluate on additional/smaller datasets, clarify algorithmic details, and release code.  
- Recommendation: Borderline reject — reasonable and potentially useful idea but current validation is insufficient; strengthening experiments and controls could make it a solid contribution.
