This paper proposes an online, under-complete dictionary learning framework for fMRI in which sparsity is enforced on atoms via an ℓ1 constraint while spatial smoothness/compactness is promoted via a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty. The method adapts Mairal et al.’s online sparse coding to incorporate a structured regularizer with block-coordinate updates solved via few FISTA steps, and is demonstrated on HCP task fMRI against CanICA and standard online DL. The goal is to yield more interpretable, blob-like functional components that generalize across subjects at modest extra computational cost.

Relation to prior work: The idea of spatially structured sparsity for brain maps is well explored (TV–ℓ1 dictionary learning; GraphNet; structured sparse PCA), and online matrix factorization for large-scale settings is also established. The originality here lies mainly in marrying a Laplacian smoothness prior with ℓ1-constrained atoms in an online DL scheme and showing it scales.

Quality: The optimization is reasonable and grounded in standard theory, but several claims need better support. Reported gains over simpler baselines are modest and sometimes favor CanICA on EV and prediction. The rationale for when/why Sobolev+ℓ1 helps (e.g., low-data regimes, stability) is asserted rather than systematically analyzed; ablations (ℓ1-only vs Laplacian-only vs combined) and sensitivity to γ, τ are missing or unclear. Convergence and “1–2 FISTA iterations to 1e−6” merit quantitative evidence (tolerances, wall-clock breakdown, effect of γ). Comparisons omit stronger spatial baselines (TV–ℓ1 DL, GraphNet/NMF with smoothness).

Clarity: The manuscript reads rushed, with typos and draft artifacts (e.g., line 146), making it tedious to follow. Important experimental details (train/test splits, stability metric definition, statistical significance) need clearer presentation; figures/tables referenced but not fully interpretable from text.

Originality: Moderate. The combination is interesting in an online DL context, but conceptually close to prior structured sparsity work; positioning vs TV-based priors and structured SPCA should be sharpened.

Significance: Potentially useful for interpretable neuroimaging decompositions, but current empirical evidence is not compelling enough for NeurIPS: improvements are minor/unclear, and interpretability claims are largely qualitative.

Pros for acceptance:
- Interesting, scalable formulation; relevant to large neuroimaging datasets.
- Plausible pathway to more interpretable components; stability hints are encouraging.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited quantitative gains; weak analysis of why/when it helps.
- Missing ablations and stronger baselines.
- Clarity issues and draft-level presentation.

Recommendation: Weak reject. Strengthen with thorough ablations, stronger spatial baselines, rigorous stability/EV/prediction analyses with statistics, convergence/runtime breakdowns for γ, and a carefully edited manuscript.
