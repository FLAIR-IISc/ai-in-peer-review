- Proposal: Smooth-SODL — an online dictionary-learning framework enforcing atom sparsity and spatial smoothness via a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty to produce compact, blob-like components.  
- Algorithm: Codes via ridge regression; dictionary updated online with block coordinate descent; atom updates solved as convex GraphNet-style denoising problems using FISTA.  
- Contribution: Extends Mairal et al. (2010) by adding spatial smoothness while preserving scalability to large n and p.  
- Empirical claims: Improved interpretability and stability vs SODL and CanICA, similar/better predictive performance, clear advantage in low-data regimes, runtime ≈3× SODL and faster than CanICA.  
- Relation to prior work: Well positioned among online dictionary learning and structured regularization literature; novelty is integrating Laplacian smoothness into online BCD updates.  
- Algorithmic soundness: Atom updates are convex/strongly convex and FISTA is appropriate; online BCD follows established frameworks with standard convergence to stationary points.  
- Missing theory: No new theoretical analysis on identifiability, sample complexity, or convergence rates with the Laplacian term.  
- Implausible empirical optimization claim: Statement that 1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e-6 accuracy is unsupported; stopping rules and tolerance schedules are not specified.  
- Heuristic schedules: Discussion of Lipschitz blow-up and time-varying γt is heuristic; needs principled schedule or bounds.  
- Experimental strengths: Large-scale HCP study and convincing qualitative spatial improvements; small-sample EV advantage is notable.  
- Experimental weaknesses: Potentially biased comparisons (e.g., pre-smoothing applied unevenly); CanICA often wins predictive benchmarks, which should be reconciled with interpretability claims.  
- Missing ablations: No comparisons for ridge vs ℓ1 codes, γ=0 vs γ>0 under equal sparsity, Laplacian vs TV, or effect of constraint set (ℓ1 ball vs simplex).  
- Reproducibility issues: Code promised but not provided; paper lacks exact hyperparameters, stopping criteria, boundary conditions, CV splits, seeds, Hungarian matching and stability metric definitions.  
- Clarity problems: Substantial proofreading needed — typos, inconsistent notation, unclear equations, undefined operators and constraint/projector details, and misleading claims (e.g., “parameter free” BCD).  
- Originality: Moderate — integration is practical and nontrivial but composes existing ingredients rather than introducing a new learning principle.  
- Significance: Useful for large-scale neuroimaging due to scalability, interpretability, and early-regime advantages, but impact depends on stronger empirical rigor and presentation.  
- Suggested improvements: Add ablations, ensure fair preprocessing, detail FISTA/optimization settings and complexity, remove overstatements, release code/pseudocode with hyperparameters and seeds, and clean up presentation.  
- Acceptance arguments: Scalable, practical, yields more interpretable/stable atoms, beneficial low-data behavior, and integrates with existing pipelines.  
- Rejection arguments: Lacks theoretical analysis, clarity and writing issues, limited ablations and possibly unfair comparisons, and no consistent predictive superiority.
