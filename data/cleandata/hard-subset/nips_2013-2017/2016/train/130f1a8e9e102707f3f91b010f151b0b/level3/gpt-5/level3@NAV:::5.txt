Summary. The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning method for brain images that constrains dictionary atoms to be sparse (via an l1 constraint) and spatially “structured” (via a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty), while using ridge-coded coefficients. The algorithm is a block-coordinate method patterned on Online Dictionary Learning (Mairal et al., 2010), with FISTA-based atom updates to handle the Laplacian term. Experiments on HCP task-fMRI aim to show more interpretable, smoother atoms with competitive explained variance and reasonable runtimes, compared to SODL and CanICA. This connects to prior structured sparsity for neuroimaging (e.g., TV-l1/GraphNet, Abraham; Grosenick; Varoquaux), but adapts these ideas to the online DL setting.

Strengths.
- Practical problem with potential impact for large-scale neuroimaging; scalability and online updates are appropriate.
- Clear attempt to integrate spatial structure into ODL; empirical runtime and basic ablations (γ=0) are reported.
- Preliminary results suggest improved visual interpretability and reasonable predictive performance.

Weaknesses.
Quality:
- Novelty is weak: the method is essentially ODL (Mairal 2010) with a Laplacian penalty on atoms, i.e., a GraphNet-style regularizer applied within ODL; the theoretical and algorithmic deltas are modest for NeurIPS.
- Motivation for Sobolev regularization is underdeveloped: “structure” is not defined quantitatively (e.g., smoothness, compactness), nor is it shown why Laplacian (vs TV) best induces blobs; provide measures of compactness/smoothness and a comparison to TV-l1.
- Eq. (2) requires justification: why l2 on codes instead of the l1 coding used in Mairal 2010? Analyze consequences for identifiability, sparsity of representations, and interpretability. Include an ablation with l1 codes.
- Notational/technical issues: Eq. (1) stacking and symbols are inconsistent; e.g., likely [V1,…,Vk] with consistent use of k (Algorithm 2 uses r). Please correct “[V^1, V^2, …, V^r]^T” vs column-stacked V, and remove the ambiguous reference to “line 146.” The “global optimum” language for subproblems should be qualified.
Clarity:
- The paper needs substantial language/presentation polishing; many typos (“intepretable,” “there is not step size,” etc.) and undefined terms hamper readability.
Originality:
- Prior work (TV-l1 DL; GraphNet; multi-subject DL) closely covers the main ingredients; clearer differentiation and deeper analysis are needed.
Significance:
- Potentially useful tool, but limited by weak novelty and incomplete justification; add stronger baselines (TV-l1/Elastic-Net atoms), quantitative structure metrics, and theory on convergence/scaling with γ.

Pros for acceptance:
- Scalable implementation; relevant application; promising interpretability.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty; unclear motivation for Sobolev; missing analyses; presentation issues.

Recommendation: Reject, but potentially promising after substantial revision.
