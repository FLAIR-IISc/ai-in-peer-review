Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an online structured dictionary learning method for neuroimaging in which dictionary atoms are constrained to be sparse (via an L1-type constraint set C) and spatially contiguous/smooth (via a Sobolev/Laplacian prior). The formulation extends the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. by adding a Laplacian penalty on atoms and solving the resulting block-coordinate updates with a small number of proximal gradient iterations. The approach is related to prior structured sparsity in neuroimaging (e.g., TV+L1, GraphNet) but aims to retain the scalability of online dictionary learning. Experiments on HCP task-fMRI Z-maps compare Smooth-SODL against SODL, CanICA, and (partly) PCA on explained variance, visual quality, stability, and behavioral prediction.

Strengths
- Addresses an important need: scalable, structured decompositions for large neuroimaging datasets.
- Methodologically grounded: extends a well-established online dictionary learning framework with a classical Sobolev/Laplacian prior; block-coordinate updates are standard and the Laplacian-regularized subproblem admits efficient first-order solutions.
- Clear potential utility: producing compact, blob-like atoms can aid interpretability, ROI definition, and downstream prediction; could be adapted to other imaging tasks (e.g., segmentation, regional sensing).
- Online scalability and implementation intent (Nilearn) increase potential impact.
- Rebuttal provided useful additional results; they help clarify the technical soundness and improve the rating.

Weaknesses
- Empirical support for the Sobolev prior is weak in the current draft. Figure 2 does not clearly or quantitatively demonstrate how Smooth-SODL surpasses SODL or CanICA on “structure.” The claim that atoms are more “structured” is not quantified.
- Presentation and notation issues significantly impede understanding (see detailed comments).
- Some algorithmic details (e.g., a principled derivation/solution to the Laplacian-regularized subproblem) are not fully spelled out.
- Figures hinder direct comparison (different slices across methods; unclear legends/baselines).
- Missing/incomplete references and multiple typos.

Quality
- Technical framework is solid and builds on standard BCD/online methods. However, there are inconsistencies and likely typos in equations and notation (e.g., factors of 1/2, transpose placement, and the per-atom update), and a lack of explicit derivations for key steps.
- Empirical evaluation partially supports scalability and utility but does not convincingly isolate the gain from the Sobolev prior with neuroscience-justified metrics.

Clarity
- Currently below NeurIPS standards due to confusing descriptions, typos, and notation inconsistencies. The main ideas are discernible, but the manuscript needs substantial editing to be self-contained and reproducible.

Originality
- The main novelty is the incorporation of a Sobolev/Laplacian prior into online dictionary learning for spatial structure, connecting to GraphNet/TV-like ideas in a scalable setting. This is an incremental but useful contribution.

Significance
- If clarified and supported with quantitative evidence, the method could be a practical tool for neuro/medical imaging and beyond. The impact hinges on demonstrating concrete advantages over strong baselines with principled metrics.

Requested revisions and concrete suggestions
1) Quantitative evaluation of “structure” and neuroscientific plausibility:
- Report per-atom and aggregate metrics: Laplacian energy v^TΔv, total variation, number/size of connected components, spatial autocorrelation, compactness (e.g., isoperimetric quotient), within-/between-network overlap with known atlases.
- Stability/reproducibility: split-half correspondence using Hungarian matching and mean correlations; also report adjusted Rand index on binarized maps; include confidence intervals.
- Control for sparsity/thresholds across methods when comparing structure to ensure fair comparisons (e.g., match L0/L1 norms or explained variance).

2) Figures:
- Use identical anatomical slices across methods to enable direct visual comparison; keep color scales and thresholds consistent (or provide matched-threshold and matched-sparsity panels).
- Clarify the prediction figure (currently Fig. 2c; called Fig. 3 in text): specify targets, features (are U_i the regressors?), regression model (ridge? hyperparameters? CV protocol and folds), leakage prevention, and all baselines (include PCA everywhere). Explicitly indicate which curves support statements like “R^2 > 0.1.”
- Add quantitative summaries (e.g., bar plots with error bars) of the structure metrics above.

3) Algorithmic/mathematical details:
- Provide a detailed derivation for the code update (ridge): the objective should be 1/2||X−Vu||^2 + (α/2)||u||^2 to match Eq. (6). Fix factor-of-2 inconsistencies.
- Give an explicit solution path for the atom update: derive the proximal gradient step for F_γ̃(v,a)=1/2||v−a||^2 + (γ̃/2)v^TΔv with projection onto C (simplex or L1-ball). Include step size choice via Lipschitz constant of I + γ̃Δ; justify the bound (e.g., ||Δ||2 ≤ 2D·4 with boundary conditions) and the dependence on A_t[j,j]/t. If FFT-based updates are used, provide conditions and complexity.
- Clarify the update in Eqs. (7)-(8): define Ajt, A_t[j,j], B_jt unambiguously; correct the term “V_j + A^{-1}(B_jt − V A_jt)” to the standard residual form (as in Mairal et al.) and ensure all dimensions/transposes are correct. In Alg. 2, “for j = 1 to r” should be “k.”

4) Notation and sign conventions:
- The Laplacian penalty: Ω_Lap(v)=1/2 v^TΔv with Δ=∇^T∇ is PSD; clarify sign (vs. −div∇) and remove ambiguous factors. Ensure Ω_Lap ≥ 0 holds with the chosen Δ and boundary conditions.
- Consistently distinguish vectors (columns) and rows; fix transpose placements in the decomposition Xi ≈ V U_i and in ridge updates.

5) References and baselines:
- Ensure a complete HCP reference (Van Essen et al., NeuroImage 2013) and any other missing bibliographic details. Cite related TV+L1 atlas learning work (Abraham et al.) with full details.
- Include PCA consistently as a baseline across all panels; if tCanICA is used, clearly define how thresholding is set.

6) Writing and editing:
- Fix numerous typos and grammar (“interpretable,” “we typically use we use,” “Graph-Net penalty can imposes,” duplicated words, stray bold sentence, header artifacts like “th Conference…”).
- Provide a brief algorithm box or appendix that makes the method reproducible (hyperparameters, initialization, stopping criteria, batching).

Rebuttal impact
The additional results in the rebuttal were informative and improved my technical assessment from marginally below average to borderline-positive. Please include these results (and their quantitative metrics) in the revision.

Pros
- Practical, scalable extension of online dictionary learning with spatial structure.
- Potentially useful to neuroimaging practitioners; code availability via Nilearn is a plus.
- Sound optimization framework; convergence properties inherited from prior work.

Cons
- Insufficient quantitative evidence for the added value of the Sobolev prior.
- Clarity and notation issues; some equations likely contain errors.
- Figures do not allow clean comparisons; predictive analyses underspecified.

Overall recommendation
Weak reject (encourage resubmission after revision). With stronger quantitative evidence, clearer algorithmic derivations, corrected notation, and improved presentation, this work could be impactful.
