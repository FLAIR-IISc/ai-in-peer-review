- Proposal: novel multivariate online dictionary learning for brain images combining L1 sparsity with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial smoothness.  
- Main contribution: L1 + Laplacian yields structured, sparse, and spatially coherent decompositions that better delineate brain regions.  
- Methodological extension: builds on Mairal et al. (2010) online dictionary learning with modest extra computational cost.  
- Empirical validation: demonstrated on a large HCP fMRI dataset with improved interpretability and capture of inter-subject variability.  
- Problem importance: addresses principled, data-driven ROI definition in neuroimaging beyond anatomical priors and segmentation.  
- Relation to prior work: clearly connects to clustering, PCA, ICA, and prior dictionary learning literature; related work coverage is reasonable.  
- Strength — Novelty: combining L1 sparsity and Laplacian regularization in this context appears new and practically useful.  
- Strength — Scalability: online learning framework enables handling large neuroimaging datasets.  
- Strength — Empirical results: shows better interpretability, stability, and predictive performance than SODL and CanICA; learning curves support benefit especially with limited data.  
- Strength — Practical impact: relevant tool for neuroimaging with planned Nilearn implementation release.  
- Weakness — Theory: lacks rigorous theoretical analysis, convergence, optimality, or uniqueness guarantees for the nonconvex problem.  
- Weakness — Algorithm guarantees: impact of Laplacian penalty on convergence is not analyzed; parameter γ shows potential instability.  
- Weakness — Clarity: occasional dense technical descriptions and specific unclear points (e.g., line 146) that need streamlining.  
- Weakness — Comparisons: limited comparison to other spatially regularized dictionary methods (e.g., TV-based approaches).  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, empirically strong, scalable, and will release code — merits acceptance despite theoretical gaps.  
- Rejection concerns: lack of theory, experiments mainly on one dataset, and presentation clarity could limit robustness; overall recommendation: Strong Accept (post-rebuttal).
