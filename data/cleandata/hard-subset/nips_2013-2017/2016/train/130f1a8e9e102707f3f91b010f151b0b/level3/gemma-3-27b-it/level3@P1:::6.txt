This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), for decomposing brain images into structured and sparse components. The core idea is to extend existing online dictionary learning techniques by incorporating both an L1-norm penalty to encourage sparsity and a Laplacian penalty to enforce smoothness, leading to spatially localized “blobs” representing brain regions. The authors motivate this approach by arguing that inter-subject variability in neuroimaging data contains valuable information often discarded by standard statistical analyses. They aim to automatically delineate brain structures from functional images, avoiding the need for manually defined Regions of Interest (ROIs).

The work builds upon the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010), addressing the limitations of both univariate models and traditional ROI-based approaches.  It also relates to clustering methods for defining spatial units, but positions itself closer to PCA/ICA approaches with added sparsity and structure. The authors acknowledge related work in structured sparsity (e.g., total variation regularization) but highlight the computational efficiency of their online learning approach, which allows scalability to large datasets, a crucial factor in neuroimaging.  They draw parallels to recent approaches using sparse representations for brain mapping (Varoquaux et al., 2016) but emphasize their method’s theoretical grounding and scalability advantages.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of L1 and Laplacian regularization within an *online* dictionary learning framework appears novel and addresses a practical challenge in neuroimaging – extracting meaningful, interpretable structures from large-scale, variable data.
* **Scalability:** The online learning aspect is a significant strength.  Handling large neuroimaging datasets (high voxel counts and potentially many subjects) is a major hurdle for many methods, and this work directly addresses that.
* **Empirical Results:**  The experiments on the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset demonstrate promising results. The authors show that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable dictionaries compared to standard SODL and CanICA, with improved performance on predicting behavioral variables. The learning curves presented are insightful, showing the benefits of the proposed method, especially with limited data.
* **Practical Relevance:** The ability to automatically delineate brain regions without relying on manually defined ROIs has significant practical implications for neuroimaging research.
* **Clear Problem Statement:** The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, and the authors effectively present the challenges in current neuroimaging analysis pipelines.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks rigorous theoretical analysis. While the authors mention convergence guarantees based on previous work (Bertsekas, 1999; Mairal et al., 2010), there is no proof of convergence for *this specific* non-convex problem, nor any analysis of the impact of the Laplacian penalty on the optimization landscape. The choice of the hyperparameters (α, γ, τ) is also not theoretically motivated, relying instead on empirical tuning.
* **Non-Convexity:** The method is explicitly acknowledged to be non-convex, raising concerns about the potential for getting stuck in local optima. While the authors show stability across different runs, a more thorough investigation of the solution landscape would be valuable.
* **Clarity and Presentation:**  While generally well-written, some sections are dense and could benefit from more clarity. The connection between equations and the described algorithms could be strengthened. The reviewer’s note regarding line 146 is valid, and streamlining this section would improve readability. (Note: post-rebuttal, the reviewer indicates this issue has been addressed).
* **Comparison to Alternatives:** While the comparison to CanICA and SODL is helpful, the authors could also consider comparing their approach to other recent methods for structured sparse representation learning in neuroimaging (e.g., those based on total variation or graph-based regularization).



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful method for a challenging problem in neuroimaging.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach.
* The scalability of the method is a significant strength, making it applicable to large-scale datasets.
* The authors have provided a clear and well-motivated problem statement.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of theoretical analysis is a significant weakness.
* The non-convexity of the problem and the absence of convergence guarantees raise concerns about the robustness of the method.
*  While improved after rebuttal, the original clarity and presentation issues detracted from the overall impact of the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper that addresses an important problem in neuroimaging. While the lack of theoretical analysis and the non-convexity of the problem are concerns, the novel approach, compelling empirical results, and scalability of the method make it a worthwhile contribution.  The improvements made in response to the reviewer’s concerns regarding clarity further strengthen the paper.  I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that future work should focus on providing more theoretical guarantees and exploring the properties of the solution landscape.
