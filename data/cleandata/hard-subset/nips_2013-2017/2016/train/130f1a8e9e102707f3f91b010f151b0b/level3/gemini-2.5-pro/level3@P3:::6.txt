### Review

**Summary**

This paper proposes "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL), a novel method for decomposing large-scale brain imaging data into a set of sparse and spatially structured components. The core contribution is the extension of the scalable online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by introducing a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages spatial smoothness, causing the learned components to form contiguous, blob-like regions, which are more neuroscientifically interpretable than the scattered patterns often produced by standard sparse models. The proposed algorithm uses a Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) scheme that remains computationally efficient, scaling to massive datasets where both the number of subjects and voxels are large. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments on the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset that their method produces more stable and interpretable results than standard Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA (CanICA), particularly in low-to-medium data regimes.

**Quality**

The paper presents a solid piece of empirical work. The technical approach is sound, and the motivation to add a spatial smoothness prior to a scalable dictionary learning framework is well-justified for neuroimaging applications.

*   **Strengths:** The experimental evaluation is a major strength. The authors compare their method against relevant state-of-the-art baselines on a large, challenging, real-world dataset. The metrics used for evaluation are comprehensive, covering qualitative assessment, explained variance, stability, and predictive performance on behavioral variables. The results compellingly show the benefits of the proposed regularizer: the learned dictionaries are visually more coherent (Fig. 2a), more stable across data splits, and capture inter-subject variability effectively, especially when data is limited (Table 1). The work is presented as a complete contribution, with a clear algorithm and promising results.

*   **Weaknesses:** The primary weakness of the paper is its lack of theoretical analysis. The objective function in (2) is non-convex, meaning the proposed BCD algorithm is only guaranteed to converge to a local minimum. The paper does not discuss the properties of the solution space, such as the existence or uniqueness of a solution. While the authors state that the convergence is guaranteed by satisfying the hypotheses of Mairal et al. (2010), the addition of the Laplacian term fundamentally changes the dictionary update sub-problem. A more thorough discussion of the optimization landscape and the conditions under which the algorithm performs well would significantly strengthen the work. The paper relies almost entirely on empirical evidence to validate the method, which, while strong, leaves open questions about the algorithm's theoretical behavior.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity could be significantly improved in the description of the core algorithm.

*   The derivation of the BCD dictionary update in Section 3.1 is difficult to follow. The transition to Equation (7) and the subsequent description in Algorithm 2 are not self-contained. Specifically, the paper states to "refer to [16] for the details" (line 146) for the second argument of the function `Fγ̃`. However, this is a new sub-problem involving the Laplacian term, and its derivation is non-trivial. It is unclear how the term `(Bjt − V Aj)` from the original formulation is used to construct the argument `Vj + At[j, j]−1(Bjt −VAj)`. A clearer, step-by-step derivation of this update rule is essential for the reader to understand the algorithm and for the work to be reproducible.

*   The discussion on hyperparameter selection is practical but lacks some intuition. For example, the relationship between the step size (related to the Lipschitz constant `Lt`) and the choice of `γ` is stated in Eq. (9) but without much explanation, making it hard to grasp the practical implications for setting this parameter.

**Originality**

The work is original in its combination of existing techniques to solve a challenging problem. While online dictionary learning and Laplacian regularization are not new concepts in themselves, their integration into a single, scalable framework for neuroimaging data analysis is novel. The paper clearly positions itself with respect to prior work, highlighting how it advances beyond standard online dictionary learning (which lacks structure) and previous structured decomposition methods (which lack scalability). The contribution is a practical and meaningful extension of the state of the art.

**Significance**

The paper has high potential for significance and impact, particularly within the neuroimaging community. The problem of identifying meaningful brain regions from large population cohorts is fundamental. The proposed method offers a principled and, crucially, scalable solution. As neuroimaging datasets continue to grow in size (e.g., UK Biobank, HCP), online algorithms like the one proposed here will become indispensable. The ability to learn spatially coherent and interpretable brain networks at scale, without the computationally prohibitive cost of older methods, is a valuable contribution that researchers and practitioners are likely to use and build upon. The demonstrated improvements in stability and interpretability make the method a strong candidate for data-driven ROI definition and dimensionality reduction in future neuroimaging studies.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and practical problem in a large and active research community.
*   The proposed method is a novel and useful combination of ideas that yields a scalable and effective algorithm.
*   Strong and convincing empirical results on a large-scale, real-world dataset demonstrate clear advantages over existing state-of-the-art methods.
*   The work is likely to have a high impact and be widely adopted by practitioners in the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The paper lacks theoretical analysis regarding the non-convex optimization problem, leaving questions about the properties of the obtained solutions unanswered.
*   The clarity of the core algorithmic description is insufficient, particularly regarding the dictionary update step, which hinders reproducibility and full understanding.

In summary, this is a strong empirical paper with high practical significance. The weaknesses related to theoretical depth and clarity are notable, but the demonstrated real-world utility of the method makes a compelling case for its acceptance. The clarity issues, especially concerning the algorithm's derivation, should be addressed in a final version.
