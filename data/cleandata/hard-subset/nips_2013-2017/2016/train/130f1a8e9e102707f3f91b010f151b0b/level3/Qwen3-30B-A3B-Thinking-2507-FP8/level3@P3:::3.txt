

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes an extension of online dictionary learning (SODL) by Mairal et al. (2010) with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms, aiming to extract structured brain regions from fMRI data. The authors build upon the online dictionary learning framework to handle large-scale brain imaging data, arguing that their "Smooth-SODL" model produces more interpretable, spatially structured brain maps compared to standard sparse dictionary learning and ICA-based methods. The work extends previous structured dictionary learning approaches (e.g., Varoquaux et al. 2012) by adapting them to online learning settings, with the claim that the Laplacian penalty can be incorporated with minimal computational overhead.

## Quality

The technical approach is sound, with well-formulated optimization problem (2) and clear algorithmic details (Algorithms 1-2). The convergence guarantees are properly established by connecting to existing results. However, the paper falls short in providing rigorous evidence for its main claims. The primary results rely on visual comparisons of brain "blobs" (Fig. 2a), which are subjective and not clearly interpretable to an untrained eye. The paper fails to demonstrate how these blobs correspond to known brain regions or functions in a quantitative manner.

The most concerning quality issue is the inadequate validation approach. The paper claims that Smooth-SODL better captures inter-subject variability, yet it does not properly consolidate brain-region differences across subjects or use inter-subject consistency as a validation metric. Instead, it compares explained variance (Fig. 2b) and behavioral prediction (Fig. 2c), but these metrics don't convincingly demonstrate the added value of the spatial regularization. In fact, as noted by the authors themselves, CanICA achieves higher explained variance (66% vs 58% for Smooth-SODL), though it produces dense, less interpretable maps.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient mathematical detail for reproducibility. The algorithmic description is thorough, and the implementation details (e.g., using FISTA with 1-2 iterations) are appropriate. However, the visual results (Fig. 2a) lack proper labeling of known brain regions, making it difficult to assess whether the "blobs" correspond to meaningful neuroanatomical structures. The paper would benefit from including standard brain atlases for comparison or quantitative metrics of spatial coherence.

## Originality

The core idea of adding a Sobolev prior to dictionary learning is somewhat novel, but the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from related work. The authors cite Varoquaux et al. (2012) as using structured dictionary learning for brain imaging, but don't clearly explain how their online approach with Laplacian penalty differs from this prior work. The claim that their method "non-trivially extends" SODL (Mairal et al. 2010) is somewhat overstated, as the Laplacian penalty is a standard technique in image processing.

## Significance

The significance claim is the paper's weakest aspect. The authors argue that spatial regularization is necessary to capture inter-subject variability, but their own data (Table 1) shows that the performance gap between Smooth-SODL and SODL diminishes as more data is added. Given that they use the Human Connectome Project dataset (500 subjects), which is large enough to overcome typical data scarcity issues, the need for spatial smoothing appears questionable. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this method solves a real problem in brain imaging that existing approaches cannot address.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Well-formulated optimization problem with convergence guarantees
- Appropriate handling of large-scale brain imaging data
- Clear algorithmic description with implementation details
- Demonstrates reasonable computational efficiency (3x slower than SODL)

**Weaknesses:**
- Main results rely on subjective visual comparison of brain "blobs"
- Inadequate validation of interpretability and clinical relevance
- Fails to address whether spatial regularization is necessary for large datasets like HCP
- Does not properly compare against related structured dictionary learning approaches
- Overstates significance without strong empirical evidence

## Recommendation

While the paper presents a technically sound extension of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty, the claimed significance is not adequately supported by the evidence. The visual comparisons are insufficient to demonstrate that the proposed method yields more interpretable or clinically meaningful brain regions. The paper would benefit from incorporating more objective metrics of spatial coherence, comparing against standard brain atlases, and addressing why spatial smoothing is necessary for the large dataset they use (HCP with 500 subjects).

The paper's main contribution appears to be a computational extension of dictionary learning, but the claimed scientific contribution to brain imaging is weak. Without stronger evidence that the spatially structured dictionaries provide meaningful biological insights beyond what can be achieved with standard methods on large datasets, the significance is unclear.

I recommend rejection, as the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method advances the state of the art in brain imaging or provides significant new insights into brain organization. The authors should first establish whether spatial regularization actually improves interpretability in a quantifiable way before claiming it better captures inter-subject variability.
