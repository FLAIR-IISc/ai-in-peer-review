This paper proposes "Smooth-SODL," a method for decomposing fMRI data into a dictionary of sparse and spatially structured functional components. The central idea is to extend the well-established online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty in addition to the standard L1 sparsity-inducing constraint. This approach aims to produce more interpretable, "blob-like" brain regions compared to methods that only enforce sparsity. The work is positioned as a scalable and principled alternative to slower, batch-based structured decomposition techniques and an improvement over unstructured sparse models.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality:** The main contribution—the integration of a Sobolev penalty into a scalable online dictionary learning algorithm for this application—is a novel and well-motivated combination of existing techniques. It directly addresses the important problem of learning structured, data-driven brain atlases from large datasets.
*   **Significance:** If effective, a fast algorithm that produces high-quality, interpretable functional brain maps would be a significant contribution to the neuroimaging community. The qualitative results (Fig. 2a) are promising, demonstrating that the proposed regularization yields visually cleaner and more spatially coherent components than the baseline.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Quality:** The claims of superiority are not well-supported by the quantitative experiments. On the full dataset, the performance gains of Smooth-SODL over the standard SODL in terms of explained variance and predictive power are marginal (Fig. 2b, 2c). The paper’s main quantitative argument rests on its advantage in the low-data regime (Table 1), but it fails to offer a compelling analysis for why the structural prior does not translate to more significant benefits when sufficient data is available. This leaves the practical utility of the added complexity in question.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is in poor shape and appears to be a premature draft. It contains several typographical and formatting errors, and more critically, leftover editorial comments. For instance, a sentence in Section 6 begins, "As rightly pointed out by one of the reviewers," and a derivation in equation (7) is hand-waved away with "refer to [16] for the details." This lack of polish makes the paper difficult to follow and severely undermines its scientific credibility.

In summary, while the core concept is sound and addresses a relevant problem, the paper is let down by an unconvincing empirical evaluation and a very rushed presentation. The authors need to provide a stronger quantitative justification for their method and thoroughly revise the manuscript to a professional standard before it can be considered for publication.
