This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning framework for neuroimaging that enforces sparsity and spatial structure on dictionary atoms. The key idea is to combine a sparsity-inducing constraint on atoms (e.g., ℓ1 ball or simplex) with a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty to promote compact, piecewise-smooth “blob-like” components. Codes are obtained via ridge regression; the dictionary is updated online via block coordinate descent, with each atom update cast as a convex GraphNet-style denoising problem solved by FISTA. The method extends the online sparse dictionary learning of Mairal et al. (JMLR 2010) by adding spatial smoothness at modest extra cost, and it scales to large n and p. Experiments on HCP task fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6×10^5) compare Smooth-SODL with SODL and CanICA. The authors report improved interpretability and stability, similar or better predictive performance than SODL and thresholded ICA, and a clear advantage in low-data regimes, with a runtime about 3× SODL and faster than CanICA.

Relation to prior work: The paper is well positioned relative to online dictionary-learning (Mairal et al. 2010; Mensch et al. 2016) and structured regularization in neuroimaging (TV-ℓ1 atlases of Abraham et al.; GraphNet of Grosenick et al.; structured sparse PCA of Jenatton et al.; group-level ICA such as CanICA and MELODIC; Varoquaux et al.’s multi-subject mapping). The novelty is specifically the integration of a Laplacian penalty on atoms within an online BCD dictionary update, preserving scalability while improving spatial structure.

Quality:
- Algorithmic soundness: Casting atom updates as convex, strongly-convex problems with Laplacian smoothness is sensible; using FISTA is appropriate. The link to GraphNet is accurate. The online BCD framework follows Mairal et al., and convergence to a stationary point is standard for such alternating minimization.
- Missing or weak theory: Claims are not supported by new theoretical analysis. There is no characterization of how the Laplacian term affects identifiability, sample complexity, or convergence rates. The statement that “1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e-6 accuracy” is implausible without evidence; a practical stopping rule and tolerance schedule should be specified. The discussion of Lipschitz blow-up and a time-varying γt is heuristic; a principled schedule or bound would strengthen the work.
- Experimental evaluation: The HCP study is relevant and large-scale. The qualitative improvements in spatial structure are convincing, and the small-sample EV advantage is compelling. However, some choices could bias comparisons (e.g., pre-smoothing applied to CanICA but not to SODL/Smooth-SODL). The predictive benchmarks show CanICA often winning; the narrative should reconcile the interpretability/stability gains with these outcomes. Ablations are missing: ridge vs ℓ1 codes; γ=0 vs >0 under identical sparsity; Laplacian vs TV; effect of the constraint set (ℓ1 ball vs simplex).
- Reproducibility: Code is “to be released,” but the paper should include enough specifics (exact hyperparameters, stopping criteria, boundary conditions for ∆, cross-validation splits, seeds). The Hungarian matching details and stability metric definition should be fully specified.

Clarity:
- The manuscript needs substantial proofreading. There are typos, missing words, inconsistent notation (e.g., switching between V and v), leftover comments, and some unclear equations (e.g., Eq. 7/8 references). The constraint set C and its projection should be clearly defined for each experiment (ℓ1 ball vs simplex) and whether positivity is enforced. Define ∇ operators and ∆ with boundary conditions. The claim that BCD is “parameter free” is misleading given FISTA step-sizes and stopping tolerances. Figures require legible captions and consistent use of thresholds (avoid thresholding only some methods).

Originality:
- Moderate. The specific combination—online dictionary-learning with Laplacian-regularized atoms and sparsity constraints—is a practical and nontrivial integration, but each ingredient exists. Compared to TV-ℓ1 atlases, adopting Laplacian smoothness trades edge-preserving TV for simpler quadratic smoothing, enabling efficient online updates. The contribution is primarily engineering and scalability rather than a new learning principle.

Significance:
- For large-scale neuroimaging, a fast, online method that yields interpretable, spatially coherent atoms is valuable. The early-regime advantage (EV with few subjects) and improved stability are practically important. However, given that predictive performance is not consistently superior and EV lags CanICA (as expected), the impact hinges on interpretability/stability and scalability. With stronger empirical rigor and cleaner presentation, this could be a useful tool that others adopt.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
- Provide ablation studies: γ=0 vs optimal γ; Laplacian vs TV; ridge vs ℓ1 codes; effect of constraint set; effect of mini-batch size η and number of passes T.
- Ensure fair preprocessing across methods. Either smooth all methods identically or justify differences; report robustness to smoothing.
- Detail optimization: FISTA tolerance, max iterations per atom, warm-starting, stopping criteria; practical schedules for αt and γt with justification; computational/memory complexity per update in terms of p, k, η.
- Clarify the “global optimum” claim for the atom subproblem and remove overstatements about accuracy after 1–2 iterations.
- Strengthen reproducibility: release code or pseudo-code with all hyperparameters; specify cross-validation folds and seeds; provide exact γ, τ ranges and selection procedure.
- Improve presentation: fix typos and leftover comments; unify notation; define ∆ and boundaries; expand figure captions; add quantitative reproducibility tables (mean±SEM correlations across splits).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Scalable, practical method addressing a real need in neuroimaging.
- Produces more interpretable and more stable spatial atoms than SODL at modest extra cost.
- Demonstrates clear advantages in low-data regimes and competitive performance overall.
- Integrates well with existing pipelines (e.g., Nilearn), increasing potential impact.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient theoretical analysis of the proposed regularization in the online setting.
- Clarity issues and careless writing detract from credibility.
- Limited ablations and potentially unfair comparisons reduce evidential strength.
- Predictive performance is not consistently improved over strong baselines.

Overall: promising and potentially useful, but the paper requires significant polishing, clearer methodology, and stronger empirical support to reach the standards of NIPS.
