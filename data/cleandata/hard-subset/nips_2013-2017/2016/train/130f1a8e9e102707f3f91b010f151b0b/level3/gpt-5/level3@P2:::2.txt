Summary
This paper proposes an online structured sparse dictionary learning algorithm for neuroimaging that extends Mairal et al. (2010) by adding a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. The goal is to obtain atoms that are both sparse and spatially structured (piece-wise smooth, compact “blobs”), enabling more interpretable “regions” that capture inter-subject variability in task fMRI data. The model constrains atoms via an l1 (or simplex) constraint to induce sparsity and uses a Laplacian quadratic penalty to impose smoothness; codes are obtained via ridge regression. Optimization follows the standard online block-coordinate strategy with sufficient statistics (A, B), and atom updates solve a denoising GraphNet-like subproblem with FISTA inside BCD. The method scales online to large n and p. Experiments on HCP task fMRI compare against SODL and CanICA, reporting runtime, explained variance, stability/reproducibility, and behavioral prediction.

Relation to prior work: Online dictionary learning for large-scale settings (Mairal et al., Mensch et al.) is well established, while spatially structured decompositions (e.g., TV- or GraphNet-regularized models; Abraham; Jenatton; Grosenick; Varoquaux) have generally relied on slower alternating optimization. The paper’s main novelty is integrating a Laplacian smoothness prior into the online SODL framework with near-constant overhead, yielding a scalable structured decomposition.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical gap: scalable structured decompositions for neuroimaging.
- Sound formulation that reduces atom updates to a strongly convex denoising problem amenable to fast first-order methods; careful discussion of Lipschitz constants and regularization scaling.
- Online algorithm preserves the favorable memory/runtime profile of SODL and demonstrates modest overhead (≈2–3×).
- Potentially useful beyond the presented application (e.g., segmentation, ROI discovery, regional sensing).
- Rebuttal provided additional results that improved the technical impression; these should be incorporated.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evidence for “more structured, meaningful” atoms is not convincingly quantified. Claims rely largely on visual inspection with heterogeneous slices and inconsistent thresholding, making comparison difficult.
- The neuroscientific relevance of “structure” is not operationalized with explicit, justified metrics (e.g., contiguity, compactness, atlas overlap, connected components).
- Some mathematical details and notation are confusing or inconsistent (e.g., transpose/indices and terms in Eqs. (6) and (8); references to Aj, At[j,j], Bjt, VAjt), and the text has numerous typos that impede clarity.
- Baselines and figures are not consistently or fairly presented (e.g., PCA missing in prediction plot; different slices across methods; smoothing choices differ across models).
- Several references and details (e.g., HCP) need to be clearly cited in-text and in the bibliography.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The optimization framing is solid and consistent with prior online dictionary learning. The atom-update subproblem is a strongly convex quadratic with a straightforward FISTA solution. However, the paper should:
  - Provide precise statements about convergence when using time-varying αt, γt, and mini-batches, and clarify whether γ is fixed or adapted in practice.
  - Report concrete stopping criteria, tolerances, and iteration counts for FISTA to support the “1–2 iterations suffice” claim.
  - Explicitly derive the ridge coding step (Eq. 6) and show how the SVD is used for efficiency.
- Empirical support: While runtimes and explained variance are informative, the main claim—recovering compact, interpretable brain regions—needs quantitative validation (see requests below).

Clarity
- The manuscript is difficult to follow in places due to typos and notation inconsistencies (e.g., “xperiments,” “intepretable,” “can imposes,” stray bold text). Equations (6) and (8) should be rechecked for signs, indices, and matrix dimensions; define all symbols where first used.
- Figures: For visual comparisons, show the same anatomical slices for all methods, the same color scales, and consistent thresholding policies (or, ideally, no thresholding for any method). Legends should clearly define all baselines; ensure PCA is included where relevant.
- Please cite the HCP dataset clearly in the main text and ensure the corresponding reference is complete.

Originality
- The idea of imposing smoothness/structure on dictionary atoms is not new, but integrating a Laplacian prior within an online SODL framework with low overhead is a useful and non-trivial engineering/research contribution. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from TV- and heuristic approaches by preserving online scalability and convergence properties.

Significance
- If convincingly demonstrated, the approach could become a practical tool for data-driven ROI discovery in large cohorts and might generalize to other biomedical imaging tasks. Current evidence suggests promise, but the lack of robust quantitative evaluation of spatial structure limits the strength of the claim.

Requested revisions and clarifications
- Quantify “structure” and neuroscientific relevance:
  - Spatial metrics: number of connected components per atom; compactness (e.g., radius of gyration), Moran’s I/spatial autocorrelation; Laplacian energy; total variation; proportion of mass in the largest component.
  - Reproducibility: split-half stability with optimal matching, reporting mean/median correlations with confidence intervals, and permutation-based significance.
  - Anatomical validity: Dice/IoU overlap with standard atlases (AAL, Yeo 7/17, Glasser MMP) and task-relevant ROIs; report multiple comparisons correction.
  - Behavioral relevance: clarify that predictions use codes Ui; compare against PCA/CanICA/SODL with identical dimensionality and regularization; include PCA in Fig. 3; report cross-validated R2 with CIs and statistical tests.
- Fair baselines and visualization:
  - Use consistent preprocessing across methods (either all smoothed or all unsmoothed) or provide both and analyze the impact.
  - Match components via a standard procedure (e.g., Hungarian) and show identical slices, scales, and either no thresholding or the same thresholding policy for all methods.
- Algorithmic details:
  - Provide the derivation and computational cost of Eq. (6) using SVD; discuss numerical stability.
  - Specify stopping criteria and tolerances for FISTA; report average iterations/time per atom update.
  - Clarify the practical choice of αt and γ (fixed vs. adaptive), and reconcile with the theoretical scaling in Eq. (9).
- Notation and typos:
  - Fix inconsistencies in Eqs. (6) and (8) (transposes, indices, Aj vs. At[j,j], Bjt, VAjt); define all variables at first occurrence.
  - Clean typos and stray formatting (examples noted above).
- References:
  - Ensure proper in-text citation and bibliographic entry for the HCP dataset and other missing/partial references.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses a relevant and practical problem with a scalable, principled method.
- Solid optimization setup; extends a widely used online framework with minimal overhead.
- Potential for impact in neuroimaging pipelines; code release via Nilearn would aid adoption.
- Rebuttal strengthened the technical case.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Insufficient quantitative evidence that the learned atoms are more spatially meaningful or neuroscientifically valid than baselines.
- Clarity issues (notation, typos, figures) hinder reproducibility and evaluation.
- Some baseline choices and visual comparisons are not strictly comparable.

Recommendation
Borderline. I lean toward weak reject unless the authors incorporate the requested quantitative evaluations of spatial “structure,” clarify algorithmic details (especially Eqs. (6)/(8) and stopping criteria), ensure fair/consistent baselines and visualizations, and fix presentation issues. With these improvements (many of which appeared in the rebuttal), the paper would present a compelling, useful contribution.
