Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning framework for brain images that combines sparsity and spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. Sparsity is enforced by constraining atoms in an ℓ1-type compact set (e.g., simplex), while spatial smoothness and compactness are promoted by a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty on each atom. Codes are updated via ridge regression, and atoms are updated by block-coordinate descent using a proximal step (FISTA) for the Laplacian-regularized denoising subproblems. The method extends the online sparse coding approach of Mairal et al. (JMLR 2010) by adding a spatial prior, with a reported 2–3× runtime overhead yet retaining scalability to large n and p. The formulation is close in spirit to GraphNet-style penalties and to prior work on structured sparsity and TV-ℓ1 for neuroimaging, but differs by embedding the spatially structured regularizer in an online, scalable dictionary-learning procedure.

Empirically, the authors evaluate on HCP task fMRI (n≈500, p≈2.6e5) with k=40 atoms. Compared to standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a multi-subject PCA/ICA baseline (CanICA), Smooth-SODL yields atoms that are more spatially compact and interpretable, improves stability/reproducibility across splits, and performs competitively on explained variance and behavioral prediction (underperforming PCA/ICA on EV, as expected, but outperforming SODL and thresholded ICA on several behavioral targets). Learning curves show that spatial regularization is especially beneficial in the small-to-medium data regime.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The optimization strategy is reasonable: online updates of codes with ridge regression, sufficient statistics (A, B) accumulation, and per-atom proximal updates for Laplacian-regularized denoising under a compact sparsity constraint. The link to FISTA and the Lipschitz constant for the Laplacian term are appropriate. However:
  - Existence/uniqueness: The paper does not prove existence or uniqueness of a solution for the constrained, Laplacian-regularized atom subproblems nor for the full nonconvex problem. Local convergence of the BCD inner loop is plausible, and the connection to [16] suggests convergence to a stationary point, but this is not formalized for the added Laplacian term and time-varying regularization.
  - Theoretical guarantees: There is no global optimality or consistency result; claims rest primarily on empirical performance. The discussion of how γ should scale with At[j,j]/t to avoid vanishing steps (and the suggested αt ∝ t^−1/2) is helpful, but a formal statement with assumptions (e.g., bounded data, coercivity of the penalized objective under the constraint set) would strengthen the work.
  - Evaluation is thorough on one large dataset/contrast and includes robustness over γ, τ, but comparisons to alternative structured penalties (e.g., TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning, ElasticNet-constrained atoms) are missing; citing [Abraham MICCAI] and related structured sparse PCA/GraphNet is good, but a direct empirical comparison would calibrate the gains from Laplacian vs TV.

Clarity
- Overall organization is clear, with a decent balance of model, algorithm, and experiments. Derivations of the update rules are sketched and point to [16], and practical guidance on hyperparameters is appreciated.
- Specific issue: at line 146, the presentation is unclear (the notation for the objective and the dependence on t/γ appears ambiguous). Please fix the notation and explicitly define all symbols at first use.
- Minor suggestions: clearly state the constraint set used in all experiments up front (simplex vs ℓ1 ball), and provide a compact pseudo-code for the per-atom proximal step including projection onto C. A small ablation on ridge vs ℓ1 codes would help readers decide which regime to use.
- After rebuttal: I am satisfied with the authors’ clarifications on the algorithmic details and parameter schedules; I raise the clarity/presentation score from 2 to 3.

Originality
- The combination of an online dictionary-learning framework with a Laplacian prior on atoms is a meaningful and nontrivial extension of [16]. While Laplacian/GraphNet and TV-ℓ1 priors are known in neuroimaging, their integration into a scalable online BCD with per-atom proximal denoising and practical scheduling of γ is novel and practically valuable.

Significance
- The problem—deriving interpretable, spatially localized components that capture inter-subject variability at scale—is important for neuroimaging. The method is likely to be adopted by practitioners, especially with a planned Nilearn implementation. The improvements in interpretability, stability, and predictive utility (relative to SODL) and the favorable runtime trade-off suggest real impact. Broader evaluation across multiple tasks/modalities and stronger theory would further increase significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a scalable, practically effective algorithm.
- Novel and well-motivated integration of spatial Laplacian regularization into online dictionary learning.
- Clear empirical gains in interpretability and stability; competitive performance on EV and behavior prediction.
- Careful practical guidance on hyperparameters; reasonable runtime overhead; open-source intent.

Cons against acceptance
- No theoretical guarantees beyond intuition and analogy to [16]; existence/uniqueness not established.
- Evaluation centered on a single dataset/contrast; lacks direct comparison to TV-ℓ1 or other structured decompositions.
- Some presentation/notation issues (e.g., line 146) and limited ablations (e.g., choice of code penalty).

Requested changes
- Clarify the ambiguous notation at line 146 and define all symbols locally.
- Add a brief discussion on existence/uniqueness for the atom subproblem under Laplacian + compact constraint; at minimum, state conditions ensuring a minimizer exists.
- If space permits, include a small-scale comparison to TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning or structured sparse PCA, or at least discuss expected differences.
- Report sensitivity to k and to the choice of ridge vs ℓ1 codes.
