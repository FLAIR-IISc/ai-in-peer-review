Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning approach for large-scale brain imaging that enforces both sparsity and spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms. Sparsity is via an l1-type constraint on each atom (implemented as simplex/ℓ1-ball projection), while smoothness is imposed with a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty; codes are obtained with ridge regression. The algorithm extends the online matrix factorization of Mairal et al. (JMLR 2010) by inserting a Laplacian-regularized per-atom update, solved efficiently with a few FISTA iterations inside a BCD loop, yielding a 2–3× overhead relative to vanilla online DL. The approach connects to GraphNet-style penalties (Grosenick et al.), structured sparsity/SPCA (Jenatton et al.), and prior total-variation constrained atlas learning (Abraham et al., Varoquaux et al.), while addressing their scalability limitations through an online solver.

Experiments on HCP task fMRI Z-maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) benchmark against SODL and CanICA. Smooth-SODL produces more spatially coherent, interpretable atoms, improves stability and early-sample generalization, and remains competitive on explained variance and behavioral prediction.

Strengths
- Practical and scalable: a minimal modification to a widely used online DL framework that works at HCP scale on a single CPU, with clear runtime comparisons.
- Well-motivated regularizer: sparsity plus Laplacian smoothness yields blob-like, contiguous regions that align better with neuroanatomy than l1-only SODL or dense PCA/ICA.
- Empirical breadth: qualitative maps, explained variance, split-half stability, learning curves, and downstream behavioral prediction. The finding that smoothness helps particularly in low-data regimes is useful.
- Relevance and impact: addresses a central neuroimaging need (data-driven ROIs/atlases) and ties into widely adopted software (Nilearn).

Weaknesses
- Theory is limited. The overall non-convex problem lacks guarantees beyond those inherited from Mairal et al.; there is no existence/uniqueness analysis for the joint problem or global convergence. The algorithmic section suggests local convergence of BCD but does not establish properties for the combined Laplacian + constraint setup beyond subproblem convexity.
- The update notation around Eq. (7–8) is confusing and key steps are under-explained. In particular, the use of Fγ(At[j,j]/t)−1(·,·) is ambiguous about whether the scaling applies to γ or to the argument—see the clarity note below.
- Evaluation is almost entirely empirical; no sample-complexity, identifiability, or consistency analysis is attempted, and the safe range for γ is presented heuristically.
- Baseline and preprocessing differences (e.g., CanICA pre-smoothed while SODL/Smooth-SODL not; hard-thresholded tCanICA) complicate fairness. While motivated, these choices should be more explicitly justified and sensitivity-reported.

Quality
- Technically sound at the subproblem level: per-atom updates are strongly convex (with Laplacian) under convex compact constraints, and FISTA is appropriate. However, the paper does not prove existence/uniqueness for the full factorization or give convergence rates/guarantees for the overall non-convex scheme with time-varying γ,α. The bound on the Lipschitz constant and the γ schedule are heuristic; stability to γ,τ is explored empirically.
- Experiments are strong and reproducible in spirit (public data, multiple metrics), though code availability is “soon” rather than provided.

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally understandable but contains several notational and editorial issues and could be tightened.
- Specific issue (line 146): in the atom update, the expression Vj ← argminv∈C Fγ(At[j,j]/t)−1(v, Vj + At[j,j]−1(Bjt − VAj)) is ambiguous. Fγ̃(v,a) was defined with γ̃ as a scalar; here it reads as Fγ(something)−1, which could be interpreted as applying an inverse to F or to its first argument. Please rewrite to make clear that γ̃ = γt/(At[j,j]) (or equivalent) and that the shift a = Vj + At[j,j]−1(Bjt − VAj) is the standard residual correction. Also specify boundary conditions and discretization conventions for ∆ to justify the Lipschitz bound 1 + 4Dγ̃.
- Typos/grammar: “intepretable,” “xperiments,” “th Conference…,” and several small issues in Section 3.2. Please proofread.

Originality
- Incremental yet meaningful. Combining an ℓ1 constraint with a Laplacian penalty within online DL and showing it scales is a practical contribution. Related structured penalties (TV/GraphNet) and structured DL exist, but prior structured decompositions in neuroimaging are typically slow batch or alternating schemes. This paper’s novelty is in the online, efficient integration and demonstration at scale.

Significance
- Likely to be used by practitioners: yields more interpretable, stable maps with small computational overhead and integrates with common tooling. The empirical advantages (especially in low-data regimes) are compelling for cohort analyses and atlas learning.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, scalable method with clear practical value and strong empirical evidence.
- Improves interpretability/stability without large runtime penalties.
- Uses public data and multiple evaluation angles; learning-curve analysis is insightful.
- Well-situated within existing literature and toolchains.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical development: no existence/uniqueness for the joint problem, no global guarantees, and heuristic parameter schedules.
- Some clarity/presentation issues in the algorithmic section and notation.
- Baseline preprocessing choices could bias comparisons; more sensitivity analysis is warranted.
- Code not yet available at submission time.

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify the exact γ schedule and its dependence on At[j,j]/t; provide a practical rule-of-thumb and report sensitivity.
- Specify Laplacian discretization and boundary conditions; this affects both the Lipschitz constant and the spatial bias (e.g., toward smoother edges).
- Report results with/without pre-smoothing across all methods, or add a CanICA variant without smoothing, to isolate the effect of spatial priors.
- Add a brief existence argument for a minimizer of (U,V) under compact constraints (or clarify why not), and state explicitly that uniqueness is not expected.

Overall recommendation
- Initial: borderline accept (weak accept) due to strong empirical impact despite limited theory and clarity issues.
- After rebuttal: the authors clarified the atom-update derivation and γ scaling and committed to releasing code; I am satisfied regarding the algorithmic questions and raise the clarity/presentation score from 2 to 3.
