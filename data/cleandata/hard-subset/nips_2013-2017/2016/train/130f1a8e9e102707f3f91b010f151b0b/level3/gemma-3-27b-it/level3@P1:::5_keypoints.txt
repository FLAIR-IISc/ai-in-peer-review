- Proposal: Smooth-SODL extends online dictionary learning by adding a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms for brain imaging decomposition.  
- Motivation: Leverages inter-subject variability to define ROIs without explicit segmentation, arguing sparsity + spatial smoothness yields meaningful brain regions.  
- Method claim: Adding the Laplacian penalty increases runtime only 2–3x while better capturing inter-subject variability.  
- Empirical claim: Experiments on HCP task fMRI show improved interpretability and competitive performance versus standard SODL and CanICA.  
- Strength — Scalability: Online algorithmic framework handles large neuroimaging datasets efficiently.  
- Strength — Interpretability: Spatial regularization encourages atoms that look like coherent brain regions (“blobs”).  
- Strength — Empirical support: Reported gains in explained variance and behavioral prediction, especially early in training.  
- Strength — Clear problem formulation: Authors clearly state the goal of leveraging variability to define ROIs without segmentation.  
- Weakness — Limited novelty: Core contribution (Laplacian penalty added to SODL) is incremental relative to Mairal et al. (2010).  
- Weakness — Theoretical gap: No strong theoretical justification for using the Sobolev/Laplacian penalty or precise definition of the sought “structure.”  
- Weakness — Design choices unclear: Using an l2 penalty on codes (Ui) instead of the original l1 lacks sufficient justification beyond stability claims.  
- Weakness — Clarity and presentation problems: Writing contains typos, grammatical errors, unclear notation, and ambiguous references.  
- Weakness — Baseline comparisons insufficient: More thorough comparison to other ROI-definition and spatial clustering methods is needed.  
- Overall assessment: Technically sound extension but concerns about novelty, theoretical grounding, clarity, and evaluation limit endorsement.  
- Acceptance arguments: Scalable method, promising empirical results, and a well-articulated problem motivate further interest.  
- Rejection arguments: Incremental contribution, weak theoretical justification, presentation flaws, and limited baseline comparison argue against acceptance.  
- Recommendation: Reject; major revision required to strengthen novelty justification, theory, clarity, and experimental comparisons.
