

This paper proposes an extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging data by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatial contiguity in dictionary atoms. The authors build upon Mairal et al.'s (2010) Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (SODL) framework, adding a Laplacian regularization term to ensure atoms form spatially structured "blobs" rather than scattered patterns. This addresses a key challenge in neuroimaging where traditional methods struggle with inter-subject variability and ROI definition.

The main contribution is a technically sound extension of existing dictionary learning methods with a novel spatial regularization term. The Smooth-SODL algorithm maintains the online nature of SODL, allowing scalability to large datasets (up to 500 subjects with 260k voxels), with only a 2-3x runtime increase. The algorithmic approach follows a well-established framework with appropriate convergence guarantees, and the theoretical foundation is solid. The paper's application to HCP fMRI data is relevant and timely for neuroimaging research.

The paper's strengths include a clear problem statement addressing the limitations of current ROI definition methods in neuroimaging, a well-motivated extension of existing work, and practical implementation details showing the method's scalability. The authors demonstrate the method's utility in extracting interpretable brain regions that better capture inter-subject variability compared to SODL and CanICA. The method's computational efficiency is particularly noteworthy for large-scale neuroimaging applications.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses in empirical evaluation and presentation. The most critical issue is that Figure 2 does not quantitatively demonstrate the claimed advantage of the Sobolev prior. The visual comparisons are misleading as different slices are shown for different methods, making direct comparison impossible. The claim that Smooth-SODL produces more "structured" atoms is not supported by any quantitative metrics or neuroscience-justified criteria. The paper should have provided metrics like spatial coherence scores, blob compactness measures, or comparisons of atom shapes against known anatomical structures.

The mathematical presentation contains inconsistencies that undermine technical credibility. Equations (6) and (8) have unclear transpose placements and inconsistent variable references. For example, Equation (6) should show the ridge regression solution as (V^TV + αI)^{-1}V^TX_t, but the current formulation is ambiguous. The authors should include a derivation of the ridge solution in the appendix to clarify this. Additionally, the paper misses a reference to the Human Connectome Project dataset (HCP) in the main text, which is a key component of the experiments.

Presentation issues significantly impair readability. The manuscript contains multiple typos, inconsistent notation (e.g., "Graph-Net penalty can imposes"), and editing problems that make it difficult to follow the technical arguments. Figure 3's caption fails to explain baselines (missing PCA comparison) and the meaning of R^2 > 0.1 claims. The paper would benefit from clearer explanations of what is being predicted in Figure 3 and how the prediction task relates to the dictionary learning framework.

The novelty is somewhat incremental, as the core idea of incorporating spatial structure into dictionary learning has been explored in prior work (e.g., [1, 25]). However, the specific extension to online learning with computational efficiency is valuable. The significance lies in the potential to improve ROI extraction in neuroimaging, which could benefit researchers in brain mapping and clinical applications. The method's extension to other imaging domains (e.g., image segmentation) is a promising direction.

The paper's technical quality is good but would be significantly strengthened by addressing the empirical evaluation gaps. The authors have provided additional results in the rebuttal that improve the evaluation, and these should be incorporated into the final manuscript. The requested quantitative metrics for "structured" atoms, clear neuroscience-justified criteria for evaluation, and corrected mathematical formulations would substantially improve the paper's impact.

In conclusion, this paper presents a technically sound extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging with promising applications. The main weaknesses lie in the inadequate empirical evaluation and presentation issues that prevent readers from fully assessing the claimed advantages. With revisions addressing the quantitative evaluation gaps, mathematical inconsistencies, and presentation problems, this work would make a valuable contribution to the field. As currently written, the paper's empirical claims are not sufficiently supported, which limits its impact despite the promising methodological extension. I recommend acceptance with major revisions addressing the empirical evaluation and presentation issues.
