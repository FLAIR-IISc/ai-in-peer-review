## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method with the addition of a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage spatially smooth dictionary atoms for decomposing brain images. The motivation stems from the desire to automatically define brain regions from inter-subject variability in fMRI data, moving beyond traditional ROI-based approaches. The method builds directly upon the existing work of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a spatial regularization term. 

The authors aim to address a relevant problem in neuroimaging – the principled definition of regions of interest – and the online learning aspect is appealing for large datasets like those from the Human Connectome Project, which they demonstrate on. The experimental evaluation on HCP data compares the proposed method against ICA-based approaches (CanICA) and standard online dictionary learning (SODL), showcasing improvements in dictionary interpretability, explained variance, and prediction of behavioral scores. The authors also analyze the impact of hyperparameter selection. 

However, the paper suffers from several significant weaknesses. The novelty is questionable; extending online dictionary learning with a Sobolev penalty, while potentially effective, feels incremental and may not meet the standards for NIPS. The justification for the Sobolev regularization remains unclear. The paper doesn’t adequately define what “structure” means in this context, nor convincingly argue *why* promoting smooth, blob-like atoms improves the decomposition, beyond intuition. The potential error in Eq.(1) and the lack of justification for the l2 norm in Eq.(2) (compared to the l1 norm used in Mairal et al., 2010) are concerning technical issues. The reference to “line 146” is also a minor but distracting clarity issue.

Furthermore, the paper’s language and presentation are notably poor. Numerous typos and awkward phrasing impede understanding. While the experiments demonstrate some improvements, the analysis could be more rigorous. The discussion of computational cost feels superficial. The authors claim only a factor of 2-3 increase in runtime, yet the details of the optimization algorithm and the computational impact of the Laplacian penalty are not fully explored. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging.
* Online learning approach enables scalability to large datasets.
* Demonstrates some improvements over existing methods on HCP data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Weak novelty: primarily an extension of existing work.
* Unclear motivation for Sobolev regularization.
* Technical errors and lack of justification in the method description.
* Poor writing quality and presentation.



**Overall, I recommend rejection.** The paper presents an idea with some potential, but the lack of strong novelty, unclear motivation, technical issues, and poor presentation significantly detract from its quality. Substantial revisions, addressing the points outlined above, would be needed for reconsideration.
