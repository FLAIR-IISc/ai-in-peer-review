This paper proposes a novel approach to dictionary learning for functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) data, combining Sobolev (smoothness) and L1 (sparsity) regularization within the dictionary learning framework. The method, dubbed Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), aims to extract structured and sparse components from fMRI data, which can be used to define brain regions for statistical analysis.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of existing methods and the need for a new approach. The proposed method is an extension of the online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010), and the authors demonstrate its ability to extract structured and denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability in small, medium, and large-scale regimes.

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of the proposed method, including the optimization algorithm and the choice of hyper-parameters. The experimental results are also well-presented, and the authors provide a comparison with state-of-the-art methods, including Canonical ICA (CanICA) and sparse online dictionary-learning (SODL).

However, the paper has some limitations. The experimental improvements over simpler methods are minor or unclear, and the authors do not provide a sufficient explanation or analysis for the observed (lack of) performance gains. Additionally, the manuscript seems rushed and contains draft comments, which can make the reading tedious and sometimes confusing.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel combination of Sobolev and L1 regularization within the dictionary learning framework, which is an interesting and non-trivial extension of existing methods. However, the paper does not provide a clear explanation of how this combination improves the performance of the method, and the authors do not discuss the potential limitations of this approach.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in neuro-imaging, and the proposed method has the potential to be used in a variety of applications, including brain region definition and statistical analysis. However, the paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the proposed method advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, and the authors do not discuss the potential impact of their method on the field.

Overall, I would give this paper a score of 6 out of 10. While the paper proposes an interesting and novel approach to dictionary learning for fMRI data, it has some limitations, including minor or unclear experimental improvements and a lack of clear explanation or analysis for the observed performance gains. Additionally, the manuscript could benefit from a more thorough editing to improve clarity and readability.
