This paper proposes a novel multivariate online dictionary learning method, Smooth-SODL, for decomposing brain images with structured and sparse components. The key contribution lies in extending the existing online dictionary learning framework [Mairal et al., 2010] by incorporating a Sobolev penalty to enforce spatial smoothness and compactness in the learned dictionary atoms, effectively representing brain regions as “blobs” rather than scattered activations. The authors motivate this approach by arguing that existing methods often discard inter-subject variability, which contains valuable information, and that principled region-of-interest (ROI) definition remains a challenge in neuroimaging.

The paper builds on a rich body of work in dictionary learning, sparse coding, and neuroimaging. The connection to online dictionary learning [Mairal et al., 2010; Mensch, 2016] is clearly established, and the authors rightly position their work within the broader context of techniques like PCA, ICA [Beckmann & Smith, 2009], and clustering for brain image analysis [Hibar, 2013; Varoquaux et al., 2013]. The use of spatial regularization via Laplacian penalties draws parallels to existing approaches utilizing Total Variation (TV) regularization [Abraham, 2016; Dohmatob, 2016], however, the authors present a computationally efficient online learning framework that addresses the scalability issues associated with such methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound, building upon a well-established framework. The extension to incorporate the Sobolev prior appears logically motivated and the algorithmic description (BCD with FISTA updates) is reasonable. The online learning approach is crucial for handling the large-scale datasets common in neuroimaging.
* **Novelty:**  The combination of online dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior for spatially structured decomposition represents a novel contribution. It provides a scalable alternative to existing methods that struggle with computational cost when enforcing spatial smoothness.
* **Potential Significance:**  The proposed method has the potential to be a valuable tool for neuroscientists, offering a data-driven approach to ROI definition and capturing inter-subject variability.  The ability to extract interpretable, spatially localized features could be beneficial for various applications, including image segmentation and disease characterization.
* **Scalability:** The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to arbitrarily large datasets, which is a critical advantage in neuroimaging.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Empirical Evaluation:** The initial empirical evaluation was weak and did not convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the Sobolev prior. Figure 2 lacked quantitative comparisons and a clear visual demonstration of improved “structure” in the learned dictionaries. The claim of improved interpretability felt subjective without concrete metrics.
* **Presentation & Clarity:** The manuscript suffered from several issues related to presentation and clarity. There were numerous typos, inconsistencies in notation (particularly concerning matrix transposes and signs in equations 6 and 8), and occasionally confusing descriptions. These issues hampered understanding.
* **Missing Details:** Several algorithmic details were lacking, particularly the explicit derivation of the solution to Equation (6). While mentioning its relation to GraphNet [Grosenick et al., 2015], it would benefit from a more detailed explanation or inclusion of the derivation in the appendix.
* **Figure 2 limitations**: Presenting different slices for different methods in Figure 2 makes direct visual comparison difficult. Moreover, the assertion of improved “structure” requires quantitative support.
* **Figure 3 ambiguities**: The figure lacks a clear description of what is being predicted. The use of `U_i` in the prediction task isn’t explained. The inclusion of PCA as a baseline is missing from the legend, and the claim of R^2 > 0.1 is vague.
* **Reference Completeness:** The reference to the Human Connectome Project dataset [Van Essen et al., 2013] was missing.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging: principled ROI definition.
* Presents a novel and technically sound approach.
* Offers a scalable solution for large-scale datasets.
* The rebuttal provided additional experimental results and clarifications that improved the paper’s quality.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Initial empirical evaluation was unconvincing.
* Manuscript suffered from significant clarity and presentation issues.
* Algorithmic details were incomplete.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging, but its initial quality was hindered by weaknesses in empirical evaluation and presentation. The authors’ responsiveness to the review and the additional results provided in the rebuttal were encouraging. The clarification on the smoothness parameter selection and additional quantitative results regarding explainability were particularly helpful.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend *acceptance with minor revisions*. The authors should address the following:

1. **Incorporate the additional results from the rebuttal into the main manuscript.**
2. **Thoroughly proofread and edit the manuscript** to eliminate typos, inconsistencies in notation, and improve clarity.
3. **Provide the explicit mathematical solution/derivation for Equation (6)** (e.g., in an appendix).
4. **Revise Figure 2** to show consistent slices for all methods, enabling a fair visual comparison. Include quantitative metrics to support the claim of improved dictionary structure (e.g., smoothness measures).
5. **Clarify Figure 3**: Provide a clear description of the prediction task, explain the use of `U_i`, and ensure a complete legend.
6. **Add the missing reference** to the Human Connectome Project dataset [Van Essen et al., 2013].



Addressing these points will significantly enhance the clarity, completeness, and impact of the paper.
