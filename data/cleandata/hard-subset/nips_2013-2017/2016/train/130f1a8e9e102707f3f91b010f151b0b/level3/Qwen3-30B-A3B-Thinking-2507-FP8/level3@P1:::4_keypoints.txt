- Proposal: Smooth-SODL extends online dictionary learning with L1 sparsity plus a Laplacian spatial smoothness penalty to extract structured, compact ROIs from fMRI.
- Technical contribution: integrates spatial smoothness into online DL via a block-coordinate descent algorithm that retains efficiency (≈2–3× slowdown vs SODL).
- Relationship to prior work: builds on structured/multi-subject dictionary learning (e.g., Varoquaux) but uses online optimization for scalability.
- Strengths: well-motivated ROI problem, technically sound algorithm with convergence guarantees, and scalable implementation.
- Experimental validation: thorough experiments on a large HCP dataset showing more interpretable maps and improved stability vs SODL and CanICA.
- Quantitative gains are modest: small improvements in explained variance (55% vs 50%) and prediction performance.
- Small-sample claim is misleading: reported "14-fold" gain compares extreme small-sample numbers and diminishes with more data, indicating benefits mainly for small datasets.
- Theoretical gap: lacks principled analysis of why spatial regularization helps and no rigorous study of hyperparameter interactions or the claimed "safe range."
- Clarity and presentation: manuscript is poorly organized, contains draft comments, and presents algorithmic details confusingly.
- Incomplete relation to prior work: does not clearly articulate differences from existing structured dictionary learning approaches.
- Originality: combination is novel, but spatial regularization is not new in neuroimaging, limiting novelty.
- Significance: contribution appears incremental rather than transformative given modest empirical gains.
- Pros for acceptance: addresses an important neuroimaging problem, is technically sound, scalable, and produces interpretable maps.
- Cons for acceptance: weak empirical improvements, insufficient theory, poor writing, and overstated claims.
- Overall: technically competent but undermined by marginal gains, theoretical shortcomings, and poor presentation; not sufficiently strong in current form for NIPS.
