## Review of “Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning”

This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method with combined L1 and Sobolev regularization to decompose brain images, aiming to extract structured, spatially localized functional components. The motivation stems from the need to define Regions of Interest (ROIs) in neuroimaging data in a principled way, leveraging inter-subject variability which is often discarded. The approach extends existing online dictionary learning work by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to encourage smoothness in the learned dictionary atoms. While the idea of combining sparsity and smoothness regularization for brain image decomposition is promising, the paper suffers from a lack of convincing experimental results and clarity in presentation.

The core contribution of this work lies in the non-trivial extension of Mairal et al. (2010) by adding a Sobolev penalty. This combination aims to address limitations of both purely sparse and purely smooth representations, potentially offering improved interpretability and capturing of meaningful brain structures. This builds upon a recognized need for better ROI definition in neuroimaging, as highlighted in the introduction, and connects to related work in clustering and dimensionality reduction techniques like PCA and ICA commonly used in fMRI analysis (e.g., Beckmann & Smith, 2009). The online learning aspect is also valuable, addressing scalability concerns with large datasets, a common issue in neuroimaging.

However, the claimed improvements are modest and, in some cases, not clearly demonstrated. While the authors report a gain in explained variance early in the learning curve, the ultimate performance on test data appears comparable to existing methods like CanICA. The qualitative comparisons (Figure 2a) suggest visual improvements in smoothness, but a rigorous quantification of this improvement is missing. The experiments conducted on the HCP dataset are a reasonable choice, but the analysis feels incomplete. For instance, the authors acknowledge the potential overfitting of RAW models but don't explore this in sufficient detail with respect to their proposed method.  The hyperparameter selection discussion (section 3.2) is convoluted and doesn’t offer clear guidance for practical application. The claim of a 14-fold gain in explained variance at the beginning of training (paragraph starting “Is spatial regularization really needed?”) is significant but lacks sufficient context. How much data was used to reach this point and is this improvement sustained?

A significant weakness is the presentation. The manuscript reads as rushed and contains remnants of draft comments. The writing is often dense and repetitive, making it challenging to follow the arguments. The algorithm description (section 2 and 3) is highly technical and requires substantial effort from the reader to fully grasp. Equations are presented without sufficient explanation, and the connection between the mathematical formulation and the biological interpretation is sometimes unclear. The discussion of convergence is brief and relies on referencing previous work without providing sufficient detail for independent verification. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The idea of combining sparsity and smoothness regularization is novel and potentially useful for brain image decomposition.
*   The online learning approach addresses scalability concerns relevant to large neuroimaging datasets.
*   The authors provide an implementation as part of the Nilearn package, which could be beneficial to the community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The manuscript lacks clarity and suffers from poor writing quality, making it difficult to understand and evaluate.
*   The analysis of results is superficial and lacks depth.
*   The hyperparameter tuning discussion is unclear and lacks practical guidance.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the core idea is interesting, the limited experimental evidence and the lack of clarity in presentation outweigh the potential benefits. The paper requires significant revision, including a more thorough experimental evaluation, a more lucid explanation of the method, and careful editing to address the numerous writing issues. Without these changes, the paper does not meet the quality standards for NIPS. The authors should focus on providing more compelling evidence of the benefits of their approach and improving the clarity and accessibility of their presentation.
