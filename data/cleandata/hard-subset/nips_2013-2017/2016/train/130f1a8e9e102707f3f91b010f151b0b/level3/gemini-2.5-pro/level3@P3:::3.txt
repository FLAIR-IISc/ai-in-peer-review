This paper proposes a method for learning sparse and spatially structured component maps from collections of brain images. The goal is to identify brain regions of interest from population data in a data-driven way. The authors extend the well-known online sparse dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by adding a Sobolev (Laplacian) prior to the objective function. This prior penalizes the spatial gradients of the dictionary atoms, encouraging them to be smooth and form compact "blobs" rather than scattered patterns. The resulting algorithm, "Smooth-SODL," retains the scalability of the original online method, with only a modest increase in computational cost. The authors apply their method to a large task-fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (HCP) and compare it to standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA (CanICA).

**Strengths:**
*   The core idea of integrating a spatial structure prior into a scalable online dictionary learning framework is a valuable contribution. Many existing methods for structured decomposition are computationally expensive and do not scale to massive datasets like the HCP, whereas this approach remains efficient.
*   The paper presents a clear and technically sound extension of an established algorithm. The derivation of the update steps is well-explained, and the authors correctly identify the computational trade-offs. The fact that the added complexity only results in a 2-3x increase in runtime is a strong practical advantage.
*   The application to a large, contemporary, and publicly available dataset (HCP) is a definite plus, making the work relevant to the neuroimaging community.
*   The authors provide an analysis of the benefit of the smoothness prior in a low-data regime (Table 1), which is a good piece of evidence supporting their claim that spatial regularization is particularly useful when data is scarce.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness of the paper lies in its experimental evaluation, which does not provide clear and compelling evidence for the superiority of the proposed model.

1.  **Subjective and Unconvincing Validation:** The main evidence for the model's benefit comes from a qualitative visual comparison of the learned dictionary atoms (Figure 2a). The authors claim their method produces more structured and interpretable "blobs". However, to an untrained eye, the visual difference between the results of SODL and Smooth-SODL is not striking. While the components from Smooth-SODL are visibly smoother, this is an unsurprising and direct consequence of adding a smoothness penalty; it does not, by itself, prove that these components are a better or more accurate representation of the underlying neural organization. The validation feels somewhat circular: a smoothness prior is added, and the result is praised for being smoother.

2.  **Quantitative Evaluation is Ambiguous:** The quantitative results do not clearly demonstrate a benefit. In terms of explained variance (Fig 2b), Smooth-SODL is better than SODL but significantly worse than CanICA. In the behavioral prediction task (Fig 2c), it again outperforms SODL but is generally outperformed by CanICA. The authors argue that CanICA components are dense and uninterpretable, which is a fair point, but this does not excuse the need for a more robust quantitative validation of the proposed method's own merits beyond simply being better than an unstructured sparse model.

3.  **Lack of Inter-Subject Consistency as a Validation Metric:** The paper is motivated by the idea of defining brain regions by modeling variability across a population. A powerful way to validate such a model would be to assess the consistency of the learned components across different subjects or subgroups. For instance, do the learned dictionaries generalize well to held-out subjects? Can the learned "blobs" be reliably identified in new individuals? The paper reports a "stability" metric (mean correlation between atoms across data splits), where Smooth-SODL (0.4) improves upon SODL (0.1), which is promising. However, this result is mentioned only briefly and is not a central part of the evaluation. A more thorough analysis of inter-subject consistency would provide much stronger evidence than the current subjective visual inspection.

4.  **Necessity of the Prior on Large, High-Quality Data:** The paper uses the HCP dataset, which is known for its high quality and large number of subjects. With such abundant data, the problem is arguably less about data scarcity and noise (which spatial smoothing is excellent at combating) and more about modeling complex, subtle variations. It is possible that with enough data, an unstructured model like SODL could converge to similarly smooth components if they represent the true underlying signal. The authors' own learning curve analysis (Table 1) shows that the gap between SODL and Smooth-SODL narrows as the number of subjects increases, supporting this point. While valuable for smaller datasets, the impact of the Sobolev prior in this large-scale setting is not clearly established as being critical.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithmic contribution is clear. However, the experimental section could be improved with a clearer justification for the chosen metrics and a more critical interpretation of the results.

**Originality:** The combination of a Laplacian/Sobolev prior with the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. is a novel contribution, particularly in its application to large-scale neuroimaging data.

**Significance:** The work addresses an important and timely problem: scalable, data-driven discovery of functional brain organization. The proposed method has the potential to be very useful. However, its significance is currently limited by the weak experimental validation. Without a more convincing demonstration of its benefits over simpler alternatives, it is difficult to judge the practical impact of the contribution.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and technically sound idea for scalable structured dictionary learning. The main shortcoming is an evaluation that relies too heavily on subjective visual assessment and provides ambiguous quantitative results. To be a strong contribution, the paper would need to provide a more rigorous and objective validation of why the smoother components produced by the model represent a more meaningful decomposition of the brain data, for instance by demonstrating improved consistency across subjects or better performance on a more neuroscientifically relevant downstream task.
