This paper proposes a novel approach to functional MRI (fMRI) data analysis – Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL) – combining Sobolev (smoothness) regularization with L1 (sparsity) regularization within an online dictionary learning framework. The goal is to extract interpretable, spatially localized functional components (brain regions) from fMRI data, accounting for inter-subject variability. While the idea of combining these regularization techniques is interesting and potentially valuable, the paper suffers from several weaknesses that significantly detract from its overall contribution.

The core novelty lies in the combination of Sobolev and L1 regularization. Existing work, such as Mairal et al. (2010), has established online dictionary learning as an efficient method for large-scale data decomposition. The authors build upon this by introducing a Laplacian penalty to encourage smoothness in the learned dictionary atoms, complementing the sparsity induced by the L1 norm. This is a sensible extension, aiming to address a limitation of standard dictionary learning which can produce noisy, fragmented representations. Related work in spatially structured decomposition, like the TV-L1 approach in Abraham (2013) and Varoquaux et al. (2016), is acknowledged, but the authors position their work as offering a computationally efficient alternative to slower, alternating optimization methods. They also connect their approach to existing methods like ICA and PCA, framing it as a constrained linear decomposition problem.

However, the empirical validation of this approach is unconvincing. While the authors demonstrate improvements over standard SODL and CanICA on certain metrics (explained variance, reproducibility, prediction of behavioral scores), these improvements are often minor and, crucially, lack a thorough analysis. The claim that Smooth-SODL "better captures inter-subject variability" is not convincingly demonstrated. The authors present learning curves showing an initial advantage in explained variance, but the gap closes as more data is used, suggesting the benefit diminishes with larger datasets, a critical point not sufficiently discussed. The performance on behavioral prediction is better than SODL, but comparable to CanICA, raising questions about the added value of the spatial regularization.  Furthermore, the presented results lack statistical significance testing, making it difficult to determine if the observed improvements are genuine or due to chance.

A major weakness is the insufficient explanation for the observed (lack of) performance gains. The authors mention the need to carefully select hyperparameters (α, γ, τ), but the rationale behind the chosen values and their impact on the results is not fully explored.  The discussion of the theoretical conditions for convergence and the impact of the Laplacian regularization parameter γ (equation 9) feels incomplete and potentially obscures crucial limitations. Why does the benefit of spatial regularization diminish with more data? What are the trade-offs between sparsity and smoothness, and how do they affect the interpretability of the learned components? These questions remain largely unanswered.

Beyond the technical shortcomings, the manuscript suffers from significant clarity issues. The writing feels rushed and contains draft-like comments (e.g., “line 146”). The organization is sometimes convoluted, and the sheer density of information makes it difficult to follow the key arguments.  The frequent use of abbreviations without initial definition (e.g., ROI, HCP) further adds to the confusion. The presentation of results in multiple figures (particularly Fig. 2) with extensive annotations makes it difficult to quickly grasp the main findings.  A more streamlined and carefully edited manuscript would significantly improve its readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of Sobolev and L1 regularization within an online dictionary learning framework.
*   Potential for improved interpretability of learned functional components.
*   Scalability to large datasets due to the online learning approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor or unclear experimental improvements over simpler methods.
*   Insufficient analysis and explanation for the observed (lack of) performance gains.
*   Poor clarity and organization of the manuscript; reading is tedious.
*   Lack of statistical significance testing.
*   Draft-like comments and inconsistencies in the writing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea, but its execution and presentation are significantly flawed. The empirical results do not strongly support the claims of improved performance, and the lack of thorough analysis leaves many critical questions unanswered. The manuscript's clarity issues further detract from its overall quality.  While the concept has merit, it requires substantial revision and further validation before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the identified weaknesses. The authors should focus on providing a more compelling empirical evaluation, a deeper theoretical analysis, and a significantly improved manuscript clarity.
