## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning framework incorporating a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially smooth and compact dictionary atoms, intended for identifying interesting brain regions from fMRI data. The motivation – leveraging inter-subject variability to define principled Regions of Interest (ROIs) – is well-articulated and relevant to current challenges in neuroimaging. Extending the existing online dictionary learning work of Mairal et al. (2010) with a structural penalty is a promising direction, given the limitations of purely data-driven or anatomically-defined ROIs. The authors correctly position their work within the context of existing methods like ICA/PCA, clustering, and prior approaches using spatial penalties.

The primary strength of this work lies in its potential scalability. Building upon online learning allows the method to handle the high dimensionality and large datasets common in fMRI analysis, a significant advantage over batch methods. The experiments on the HCP dataset demonstrate the feasibility of the approach and show promising results in terms of interpretability and capturing inter-subject variability. The comparison with CanICA, SODL, and even raw data prediction highlights the benefits of the proposed structured sparsity.

However, a critical weakness is the lack of rigorous theoretical analysis. While the authors acknowledge the BCD algorithm's convergence guarantees based on prior work, there’s no discussion of the theoretical properties of the proposed Laplacian penalty – how it affects the recovery of underlying structures, or the conditions under which it guarantees sparsity and smoothness simultaneously. The impact of the regularization parameter γ is discussed empirically, but a theoretical understanding of its optimal setting would be valuable. Furthermore, the claim of only a “factor of 2 or 3” increase in running time compared to Mairal et al. (2010) feels understated given the added complexity of the Laplacian penalty and requires more detailed justification.

The manuscript quality is also concerning. Numerous careless writing errors and remnants of editorial comments ("146 XXX: I don't understand...") suggest insufficient proofreading. While the ideas are valuable, the presentation detracts from the paper's overall impact. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in neuroimaging – principled ROI definition.
*   Scalable online learning approach suitable for large fMRI datasets.
*   Promising empirical results demonstrating improved interpretability and performance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of theoretical analysis regarding the properties of the proposed method.
*   Poor manuscript quality and lack of thorough editing.
*   Limited justification for the claimed computational cost.



**Overall Assessment:** I rate this paper as **weak reject**. The core idea is interesting and has potential, but the lack of theoretical grounding and the poor manuscript quality prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. Addressing the theoretical concerns and significantly improving the writing would be necessary for a future submission.
