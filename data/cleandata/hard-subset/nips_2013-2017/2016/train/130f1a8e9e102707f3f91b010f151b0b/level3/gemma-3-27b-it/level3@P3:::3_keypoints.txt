- Proposes adding a Sobolev (Laplacian) prior to online dictionary learning to produce spatially smoother, more interpretable fMRI dictionary atoms.  
- Core technical contribution: adapts online dictionary learning to include a Laplacian penalty with modest computational overhead (reported 2–3× slower).  
- Novelty: integrating spatial regularization into scalable online dictionary learning is an interesting and useful extension for neuroimaging.  
- Scalability: retains online approach, making the method applicable to large datasets like the Human Connectome Project.  
- Computational efficiency: reported modest runtime increase compared to baseline online dictionary learning.  
- Presentation: paper is generally well written with a clear mathematical formulation.  
- Primary evaluation is subjective, relying on visual inspection of learned atoms, which limits interpretability and reproducibility.  
- Quantitative results (explained variance, behavioral prediction) are limited and not convincingly demonstrating clear benefits.  
- Data dependence: benefits may be marginal with high-quality large datasets; smoothing may not solve fundamental data limitations.  
- Lack of inter-subject consolidation: evaluation focuses on individual decompositions instead of consistency across subjects, weakening validation.  
- Some claims (e.g., "14-fold gain" in early training) are potentially overstated given low absolute explained variance.  
- Related work is cited appropriately but the related work section could include more recent advances in spatially regularized dictionary learning for neuroimaging.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel extension, scalable to large datasets, and clear theoretical grounding.  
- Rejection arguments: subjective evaluation, insufficient quantitative evidence, risk of masking data characteristics, and inadequate treatment of inter-subject variability.  
- Recommendation: Reject in current form; authors should provide objective metrics, cross-subject consistency analysis, thorough hyperparameter ablation, and stronger quantitative comparisons to state-of-the-art.
