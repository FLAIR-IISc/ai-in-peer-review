This paper proposes a method for learning spatially structured dictionary atoms from neuroimaging data, specifically fMRI activation maps. The key contribution is an extension of the online sparse dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) to include a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty. This penalty encourages the learned atoms to be spatially smooth and contiguous, forming "blobs" that are more interpretable as brain regions of interest (ROIs) compared to the scattered patterns often produced by standard sparse models. The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to the large datasets common in modern neuroimaging, such as the Human Connectome Project (HCP). The authors evaluate their method, named Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA (CanICA) on a large fMRI dataset.

**Quality**

The paper's technical approach is built upon a solid and well-established framework. The extension to include a Laplacian penalty is a reasonable and principled way to enforce spatial smoothness. The block-coordinate descent optimization strategy appears technically sound.

However, the empirical support presented in the initial manuscript was weak. The central claim is that the Sobolev prior leads to more structured and meaningful dictionary atoms, yet the paper did not provide a clear, quantitative demonstration of this advantage. The visual evidence in Figure 2 was unconvincing, and the performance benefits in the prediction task were modest. The additional results provided during the author response phase were informative and helped clarify the benefits of the proposed regularization, particularly in low-data regimes. Including these results in a revised manuscript is essential to adequately support the paper's claims.

There are also several technical and notational issues that detract from the paper's quality and need to be addressed:
*   In Equation (1), the dimensions seem inconsistent. `Xi` is described as a p-dimensional row vector, yet the product `VUi` (with V in R^(p x k) and Ui in R^(k x 1)) results in a p-dimensional column vector. Please clarify if it should be `Xi^T ≈ VUi`.
*   Please double-check the updates in Equation (8). The term `(Bjt −VAj)` seems to be missing a transpose on the code matrix `U`. Based on the definition of `Bt` and `At`, the update might be expected to involve terms like `(B_t - V A_t)`. Please verify the derivation and correctness of this equation.
*   The derivation for the code update in Equation (6) is standard but should be made explicit, perhaps in an appendix, for completeness.

**Clarity**

The manuscript suffers from significant clarity issues that impede understanding. It is in need of careful proofreading and editing. There are numerous typos, grammatical errors, and awkwardly phrased sentences.
*   Figure 2a is difficult to interpret because different anatomical slices are shown for different methods, preventing a direct comparison of the resulting atoms. The claim that Smooth-SODL atoms are more "structured" is subjective and not visually obvious from the provided images.
*   Figure 2c (originally Figure 3) is confusing. The caption does not clearly state what behavioral variables are being predicted or what features are used for prediction (presumably the learned codes `Ui`?). The legend is incomplete (PCA is mentioned in the text but missing from the plot legend), and the claim that "only variables predicted with a positive mean... R-score are reported" is ambiguous.
*   Specific examples of typos and awkward phrasing include: "intepretable" (interpretable), "can imposes" (can impose), "out-performs all others algorithms" (outperforms all other algorithms), and duplicated words ("a a positive mean").

**Originality**

The main original contribution is the integration of a Sobolev prior into a scalable online dictionary learning algorithm for neuroimaging. While online dictionary learning and structured sparsity are not new concepts in themselves, their combination in this manner to create a fast and scalable algorithm for learning structured brain maps is novel. The paper clearly situates its contribution relative to previous work, such as Mairal et al. (2010) for the online framework and Varoquaux et al. (2011, 2015) for structured decompositions, but it was missing a proper citation for the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset [20].

**Significance**

The proposed method has the potential to be a useful tool for researchers in neuroimaging and medical imaging. The problem of defining meaningful functional regions in a data-driven way is of high importance, and a scalable algorithm that produces structured, interpretable components is a valuable contribution. If proven effective, the approach could be widely adopted for ROI definition and dimensionality reduction. Furthermore, the general idea of scalable learning of spatially structured features could be applicable to other domains involving regional sensing or image segmentation.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated combination of online dictionary learning and a Sobolev prior to learn spatially structured components.
*   The method is scalable, addressing a key challenge in large-scale neuroimaging data analysis.
*   The problem it addresses—learning data-driven brain atlases—is significant for the neuroimaging community.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The paper is poorly written, with numerous typos and clarity issues that make it difficult to follow.
*   Initial empirical evaluation was weak and did not provide clear quantitative evidence for the superiority of the proposed spatial regularization.
*   The figures are not well-designed for clear comparison and lack sufficient detail in their captions.
*   There are several notational inconsistencies and potential typos in the mathematical formulations.

**Recommendation**

This paper presents a promising idea with potential for significant impact. However, the manuscript in its current form is not ready for publication due to major issues with clarity and the weakness of the empirical evidence presented. Based on the additional results provided in the rebuttal, the technical contribution appears more solid. I would be willing to raise my score if the authors thoroughly revise the manuscript to address the issues listed above, in particular by:
1.  Integrating the new empirical results from the rebuttal into the main paper.
2.  Providing a quantitative, neuroscience-justified metric to demonstrate that the learned atoms are indeed more "structured".
3.  Completely rewriting and proofreading the text for clarity, grammar, and typos.
4.  Correcting all mathematical and notational errors.
5.  Remaking the figures to allow for fair, direct comparisons between methods.
