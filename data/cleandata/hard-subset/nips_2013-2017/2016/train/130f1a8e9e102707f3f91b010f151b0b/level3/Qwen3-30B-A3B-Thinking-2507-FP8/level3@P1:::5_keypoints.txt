- Proposal: Smooth-SODL extends Mairal et al.'s online dictionary learning by adding a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce spatially structured, sparse atoms for fMRI data.  
- Goal: Extract interpretable brain regions by leveraging inter-subject variability, claiming only 2–3× slower runtime with improved interpretability.  
- Strong empirical validation on HCP fMRI data demonstrating clearer dictionary interpretability and better small-data performance.  
- Learning curves show Smooth-SODL greatly outperforms SODL in limited-data regimes (241 subjects: 31% vs 2% explained variance).  
- Practical contribution: implementation provided in Nilearn and comparisons to CanICA and SODL across stability, reproducibility, and behavioral prediction.  
- Core novelty is limited: adding a Laplacian penalty to SODL is a straightforward modification, not a major methodological advance.  
- Prior work inadequately cited/distinguished — similar structured dictionary learning (e.g., TV regularization) addresses spatial structure albeit with higher cost.  
- Justification for the Laplacian penalty is weak and "structured" is not rigorously defined beyond vague terms like "blobs" or "piece-wise smooth."  
- The switch from l1 to l2 penalty on codes (α‖Ui‖₂² vs α‖Ui‖₁) is unexplained and unsupported by evidence that l2 is preferable for brain imaging.  
- Technical/notation errors: Eq. (1) likely missing a transpose on V; ambiguous references (e.g., "line 146") and unclear derivations (Eq. 7, simplex constraint).  
- Writing and presentation issues: multiple typos and sections lacking clarity impede understanding.  
- Significance is modest: method is technically sound but incremental, scalability claims are not new, and runtime overhead (≈3×) is non-trivial for large-scale use.  
- Behavioral prediction gains are marginal and CanICA still outperforms Smooth-SODL in that task.  
- Overall recommendation: useful practical tool for brain imaging with interpretable benefits, but insufficient novelty and rigor for a top ML venue; more appropriate for neuroscience/medical imaging venues.
