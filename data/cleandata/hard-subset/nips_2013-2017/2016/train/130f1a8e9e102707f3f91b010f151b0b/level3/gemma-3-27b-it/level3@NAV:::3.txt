## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes extending online dictionary learning with a Sobolev prior to improve brain region identification from fMRI data. The core idea – leveraging both sparsity and smoothness to delineate brain structures – is intuitively appealing and builds upon prior work in dictionary learning for neuroimaging, such as Mairal et al. (2010) and more recent efforts utilizing spatial regularization (e.g., Abraham, 2016). The authors aim to address the challenge of defining regions of interest (ROIs) in a data-driven manner, moving beyond traditional segmentation or reliance on pre-defined atlases. 

The paper demonstrates a mathematically sound approach to incorporating the Sobolev prior within an online learning framework, building upon existing algorithms. The inclusion of practical considerations like hyperparameter tuning and initialization are also positive aspects. However, the strength of the improvement offered by the proposed method remains unclear. The primary results rely heavily on visual comparisons of “blobs”, which are subjective and difficult to assess without specialized neuroimaging knowledge. While the authors attempt to quantify performance through explained variance and prediction of behavioral scores, these metrics don’t definitively demonstrate a substantial benefit over existing methods like CanICA, particularly given the large dataset size. 

A significant weakness lies in the validation strategy. With the Human Connectome Project dataset, the paper doesn’t convincingly argue that smoothing (enforced by the Sobolev prior) is addressing a true data scarcity issue. The reviewer expected to see more rigorous validation, specifically, consolidation of identified brain regions across subjects, and perhaps the use of inter-subject consistency as a measure of reliability. Simply showing that the resulting “blobs” *look* more structured doesn’t constitute compelling evidence of improved performance. The comparison to a hard-thresholded CanICA further complicates the interpretation, as this is arguably an unfair comparison given the inherent differences in modeling assumptions. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound extension of online dictionary learning.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging – data-driven ROI definition.
*   Practical considerations included (hyperparameter tuning, implementation details).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited evidence of a significant improvement over existing methods.
*   Subjective evaluation based on visual inspection.
*   Insufficient validation demonstrating the benefit of the proposed approach.
*   The significance is unclear, particularly given the large dataset used.



In its current form, this paper feels incomplete. While the technical contribution is solid, the lack of robust validation and clear demonstration of significance prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. I suggest the authors focus on more quantitative and reproducible validation strategies to bolster their claims.
