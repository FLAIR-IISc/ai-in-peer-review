

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a novel extension of online dictionary learning for brain imaging applications, introducing a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms. The authors extend the SODL framework (Mairal et al., 2010) to incorporate structured sparsity, arguing that this better captures brain regions as spatially compact, blob-like patterns rather than scattered activations. The method is evaluated on HCP task fMRI data, with comparisons to standard SODL and CanICA. The approach is positioned as addressing the problem of defining brain regions in a principled way using inter-subject variability, rather than relying on anatomical segmentation or manual ROI definition.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging where spatially structured representations are desirable for interpretability. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the method across multiple metrics (visual quality, explained variance, stability, prediction performance) and demonstrating clear improvements over SODL in terms of spatial structure and interpretability. The authors show that their method outperforms competitors in small-to-medium data regimes, which is particularly relevant for neuroimaging where data collection is expensive. The computational efficiency claim (3x slowdown compared to SODL) is reasonable and well-supported by empirical results.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness is the lack of theoretical analysis to support the proposed approach. While the paper presents a well-motivated heuristic for combining sparsity and spatial structure, it fails to provide any theoretical guarantees about convergence, optimality, or how the regularization parameters affect the solution. The authors claim their method "non-trivially extends" the work of Mairal et al., but without theoretical analysis to demonstrate why their structured approach is superior to standard methods in a principled way, this claim remains unsubstantiated.

The manuscript quality is poor, with numerous writing issues that undermine credibility. The text contains numerous grammatical errors, inconsistent notation (e.g., switching between V and v), and leftover reviewer comments (e.g., "146 XXX: I don't understand the switch between V and v"). These issues suggest the authors did not carefully proofread the manuscript, which is particularly problematic for a scientific paper where precise notation is essential.

The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of how the Laplacian penalty specifically relates to brain anatomy. While the authors note that the resulting atoms resemble known functional brain organization, they do not provide evidence that the Laplacian penalty is the optimal choice for this particular problem. Alternative spatial regularization approaches (e.g., total variation) are mentioned in the related work but not compared or justified.

**Quality:**
The paper demonstrates good experimental quality with appropriate comparisons and metrics, but the lack of theoretical analysis significantly weakens the technical contribution. The algorithm is well-described and appears to work as claimed, but without theoretical justification, it's difficult to assess whether the method is fundamentally sound or merely empirically effective.

**Clarity:**
The clarity is compromised by poor writing and numerous editing errors. Key concepts like the Laplacian penalty and its relationship to brain structure are not explained with sufficient precision. The manuscript would benefit from a thorough rewrite to address the numerous grammatical issues, inconsistent notation, and unclear explanations.

**Originality:**
The combination of online dictionary learning with spatial structure is somewhat novel in the context of brain imaging. The authors properly cite related work, including Varoquaux's work on structured dictionary learning for brain imaging. However, the novelty is somewhat limited by the fact that the Laplacian penalty for spatial smoothness is a standard technique in image processing, and the extension to online learning is relatively straightforward.

**Significance:**
The problem addressed is significant for neuroimaging, as better methods for identifying spatially structured brain regions could improve the interpretability of functional imaging data. However, the significance of the specific technical contribution is limited by the lack of theoretical analysis and the fact that the method is primarily an extension of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging with a method that shows promising empirical results. However, the lack of theoretical analysis and poor manuscript quality significantly undermine the contribution. The technical contribution appears to be primarily an empirical extension of existing techniques rather than a fundamental advance. While the results are interesting and the topic is relevant, the paper would benefit from a thorough theoretical analysis and careful proofreading before publication.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the topic is valuable and the experiments are well-executed, the lack of theoretical analysis and poor manuscript quality make this paper unsuitable for publication at NIPS. The authors should either provide a theoretical justification for their approach or substantially revise the manuscript to address the numerous writing and clarity issues before resubmission.
