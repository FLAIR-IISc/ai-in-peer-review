**Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"**

**Summary**

This paper proposes a method for learning functional brain regions from large-scale fMRI datasets. The approach is based on an online dictionary learning framework, where the goal is to decompose a collection of subject-level brain maps into a set of shared spatial components (dictionary atoms) and subject-specific loadings. The main technical contribution is the incorporation of a structured penalty on the dictionary atoms, combining an l1-norm constraint to enforce sparsity with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage spatial smoothness and contiguity. This extends the online dictionary learning algorithm of Mairal et al. (2010) to learn atoms that are not just sparse but also spatially structured, resembling blobs, which is desirable for neuroscientific interpretability. The method is evaluated on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project, where it is compared against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a multi-subject ICA-based method (CanICA).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound. Extending the online dictionary learning framework with a Laplacian penalty is a valid and well-motivated way to enforce spatial structure. The derivation of the block-coordinate descent update steps appears correct. However, the experimental support for the method's superiority is mixed. While the paper demonstrates a clear and significant advantage in low-data regimes (Table 1), the improvements in the large-data setting are less pronounced. For instance, in terms of explained variance and predictive power for behavioral variables (Fig. 2b, 2c), the proposed Smooth-SODL offers only a modest improvement over the unstructured SODL baseline and is outperformed by the dense CanICA model. The paper lacks a deep discussion of these trade-offs. For example, why does enforcing a neuroscientifically plausible structure not lead to better predictive performance? A more thorough analysis of the results is needed to turn this from a description of a new model into a complete scientific contribution.

**Clarity:** The manuscript's clarity is a significant weakness. It appears to have been written in a hurry and would benefit from substantial editing. There are several typos (e.g., "comput" instead of "compute" in Section 2) and awkward phrasings that make it tedious to read. The algorithmic description relies heavily on referring the reader to external work (e.g., "[16] for the details" inside an equation block), which hinders self-containment and reproducibility. The presentation of the experimental results is adequate, but the accompanying discussion and interpretation are too superficial. For example, the claim of superiority in "large-scale regimes alike" in the abstract and conclusion is not fully supported by the experiments, which show the method's main advantage is in the small-to-medium data regime, with performance converging with the baseline as the dataset size grows.

**Originality:** The core idea of combining sparsity and spatial smoothness penalties for matrix decomposition in neuroimaging is not new (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2011; Abraham et al., 2013). However, the main novelty here lies in integrating a smoothness prior (specifically, a Laplacian penalty) into a fast and scalable *online* dictionary learning framework. Previous works with structured penalties often relied on slower batch optimization algorithms. This contribution is incremental but valuable, as it directly addresses the computational challenges posed by ever-growing neuroimaging datasets. The paper does a reasonable job of positioning itself relative to prior work but could have strengthened its case by providing a more direct comparison (e.g., a runtime benchmark) against older, non-online structured methods to concretely demonstrate the scalability advantage.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem: the data-driven discovery of functional brain parcels from population data. A scalable algorithm that produces interpretable and meaningful components would be of great interest to the neuroimaging community. The proposed method shows promise, particularly its strong performance when data is limited, which is a very common scenario in clinical studies. However, the limited performance gains in the large-data setting and the lack of a clear win on predictive tasks temper the overall significance. If the method's advantages could be more clearly demonstrated or the trade-offs better understood and articulated, its impact would be substantially greater.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem in computational neuroscience.
*   The proposed method provides a novel integration of a spatial smoothness prior into a highly scalable online dictionary learning framework, which is a valuable engineering contribution.
*   The experiments demonstrate a clear and substantial performance improvement in the low-data regime, a common and important use case.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The manuscript is poorly written and suffers from a lack of polish, which impedes understanding and suggests a rushed submission.
*   The experimental results are not consistently strong. The improvements over the unstructured baseline are modest in the large-data setting, and the method is outperformed on key quantitative metrics by a standard ICA approach.
*   The paper lacks a sufficient analysis and discussion of its results, failing to provide insight into the observed performance trade-offs between model complexity, interpretability, and predictive power.
*   The claims of superiority in large-scale regimes are not fully substantiated by the provided learning curves, which show performance converging with the baseline model.

**Conclusion**

While the paper presents an interesting and well-motivated idea for scalable, structured dictionary learning, the execution in terms of both the manuscript's clarity and the depth of the experimental analysis is currently below the standard expected for NIPS. The weaknesses in the presentation and the unconvincing nature of some of the experimental results outweigh the strengths. I would recommend rejection at this time but would encourage the authors to revise the paper substantially by improving the writing, expanding the experimental analysis with a deeper discussion of the results, and clarifying the specific conditions under which their method provides the most benefit.
