This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method incorporating both sparsity and structural constraints for decomposing brain images. The authors aim to delineate brain structures from functional images by combining an l1-norm penalty for sparsity with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce smoothness in the learned dictionary atoms. This extends the online dictionary learning work of Mairal et al. (2010) and is evaluated on fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project.

The core idea builds on established techniques in neuroimaging, where defining Regions of Interest (ROIs) is crucial for statistical power and signal-to-noise ratio. The paper positions its approach as a data-driven method for ROI definition, leveraging population-level variability to discover these regions. This relates to existing clustering-based approaches (e.g., [21, 14]) but frames the problem as a linear decomposition with spatial and sparse penalties, similar to ICA/PCA methods used in brain imaging [5]. The online learning framework, inherited from Mairal et al. (2010), allows for scalability to large datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability:** The online learning approach allows the method to handle large-scale neuroimaging datasets, a significant practical benefit.
* **Potential for Interpretability:** The combination of sparsity and smoothness constraints aims to produce dictionary atoms that represent biologically plausible brain regions, improving interpretability compared to purely data-driven methods like standard ICA.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments on HCP data demonstrate that the proposed method can achieve competitive performance in terms of explained variance and prediction of behavioral variables, outperforming SODL [16] and, in some cases, CanICA [22].
* **Implementation Availability:** The promise to release the implementation as part of the Nilearn package will facilitate reproducibility and broader adoption.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** The primary contribution – adding a Laplacian penalty to online dictionary learning – feels incremental. While the application to neuroimaging is relevant, the core algorithmic innovation is modest and may not meet the high bar for novelty expected at NIPS. The extension from Mairal et al. (2010) appears relatively straightforward.
* **Motivation & Justification:** The motivation for using a Sobolev penalty, beyond a general desire for "structured" atoms, is weak. The paper doesn't clearly define what constitutes desirable structure or why Laplacian regularization is the most appropriate choice. A more rigorous justification linking the smoothness prior to underlying assumptions about brain organization is needed. The choice of an l2-norm on the codes (Ui) in Eq.(2) is not adequately explained, especially given the l1-norm used in the original Mairal et al. (2010) work.
* **Technical Presentation:** The paper suffers from poor language and numerous typos, hindering clarity. Several statements are imprecise or lack sufficient detail. For example, the reference to "line 146" is unclear. Eq.(1) likely has a notational error and should be transposed.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors address hyperparameter tuning, the discussion feels somewhat superficial. The interplay between α, γ, and τ, and their impact on the results, could be explored more thoroughly.
* **Comparison to Existing Methods:**  While comparisons are made to CanICA and SODL, the discussion of related work lacks depth. More attention should be paid to existing spatially regularized dictionary learning methods (e.g., TV-l1 regularization [1, 3]) and their differences from the proposed approach.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The method offers a scalable and interpretable approach to ROI definition in neuroimaging.
* The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance on a large, publicly available dataset.
* The online learning framework makes the method practical for large-scale analyses.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the approach is limited.
* The motivation for the chosen regularization strategy is weak.
* The paper suffers from poor writing quality and lacks sufficient technical detail.
* The method does not demonstrably advance the state of the art in a significant way.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound but incrementally novel method for dictionary learning in neuroimaging. While the scalability and potential for interpretability are valuable, the limited novelty and weaknesses in presentation raise concerns. I believe the paper, in its current form, falls short of the standards for acceptance at NIPS.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. 

I would encourage the authors to revise the paper by: (1) providing a more compelling justification for the choice of the Laplacian penalty; (2) addressing the technical shortcomings and improving the clarity of the writing; (3) thoroughly comparing the proposed method to existing spatially regularized dictionary learning approaches; and (4) strengthening the claims of novelty and significance. A more substantial revision addressing these points could potentially warrant reconsideration.
