- Proposes Smooth-SODL: online dictionary learning with sparsity and Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty to yield piecewise-smooth, blob-like brain image atoms.
- Extends SODL (Mairal et al. 2010) to include spatial regularization with modest computational overhead and FISTA-based per-atom updates.
- Evaluated on HCP task fMRI against CanICA and unstructured SODL; reports better interpretability and stability, competitive explained variance, and reasonable runtime.

Strengths:
- Addresses scalable learning of interpretable, spatially localized components for large neuroimaging cohorts.
- Sound optimization design: per-atom convex subproblems, simple FISTA solver, retains online memory efficiency.
- Empirical qualitative gains in spatial structure, interpretability, and stability over baselines.
- Large-scale HCP setup with multiple evaluation axes (EV, stability, runtime, behavioral prediction).
- Runtime overhead modest (~2–3× SODL) and much lower than single-batch methods.

Weaknesses:
- No new theoretical analysis: no convergence/stationarity guarantees for alternating scheme, no rates, and no justification for αt/γt schedules.
- Manuscript quality poor: many typos, leftover comments, inconsistent notation, and copy-paste artifacts.
- Experimental gaps: potentially unfair baselines, differing pre-smoothing, lack of comparisons to TV-L1/GraphNet and PCA/ICA with matched preprocessing.
- Coding choice unclear: uses ridge codes but lacks comparison to L1 coding within the same framework.
- Reproducibility/statistics lacking: no error bars, confidence intervals, significance tests, or detailed data/split descriptions.
- Missing implementation details: Laplacian construction, masking, boundary conditions, simplex vs L1 projection, FISTA stopping criteria, and memory/runtime scaling.
- Some claims are overstated or unclear (e.g., “parameter free” BCD, “global optimum” statements).

Quality and completeness:
- Optimization design reasonable but lacks theoretical backing and sufficient ablations.
- Work is more an engineering/practical extension than a theoretically grounded novelty.

Clarity:
- Organization acceptable but writing, notation, and figure/table presentation need major cleanup.

Originality:
- Practical and useful combination (online dictionary learning + Laplacian prior) but conceptually incremental relative to prior structured penalties.

Significance:
- Potentially useful for large-cohort ROI discovery if cleaned, validated, and expanded with stronger baselines and theory.

Suggested improvements:
- Provide convergence/stationarity analysis or justify/remove αt/γt schedules; analyze their effects.
- Add ablations: ridge vs L1 codes, simplex vs L1-ball, varying γ and τ, and FISTA tolerance/iterations.
- Strengthen baselines: TV-L1 dictionary learning, GraphNet decompositions, matched PCA/ICA pipelines.
- Report error bars, statistical tests, multiple seeds/splits, and release full code/scripts.
- Clarify implementation: Laplacian discretization, masking, FFT updates, memory scaling, stopping criteria, Hungarian matching, and normalization.
- Thorough proofreading: fix typos, remove comments, and make notation consistent.

Questions for authors:
- How is Δ (grid spacing, boundary, masking) constructed and is it isotropic?
- Why enforce non-negativity via the simplex for signed Z-maps; tried L1-ball without Rp+?
- How sensitive are results to FISTA tolerance and how many iterations are used per atom?
- Can you compare L1-coded SODL to Smooth-SODL?
- What is the memory footprint for p≈10^6, k≈100?

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, large-scale neuroimaging problem with a practical, scalable extension and clear qualitative gains.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient theoretical analysis, poor manuscript quality, and incomplete/unfair experimental comparisons with weak statistical support.

Recommendation:
- Reject (borderline); promising idea but requires substantial rewrite, stronger baselines/ablations, and basic convergence analysis before reconsideration.
