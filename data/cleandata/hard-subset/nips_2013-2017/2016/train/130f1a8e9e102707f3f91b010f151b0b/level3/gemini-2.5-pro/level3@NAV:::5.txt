This paper proposes Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), a method for learning structured and sparse component maps from large-scale brain imaging data. The core idea is to extend the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty is intended to enforce spatial smoothness, resulting in components that are more interpretable as distinct brain regions or "blobs." The online nature of the algorithm ensures scalability to massive datasets. The authors demonstrate its application on fMRI data, comparing it against standard sparse online dictionary learning and ICA-based methods.

The primary strength of this work is its focus on a significant problem: deriving interpretable, data-driven brain atlases from large neuroimaging cohorts. The online formulation is well-suited for the scale of modern datasets, and the experimental results show that the proposed regularization does produce qualitatively more structured and visually appealing component maps compared to the baseline.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that temper my enthusiasm.

**Originality:** The novelty of the proposed method is limited. The contribution is essentially the application of a standard Sobolev regularizer to the well-established online dictionary learning framework. While this combination is sensible for the target application, this incremental step may not meet the originality standards expected at NeurIPS.

**Quality and Clarity:** The motivation for the Sobolev regularization is insufficiently justified. The paper does not provide a formal definition of the "structure" it aims to capture or a clear rationale for why this specific penalty is the most appropriate choice. Furthermore, several technical aspects are unclear or potentially erroneous. There appears to be a notational error in Eq.(1), which should likely involve the transpose of the component matrix. More importantly, the use of an l2 norm on the codes in Eq.(2) is a key departure from Mairal et al. (2010), which used an l1 norm for sparse coding. This choice is not adequately explained or analyzed, despite its significant impact on the resulting decomposition.

**Presentation:** The manuscript requires substantial revision for clarity. It contains numerous grammatical errors and typos that impede readability. References to specific parts of the text, such as an unspecified "line 146", are ambiguous and unhelpful to the reader.

In summary, while the paper addresses an important problem, its limited novelty, unclear motivation, and poor presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. A major revision addressing the justification for the modeling choices and improving the overall clarity would be necessary.
