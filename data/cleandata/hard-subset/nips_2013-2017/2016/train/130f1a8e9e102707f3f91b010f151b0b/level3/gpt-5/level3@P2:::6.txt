Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning method for brain images that enforces both sparsity and spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. Sparsity is induced through a convex constraint on the atoms (e.g., simplex or l1-ball), while spatial smoothness/compactness is enforced via a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty. Codes are obtained by ridge regression. The algorithm adapts the online block-coordinate descent framework of Mairal et al. (2010) to include a Laplacian-regularized per-atom update, solved efficiently with a few iterations of FISTA; the authors report a 2–3x runtime increase over vanilla SODL. Experiments on HCP task-fMRI maps compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA, emphasizing interpretability, stability, and predictive utility of learned components.

The approach is technically close to GraphNet-style penalties (Grosenick et al., NeuroImage 2013) and to TV-regularized decompositions previously explored for neuroimaging (e.g., Abraham et al., MICCAI; Varoquaux et al., IPMI), but those works typically rely on slow alternating minimization in batch mode. The main advance here is to integrate a Laplacian structural prior into an online dictionary-learning procedure with practical scalability, building on [16] and related online MF/SC work [17].

Quality
- Algorithmic soundness: The per-atom subproblem is a strongly convex quadratic with a simple convex constraint, so existence and uniqueness of the per-step minimizer hold; the use of FISTA is appropriate with a computable Lipschitz constant. However, the paper does not provide a formal convergence guarantee of the overall nonconvex procedure beyond the analogy to [16]. In particular, with the added Laplacian term and time-varying γ, it would be helpful to state conditions ensuring convergence to a stationary point (e.g., citing standard block-coordinate results for nonconvex problems with strongly convex subproblems), or to clarify which assumptions of [16] still apply here without modification.
- Theoretical guarantees: There is no proof of existence/uniqueness for the joint problem (expected due to nonconvexity) and no global convergence guarantee. The discussion of the Lipschitz constant and γ schedule (Eq. 9) is valuable, but currently heuristic.
- Empirical evaluation: Strong and carefully designed. Comparisons cover interpretability, explained variance (EV) on train/test, stability/reproducibility across splits, runtime, and downstream prediction of behavioral measures. The HCP dataset (n=500, p≈2.6e5) is appropriate to stress scalability.
- Practical guidance: The hyperparameter discussion is useful (effects of τ, γ; αt ~ t^−1/2; CV for EV), but the method would benefit from more actionable default settings and sensitivity analyses (e.g., sweeps of γ and τ with reproducibility vs EV trade-offs; robustness to k).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with helpful algorithm boxes. A few issues hinder readability:
- Specific issue (line 146): there is a confusing/likely erroneous use of “maxUi” where a “minUi” is intended in the description of the reconstruction term; please correct. Also, in the mini-batch description, the indexing Xt,…,Xη is ambiguous—clarify that η denotes batch size, not the last sample index.
- Define boundary conditions and grid spacing for ∆; the constant “1+4Dγ̃” depends on discretization and boundary handling (Neumann vs Dirichlet), so a short derivation or citation would help.
- Be consistent about whether sparsity is enforced on atoms via a constraint (as in the main text) or an l1 penalty (mentioned in the abstract/intro); the current phrasing can mislead readers about whether the atom update is penalized or constrained.
- The claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations yield 1e−6 accuracy seems optimistic; please clarify if this refers to the relative decrease needed for good downstream performance rather than absolute optimality.

After rebuttal: the authors clarified the algorithmic choices and resolved my main concern about the dictionary update; with minor edits addressing the above points, I raise the clarity/presentation assessment from 2 to 3.

Originality
Moderate to good. Laplacian/GraphNet and structured sparsity are well known, and online dictionary learning is established. The novelty lies in marrying a Laplacian prior on atoms with an online BCD scheme that remains simple and fast, with a concrete γ schedule and practical implementation choices. To my knowledge, there is no prior online approach achieving similar spatial structure at this scale with comparable runtime.

Significance
High for neuroimaging applications and broadly relevant for scalable structured matrix factorization. The improvements in interpretability and stability at modest computational cost make this attractive to practitioners. While EV is lower than ICA on average, the stability and interpretability gains are compelling, especially in low- to medium-data regimes (clear advantage early in learning curves). Public availability in Nilearn would further amplify impact.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Expand on the theoretical status: state clearly that the algorithm converges to a stationary point under standard assumptions (cite Bertsekas, Tseng) and specify which ones are met here.
- Provide a brief derivation (or cite) for the Lipschitz constant of the per-atom objective under your discretization, and specify boundary conditions.
- Include a small synthetic experiment with known ground-truth blobs to quantify recovery vs γ and τ, complementing the HCP results.
- Compare to a TV-regularized baseline (batch if no online solver) on a smaller subset to contextualize Laplacian vs TV structure.
- Report memory usage and wall-clock breakdown (coding vs dictionary update) to guide practitioners.
- Clarify whether the constraint set is the simplex or l1 ball in all experiments and why; simplex enforces nonnegativity—discuss its effect on interpretability.

Pros
- Simple, scalable method integrating spatial structure into online dictionary learning
- Strong empirical results on large-scale data; improved interpretability and stability
- Modest runtime overhead; practical implementation details and hyperparameter guidance
- Relevance to ROI learning and population-level neuroimaging; planned open-source release

Cons
- No formal convergence/stationarity guarantee beyond analogy to [16]
- Evaluation centered on one dataset/task; limited comparisons to other structured penalties (e.g., TV)
- Hyperparameter selection remains partly heuristic; defaults could be clearer
- Some clarity/notation issues and minor typos

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a practically useful and methodologically sound extension of online dictionary learning that yields interpretable, stable components at scale, with convincing empirical evidence. Strengthening the clarity and adding modest theoretical and comparative context would further improve the contribution.
