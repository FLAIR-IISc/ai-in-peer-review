Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), an online matrix factorization method for brain images that adds spatial structure to dictionary atoms. Each atom is constrained to be sparse (via an L1-type constraint or simplex constraint) and spatially smooth (via a Laplacian/Sobolev penalty), while codes are obtained by ridge (or optionally L1) regression. The optimization is cast within a block-coordinate descent framework à la Mairal et al. (2010), with per-atom convex subproblems solved by proximal gradient (FISTA). The authors argue the method scales to large n and p with modest overhead versus standard online dictionary learning (SODL), and demonstrate on HCP task-fMRI Z-maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) improved interpretability and stability versus SODL and CanICA (Varoquaux et al., 2010), with small runtime cost.

The work builds on: (i) online sparse coding/dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010; Mensch et al., 2016), (ii) spatially-structured penalties used in neuroimaging (GraphNet/Laplacian smoothing; Grosenick et al., 2013; Smooth Lasso), and (iii) spatially-structured decompositions for fMRI (TV-L1 dictionary learning; Abraham et al., MICCAI 2013; structured sparse PCA; Jenatton et al., AISTATS 2010). The novelty is to integrate a Laplacian prior on atoms into an online dictionary learning framework with per-atom proximal updates, targeting scalability for cohort-level brain mapping.

Strengths
- Timely and relevant problem: learning interpretable, spatially localized components for population neuroimaging at scale.
- Algorithmic design is sensible: per-atom convex subproblems with standard proximal solvers; clear path to scalability; minimal additional complexity compared to SODL.
- Empirical results on a large public dataset (HCP) with multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, prediction). The learning-curve analysis is insightful: spatial regularization helps in low-data regimes.
- Practical guidance on hyper-parameters and implementation details; planned integration into an open-source neuroimaging library is valuable for impact.

Weaknesses
- Theory: The paper lacks a careful theoretical treatment of the online algorithm with Laplacian-regularized, constrained atoms. The convergence discussion largely references Mairal et al. without verifying the additional assumptions introduced by the Laplacian penalty and time-varying regularization γt. No proof of almost sure convergence to a stationary point, no regret analysis, and no guarantees on the “1–2 FISTA iterations to 1e-6 accuracy” claim (which is implausible without strong warm-start justification). The proposed γt schedule is heuristic.
- Evaluation breadth and fairness: Only one task from HCP is considered; results may not generalize. Baselines are limited: no comparisons to TV-L1 dictionary learning (Abraham et al.), GraphNet-based decompositions, structured SPCA/NMF, or recent online variants with structure. CanICA is evaluated with pre-smoothing and hard-thresholding, while SODL/Smooth-SODL are not smoothed; this mismatch complicates fairness. Behavior prediction shows CanICA best on test data, which weakens the claim that Smooth-SODL is broadly superior beyond interpretability.
- Clarity and presentation: The manuscript contains numerous typos, formatting issues, and even leftover editorial comments (e.g., “XXX: I don’t understand the switch between V and v”), which undermines confidence. Notation is sometimes inconsistent; some equations are hard to parse; figure/table references (e.g., Table 1) lack sufficient detail; references are incomplete in places. Important implementation details (boundary conditions for Laplacian, specifics of FFT-based acceleration, constraints actually used per experiment, code availability) are insufficiently documented.
- Methodological details: The choice between simplex and L1-ball constraints and the implied non-negativity is not consistently justified. The effect of ridge vs L1 codes is only briefly mentioned; no ablation. Matching of atoms across splits (for stability) is done via Hungarian assignment but details are sparse (thresholds, matching cost). The “global optimum in each atom update” statement is true only for the convex subproblem; the overall procedure remains nonconvex.

Quality
- Technical soundness is partial: the optimization steps are standard and likely correct, but theoretical guarantees in the online setting with Laplacian constraints are not established. Some runtime claims are anecdotal.
- Empirical support is suggestive but not comprehensive; additional datasets and baselines are needed.

Clarity
- Below NeurIPS standards due to typos, leftover comments, and inconsistent notation. The paper would benefit greatly from thorough proofreading, clearer algorithm boxes, and a more careful experimental section.

Originality
- Incremental but useful. Combining a Laplacian prior with online dictionary learning for spatially structured atoms in neuroimaging appears underexplored, though closely related to GraphNet and TV-L1 approaches.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for large-scale neuroimaging analysis and ROI discovery, particularly if the code is released. Current evaluation limits the strength of the claims.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide a convergence analysis tailored to your setting (e.g., show that the surrogate function decreases and that limit points are stationary under your assumptions; clarify the role of γt and αt schedules).
- Expand baselines: TV-L1 dictionary learning, GraphNet factorization, structured SPCA, NMF with spatial regularization. Ensure preprocessing is aligned across methods.
- Add more datasets (e.g., additional HCP tasks, resting-state), and report reproducibility with spatial overlap metrics in addition to correlation.
- Clarify implementation: Laplacian construction and boundary conditions; when FFT diagonalization is applicable; exact constraints used; warm-start strategy; stopping criteria and actual iteration counts for FISTA.
- Report hyperparameter selection protocol and sensitivity analyses; include ablations for ridge vs L1 codes and for the simplex vs L1-ball constraints.
- Thoroughly proofread and fix all typos and leftover comments.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practical problem with a scalable method.
- Improves interpretability and stability of decompositions.
- Promising empirical results and potential for community impact via open-source release.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient theoretical analysis for the proposed online variant.
- Limited and partially unfair empirical comparison; missing key baselines.
- Presentation quality below conference standards.

Recommendation
- Weak reject. The idea is promising and likely useful to the community, but the current manuscript falls short on theoretical rigor, breadth/fairness of evaluation, and clarity. With stronger theory, broader comparisons, additional datasets, and a polished presentation, I could see this work as competitive.

Confidence
- Medium.
