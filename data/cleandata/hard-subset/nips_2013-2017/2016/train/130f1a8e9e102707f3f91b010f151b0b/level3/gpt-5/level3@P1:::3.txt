This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning formulation for neuroimaging that augments sparse dictionary learning [Mairal et al., 2010] with a Sobolev/Laplacian prior on dictionary atoms to encourage spatially compact, piecewise-smooth “blobs.” Codes are found via ridge regression; atoms are updated by block coordinate descent with a Laplacian-regularized proximal step (solved by a few FISTA iterations), preserving the scalability of online learning. The method is positioned against unstructured SODL and PCA/ICA-based approaches (CanICA), with experiments on HCP task-fMRI GLM contrast maps. The authors report improved spatial interpretability and early-sample data efficiency, at a 2–3× runtime overhead relative to SODL.

Relation to prior work is appropriate: it builds on online sparse coding [16], connects the dictionary update to GraphNet-style quadratic smoothing [11], and contrasts with slower alternating-optimization approaches using TV-ℓ1 [1]. The incremental novelty is the combination of Laplacian regularization with online DL in a scalable manner for large neuroimaging matrices.

Strengths
- Scalable, practical algorithm: Extends a widely used online DL framework with minimal added complexity. The BCD + FISTA implementation and sufficient statistics reuse are well-motivated.
- Sensible prior: The Laplacian penalty is a principled way to encourage spatial coherence/compactness without resorting to expensive TV. The derivation and convergence discussion are sound.
- Clear computational story: Runtime overhead is moderate (≈3× SODL) and lower than a batch ICA baseline, with minibatch support and reasonable memory.
- Useful empirical observations: Learning curves show potential benefits in low-data regimes; stability analysis suggests improved reproducibility vs. SODL; a brief hyperparameter exploration and guidance are provided.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation leans heavily on subjective visual inspection of “blobs.” While smoothness penalties unsurprisingly yield smoother maps, the paper lacks objective spatial-structure metrics (e.g., spatial autocorrelation, number/size of connected components, compactness, overlap with anatomical/functional atlases).
- Inter-subject consistency and ROI reproducibility are not rigorously validated. A core claim is to capture population variability; stronger split-half or bootstrap matching of atoms with Dice/Jaccard overlap across held-out subjects would substantiate this.
- Downstream utility remains unclear. CanICA achieves higher explained variance and better behavioral prediction. Smooth-SODL’s advantage over SODL is mostly qualitative and in early learning; asymptotically they converge.
- Baselines and fairness: Missing comparisons with structured-sparsity priors closer to the proposed one (TV-ℓ1 DL [1], GraphNet variants [11], structured PCA [14]). Pre-smoothing for CanICA confounds interpretability; a post hoc thresholded “tCanICA” is reported, but thresholds and their impact are not standardized.
- Some technical gaps: The practical handling of the γ schedule suggested by Eq. (9) vs. fixed γ used in experiments is not clarified. Claims that 1–2 FISTA iterations suffice would benefit from empirical accuracy/runtime curves. Stopping criteria and projection details are under-specified for full reproducibility.

Quality
- The optimization formulation is coherent, and the algorithmic components are standard and correct. However, empirical claims would be stronger with rigorous statistical testing (confidence intervals, permutation tests) and ablations (ridge vs ℓ1 codes; ℓ1-ball vs simplex constraints; fixed vs time-varying γ and α).
- The behavioral prediction setup should ensure strict nested cross-validation and report statistical significance of differences.

Clarity
- Generally readable with a clear high-level motivation and algorithm summary. There are typos and some dense sections (Alg. 2, Lipschitz/step-size discussion) that could be streamlined. Figures rely on visual judgments; quantitative spatial metrics would improve clarity. Reproducibility would benefit from more implementation detail and finalized code release.

Originality
- Moderate. The idea of imposing spatial structure (Laplacian/GraphNet/TV) on neuroimaging decompositions is established; the contribution is adapting a Laplacian prior within an online DL framework at scale. This is a useful engineering advance but not a major conceptual leap.

Significance
- Potentially useful for practitioners seeking fast, structured decompositions without the cost of TV. However, given large datasets like HCP, the empirical benefits over SODL and CanICA remain modest or qualitative. The impact would be clearer with stronger evidence of improved reproducibility, anatomical/functional validity, and downstream task performance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add objective spatial metrics: Moran’s I/geary’s C for smoothness, number/size of connected components, surface-to-volume ratio, center-of-mass displacement across splits, and overlap with standard atlases (e.g., Desikan-Killiany, Yeo 7/17 networks). Report statistics across splits.
- Strengthen inter-subject validation: Split-half stability with Hungarian matching, Dice/Jaccard overlap of atom supports across cohorts and across initializations; quantify stability as a function of k, τ, γ.
- Expand baselines: TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning [1], GraphNet-based components [11], structured PCA [14], recent online DL variants [17]. Ensure fair preprocessing and thresholding.
- Downstream tasks: Evaluate classification/regression on multiple behavioral targets with nested CV, calibration plots, and statistical tests. Include tasks where spatial localization matters (e.g., lesion-symptom mapping proxies).
- Ablations and sensitivity: Ridge vs ℓ1 codes; simplex vs ℓ1-ball constraints; effect of γ schedules complying with Eq. (9); runtime vs. accuracy trade-offs for FISTA iterations.
- Release code and scripts to reproduce figures and metrics.

Arguments for acceptance
- Scalable, easy-to-implement extension of a widely used framework.
- Produces cleaner, more spatially coherent components with improved stability.
- Practical relevance for large neuroimaging datasets and ROI construction.

Arguments against acceptance
- Evidence relies largely on subjective visualization; quantitative validation is limited.
- Modest gains over strong baselines on key quantitative metrics; downstream benefits unclear.
- Incremental novelty relative to prior structured-sparsity work; missing comparisons.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The method is promising and practically useful, but the paper needs stronger, objective validation of spatial structure and inter-subject reproducibility, broader baselines, and more rigorous downstream evaluations to substantiate its claims.
