

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning for brain imaging that combines L1 sparsity and Sobolev (Laplacian) regularization to extract structured, spatially compact brain regions from fMRI data. The work extends Mairal et al.'s online dictionary learning (SODL) by adding a spatial smoothness constraint through a Laplacian penalty, aiming to better capture the functional organization of the brain. The approach builds on prior work in structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging, particularly Varoquaux et al.'s work on multi-subject dictionary learning for brain atlases, but differs by incorporating online optimization for scalability.

The main technical contribution is the integration of a spatial smoothness constraint into the online dictionary learning framework, which is non-trivial due to the coupling introduced by the Laplacian penalty. The authors derive an efficient block-coordinate descent algorithm that maintains the computational efficiency of SODL while adding the spatial structure. The algorithm is implemented in a way that only incurs a factor of 2-3 slowdown compared to SODL, making it suitable for large-scale brain imaging datasets.

The paper's strengths include a well-motivated problem statement addressing the need for principled ROI definition in neuroimaging, a technically sound algorithmic development with convergence guarantees, and a thorough experimental validation on a large-scale HCP dataset. The experiments demonstrate that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable brain maps compared to SODL (which produces noisy, scattered patterns) and CanICA (which produces dense, uninterpretable components). The method also shows better stability across data splits and superior performance in predicting behavioral variables compared to SODL and thresholded CanICA.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that significantly impact its contribution. First, the experimental improvements over SODL are marginal and not convincingly demonstrated. While the paper shows that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable maps, the quantitative gains in explained variance (55% vs 50% on test data) and prediction performance are modest. The paper claims "14-fold gain" in explained variance with small datasets, but this is misleading as it compares 31% (Smooth-SODL) to 2% (SODL) - a substantial difference that diminishes as more data is added, suggesting the benefit is primarily for small datasets rather than the large-scale regime the method claims to address.

Second, the paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis to explain why the spatial regularization provides these benefits. The authors observe that spatial regularization helps with small datasets but don't provide a principled explanation for why this is the case, nor do they analyze how the hyperparameters (γ, τ) interact to produce these effects. The "safe range" of parameters mentioned is not supported by rigorous analysis.

Third, the manuscript suffers from poor clarity and organization. There are numerous draft comments in the text (e.g., "line 146"), and the explanation of the algorithmic details is sometimes confusing. The relationship between the proposed method and related work is not clearly articulated - for example, the connection to Varoquaux's multi-subject dictionary learning work is superficial, and the paper doesn't adequately distinguish itself from existing structured dictionary learning approaches.

The significance of the work is limited by the modest gains and lack of theoretical grounding. While the problem of ROI definition in neuroimaging is important, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this method represents a significant advance over existing approaches. The claimed "non-trivial extension" of SODL is not well-justified, as the Laplacian penalty has been used in other contexts for spatial regularization.

Quality: The method is technically sound and well-validated on a large dataset, but the experimental analysis lacks depth. The paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for the observed performance improvements.

Clarity: The manuscript is poorly organized with confusing explanations and draft comments that detract from readability. The algorithmic details are presented in a way that requires significant effort to understand.

Originality: The combination of online dictionary learning with spatial smoothness is novel, but the novelty is limited as spatial regularization has been used in other contexts for brain imaging. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from prior work in structured dictionary learning.

Significance: The contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The modest performance gains don't justify the claim that the method "shall become the de facto way" for ROI extraction.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging with a technically sound algorithm that scales to large datasets. The experimental validation is thorough, and the method shows promise for producing interpretable brain maps.

Arguments against acceptance: The experimental results don't convincingly demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, the theoretical justification is weak, the manuscript is poorly written, and the significance of the contribution is overstated.

Overall, while the paper presents a technically sound approach to an important problem in neuroimaging, the marginal gains, lack of theoretical insight, and poor presentation significantly undermine its contribution. The paper would benefit from a more thorough theoretical analysis of why spatial regularization helps, a clearer presentation of the results, and a more modest assessment of the contribution. The current version does not represent a sufficiently strong contribution for NIPS.
