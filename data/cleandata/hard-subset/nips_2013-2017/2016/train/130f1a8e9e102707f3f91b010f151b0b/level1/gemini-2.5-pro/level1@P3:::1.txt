**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), for discovering spatially structured and sparse components from large-scale neuroimaging datasets. The goal is to identify brain regions of interest in a data-driven manner that are both interpretable (sparse and blob-like) and representative of population-level variability. The method extends the influential online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages spatial smoothness, complementing the sparsity induced by an `l1`-norm constraint. A key contribution is integrating this structured prior into an efficient online, block-coordinate descent algorithm, ensuring scalability to massive datasets. The authors demonstrate their method on a large task-fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (N=500), comparing it against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a popular group Independent Component Analysis (ICA) method, CanICA. The evaluation is comprehensive, covering qualitative assessment of the learned components, computational performance, explained variance, reproducibility, and predictive power for behavioral scores.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a highly relevant problem in neuroimaging: the principled, data-driven definition of functional brain regions from large cohorts. The proposed solution is a novel and elegant combination of existing ideas. While structured sparsity has been explored in neuroimaging before (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2011 [25]; Abraham et al., 2013 [1]), these prior works relied on slow, batch optimization schemes. The main contribution here is successfully integrating a spatial structure prior into a scalable online learning framework, which is a crucial step for handling modern, massive neuroimaging datasets. This work effectively bridges the gap between computationally efficient but unstructured methods like SODL and structured but computationally expensive batch methods.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The method is technically sound. The formulation of the objective function is well-motivated, and the derivation of the block-coordinate descent algorithm is a non-trivial extension of Mairal et al. (2010). The identification of the dictionary update subproblem as a GraphNet-like denoising problem, solvable with fast first-order methods like FISTA, is insightful. The authors provide a convincing empirical demonstration that this added complexity results in only a modest increase in runtime (a factor of 2-3), preserving the primary advantage of the online approach. The convergence guarantees are also appropriately established by showing that the assumptions of the original framework still hold.

3.  **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and compelling.
    *   The choice of a large, public dataset (HCP) and relevant baselines (SODL, CanICA) provides a strong benchmark.
    *   The use of multiple, complementary metrics (visual quality, explained variance, stability, and prediction) gives a well-rounded assessment of the model's properties.
    *   The learning curve analysis in Table 1 is particularly effective. It clearly demonstrates the value of the Laplacian regularizer, especially in the low-to-medium sample size regime, which is a very common scenario in clinical and cognitive neuroscience studies. This result strongly justifies the proposed extension over standard SODL.
    *   The qualitative results in Figure 2(a) are visually convincing, showing that Smooth-SODL produces maps that are cleaner and more anatomically plausible than those from competing methods.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions**

1.  **Choice of Spatial Prior:** The paper uses a Laplacian penalty, which corresponds to an `l2`-norm on the image gradient. An alternative and widely used prior for encouraging piece-wise constant regions is the Total Variation (TV) norm, an `l1`-norm on the gradient (as used in [1]). A brief discussion motivating the choice of the Laplacian over TV would strengthen the paper. A likely reason is that the differentiability of the Laplacian penalty leads to a simpler and faster dictionary update subproblem than the non-smooth TV penalty. Acknowledging this trade-off between model choice and computational efficiency would be valuable.

2.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper rightly notes that hyperparameter selection is a difficult problem. While the empirical study in Fig. 1 is useful, the guidance provided is still somewhat high-level. The data-adaptive choice for `alpha` is interesting but could benefit from more justification. For the key new parameter, `gamma`, the guidance is to use cross-validation. It would be helpful if the authors could provide more intuition on how one might set a reasonable search range for `gamma` based on properties of the data, such as the signal-to-noise ratio or the expected spatial scale of the target regions.

3.  **Interpretability vs. Predictive Power:** The behavioral prediction results (Fig 2c) are interesting. While Smooth-SODL outperforms other interpretable models (SODL, tCanICA), the dense, unthresholded CanICA model performs best overall. This highlights a potential trade-off: enforcing structural priors for interpretability might discard some fine-grained, spatially complex information that is nonetheless predictive. This is an important nuance, and a more detailed discussion of this trade-off would be a valuable addition to the paper.

4.  **Clarity of Algorithmic Details:** The dictionary update step (Eq. 7-8) is the core technical novelty. While the paper provides the necessary components, the derivation is quite compressed. Expanding on the term `Vj + At[j, j]−1(Bjt −VAj)` and explaining its intuition as a proxy for the data-fitting term's gradient would improve readability for non-experts. Furthermore, the FFT-based approach for the dictionary update is mentioned but deferred to supplementary materials. A sentence or two in the main text summarizing when this approach is applicable (e.g., for periodic boundary conditions) and its potential speed benefits would be welcome.

**Summary of Recommendation**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, scalable, and effective solution to an important problem in computational neuroscience. The combination of online learning with structured spatial priors is a significant contribution. The method is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by a thorough and convincing set of experiments. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to opportunities for deeper discussion and clarification. They do not detract from the overall strength of the work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant, practical problem with a novel and scalable approach.
*   Technically sound extension of a well-established framework.
*   Strong and comprehensive experimental validation on a large, relevant dataset.
*   Clearly demonstrates the value of the proposed spatial regularization, particularly in common low-data regimes.
*   The work is likely to be highly influential and widely used by researchers in neuroimaging.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The discussion could be slightly improved regarding the choice of prior and the trade-off between interpretability and predictive performance.

Overall, the strengths of the paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend **acceptance**.
