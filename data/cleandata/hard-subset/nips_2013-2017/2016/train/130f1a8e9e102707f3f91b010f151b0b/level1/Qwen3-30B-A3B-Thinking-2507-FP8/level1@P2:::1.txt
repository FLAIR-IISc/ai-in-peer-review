

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) that incorporates spatial structure through a Laplacian penalty to learn interpretable brain regions from functional imaging data. The authors address a key challenge in neuroimaging: defining brain regions in a principled way without relying on segmentation or cross-modality correspondence. Their approach extends the SODL framework to impose spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms, creating "blobs" rather than scattered patterns, while maintaining computational efficiency. The method is evaluated on 500 subjects from the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset, comparing against SODL and CanICA (a standard ICA-based method).

The paper makes a significant technical contribution by addressing the computational challenge of incorporating spatial structure into online dictionary learning. Previous structured approaches (e.g., [1, 25]) relied on slow alternating optimization, while online methods like SODL [16] lacked spatial constraints. The authors show that their Laplacian penalty can be efficiently integrated into the online framework, with only a 2-3x increase in running time. This is a non-trivial extension, as spatial structure introduces couplings in the optimization problem, typically requiring more complex algorithms.

Quality is strong. The mathematical formulation is sound, with clear theoretical justification for the Laplacian penalty (equation 4) and convergence guarantees. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using multiple metrics: visual inspection of dictionary atoms, explained variance (Fig. 2b), stability (mean correlation between atoms across data splits), and predictive performance for behavioral variables (Fig. 2c). The authors demonstrate that Smooth-SODL extracts more interpretable, spatially structured regions than SODL (which produces "thick rings of small nonzero values") and CanICA (which produces dense, uninterpretable maps requiring hard-thresholding). Their learning curves (Table 1) show a 14-fold advantage of Smooth-SODL over SODL in small-data regimes, with comparable performance as data increases.

Clarity is excellent. The paper is well-organized, with clear sectioning and logical flow. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. The figures (particularly Fig. 2) effectively illustrate key results, and the supplementary materials provide necessary technical details. The authors provide sufficient implementation details for reproducibility, including plans to release code via Nilearn.

Originality is strong. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work: it's not merely a combination of existing techniques but a non-trivial extension of online dictionary learning that preserves computational efficiency while incorporating spatial structure. The authors cite relevant neuroimaging literature (e.g., [21, 14, 18]) and position their work against prior structured dictionary learning methods ([1, 25]), demonstrating how their approach addresses computational limitations. While the Laplacian penalty is known in imaging, its integration into online dictionary learning for neuroimaging is novel.

Significance is high. The problem of extracting interpretable brain regions is central to neuroscience, and the authors demonstrate that their method better captures inter-subject variability across small, medium, and large datasets. Their ability to predict behavioral variables (Fig. 2c) shows practical utility beyond just interpretability. The method's scalability to HCP-scale datasets (500 subjects) makes it relevant to current large-scale neuroimaging studies. The authors' claim that such online methods "shall become the de facto way to do dimensionality reduction and ROI extraction" is well-supported by their results.

Strengths: The technical extension is non-trivial yet well-executed, the experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing, the method is efficient (only 2-3x slower than SODL), and the results have clear implications for neuroimaging practice. The paper provides valuable guidance for hyperparameter selection (Fig. 1) and acknowledges limitations (e.g., need for careful γ tuning).

Weaknesses: The paper could better address the limitations of the Laplacian penalty (e.g., how it handles complex brain structures that aren't piece-wise smooth). A more direct comparison with TV-`1 methods [1] would strengthen the novelty claim. Some technical details about computational complexity (e.g., exact number of FISTA iterations needed) could be clarified.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper makes a significant contribution to both machine learning (efficient structured online learning) and neuroimaging (interpretable brain region extraction). The method is technically sound, well-evaluated, and addresses a real-world problem with practical impact. The results demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods across multiple metrics.

Arguments against acceptance: The novelty claim could be strengthened by more thorough comparison with other structured dictionary learning methods. The Laplacian penalty's limitations are not fully explored.

Overall, this is a strong NIPS contribution. The paper advances the state of the art in structured dictionary learning with a method that is both theoretically sound and practically useful for large-scale neuroimaging. The authors have addressed the key challenges of scalability and interpretability, and their results demonstrate clear benefits over existing approaches. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
