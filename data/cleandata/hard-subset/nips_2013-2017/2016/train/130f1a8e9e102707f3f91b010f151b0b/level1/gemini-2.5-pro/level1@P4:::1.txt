This paper proposes a method called Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL) for decomposing large sets of brain images into a dictionary of sparse and spatially structured components. The work is a direct and non-trivial extension of the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010), which is a widely used method for large-scale matrix factorization. The key contribution is the introduction of a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages spatial smoothness, causing the learned components to form coherent, blob-like structures that are more interpretable as brain regions, as opposed to the scattered patterns that can result from standard `1-norm sparsity alone. The authors derive an efficient block-coordinate descent algorithm to solve the resulting optimization problem, demonstrating that the additional computational cost of incorporating the spatial structure is modest. The method is positioned as a scalable alternative to previous structured decomposition techniques (e.g., using Total Variation penalties) that relied on slower batch optimization, and as a more interpretable alternative to non-sparse methods like PCA/ICA which are common in neuroimaging.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the objective function in Equation (2) is a well-principled combination of a data fidelity term, a penalty on the codes, a sparsity constraint on the dictionary atoms, and the novel Laplacian smoothness penalty. The derivation of the online algorithm (Algorithm 1) logically follows from the framework of Mairal et al. (2010), with the main technical challenge being the dictionary update step (Algorithm 2). The authors correctly identify this subproblem as a compactly-constrained minimization of a strongly-convex quadratic function and propose an appropriate and efficient solver (FISTA).

The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides strong support for the paper's claims. The use of the large-scale HCP dataset is appropriate. The comparison against relevant baselines—unstructured Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (SODL) and the widely-used CanICA—is well-designed. The evaluation metrics cover multiple important aspects: qualitative visual inspection of the learned components, quantitative measures of explained variance and stability, and performance on a downstream predictive task. The learning curve analysis presented in Table 1 is particularly compelling, as it clearly demonstrates the value of the spatial regularizer, especially in lower-data regimes where the prior provides crucial information that is otherwise lacking. The claims are well-supported by these results.

**Clarity**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem of discovering structured brain regions from population data and situates the proposed contribution within the existing literature. The model and algorithm are presented with sufficient mathematical detail. Figures are used effectively to illustrate the results; in particular, Figure 2 provides a concise and powerful comparison of the outputs from the different models. The qualitative difference between the Smooth-SODL components and those from SODL and CanICA is immediately apparent. The supplementary material provides additional details on the algorithm and convergence, which is helpful. One minor suggestion for improvement would be to slightly expand the derivation of Equation (7) in the main text to make the paper more self-contained.

**Originality**

The work presents a novel and valuable combination of existing techniques. While online dictionary learning and Laplacian regularization are individually well-established, their integration into a scalable, online framework for structured dictionary learning is new. This work clearly distinguishes itself from Mairal et al. (2010) through the addition of the structure-inducing penalty. It also clearly differentiates itself from prior work on structured sparse decomposition in neuroimaging (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2011; Abraham et al., 2014) by virtue of its online nature, which provides a significant advantage in scalability. The paper does an excellent job of citing and explaining its relationship to this prior work.

**Significance**

The significance of this work is high, particularly for the neuroimaging community but also as a general methodological contribution for spatio-temporal data analysis. The increasing size of neuroimaging datasets (e.g., HCP, UK Biobank) necessitates the development of scalable analysis methods. This paper provides a practical and effective tool for data-driven discovery of functional brain regions ("atlases") from such large datasets. The ability to learn interpretable, structured components at a low computational cost is a major advance over existing methods. The provided learning curve analysis suggests the method is also highly beneficial for smaller-scale studies, where the spatial prior can compensate for a lack of data. Given that the implementation is slated for inclusion in a popular open-source library (Nilearn), the method is likely to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners.

**Strengths:**
*   Addresses an important and practical problem: scalable, data-driven discovery of interpretable brain regions.
*   The proposed online algorithm for structured dictionary learning is novel, efficient, and technically sound.
*   Comprehensive experimental validation on a large, real-world dataset demonstrates clear improvements over state-of-the-art baselines in terms of interpretability, stability, and performance in low-data regimes.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The method introduces additional hyperparameters (specifically, the Laplacian weight γ) that require tuning, for which the paper relies on cross-validation. While an empirical study is provided, a more principled or automated approach to setting this parameter would enhance the method's usability.
*   The paper focuses exclusively on the Laplacian penalty for inducing structure. A brief discussion on the relative merits of this choice compared to other priors, such as Total Variation (which promotes piece-wise constant regions with sharp boundaries), would have been informative.

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound method with high potential for impact. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the contribution.
