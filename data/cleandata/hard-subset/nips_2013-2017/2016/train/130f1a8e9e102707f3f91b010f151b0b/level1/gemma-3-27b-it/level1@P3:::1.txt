This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method (Smooth-SODL) for decomposing brain images, aiming to extract structured and sparse components representing brain regions. The key contribution lies in combining sparsity (L1 norm) with a spatial smoothness constraint (Laplacian penalty) within an online learning framework, extending previous work by Mairal et al. (2010). The authors demonstrate the method's scalability and improved performance on HCP data, showing better interpretability and capture of inter-subject variability compared to ICA and standard online dictionary learning.

The paper addresses a significant problem in neuroimaging – how to define regions of interest (ROIs) in a principled way, avoiding the limitations of manual segmentation or relying on pre-defined atlases. The motivation for learning these regions from inter-subject variability is well-articulated. The proposed method is a logical extension of existing online dictionary learning techniques and addresses a clear need for algorithms that can handle large-scale brain imaging datasets while incorporating meaningful spatial priors.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The method appears technically sound, building upon established concepts in dictionary learning, sparsity, and regularization. The BCD algorithm, adapted from Mairal et al. (2010), is a reasonable approach to optimization. The inclusion of the Laplacian penalty is well-justified and implemented with clear mathematical formulation.
* **Scalability:** The online learning approach is crucial for handling the massive datasets common in neuroimaging (high voxel count and/or large subject numbers). This is a major advantage over batch-processing methods.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset provide a compelling demonstration of the method's effectiveness.  The comparisons to CanICA and standard SODL are relevant, and the results suggest that the spatial regularization improves interpretability and stability. The ablation study showing improvements over standard SODL, particularly with limited data, is a strong point.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the method is presented in a logical manner.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The discussion of hyperparameter tuning and the practical considerations regarding the computational cost are valuable additions. The mention of availability through Nilearn is also positive.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty Beyond Combination:** While the combination of L1 and Laplacian regularization in an *online* dictionary learning setting is a valuable contribution, the individual components (sparsity, smoothness penalties, online learning) are not novel in themselves. The paper could benefit from more clearly articulating *why* this specific combination is particularly well-suited for brain imaging data and what makes it different beyond simply applying known techniques.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments demonstrate sensitivity to the choice of hyperparameters (α, γ, τ).  While the authors provide guidance, a more systematic exploration of the parameter space and a discussion of robustness to parameter variations would be beneficial.  The “safe range” described is a bit vague.
* **CanICA Comparison:** The comparison to CanICA feels somewhat uneven. CanICA is presented without any attempt at thresholding initially, leading to “dense and noisy” results. Then, a thresholded version (tCanICA) is introduced for comparison. This makes the comparison less fair. A more rigorous comparison would involve optimizing the parameters of CanICA and applying appropriate post-processing steps to all methods to ensure a fair evaluation. Also, CanICA has inherent limitations as a single-batch method, so its inherent inability to scale should be explicitly stated, framing the benefits of the online approach more strongly.
* **Behavioral Prediction:** The results on behavioral prediction are promising, but the reported R2 values are relatively modest.  It would be helpful to see a more detailed analysis of which behavioral variables are best predicted and a comparison to simpler baseline models.
* **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors mention convergence guarantees, there's a limited discussion of the theoretical properties of the proposed method, particularly regarding the impact of the Laplacian penalty on the solution.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the authors quantify the running time, they don't delve into the specific computational bottlenecks or potential optimization strategies beyond mentioning the use of FISTA.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and challenging problem in neuroimaging.
* Technically sound and well-motivated.
* Demonstrates promising empirical results on a large, publicly available dataset.
* Scalable to large datasets due to the online learning framework.
* Provides a practical and potentially valuable tool for brain imaging research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Incremental novelty – primarily a combination of existing techniques.
* Sensitivity to hyperparameters.
* Uneven comparison to CanICA.
* Limited theoretical analysis.


**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates promising results. While the novelty is somewhat incremental, the combination of techniques and the application to a challenging neuroimaging problem are valuable. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above in a revision. Specifically, a more thorough discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity, a more rigorous comparison to CanICA, and a deeper exploration of the theoretical implications of the Laplacian penalty would strengthen the paper significantly.  Addressing these points will elevate the paper from a good contribution to a very strong one.
