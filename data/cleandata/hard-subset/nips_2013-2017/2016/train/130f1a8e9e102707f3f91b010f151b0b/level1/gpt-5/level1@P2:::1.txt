Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning framework for brain images that combines (i) sparsity constraints on the dictionary atoms via an ℓ1-type constraint set (optionally with non-negativity) and (ii) spatial structure via a Laplacian (Sobolev/GraphNet-style) penalty on the atoms. Codes are obtained via ridge regression (with the option to use ℓ1 codes), and dictionary updates are performed by block-coordinate descent (BCD) with a proximal gradient solver (FISTA) for the Laplacian-regularized, convex per-atom subproblems. The method is positioned as a non-trivial extension of Mairal et al.’s online sparse dictionary learning (SODL, JMLR 2010) to incorporate spatial structure with modest overhead. Experiments on HCP task-fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) compare to SODL and multi-subject PCA/ICA (CanICA), evaluating runtime, explained variance (EV), stability/reproducibility, interpretability, and behavioral prediction. The approach relates to structured sparsity for neuroimaging (TV-ℓ1 atlas learning; Abraham et al.), GraphNet (Grosenick et al.) and multi-subject dictionary learning (Varoquaux et al.), while building on online factorization (Mairal; Mensch).

Quality
Strengths:
- The optimization formulation is convex in each block and the per-atom update with Laplacian + convex constraint set is standard and well-posed. The use of pre-accumulated sufficient statistics (A, B) mirrors Mairal’s algorithm and preserves scalability.
- The discussion of Lipschitz constants and the impact of γ on step sizes is technically sound; highlighting the need for γ scaling with At[j,j]/t is useful in practice.
- Empirically, the overhead vs SODL is ~3× for p≈2.6e5, which is reasonable for gaining spatial structure; runtime comparisons include CanICA.
- Experiments are reasonably thorough for a single-dataset study: qualitative atom assessment, EV on train/test, cross-split stability with Hungarian matching, and behavioral prediction.

Weaknesses:
- Convergence claims are largely inherited from [16] and assume exact subproblem solves; in practice, the paper recommends 1–2 FISTA steps per atom and suggests time-varying γ and α. The conditions under which inexact BCD with time-varying regularization retains convergence guarantees are not established (or cited).
- The Laplacian penalty enforces smoothness but not edge-preservation; TV is a more standard choice for blob-like ROIs. The paper motivates Laplacian as “imposing blobs” but provides no quantitative comparison to TV-regularized online or batch baselines; prior work [Abraham; FAASTA] suggests TV can yield sharper, more interpretable regions.
- Hyperparameter selection is only sketched. The proposed αt∼t−1/2 and guidance on γ scaling are heuristic; the cross-validation protocol for (γ, τ) lacks details (search ranges, criteria, splits). Reproducibility would benefit from clearer defaults and sensitivity analyses.
- Evaluation is limited to one HCP contrast (Math vs Story) and k=40; no ablations on k, boundary conditions for ∆, or non-negativity vs signed atoms. The reported stability gains (0.4 vs 0.1) are promising but may depend on τ and γ.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable, with explicit algorithms. Some typos and minor notational slips remain, but do not impede understanding.
- A clearer exposition of the constraint set C (ℓ1 ball vs positive simplex) and its neuroscientific implications (e.g., non-negativity for interpretability) would help. Likewise, specifying boundary conditions and implementation details for the 3D Laplacian would aid reproducibility.
- Figures (referenced) seem informative; including quantitative stability and EV curves with confidence intervals would strengthen claims.

Originality
- The core idea—adding a spatial smoothness term on dictionary atoms within an online dictionary-learning framework—is incremental conceptually, but the algorithmic integration is non-trivial and practically valuable for large-scale neuroimaging. To my knowledge, prior structured decompositions (TV-ℓ1 atlases) have been batch/slow; bringing spatial structure into an online, scalable solver is a meaningful technical contribution.
- The choice of Laplacian rather than TV is a design decision; explicit comparison to TV would clarify novelty vs alternatives.

Significance
- The method addresses a relevant and difficult problem: deriving interpretable, reproducible ROIs from large cohorts. Gains in stability and interpretability at modest computational cost are important for practitioners.
- EV lags PCA/ICA, as expected, but Smooth-SODL improves over SODL on small-sample regimes and matches asymptotically, while delivering cleaner atoms and better stability. Behavioral prediction is competitive (often better than SODL and thresholded ICA).
- If released in Nilearn, the impact on the community could be substantial.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, scalable algorithm that augments a widely used online DL framework with spatial structure at modest cost.
- Solid empirical evidence of improved interpretability and stability without sacrificing generalization, plus clear runtime benchmarking.
- Clear positioning within neuroimaging literature; addresses a real need for data-driven ROI discovery at scale.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of theoretical guarantees for inexact BCD with time-varying γ and few FISTA steps; convergence statements are stronger than what is proven.
- Missing comparisons to TV-regularized baselines and to non-online structured methods beyond SODL/CanICA, limiting conclusions about the best choice of spatial prior.
- Limited scope of experiments (single task/dataset, fixed k), and hyperparameter selection is under-specified.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Provide conditions (or a citation) for convergence of inexact BCD with approximate proximal solves and time-varying regularization; otherwise temper the claim.
- Add a TV-regularized baseline (batch or efficient variants, e.g., FAASTA) and discuss trade-offs (smoothness vs edges, runtime).
- Detail hyperparameter search ranges, cross-validation splits, and boundary conditions; include an ablation over k and a second dataset/contrast.
- Clarify the role of non-negativity (simplex) vs signed atoms and report its effect on interpretability and performance.

Overall assessment
The paper meets the NIPS criteria reasonably well: technically sound with a useful algorithmic contribution (Quality), clearly presented with runnable pseudocode and practical insights (Clarity), moderately novel in integrating spatial smoothness into online DL at scale (Originality), and practically significant for neuroimaging ROI learning (Significance). I lean toward acceptance, contingent on tempering convergence claims and strengthening the experimental comparisons. My recommendation is weak accept.
