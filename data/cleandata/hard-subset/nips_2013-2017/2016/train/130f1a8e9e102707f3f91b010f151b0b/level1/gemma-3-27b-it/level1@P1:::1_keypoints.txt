- Proposal: multivariate online dictionary learning combining L1 sparsity and a Laplacian penalty to extract spatially smooth, sparse brain-region components.  
- Novelty: simultaneous use of sparsity and spatial smoothness in a scalable online framework is a genuine contribution.  
- Scalability: online learning addresses large-scale neuroimaging datasets and shows reasonably efficient performance versus CanICA.  
- Theoretical foundation: clear formulation and discussion of convergence properties; connections to GraphNet and related theory.  
- Empirical validation: experiments on the HCP dataset show improved behavioral prediction and comparisons to CanICA and SODL.  
- Practical considerations: hyperparameter tuning and initialization are discussed; implementation planned for release via Nilearn.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: method appears quite sensitive to γ and τ with a narrow optimal range and only qualitative guidance provided.  
- Computational cost: still ~3x slower than vanilla SODL; FFT acceleration mention is buried and bottlenecks need deeper analysis.  
- Baseline comparisons limited: CanICA results shown as thresholded (tCanICA), hindering direct comparison; PCA baseline is weak given structured sparsity focus.  
- Laplacian penalty justification weak: need clearer rationale for assuming blob-like regions and visual examples of its effect on atoms.  
- Reproducibility concerns: code release planned but paper lacks details on random seeds and software versions.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important ROI problem with a novel, scalable method, solid theory, and promising large-dataset results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: sensitivity to hyperparameters, modest speed/complexity tradeoff, and insufficient/fair baseline comparisons.  
- Overall assessment: solid contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: provide systematic hyperparameter analysis, detail computational bottlenecks/optimizations, compare to unthresholded CanICA, visualize/explain Laplacian effects, and give implementation specifics for reproducibility.
