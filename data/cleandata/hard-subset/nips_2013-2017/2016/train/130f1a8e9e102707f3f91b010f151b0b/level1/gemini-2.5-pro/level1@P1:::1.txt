### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL), a method for decomposing large collections of brain images into a set of sparse and spatially structured components, or "atoms". The core idea is to extend the highly scalable online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) [16] by incorporating a spatial smoothness prior on the dictionary atoms. This is achieved by adding a Laplacian regularization term (`v^TΔv`) to the objective function, which encourages the atoms to be smooth, blob-like regions, rather than scattered sets of voxels. The optimization is handled via a Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) scheme that fits within the online learning paradigm. The dictionary update step, which is the main technical novelty, requires solving a convex problem that the authors identify as an instance of GraphNet denoising, solvable efficiently with first-order methods like FISTA.

This work is well-situated within the context of dictionary learning and its applications in neuroimaging. It directly builds upon the foundational online dictionary learning work [16], which made such methods applicable to massive datasets. It addresses a key limitation of standard sparse models in neuroimaging, which often produce noisy, difficult-to-interpret spatial maps. Previous works have incorporated spatial structure, notably using Total Variation (TV) penalties (e.g., Abraham et al. [1], Varoquaux et al. [25]), which are excellent at finding piece-wise constant regions. However, these methods typically rely on slower, batch-mode alternating minimization schemes that do not scale well. The main contribution of this paper is successfully integrating a spatial structure penalty into a fast online framework, thus providing a practical tool for large-scale, structured matrix factorization in neuroimaging.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Practicality:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem: the automated, data-driven discovery of functional brain regions (or atlases) from large population datasets. The proposed method provides a scalable solution that balances data fidelity with crucial priors (sparsity and spatial contiguity) for generating interpretable results. The development of an algorithm that is only 2-3 times slower than its unstructured counterpart makes structured dictionary learning feasible for datasets like the Human Connectome Project (HCP), a significant practical achievement.

2.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare their method against strong and relevant baselines: the unstructured online equivalent (SODL) [16] and a standard neuroimaging pipeline (CanICA) [22]. The evaluation is multi-faceted, covering computational time, qualitative interpretability of the learned dictionaries, quantitative model fit (explained variance on held-out data), model stability across data splits, and performance on a downstream predictive task. This rigorous approach provides strong support for the claims made.

3.  **Key Experimental Insight:** The learning curve analysis presented in Table 1 is particularly compelling. It clearly demonstrates the value of the spatial regularizer, especially in the low-data regime, where Smooth-SODL shows a dramatic (14-fold) improvement in explained variance over SODL. This finding is crucial, as most neuroimaging studies operate in a "small n, large p" setting, where such regularization is most needed. This result elegantly justifies the additional model complexity.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and the methodology is presented with sufficient detail. The connection of the dictionary update subproblem to existing work on GraphNet is insightful and provides a clear path for implementation. The commitment to release the code as part of the widely-used Nilearn library is a major strength, ensuring high potential for impact and reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Incremental Technical Novelty:** While the application and engineering are excellent, the core technical components (online BCD for dictionary learning, Laplacian regularization, FISTA) are all well-established. The primary contribution lies in the successful synthesis and efficient implementation of these components for a specific and important application domain. This is more of an observation on the nature of the contribution than a major flaw.

2.  **Discussion of Regularizer Choice:** The paper motivates the Laplacian penalty primarily on computational grounds, as it integrates cleanly into the online framework. However, there is little discussion on the relative merits of the Laplacian versus other spatial priors, such as the Total Variation (TV) norm, from a modeling perspective. TV penalties encourage piece-wise constant regions with sharp boundaries, which may be more anatomically plausible for defining distinct brain parcels than the smooth regions produced by a Laplacian. A brief discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Hyper-parameter Sensitivity:** As the authors correctly note, hyper-parameter selection is a challenging aspect of dictionary learning models. The proposed model has at least three key parameters (`α`, `γ`, `τ`). While the paper presents a study of their effects and suggests cross-validation as a tuning strategy, this can be computationally demanding. The data-adaptive choice for `α` is helpful, but the model's performance remains sensitive to the choice of the sparsity (`τ`) and smoothness (`γ`) parameters. This practical difficulty could be discussed in more detail.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and practical problem in a high-impact scientific domain (neuroimaging).
*   Presents a novel and effective combination of existing techniques to create a scalable algorithm for structured dictionary learning, a significant improvement over previous non-scalable methods.
*   The empirical evaluation is extensive, well-designed, and provides strong evidence for the method's superiority over relevant baselines, particularly in terms of interpretability, stability, and performance in low-data regimes.
*   The paper is clear, well-written, and promises a public implementation in a major software package, suggesting high potential for impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The core algorithmic components are not new; the contribution is primarily in their novel combination and application.
*   The choice of the Laplacian regularizer over alternatives like Total Variation is justified mainly by convenience, with limited discussion of the modeling implications.
*   The method's performance relies on careful tuning of multiple hyper-parameters, which remains a practical challenge.

**Recommendation:** I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper presents a complete, technically sound, and well-validated piece of work. It makes a significant practical contribution by making structured dictionary learning scalable to massive datasets, which is of great interest to both the machine learning and neuroimaging communities. The strengths, particularly the thoughtful empirical evaluation and high potential for impact, far outweigh the weaknesses.
