Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method for multi-subject brain images in which dictionary atoms (spatial maps) are both sparse and spatially structured. Sparsity is enforced by constraining each atom to lie in an ℓ1-type compact set (e.g., ℓ1-ball or nonnegative simplex), while spatial structure (piecewise smooth “blobs”) is promoted by adding a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty to the atoms. Codes are estimated with ridge regression. The algorithm extends Mairal et al. (2010) online sparse dictionary learning by replacing the atom update from a simple Euclidean projection to a proximal step for a convex quadratic-plus-Laplacian with a projection onto the constraint set, solved by FISTA, cycled over atoms in a block-coordinate scheme. The authors argue that this retains near-linear scalability and only incurs a 2–3× runtime increase over vanilla SODL.

Related work includes: TV-ℓ1 spatial regularization for brain maps and offline structured dictionary learning (e.g., Abraham MICCAI 2013; Varoquaux IPMI 2011), structured sparse PCA (Jenatton et al., AISTATS 2010), GraphNet for spatially smooth sparse models (Grosenick et al., NeuroImage 2013), and recent scalable online matrix factorization/dictionary learning (Mairal JMLR 2010; Mensch ICML 2016). The novelty here is the integration of a Laplacian prior on the dictionary atoms into an online DL framework suitable for very large n and p, with practical heuristics for stable updates.

On HCP task fMRI maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40), the method yields more spatially structured and more reproducible maps than SODL and CanICA, while achieving competitive explained variance and behavioral prediction. Reported runtime is reasonable (≈3× SODL, faster than CanICA in this setup).

Strengths
- Clear and practically motivated objective: learning population-level ROIs that are sparse, spatially coherent, and scalable to large cohorts.
- Sensible modeling choice: Laplacian smoothness trades some of the brittleness of TV for faster, simpler proximal steps with FFT/FISTA possibilities; nonnegativity/simplex constraints support interpretability.
- Scalable algorithm design that closely follows a well-understood online DL framework and preserves its “streaming” nature; simple code updates (ridge) and atom-wise separability.
- Solid empirical evaluation on a large, public dataset with multiple angles: explained variance, stability/reproducibility across splits, qualitative interpretability, and downstream behavioral prediction. The learning-curve analysis nicely illustrates benefits in low/medium data regimes.
- Practical guidance on hyperparameters and implementation details (mini-batches, projections, tuning ranges).

Weaknesses and questions
- Theoretical guarantees with inexact inner solves: While the dictionary subproblem is convex, convergence claims inherited from Mairal (2010) typically require either exact solves or decreasing errors. The paper states that 1–2 FISTA iterations suffice “empirically” and still claims convergence “since all hypotheses of [16] are satisfied.” Please clarify whether you use inexact BCD with summable errors or warm starts, and formalize conditions under which convergence still holds. Otherwise, temper the claim.
- Time-varying γ: To avoid vanishing step sizes, you propose γt scaling with A_t to keep the Lipschitz constant bounded. This changes the objective over time. What is the limiting objective? Is the method optimizing a well-defined surrogate? Empirical impact of annealing γ should be quantified.
- Baselines: The most relevant structured baseline—TV-ℓ1 (or GraphNet/TV-Smoothed) dictionary learning—was not compared, even at smaller scale where offline alternations are feasible. Comparing to a strong structured baseline on a subset would better support the claim that Laplacian regularization is the right compromise between structure and scalability.
- Choice of where sparsity lives: You enforce sparsity on the atoms and ridge on the codes. This differs from common practice (sparse codes), and from prior multi-subject models in neuroimaging that regularize the spatial maps (often with TV) and sometimes also use sparse codes. A brief ablation (ridge vs ℓ1 codes; ℓ1 vs simplex on atoms; with/without nonnegativity) would help disentangle design choices and interpretability.
- Metric details and statistical rigor: Stability/reproducibility is reported as mean correlation across splits, but the matching procedure and confidence intervals are not detailed. Behavioral prediction lacks exact cross-validation protocol and uncertainty estimates. Please report variance/error bars, matching procedures (e.g., Hungarian matching specifics), and statistical tests.
- Laplacian constant and boundary conditions: The Lipschitz bound 1+4Dγ depends on discretization and boundary conditions; please specify (Neumann/Dirichlet; voxel spacing). In brain imaging, anisotropic spacing and masking affect ∥Δ∥; practical guidance would help.
- Claims about “1–2 FISTA iterations to 1e-6 accuracy” are not credible in general; likely you mean small optimality gap given warm starts. Please rephrase and provide actual stopping criteria and empirical gaps.

Quality
- The algorithmic development is technically sound and grounded in convex optimization; however, the convergence discussion should be tightened for inexact inner loops and time-varying regularization.
- Experiments are substantial and relevant, but would benefit from stronger baselines (TV-ℓ1), ablations, and uncertainty reporting.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with actionable algorithmic details. Some typos and terminology (e.g., “intepretable”) should be fixed. Be explicit about the effect of the simplex constraint (nonnegativity) and its motivation.

Originality
- The core idea—Laplacian-regularized atoms in an online DL framework—is an incremental but meaningful extension of established methods. Relative to TV-regularized approaches, the contribution is primarily computational (online, scalable) and practical.

Significance
- For neuroimaging and large-scale matrix factorization, this is useful: improved interpretability and stability at modest extra cost. For a NIPS audience, the methodological novelty is moderate, but the scalable integration and thorough empirical study on a large dataset provide value.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal convergence conditions for inexact inner updates, or empirical evidence showing inner optimality gaps shrinking with iterations.
- Compare against a TV-ℓ1/GraphNet-regularized baseline on a smaller subset to isolate the benefit of Laplacian vs TV.
- Add ablations: codes with ℓ1 vs ridge; atoms with/without nonnegativity; effect of τ vs γ grids; sensitivity plots with confidence intervals.
- Clarify γt annealing and its practical schedule; discuss the limiting objective and robustness.
- Report error bars and statistical tests for EV, stability, and prediction; detail matching and cross-validation procedures.
- Discuss memory footprint and implementation details (sparse operators, FFT options, masking).

Pros
- Scalable, simple algorithm with small overhead over SODL.
- Produces more interpretable and more stable brain maps.
- Strong, large-scale empirical application with multiple evaluation angles.
- Practical implementation likely to be adopted (Nilearn integration).

Cons
- Limited theoretical clarity on inexact updates and time-varying regularization.
- Missing strong structured baselines and ablations.
- Some experimental reporting lacks uncertainty and protocol specifics.
