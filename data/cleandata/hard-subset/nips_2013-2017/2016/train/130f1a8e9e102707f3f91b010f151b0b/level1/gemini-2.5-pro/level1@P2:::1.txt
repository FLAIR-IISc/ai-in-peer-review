### Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper presents a novel method, "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL), for discovering spatially coherent and sparse components from large-scale neuroimaging data, such as fMRI maps. The goal is to learn a dictionary of brain regions (atoms) that can represent individual subject maps as sparse linear combinations. The key contribution is the extension of the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) to incorporate a spatial structure-inducing penalty. Specifically, the authors combine a standard `l1`-norm constraint on the dictionary atoms to enforce sparsity with a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness, encouraging the atoms to form compact, blob-like regions rather than scattered patterns. The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to massive datasets with a large number of subjects, which is a critical need for modern neuroimaging studies like the Human Connectome Project (HCP).

The work is well-situated within existing literature. It directly builds upon the seminal work on online dictionary learning for matrix factorization [16] and adapts it to the specific needs of neuroimaging. While structured sparsity priors (like Total Variation or Laplacian penalties) have been used before in neuroimaging for matrix factorization (e.g., [1, 25]), these have typically relied on slower, offline (batch) optimization schemes that do not scale well. This paper's main novelty lies in successfully integrating a spatial prior into a highly efficient and scalable online learning algorithm, thereby bridging a gap between powerful statistical models and practical computational feasibility for large datasets. The method is benchmarked against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a widely used group Independent Component Analysis (ICA) method in neuroimaging, CanICA [22].

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and challenging problem in computational neuroscience: the data-driven identification of functional brain regions from large population cohorts. As datasets continue to grow in size, scalable methods like the one proposed here are essential. This work provides a principled way to extract interpretable and stable brain networks, which has direct applications in creating functional atlases and biomarkers. The promise to release the code in a popular library (Nilearn) further enhances its potential impact.

2.  **Novel and Effective Algorithm:** The primary contribution—integrating a Laplacian penalty into the online dictionary learning framework—is a non-trivial and valuable extension. The authors provide a clear derivation of the block-coordinate descent update steps and demonstrate empirically that this added structure comes at a modest computational cost (a 2-3x increase in runtime compared to the unstructured version), while remaining significantly faster than competing batch methods like CanICA.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength of this paper. The authors use a large, challenging, and publicly available dataset (HCP). They compare their method against well-chosen and relevant baselines. The evaluation is comprehensive, using a suite of qualitative (visual inspection of maps) and quantitative metrics, including explained variance, stability across data splits, and, importantly, relevance for a downstream task (predicting behavioral scores). The learning curve analysis in Table 1 is particularly effective in demonstrating the value of the spatial prior, showing that Smooth-SODL provides a substantial advantage over unstructured SODL, especially in the more common small-to-medium data regime.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The problem formulation, algorithm, and experimental setup are described with sufficient detail for an expert to understand the work. The figures are informative and support the main claims of the paper effectively.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper rightly acknowledges that selecting the three main hyperparameters (sparsity `τ`, code regularization `α`, and spatial smoothness `γ`) is a difficult problem. While the analysis in Figure 1 is useful for understanding their effects, the proposed solution of using K-fold cross-validation can be computationally very expensive, especially for a method whose main selling point is scalability. A more detailed discussion on practical guidance or a more automated, data-driven approach for setting these parameters would significantly improve the usability of the method.

2.  **Justification for Laplacian Penalty:** The authors choose a Laplacian penalty (`l2`-norm of the gradient) to enforce smoothness. An alternative and widely used regularizer for encouraging piece-wise constant regions with sharp boundaries is the Total Variation (TV) norm (`l1`-norm of the gradient), which is mentioned in related work [1]. A brief discussion on the motivation for choosing the Laplacian penalty over TV would strengthen the paper. For instance, the choice might be justified by the differentiability of the objective function, which simplifies optimization, but this comes at the cost of potentially over-smoothing sharp boundaries between functional regions.

3.  **Predictive Performance Trade-off:** The results in Figure 2(c) show that while Smooth-SODL outperforms other interpretable models (SODL, tCanICA) in predicting behavioral variables, it is still surpassed by the standard (dense, uninterpretable) CanICA model. This suggests a trade-off between model interpretability/stability and raw predictive power. While the authors' focus is on interpretability, this result could be discussed more deeply. It raises the interesting question of whether the imposed structure, while improving interpretability, might be discarding some subject-specific variance that is predictive of behavior.

**Evaluation**

*   **Quality: High.** The paper is technically sound. The algorithmic derivation is correct and builds on established optimization principles. The claims are well-supported by a strong and thorough experimental section. The authors are candid about limitations, such as runtime costs and predictive performance trade-offs.
*   **Clarity: Excellent.** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides clear motivation and context, and the proposed method is explained in sufficient detail.
*   **Originality: Good.** While the core components (online dictionary learning, spatial regularization) are not new in isolation, their combination into a scalable algorithm for this specific and important application is a novel and valuable contribution. It effectively operationalizes structured matrix factorization for large-scale neuroimaging.
*   **Significance: High.** This work offers a practical and powerful tool for a key challenge in a data-rich scientific field. The method is likely to be used by other researchers and provides a solid foundation for future work on scalable, structured models for brain mapping.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution by developing a scalable algorithm for learning structured and interpretable brain networks. The strengths in significance, algorithmic novelty, and empirical validation clearly outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
