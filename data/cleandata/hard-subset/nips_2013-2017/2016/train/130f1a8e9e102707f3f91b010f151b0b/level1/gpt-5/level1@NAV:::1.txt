This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning framework for neuroimaging that enforces sparsity and spatial structure on dictionary atoms by combining an l1 constraint with a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty. Codes are obtained via ridge regression, and dictionary updates are done by block-coordinate descent with FISTA inner steps. The approach scales to large n and p, targeting interpretable, blob-like components that delineate putative brain regions. Empirically, on HCP task fMRI Z-maps, Smooth-SODL yields more structured, reproducible atoms than vanilla sparse online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., JMLR 2010) and denser PCA/ICA baselines (e.g., CanICA), with only a 2–3× runtime overhead. This situates the work between prior structured decompositions using TV/GraphNet penalties (e.g., Abraham MICCAI; Grosenick NeuroImage; Jenatton et al., AISTATS) and scalable online DL (Mairal; Mensch et al., ICML), offering a practically useful synthesis.

Quality: The algorithmic formulation is sound, the optimization is carefully engineered, and convergence inherits standard guarantees of online DL (to local optima). The practical guidance on αt ~ t^-1/2 and γ scheduling (Eq. 9) is valuable. Empirical evaluation covers interpretability, explained variance, stability, prediction, and runtime. However, comparisons omit strong structured baselines (e.g., TV-l1 DL, GraphNet-based factorization), statistical uncertainty is largely absent (no CIs across splits), and only one HCP contrast is shown. The claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations achieve 1e-6 accuracy seems optimistic and should be clarified.

Clarity: Generally well organized, but there are typos and occasional imprecision (e.g., “xperiments,” “intepretable”), and boundary conditions and neighborhood system for the Laplacian are not fully specified. The choice of simplex (nonnegativity) vs. l1-ball should be motivated vis-à-vis negative BOLD effects.

Originality: The combination of Laplacian-regularized atoms with online DL and efficient BCD/FISTA updates is a modest but meaningful advance in scalable structured decompositions. The γ scheduling insight is novel and practical.

Significance: For ML for neuroimaging, the contribution is useful: improved interpretability/stability at small-to-medium sample sizes with scalable training. For the broader NeurIPS audience, impact is moderate.

Pros for acceptance:
- Scalable, practical algorithm with clear engineering contributions
- Improved interpretability and stability with minor runtime cost
- Solid connection to and extension of established online DL
- Useful hyperparameter scheduling guidance

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing comparisons to strongest structured baselines (TV-l1, GraphNet DL)
- Limited dataset/task diversity and no statistical uncertainty
- Some clarity issues and unverified inner-loop convergence claims
- Modest novelty beyond integrating known components

Suggestions: Add TV-l1/GraphNet DL baselines, report CIs over multiple splits, detail Laplacian discretization, ablate simplex vs. l1-ball and ridge vs. lasso codes, and verify inner-loop tolerances.
