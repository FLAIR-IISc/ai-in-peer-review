

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning that incorporates spatial structure through a Laplacian penalty to better extract interpretable brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors build upon Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework, adding a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to enforce piece-wise smooth, compact atoms that better match known brain organization. The method maintains the online scalability of the original work while significantly improving interpretability and capturing inter-subject variability across small, medium, and large datasets.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-motivated problem formulation, rigorous algorithmic development, and comprehensive experimental validation on HCP fMRI data. The convergence guarantees and practical implementation details (including hyperparameter tuning strategies) strengthen the technical contribution. The experimental results clearly show that Smooth-SODL produces more spatially structured, denoised dictionaries compared to SODL and CanICA, with better stability (0.4 vs. 0.1 for CanICA) and comparable predictive performance for behavioral variables.

Clarity is excellent: the paper is well-organized with clear explanations of the method, algorithms, and results. The figures effectively illustrate the interpretability advantages of the proposed approach. The related work section properly contextualizes the contribution against previous structured dictionary learning methods (which were computationally prohibitive) and ICA-based approaches.

The originality is strong - while spatial regularization has been explored in neuroimaging (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2011), this is the first to integrate it with online dictionary learning while maintaining computational efficiency. The significance is high, as the method directly addresses a critical challenge in neuroimaging: extracting interpretable, spatially coherent brain regions from massive datasets without relying on pre-defined ROIs.

The primary weakness is limited discussion of how the Laplacian penalty specifically interacts with the sparsity constraint to achieve the observed benefits. While the authors acknowledge the hyperparameter sensitivity, a deeper theoretical analysis of this interplay would strengthen the contribution. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it advances both the technical capabilities of dictionary learning and its application to a significant scientific domain. The implementation in Nilearn further enhances its practical impact.
