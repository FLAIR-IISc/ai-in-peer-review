

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary of Main Ideas
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework for brain imaging applications. The key innovation is incorporating a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty to impose spatial structure on dictionary atoms, making them piece-wise smooth and compact (blobs) rather than scattered patterns. The authors motivate this approach by arguing that brain regions should be defined based on inter-subject variability rather than anatomical segmentation or predefined ROIs. The paper builds upon previous work in structured dictionary learning (e.g., Abraham et al., 2013) but extends it to an online setting for scalability, addressing a computational limitation of previous structured approaches that used slow alternating minimization.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound extension of the online dictionary learning framework. The optimization problem is well-defined, with clear mathematical formulation of the sparsity (ℓ1) and spatial structure (Laplacian) penalties. The authors derive a Block Coordinate Descent algorithm that incorporates these penalties while maintaining computational efficiency. The convergence analysis is appropriately grounded in existing optimization theory (referencing Bertsekas, 1999), and the implementation details (including the use of FISTA for the dictionary updates) are sound. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against SODL (the baseline) and CanICA on a large-scale HCP dataset (500 subjects, 260k voxels). The authors evaluate multiple metrics including visual quality, explained variance, stability, reproducibility, and predictive performance on behavioral variables. The computational efficiency claim (only 2-3x slower than SODL) is well-supported by empirical results (340s vs. 110s on the same dataset).

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by highlighting limitations of existing ROI approaches and the need for principled region definition. The mathematical formulations are precise, though some readers may require background in image processing to fully appreciate the Laplacian operator implementation. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are sufficiently detailed for implementation, and the experimental results are presented with appropriate visualizations (Figure 2) and quantitative metrics (Table 1). The discussion of hyperparameter selection is particularly thorough, addressing how τ, α, and γ affect the results.

## Originality
The paper's main contribution is the integration of Laplacian regularization into the online dictionary learning framework, which is a non-trivial extension of Mairal et al. (2010). While structured dictionary learning has been explored before (e.g., Abraham et al., 2013; Varoquaux et al., 2014), those approaches used slow alternating minimization rather than online learning. The authors clearly articulate this distinction, noting that previous structured methods were computationally prohibitive for large-scale brain imaging data. The paper demonstrates that their method achieves comparable computational complexity to unstructured online dictionary learning (only 2-3x slower) while adding spatial structure. This represents a meaningful technical advancement over previous structured approaches. However, the paper could more explicitly differentiate their specific contribution from the broader literature on structured dictionary learning.

## Significance
The work addresses an important problem in neuroimaging: defining brain regions that capture meaningful inter-subject variability. The authors demonstrate that their method extracts more interpretable dictionaries that better capture variability, especially in small-to-medium datasets (Figure 2, Table 1). The 14-fold gain in explained variance with limited data (241 subjects vs. 17) is particularly compelling. The method's scalability to large datasets (HCP with 500 subjects) makes it practically useful for modern neuroimaging studies. The ability to predict behavioral variables (Figure 2c) further demonstrates the practical utility of the extracted features. The proposed approach has clear potential to become a standard tool for ROI extraction in brain imaging, as the authors claim.

## Strengths
- Clear extension of online dictionary learning with spatial structure
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple metrics
- Strong computational efficiency claims (only 3x slower than baseline)
- Detailed analysis of hyperparameter effects
- Practical implementation in Nilearn (mentioned in conclusion)
- Demonstrates significant advantages in small-data regimes

## Weaknesses
- The novelty claim could be strengthened by more precise differentiation from previous structured dictionary learning approaches
- Comparison to CanICA is somewhat limited (CanICA is an ICA-based method with no sparsity constraint, making it an imperfect baseline)
- The paper could provide more detail on the 3D implementation of the Laplacian penalty
- Interpretability claims are somewhat subjective; more objective metrics would strengthen the argument
- Limited discussion of potential limitations (e.g., performance on different imaging modalities)

## Recommendation
The paper makes a strong technical contribution to the field of structured dictionary learning with clear relevance to neuroimaging. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrated to be both effective and efficient. While the novelty claim could be slightly strengthened, the paper represents a meaningful advancement over previous structured dictionary learning methods by making them computationally feasible for large-scale brain imaging data. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches, particularly in small-data regimes. The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. I recommend acceptance.
