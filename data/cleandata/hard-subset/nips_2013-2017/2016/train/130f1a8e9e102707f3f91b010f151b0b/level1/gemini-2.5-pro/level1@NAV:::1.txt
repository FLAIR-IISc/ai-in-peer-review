This paper presents "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL), a method for decomposing large-scale brain imaging datasets into a dictionary of sparse and spatially structured components. The core contribution is the extension of the well-known online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) to include a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty enforces spatial smoothness, ensuring the learned components are blob-like and more neuroanatomically plausible than those produced by standard sparsity-only methods. This work offers a scalable, online alternative to previous batch-mode methods that use structured penalties (e.g., TV-l1 penalties) and aims to produce more interpretable components than traditional neuroimaging decomposition techniques like ICA.

The paper has several significant strengths that argue for its acceptance:
*   **Significance:** It addresses a critical and highly relevant problem in neuroimaging: the scalable, data-driven discovery of functional brain regions from massive datasets. The proposed method is a practical tool that could be widely adopted.
*   **Quality:** The technical contribution is sound, and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors use a large, public dataset (HCP) and compare their method against strong and relevant baselines (SODL, CanICA). The evaluation spans multiple criteria, including visual quality, explained variance, stability, and performance on a downstream behavioral prediction task. The analysis showing that the smoothness prior is most beneficial in low-data regimes is a particularly insightful finding.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the method is described in sufficient detail, and the results are presented logically.

The paper also has some minor weaknesses that could be addressed:
*   **Originality:** The contribution, while effective and non-trivial, is an incremental extension combining two well-established techniques (online dictionary learning and Laplacian regularization). The authors are transparent about this, but the conceptual novelty is limited.
*   **Practicality:** The selection of hyperparameters, particularly the smoothness parameter γ, appears to be a delicate issue. While the paper provides an empirical analysis and suggests cross-validation, the method would be strengthened by more concrete guidance or an adaptive scheme for setting this parameter, which could significantly impact the quality of the learned dictionary.
*   **Clarity:** The mention of an alternative dictionary update via FFT is relegated to the supplementary materials. A brief sentence in the main text clarifying the conditions under which this approach is advantageous would be beneficial for the reader.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a well-motivated and technically sound method with a high-quality experimental evaluation. It represents a solid contribution to the field by providing a scalable solution to an important problem in computational neuroscience.
