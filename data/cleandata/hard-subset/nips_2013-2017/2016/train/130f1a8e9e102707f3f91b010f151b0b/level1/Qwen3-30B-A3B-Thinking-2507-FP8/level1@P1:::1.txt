

# Peer Review: "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) for brain imaging applications. The authors address a critical gap in existing methods: standard sparse dictionary learning produces spatially scattered atoms that are not ideal for brain imaging where we expect spatially coherent regions. They introduce a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness (making atoms "blobs" rather than scattered patterns) while maintaining sparsity. The method builds upon Mairal et al.'s framework with only a modest computational cost increase (factor of 2-3), making it scalable to large datasets like HCP (500 subjects). The paper demonstrates that their method produces more interpretable brain regions that better capture inter-subject variability across small, medium, and large datasets compared to SODL (Mairal et al., 2010) and CanICA (Varoquaux et al., 2011).

## Strengths

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound extension of online dictionary learning. The mathematical formulation is clear, and the algorithmic derivation is well-justified. The authors provide a thorough experimental validation on real brain imaging data (HCP task fMRI), including learning curves showing performance gains with small datasets, which addresses a key practical challenge in neuroimaging. The convergence analysis is well-supported by theoretical guarantees from the literature.

**Originality**: The combination of online dictionary learning with spatial structure via Laplacian penalty is novel for this application. The paper clearly differentiates from previous work (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2010; Abraham et al., 2014) which used slower alternating optimization methods. The factor of 2-3 computational slowdown is a significant improvement over prior structured approaches, making the method practically relevant for large-scale brain imaging.

**Significance**: The problem of extracting interpretable brain regions from fMRI data is highly relevant to neuroscience. The authors demonstrate that their method produces more interpretable brain maps (Fig. 2) and better captures inter-subject variability across different data regimes. Their method could become a standard tool for brain imaging analysis, particularly for small datasets where spatial structure is critical.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and logically organized. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the experimental results are clearly presented with appropriate visualizations (Fig. 2). The supplementary materials appear to contain sufficient technical details for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

**Lack of comparison with alternative spatial penalties**: The paper uses a Laplacian penalty but doesn't systematically compare it with other spatial smoothness approaches (e.g., total variation). This is particularly important since TV-based methods have been used successfully in brain imaging (Abraham et al., 2014; Varoquaux et al., 2010). The authors should provide a brief comparison to justify their choice.

**Limited analysis of method limitations**: The paper claims the method works best for small datasets (Fig. 2(c), Table 1) but doesn't thoroughly analyze when the approach might fail. For example, what happens when brain regions are not spatially smooth (e.g., in cases of certain pathologies)? The authors should discuss these limitations.

**Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art structured methods**: While the paper compares with SODL and CanICA, it doesn't compare with other recent structured dictionary learning approaches (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2010; Jenatton et al., 2011). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the claim of superiority.

**Hyperparameter sensitivity**: The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but doesn't fully explore how sensitive the results are to parameter choices beyond the presented figures. A more systematic analysis would be valuable.

## Arguments for and against acceptance

**For acceptance**:
- Novel combination of online learning with spatial structure for brain imaging
- Clear computational advantage over previous structured approaches
- Thorough experimental validation showing improved interpretability and performance across data regimes
- Practical implementation in Nilearn library (mentioned in paper)
- Well-written with clear theoretical and experimental contributions

**Against acceptance**:
- Limited comparison with alternative spatial smoothness penalties
- Incomplete discussion of method limitations
- Some claims about performance advantages could be better supported with more comprehensive comparisons
- The learning curve (Table 1) is somewhat limited to one specific dataset size

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of machine learning and neuroimaging. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses a real problem in brain imaging analysis. The computational efficiency of the proposed method (only 2-3x slower than standard SODL) is particularly impressive and makes the approach practically useful for large-scale brain imaging studies.

While the paper has some minor weaknesses in the comparative analysis and discussion of limitations, these do not undermine the overall contribution. The work is clearly significant for the neuroimaging community, and the method has the potential to become a standard tool for brain region extraction.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS 2016. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a valuable contribution to the field. The minor weaknesses noted above could be addressed in a camera-ready version with additional comparisons and a more thorough discussion of limitations.
