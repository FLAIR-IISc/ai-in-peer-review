Summary
The paper proposes Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), an online matrix factorization framework that learns dictionary atoms that are both sparse (via an l1-type constraint on atoms) and spatially structured (via a Laplacian/Sobolev penalty). Codes are obtained by ridge regression. The method extends the online sparse dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty during the dictionary update, solved with a proximal-gradient step (FISTA) followed by projection onto a simple convex set (e.g., l1-ball or simplex). The authors argue this adds only a small constant-factor overhead.

Empirically, on HCP task fMRI Z-maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40), Smooth-SODL is compared to standard SODL and a multi-subject PCA/ICA baseline (CanICA). The results suggest: (i) smoother, more blob-like atoms than SODL; (ii) improved stability/reproducibility across splits compared to SODL and CanICA; (iii) explained variance lower than CanICA but higher than SODL; and (iv) competitive behavioral prediction, better than SODL and thresholded CanICA but worse than CanICA. Runtime is reported to be ≈3x SODL and faster than CanICA on a single CPU.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on online dictionary learning (Mairal et al.), and is related to structured sparsity frameworks such as GraphNet (Grosenick et al.) and structured sparse PCA (Jenatton et al.). Total-variation and TV-l1 penalties have been used for spatially-structured decompositions in neuroimaging (Abraham et al.; Varoquaux et al.), but typically via slow alternating minimization. Recent scalable dictionary learning (e.g., Mensch et al.) addresses speed but not spatial structure. The contribution here is the integration of Laplacian structure into an online BCD scheme at scale, with convergence/stationarity guarantees akin to Mairal et al.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Formulation: The objective and constraints are standard, and the per-atom subproblem is convex. Using ridge codes and an l1/simplex-constraint on atoms is reasonable: signed variability can be handled through signed codes even with nonnegative atoms.
- Optimization: The code step solves a ridge problem efficiently via SVD. The dictionary update reduces to a smooth quadratic + Laplacian with a simple constraint, solvable by proximal-gradient with projection; Lipschitz bound L=1+γ̃·(spectral norm of Laplacian) is reasonable, though the fixed constant 4D assumes a specific discretization and boundary condition—the paper should clarify this. The claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations suffice to 1e-6 accuracy is unlikely; more plausible is that few iterations suffice to reach a good practical solution. The subproblem is convex, hence the per-atom update achieves a global optimum.
- Convergence: The online BCD inherits Mairal et al.’s convergence under standard assumptions. However, the proposed scaling suggestion for γ_t (Eq. 9) to avoid vanishing step sizes is discussed, while experiments seem to use fixed γ. Clarification on whether γ is adapted online is needed.
- Complexity: The added Laplacian gradient step is linear in p and compatible with large p. The reported runtime improvements appear optimistic given n, p, and k; details such as number of passes/epochs, stopping criteria, and FFT implementation would improve credibility.
- Experiments: Quantitative metrics (explained variance, stability via Hungarian matching, predictive R2) are appropriate. However, reported results lack confidence intervals or statistical tests, and some evaluation details (exact EV computation, tCanICA threshold choice, number of runs) are missing.

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable and well-structured, but several issues reduce clarity:
- Typos and minor inconsistencies (“xperiments,” “intepretable,” section numbering “.2,” etc.).
- The Lipschitz constant derivation for the Laplacian, boundary conditions, and grid spacing should be specified.
- Algorithmic details: number of epochs T, mini-batch size rationale, stopping conditions for FISTA and the outer BCD loop, and whether γ_t or α_t schedules are actually used in experiments.
- The hyperparameter selection section is informative but refers to figures not present here; a concise, reproducible strategy (e.g., ranges, CV protocol) would help.
- The claim “parameter-free” for BCD is misleading; while step sizes are fixed, several hyperparameters (α, γ, τ, k) still require tuning.

Originality
The main novelty is incorporating a Laplacian structural prior into an online dictionary learning scheme in a way that preserves scalability and practical convergence. While Laplacian/TV regularization is not new in neuroimaging decompositions, prior work typically used slower solvers. This paper’s contribution is incremental but meaningful: a practical algorithmic integration and empirical validation at scale.

Significance
Interpretable, stable decompositions that respect spatial structure are important for ROI discovery and group-level neuroimaging analysis. The method seems to improve interpretability and stability at modest computational cost, which is valuable for large datasets. The empirical scope is somewhat narrow (one task contrast), and comparisons omit TV-l1 or other structured baselines due to speed; nonetheless, the approach is likely to be used and extended, especially if integrated in Nilearn.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, practical extension of a well-known online framework to handle spatial structure.
- Scalable implementation with modest overhead relative to SODL.
- Improved interpretability and stability over SODL; competitive predictive performance.
- Relevance to large-scale neuroimaging; potential impact via open-source release.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited experimental breadth: single dataset/task; no direct comparison to TV-l1/structured baselines (even at small scale).
- Missing methodological details (epochs, stopping, exact hyperparameter protocols); some claims (FISTA iterations, runtime) seem optimistic.
- Lack of uncertainty quantification (error bars) and ablation (effect of Laplacian vs atom sparsity vs code penalty).
- Some clarity issues and typos; inconsistencies regarding γ scheduling.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full experimental details: number of passes, stopping criteria, hardware, and whether FFT is used; report wall-clock per epoch.
- Add error bars over multiple splits/runs and statistical tests for EV, stability, and prediction metrics.
- Include a small-scale comparison to a TV-l1 or structured sparse PCA baseline to contextualize the choice of Laplacian vs TV.
- Clarify the Laplacian discretization and Lipschitz bound; correct the FISTA convergence claim or rephrase as practical sufficiency.
- Explicitly state whether γ_t and α_t schedules are used in reported experiments; if constant, justify and discuss sensitivity.
- Add ablations: ridge vs l1 codes; with/without nonnegativity; effect of k.

Overall
A solid, incremental contribution that addresses an important practical gap (scalable structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging). With improved experimental rigor and clarity, it would be a strong paper.
