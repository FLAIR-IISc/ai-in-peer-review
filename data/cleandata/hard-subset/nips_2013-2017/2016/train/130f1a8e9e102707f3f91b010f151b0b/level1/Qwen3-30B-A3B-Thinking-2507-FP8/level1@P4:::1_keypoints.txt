- Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online dictionary learning extension with a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty to produce piece-wise smooth, compact brain-region atoms.  
- Motivates region definition based on inter-subject variability rather than anatomical segmentation or predefined ROIs.  
- Extends prior structured dictionary learning to an online, scalable setting, addressing computational limits of earlier alternating-minimization methods.  
- Optimization is well-formulated combining ℓ1 sparsity and Laplacian spatial regularization.  
- Derives a Block Coordinate Descent algorithm and uses FISTA for efficient dictionary updates.  
- Convergence analysis is appropriately grounded in existing optimization theory.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against SODL and CanICA on a large HCP dataset (500 subjects, 260k voxels).  
- Metrics include visual quality, explained variance, stability, reproducibility, and behavioral prediction.  
- Empirical computational efficiency: only 2–3× slower than unstructured SODL (340s vs. 110s).  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; algorithms and implementation details are sufficiently described.  
- Hyperparameter selection (τ, α, γ) is discussed in detail.  
- Originality: non-trivial integration of Laplacian regularization into online dictionary learning, enabling structured atoms at near-online complexity.  
- Could more explicitly differentiate the specific contribution from broader structured dictionary learning literature.  
- Significance: addresses an important neuroimaging problem, scales to large datasets, and shows strong gains in limited-data regimes (14× explained variance improvement in a small sample).  
- Strengths: clear methodological extension, comprehensive experiments, strong efficiency claims, thorough hyperparameter analysis, practical Nilearn implementation, benefits in small-data settings.  
- Weaknesses: novelty claim could be sharpened; imperfect baseline comparison to CanICA; lacks detail on 3D Laplacian implementation; interpretability claims partly subjective; limited discussion of modality-specific limitations.  
- Recommendation: accept — technically sound, effective, efficient, reproducible, and a meaningful advancement for large-scale neuroimaging region extraction.
