**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method, called Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The method combines an `1-norm constraint for sparsity and a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty for imposing piece-wise smooth and compact structures. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on a large public dataset, showing improvements over traditional `1-regularized dictionary learning and other state-of-the-art methods.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon the online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) and extends it to incorporate spatial structure. The authors also compare their approach to other methods, such as Canonical ICA (CanICA) and sparse online dictionary-learning (SODL), highlighting the advantages of their approach in terms of interpretability and performance.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective incorporation of spatial structure**: The paper proposes a novel approach to incorporating spatial structure into dictionary learning, which is essential for brain imaging applications.
2. **Improved interpretability**: The authors demonstrate that their approach leads to more interpretable dictionaries, which is critical for understanding brain function and organization.
3. **Competitive performance**: The paper shows that Smooth-SODL outperforms other state-of-the-art methods in terms of explained variance, stability, and prediction of behavioral variables.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational complexity**: While the authors claim that their approach is computationally efficient, the running time of their algorithm is still significantly higher than that of SODL.
2. **Hyper-parameter tuning**: The paper highlights the importance of hyper-parameter tuning, but the authors do not provide a clear strategy for selecting the optimal values of these parameters.
3. **Limited evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the performance of Smooth-SODL on a single dataset, which may not be representative of all brain imaging applications.
