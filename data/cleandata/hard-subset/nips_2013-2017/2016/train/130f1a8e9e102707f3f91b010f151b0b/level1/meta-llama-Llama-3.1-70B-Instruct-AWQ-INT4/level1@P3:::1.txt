**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary-learning method, called Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The method extends the online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on a large public dataset, showing improvements over traditional `1-regularized dictionary learning in terms of interpretability and ability to capture inter-subject variability.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Novel extension of online dictionary learning**: The paper proposes a novel extension of online dictionary learning by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to impose spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. This is a significant contribution to the field of neuroimaging analysis.
2. **Improved interpretability**: The authors demonstrate that their method produces dictionaries that are more interpretable and better capture inter-subject variability compared to traditional `1-regularized dictionary learning.
3. **Scalability**: The method is designed to scale to arbitrarily sized datasets, making it suitable for large-scale neuroimaging analysis.
4. **Theoretical guarantees**: The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the convergence of their algorithm, which is a significant advantage over other methods.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Computational cost**: The method is slower than traditional `1-regularized dictionary learning, which may be a limitation for very large datasets.
2. **Hyper-parameter tuning**: The authors acknowledge that hyper-parameter tuning is a difficult problem in dictionary learning, and their method is no exception.
3. **Comparison to other methods**: While the authors compare their method to traditional `1-regularized dictionary learning and CanICA, they do not compare it to other state-of-the-art methods in neuroimaging analysis.
