This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, aiming to extract structured and sparse components representing brain regions. The core idea is to combine a sparsity-inducing L1 penalty with a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially smooth, blob-like dictionary atoms. The method extends prior work on online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) and is demonstrated on fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project.

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in neuroimaging and machine learning. The motivation—addressing inter-subject variability and defining regions of interest (ROIs)—is a central challenge in the field. The use of dictionary learning for this purpose connects to a growing trend in applying sparse decomposition techniques to neuroimaging data (e.g., Abraham, 2016; Baldassarre et al., 2013). The authors correctly position their work within the context of clustering-based ROI definition (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2012) and PCA/ICA approaches (e.g., Beckmann & Smith, 2004), highlighting the novelty of combining sparsity and spatial smoothness in an online learning framework.  Related work such as structured sparsity models (Varol & Davatzikos, 2011) and TV-L1 minimization (Dohmatob, 2015) are also appropriately referenced.  The paper acknowledges recent advances in online learning (Mensch, 2014) but argues its approach allows for integration of structured penalties more efficiently than existing methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of L1 and Laplacian penalties within an *online* dictionary learning framework appears to be a genuine contribution. While individual components (sparse and structured representations) have been explored, their simultaneous application in a scalable online setting is a valuable extension.
* **Scalability:**  Leveraging online learning addresses a crucial limitation of many existing methods when dealing with large-scale neuroimaging datasets.  The authors demonstrate reasonably efficient performance compared to other methods (CanICA).
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The formulation of the problem and the discussion of convergence properties (citing Bertsekas, 1999) lend credibility to the approach. The link to GraphNet models is insightful.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on the HCP dataset, using a well-established benchmark, provide reasonable evidence of the method's effectiveness.  The comparison to CanICA and standard SODL is relevant. The demonstration of improved performance in predicting behavioral variables strengthens the findings.
* **Practical Considerations:** The inclusion of a discussion on hyperparameter tuning and initialization is a sign of a well-considered approach. The intention to release the implementation via Nilearn is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper addresses hyperparameter tuning, Fig. 1 suggests the method is quite sensitive to the choice of γ and τ. The optimal range appears narrow, and the guidance provided is somewhat qualitative. A more systematic analysis of hyperparameter impact would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** While scalable, the method is still 3x slower than the vanilla SODL. A more detailed analysis of the computational bottleneck and potential optimizations would be valuable. The comment about FFT acceleration is buried at the end of Section 2.3.
* **Comparison to Baselines:**  The comparison to CanICA is somewhat limited, particularly as the CanICA results are presented as *thresholded* versions (tCanICA), making a direct comparison of unthresholded models difficult. The PCA baseline is also a bit weak, given the focus on structured sparsity.
* **Clarity of Laplacian Penalty:** The justification for using the Laplacian penalty (i.e., *why* blobs are a good assumption for brain regions) could be more thoroughly explained.  A visual illustration of the effect of the Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms would be helpful.
* **Reproducibility:** While the intention to release code is great, ensuring full reproducibility would necessitate details on random seeds and specific software versions used.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging – defining brain regions in a data-driven manner – and proposes a novel and scalable solution.
* The method shows promising results on a large, publicly available dataset.
* The theoretical foundation and discussion of practical considerations are well-developed.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The method's sensitivity to hyperparameters and the relatively modest computational speedup compared to simpler methods raise concerns.
* The comparison to baselines could be more comprehensive and fairer.
* The justification for the Laplacian penalty could be strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The novelty of combining sparsity and spatial smoothness in an online learning framework is significant, and the empirical results are encouraging. While the method has some limitations, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1. Provide a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and provide more concrete guidance on hyperparameter selection.
2. Elaborate on the computational bottlenecks and explore potential optimizations.
3. Include a more comprehensive comparison to relevant baselines, including a direct comparison to untrhesolded CanICA.
4. Provide a more intuitive explanation of the rationale for using the Laplacian penalty and perhaps include a visual example.
5. Detail the implementation specifics (random seeds, software versions) to facilitate reproducibility.
