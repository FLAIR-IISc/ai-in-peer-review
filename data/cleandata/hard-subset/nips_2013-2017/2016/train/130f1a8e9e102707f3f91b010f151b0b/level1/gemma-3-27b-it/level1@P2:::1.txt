This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, aiming to identify brain regions with structured and sparse components. The core idea is to combine an L1 penalty for sparsity with a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness, leading to blob-like structures representing brain regions. The method extends existing online dictionary learning work (Mairal et al., 2010) and demonstrates promising results on HCP fMRI data.

**Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound. The combination of L1 and Laplacian penalties is a reasonable approach for extracting spatially localized and sparse features from brain images. The extension of online dictionary learning is well-motivated, and the authors acknowledge the computational cost increase (factor of 2-3). The theoretical justification for the Laplacian penalty inducing smoothness is solid, and the connection to graph-based methods like GraphNet is insightful. The experiments appear well-designed, comparing against relevant baselines (CanICA, SODL) and utilizing a large dataset (HCP). The ablation study showing the impact of the Laplacian penalty is particularly strong. The algorithmic details are provided, including convergence guarantees (referencing previous work). However, the discussion of the Lipschitz constant and its impact on convergence feels a bit rushed and could benefit from more clarity. The practical considerations section, especially concerning hyperparameter tuning, is detailed and helpful.

**Clarity (7/10):** The paper is generally well-written, but some sections could be improved for clarity. The notation is consistent, but the mathematical formulations might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with dictionary learning and Sobolev spaces. The transition between the introduction and the detailed method description could be smoother. While the authors attempt to explain the rationale behind each step, some explanations are concise and require the reader to already be familiar with the background. For example, the description of the block coordinate descent (BCD) algorithm could be expanded. The explanation of the relationship between the problem setup and existing work (PCA, ICA) in the introduction is a bit meandering.  The experimental section is well-structured, but the figures could benefit from clearer labels and explanations.

**Originality (7/10):** The core idea of combining L1 and Laplacian penalties for dictionary learning isn't entirely novel—similar approaches have been used in image processing and other fields. However, its application to multivariate brain imaging and specifically within an *online* dictionary learning framework is a significant contribution. The extension of the Mairal et al. (2010) work to handle structured sparsity in this context is a valuable addition. The authors do a reasonable job of citing related work, but a more thorough discussion of existing methods for spatially regularized dictionary learning in neuroimaging (e.g., TV-L1 methods) would strengthen the originality argument.  While they mention [1, 25], a more comprehensive overview of the landscape would be useful.

**Significance (8/10):** The results are significant. The method demonstrates improved performance compared to existing approaches in extracting interpretable and structured brain regions. The ability to handle large-scale datasets is a key advantage. The fact that the method can capture inter-subject variability and predict behavioral scores suggests potential applications in understanding brain function and individual differences. The online learning aspect is crucial for scalability, making the method applicable to increasingly large neuroimaging datasets. The comparison to CanICA and SODL provides strong evidence of the benefits of the proposed approach. The demonstration that spatial regularization is particularly helpful when data is limited is important.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-motivated extension of online dictionary learning.
*   Demonstrated improvement over state-of-the-art methods on a large, publicly available dataset (HCP).
*   Scalable to large datasets due to the online learning framework.
*   Potential for applications in understanding brain function and individual differences.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation with ablation studies.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The clarity of the mathematical formulations and some explanations could be improved.
*   The originality is incremental rather than groundbreaking.  The core idea has precedents in related fields.
*   The discussion of the convergence properties, particularly the Lipschitz constant, is somewhat limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a solid technical contribution with significant potential impact. While the clarity could be improved in some areas and the originality is incremental, the strong experimental results, scalability, and relevance to neuroimaging make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. The authors have addressed a practical problem in brain imaging and proposed a viable solution with clear advantages over existing methods.
