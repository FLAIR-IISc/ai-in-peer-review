## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a novel multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, aiming to identify spatially coherent brain regions. The core idea is to combine sparsity-inducing L1 regularization with a Laplacian penalty to encourage smoothness and compactness of the learned dictionary atoms (representing brain regions). This extends the existing online dictionary learning work of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating spatial structure, with a reported modest increase in runtime. The motivation stems from the limitations of univariate models and the need for principled, data-driven region-of-interest (ROI) definition in neuroimaging, moving beyond traditional segmentation or reliance on pre-defined atlases.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a relevant problem in neuroimaging – the extraction of meaningful spatial patterns from large-scale brain data. The proposed method builds logically upon established techniques (online dictionary learning, sparsity, and smoothness priors) and presents a clear mathematical formulation. The experiments on the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset are a significant strength, demonstrating the method's scalability and providing a comparative analysis against ICA-based approaches (CanICA) and standard online dictionary learning. The results suggest that the proposed method achieves a better trade-off between sparsity, interpretability, and predictive power, particularly when data is limited. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge the increased runtime compared to standard SODL, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and scalability with varying data sizes would be beneficial. The hyperparameter tuning discussion, while present, feels somewhat cursory. The impact of different choices for the sparsity constraint set (simplex vs. l1-ball) isn't thoroughly explored. Furthermore, the comparison to CanICA is somewhat unfair, as CanICA isn’t explicitly designed for sparse representations. A comparison to other spatially regularized decomposition methods (e.g., those employing Total Variation regularization) would strengthen the contribution. Finally, the writing could be more concise; the paper is somewhat verbose and repetitive.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in neuroimaging.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated method combining sparsity and smoothness.
*   Demonstrates scalability and promising results on a large, publicly available dataset.
*   Offers a potential alternative to traditional ROI definition methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational analysis could be more thorough.
*   Hyperparameter tuning discussion is limited.
*   Comparison to other spatially regularized methods is missing.
*   Writing could be more concise.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution with the potential to be valuable to the neuroimaging community. While some improvements in clarity, computational analysis, and comparative evaluation would be beneficial, the novelty of the approach and promising experimental results warrant acceptance, potentially with minor revisions.
