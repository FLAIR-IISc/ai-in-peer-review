The paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images into structured and sparse components. The core idea is to combine an l1-norm penalty for sparsity with a Laplacian penalty to enforce smoothness and compactness of the learned dictionary atoms, effectively representing brain regions as “blobs.” The method extends existing online dictionary learning approaches and aims to address inter-subject variability in neuroimaging data.

This work builds upon previous research in dictionary learning for neuroimaging, specifically referencing Mairal et al. (2010) and related online learning techniques. It also connects to existing methods for region-of-interest (ROI) definition, clustering approaches in brain imaging (e.g., [21, 14]), and independent/principal component analysis (ICA/PCA) methods [5]. The authors position their work as a bridge between sparse decomposition methods and spatially structured approaches, addressing limitations of both. Related work in structured sparsity [1, 3] and online learning [16, 17] is also discussed.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of l1 and Laplacian penalties within an *online* dictionary learning framework appears to be a novel contribution. While spatial regularization has been used in dictionary learning before [1], implementing it efficiently in an online setting is a significant advance, particularly for large datasets.
*   **Scalability:** The online learning approach is explicitly designed to handle large-scale datasets, a crucial requirement for modern neuroimaging analysis.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the model (2) is well-defined, and the authors discuss convergence properties based on existing literature [6, 16]. The derivation of the algorithm (Algorithms 1 & 2) seems reasonable.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on HCP data provide some evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The results demonstrate improvements in explained variance and predictive power compared to SODL and CanICA, particularly in the initial stages of learning and when predicting behavioral variables.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The algorithm is described in detail, and the experimental setup is clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning acknowledges the difficulty of parameter selection (α, γ, τ), and while a data-adaptive approach for α is proposed, the optimal setting for γ is determined via cross-validation, which adds computational cost. The sensitivity analysis presented in Fig. 1 is somewhat limited. A more systematic exploration of the parameter space, and its effect on the results, would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:** While the method is scalable, the running time is still significantly slower than the base SODL algorithm (3x slower). The authors attribute this to the Laplacian penalty, but a more detailed analysis of the computational bottleneck would be valuable.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to CanICA, while present, feels incomplete. The authors acknowledge the need for thresholding CanICA for interpretability, but the comparison with the thresholded version (tCanICA) is limited. A more thorough comparison with other established ROI definition or spatial clustering methods would strengthen the paper.
*   **Clarity of Laplacian Penalty:** While the mathematical definition of the Laplacian penalty is provided, the intuition behind its impact on the learned dictionary atoms could be further clarified with illustrative examples.
*   **Lack of Ablation Study:** An ablation study examining the contribution of each penalty (l1 and Laplacian) individually would help to isolate the benefits of the combined approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The method presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging.
*   The online learning framework enables scalability to large datasets.
*   The experimental results demonstrate promising performance improvements over existing methods.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost is higher than the base SODL algorithm.
*   The hyperparameter tuning process is complex and requires cross-validation.
*   The comparison to state-of-the-art methods could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging analysis. The proposed method offers a promising approach to extracting structured and interpretable brain regions from large-scale data. While there are some minor weaknesses related to computational cost, hyperparameter tuning, and the breadth of the comparative analysis, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should: 1) Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost and potential optimizations. 2) Expand the hyperparameter sensitivity analysis and discuss guidelines for parameter selection. 3) Conduct a more thorough comparison to relevant state-of-the-art methods, including a fair comparison with thresholded CanICA. 4) Consider including an ablation study to assess the individual contributions of the l1 and Laplacian penalties.
