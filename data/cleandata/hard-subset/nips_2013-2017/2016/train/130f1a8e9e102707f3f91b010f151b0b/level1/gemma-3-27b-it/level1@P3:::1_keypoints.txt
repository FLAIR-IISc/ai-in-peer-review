- Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online multivariate dictionary learning method combining L1 sparsity and a Laplacian spatial smoothness penalty to decompose brain images.  
- Demonstrates scalability and improved interpretability on HCP data, capturing inter-subject variability better than ICA and standard SODL.  
- Addresses the important neuroimaging problem of principled ROI definition learned from inter-subject variability.  
- Method is a logical extension of existing online dictionary learning techniques and handles large-scale datasets with spatial priors.  
- Technical approach is sound: BCD optimization adapted from Mairal et al. (2010) with a well-justified Laplacian penalty and clear math.  
- Online framework provides a major scalability advantage over batch methods for high voxel counts and many subjects.  
- Empirical validation on HCP is convincing, with relevant comparisons to CanICA and SODL and an ablation study showing benefits, especially with limited data.  
- Paper is well-written and organized, with clear motivation and logical method presentation.  
- Practical considerations (hyperparameter tuning, computational cost discussion) and Nilearn availability are useful.  
- Novelty is limited: components (sparsity, smoothness, online learning) are not individually new; need clearer argument for the specific combination’s uniqueness.  
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters (α, γ, τ) is a concern; parameter space exploration and robustness analysis are insufficient.  
- Comparison to CanICA is uneven and potentially unfair; CanICA parameters and post-processing should be optimized for a rigorous comparison, and its scaling limitations should be stated.  
- Behavioral prediction results are modest (low R2); more analysis of which behaviors are predicted and comparison to simple baselines is needed.  
- Limited theoretical analysis: convergence and the specific impact of the Laplacian penalty on solutions are under-discussed.  
- Computational cost discussion lacks detail on bottlenecks and optimization strategies beyond using FISTA.  
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, technically sound, scalable, promising empirical results, and practical utility.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, hyperparameter sensitivity, uneven baselines, and limited theory.  
- Overall recommendation: accept, contingent on revisions addressing hyperparameter sensitivity, fairer CanICA comparison, and deeper theoretical discussion of the Laplacian penalty.
