Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework tailored to neuroimaging that produces spatially sparse and smooth components (“atoms”). The key idea is to regularize the dictionary atoms, not the codes: sparsity is enforced by constraining atoms to an ℓ1 ball (or simplex for nonnegativity), and spatial coherence is encouraged via a quadratic Laplacian penalty. Codes are estimated via ridge regression. Optimization follows the online/stochastic surrogate minimization of Mairal et al. (2010), with block coordinate updates: ridge-coded mini-batches, and per-atom projected (accelerated) gradient steps on a strongly convex quadratic with Laplacian. The authors argue that this integrates spatial structure at a modest computational overhead (≈2–3× SODL), while scaling to large n and p.

This sits between sparse dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) and spatially structured decompositions (e.g., TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning for brain maps; Abraham et al., MICCAI 2014; structured sparse PCA, Jenatton et al., AISTATS 2010; GraphNet, Grosenick et al., NeuroImage 2013). Unlike prior spatially structured approaches that rely on slow alternating minimization, the contribution is an online algorithm with a Laplacian prior integrated in the dictionary-update step.

Experiments on HCP task fMRI compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA. The authors report: (i) improved spatial interpretability and stability of atoms; (ii) higher explained variance than SODL (though below ICA), especially in low-data regimes; (iii) competitive behavioral prediction vs baselines; and (iv) reasonable run time.

Quality
- Correctness and algorithmic soundness: The formulation is sensible, and the per-atom subproblem is a convex quadratic with a simple Lipschitz constant and an easy projection onto C (ℓ1-ball or simplex). The online surrogate and block-coordinate updates follow Mairal et al. However, there are a few technical ambiguities:
  - The paper alternates between claiming exact per-atom minimization and using “1–2 iterations of FISTA” with 10^-6 accuracy. Two iterations cannot guarantee such accuracy in general; if warm starts yield near-stationary points, please clarify. If approximate updates are used, discuss implications for the online convergence guarantees.
  - The Lipschitz constant and γ schedule: the dependence Lt = 1 + 4D γ t / A_t[j,j] suggests γ should decay with t; yet earlier γ is treated as a fixed hyperparameter tuned by CV. Reconcile these viewpoints or provide a principled adaptive schedule and state how it was used in experiments.
  - Constraint set: using a simplex implies nonnegative atoms; many fMRI contrasts contain positive and negative lobes. The choice should be justified and its effect ablated (ℓ1-ball with signs vs simplex).
- Empirical evaluation: The experimental design is broadly reasonable (large dataset, multiple metrics). Strengths include split-half stability via Hungarian matching, learning curves, and behavior prediction. Yet several details are missing:
  - Fairness and preprocessing: CanICA is run with spatial smoothing; SODL/Smooth-SODL are not. This choice affects both EV and interpretability. Consider equalizing smoothing or comparing to spatially constrained ICA.
  - Hyperparameter selection: How were τ and γ chosen (folds, grids, held-out split)? The paper mentions CV and “safe ranges” but lacks specifics. Provide sensitivity plots (Fig. 1 is referenced but not explained here).
  - Behavior prediction: Specify the target regression model, regularization selection, cross-validation protocol, and confound control (e.g., head motion, age, sex). Report confidence intervals or statistical tests for differences.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Pseudocode is helpful, and the connection to GraphNet-like denoising is clear. Some notation/typos (“xperiments”, “intepretable”) and minor LaTeX issues aside, the main derivations are understandable. Please clarify:
- Boundary conditions and masking for the Laplacian (and whether FFT-based updates require periodic boundaries).
- Memory and computational complexity per iteration (cost of applying Δ, storage of B_t ∈ R^{p×k}).
- Exact projection operator when both Laplacian penalty and simplex/ℓ1 constraints are present (accelerated projected gradient is implied).

Originality
The idea of embedding a Laplacian penalty in online dictionary learning to enforce spatially smooth, sparse atoms in neuroimaging is a modest but meaningful extension of established methods. Compared to TV-based approaches (which better promote piecewise constancy/compactness), the choice of Laplacian trades edge preservation for speed and simplicity; framing this trade-off and demonstrating that online integration is practical is the novelty.

Significance
The contribution is practical and likely useful to the community: scalable, structured decompositions with better interpretability and stability than SODL and without the computational burden of TV. The improvements in small-sample regimes and the moderate computational overhead are compelling for large neuroimaging cohorts. That said, improving interpretability/stability with a Laplacian over SODL is not unexpected; the significance hinges on robust evidence and fair comparisons.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, practical algorithm extending a widely used online DL method to structured atoms with minor overhead.
- Large-scale neuroimaging application with multiple evaluation axes; improved stability and interpretability.
- Solid engineering choices (mini-batching, warm starts) and an implementation plan (Nilearn).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond integrating a standard quadratic smoothness prior into Mairal’s framework.
- Evaluation fairness and hyperparameter selection are under-specified; some choices (nonnegativity, smoothing) may bias comparisons.
- Theoretical claims about convergence vs approximate per-atom solves and γ scheduling need tightening.
- Lack of ablations (Ridge vs ℓ1 codes; simplex vs signed atoms; Laplacian vs TV on smaller subsets).

Suggestions
- Provide an ablation study isolating the effects of (i) Laplacian, (ii) nonnegativity, and (iii) code penalty (Ridge vs ℓ1).
- Clarify and, if used, justify an adaptive γ_t schedule; otherwise, bound Lt and report the chosen fixed γ.
- Add a small-scale comparison to a TV-regularized dictionary learner to illustrate interpretability/sparsity differences versus computational cost.
- Equalize preprocessing across methods or include spatial ICA baselines; report EV/stability with and without smoothing.
- Expand behavior prediction methodology, control confounds, and report uncertainty.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. While the methodological advance is incremental, the work addresses an important practical gap (scalable spatially structured decompositions) and presents promising empirical benefits. Strengthening the clarity of the optimization details and the fairness/rigor of the evaluation would solidify the case.
