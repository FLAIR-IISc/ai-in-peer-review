## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the crucial problem of defining Regions of Interest (ROIs) in neuroimaging, moving beyond manual segmentation or relying on other modalities. The authors propose a novel approach utilizing the inter-subject variability within statistical maps to learn these ROIs via a structured sparse online dictionary learning (Smooth-SODL) method.  The core idea is to decompose population-level brain images into a set of spatially localized, sparse “atoms” representing brain regions.  The method extends existing online dictionary learning techniques by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness, resulting in more interpretable and robust regions. The paper presents a detailed algorithm, theoretical justification, and empirical validation on a large-scale HCP dataset, demonstrating improved performance compared to standard online dictionary learning and ICA-based methods.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions its contribution within the context of ROI definition in neuroimaging, highlighting the limitations of univariate methods and the challenges of segmentation and cross-modality correspondence. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work in clustering-based ROI definition, PCA/ICA for functional unit extraction, and sparse coding techniques.  They accurately note the existing limitations in scaling structured decompositions to large datasets, which their online learning approach directly addresses. The connections drawn to TV-l1 minimization and related work in structured sparsity are relevant and well-cited.  It would be helpful to briefly mention recent advances in deep learning approaches for brain parcellation, though the authors' focus on a dictionary learning framework is well-justified given the interpretability benefits.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of online learning, structured sparsity (Laplacian penalty), and application to inter-subject variability for ROI definition is a significant contribution.  This approach provides a principled and data-driven way to define regions without *a priori* assumptions.
* **Scalability:** The online learning framework allows the method to handle the massive datasets characteristic of modern neuroimaging (high voxel count and large subject numbers). This is a critical strength, as many existing structured decomposition methods struggle with scalability.
* **Interpretability:** The spatially smooth and sparse atoms produced by Smooth-SODL are inherently more interpretable than dense, unstructured representations.  The qualitative results visually demonstrate this benefit.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset are well-designed, comparing against relevant baseline methods (CanICA, SODL) using both quantitative metrics (explained variance, reproducibility) and qualitative assessments. The inclusion of behavioral prediction tasks adds further value.
* **Theoretical Grounding:**  The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the model and a justification for the algorithmic choices, including the convergence guarantees of the BCD approach.
* **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning and initialization provides valuable guidance for practitioners. The authors acknowledge and address the potential issues related to the choice of γ.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors address hyperparameter selection, the sensitivity to α, γ, and τ is apparent in the results. Further investigation into adaptive hyperparameter selection strategies beyond the time-varying α could be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:**  While significantly faster than some alternatives, the Smooth-SODL method is still 3x slower than standard SODL.  Exploring further algorithmic optimizations (e.g., more efficient Laplacian regularization solvers) could be valuable. The potential for GPU acceleration could be mentioned.
* **Comparison to Deep Learning Methods:**  As mentioned previously, a brief discussion of recent deep learning-based brain parcellation methods would contextualize the work within the broader field. While the authors prioritize interpretability, acknowledging the performance of deep learning approaches and outlining the trade-offs would strengthen the paper.
* **Specificity of Laplacian Regularization:** The use of a simple discrete Laplacian might not be optimal for capturing the complex geometry of the brain. Exploring alternative spatial regularization schemes (e.g., graph-based methods) could potentially improve performance.
* **Reproducibility Details:** While the authors mention implementation will be made available, providing more details about the specific software libraries used (beyond Nilearn) would enhance reproducibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in neuroimaging.
* The proposed method offers a significant advantage in terms of scalability and interpretability compared to existing techniques.
* The empirical results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of Smooth-SODL on a large-scale dataset.
* The theoretical analysis and practical considerations add to the overall quality of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The method's computational cost, while manageable, is higher than simpler alternatives.
*  The sensitivity to hyperparameters requires careful tuning, and more sophisticated adaptive strategies might be needed.
* A more thorough discussion of the limitations and comparison to recent deep learning methods would strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant impact on the field of neuroimaging. The authors have developed a novel, scalable, and interpretable method for defining brain regions, addressing a long-standing challenge in the field. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper far outweigh them. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper, with the following minor revisions:

1. Briefly discuss recent deep learning approaches for brain parcellation and contextualize the trade-offs with the proposed dictionary learning method.
2. Explore potential algorithmic optimizations to reduce the computational cost, and mention the possibility of GPU acceleration.
3.  Provide more specific details about the software libraries used to enhance reproducibility.
4. Consider discussing alternative spatial regularization schemes beyond the discrete Laplacian.
