This paper proposes a method for learning brain regions, or functional parcels, from large-scale neuroimaging data by modeling inter-subject variability. The core contribution is an extension of the online sparse dictionary learning (SODL) framework to incorporate a spatial smoothness prior on the learned dictionary atoms (the brain regions). This is achieved by adding a Laplacian regularization term to the objective function. The resulting algorithm, termed Smooth-SODL, remains computationally efficient and scalable due to its online nature, making it suitable for modern large datasets. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments on the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset that their method produces more structured, interpretable, and reproducible brain maps compared to standard SODL and a popular ICA-based method (CanICA), while incurring only a minor computational overhead.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Timeliness:** The problem of identifying meaningful, data-driven brain parcellations from large population cohorts is a fundamental and highly relevant challenge in neuroimaging. This work addresses the need for methods that are not only statistically sound but also computationally scalable to handle datasets with thousands of subjects. By building upon the highly successful online dictionary learning framework, the paper provides a practical and powerful tool for the field.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed model is well-grounded and methodologically sound. The combination of a sparsity-inducing constraint (projection onto a simplex or l1-ball) and a smoothness-inducing penalty (Laplacian) is a principled way to obtain the desired structured components. The paper correctly identifies that this can be solved within the online Block Coordinate Descent (BCD) framework of Mairal et al. (2010), and the derivation of the dictionary update step is clear. The claim of inheriting convergence guarantees is justified.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is a major strength of this paper. The authors use a large, publicly available dataset (HCP), which lends credibility to the results. The comparison against relevant and widely-used baselines (SODL, CanICA) is comprehensive. The evaluation is performed using a good range of metrics, including visual quality, explained variance, stability/reproducibility, and, importantly, the utility of the learned representations for a downstream prediction task (predicting behavioral scores). The learning curve analysis in Table 1 is particularly effective at demonstrating the value of the spatial regularizer, especially in lower data regimes.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The motivation is well-articulated in the introduction, the model is presented formally, and the relationship to prior work is clearly explained. The figures and tables are informative and support the paper's claims effectively. The authors are also upfront about computational costs, which is a very useful practical detail.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Novelty:** While the combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian prior is novel in this context and demonstrably useful, the individual components are well-established. The core contribution lies in the efficient integration and application of these ideas to a significant problem in neuroimaging. This is more of a strong application/systems contribution than a fundamental algorithmic one, which is perfectly acceptable for NeurIPS, but the framing could be slightly more modest.

2.  **Discussion of the Dictionary Update:** The description of the dictionary update sub-problem (Eq. 7/8) is somewhat brief. While details are likely in the supplement, the main text could benefit from a little more intuition. The problem is effectively a proximal step for the Laplacian penalty. Stating that the update involves solving a spatially-regularized denoising problem (a well-understood sub-problem) might make the connection to other literature (e.g., total variation denoising) clearer. The empirical claim that 1-2 FISTA iterations suffice is plausible but could be briefly justified (e.g., by noting that the outer online loop is robust to inexact solutions of the subproblem).

3.  **Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper provides a good empirical study of the hyperparameters but the practical guidance could be stronger. The authors suggest k-fold cross-validation for setting γ and τ, but this can be computationally prohibitive, especially for large datasets. While they provide a useful data-adaptive rule for α, it would strengthen the paper to offer a more efficient strategy or at least a practical rule of thumb for setting the crucial spatial regularization parameter γ based on data properties (e.g., noise levels or image smoothness).

4.  **Nuances in Experimental Comparisons:**
    *   The prediction results in Figure 2(c) show that CanICA performs best on test data for predicting behavioral variables, slightly contradicting the narrative of Smooth-SODL's general superiority. This is an interesting result and deserves more discussion. It might suggest that while the sparse and structured maps from Smooth-SODL are more interpretable, the dense, unconstrained components from CanICA may retain more subtle variance that is useful for prediction. This is a classic interpretability-vs-performance trade-off that would be valuable to discuss explicitly.
    *   In the experiments, a simplex constraint was used for the dictionary atoms, enforcing non-negativity. This choice should be briefly motivated. Is it because the input Z-maps were one-sided, or is there a general benefit to non-negative components for interpretability in this domain?

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper tackles an important problem with a scalable and well-motivated method. The extension of online dictionary learning is elegant and effective. The experimental validation is strong, thorough, and conducted on a large-scale, relevant dataset. The results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed spatial regularization in terms of interpretability, stability, and data efficiency. The work represents a significant practical contribution to the analysis of large neuroimaging cohorts.

*   **Con-acceptance:** The core algorithmic novelty is moderate, as it combines existing techniques. The discussion on some technical details and the practical guidance for hyperparameter selection could be improved. The results of the prediction experiment could be discussed with more nuance.

**Decision**

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a well-executed piece of research that provides a valuable and scalable tool for a challenging and important problem. The empirical evidence is compelling. I recommend acceptance.
