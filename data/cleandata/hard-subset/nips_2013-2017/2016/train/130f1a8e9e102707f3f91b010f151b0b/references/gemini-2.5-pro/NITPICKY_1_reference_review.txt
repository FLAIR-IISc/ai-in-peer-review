**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL), a method for learning structured brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging datasets. The approach extends the well-known online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. [16] by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages the learned components to be spatially smooth and blob-like, which is desirable for defining neurobiologically plausible regions of interest (ROIs). The authors propose a block-coordinate descent algorithm to solve the optimization problem and demonstrate its effectiveness on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project, comparing it to standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a group-level ICA method (CanICA).

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses a crucial and challenging problem in neuroimaging: defining functional brain regions in a data-driven manner that is scalable to massive datasets. The limitations of existing methods (e.g., lack of structure in sparse models, computational cost of structured batch models) are well-articulated.

2.  **Methodological Contribution:** The proposed extension is a natural and principled way to incorporate spatial priors into a powerful online learning framework. Maintaining the online nature of the algorithm is a key advantage, making structured decompositions feasible for datasets that are too large for traditional batch methods.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a relatively thorough empirical validation on a large, public dataset. The comparison against both a sparse (SODL) and a dense (CanICA) baseline is appropriate. The inclusion of multiple evaluation metrics—qualitative assessment, explained variance, stability, and predictive power for behavioral variables—provides a multi-faceted view of the method's performance. The learning curve analysis in Table 1 is particularly compelling, demonstrating the value of the spatial regularizer in low-data regimes.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising direction, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws ranging from minor presentational issues to significant methodological ambiguities, which collectively undermine the quality of the contribution.

1.  **Lack of Polish and Clarity:** The manuscript exhibits a general lack of care in its preparation.
    *   The bibliography is highly problematic, with numerous incomplete and inconsistently formatted entries (e.g., [1], [3], [6], [11] are missing key information like conference/journal, year, or page numbers). This carelessness is unbecoming of a top-tier conference submission.
    *   There are recurring typos (e.g., "intepretable," "over-fits") and grammatical errors (e.g., "best than all the other models"). The phrasing is often awkward (e.g., "Nilearn open-source library Python library").
    *   Figure 2's presentation is poor. The main description of subplot (a) is embedded in the main text rather than a self-contained caption. The use of "N.B." is informal.

2.  **Methodological Ambiguity and Inconsistencies:** Several key technical details are either unclear or contradictory.
    *   **Hyperparameter Treatment:** Section 2 formulates the model with fixed regularization parameters `α` and `γ`. However, Section 3.2 suggests using time-varying, adaptive choices `α_t ~ t^(-1/2)` and `γ_t = O(‖A_t‖_∞,∞ / t)`. This is a critical discrepancy. If the parameters are time-varying, the objective function changes at each step, and the convergence claims, which rely on the framework of [16] for a fixed objective, require much stronger justification. The experiments appear to use fixed `γ` values (`10^3`, `10^4`), contradicting the guidance in Eq. (9). This must be clarified.
    *   **Choice of Constraint Set:** The experiments use a simplex constraint `C := S_p(τ)`, which enforces non-negativity on the dictionary atoms. The input data, however, are Z-score maps from a contrast, which are inherently signed (containing both positive and negative values). The motivation for enforcing non-negativity is absent and seems inappropriate for this data type.
    *   **Algorithmic Details:** Algorithm 2, which describes the dictionary update, is uninformative. Line 4 merely restates the optimization problem rather than specifying the update steps (e.g., the FISTA iterations mentioned in the text).

3.  **Experimental Design Flaws:**
    *   **Confounded Comparison:** A major confound in the experimental comparison is that the CanICA baseline was run on spatially smoothed data (6mm FWHM), whereas SODL and Smooth-SODL were not. The authors justify this as a "necessary preprocessing step" for CanICA. While perhaps true in practice, this prevents a fair comparison of the methods' intrinsic ability to produce structured maps. The smoothness of the CanICA maps stems from pre-processing, while for Smooth-SODL it stems from the model itself. This makes the qualitative comparison in Fig. 2a difficult to interpret. A more rigorous comparison would involve evaluating all methods on both smoothed and unsmoothed data.
    *   **Understated Limitations:** The stability analysis reports a mean correlation of 0.4 for Smooth-SODL. While this is an improvement over the baselines, a score of 0.4 is quite low in absolute terms, suggesting considerable variability in the learned dictionaries across data splits. This limitation is not adequately discussed. Furthermore, in the behavioral prediction task (Fig. 2c), the "uninterpretable" dense CanICA model is the top performer on test data. This highlights a classic interpretability-performance trade-off that the paper largely ignores, weakening the claim that the proposed method is superior overall.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper presents a valuable and scalable approach to a significant problem, the current manuscript is not ready for publication at NIPS. The combination of poor presentation, critical methodological ambiguities, and confounds in the experimental design severely diminishes its impact. The work shows promise, but the authors need to significantly revise the paper to address the numerous issues detailed above. A future submission would require a thoroughly proofread and polished manuscript, a clear and consistent description of the model and algorithm (particularly regarding the hyperparameters), a more rigorous and fairly controlled experimental comparison, and a more nuanced discussion of the method's limitations and trade-offs.
