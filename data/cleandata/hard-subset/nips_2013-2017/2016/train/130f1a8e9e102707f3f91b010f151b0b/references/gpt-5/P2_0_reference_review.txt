Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging in which dictionary atoms are both sparse and spatially smooth. Concretely, each atom Vj is constrained to lie in a sparsity-inducing set (e.g., l1-ball or simplex) and penalized by a Laplacian quadratic form to promote compact, blob-like structure. Codes are obtained via ridge regression. The algorithm adapts the online block-coordinate descent of Mairal et al. (JMLR 2010) to this structured setting: code updates via closed-form ridge, and atom updates via solving a convex denoising subproblem with smooth term 1/2||v−a||^2 + (γ̃/2) v^TΔv and projection onto C using FISTA. The authors discuss step-size/Lipschitz issues and a time-varying γt to avoid small steps. Experiments on 500 HCP subjects’ task-activation maps (k=40) compare to CanICA (Varoquaux et al.) and unstructured SODL, reporting explained variance, stability/reproducibility, qualitative interpretability, early-learning curves, and behavior prediction. The method is claimed to be ~3x slower than SODL but faster than CanICA, and produces more interpretable, more stable components than SODL while approaching CanICA’s explained variance.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The formulation is reasonable and the atom subproblem is convex and well-handled by first-order methods when the Laplacian term is treated in the smooth part and sparsity is implemented via projection onto C. However, several aspects are underspecified or potentially problematic:
  - The stated guarantee that the per-atom BCD update “is guaranteed to converge to a global optimum” requires solving the convex subproblem to convergence; yet the paper states “1 or 2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient to reach an accuracy of 1e−6,” which is implausible in general and contradicts the need to actually converge. If in practice only a few iterations are run (inexact BCD), the convergence claims should be adapted accordingly (e.g., cite inexact BCD theory or provide empirical tolerance studies).
  - The proposed time-varying γt (and αt) schedules change the objective over time. This can be pragmatic but weakens the link to the convergence proofs of [16], which assume a fixed objective. Please clarify whether the theoretical guarantees still hold under these schedules, or present this as a heuristic with empirical support.
  - The “lim n→∞” in the objective is nonstandard in finite-sample presentations; if this is meant to denote an expected risk approximated online, please state it clearly.
  - Fairness of baselines: SODL is run without spatial structure, as intended, but codes are ridge-regularized here (not l1), which differs from typical sparse-coding SODL settings and may disadvantage SODL. Conversely, CanICA is run with pre-smoothing (required) while the proposed methods are not. Please justify these choices and, ideally, report SODL with l1 codes and/or SODL with the same preprocessing, and include a structured baseline such as TV-l1 dictionary learning (e.g., Abraham et al.) on a subset where it’s computationally feasible.
  - Statistical rigor: The stability metric (mean inter-split correlation with Hungarian matching) is appropriate, but details are missing (number of splits, variance/error bars). For behavioral prediction, the cross-validation protocol, feature selection (to avoid leakage in RAW), model class, and hyperparameter tuning need to be precisely described.

- Empirics: The HCP experiment is valuable, the early learning curve demonstrating benefits of spatial regularization in low-data regimes is compelling, and interpretability/stability improvements are credible. However, quantitative comparisons lack error bars, ablations are limited, and significance tests are absent.

Clarity:
The paper is generally understandable but needs substantial polishing:
- Notational issues (e.g., “maxUi” should be “minUi”; inconsistent factors in ΩLap; missing or misnumbered steps; statements like “1–2 iterations to 1e−6”).
- The use of the limit in Eq. (2) and the role of time-varying αt/γt should be explained more clearly.
- Some claims (guaranteed convergence of Alg. 2; FISTA iteration counts) are overstated.
- Several references are incomplete or mismatched; figure captions and text occasionally contain typos and informal language.

Originality:
The main novelty is incorporating a Laplacian smoothness penalty on atoms into the online dictionary learning framework with efficient per-atom updates and demonstrating scalability to large neuroimaging datasets. Conceptually, it is a relatively natural extension of Mairal et al., closely related to GraphNet-style regularization, and to prior spatially-structured dictionary learning (TV-l1). The contribution is incremental but meaningful: making structured decompositions practical at scale with modest overhead.

Significance:
For neuroimaging, the ability to learn reproducible, interpretable, spatially-structured components online is useful. The improved early-sample performance and stability, plus near-CanICA explained variance with better interpretability, suggest practical impact. Planned integration into Nilearn would increase adoption. From a NeurIPS perspective, the methodological advance is moderate; significance is more on the application/practical side than on core theory.

Pros:
- Clear motivation for spatially-structured atoms in population neuroimaging.
- Simple, scalable modification of online dictionary learning with minor runtime overhead.
- Convex per-atom updates with standard first-order tools; practical implementation details.
- Solid dataset and relevant metrics; improved interpretability and stability; useful early-learning behavior.

Cons:
- Overstated/conflated convergence claims; inexact inner solves and time-varying hyperparameters undermine the theoretical story.
- Baselines and protocols could be more comprehensive and better controlled (SODL with l1 codes, structured offline baselines, more tasks).
- Limited statistical reporting (error bars, variance across splits), and some clarity/notation/reference issues.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Clarify the cross-validation protocol for γ, τ, and α, and whether αt/γt are fixed or adapted online during training and how this interacts with guarantees.
- Report sensitivity to the number of FISTA iterations per atom and provide a runtime/accuracy trade-off; consider citing inexact BCD results if applicable.
- Add SODL with l1 codes and a structured baseline (TV-l1) on a smaller subset to contextualize gains.
- Provide stability/reproducibility with confidence intervals over multiple splits; detail the Hungarian matching procedure.
- Clarify data leakage prevention in RAW baseline and prediction setup (nested CV).
- Fix typos/notation and complete references; explain the “lim n→∞” formulation.

Overall assessment:
The paper addresses an important practical problem and presents a useful, scalable algorithmic variant with convincing qualitative and some quantitative benefits. However, the theoretical claims are overstated relative to what is actually implemented, and the empirical evaluation, while promising, needs stronger baselines and more careful statistical reporting. If the clarity and evaluation issues are addressed, this would be a solid contribution. As is, I view it as borderline; leaning toward a weak accept if the above concerns can be remedied.
