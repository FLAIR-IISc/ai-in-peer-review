

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning that incorporates spatial regularization via a Laplacian penalty to learn structured brain regions from fMRI data. The core idea is to use inter-subject variability to define functional regions, rather than relying on anatomical segmentation or predefined ROIs. The paper builds on [16] by adding spatial smoothness constraints to the dictionary atoms, with the claim that this results in more interpretable brain regions that better capture inter-subject variability.

**Strengths:**
- The problem of defining functional brain regions is important in neuroimaging, and the paper addresses a genuine limitation in current approaches.
- The extension to spatial regularization is conceptually sound, and the experimental results (Fig. 2) show that the proposed method produces more spatially structured dictionary atoms compared to vanilla SODL.
- The computational complexity analysis shows only a modest increase (≈3x) over the baseline SODL, which is impressive for incorporating spatial constraints.

**Critical Weaknesses:**

1. **Notational and Typographical Inconsistencies:** The paper contains numerous notational errors and typos that undermine its technical credibility. In equation (2), the constraint is written as "V1, . . . ,Vk ∈ C" while C is defined in equation (3) as a subset of Rp (not Rp×k), creating a fundamental inconsistency. The paper repeatedly uses "intepretable" instead of "interpretable" (12 instances), "ceteris" instead of "ceteris paribus," and "a a" instead of "a" in Section 6. These are unprofessional errors for a NIPS submission.

2. **Methodological Ambiguities:** The paper claims to use a "Laplacian penalty" but fails to properly define how this penalty operates in 3D space. Equation (4) states ΩLap(v) := 1/p∑k=1 (∇xv)2k + (∇yv)2k + (∇zv)2k, which is mathematically problematic since k is used as both the index for voxels and the number of dictionary atoms. The Laplacian should be defined with respect to the spatial dimensions, not as a sum over the index k.

3. **Experimental Details Missing:** The paper states "the input data X were shuffled and then split into two groups of the same size" but fails to specify the random seed or whether the split was stratified by subject. The hardware specifications for the 530s runtime of CanICA are missing, making the speed comparison unreliable. The paper also doesn't explain why they chose k=40 atoms for the HCP dataset.

4. **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The paper claims "we found that 1 or 2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient" but provides no convergence plots or error metrics to support this. The statement that "the major structures (parietal lobes, sulci, etc.) in each atom are reproducible across the different models" lacks statistical evidence—there are no correlation coefficients or p-values provided to support this claim.

5. **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper switches between "spatially smooth" and "piece-wise smooth" without clarifying the distinction. It also uses "structured" and "spatially-localized" interchangeably without defining what "structured" specifically means in this context.

6. **Hyperparameter Justification:** The paper uses α = αt ∼ t−1/2 but doesn't provide any evidence for this choice beyond citing [26]. The safe range for (γ, τ) is claimed to exist (Fig. 1), but the paper doesn't explain how they determined this range or provide a systematic exploration of the hyperparameter space.

**Other Issues:**
- The paper cites [1] as "very novel" but fails to explain how their approach differs from it beyond claiming "our method enjoys the same convergence guarantees." A detailed comparison would strengthen the paper.
- The claim that "spatial regularization is needed in small-scale regimes" is not properly supported by the learning curve analysis (Table 1). The 14-fold gain at 17 subjects is impressive, but the paper doesn't explain why this gain diminishes as more data is added.
- The paper states "we did not perform pre-smoothing for the SODL of Smooth-SODL models" but doesn't clarify whether this is a methodological choice or a technical limitation.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging and presents a technically interesting extension to dictionary learning, the numerous notational errors, methodological ambiguities, and lack of experimental details significantly undermine its credibility. The paper reads like a draft with insufficient proofreading and lacks the precision expected at NIPS. The minor technical issues identified above collectively constitute a substantial number of flaws that would prevent the paper from being accepted at NIPS, even if the core idea is promising. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmission.
