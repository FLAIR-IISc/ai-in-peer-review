Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging that augments Mairal et al.’s Sparse Online Dictionary Learning with a Laplacian (GraphNet-style) penalty on the dictionary atoms, plus sparsity-inducing constraints (ℓ1-ball or simplex). The goal is to learn spatially localized, blob-like, sparse components that capture population-level variability and can serve as ROIs. The per-sample code is updated via ridge regression; dictionary atoms are updated by block-coordinate descent, where each atom solves a convex quadratic with Laplacian smoothing under a convex constraint, handled efficiently with a few FISTA iterations (warm-started). Experiments on HCP task fMRI (n=500, p≈260k, k=40) show improved spatial structure, stability, and early data-regime explained variance over vanilla SODL, with only a 2–3× runtime overhead and competitive explained variance relative to CanICA. The approach is slated for inclusion in nilearn.

Relation to prior work
The method is positioned between PCA/ICA decompositions commonly used in fMRI and structured sparse decompositions (TV-ℓ1, GraphNet) that are typically slow in batch optimization. Prior structured sparse PCA/dictionary learning (e.g., TV-ℓ1 atlases, GraphNet, structured sparsity) is cited; the novelty here is to bring spatially structured priors into a scalable online framework with convergence properties comparable to online DL, and to demonstrate practical gains at HCP scale.

Strengths
- Practical, scalable contribution: A simple, modular extension of online DL that incorporates a ubiquitous spatial prior (Laplacian) with minimal computational overhead, enabling large p and n on commodity hardware.
- Sound optimization design: Each atom update is a convex, strongly convex problem with a clear Lipschitz constant; projection onto ℓ1/simplex is efficient; warm-started first-order updates make sense in an online BCD loop.
- Empirical value: Clear qualitative gains in interpretability (compact blobs without ringing), improved stability across splits, and substantial advantage in the small/medium data regimes versus SODL. Careful reporting of train vs. test explained variance and use of Hungarian matching to compare atoms.
- Relevance and impact: Robust ROI learning directly from population variability is important for neuroimaging; inclusion in nilearn will likely drive adoption.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental methodologically: Laplacian/GraphNet priors for structured components and sparse/positive constraints are well known; the main novelty is their integration into an online DL scheme. This is valuable but not a conceptual leap.
- Missing strong baselines: No empirical comparison to TV-ℓ1/GraphNet-based dictionary learning (e.g., Abraham et al., FAASTA) or structured sparse PCA, even on smaller subsets. Dismissing them as “too slow” is understandable for full HCP, but a small-scale quantitative comparison would strengthen claims.
- Theoretical clarity: The “Lipschitz blow-up” discussion (Eq. 9) and the suggested time-varying γ schedule are confusing. Under ridge codes, A_t[j,j]/t should stabilize to E[U_j^2], so L_t need not blow up for fixed γ. Please clarify the derivation, assumptions ensuring bounded L_t, and whether γ_t is actually used.
- Over-strong convergence/accuracy claims: The statement that 1–2 FISTA iterations “reach 1e-6 accuracy” is not credible without warm-start explanation and a precise stopping criterion (e.g., projected gradient norm). Provide details and ablation (number of inner iterations vs. quality/runtime).
- Evaluation breadth/fairness: Only one HCP contrast and k=40. Consider sensitivity to k, and to the choice of constraint (simplex vs ℓ1-ball). CanICA is dense and not positivity constrained; comparing to nonnegative ICA or sparse PCA would be fairer. Also compare SODL with light pre-smoothing to decouple smoothing vs. Laplacian effects.
- Reproducibility details: Specify boundary conditions and construction of Δ on masked brain volumes, normalization/preprocessing, and memory footprint. Code availability is promised but not yet provided.

Questions for the authors
- Do you warm-start FISTA from the previous atom iterate? What is the exact stopping rule? How many inner iterations are used on average?
- How is Δ constructed on irregular brain masks (Neumann vs periodic boundaries)? Any FFT-based speedups actually used in practice?
- How sensitive are results to positivity (simplex) vs. signed ℓ1 constraints?
- Can you provide small-scale comparisons to TV-ℓ1/GraphNet dictionary learning and to nonnegative ICA/sparse PCA?
- How does runtime/memory scale with p and k? Any multi-threading?

Typos/clarity
- Several typos (“intepretable”, “unintepretable”, grammar). Clean up Eq. formatting and the derivation around Eq. 7–9. Avoid claiming “global optimum” for the overall nonconvex problem; per-atom subproblems are globally optimal, but overall convergence is to a stationary point.

Pros for acceptance
- Enables spatially structured online DL at scale with demonstrated practical gains and likely broad impact in neuroimaging tooling.
- Clear, implementable idea with convergence of subproblems and modest overhead.
- Improves interpretability and stability, and markedly helps in low-data regimes.

Cons for acceptance
- Methodological novelty is moderate; key components are known, integration is the main contribution.
- Missing comparisons to the most relevant structured baselines; some theoretical/empirical claims need clarification.

Recommendation
Weak accept. While not flawless and somewhat incremental, the paper introduces a practical, scalable path to structured online decompositions that is likely to influence practice and future work. With stronger baselines and clarified theory, this would be an easy accept. My bias toward bold, enabling directions leads me to recommend acceptance.
