Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method for neuroimaging in which dictionary atoms are constrained to be sparse and spatially smooth. Sparsity is enforced via a compact constraint set on atoms (l1-ball or simplex), while spatial coherence is enforced via a Laplacian quadratic penalty ΩLap(v) = 1/2 v^T Δ v, with Δ = ∇^T∇. The algorithm follows Mairal’s online block-coordinate descent framework: (i) codes are updated via ridge regression (or optionally l1), (ii) atoms are updated one-by-one by solving a convex denoising/projection subproblem of the form min_{v∈C} 1/2‖v−a‖^2 + (γ̃/2) v^T Δ v using FISTA with projection onto C. The method targets large-scale settings (p ~ 10^5–10^6, n up to 10^5), claims a 2–3x runtime over standard SODL, and demonstrates improved interpretability and stability of components on HCP task fMRI statistical maps (n=500, k=40), with analyses of explained variance, cross-split stability, learning curves, and behavioral prediction.

Relation to prior work is appropriate: this extends online DL [Mairal’10] to structured atoms, connects to GraphNet-style smoothness [Grosenick], and contrasts with TV-ℓ1 approaches for atlas learning [Abraham; Varoquaux], which are slower/offline. It also positions itself relative to PCA/ICA (CanICA).

Strengths
- Problem relevance and impact: Addressing inter-subject variability with structured, multivariate decompositions is important for robust ROI definition and dimensionality reduction in population neuroimaging.
- Algorithmic framing: The atom update reduces to a standard smooth quadratic plus indicator of C, for which efficient projected FISTA is reasonable. Projection onto l1-ball/simplex is linear-time. The observation that Δ has bounded spectrum (≈4D) under typical grid discretizations is correct and gives practical step sizes.
- Scalability: The method inherits online memory/runtime advantages; reported runtimes (≈5.6 min vs 1.7 for SODL and 8.8 for CanICA) are encouraging.
- Empirics: Qualitative maps look cleaner and more blob-like than SODL, and far more interpretable than dense ICA maps. Learning curves showing substantial gains over SODL at small n are valuable. Stability across splits improves with spatial regularization. Code release in Nilearn will help adoption.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Methodological clarity/rigor:
  - Time-varying regularization: To control the Lipschitz constant, the paper suggests γ_t = O(‖A_t‖∞,∞/t), since γ̃ = γ (A_t[j,j]/t)^{-1} in the atom subproblems. This implies the effective smoothness penalty decays with t, tending toward the unregularized objective as data accumulates. This changes the optimization target and lacks a convergence or consistency analysis; it also conflicts with the goal of persistent spatial structure. A principled alternative (e.g., normalizing A_t, rescaling atoms, or using a fixed γ with backtracking/diagonal preconditioning) should be discussed.
  - Inner solver claims: The statement that “1 or 2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient to reach an accuracy of 1e-6” is not credible for generic Δ-regularized problems; please report actual iteration counts, stopping criteria, and residuals. If you intentionally do very few inner iterations, discuss convergence of inexact BCD and its practical effects.
  - Constraint choice: Using a simplex Sp(τ) enforces nonnegativity and ℓ1 sparsity on atoms. This is a strong modeling choice; please justify nonnegativity (e.g., interpretability, identifiability) and contrast with symmetric ℓ1-balls. An ablation comparing simplex vs ℓ1-ball would help.
  - Derivation details: The step from the global objective to the per-atom surrogate (Eq. 7) is central and currently deferred to [16]; please include the derivation or full expression for the surrogate a = V_j + A_t[j,j]^{-1}(B_{jt} − V A_jt) in the main text. Clarify boundary conditions for ∇ and how brain masks/voxel anisotropy are handled.

- Experimental design:
  - Comparisons: The most direct baselines—TV-ℓ1 structured dictionary learning/atlas extraction [Abraham; Varoquaux; FAASTA]—are cited but not included in benchmarks. Even small-scale comparisons would strengthen claims about the speed/quality trade-off of Laplacian vs TV priors.
  - CanICA preprocessing: You smooth the input for CanICA (FWHM 6mm) but not for SODL/Smooth-SODL. This makes comparisons on explained variance and interpretability difficult to interpret. Please either compare on the same preprocessed data or justify the asymmetry.
  - Stability and matching: Stability is reported as mean correlation after Hungarian matching, but lacks confidence intervals and details on matching constraints/sign handling. Include variance across splits and a description of the matching protocol.
  - Behavioral prediction: The predictive pipeline is under-specified (regressor type, hyperparameter tuning, nested CV, feature scaling). The RAW baseline uses univariate feature selection; ensure selection is performed within CV folds to avoid leakage. Report error bars and significance tests. It would also be informative to compare using atom maps as spatial ROIs for summary features vs codes U as features.
  - Sensitivity and ablations: Results are for one task and k=40. Please report sensitivity to k, τ, γ (beyond the qualitative “safe range”), and to code penalties (ridge vs lasso).

- Presentation:
  - The manuscript contains typos, minor inconsistencies, and missing figure panels; some references are incomplete. Tighten notation (e.g., consistently denote Δ’s spectrum and sign), and clarify claims such as “global optimum at each iteration” (true for the convex per-atom subproblem, not the overall nonconvex problem).

Significance and originality
The paper’s main novelty is integrating a simple Laplacian smoothness prior on atoms into an online DL framework with efficient per-atom updates. While the ingredients are known (Mairal’s online BCD, GraphNet-like quadratic smoothness, projections onto l1/simplex), their combination in a scalable neuroimaging pipeline and the empirical demonstration on HCP are useful contributions. The methodological step is incremental, but the practical impact for large neuroimaging cohorts could be substantial if the approach is robust and reproducible.

Questions for the authors
- Do you keep γ fixed in your final runs, or use the time-varying γ_t? How sensitive are results to this schedule?
- How many FISTA iterations are actually used per atom update? What is the stopping rule, and do you backtrack or precondition?
- What boundary conditions and masking do you use for ∇/Δ? Is Δ applied in physical or voxel units?
- For behavioral prediction, what models and hyperparameters are used? Is tuning nested within CV?
- Can you include a baseline with TV-ℓ1 structured DL (even on a subset) and an ablation of simplex vs l1-ball constraints?

Pros
- Addresses an important problem with a scalable method.
- Clear improvement in interpretability and stability at modest runtime cost.
- Sensible algorithmic design; easy-to-implement components.
- Promises code release in a widely used library.

Cons
- Some methodological choices (γ_t schedule, FISTA iteration claims) lack rigor.
- Missing key baselines and ablations; uneven comparison to CanICA.
- Under-specified predictive analysis; limited uncertainty reporting.
- Clarity/polish issues.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the γ/inner-solver issues and strengthening the experimental comparisons.
