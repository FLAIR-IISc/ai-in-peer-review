

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning that incorporates Laplacian regularization to learn spatially structured brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors argue that traditional approaches to ROI definition are limited by segmentation and cross-modality issues, and instead propose to leverage inter-subject variability to define regions. The key innovation is adding a Laplacian penalty to the dictionary atoms to enforce spatial smoothness while maintaining sparsity.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging with potentially significant practical implications. The experimental validation on HCP data is thorough, comparing against multiple baselines (SODL, CanICA) using multiple metrics (explained variance, reproducibility, behavioral prediction). The implementation as part of Nilearn is a valuable contribution. The authors correctly identify the need for structured spatial representations in brain imaging data.

## Critical Weaknesses

**Mathematical Inconsistencies:** The paper contains several critical mathematical errors that undermine the technical soundness of the proposed method. In equation (4), the authors define ΩLap(v) = 1/p∑(∇xv)^2_k + ... but then claim it equals 1/2v^TΔv. The 1/p factor is inconsistent with the standard Laplacian definition, and the 1/2 factor is also problematic as it's not properly justified. This inconsistency appears throughout the paper, creating confusion about the exact form of the regularization.

**Algorithmic Details:** The paper claims that "a single iteration is sufficient for the dictionary update sub-routine in Alg. 2 to converge to a qualitatively good dictionary," but provides no empirical evidence to support this claim. The paper also states "it is possible to perform the dictionary updates in the Fourier domain, via FFT" without explaining how this would work with the Laplacian penalty, which is not diagonal in the Fourier domain.

**Notation Confusion:** The paper uses inconsistent notation for key quantities. For instance, in equation (7), the authors write "Vj + At[j, j] −1(Bjt −VAjt )" which contains a confusing mix of parentheses and formatting. In equation (9), the expression "γ = γt = O (max 1≤j≤k At[j, j] )" is mathematically imprecise and should be written as γ = O(max_j At[j,j]/t).

**Experimental Comparison Issues:** The comparison with CanICA is unfair because the authors pre-smooth the SODL and Smooth-SODL models but apply smoothing to CanICA as a "necessary preprocessing step." This violates the principle of fair comparison. The paper should have applied consistent preprocessing to all methods.

**Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper claims "K-fold cross-validation with explained variance metric was retained as a good strategy" for hyperparameter selection, but provides no evidence that this is optimal. The paper also fails to discuss how the choice of τ (sparsity parameter) affects the spatial structure of the learned atoms, which is the core contribution of the paper.

**Theoretical Justification:** The paper states that "the Laplacian penalty is meant to impose blobs" but provides no theoretical or empirical justification for why the Laplacian penalty specifically creates blob-like structures rather than other spatial patterns. The authors should have compared with alternative spatial regularizers (e.g., total variation) to demonstrate the superiority of their approach.

**Implementation Details:** The paper claims "a factor of 2 or 3 on the running time" compared to SODL but provides no details about the hardware or implementation specifics that would allow reproduction of these results. The claim that "340s (≈ 5.6 minutes)" is "slightly less than 3 times slower" is misleading without specifying the exact hardware and software environment.

**Reference Issues:** The references contain numerous formatting inconsistencies, including missing publication years, page numbers, and conference names. Several citations (e.g., [1], [2], [3]) are incomplete in the reference list.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging with a potentially useful method, the numerous mathematical inconsistencies, unclear algorithmic details, and lack of proper experimental comparison undermine the technical soundness of the work. The paper claims to extend existing methods with minimal computational overhead, but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support this claim. The mathematical errors in the core formulation (equation 4) are particularly concerning as they directly impact the proposed method.

The paper also fails to properly distinguish its contribution from previous work on spatially-structured dictionary learning (e.g., [1] which uses TV-`1 minimization). The authors incorrectly characterize [1] as "mostly heuristic" without providing evidence, while their own method lacks the same level of theoretical justification.

Despite the potentially valuable application domain, the technical shortcomings and lack of rigorous presentation make the paper unsuitable for NIPS. The minor methodological flaws are not just technicalities but directly impact the validity of the core contribution. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these issues too substantial to overlook, even though the problem area is significant.

I recommend rejection. The authors should address the mathematical inconsistencies, provide proper theoretical justification for the Laplacian penalty, and conduct a fairer experimental comparison before resubmission.
