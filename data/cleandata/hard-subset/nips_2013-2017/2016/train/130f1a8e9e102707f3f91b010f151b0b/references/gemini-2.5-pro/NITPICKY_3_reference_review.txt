### NIPS Review

**Summary:**
This paper proposes an extension to the online sparse dictionary learning (SODL) framework to incorporate a spatial smoothness prior, termed Smooth-SODL. The goal is to learn a dictionary of brain maps (atoms) from a large cohort of subject-level statistical maps that are not only sparse but also spatially coherent (i.e., blob-like), which is neuroanatomically more plausible than scattered patterns. The smoothness is enforced via a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. The authors adapt the block-coordinate descent (BCD) online learning algorithm from Mairal et al. (2010) to accommodate this new regularization term, showing that the computational overhead is modest. The method is validated on a large task-fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (HCP), demonstrating that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable and stable dictionaries than standard SODL and more structured maps than conventional ICA-based methods (CanICA), particularly in lower data regimes.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Well-motivated Problem:** The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in neuroimaging: defining functionally relevant brain regions in a data-driven manner from large-scale population data. The limitations of existing methods (unstructured components, computational scaling) are clearly articulated.
2.  **Novel Methodological Contribution:** The integration of a Laplacian spatial prior into the scalable online dictionary learning framework is a novel and valuable contribution. It elegantly combines the benefits of sparsity with spatial structure, which is critical for the interpretability of neuroimaging results.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are conducted on a large, well-regarded public dataset (HCP). The choice of baselines (SODL, CanICA) is appropriate, and the evaluation is comprehensive, covering qualitative assessment, explained variance, stability, and predictive power for behavioral variables.
4.  **Compelling Low-Data Regime Performance:** The learning curve analysis (Table 1) provides a strong argument for the utility of the spatial prior. The reported 14-fold improvement in explained variance over SODL when using few subjects clearly demonstrates the regularizing effect of the smoothness penalty and carves out a clear niche for the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, while presenting a promising idea, is marred by a significant number of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a general lack of technical precision that, in aggregate, undermine confidence in the work and its reproducibility.

1.  **Lack of Rigor in Formulation:**
    *   The objective function in Eq. (2) includes a `lim n→∞`, which is appropriate for the analysis of stochastic approximation algorithms but is misplaced in the definition of the optimization problem for a finite dataset of size `n`. This is technically imprecise.
    *   In Eq. (1), the dimensions are inconsistent. If `Xi` is a `p`-dimensional row vector (as stated in the text, from a stack `X ∈ R^(n×p)`), then `VUi` (where `V ∈ R^(p×k)` and `Ui ∈ R^(k×1)`) results in a `p×1` column vector. The equation should likely be `X_i^T ≈ V U_i` or `X_i ≈ U_i^T V^T`. This notational sloppiness appears early and sets a poor tone.
    *   The choice of a Ridge (`l2`) penalty on the codes is justified weakly by stating it leads to "stable codes." A more thorough justification or comparison to the `l1` penalty used in the original SODL framework [16] is warranted, especially in the context of the neuroimaging application.

2.  **Clarity and Reproducibility Issues in Algorithm Description:**
    *   The derivation of the dictionary update in Eq. (7) is opaque. Key details are deferred to [16], and the notation `Fγ̃(At[j,j]/t)−1(...)` is highly confusing. It appears to be a re-parameterization of the F-function with `γ̃ = γ * (t / At[j, j])`, but this is not explicitly defined, hindering understanding of the core update step.
    *   Algorithm 2 specifies a `while` loop with a vague "stopping criteria not met." The text later states that "a single iteration is sufficient," which is a critical detail for implementation and complexity. This practical finding should be reflected in the formal algorithm description for clarity and reproducibility.
    *   There is an inconsistency regarding the regularization parameters. The paper discusses a data-adaptive choice for `γ` in Eq. (9) to manage the Lipschitz constant, and for `α` in Eq. (10). However, the experiments appear to use fixed, cross-validated values for these parameters. This disconnect between the theoretical discussion and the practical implementation is confusing.

3.  **Formatting and Presentation:**
    *   The paper suffers from several typographical errors (e.g., "comput" instead of "compute" in Section 2).
    *   The reference list is exceptionally poorly formatted. There are numerous inconsistencies: missing conference/journal names, incorrect author lists, superfluous commas (e.g., "A. Abraham, MICCAI, , ,"), and inconsistent use of "et al.". This lack of care in the bibliography is concerning and reflects poorly on the overall diligence of the authors.

4.  **Minor Experimental Concerns:**
    *   The report of runtime "on a single CPU of laptop" is too vague to be a meaningful benchmark.
    *   While the authors are commendably transparent about the predictive performance (Fig 2c), the fact that the dense CanICA model performs best at predicting behavioral variables somewhat weakens the claim that Smooth-SODL's components "better capture inter-subject variability." This tension should be discussed more thoroughly.

**Recommendation:**
Reject.

While the core idea of this paper is strong and the results are promising, the execution is not up to the standard expected for NIPS. The paper is undermined by a litany of small but significant issues, including notational imprecision in the core model formulation, a lack of clarity in the algorithmic descriptions, inconsistencies between theoretical suggestions and practical implementation, and a striking lack of care in formatting and referencing. The accumulation of these flaws makes the work difficult to fully trust and reproduce. The authors have a solid foundation for a very good paper, but it requires a thorough revision to address the technical clarity, precision, and overall polish before it is suitable for publication at a top-tier venue.
