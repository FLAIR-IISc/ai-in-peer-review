Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method for neuroimaging that enforces sparsity and spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms via an l1-type constraint (l1 ball or simplex) together with a quadratic Laplacian penalty. Codes are obtained by ridge regression (as opposed to the usual sparse codes), and atoms are updated in a block-coordinate fashion using a proximal step (FISTA) for the Laplacian-regularized quadratic with a convex constraint C. The motivation is to discover spatially localized, blob-like “regions” (atoms) from population variability of statistical maps, and to do so at scale. Experiments on HCP fMRI GLM maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5) compare Smooth-SODL to SODL [Mairal JMLR 2010] and CanICA [Varoquaux Neuroimage 2010], evaluating explained variance, stability, qualitative interpretability, and downstream behavioral prediction.

This work sits at the intersection of online dictionary learning [Mairal et al., JMLR’10; Mensch et al., ICML’16], structured sparsity/GraphNet [Grosenick et al., NeuroImage’13], structured sparse PCA [Jenatton et al., AISTATS’10], and TV-regularized decompositions for neuroimaging [Abraham MICCAI’13; Varoquaux et al., IPMI’10; PRNI’13]. The main claimed novelty is the integration of a Laplacian prior on atoms into the online BCD framework at near-vanilla cost.

Strengths
- Well-motivated application: learning spatially localized population-level components is highly relevant in neuroimaging.
- Scalable solver design: atom-wise proximal updates keep complexity close to standard online DL, with plausible runtimes on large 3D data.
- Sensible empirical evaluation dimensions: EV, stability across splits (Hungarian matching), qualitative maps, and behavioral prediction are appropriate.
- The idea of imposing compact, smooth atoms while keeping online scalability is practically valuable.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and methodology
- Objective definition: Eq. (2) mixes a “lim n→∞” expected risk with finite-sample algorithms/metrics. The training objective should be consistently stated (finite sample or expectation), and convergence claims should be aligned to that objective.
- Codes vs atoms sparsity: the method uses ridge-regularized codes and sparsity on atoms via constraints. This diverges from standard sparse coding. The paper should justify this design (identifiability, scaling invariances, interpretability) and provide an ablation (ridge vs l1 codes; l2 vs l1/simplex constraints on atoms).
- Convergence claims: The paper states “Convergence is guaranteed as in [16]” and that Alg. 2 “is guaranteed to converge to a global optimum at each iteration.” The latter is true only at the subproblem level; overall convergence in the nonconvex joint problem is to a stationary point under fixed hyperparameters and exact subproblem solutions. However, the paper also:
  - Uses time-varying αt ~ t^-1/2 and suggests time-varying γt (Eq. 9). These alter the objective over time and invalidate a direct appeal to [16].
  - Performs inexact atom updates (“1 or 2 FISTA iterations are sufficient to reach 1e-6 accuracy”), which is implausible and unsubstantiated; inexact BCD requires conditions for convergence.
  Please either provide a rigorous convergence argument under your inexact and time-varying scheme, or refrain from making guarantees.
- Lipschitz constant and FFT claims: The bound LF = 1 + 4D γ̃ presumes a particular discretization/boundary condition. Please specify ∇ and ∆ definitions (stencil, boundary conditions) and justify the spectral bound. The FFT-based alternative requires periodic BCs; with constraints C, the proximal is not closed-form—clarify what is done.
- Projection and constraints: Using simplex Sp(τ) imposes nonnegativity on atoms. Z-score GLM maps can be signed; the paper needs to justify positivity, or report results also for l1-ball without positivity. How is τ chosen in practice (values, CV protocol)?
- Fairness of baselines: CanICA is run on smoothed data (FWHM 6mm) while SODL/Smooth-SODL are not pre-smoothed. This is not an apples-to-apples comparison. Please either harmonize preprocessing or report both settings.
- Stability metric: The stability analysis is underspecified (choice of similarity measure, sign/scale alignment, masking, matching procedure). Provide exact protocol and confidence intervals.
- Behavioral prediction: The learning model for prediction (ridge? lasso? cross-validation details, feature standardization, confounds) is not described, and only outcomes with positive mean R^2 are reported, which introduces selection bias. Please fully specify the pipeline and report all outcomes with proper CV and uncertainty.
- Hyperparameter selection: You recommend K-fold CV on EV for γ and τ, but also propose time-varying αt, γt. What was actually used in each experiment? Provide chosen values and sensitivity curves.
- Runtime reporting: Hardware, BLAS/FFT backends, and implementation details are missing; “single CPU of laptop” is not reproducible.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typographical and formatting errors hinder readability: “th Conference …” header, repeated misspellings (“intepretable”, “intepretability”, “volumic”), equation typos (e.g., “maxUi” instead of min; missing ε in O(L/√ε); mangled Eq. 7/8 subscripts/superscripts), and inconsistent notation (Vj vs V j). Please proofread thoroughly.
- References are severely incomplete (missing years/venues/pages for many entries), and some attributions are imprecise (e.g., GraphNet). This is below NIPS standards.
- Figures/Tables: The text refers to Fig. 1 and Tab. 1; only partial descriptions appear in the submission. Please include all figures/tables with readable captions and quantitative values.

Originality
- The core idea—adding a Laplacian prior on atoms within online DL—is incremental relative to TV/GraphNet-based decompositions and online DL. The novelty is mainly engineering: bringing a known regularizer into an online BCD with per-atom proximal steps. This is useful, but the conceptual contribution is modest without stronger theory or compelling empirical advantages.

Significance
- The approach is promising for scalable, structured neuroimaging decompositions. However, the current empirical evidence is mixed: CanICA has higher EV and better behavioral prediction in your own results; gains are in interpretability and stability, but these need more rigorous quantification. Without clearer, reproducible improvements, impact is uncertain.

Pros
- Relevant problem; practical, scalable algorithmic design.
- Sensible use of Laplacian prior for compact atoms; qualitative maps are cleaner.
- Stability improvements over SODL are plausible and important.

Cons
- Methodological gaps (convergence under inexact/time-varying updates).
- Incomplete experimental specification; questionable fairness of baselines.
- Presentation quality issues (typos, equations, references); missing figures/tables.
- Limited novelty beyond engineering integration; mixed quantitative wins.

Questions for the authors
1) Please clarify the exact discretization and boundary conditions for ∆, and justify the spectral bound used in the FISTA step size.
2) Did you constrain atoms to be nonnegative (simplex) in all experiments? If so, why is this appropriate for signed GLM maps?
3) What was the exact model and CV protocol for behavioral prediction?
4) Do your convergence claims still hold with αt and γt changing over t and inexact atom updates?
5) Can you provide quantitative interpretability/compactness metrics (e.g., cluster size, spatial autocorrelation) and ablations for codes sparsity vs atoms sparsity?

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is interesting and practically relevant, but the submission has too many technical and presentation issues to meet NIPS standards in its current form. A revised, cleaner version with rigorous convergence discussion (or toned-down claims), reproducible and fair experiments, complete references, and stronger quantitative evidence could be competitive.
