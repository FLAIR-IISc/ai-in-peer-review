- Summary: Proposes Smooth-SODL, an online structured sparse dictionary learning method with a Laplacian penalty to learn spatially localized ROIs from inter-subject variability, validated on HCP data with theoretical and empirical support.

- Related work: Positions method against clustering, PCA/ICA, and sparse coding, notes scaling limitations of prior structured decompositions, and connects to TV-l1 and structured sparsity literature; suggests briefly citing deep learning parcellation work.

- Novelty: Combines online learning, structured sparsity, and inter-subject variability for principled, data-driven ROI definition.

- Scalability: Online framework enables handling high voxel counts and large subject numbers better than many existing structured methods.

- Interpretability: Produces spatially smooth, sparse atoms that are more interpretable than dense/unstructured representations.

- Empirical validation: Strong experiments on HCP comparing to CanICA and SODL using explained variance, reproducibility, and behavioral prediction.

- Theoretical grounding: Provides clear mathematical formulation and convergence justification for the BCD algorithm.

- Practical guidance: Discusses hyperparameter tuning and initialization, and acknowledges sensitivity to γ.

- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Results show notable sensitivity to α, γ, and τ; adaptive selection strategies beyond time-varying α are needed.

- Computational cost: Smooth-SODL is about 3x slower than standard SODL; further optimization and GPU acceleration should be explored.

- Comparison to deep learning: Lacks discussion/comparison to recent deep learning brain parcellation methods and trade-offs with interpretability.

- Laplacian specificity: Simple discrete Laplacian may not capture complex brain geometry; alternative spatial regularizers (e.g., graph-based) could help.

- Reproducibility details: Promises code release but should specify software libraries beyond Nilearn for better reproducibility.

- Arguments for acceptance: Method is novel, scalable, interpretable, empirically convincing on large data, and theoretically sound.

- Arguments against acceptance: Higher computational cost, hyperparameter sensitivity, and limited discussion of deep learning baselines.

- Overall assessment: Strong paper likely to impact neuroimaging; strengths outweigh minor weaknesses.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.

- Minor revision requests: (1) Add brief discussion of deep learning parcellation and trade-offs, (2) consider/mention algorithmic/GPU optimizations, (3) provide specific software/library details, (4) discuss alternative spatial regularization schemes.
