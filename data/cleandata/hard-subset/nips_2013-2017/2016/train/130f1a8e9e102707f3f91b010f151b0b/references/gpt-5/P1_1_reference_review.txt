Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), an online matrix factorization method for neuroimaging that augments the standard sparse online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. with a Laplacian (∇T∇) penalty on the dictionary atoms. The atoms are constrained to be sparse via an l1-ball or simplex constraint, while codes are obtained by ridge regression (with the option to use l1). The method is optimized by a block-coordinate approach: ridge coding per mini-batch and atom-wise dictionary updates that solve a denoising-like quadratic with Laplacian regularization and a convex constraint using projected first-order methods (FISTA). The goal is to learn spatially localized, blob-like components that better capture inter-subject variability in brain maps than unstructured factorizations. The paper evaluates on HCP task fMRI Z-maps, comparing Smooth-SODL to standard SODL and CanICA, reporting improved interpretability and stability at modest runtime overhead, and competitive explained variance and behavioral prediction.

This work sits at the intersection of online dictionary learning [Mairal 2010, Mensch 2016], spatially structured regularization (GraphNet, TV-l1) [Grosenick 2013; Abraham MICCAI; Varoquaux et al.], and multi-subject decompositions (CanICA, MELODIC). Prior structured dictionary learning for brain imaging has typically used slow alternating minimization; here the claim is an efficient online approach with similar convergence guarantees to SODL while imposing spatial smoothness.

Strengths
- Practical, scalable extension: The addition of a Laplacian penalty within the online BCD framework is conceptually simple and integrates well with the SODL sufficient statistics. The reported 2–3x runtime overhead vs SODL is attractive for large-scale neuroimaging.
- Well-motivated objective: Spatially smooth and sparse atoms are appropriate for defining interpretable ROI-like components, and the use of positivity/simplex constraints is sensible in this context.
- Empirical evaluation on a large, relevant dataset (HCP task fMRI), with multiple metrics: explained variance, atom stability across splits, behavioral prediction, and qualitative interpretability.
- Useful insights: Learning curves showing that spatial regularization helps particularly in the low-data regime; discussion of hyperparameter regimes and practical choices (mini-batches, αt ∝ t−1/2).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to existing structured sparse coding: Adding a quadratic Laplacian to SODL is a relatively straightforward variant, closely related to GraphNet-style smoothing and structured PCA (Jenatton et al.), and to prior TV-regularized dictionary learning in neuroimaging (Abraham; Varoquaux). The paper would benefit from stronger positioning vs these methods.
- Missing or weak baselines: No comparison to TV-regularized dictionary learning (e.g., TV-l1), structured sparse PCA, or spatially constrained ICA/NMF. Since the stated contribution is “structured atoms at scale,” comparisons to other structured approaches are crucial.
- Some technical claims are imprecise or questionable:
  - The statement that 1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e−6 accuracy seems unrealistic; please report actual stopping criteria and per-atom iteration counts.
  - Eq. (9) and the discussion of “Lipschitz blow-up” with t are unclear. With ∆ = ∇T∇, the Lipschitz constant is 1 + γ̃‖∆‖, where γ̃ = γ / (At[j,j]/t). As t grows, At[j,j]/t converges to the average code energy; the Lipschitz constant should not diverge with t. Please provide a correct derivation and practical guidance on γ scheduling (if any).
  - The guarantee “global optimum at each iteration” applies per atom subproblem; overall the algorithm converges to a stationary point. This should be stated more carefully.
- Experimental details and reproducibility need strengthening:
  - Hyperparameters: The ranges and selection protocol for γ and τ (and positivity vs signed atoms) should be clearly specified. The use of αt ∝ t−1/2: how sensitive are results to this schedule?
  - Stability metric: Specify precisely the matching procedure (Hungarian), sign handling, normalization, number of splits and confidence intervals.
  - Predictive analyses: Describe the predictive model, confound handling (e.g., motion, age), cross-validation protocol, and hyperparameter tuning. Otherwise the R2 comparisons risk being confounded.
  - Runtime: Provide number of passes T and total updates; current wall-clock numbers seem very low for p ≈ 2.6×105 and k = 40.
- Clarity/organization: The paper is generally readable but contains typos and notation inconsistencies (e.g., mixed use of ∆ and Laplacian; “1 or 2 iterations … 10−6”; “parameter-free” while FISTA needs step sizes; minor grammar issues). The supplementary materials are referenced for key elements (FFT updates, convergence assumptions), but not available here.

Quality
Overall technically sound for an incremental algorithmic contribution; the optimization subproblems are convex and standard. However, some theoretical statements need correction/clarification, and key implementation details (stopping criteria, boundary conditions, mask handling for ∆, projection operators) should be specified to ensure reproducibility.

Clarity
Moderately clear, but could be improved by tightening the derivations, fixing typos, and expanding the methods and evaluation sections with precise protocols.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of Laplacian/GraphNet regularization is well-known; the main novelty is its integration into an online SODL framework with empirical demonstration at scale.

Significance
Moderate for neuroimaging practitioners: the method yields more interpretable and stable components at reasonable cost. Broader machine learning impact is limited unless stronger comparisons and theoretical insights are provided.

Questions for the authors
1. Please clarify Eq. (9) and the dependence of the Lipschitz constant on t; do you actually decay γt in practice?
2. How many FISTA iterations per atom update are used on average; what is the stopping rule?
3. What boundary conditions and brain mask handling do you use for ∆? Is FFT actually used given irregular masks?
4. Provide precise hyperparameter grids and selection criteria; how sensitive are results to τ and γ?
5. How is stability computed (splits, matching, sign resolution), and can you report confidence intervals?
6. What predictor and CV protocol are used in behavioral prediction? Are potential confounds regressed out?

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method that improves interpretability/stability of learned brain regions.
- Solid empirical evidence on a large public dataset.
- Clear utility for ROI discovery and dimensionality reduction in neuroimaging.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty; missing comparisons to other structured methods.
- Some technical claims need correction and more rigorous presentation.
- Reproducibility and experimental detail need strengthening.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper addresses an important practical problem and shows promising results, but the novelty is modest and the evaluation and technical presentation require additional rigor. Addressing the missing baselines, clarifying the optimization details (especially Eq. (9)), and tightening the experimental protocols would substantially strengthen the contribution. Suggestions above are intended to be constructive toward a future revision.
