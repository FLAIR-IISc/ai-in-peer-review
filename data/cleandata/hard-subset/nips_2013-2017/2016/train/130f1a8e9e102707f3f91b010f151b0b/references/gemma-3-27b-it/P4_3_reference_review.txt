This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), for learning brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data. The core idea is to leverage inter-subject variability to define regions by learning a sparse dictionary of spatially smooth component maps. The method extends existing online dictionary learning techniques by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatial smoothness. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project, showing improvements in dictionary interpretability, stability, and predictive power compared to existing methods like ICA and standard sparse dictionary learning.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a crucial problem in neuroimaging – principled region of interest (ROI) definition. The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches and positions Smooth-SODL as a promising solution. The technical presentation is generally sound, with a clear formulation of the optimization problem and a detailed explanation of the algorithm. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including comparisons with established methods on a publicly available dataset, and metrics covering explanation variance, stability, and prediction accuracy. The related work section is also strong, accurately situating the contribution within the existing literature. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for spatial smoothness is a novel contribution. While both online dictionary learning and spatial regularization have been explored individually, their combination appears to be new and addresses a significant challenge in neuroimaging.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is mathematically well-defined, and the algorithm is presented with sufficient detail for reproduction. The BCD approach is a standard technique for solving this type of problem, and the authors appropriately address the computational complexity considerations. The discussion of the Lipschitz constant and the choice of hyperparameters is helpful.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset are well-designed and demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. The results show improvements over competing methods across several key metrics. The inclusion of both quantitative and qualitative results strengthens the findings.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise and informative.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is important, and the proposed solution has the potential to advance the field of neuroimaging by providing a more principled and automated approach to ROI definition.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper discusses hyperparameter selection, the sensitivity of the results to the choice of α and γ is not fully explored. A more systematic analysis of the impact of these parameters would be beneficial. The discussion of the optimality regime could be expanded, including more practical guidelines for selecting these values.
*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the increased computational cost compared to standard SODL, a more detailed analysis of the runtime performance for different dataset sizes and dictionary sizes would be helpful. The claim of a "factor of 2 or 3" increase seems optimistic based on the reported times (340s vs 110s is a factor of ~3.1).
*   **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to CanICA is somewhat limited, particularly the use of a hard-thresholded version (tCanICA). A more direct comparison to optimized CanICA components, or more advanced spatial smoothing techniques, could provide a stronger baseline.
* **Lack of Statistical Testing:** There is no statistical significance testing performed on the quantitative results. While the trends are clear, statistical tests would increase confidence in the findings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for learning brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach and show improvements over existing methods.
*   The problem addressed is important, and the solution has the potential to advance the field.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter selection is not fully explored.
*   The computational cost is a concern, and a more detailed analysis would be beneficial.
*   The comparison to state-of-the-art methods could be more comprehensive, and statistical significance testing is missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed Smooth-SODL method is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated. The potential impact of this work is significant. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding hyperparameter sensitivity, computational cost analysis, and statistical significance testing. Specifically, I suggest adding a section with a more comprehensive ablation study demonstrating the impact of the Laplacian penalty and a discussion of the computational scaling. Also, adding p-values to the quantitative results would strengthen the findings.
