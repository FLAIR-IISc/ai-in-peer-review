This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of defining brain regions of interest (ROIs) in a data-driven manner from large-scale neuroimaging datasets. The central idea is to model inter-subject variability not as noise, but as a source of information for discovering spatially coherent brain networks. To achieve this, the authors propose "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning" (Smooth-SODL), a novel multivariate decomposition method. The proposed model extends the well-known online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) [16] by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages the learned atoms (the "brain regions") to be spatially smooth and blob-like, in addition to being sparse (enforced by an l1-ball constraint). The model is optimized using an online block-coordinate descent algorithm, which makes it scalable to massive datasets where both the number of subjects and voxels are large. The authors provide a compelling empirical evaluation on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (HCP), comparing their method against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a popular group-level ICA method (CanICA).

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Motivation and Clear Contribution:** The paper is very well-motivated. The need for principled, data-driven methods to define functional ROIs that are both stable and interpretable is a significant one in neuroimaging. The authors' proposal to extend a scalable online learning framework with a spatial structure prior is a direct and elegant solution to this problem. The contribution is clear: a computationally efficient method for learning structured, sparse components from large-scale brain data.

2.  **Sound and Scalable Methodology:** The extension of the SODL framework is technically sound. The formulation of the objective function (Eq. 2) is principled, combining a data fidelity term with penalties on both the codes (Ridge) and the dictionary atoms (Laplacian for smoothness, l1-ball for sparsity). The algorithmic solution via block-coordinate descent is appropriate, and the analysis of the dictionary update step as a GraphNet-like denoising problem is insightful. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that this added structural prior comes at a modest computational cost (a ~3x increase in runtime over SODL), preserving the key advantage of the online approach.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and support the paper's claims effectively.
    *   The use of the large HCP dataset demonstrates the method's scalability and relevance to modern neuroimaging.
    *   The choice of baselines (unstructured SODL and dense CanICA) allows for a clear demonstration of the benefits of the proposed structured regularization.
    *   The evaluation metrics are comprehensive, covering qualitative visual inspection, quantitative measures of explained variance and stability, and a downstream task (prediction of behavioral variables).
    *   The learning curve analysis in Table 1 is particularly effective. It provides strong evidence that the spatial regularization is most beneficial in lower sample size regimes, which is a very common scenario in clinical studies, thus broadening the potential impact of this work beyond massive datasets like HCP.

4.  **High Potential for Impact:** The proposed method offers a practical and powerful tool for neuroimaging researchers. It strikes an excellent balance between model expressiveness, interpretability, and computational feasibility. The learned maps are qualitatively superior to the baselines, appearing less noisy and more anatomically plausible. The plan to integrate the implementation into the popular Nilearn library significantly increases the likelihood that this work will be adopted and built upon by the community.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Justification for Laplacian vs. Other Structural Priors:** The paper positions itself against methods using Total Variation (TV) regularization [1] but doesn't discuss the motivation for choosing a Laplacian penalty over a TV penalty. The Laplacian (an L2-norm on the gradient) promotes smooth variations, while TV (an L1-norm on the gradient) promotes piecewise-constant regions with sharp boundaries. The latter might be a more realistic prior for distinct functional brain areas. While the Laplacian is computationally convenient due to its differentiability, a brief discussion justifying this choice from a modeling perspective, or acknowledging the trade-offs, would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Discussion on Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper is honest about the difficulty of hyperparameter tuning, which is commendable. However, the reliance on k-fold cross-validation for setting the key parameters `γ` (smoothness) and `τ` (sparsity) can be very computationally expensive, especially in a large-scale online setting. While the authors present a stability analysis (Fig. 1, described in the text), more guidance on heuristics or more efficient tuning strategies would improve the practical utility of the method. For instance, is there a theoretical relationship between the data variance, noise level, and optimal `γ`?

3.  **Choice of Code Regularization:** The authors opt for a Ridge (`l2`) penalty on the codes `U` for stability, in contrast to the `l1` penalty often used in sparse coding. This is a reasonable choice, but the implications are not fully discussed. An `l2` penalty leads to dense codes, meaning each subject's brain map is represented as a combination of *all* dictionary atoms. An `l1` penalty would imply that a subject's map is represented by a small subset of atoms, which could be an interesting model of subject-specific network recruitment. A sentence or two justifying the `l2` choice and its modeling implications would be beneficial.

4.  **Clarity on "Regions":** The paper uses the term "learning brain regions" and "ROIs." While the learned dictionary atoms are spatially localized patterns, they are continuous-valued "soft" parcels, not discrete regions with hard boundaries. This is a common and acceptable approach, but the terminology could be slightly more precise (e.g., "learning functional modes" or "basis maps"). If the goal is truly to derive discrete ROIs, the paper should mention how one might post-process these maps (e.g., via thresholding or watershed segmentation).

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a solid contribution to both the machine learning and computational neuroscience fields. It presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound method that addresses a real-world problem. The experimental validation is thorough and convincingly demonstrates the advantages of the proposed approach over existing methods. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate mostly to clarifying modeling choices and practical considerations, rather than fundamental flaws. The work is original, significant, and clearly presented. It advances the state-of-the-art in data-driven functional brain parcellation and is highly likely to be impactful. I strongly recommend acceptance.
