

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging that incorporates spatial structure into brain region discovery. The authors address a key challenge in neuroimaging: the lack of principled methods for defining brain regions (ROIs) that capture meaningful inter-subject variability rather than relying on anatomical segmentation or predefined ROIs. The approach extends the Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (SODL) framework [16] by adding a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms, resulting in more interpretable brain regions. While structured dictionary learning has been applied to neuroimaging (e.g., [1, 25]), these prior approaches typically use slow alternating optimization rather than scalable online methods. The authors position their work as the first to successfully integrate spatial structure into online dictionary learning for large-scale neuroimaging.

## Strengths

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it addresses a genuine problem in neuroimaging analysis with a well-motivated solution. The authors provide a compelling rationale for why spatially structured dictionaries are needed (to capture meaningful brain organization rather than noise) and demonstrate that their approach produces more interpretable regions than existing methods. Second, the technical contribution is solid: the Laplacian penalty for spatial structure is well-integrated into the online dictionary learning framework, with only a modest computational cost (3x slower than SODL, while CanICA is 5x slower). Third, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, comparing Smooth-SODL against relevant baselines (SODL, CanICA) across multiple metrics (visual quality, explained variance, stability, prediction performance). The learning curve analysis showing Smooth-SODL's superior performance with limited data is particularly convincing. Finally, the paper provides valuable practical insights on hyperparameter selection, which is a known challenge in dictionary learning.

## Weaknesses

The main weaknesses concern novelty and theoretical grounding. While the Laplacian penalty for spatial structure is a reasonable choice for neuroimaging, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish this approach from prior work. For instance, [1] already uses a TV-`1 penalty for brain atlas learning, and the authors don't clearly explain how their method improves upon this work. The theoretical analysis is also somewhat limited; while convergence is mentioned, the paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of how the Laplacian penalty affects the optimization landscape or statistical properties of the learned dictionaries. The significance could also be strengthened by more explicitly demonstrating how the learned regions lead to new neuroscientific insights rather than just improved statistical metrics.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Clarify novelty**: Provide a more detailed comparison with [1] and other structured dictionary learning approaches in neuroimaging, explicitly stating how Smooth-SODL differs and improves upon them.

2. **Strengthen theoretical analysis**: Add a section discussing the theoretical implications of the Laplacian penalty, such as how it affects the sparsity-structure trade-off or the statistical properties of the learned dictionaries.

3. **Improve neuroscientific validation**: Include a more detailed analysis of how the learned regions correspond to known brain networks, perhaps by comparing with established atlases or providing a neuroscientific interpretation of the top-ranked regions.

4. **Clarify the relationship between spatial structure and performance**: The paper shows that spatial regularization helps with small data regimes, but doesn't clearly explain *why* this is the case from a theoretical perspective.

5. **Expand the experimental comparison**: Consider adding comparisons with other structured dictionary learning methods (e.g., those using total-variation penalties) to better establish the relative merits of the Laplacian approach.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound and well-executed extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging. It addresses a genuine problem with a practical solution that has the potential to improve brain region discovery in neuroimaging analysis. The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. While the novelty is somewhat incremental (building on existing dictionary learning and structured learning techniques), the paper makes a valuable contribution to the neuroimaging community by providing a computationally efficient method for learning structured brain regions.

The paper meets NeurIPS quality standards with solid technical content, clear presentation, and a well-designed experimental evaluation. The significance is demonstrated through improved interpretability and performance on relevant metrics. While the novelty could be more pronounced, the work is a meaningful contribution to the field of neuroimaging analysis.

**Recommendation: Accept** 

The paper would benefit from the suggested improvements, particularly in clarifying the novelty and strengthening the theoretical analysis, but these are minor issues that could be addressed in a camera-ready version. The work is significant, technically sound, and well-executed, making it a strong candidate for NeurIPS.
