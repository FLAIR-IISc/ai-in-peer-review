### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a method called Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL) to identify brain regions of interest (ROIs) from large-scale neuroimaging data. The central idea is to model statistical maps from a population of subjects as linear combinations of a shared dictionary of spatial components, or "atoms". To ensure these atoms are interpretable as brain regions, the authors formulate a dictionary learning problem that encourages both sparsity (via an `l1`-ball constraint) and spatial smoothness/compactness (via a Laplacian penalty). The key contribution is the development of a scalable, online optimization algorithm based on Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD), which extends the work of Mairal et al. (2010) [16]. This online approach allows the method to handle massive datasets where both the number of subjects and the number of voxels are large. The authors evaluate their method on a large task-fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (HCP), comparing it to standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA (CanICA). They assess the methods based on the interpretability of the resulting components, explained variance, stability, and predictive power for behavioral variables.

The work is situated within the field of data-driven functional brain parcellation. It builds directly upon the online dictionary learning framework of [16] and incorporates spatial priors, a concept explored in batch settings by works like Varoquaux et al. (2011) [25] and Abraham et al. (2014) [1] who used penalties such as Total Variation. The novelty of the current work lies in integrating a spatial smoothness penalty into a computationally efficient online learning framework, thereby enabling structured decompositions on a massive scale.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The model is a well-posed optimization problem (Eq. 2) that appropriately combines a data-fidelity term with sparsity and smoothness regularizers. The proposed BCD optimization scheme is a standard and suitable choice. The derivation of the dictionary update sub-problem (Eq. 7) is correct, and the authors correctly identify it as a strongly-convex problem solvable by fast first-order methods like FISTA. The discussion of the Lipschitz constant and the practical guidance for setting the `γ` parameter (Eq. 9) demonstrate a careful analysis of the algorithm's properties. The empirical evaluation is strong, using a large, public, and challenging dataset (HCP). The choice of metrics (explained variance, stability, prediction score) is comprehensive and allows for a multi-faceted comparison of the different methods. The learning curve analysis presented in Table 1 is particularly valuable, as it clearly demonstrates the benefit of the proposed spatial regularization, especially when data is limited.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the problem is clear, and the proposed model is introduced systematically. The description of the algorithm is sufficient for an expert to understand the main steps. However, there are some areas for improvement. The discussion on hyper-parameter tuning in Section 3.2 refers to "Fig. 1", but this figure is not included in the manuscript, making it difficult to fully assess the reported findings on parameter sensitivity. While the algorithmic components are described, a more detailed pseudocode block for the dictionary update sub-routine (solving Eq. 8 with FISTA) would improve reproducibility. Finally, in Figure 2(c), the text mentions a "RAW" model, but this label does not appear in the plot's legend, causing minor confusion.

**Originality:** The primary contribution is the development of a scalable *online* algorithm for *structured* sparse dictionary learning. While the constituent parts—online dictionary learning [16] and spatial regularization for brain maps [1, 25]—are not new, their combination into an efficient, large-scale algorithm is novel and non-trivial. The paper successfully bridges the gap between computationally expensive but well-structured batch methods and highly scalable but unstructured online methods. It clearly articulates this contribution relative to prior work, highlighting the significant speed-up over alternative approaches that rely on slower alternated minimization. The work represents a valuable engineering and algorithmic contribution that enables existing ideas to be applied at a previously prohibitive scale.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of high significance in the neuroimaging community: the data-driven definition of functional brain regions from large population cohorts. As datasets like HCP and the UK Biobank become standard, scalable and robust methods for dimensionality reduction and feature extraction are critical. The proposed Smooth-SODL provides a principled and computationally tractable solution. The results demonstrate that the method produces more interpretable and stable brain networks than its unstructured counterpart (SODL) and hard-thresholded ICA, while maintaining competitive performance. The promise of releasing the implementation in a widely-used open-source library (Nilearn) will further enhance its potential impact and encourage adoption by practitioners and researchers. The finding that spatial regularization is most beneficial in smaller-sample regimes is an important practical insight for the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper presents a scalable and technically sound algorithm for an important and unsolved problem in large-scale neuroimaging analysis.
*   **Pro:** The experimental validation is thorough, conducted on a large, modern dataset, and uses multiple relevant metrics to provide a nuanced comparison against appropriate baselines.
*   **Pro:** The work represents a significant practical advance by making structured dictionary learning computationally feasible for massive datasets, a key challenge in the field.
*   **Pro:** The results clearly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed spatial regularization in terms of component interpretability and stability, particularly in data-limited scenarios.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Con:** The core technical novelty is arguably incremental, as it combines existing concepts (online dictionary learning and Laplacian regularization) rather than introducing a fundamentally new model or optimization principle.
*   **Con:** The clarity of the paper is slightly hampered by a missing figure (Fig. 1) referenced in the text, which makes the hyper-parameter analysis difficult to verify.
*   **Con:** The paper could be strengthened by a brief discussion motivating the choice of a Laplacian penalty over other spatial regularizers like Total Variation, which is used in related work [1] and may be more suitable for defining regions with sharp boundaries.

Overall, the strengths of the paper, particularly its technical soundness, practical significance, and strong empirical results, outweigh the weaknesses. It is a solid contribution to the field of computational neuroimaging and machine learning.
