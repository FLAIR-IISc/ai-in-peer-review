- Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online sparse dictionary learning method with a Laplacian penalty to discover spatially localized, smooth brain regions from population fMRI.  
- Frames region definition as a decomposition problem to handle inter-subject variability and capture shared activity patterns.  
- Extends online dictionary learning to include spatial smoothness, improving interpretability and performance over standard SODL.  
- Demonstrates method on a large HCP dataset, showing improved dictionary quality, reproducibility, and predictive power versus ICA-based approaches and SODL.  
- Correctly situates work relative to univariate, ROI, clustering, PCA/ICA, and online learning literature, and cites spatial regularization and GraphNet connections.  
- Suggests need for more discussion of recent scalable sparse online learning advances.  
- Novelty: combines online learning scalability with Laplacian-based spatial regularization for interpretable components.  
- Scalability: online framework handles large neuroimaging datasets better than batch methods like ICA.  
- Empirical validation: well-designed experiments and ablation study (varying γ) show consistent improvements and insights on spatial regularization.  
- Practicality: offers guidance on hyperparameter tuning, initialization, and provides an implementation in Nilearn.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized with clear method explanation.  
- Theoretical analysis is limited; more rigorous study of the Laplacian penalty’s impact on convergence and solution quality is needed.  
- Comparisons to related work feel superficial; should more thoroughly compare to modern sparse ICA and methods with spatial priors.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: parameter choices (α, γ, τ) may be important; a systematic sensitivity analysis is recommended.  
- Interpretation of atoms: more quantitative linkage to known brain networks, anatomy, or cognitive functions would strengthen claims.  
- Behavioral prediction: R^2 values are modest; deeper investigation into component–behavior relationships is warranted.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, empirically strong, practical, scalable method with clear contributions.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited theory, insufficient comparisons, and hyperparameter sensitivity concerns.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with significant potential, meriting acceptance subject to addressing noted weaknesses.  
- Recommendation: accept; authors should expand theoretical analysis, comparisons, sensitivity analyses, and add quantitative visualizations linking components to known networks.
