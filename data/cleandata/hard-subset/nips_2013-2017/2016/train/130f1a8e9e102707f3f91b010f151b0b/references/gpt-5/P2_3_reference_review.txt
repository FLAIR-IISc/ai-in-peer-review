Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning method for neuroimaging that enforces both sparsity and spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms by combining an l1-type constraint set (e.g., l1-ball or simplex) with a Laplacian penalty on the atoms. Codes are obtained via ridge regression rather than l1, and dictionary updates are handled by block-coordinate descent with a proximal step (FISTA) for each atom. The authors argue that this adds spatial structure at minor computational cost over standard online sparse dictionary learning (SODL, Mairal et al.). On a large HCP task-fMRI dataset (n=500, p≈2.6×10^5, k=40), Smooth-SODL yields more interpretable, spatially compact “blob-like” components, higher stability across splits, competitive explained variance, and prediction performance comparable to PCA/ICA variants while remaining computationally efficient. They discuss hyperparameter selection and provide learning curves showing that the gains over SODL are largest in low-data regimes.

Quality
- Methodological soundness: The optimization framework is sensible. The subproblem per atom is a strongly convex quadratic with a Laplacian term and a simple convex constraint; using FISTA with projections onto the constraint set is appropriate. Using ridge codes stabilizes the inner least-squares step and simplifies the online updates. The block-coordinate descent structure mirrors Mairal et al., and convergence to a stationary point is reasonable under the stated assumptions.
- Rigor and limits: Some claims need stronger support. The practical observation that 1–2 FISTA iterations suffice is empirically motivated; no stopping criteria or tolerance schedule is specified, and the potential impact of inexact updates on overall convergence is not analyzed. The discussion on the Lipschitz constant blow-up and the suggested scaling γt = O(‖At‖∞∞/t) is insightful but not formalized into a principled schedule or accompanied by sensitivity/ablation studies. The empirical evaluation is solid but limited to one contrast/task; error bars and statistical tests are largely absent.
- Experimental design: The comparisons (SODL, CanICA, thresholded CanICA) are relevant. The demonstration of improved stability and early learning curves is compelling. However, details critical for reproducibility are missing: preprocessing/normalization of inputs, exact projection set (l1-ball vs simplex), if and why non-negativity is imposed (simplex implies non-negative atoms), specifics of the behavioral prediction pipeline (model class, regularization, and whether feature selection for RAW is nested within CV to avoid leakage). These omissions make it hard to assess fairness and to reproduce results.
Overall, the work is technically sound with well-justified design choices, but would benefit from more complete methodological and evaluation details.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-structured and readable, with a clear presentation of the objective, algorithms, and empirical findings. The link to GraphNet and structured sparsity is appropriate.
- There are many typographical errors and minor grammatical issues. More importantly, key implementation details are glossed over (see above). The supplementary material is referenced for FFT-based updates and convergence details but not available here; given that the novelty hinges on efficient updates, including at least a brief complexity analysis and key derivations in the main text would be helpful.

Originality
- The main novelty is to integrate a Laplacian smoothness prior on dictionary atoms into the online dictionary-learning framework, retaining scalability while improving interpretability. Prior structured dictionary-learning with TV/GraphNet penalties exists but is typically offline and slow; this online adaptation is a practical and useful extension.
- On the coding side, ridge codes (vs l1) are not new, but the combination with structured atom regularization and online updates in neuroimaging at this scale is a meaningful contribution.
- The work is primarily an engineering advance with modest theoretical novelty; nonetheless, the combination and its demonstrated empirical benefits are nontrivial.

Significance
- Practical impact: The ability to learn smooth, sparse, interpretable components online at HCP scale is valuable to the neuroimaging community. The 3× runtime overhead over SODL while delivering markedly better interpretability is attractive.
- Scientific impact: The improvements in stability and early-sample efficiency are important for applications with limited data and for robust ROI definition. Gains in explained variance are modest relative to PCA/ICA (as expected), but the trade-off in interpretability favors the proposed method.
- Breadth: The method should transfer to other modalities (rest fMRI, PET) and tasks, but demonstrations are limited to one contrast.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated objective that aligns with neuroscientific needs (ROI interpretability and stability).
- Sound optimization strategy with scalable online updates; sensible use of proximal gradient for Laplacian smoothing.
- Strong large-scale experiment; compelling qualitative improvements and improved stability; useful learning-curve analysis.
- Reasonable runtime and plan to release code in Nilearn.

Weaknesses
- Missing details on constraints (positivity), preprocessing, and behavioral prediction protocol; risk of unfair comparisons or leakage.
- Limited dataset scope (single task/contrast); no error bars or statistical testing.
- The time-varying hyperparameter schedules (αt, γt) are heuristics without ablations; the claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations suffice lacks robustness checks.
- Theoretical discussion is light beyond standard BCD arguments; no guarantees under inexact inner solves.
- Some baselines are missing (e.g., TV-regularized dictionary learning, nonnegative matrix factorization with spatial regularization).

Questions for authors
- Are atoms constrained to be nonnegative (simplex)? If so, how does this affect signed fMRI contrast maps? Did you consider signed constraints or separate positive/negative atoms?
- How was feature selection for RAW and hyperparameter tuning for prediction performed—fully nested within CV?
- Can you provide ablations for αt and γt schedules and the number of FISTA iterations?
- What preprocessing (normalization, masking, detrending) was applied?

Suggestions
- Clarify and justify the constraint set choice; report results with and without non-negativity.
- Add nested CV details and leakage safeguards; include error bars and statistical tests.
- Include an ablation study on hyperparameters and inner-solver accuracy.
- Compare against a TV-regularized offline baseline (even if slower) on a subset to quantify the speed/quality trade-off.
- Expand to additional tasks/datasets to demonstrate robustness.

Overall evaluation
- Quality: good but missing important details
- Clarity: generally clear, needs polishing and more implementation specifics
- Originality: incremental but meaningful
- Significance: high practical value for neuroimaging; moderate broader ML impact

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a practical, scalable advance with clear empirical benefits in a relevant application, though it should strengthen methodological details and evaluation rigor.
