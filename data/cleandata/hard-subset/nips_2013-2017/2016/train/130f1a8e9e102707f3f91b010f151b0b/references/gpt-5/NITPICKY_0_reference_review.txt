Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging that augments standard sparse online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., JMLR 2010) with a Laplacian (GraphNet-style) regularization on the dictionary atoms to encourage spatially smooth, blob-like, and sparse components. Codes are estimated with ridge regression; atoms are constrained in an L1 ball or simplex, and updated via block coordinate descent with a FISTA inner loop to handle the Laplacian-regularized denoising subproblem. Experiments on HCP task fMRI (500 subjects, Math vs Story contrast) compare to SODL and CanICA in terms of explained variance, stability, interpretability, and behavioral prediction. The authors claim comparable scalability to SODL (2–3× slower), improved interpretability/stability over SODL, and competitive predictive performance.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on online dictionary learning (Mairal et al.), and draws connections to GraphNet (Grosenick et al.) and structured sparse decomposition in neuroimaging (Abraham et al.; Varoquaux et al.). However, “graph/Laplacian-regularized dictionary learning” has been explored outside neuroimaging (e.g., structured/graph-regularized DL and sparse coding literature), and this connection is not adequately acknowledged or contrasted.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant practical need in neuroimaging: scalable, structured, interpretable multivariate decompositions at population scale.
- Algorithmic idea is simple and potentially useful: keep the online SODL machinery, replace atom projection with a light FISTA step for Laplacian-regularized denoising under a convex constraint.
- Empirical results suggest better interpretability and some stability gains over unstructured SODL, with modest computational overhead.
- Large public dataset; reasonable baselines (SODL, PCA/ICA).

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality (methods, correctness)
- Objective/notation issues: ΩLap(v) is defined with inconsistent factors: “1 p∑k (∇x v)^2_k + … = 1/2 v^T Δ v” mixes an unexplained 1/p and a 1/2 factor. Δ is defined as ∇^T∇ (PSD), but referred to as “Laplacian” (which in PDEs is typically negative semi-definite). Please fix sign and constants.
- Dictionary update derivation (Eq. 7–8) is opaque. The mapping from the standard SODL quadratic with weight A_t[j,j] to the scaled denoising objective F_{γ̃} with γ̃ appears as “Fγ(At[j,j]/t)−1(…)” which is unclear and dimensionally confusing. A clean derivation is needed (what is γ̃ exactly?).
- “Parameter free: there is no step size to tune” contradicts the fact that the inner FISTA step needs 1/L. Later you compute L = 1 + γ̃ L_Ω; then you also introduce L_t = 1 + 4D γ (A_t[j,j]/t)^{-1}. This sudden dependence on A_t is not properly justified.
- Claim “1 or 2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient to reach 1e-6 accuracy” is not credible without evidence; also incompatible with the earlier statement that each atom update “is guaranteed to converge to a global optimum.” If you use only 1–2 iterations, you are not solving the convex subproblem to optimality; quantify the impact of inexactness on convergence of the outer BCD.
- The suggestion to use FFT for the Laplacian update ignores masks and irregular boundaries typical in fMRI; Δ is not circulant on brain masks. Explain boundary conditions and when FFT is valid.
- Hyperparameters: you propose α_t ∼ t^{-1/2} (citing Ying & Zhou, which is for online classification), and γ_t scaling with A_t to avoid vanishing step sizes. These time-varying schedules change the objective relative to Eq. (2) and undermine the convergence guarantees borrowed from [16], which assume fixed regularization. Either analyze the resulting algorithm or keep fixed parameters in theory and practice.
- Constraints: you primarily use the simplex S_p(τ), i.e., positivity plus fixed L1-norm, which changes scale identifiability and interpretability (codes can be negative, leading to subtractive combinations of positive atoms). Discuss and/or constrain codes; provide ablations on positivity vs no-positivity.

Clarity
- The paper has many typos, grammar issues, and notation glitches that impede understanding: e.g., “intepretable/intepretability” (throughout), “volumic,” “we typically use we use,” missing spaces around symbols, Algorithm 2 loops “for j=1 to r” (what is r?), ambiguous “lim n→∞” objective, undefined stopping criteria, undefined preprocessing (mask, centering, scaling), and undefined Δ boundary conditions.
- Key elements are missing or insufficiently specified: number of passes T, mini-batch handling in At/Bt (rank-η updates), stability metric details (matching setup, correlation definition), explained variance definition on test data, regression model used for behavioral prediction (ridge? CV protocol?), splits/seed control, and whether SODL baseline uses ridge- or lasso-coding (fairness).

Originality
- Adding a Laplacian penalty on atoms within online DL is a reasonable combination, but not fundamentally new; similar graph/Laplacian-regularized DL and sparse coding have been studied. Missing citations to prior “graph-regularized dictionary learning” weaken the novelty claim; clarify what is new (the specific way to embed Laplacian into Mairal’s online BCD with a cheap denoiser? any new analysis?).

Significance
- Potentially useful for large neuroimaging cohorts; modest computational overhead is attractive. However, the empirical support is weakened by:
  - Figures/Tables (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Table 1) are referenced but not provided here; claims on EV, stability, and learning curves cannot be verified.
  - Baseline fairness: CanICA is applied to pre-smoothed data whereas SODL/Smooth-SODL are not. This confounds interpretability and EV comparisons.
  - Thresholded CanICA (tCanICA) is not a standard method and is sensitive to threshold choice; report sensitivity or avoid as a primary baseline.
  - Single contrast (Math vs Story); no ablation on γ, τ beyond qualitative comments; no reproducibility stats (variance over runs).
  - No code at review time; reproducibility is limited.

Pros
- Practical, scalable approach; straightforward to implement in existing online DL pipelines.
- Empirical improvements in interpretability/stability are plausible and valuable.
- Uses a large, relevant dataset.

Cons
- Derivation/notation inconsistencies; theoretical claims overstate convergence and accuracy.
- Incomplete/misaligned baselines and missing details/figures undermine empirical claims.
- Limited novelty relative to graph-regularized DL literature; missing citations.
- Many presentation and formatting issues.

Questions for authors
1) Provide a clean derivation of Eq. (7–8), explicitly identifying γ̃ and the Lipschitz constant used by FISTA under the actual mask/discretization.  
2) Do you solve the atom subproblem to optimality? If not, quantify the effect of inexact updates on the outer convergence; provide empirical sensitivity to number of FISTA iterations.  
3) What regression model and CV protocol were used for behavioral prediction? Are results robust across targets and splits?  
4) How was SODL instantiated (ridge or lasso codes)? Provide a fair apples-to-apples comparison.  
5) Report ablations on positivity, smoothing for all methods, and sensitivity to γ, τ (with quantitative metrics).  
6) Will you release code and exact preprocessing details (mask, Δ, normalization, T, seeds) to ensure reproducibility?

Minor issues/typos (non-exhaustive)
- Multiple misspellings: “intepretable/intepretability,” “volumic,” grammar errors.  
- Alg. 2 “for j=1..r”: r should be k.  
- “1 p∑” in Eq. (4) likely “∑_{k=1}^p”; remove stray 1/p.  
- “There is not step size to tune” → “no step size,” but see comment above.  
- “th Conference on NIPS 2016” stray line.  
- Figures/Tables not present; ensure captions match content and axes are defined.

Recommendation: Reject
While the idea is promising and likely useful, the submission has numerous technical, clarity, and experimental issues that collectively fall short of NeurIPS standards. A revised version with corrected derivations, stronger and fairer empirical validation, complete related work, and careful writing could be competitive. Confidence: high.
