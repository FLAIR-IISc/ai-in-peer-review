- Proposes Smooth-SODL: online dictionary learning with sparse atoms and Laplacian quadratic penalty for spatial smoothness in large-scale neuroimaging.
- Extends Mairal et al.’s online DL by adding atom sparsity constraints (L1-ball or simplex) and a Laplacian smoothness penalty, with ridge codes and BCD atom updates via projected FISTA.
- Empirically evaluated on HCP task fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) against SODL and CanICA, showing more localized/denoised atoms, higher stability, better small-n generalization, modest EV vs ICA, competitive behavioral prediction, and ≈3× runtime over SODL.
- Connects to prior work (online DL, large-scale factorization, structured sparsity/SPCA, GraphNet, TV-regularized DL); novelty is practical integration of Laplacian smoothing into online DL with minimal overhead.
- Algorithmic soundness: BCD with ridge codes and projected FISTA for Laplacian-regularized atom updates is reasonable and correctly identified as a denoising+projection step.
- Convergence claims problematic: time-varying αt and especially γt change the objective over time, so existing online DL convergence theory does not directly apply.
- FISTA accuracy claim (1–2 iterations to 1e−6) is implausible; should be tempered and supported with reported inner-iteration counts and tolerances.
- Lipschitz bound L = 1 + 4Dγ̃ assumes a Laplacian spectral scaling that must be stated (boundary conditions, grid spacing); FFT option mentioned but not evaluated.
- Experiments are large-scale and compelling, but several crucial details are missing for reproducibility and fairness.
- Missing details for stability metric: matching procedure (e.g., Hungarian), number of splits, and error bars are not reported.
- Prediction setup unclear: model class and regularization (e.g., ridge), nested CV, exact features used (codes vs voxels), and leakage precautions for RAW variable selection are unspecified.
- Hyperparameter selection is under-specified: how τ and γ were chosen, whether positivity/simplex was used for all models, and sensitivity to k are not described.
- Preprocessing fairness issue: CanICA was run on smoothed data (FWHM 6mm) while others were not, which could bias comparisons; matched preprocessing should be justified or included.
- Results show moderate EV and R2 differences; stability improvements are meaningful but lack statistical uncertainty (bootstraps/CIs).
- Important baselines missing: TV-ℓ1 DL (e.g., Abraham) and GraphNet-style factorizations are not compared, leaving open whether Laplacian is optimal.
- Clarity is generally good with useful algorithm boxes, but notational glitches, minor typos, and unclear derivations (e.g., scaled γ̃) need fixing.
- Motivation for nonnegativity/simplex constraint is absent; Z-maps can be signed, so simplex vs L1-ball should be justified or compared.
- Originality is incremental: integrates known Laplacian/GraphNet priors into online DL with engineering benefits rather than a new theoretical advance.
- Significance for neuroimaging practice is high: scalable method yielding reproducible, interpretable ROIs on large cohorts.
- Pros: timely large-scale focus, simple implementable extension, improved interpretability/stability, reasonable runtime overhead, public code intent.
- Cons: unsupported convergence claims with time-varying regularization, unrealistic FISTA accuracy claim, missing experimental details and uncertainty estimates, fairness concerns, limited baseline coverage, modest predictive gains.
- Suggested improvements: provide full derivation of atom update and γ̃ scaling, state Laplacian spectral assumptions, fix or justify time-varying regularization for convergence, temper FISTA claims and report inner-loop settings.
- Suggested experiments: report matching procedure and splits for stability, full predictive modeling details with nested CV and error bars, hyperparameter search details, ablations (simplex vs L1, effect of k, sensitivity to γ, τ), preprocessing-matched comparisons, and add TV-ℓ1/GraphNet baselines on subsets.
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept—practical and useful contribution but requires stronger theoretical justification for time-varying regularization and improved experimental rigor to be a solid NIPS contribution.
