- Proposal: Smooth-SODL adds a Laplacian spatial smoothness penalty to online sparse dictionary learning to define ROIs from inter-subject variability in neuroimaging maps.
- Evaluation: Tested on 500 HCP subjects; yields more interpretable, spatially localized atoms, better behavior prediction, and advantages in small-data regimes versus SODL and thresholded CanICA.
- Computational cost: Spatial regularization incurs only a 2–3× overhead compared to standard SODL, making it practical for large datasets.
- Technical soundness: Model formulation, optimization, and convergence guarantees are rigorous and well-grounded in the literature.
- Comprehensive experiments: Compared across visual quality, explained variance, stability, reproducibility, and prediction performance with compelling learning-curve results.
- Practical significance: Addresses ROI definition problem with clear empirical benefits and planned integration into Nilearn for community use.
- Limitations discussed: Authors acknowledge hyperparameter tuning challenges and trade-offs between sparsity, interpretability, and explained variance.
- Limited method comparisons: Paper does not fully contrast Smooth-SODL with recent structured dictionary learning methods (e.g., TV-ℓ1 approaches).
- Hyperparameter guidance lacking: Quantitative sensitivity analysis and concrete selection guidance for γ and τ are insufficient.
- Spatial-structure evidence limited: No quantitative metrics (e.g., blobiness or compactness) provided to support claims about spatial localization.
- Missing comparisons: Does not compare against other state-of-the-art structured methods like structured sparse PCA or multi-subject dictionary learning.
- Reproducibility concern: Implementation is promised in Nilearn but no code link or DOI is provided yet.
- Overall assessment: Strong, well-written, and technically rigorous paper making a meaningful contribution to neuroimaging analysis.
- Recommendation: Accept (NIPS) — benefits and evidence outweigh the noted weaknesses.
- Suggestion 1: Expand related work to compare explicitly with recent structured dictionary learning and spatial-constraint methods.
- Suggestion 2: Add quantitative spatial-structure metrics (e.g., spatial compactness or blobiness) for learned atoms.
- Suggestion 3: Provide concrete, data-driven recommended ranges or procedures for selecting γ and τ.
- Suggestion 4: Include a direct comparison with TV-ℓ1 methods to justify the Laplacian choice.
- Suggestion 5: Add a code availability statement and link to the Nilearn repository in the final paper.
- Pro-accept: Addresses an important problem with a principled, computationally efficient method that outperforms baselines and will be accessible via Nilearn.
- Con-accept: Needs more comparisons to recent structured methods, quantitative hyperparameter/sparsity analyses, stronger spatial-structure evidence, and confirmed code availability.
