- Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online sparse dictionary learning extension that adds a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness for learning brain regions (ROIs).  
- Addresses need for principled ROIs that capture inter-subject variability instead of relying on anatomical or predefined regions.  
- Maintains computational efficiency of online dictionary learning while incorporating spatial structure.  
- Evaluated on HCP task fMRI showing improved interpretability, stability, and predictive power versus SODL and CanICA.  
- Mathematical formulation is well-motivated, with clear justification for sparsity and Laplacian penalties.  
- Algorithms are detailed (Algorithms 1 and 2) and reproducible; code integrated with Nilearn.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using explained variance, stability, prediction accuracy, and qualitative visualizations.  
- Authors acknowledge hyperparameter sensitivity and the trade-off between spatial regularization and data fidelity.  
- Runtime overhead modest: reported ~110s vs ~340s for same batch size, supporting the 2–3x slowdown claim.  
- Novel contribution: first adaptation of structured dictionary learning to online setting with spatial constraints at low computational cost.  
- Clearly differentiated from prior work that used slow alternating optimization; builds on efficient online framework.  
- Significant impact potential: principled ROI definition that better captures individual differences and is practical for large datasets.  
- Empirical advantages highlighted: higher stability (0.4 vs 0.1 for CanICA) and large improvements in explained variance (14× with limited data).  
- Strengths: strong motivation, solid extension of existing methods, thorough experiments, empirical efficiency, clear related-work placement, and practical implementation.  
- Weaknesses: limited discussion of generalization to other imaging modalities, sparse hyperparameter selection details, no theoretical guarantees on interpretability, and limited discussion of failure modes for certain brain data.  
- Overall recommendation: strong, technically sound, well-written paper with significant contribution; recommend acceptance to NIPS.
