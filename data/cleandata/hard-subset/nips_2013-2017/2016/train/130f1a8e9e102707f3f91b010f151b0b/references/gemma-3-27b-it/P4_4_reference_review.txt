This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), for learning brain regions from population-level neuroimaging data. The core idea is to leverage inter-subject variability to define spatially coherent regions, addressing the limitations of traditional univariate models and ROI-based approaches. The method extends existing online dictionary learning techniques by incorporating Laplacian regularization to encourage spatially smooth and compact dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a large HCP dataset, showcasing improvements in dictionary interpretability, explained variance, and predictive power compared to standard SODL, CanICA, and PCA.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the challenges of ROI definition in neuroimaging and the limitations of existing methods. The proposed approach is a logical extension of online dictionary learning, building upon prior work [16] and addressing the need for spatially structured decompositions. The mathematical formulation is relatively clear and concise, and the algorithmic details are well-explained. The experimental validation on the HCP dataset is thorough, including comparisons with established methods and an exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity.  The inclusion of practical considerations regarding hyperparameter tuning and initialization strengthens the paper.  The connection to related work is comprehensive, referencing relevant literature in both machine learning and neuroimaging.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization for spatially structured decomposition is a novel contribution. While online dictionary learning exists [16], incorporating spatial structure in this online setting is a significant addition.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is well-defined, and the algorithmic description is clear. The BCD approach is justified and appears appropriate for solving the optimization problem. The convergence guarantees, referencing existing work [16], add to the technical credibility.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation, methodology, and results are presented in a logical and organized manner.
* **Significance:** The problem addressed is important in neuroimaging.  The potential to automatically discover meaningful brain regions from population data is significant and could impact downstream analyses. The demonstrated improvements over existing methods suggest a practical impact.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset are well-designed and provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The comprehensive evaluation, including quantitative metrics and qualitative assessments, is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge an increase in running time compared to vanilla SODL (3x slower), the overall computational efficiency relative to existing structured decomposition methods (e.g., [1, 25]) is not comprehensively discussed. A more detailed comparison of runtime performance would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The hyperparameter tuning section provides useful observations but lacks a rigorous methodology. The "safe range" for (γ, τ) seems somewhat subjective.  A more systematic exploration of the hyperparameter space, perhaps using cross-validation as mentioned, would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While CanICA is included, the comparison to other state-of-the-art methods specifically designed for spatially structured decompositions (beyond those cited in related work) could be expanded. For example, methods utilizing spectral clustering or graph-based approaches could be considered.
* **Overstated Claims:** Some statements, such as claiming the method will “become the de facto way” of doing dimensionality reduction, feel overly ambitious.  A more cautious tone would be appropriate.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a significant problem in neuroimaging.
* The experimental results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results.
* The code will be made publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The computational cost, while reasonable, is higher than simpler methods.
* The hyperparameter tuning process could be more rigorous.
* The comparison to other state-of-the-art methods could be more comprehensive.
* Some of the claims regarding future impact are overstated.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated. The potential impact on neuroimaging research is significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised regarding computational cost, hyperparameter sensitivity, and more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
