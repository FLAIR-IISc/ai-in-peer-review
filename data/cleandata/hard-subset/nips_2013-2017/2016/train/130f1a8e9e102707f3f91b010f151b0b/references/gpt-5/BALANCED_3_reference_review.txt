Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging that encourages both sparsity and spatial smoothness of dictionary atoms. The method extends Mairal’s online sparse dictionary learning (SODL) by adding a Laplacian penalty on the atoms while maintaining online block-coordinate updates: codes are obtained by ridge regression; atoms are updated sequentially by solving a convex denoising subproblem with Laplacian regularization under simplex/ℓ1 constraints, solved with a few FISTA iterations. The authors argue the added cost is small (≈2–3× SODL). Experiments on HCP task-fMRI (n=500, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA/PCA on explained variance, reproducibility/stability, qualitative interpretability, runtime, and behavioral prediction.

This sits at the intersection of structured sparse matrix factorization and neuroimaging decompositions. It relates to: online DL (Mairal 2010, Mensch 2016), structured sparsity/SPCA (Jenatton et al., AISTATS 2010), GraphNet/TV regularization for neuroimaging (Grosenick et al., 2013; Abraham MICCAI; Varoquaux et al., IPMI/NeuroImage) and multi-subject ICA (CanICA, FSL MELODIC). Prior structured decompositions often relied on slow alternating minimization; the contribution here is an online, scalable variant incorporating Laplacian structure on atoms.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Data-driven ROIs that capture inter-subject variability are important in neuroimaging; online methods are needed at HCP scale.
- Methodic simplicity and scalability: The per-atom Laplacian-regularized update reduces to a convex denoising problem with standard first-order solvers; code updates via ridge regression are stable; the algorithm retains the online SODL flavor.
- Empirical benefits: Compared with SODL, Smooth-SODL yields more spatially coherent, blob-like atoms and higher split-half stability; runtime remains practical (≈5.6 min vs 1.7 min for SODL and 8.8 min for CanICA on the reported setup).
- Sensible diagnostics: The paper explores hyperparameter effects and learning curves; reports explained variance (EV), stability, and behavioral prediction performance.

Weaknesses and detailed assessment
Quality
- Theory: Convergence is largely inherited from SODL, but the added Laplacian and time-varying parameters introduce nuances. The suggestion that 1–2 FISTA iterations achieve 1e-6 accuracy seems unrealistic; more careful stopping and error propagation analysis is needed. The gamma schedule γt = O(‖At‖∞,∞/t) is suggested to avoid vanishing step sizes, but experiments use fixed γ (10^3–10^4). Clarify consistency between theory and practice and provide guarantees (e.g., monotone decrease of surrogate, bounded updates) under fixed γ.
- Modeling choices: Codes use ℓ2 (ridge) rather than ℓ1; sparsity is enforced only on atoms via constraint C. This deviates from standard sparse coding and may limit identifiability. An ablation comparing ℓ1 vs ℓ2 codes would help.
- Evaluation breadth: Comparisons omit strong structured baselines that explicitly impose spatial regularization (e.g., TV-ℓ1/GraphNet dictionary learning, structured SPCA). Without these, it is hard to attribute gains to the Laplacian vs general smoothing.
- Metrics: Interpretability is mostly qualitative; stability is reported as mean atom correlation but details (matching procedure, split protocol, confidence intervals) are sparse. Behavioral prediction shows CanICA often wins on test R2, weakening the claim that Smooth-SODL best captures inter-subject variability for downstream tasks.

Clarity
- Several typos and notation glitches (e.g., Eq. 7 formatting, At[j,j]/t vs its inverse inside Fγ). Please fix for reproducibility.
- Boundary conditions and implementation details for ∆ (voxel spacing, masks, handling of edges) are not specified.
- The choice of positivity/simplex constraint Sp(τ) is not justified for signed fMRI maps; does this bias atoms toward positive-only blobs? Consider allowing signed atoms or discuss preprocessing.
- Hyperparameter selection: “safe ranges” are mentioned, but a clear, reproducible selection protocol (e.g., cross-validated EV vs stability trade-off) would be valuable.

Originality
- The core idea—adding a Laplacian penalty on atoms within online DL—is incremental but useful. Prior works have used TV/GraphNet or structured sparsity for brain maps, but often in batch/offline settings. The online, scalable integration with per-atom proximal updates is the main novelty. A more direct empirical comparison to those structured methods would better position the contribution.

Significance
- The method appears practically useful for producing cleaner, more reproducible components than SODL at modest extra cost. However, lack of comparison to stronger structured baselines and mixed predictive results temper claims of advancing the state of the art. Release of code in Nilearn would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, large-scale setting with a simple, scalable extension to a widely used online DL framework.
- Demonstrates improved spatial coherence and stability over SODL with moderate computational overhead.
- Generally well-motivated and relevant to NeurIPS communities working on representation learning and neuroimaging.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical grounding for the new regularizer’s effect on convergence and the proposed parameter schedules; some claims (FISTA iterations/accuracy) are implausible as stated.
- Missing comparisons to structured baselines (TV/GraphNet DL, structured SPCA) and limited quantitative interpretability metrics.
- Mixed downstream predictive performance; CanICA often performs best on behavior prediction.
- Some clarity and reproducibility gaps (implementation specifics, positivity constraint rationale).

Questions for the authors
1) Did you test ℓ1 codes, and how do results change vs ridge codes? Any identifiability issues with sparse atoms but dense codes?
2) What boundary conditions and voxel spacing were used for ∆? How is the brain mask handled at edges?
3) How are atoms matched across splits for stability (Hungarian on correlations)? Provide statistics (mean±sd) over multiple runs.
4) Can you report performance vs a TV-regularized DL baseline or smoothed-input SODL to disentangle the benefit of explicit Laplacian regularization?
5) Why enforce positivity (simplex) on atoms for signed activation maps?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to TV/GraphNet-based DL or structured SPCA; include a baseline that smooths inputs before SODL.
- Provide quantitative interpretability metrics (e.g., spatial compactness, smoothness, Dice overlap with known atlases) and stability measures with error bars.
- Clarify and temper theoretical claims: provide practical stopping criteria for the FISTA subroutine; analyze the effect of fixed γ; report actual iteration counts and subproblem residuals.
- Explore signed atoms (ℓ1-ball without positivity) and ℓ1-coded variants; provide an ablation.
- Evaluate robustness across k, tasks (resting-state), and modalities; include sensitivity analyses for γ, τ with a principled selection protocol (e.g., stability selection).
- Improve clarity/notation and release code with exact parameter settings.

Recommendation: Weak reject
While promising and practical, the contribution is incremental and the evaluation misses critical structured baselines and quantitative interpretability evidence. With strengthened comparisons, clearer theory/practice alignment, and improved clarity, this could become a solid contribution. Confidence: medium.
