## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth-SODL, for learning spatially coherent brain regions directly from population-level neuroimaging data. The core idea is to leverage the variability *across* subjects to define meaningful regions, rather than relying on predefined ROIs or purely data-driven clustering.  Smooth-SODL extends existing online dictionary learning (SODL) by adding a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially smooth and compact dictionary atoms (representing brain regions), while maintaining sparsity.  The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach on a large-scale fMRI dataset (HCP), comparing it to ICA-based methods (CanICA) and standard SODL, evaluating performance across metrics like explained variance, stability, and predictive power on behavioral tasks.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The problem addressed—principled ROI definition without relying on prior knowledge—is important and well-motivated. The approach of using cross-subject variability to inform ROI construction is a compelling idea.
* **Technical Soundness:**  The proposed Smooth-SODL model is theoretically grounded, building on established online dictionary learning and incorporating a Laplacian penalty for spatial smoothness.  The authors clearly outline the optimization procedure (Block Coordinate Descent) and discuss convergence guarantees.  The inclusion of a data-adaptive learning rate for the regularization parameter (alpha) is a thoughtful addition.
* **Scalability:** The online learning framework is crucial for handling the large-scale nature of neuroimaging data (high dimensionality and many subjects). The authors demonstrate reasonable computational performance, indicating scalability.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset are comprehensive. The comparison against CanICA and SODL provides valuable insights, and the evaluation across multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, behavioral prediction) strengthens the conclusions.  The learning curves are particularly insightful, demonstrating the benefits of the spatial regularization, especially with limited data.
* **Clarity & Completeness:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical formulation and algorithmic details. The authors acknowledge potential limitations (e.g., hyperparameter tuning) and discuss related work.  The mention of open-sourcing the implementation is a positive step.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors discuss hyperparameter selection, the process feels somewhat ad-hoc. The optimal range for γ and τ seems to require experimentation, and a more systematic approach to hyperparameter optimization (beyond k-fold cross-validation) could be beneficial.  The reliance on the α parameter time-varying update could be clarified.
* **CanICA Implementation:** The described implementation of CanICA (thresholding the results) feels somewhat unfair. While standard practice, it’s arguably addressing a weakness *of* CanICA rather than a direct comparison of the core methods.  It would be more informative to see results with unthresholded CanICA components.
* **Limited Exploration of Laplacian Parameter (γ):**  The sensitivity analysis focuses on both τ and γ together; a more focused investigation of γ’s effect *alone* would be useful.  The theoretical justification for the γ scaling rule (equation 9) is not fully elaborated.
* **Behavioral Prediction Results:** The behavioral prediction results, while promising, could be presented more thoroughly. It would be useful to see the statistical significance of the R<sup>2</sup> scores and to compare performance against simpler baseline models (e.g., directly using the raw fMRI data).



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical formulation is rigorous, and the experimental results provide evidence to support the claims. However, the hyperparameter sensitivity and limited exploration of specific parameters (γ) weaken this aspect.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clearly written, but the discussion of the hyperparameter tuning process could be more detailed. The explanation of equation (9) requires more elaboration.
* **Originality:** The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for spatial structure is novel in the context of neuroimaging. While online dictionary learning exists, its application to ROI definition with this specific regularization is a valuable contribution.
* **Significance:** The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of neuroimaging.  Principled ROI definition is a longstanding challenge, and this method offers a data-driven approach that could improve the reliability and interpretability of neuroimaging studies.  The method’s scalability is particularly important for large-scale datasets.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Addresses an important and challenging problem in neuroimaging.
* Proposes a novel and technically sound method.
* Demonstrates promising results on a large-scale dataset.
* Provides a practical and scalable solution.

**Against:**

* Hyperparameter sensitivity requires careful tuning.
* The comparison with CanICA could be improved.
* Further investigation into the theoretical underpinnings of the γ scaling rule is needed.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of neuroimaging.  The novelty of the approach, the technical soundness of the method, and the compelling experimental results outweigh the identified weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the hyperparameter tuning discussion, providing a more detailed explanation of the γ scaling rule, and potentially including results with unthresholded CanICA components.  The authors should also consider including more statistical details regarding the behavioral prediction results.
