- Proposes Smooth-SODL: online structured sparse dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty to learn spatially localized, sparse brain regions from population fMRI.
- Aims to define ROIs data-drivenly without prior knowledge or cross-modal registration.
- Novelty lies in integrating Laplacian spatial regularization into an online dictionary learning framework.
- Method is theoretically justified and algorithmically described with reproducible equations and algorithms.
- Scalable to large datasets via online learning, demonstrated on a large HCP dataset.
- Comprehensive evaluation comparing Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA on runtime, interpretability, stability, explained variance, and behavioral prediction.
- Strength: addresses an important neuroimaging problem and offers a principled, interpretable alternative for ROI definition.
- Strength: balances sparsity and spatial coherence, showing empirical improvements over SODL.
- Strength: clear presentation, practical implementation details, and discussion of hyperparameter tuning and initialization.
- Weakness: improvements over SODL are incremental and CanICA shows superior explained variance.
- Weakness: method is sensitive to hyperparameters (α, γ, τ), requiring careful tuning and cross-validation.
- Weakness: higher computational cost than SODL, making it slower and more resource-intensive.
- Weakness: Laplacian spatial regularization is not novel per se; novelty is mainly in integration with online SODL.
- Weakness: qualitative “blob” components need more quantitative alignment with anatomical atlases or known networks.
- Qualitatively sound: derivations and experiments are correct and well-designed, though some sections (e.g., convergence) could be simplified.
- Originality: combines established techniques in a novel application; contribution is integration rather than new primitives.
- Significance: practically valuable and potentially impactful if adopted, though not a paradigm shift.
- Arguments for acceptance: solves a relevant problem, shows empirical gains over SODL, is scalable, and is well-written.
- Arguments against acceptance: gains are modest versus CanICA, requires expensive hyperparameter tuning, and is computationally heavier.
- Overall assessment: solid contribution; strengths outweigh weaknesses but not revolutionary.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, asking for quantitative alignment of blobs with atlases, methods to automate/simplify hyperparameter selection, and clearer discussion of computational cost vs. performance trade-offs.
