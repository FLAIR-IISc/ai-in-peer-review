### Summary

This paper introduces a large-scale method for learning brain regions, or parcels, from a population of neuroimaging scans. The central idea is to model inter-subject variability by decomposing a collection of subject-level statistical maps into a shared dictionary of spatial components (atoms) and subject-specific loading coefficients (codes). The authors formulate this as a dictionary learning problem. The key contribution is the development of a "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL) algorithm. This method extends the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. [16] by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms, in addition to a sparsity-inducing constraint (an `1`-ball). The Laplacian penalty encourages the learned atoms to be spatially smooth and form contiguous, blob-like regions, which is a desirable prior for functional brain parcels. The online nature of the algorithm makes it scalable to massive datasets (large numbers of subjects and/or high-resolution images), a significant advantage over previous batch methods for structured matrix factorization. The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation on a large fMRI dataset (HCP), comparing their method to standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a widely-used group ICA method (CanICA), demonstrating improvements in component interpretability, stability, and predictive power, especially in lower data regimes.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed model is a well-motivated extension of an established machine learning framework.

*   **Strengths:**
    1.  The algorithmic derivation is clear. The paper correctly identifies the dictionary update subproblem as a compactly-constrained quadratic minimization and discusses efficient first-order solvers (FISTA) and its computational complexity. The analysis showing that the proposed modification adds only a small, constant-factor overhead to the original online algorithm is convincing and important for scalability.
    2.  The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors compare against appropriate and strong baselines (SODL, CanICA). The use of multiple evaluation criteria—qualitative visual inspection, explained variance on hold-out data, component stability across data splits, and performance on a downstream prediction task (behavioral scores)—provides a multi-faceted and robust assessment of the method's value.
    3.  The learning curve analysis in Table 1 is particularly effective. It provides strong evidence for the central claim that spatial regularization is most beneficial when data is limited, demonstrating a 14-fold improvement in explained variance over SODL in a low-data setting. This is a crucial result that highlights the practical utility of the proposed regularizer.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    1.  The paper acknowledges the difficulty of hyperparameter tuning but the proposed solutions are largely empirical. The suggestion to use cross-validation is standard, but the time-varying schedule for `α` (Eq. 10) is presented without strong theoretical or empirical justification. A more detailed sensitivity analysis or a more principled selection strategy would strengthen the work's practical applicability.
    2.  The discussion on the initialization of the dictionary is brief. While the authors state that random initializations produce sensible results, given the non-convex nature of the overall problem, a more thorough investigation into the stability of the final solution with respect to initialization would be beneficial.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow.

*   **Strengths:**
    1.  The problem statement is clearly articulated in the introduction, effectively motivating the need for a scalable, structured decomposition method.
    2.  The model (Eq. 2) is presented with a clear breakdown of each term's function (data fidelity, code penalty, sparsity constraint, and smoothness penalty).
    3.  The algorithms are presented in a clean, high-level format, with sufficient detail to understand the workflow. The figures and tables are informative and support the main claims of the paper effectively. Figure 2, in particular, provides a compelling summary of the comparative results.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    1.  The derivation of the dictionary update (Eq. 7) is quite condensed and heavily relies on the reader's familiarity with [16]. While this is understandable due to space constraints, a slightly more self-contained explanation in the main text or appendix would improve readability for a broader audience.

**Originality:** The work represents a novel and useful combination of existing techniques applied to a challenging problem.

*   **Strengths:**
    1.  While online dictionary learning and Laplacian regularization are not new concepts individually, their combination into a single, scalable algorithm for neuroimaging analysis is novel. This work successfully bridges the gap between computationally expensive batch methods for structured decomposition [1, 25] and efficient but unstructured online methods [16, 17].
    2.  Applying this specific formulation to learn population-level brain parcels that capture inter-subject variability is a valuable and original contribution to the computational neuroimaging field.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    1.  The core algorithmic machinery is an extension of [16], and the spatial prior is a standard one. The novelty is therefore more in the synthesis and application rather than in fundamental algorithmic or theoretical breakthroughs. This is a minor point, as the synthesis is non-trivial and impactful.

**Significance:** The results presented are important and the proposed method is likely to be highly useful for researchers in neuroimaging and other fields dealing with large-scale spatial data.

*   **Strengths:**
    1.  The development of a fast, scalable algorithm for learning structured components from massive datasets addresses a critical and growing need in neuroscience, exemplified by large-scale projects like the Human Connectome Project. The demonstrated runtime efficiency makes sophisticated, data-driven parcellation feasible on standard hardware.
    2.  The method produces more interpretable and stable results than its unstructured counterpart, which is crucial for scientific discovery. The improved performance in predicting behavioral variables suggests that the learned components capture biologically meaningful variability.
    3.  The planned inclusion of the implementation in a widely-used open-source library (Nilearn) will significantly increase the work's impact and facilitate its adoption by the research community.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    1.  The paper presents a novel, scalable, and technically sound algorithm for a significant real-world problem.
    2.  The experimental validation is thorough, using a large, modern dataset and multiple relevant metrics to convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages over existing approaches.
    3.  The contribution is of high interest to both the machine learning community (scalable structured matrix factorization) and the applied science community (computational neuroscience).
    4.  The paper is clearly written and the results are presented transparently.

*   **Arguments against Acceptance:**
    1.  The contribution could be viewed as an incremental combination of known techniques (online dictionary learning + Laplacian regularization).
    2.  The practical utility is somewhat dependent on hyperparameter tuning, for which the paper offers limited principled guidance.

**Conclusion:** The arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. The paper makes a solid and significant contribution by developing a scalable and effective method for structured data decomposition, backed by a strong empirical study. It addresses a clear need in a high-impact domain and is likely to be influential.
