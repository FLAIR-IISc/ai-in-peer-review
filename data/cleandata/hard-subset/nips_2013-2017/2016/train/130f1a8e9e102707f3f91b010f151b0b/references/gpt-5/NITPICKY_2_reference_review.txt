Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method that augments the standard sparse online dictionary learning framework (Mairal et al.) with a spatial Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms to encourage spatially smooth, blob-like, and sparse components. Codes are estimated via ridge regression (optionally ℓ1), atoms are constrained to an ℓ1-ball or simplex, and atom updates solve a convex “GraphNet-like” denoising subproblem per atom using FISTA. The authors argue that this yields interpretable regions of interest and improved stability with modest computational overhead. Experiments on HCP task fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA, reporting qualitative gains in interpretability, higher stability across splits, competitive explained variance (EV), and behavioral prediction performance.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on online dictionary learning [Mairal et al., JMLR 2010], and is closely related to structured sparsity and GraphNet-type penalties [Grosenick et al., NeuroImage; Jenatton et al., AISTATS] and TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning for fMRI [Abraham et al., MICCAI]. Online scalable solvers for dictionary learning with structure have also been explored (e.g., Mensch et al., ICML 2016). The novelty here is integrating a Laplacian regularizer into the online BCD update with an efficient per-atom FISTA subroutine, and demonstrating its utility at HCP scale. Conceptually, the contribution is incremental but practically relevant.

Strengths
- Well-motivated application: learning spatially compact, interpretable ROIs that capture inter-subject variability is important in neuroimaging.
- Algorithmic idea is simple, implementable, and scales; the per-atom composite optimization is standard and plausible.
- Empirical results suggest improved spatial coherence and higher across-split stability of atoms versus SODL, with reasonable runtime overhead.
- The paper attempts to study hyperparameter effects and discusses practical tuning.
- Comparison to both SODL and a PCA/ICA pipeline provides a useful spectrum (sparsity vs variance capture).

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical/mathematical
- Objective (Eq. 2) uses lim n→∞ (population risk) without specifying distributional assumptions; experiments optimize finite-sample surrogates. Please clarify the stochastic setting or remove the limit for clarity.
- Several inconsistencies/typos in definitions:
  - In the bullet list, “maxUi” should be “minUi”.
  - The Laplacian penalty is defined as sum of squared gradients equals 1/2 v^T Δ v. Typically, with Δ=∇^T∇, one has v^TΔv=||∇v||^2 (no 1/2). Define Δ and boundary conditions precisely; reconcile constants.
  - The text suggests “1/p sum” in ΩLap, then equates to 1/2 v^TΔv; normalization is unclear.
- The Lipschitz constant is stated as L=1+4D γ̃. This depends on the spectral norm of Δ, which is grid- and boundary-condition dependent (e.g., ≤4D for a standard discretization). Please specify the discretization and justify the bound rigorously.
- Claim: “1 or 2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient to reach an accuracy of 1e−6.” This is not credible for general convex problems with nontrivial Δ; either report actual iteration counts and stopping criteria or revise.
- Convergence claims rely on [16], but you also (i) allow time-varying αt and suggest a time-varying γt (Eq. 9), and (ii) introduce an additional smooth term. Theoretical convergence under time-varying regularization is not discussed; the proof sketch is insufficient. In experiments γ appears fixed (1e3–1e4), which contradicts the derived γt schedule meant to prevent “blow-up.” Clarify and align theory/practice.
- “Parameter-free” BCD: the dictionary subproblem requires a step size 1/L for FISTA; although computable, this is still a parameter. The statement is misleading.
- Scaling/identifiability: with ridge codes and constrained atoms (ℓ1 ball/simplex), scaling is controlled, but this should be articulated (e.g., why choose simplex/positivity; effect on signs and interpretability).

Experimental/methodological
- Fairness of baselines:
  - CanICA is applied to smoothed data (FWHM 6mm) while SODL/Smooth-SODL are not. Smoothing changes EV and stability; justify this choice or harmonize preprocessing.
  - Thresholded CanICA (“tCanICA”) is a post hoc variant. Interpretability should be quantified consistently across methods, not by mixing raw vs thresholded versions.
- Hyperparameter selection:
  - You mention K-fold CV on EV for γ and τ, and αt∼t−1/2. The grid, folds, and whether tuning is nested w.r.t. test evaluation are not described. Report the tuning protocol and sensitivity (especially to k, τ, γ).
  - Only k=40 is shown. Stability and interpretability often vary with k; provide at least a brief sensitivity analysis.
- Stability metric: mean correlation across matched atoms is reported (Smooth-SODL 0.4 vs SODL 0.1). Please detail matching (Hungarian cost, sign handling, masking), whether correlations are computed on full maps or thresholded, and provide variance/CI across runs/splits.
- Explained variance: define precisely how EV is computed on held-out data given codes estimated by ridge. Also include PCA baseline (beyond a bar in Fig. 2) with identical preprocessing.
- Prediction experiments: using the “top 2000 voxels” RAW baseline risks selection bias if feature selection is not nested within CV. Clarify the prediction model, CV protocol, and regularization for all methods. Reporting only tasks with positive mean R^2 risks cherry-picking; include all targets or justify.
- Claims on “massive” scale: n=500, p=2.6e5 is indeed large p, moderate n; mini-batch η=20. Please report memory usage and per-epoch complexity; discuss scalability beyond one CPU/laptop.
- Missing or inconsistent content: Section numbering (”.2 Practical considerations”), references to Fig. 1 and Table 1 without visible content in the submission; numerous typos (“intepretable”, “intepretability”, “th Conference...”), notational slips (V j vs Vj), and grammar errors impede clarity.

Originality and significance
- The idea of imposing spatial smoothness (Laplacian/TV) on decomposition components is established; the main contribution is adapting it to the online SODL framework with a simple per-atom FISTA step. This is an incremental algorithmic contribution with practical utility.
- The empirical gains in stability/interpretability are promising, but the current presentation leaves too many methodological and theoretical gaps to clearly establish a robust advance.

Pros
- Addresses an important practical need (interpretable, stable brain regions).
- Simple, scalable modification of a widely used online DL framework.
- Empirical stability gains and qualitative improvements are plausible.
- Runtime overhead appears modest.

Cons
- Numerous technical inconsistencies (objective, Laplacian constants, FISTA claims, γ schedule vs practice) and unclear convergence under time-varying regularization.
- Methodological clarity concerns (preprocessing mismatch, hyperparameter tuning details, stability/EV definitions, prediction protocol).
- Limited novelty relative to existing structured sparse decomposition work.
- Many typos/formatting issues; figures/tables and key details missing or unclear.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper tackles a relevant problem with a potentially useful approach, but the current version has too many technical and presentation issues for NIPS. I encourage the authors to (i) clean up the math and convergence discussion with precise assumptions and consistent regularization, (ii) harmonize baselines and rigorously detail tuning and evaluation protocols, (iii) expand sensitivity analyses (k, τ, γ) and stability reporting, and (iv) significantly improve clarity and reproducibility (release code, include exact preprocessing and evaluation scripts). With these fixes, the contribution could be valuable to the neuroimaging community.
