## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth-SODL, for learning spatially coherent brain regions directly from population-level fMRI data. The core idea is to leverage inter-subject variability to define Regions of Interest (ROIs) in a data-driven manner, avoiding the limitations of traditional ROI definition methods (anatomical priors, activation-based selection). The method builds on online dictionary learning, adding Laplacian regularization to enforce spatial smoothness alongside sparsity. The authors demonstrate the method on a large HCP dataset, comparing it to ICA-based approaches (CanICA) and standard online dictionary learning (SODL), showing improvements in interpretability, stability, and predictive power.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself within the broader context of dimensionality reduction and ROI definition in neuroimaging. It builds upon existing work in online dictionary learning [16], sparse coding, and ICA/PCA for functional brain mapping [5].  It addresses a gap identified in the introduction – the need for methods that can effectively handle large-scale neuroimaging data while simultaneously capturing spatial structure. The authors correctly cite relevant work in clustering [21, 14] and spatially regularized decomposition methods [25, 1].  The connection to graph-based methods like GraphNet [11] is also appropriately highlighted.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization for spatially coherent ROI extraction is a novel contribution. It addresses a real challenge in neuroimaging – defining meaningful ROIs without strong a priori assumptions.
* **Scalability:** The online learning approach is well-suited for handling the large datasets common in modern neuroimaging, as evidenced by the application to the HCP dataset.
* **Interpretability:** The spatially smooth and sparse dictionary atoms are more interpretable than the dense components obtained from traditional ICA, allowing for a better understanding of underlying brain organization.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide a comprehensive empirical evaluation, comparing Smooth-SODL to several established methods (CanICA, SODL) across multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, prediction of behavioral variables). The results demonstrate consistent improvements.
* **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning and initialization provides valuable insights for practitioners.  The mention of implementation in Nilearn is a positive aspect.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the method scales well, the computational cost is still higher than the basic SODL algorithm (3x slower). While acceptable, this difference might be a limiting factor for certain applications.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The results suggest sensitivity to hyperparameters (α, γ, τ), requiring careful tuning. The authors provide guidance, but a more systematic investigation of the hyperparameter space would be beneficial.
* **CanICA Comparison:** The comparison to CanICA feels somewhat unfair, as the authors acknowledge the need for post-processing (thresholding) to make CanICA results interpretable. A fairer comparison might involve exploring different regularization techniques within CanICA itself.
* **Limited Behavioral Prediction:** While behavioral prediction is presented, the reported R-squared values are modest.  Further investigation into the relationship between the learned components and specific cognitive functions would strengthen the claims.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical analysis is limited. A more rigorous analysis of the convergence properties of the algorithm and the statistical properties of the learned components would be valuable.



**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging with a novel and technically sound approach.
* The method demonstrates clear improvements over existing techniques in terms of interpretability and stability.
* The empirical validation is thorough and provides strong evidence of the method's effectiveness.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Con:**

* The computational cost is higher than the baseline SODL method.
* Hyperparameter tuning can be challenging.
* The comparison to CanICA could be improved.
* The behavioral prediction results are modest.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed Smooth-SODL method provides a principled and scalable approach for learning spatially coherent brain regions from population-level data. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. The originality, scalability, interpretability, and empirical validation make this a strong candidate for acceptance.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

1.  Provide a more thorough discussion of the computational cost and potential optimization strategies.
2.  Consider a more comprehensive exploration of the hyperparameter space and a sensitivity analysis.
3.  Revisit the comparison to CanICA and explore alternative regularization techniques to make the comparison more equitable.
4.  Expand the analysis of behavioral prediction results and investigate the relationship between the learned components and specific cognitive functions.
