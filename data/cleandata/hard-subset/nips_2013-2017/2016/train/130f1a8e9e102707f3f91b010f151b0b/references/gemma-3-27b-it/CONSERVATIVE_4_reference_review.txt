This paper proposes a method, Smooth-SODL, for learning brain regions by combining online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization to encourage spatially smooth and sparse dictionary atoms. The motivation stems from the limitations of traditional ROI definition in neuroimaging and the desire to leverage inter-subject variability. While the problem addressed – principled ROI definition – is important, and the extension of online dictionary learning is a reasonable direction, I find the presentation lacking in crucial comparisons and rigor, leading me to a negative assessment.

The core idea isn't entirely novel; leveraging variability for region definition is common (as the authors acknowledge with citations to clustering approaches). The contribution lies in framing this as a structured sparse dictionary learning problem and proposing a specific online algorithm. However, the authors fail to convincingly demonstrate a substantial advantage over established methods, particularly given the added complexity. 

My primary concern is the limited and somewhat selective comparison. The inclusion of CanICA is helpful, but the authors spend considerable effort describing its limitations *after* presenting their results, framing the comparison in a way that favors their method. The claim that CanICA requires hard-thresholding “destroying the estimated model” feels like a post-hoc justification. More importantly, a direct comparison to *standard* (non-spatial) online dictionary learning (SODL) with careful hyperparameter tuning—beyond the quick experiment presented in Fig. 2b—is essential. The authors show a temporary advantage for Smooth-SODL early in learning, but claim asymptotic comparability. This needs to be demonstrated more definitively.  Did careful grid searches on α and τ for both SODL and Smooth-SODL confirm this asymptotic behavior? Simply stating it isn't sufficient. The comparison to RAW prediction performance, while interesting, feels tangential to the core contribution of ROI definition.

The theoretical justification is adequate but not particularly deep. The connection to GraphNets is mentioned, but the implications of using this specific approach (and its limitations) are not explored. The discussion of hyperparameter selection, while attempting to be practical, feels incomplete. The “safe range” for (γ, τ) is mentioned, but without a more systematic investigation, it’s hard to assess the robustness of the method. The statement about initialization being uncritical feels suspect; dictionary learning methods are often sensitive to initialization.

The paper lacks clarity in several places. The motivation for using a simplex constraint (Bp,`1(τ)) is not fully explained. Why not an `1-ball? The description of Algorithm 2 relies heavily on references to other papers and lacks sufficient detail for a reader unfamiliar with the prior work to fully understand the implementation. The explanation of the Lipschitz constant calculation feels tacked on and doesn't fully justify the claim that it’s computationally feasible.

Regarding originality, while the combination of Laplacian regularization and online dictionary learning is a reasonable extension, it's not a breakthrough. The core algorithmic components are well-established. The significance of the work is questionable. Does this method demonstrably improve brain region definition in a way that leads to more accurate or insightful neuroscientific findings? The evidence presented isn't compelling enough to suggest that it will.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses an important problem in neuroimaging.
* Presents a reasonable extension of online dictionary learning.
* Demonstrates some qualitative improvements in dictionary structure.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited and potentially biased comparison to existing methods.
* Lack of a thorough hyperparameter study.
* Insufficient clarity in the description of the algorithm.
* Questionable significance of the results.
* The advantage of the method over simpler baselines isn’t convincingly demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

The paper presents a technically sound method, but it lacks the rigor and thorough evaluation necessary for acceptance at NIPS. The authors need to provide a more comprehensive comparison to established methods, a more systematic hyperparameter study, and a clearer explanation of the algorithm. Without these improvements, the contribution of this work is incremental and does not justify publication in a top-tier conference like NIPS.
