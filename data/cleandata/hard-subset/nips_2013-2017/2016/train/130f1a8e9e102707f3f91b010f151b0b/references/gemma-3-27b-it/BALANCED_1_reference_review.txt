This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), for learning brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data. The core idea is to leverage inter-subject variability to define regions by learning a sparse dictionary with spatially smooth components. The authors extend existing online dictionary learning techniques by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce smoothness, and demonstrate its effectiveness on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the challenges of defining regions of interest (ROIs) in neuroimaging, particularly given the limitations of univariate approaches and the difficulties in cross-modality segmentation. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and situates the proposed method within the context of existing approaches like clustering and ICA/PCA. The authors do a good job of relating their work to prior research, referencing relevant papers in the fields of neuroimaging, sparse representation learning, and dictionary learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for spatial smoothness is a valuable contribution. This allows for scalable learning of spatially coherent brain regions, addressing a key limitation of existing methods.
*   **Scalability:** The use of an online learning approach is crucial for handling the massive datasets common in neuroimaging. The authors explicitly address this scalability and report reasonable running times.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The formulation of the problem as a penalized dictionary learning problem is well-defined, and the authors discuss the convergence properties of their algorithm.
*   **Empirical evaluation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset are comprehensive, comparing Smooth-SODL to several relevant baselines (CanICA, SODL) using a variety of metrics (explained variance, stability, prediction accuracy). The results demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method, particularly in terms of interpretability and capturing inter-subject variability.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning and initialization provides valuable insights for practitioners. The authors acknowledge the challenges of parameter selection and provide practical guidelines.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors discuss hyperparameter tuning, the impact of different hyperparameter settings isn't explored thoroughly. The sensitivity analysis (Fig. 1) seems somewhat limited in scope and lacks a rigorous quantitative evaluation of the parameter space. It isn't entirely clear how sensitive the results are to variations in α, γ, and τ.
*   **Laplacian Regularization Parameter (γ):** The choice of γ seems somewhat arbitrary. The explanation that γ should be inversely proportional to the time step (Eq. 9) is a bit hand-wavy and could benefit from more justification. Why this specific relationship holds and how it impacts the dictionary learning process requires further elaboration.
*   **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While the comparison with CanICA and SODL is valuable, a comparison with more recent advances in spatially regularized dictionary learning or other advanced neuroimaging analysis techniques (e.g., graph neural networks for brain network analysis) would strengthen the paper.
*   **Behavioral Prediction:** The behavioral prediction results are promising, but the gains over baseline methods are modest. A more in-depth analysis of the predictive power of the learned dictionaries, perhaps with different prediction algorithms, would be beneficial.  The R2 scores are relatively low, and it’s not clear how much improvement is attributable to the Smooth-SODL model versus the choice of features.
*   **Implementation Details:** Some implementation details are glossed over. For example, the specifics of the mini-batch size and the number of iterations for FISTA are mentioned but not fully justified.
* **Minor:** A few typos were noted (e.g. "smooth-SODLmodel (2)" instead of "Smooth-SODL model (2)")


**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for learning brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data.
*   The method is scalable and addresses a key limitation of existing approaches.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem, the proposed solution, and the experimental results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The hyperparameter tuning analysis is somewhat limited, and the choice of the Laplacian regularization parameter is not fully justified.
*   The comparison with state-of-the-art methods could be more comprehensive.
*   The behavioral prediction results are modest, and the contribution of the Smooth-SODL model to the improved performance is not fully established.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed Smooth-SODL method is novel, scalable, and demonstrates promising results on a large dataset. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I would recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly a more thorough investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity and a more comprehensive comparison with related work. The authors should also provide more justification for the choice of the Laplacian regularization parameter and elaborate on the interpretation of the behavioral prediction results.  Providing implementation details will also add reproducibility.
