This paper proposes a novel online dictionary learning method (Smooth-SODL) incorporating Laplacian regularization to encourage spatially smooth dictionary atoms for decomposing neuroimaging data. The core idea—using population variability to define brain regions—is conceptually related to clustering and ICA/PCA approaches common in fMRI analysis, but aims for a more principled ROI definition. The motivation, stemming from limitations of univariate analyses and manually defined ROIs, is well-articulated.

However, despite a clear presentation, the paper suffers from a common ailment of novel methodology: insufficient justification for the complexity added, and a lack of convincingly demonstrated advantage over established, simpler techniques. The authors acknowledge existing methods like CanICA and SODL, but the improvement showcased – particularly in prediction tasks – is not substantial enough to warrant a new algorithm with added hyperparameters and computational cost.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Motivation:** The problem of principled ROI definition is well-defined and relevant to the neuroimaging community.
* **Methodological Soundness:** The combination of sparsity and Laplacian regularization is theoretically reasonable for producing interpretable, spatially localized features. The online learning approach is well-suited for large datasets. The algorithmic details are presented with sufficient clarity.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The use of the large HCP dataset provides a strong foundation for evaluation. The comparison with CanICA and SODL is appropriate.
* **Detailed Hyperparameter Analysis:** The discussion of hyperparameter selection and the "optimality regime" is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Advance:** The gain over standard SODL, while present, is modest (3x running time increase for relatively small performance gains). The paper doesn't strongly justify the added complexity. The claim of “significant improvements” feels overstated.
* **Limited Superiority:** While Smooth-SODL performs well, it doesn’t consistently outperform all baselines across all metrics. CanICA, despite its limitations, demonstrates competitive performance, particularly in predictive power.
* **Overreliance on Visual Inspection:** Qualitative assessment of the dictionaries relies heavily on visual inspection, which is subjective. While the authors note reproducibility of broad structures, more quantitative evaluation of dictionary quality would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Spatial Regularization:**  The paper compares against SODL which lacks *any* spatial regularization. A comparison to, for example, a standard dictionary learning method with a simple total variation (TV) penalty would have better isolated the contribution of the proposed Laplacian regularization within the online learning framework.
* **Computational Cost:** The 3x slowdown compared to SODL isn't thoroughly investigated. The authors mention a theoretical limit on gamma, but don't discuss practical implications for scaling to even larger datasets.

**Specific Concerns:**

* The claim of “data-driven atlases” is strong. While the method produces spatially localized patterns, the degree to which these directly correspond to well-established anatomical or functional atlases isn’t convincingly demonstrated.
* The discussion of FISTA convergence feels somewhat hand-wavy (“1 or 2 iterations are sufficient”). A more rigorous analysis of convergence behavior, especially concerning the choice of gamma, would be desirable.
* The hyperparameter selection process, while detailed, appears somewhat empirical. A more systematic approach, perhaps involving cross-validation with more principled selection criteria, could strengthen the results.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The method is technically sound and offers a potentially useful extension to existing online dictionary learning techniques.
* The experiments are well-executed on a large, publicly available dataset.
* The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in neuroimaging.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The improvement over existing methods is not substantial enough to justify the added complexity of the proposed algorithm.
* The reliance on visual inspection and limited quantitative evaluation weakens the claims of superiority.
* The paper lacks a crucial comparison to simpler spatial regularization techniques.
* The novelty is incremental; it feels more like an engineering improvement to an existing algorithm than a fundamental breakthrough.




**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the work is solid and well-presented, it does not represent a significant advance over existing techniques. The added complexity of the proposed method does not translate into a compelling enough improvement in performance or interpretability to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The authors would significantly strengthen the paper by providing a more thorough comparison to simpler spatial regularization methods, offering a more rigorous analysis of the computational cost, and providing stronger quantitative evidence of the benefits of their approach. Currently, the paper feels like a “proof of concept” rather than a fully developed contribution.
