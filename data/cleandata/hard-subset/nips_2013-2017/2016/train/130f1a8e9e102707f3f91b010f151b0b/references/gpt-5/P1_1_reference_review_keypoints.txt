- Proposes Smooth-SODL: online sparse dictionary learning augmented with a Laplacian penalty to produce spatially smooth, sparse atoms using ridge coding and FISTA atom updates.  
- Aims to learn spatially localized, blob-like components for multi-subject fMRI; evaluated on HCP task maps vs SODL and CanICA.  
- Positions work at the intersection of online dictionary learning, spatial regularization (GraphNet/TV), and multi-subject decompositions, claiming efficient online scaling.  

Strengths
- Practical, scalable extension that integrates a Laplacian penalty into the online block-coordinate framework with modest (~2–3x) runtime overhead.  
- Well-motivated objective: spatially smooth, sparse, and often positive/simplex-constrained atoms suitable for interpretable ROIs.  
- Empirical evaluation on a large, relevant dataset (HCP) with multiple metrics: explained variance, atom stability, behavioral prediction, and qualitative interpretability.  
- Provides useful practical insights (benefit in low-data regimes, hyperparameter heuristics like mini-batch choices and αt ∝ t−1/2).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: adding a quadratic Laplacian to SODL is a straightforward variant closely related to GraphNet, TV-regularized dictionary learning, and structured PCA.  
- Missing important baselines: no comparisons to TV-l1 dictionary learning, structured sparse PCA, or spatially constrained ICA/NMF.  
- Some technical claims are imprecise or questionable (FISTA convergence claims, Eq. (9) Lipschitz discussion, and convergence guarantees wording).  

Technical issues to fix
- Claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e−6 accuracy is unrealistic; need to report actual stopping criteria and per-atom iteration counts.  
- Eq. (9) and the “Lipschitz blow-up” argument are unclear/incorrect — derive correctly and give practical γ scheduling guidance.  
- Convergence statement should be clarified: per-atom subproblems are solved to global optimum, overall algorithm converges to a stationary point, not a global optimum.

Experimental and reproducibility gaps
- Hyperparameter details missing: ranges and selection protocol for γ, τ, and positivity vs signed atoms; sensitivity to αt schedule unknown.  
- Stability metric lacks precise description (Hungarian matching details, sign handling, normalization, number of splits, confidence intervals).  
- Predictive analyses omit crucial details: prediction model, confound handling, cross-validation protocol, and hyperparameter tuning.  
- Runtime reporting incomplete: need number of passes T and total updates — reported wall-clock seems low for p ≈ 2.6×10^5 and k = 40.  

Clarity and presentation
- Paper readable but has typos, notation inconsistencies (∆ vs Laplacian), and references key methods/details to supplementary material not provided.  
- Some statements are labeled “parameter-free” while methods (FISTA) require step sizes; tighten derivations and fix grammar/notation.

Quality, originality, and significance
- Technically sound but incremental; optimization subproblems are standard convex problems but some theoretical statements need correction.  
- Moderate originality: Laplacian/GraphNet regularization is known; main novelty is integration into online SODL and empirical scale demonstration.  
- Moderate significance for neuroimaging practitioners (better interpretability/stability); broader ML impact limited without stronger baselines or theory.

Questions for authors (concise)
- Clarify Eq. (9): how does the Lipschitz constant depend on t, and do you decay γt in practice?  
- How many FISTA iterations per atom on average and what stopping rule is used?  
- What boundary conditions and brain-mask handling for ∆; is FFT used given irregular masks?  
- Provide hyperparameter grids and sensitivity analyses for τ and γ.  
- Exactly how is stability computed (splits, matching, sign resolution) and report confidence intervals.  
- What predictive model, CV protocol, and confound regression were used for behavioral prediction?

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method that improves interpretability and stability of learned brain regions with solid empirical evidence on a large public dataset.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty, missing comparisons to other structured approaches, some technical claims need correction, and reproducibility details are insufficient.

Recommendation
- Weak reject: promising and useful for neuroimaging but requires stronger baselines, clearer optimization/technical derivations (especially Eq. (9)), and tightened experimental protocols for acceptance.
