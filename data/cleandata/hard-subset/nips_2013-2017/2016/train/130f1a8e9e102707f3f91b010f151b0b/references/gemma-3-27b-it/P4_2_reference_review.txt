This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), for learning brain regions from fMRI data by leveraging inter-subject variability. The core idea is to decompose statistical parametric maps across a population of subjects into spatially smooth and sparse components, effectively defining regions of interest (ROIs). The method extends existing online dictionary learning techniques by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness alongside sparsity. The paper details the algorithm, provides theoretical justification, and demonstrates its performance on a large-scale HCP dataset, comparing it to ICA-based methods (CanICA) and standard SODL.

The paper builds upon prior work in online dictionary learning [16] and related techniques like ICA [5] and sparse coding [25, 1]. It addresses the limitations of existing ROI definition methods—segmentation and cross-modality correspondence—by learning ROIs directly from the data’s inherent structure. The authors correctly position their work within the context of clustering approaches [21, 14], highlighting the distinction between clustering as instance-of-cluster modeling versus their signal mixture approach. The extension of online dictionary learning with spatial regularization is a reasonable and potentially valuable contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization for spatially structured decomposition is a novel approach, particularly in the context of neuroimaging. While online dictionary learning is established [16], the spatial regularization aspect differentiates this work.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the problem (2) is clear and well-defined. The BCD algorithm is a standard approach for solving such problems, and the authors appropriately acknowledge and address the convergence concerns. The discussion of the Lipschitz constant and FISTA implementation demonstrates a strong understanding of the optimization challenges.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset are comprehensive. Comparing against CanICA and SODL provides a good benchmark. The inclusion of metrics beyond explained variance, such as reproducibility and prediction of behavioral variables, strengthens the evaluation.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the method is explained in detail. The inclusion of implementation details (Nilearn package) is a plus.
* **Addressing Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the computational cost increase compared to standard SODL and discuss the trade-offs involved in parameter selection.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The 3x increase in runtime compared to SODL, while acknowledged, is a significant drawback, especially given the already computational demands of fMRI analysis. More investigation into optimization strategies to mitigate this cost would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While a study of hyperparameter selection is provided, the optimal range for α and γ appears narrow. A more robust and automated hyperparameter tuning strategy might be valuable. The data-adaptive α selection (10) seems reasonable, but its justification could be strengthened.
* **CanICA Comparison:** The comparison to CanICA seems somewhat unfair. The authors pre-smooth the CanICA data, which could artificially limit its performance. While acknowledged, the reason for this smoothing isn't fully justified and impacts the interpretability of the comparison. It would be more informative to compare against CanICA with standard preprocessing.
* **Limited Scope of Behavioral Prediction:** The behavioral prediction results, while positive, are presented without a clear indication of statistical significance or effect size. A more rigorous statistical analysis would be necessary to draw strong conclusions.
* **Lack of Comparison to Alternatives:** While CanICA and SODL are appropriate baselines, comparing to other spatially regularized decomposition methods (e.g., those utilizing total variation regularization directly as in [3]) could further contextualize the contributions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to ROI definition in fMRI data.
* The empirical results demonstrate the benefits of spatial regularization in improving the interpretability and predictive power of learned brain regions.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the method and its limitations.
* The contribution addresses a relevant and challenging problem in neuroimaging.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The significant increase in computational cost compared to existing methods may limit the practical applicability of the approach.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity and lack of a robust tuning strategy could hinder reproducibility.
* The comparison to CanICA is potentially biased.
* The behavioral prediction results lack sufficient statistical rigor.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a solid contribution to the field of neuroimaging, presenting a novel and well-motivated method for learning brain regions. The strengths of the paper – novelty, technical soundness, and empirical validation – outweigh the weaknesses. While the computational cost and hyperparameter sensitivity are concerns, they do not invalidate the potential of the approach. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding the CanICA comparison and statistical rigor of the behavioral prediction analysis. The authors should also explore strategies for reducing the computational burden of the Laplacian regularization.
