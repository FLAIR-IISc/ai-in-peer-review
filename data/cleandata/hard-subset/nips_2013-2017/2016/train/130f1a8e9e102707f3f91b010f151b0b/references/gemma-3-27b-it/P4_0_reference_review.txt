The paper proposes a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), for learning brain regions from fMRI data by leveraging inter-subject variability. The core idea is to decompose statistical maps across a population into spatially localized and sparse components (dictionary atoms). This builds upon existing online dictionary learning techniques by adding a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness, creating “blobs” representing brain regions. The authors demonstrate improvements over traditional dictionary learning and ICA on a large HCP dataset, showing better performance in terms of interpretability, stability, and prediction of behavioral variables.

This work is closely related to a significant body of research in brain imaging and machine learning. Prior work often relies on segmentation or ROI-based analysis, which necessitates pre-defined regions or can suffer from noise. Clustering approaches have been used for spatial unit definition, but the proposed method offers a more signal-driven approach akin to PCA/ICA with sparsity and spatial regularization. The authors correctly position their work within this landscape, citing relevant papers on clustering [21, 14], ICA [5], sparse coding [25, 1], and online dictionary learning [16]. The extensions to online learning, especially to incorporate spatial structure, distinguish this work from other approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for spatially structured decomposition is a novel contribution. The method addresses a crucial problem in neuroimaging – principled ROI definition – and offers a promising alternative to traditional approaches.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed model is well-defined and the algorithmic details (BCD, FISTA) are clearly described. The theoretical justification for the convergence of the BCD scheme is mentioned. The discussion of hyperparameter selection and the convergence regime adds to the robustness of the method.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset provide substantial evidence for the effectiveness of Smooth-SODL. The comparisons with ICA and standard SODL demonstrate the benefits of spatial regularization in terms of interpretability, stability, and predictive performance. The learning curves and hyperparameter analysis are valuable.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the descriptions of the algorithm and experiments are easy to follow. The motivation for the work is well-explained in the introduction.
* **Scalability:** The online learning approach is well-suited for large-scale neuroimaging datasets, which is a significant advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge a 3x increase in running time compared to standard SODL, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and scalability limitations would be beneficial. How does this scale with increasing dataset size or image resolution?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning indicates sensitivity to parameters like γ and τ. While cross-validation is recommended, a more systematic investigation of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters could be helpful. The “safe range” mentioned in the paper could be more precisely defined.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons to ICA and SODL are provided, a comparison to more recent and advanced methods for spatially structured decomposition, beyond those cited in the related works section, could further strengthen the evaluation. The authors mention TV-l1 minimization [1], however, the context of its performance, and why their approach surpasses it, is not clear.
* **Lack of Visualization:** While the authors mention the visual inspection of dictionaries, providing more detailed visualizations of the learned atoms and their spatial distribution would enhance the understanding of the results. This is especially important given the emphasis on spatial structure.
* **Justification of Laplacian Penalty:** The authors state the Laplacian penalty enforces "blobs". A more detailed explanation of *why* this is a desirable property in the context of brain region definition, beyond simply matching expectations, could strengthen the argument.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for learning brain regions.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.
*   The method addresses an important problem in neuroimaging and has the potential to be widely adopted.
*   The online learning framework makes the method scalable to large datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational cost is higher than standard SODL.
*   Hyperparameter selection requires careful tuning.
*   The comparison to the state-of-the-art could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and well-executed contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed Smooth-SODL method offers a promising approach to learning brain regions from fMRI data, and the empirical results are convincing. While there are some minor weaknesses related to computational cost, hyperparameter sensitivity, and comparison to other methods, these do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised in the weaknesses section. Specifically, more detailed visualizations, a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods, and a more precise discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity would further strengthen the paper.
