Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning method for neuroimaging that enforces spatially sparse and smooth atoms by combining an ℓ1-type constraint on dictionary columns with a Laplacian penalty, while using ridge-penalized codes. The algorithm adapts Mairal et al.’s online dictionary learning framework to include a GraphNet-like denoising step for each atom via a few iterations of FISTA. The authors argue this yields spatially compact “blobs” that align better with functional neuroanatomy than unstructured dictionary learning, at a modest computational overhead. Experiments on HCP task fMRI maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA in terms of runtime, explained variance, stability/reproducibility, and behavioral prediction.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on online sparse coding/dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2009/2010), structured sparsity (e.g., Jenatton et al.), and GraphNet (Grosenick et al.), and is related to spatially structured decompositions in neuroimaging (e.g., TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning for brain atlases, Abraham et al.). It also connects to group ICA methods such as CanICA and MELODIC that lack explicit spatial regularization. The main claimed novelty is integrating a Laplacian smoothness penalty on atoms within an online, large-scale dictionary learning framework with practical convergence and speed.

Strengths
- Practical contribution: A simple, scalable way to enforce spatial smoothness in online dictionary learning, with a concrete implementation pathway (Nilearn).
- Computation: BCD with ridge-coded updates and per-atom FISTA yields only 2–3× slowdown vs. SODL, and faster than CanICA in the reported setup.
- Empirical scale and relevance: Uses HCP task fMRI and evaluates multiple criteria (qualitative maps, explained variance, stability across splits, behavioral prediction).
- Sensible qualitative outcomes: Atoms look cleaner and more interpretable than SODL, without the hard thresholding often used for ICA components.
- Stability vs. fidelity trade-off: Empirical evidence that Laplacian regularization improves stability at modest cost in explained variance.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty and positioning: Adding a Laplacian penalty to dictionary atoms is a natural, incremental extension of GraphNet/structured sparsity to the online setting. Prior work has enforced spatial structure via TV or graph penalties in neuroimaging decompositions. The paper would benefit from a stronger discussion of what is technically new beyond “we can do it online quickly,” and a more rigorous comparison to TV-ℓ1 DL and structured SPCA (both cited).
- Fairness of baselines: The SODL baseline in Mairal (2010) typically uses ℓ1 codes and ℓ2 constraints on atoms. Here, codes are ridge-penalized and atoms constrained to a simplex for both methods. It is unclear whether SODL was run in a configuration that matches its strengths (ℓ1 codes yield sparse representations per subject). The comparison to CanICA also involves smoothing and post-hoc thresholding choices that may bias results. Stronger baselines (e.g., TV-regularized DL, GraphNet-based factorization, structured SPCA) are missing.
- Methodological clarity:
  - The objective in Eq. (2) uses lim n→∞ of empirical losses; for a finite dataset, define the expected risk or clearly state the surrogate used online.
  - The update subproblem combines a convex quadratic with Laplacian plus a compact constraint C. The claim that “1–2 FISTA iterations suffice to reach 1e-6 accuracy” is implausible; if early stopping is used, the subproblem is not at a global optimum, undermining the guarantees cited for Alg. 2.
  - The Lipschitz constant derivation L = 1 + 4D γ̃ is fine for normalized grids, but depends on discretization; please specify boundary conditions and scaling of ∆.
  - The adaptive γ schedule (Eq. 9) to avoid small step sizes changes the objective over time. How does this interact with convergence guarantees of online DL? Is there a proof or at least a heuristic argument that the sequence of surrogate problems still converges to a stationary point of a fixed objective?
  - The choice of simplex Sp(τ) imposes positivity and an ℓ1-bound on atoms. This can help with “blobs,” but positivity and compactness are separate factors; quantify the role of positivity (e.g., ablation without nonnegativity).
- Evaluation gaps:
  - Spatial contiguity/compactness is asserted but not measured. Report metrics such as number/size of connected components per atom, spatial smoothness (e.g., total variation), or dispersion.
  - Hyperparameter selection: Details on cross-validation splits, ranges for γ and τ, and the αt schedule are sparse. Provide grids, selection criteria, and sensitivity analysis. Error bars and statistical tests are missing in the figures.
  - Behavioral prediction: Specify the predictive model (ridge/lasso/elastic net), cross-validation protocol, and whether voxel selection for RAW is performed within folds to avoid leakage. Reporting only targets with positive mean R2 can bias impressions; show full results or summarize distributions.
  - Generality: Only one task contrast and one k are shown. Include results for multiple tasks/modalities or vary k to assess robustness.
- Minor issues: Several typos and notational slips (e.g., “max” vs. “min” in Section 2; inconsistent use of limits; “intepretable”). Some claims (e.g., “guaranteed to converge to a global optimum” for the subproblem) should be qualified given inexact inner solves.

Questions for the authors
1. How were SODL’s codes penalized in your experiments? If ridge, why is this a fair instantiation of SODL; if ℓ1, please clarify and match settings across methods.
2. What is the exact FISTA stopping criterion per atom per iteration, and how many iterations are actually used? Do you warm-start from the previous Vj?
3. Please justify the adaptive γt and αt schedules theoretically or empirically; does changing γt affect the limiting objective?
4. Can you include a baseline with TV-regularized dictionary learning (e.g., Abraham et al.) and structured SPCA (Jenatton et al.)?
5. Provide quantitative spatial compactness/contiguity measures and an ablation on the positivity constraint.
6. Clarify behavioral prediction protocols (model, nested CV, leakage control) and add error bars.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and practical problem in large-scale neuroimaging with a simple, scalable method.
- Solid empirical evidence that spatial regularization improves interpretability and stability with modest computational cost.
- Likely to be adopted by practitioners, especially if released in Nilearn.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical novelty is modest; core idea is a straightforward integration of a standard smoothness penalty into an existing online DL framework.
- Baselines and evaluation are insufficient to establish clear state-of-the-art over structured competitors.
- Several methodological and clarity issues need tightening, including convergence claims and hyperparameter protocols.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is practically valuable and likely impactful for neuroimaging applications, but for NeurIPS standards it needs stronger baselines, clearer methodology and convergence discussion, and more rigorous, quantitative evaluation of spatial structure and predictive performance. I encourage the authors to revise along the lines above.
