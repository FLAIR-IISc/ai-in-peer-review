This paper presents a novel approach to defining brain regions by leveraging inter-subject variability through a structured sparse dictionary learning framework. The core idea – moving beyond treating inter-subject variability as noise and instead using it to *define* meaningful regions – is a strong one, and resonates with existing challenges in neuroimaging, particularly the principled definition of ROIs. The authors appropriately contextualize their work within the existing literature on clustering and dimensionality reduction techniques (PCA, ICA) commonly used in brain imaging. The proposed Smooth-SODL method builds upon existing online dictionary learning work and introduces a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially coherent structures, addressing a key limitation of purely data-driven approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its novel approach. While dictionary learning and sparse representations are well-established, combining them with a Laplacian penalty *within an online learning framework* to define brain regions based on population variability is a genuinely new contribution. This isn't simply an incremental improvement; it shifts the perspective on how to approach ROI definition.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The formulation of the problem as a penalized dictionary learning problem is mathematically sound, and the authors provide a clear description of the objective function and optimization procedure. The connection to GraphNet and the discussion of the Lipschitz constant are appreciated and demonstrate a strong theoretical understanding.
* **Scalability:** The online learning aspect is crucial for handling the massive datasets typical in neuroimaging. The authors acknowledge and address the computational considerations, providing some analysis of the running time compared to other methods.
* **Experimental validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset, a large and publicly available resource, provide a reasonable level of validation. The qualitative assessment of the learned dictionaries, coupled with quantitative metrics like explained variance and prediction of behavioral variables, offer compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness. The learning curves convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the spatial regularization, particularly when data is limited.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** While the authors discuss hyperparameter tuning, the selection process feels somewhat heuristic. The justification for the chosen values isn't always entirely clear, and a more systematic exploration of the parameter space (perhaps using a grid search or Bayesian optimization) would strengthen the results.
* **Comparison to state-of-the-art:** While comparisons are made to CanICA and SODL, a more comprehensive comparison to more recent, specialized ROI definition methods (e.g., those based on meta-analysis or deep learning) would be beneficial.
* **Limited biological validation:** The results are primarily assessed based on statistical metrics and visual inspection. While the qualitative assessment suggests the learned regions align with known functional organization, a more rigorous biological validation – perhaps by correlating the identified regions with independent datasets or known anatomical structures – would be valuable.
* **Clarity of Alg. 2:** The description of the BCD dictionary update (Alg. 2) feels somewhat dense and could benefit from further clarification, particularly for readers not intimately familiar with the underlying optimization techniques.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach to brain region definition.
* The theoretical formulation is sound, and the online learning framework addresses a key challenge in neuroimaging.
* The experimental results are promising and support the claims made by the authors.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex idea.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The hyperparameter tuning process is not fully justified.
* The comparison to other state-of-the-art methods is limited.
* The biological validation is somewhat lacking.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a high degree of novelty and potential significance. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths outweigh them considerably. The authors present a compelling case for their approach and provide evidence that it can effectively extract meaningful brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data. I am willing to overlook the incomplete validation and somewhat heuristic hyperparameter selection, given the originality and potential impact of the work. I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and would be a suitable addition to the NIPS program.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
