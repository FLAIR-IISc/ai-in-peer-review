Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method for neuroimaging that enforces both sparsity and spatial structure on dictionary atoms. The formulation penalizes the Laplacian of each atom to encourage smooth, blob-like regions, while constraining atoms in an ℓ1 ball or simplex to induce sparsity (and optionally non-negativity). Codes are estimated with ridge regression (with an option to switch to ℓ1), and the algorithm follows a block-coordinate descent scheme akin to Mairal et al. (JMLR’10) with per-atom updates solved via proximal gradient (FISTA) on a GraphNet-like denoising subproblem. The method targets massive data settings and claims only a 2–3x overhead relative to unstructured online DL.

On HCP task fMRI Z maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40), the authors compare Smooth-SODL to vanilla SODL and CanICA. They report: (i) more interpretable and spatially coherent atoms than SODL, (ii) improved stability across splits, (iii) superior early learning curves (explained variance on test) relative to SODL when n is small, and (iv) competitive predictive performance on behavioral scores (below CanICA, but above SODL and thresholded CanICA). They provide runtime comparisons and a qualitative hyperparameter study.

Strengths
- Timely problem and practical impact: scalable, online, spatially structured ROI discovery is important for large neuroimaging cohorts, filling a gap between fast but unstructured online DL and slow structured methods (e.g., TV-ℓ1).
- Algorithmic design: a clean integration of a Laplacian prior into online DL, using per-atom proximal updates with known Lipschitz constants; reuses the standard SODL sufficient statistics and preserves the memory footprint.
- Empirical evidence: convincingly shows qualitative benefits (less spurious fringes than ℓ1-only DL) and better stability; useful learning curve analysis demonstrating benefits in small/medium data regimes; reasonable runtime overhead.
- Clarity of goals: emphasizes interpretability and reproducibility, which are crucial in neuroimaging.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty relative to prior structured dictionary learning: The idea of imposing Laplacian/GraphNet-type smoothness on loadings/atoms is established (e.g., Grosenick et al. NeuroImage; Abraham MICCAI; Varoquaux et al. IPMI; Jenatton et al. AISTATS on structured sparse PCA). The main novelty is the online integration with small overhead. This is valuable but incremental; it would be strengthened by more direct, apples-to-apples comparisons against TV-ℓ1 or GraphNet-based decompositions (even if offline) on subsets to demonstrate the trade-off quantitatively (interpretability vs runtime vs fidelity).
- Theoretical details around time-varying regularization: To maintain reasonable FISTA steps, the paper proposes making γt scale with A_t[j,j]/t. This changes the objective over time; it is not clear whether the convergence guarantees of online DL (Mairal) still hold or to which limiting objective they converge. At minimum, a brief theoretical justification or a reference to online optimization with time-varying regularization (beyond Ying & Zhou for classification) is needed.
- Claims about FISTA iterations/accuracy: Stating that 1–2 FISTA iterations achieve 1e-6 accuracy is implausible without warm-starting and a small Lipschitz constant. Please clarify whether you warm-start from the previous iterate, what stopping criterion is used in practice, and report objective gaps/time per inner update. As written, Alg. 2 has an unspecified inner stopping rule; yet the text suggests a single pass suffices.
- Fairness of comparisons and baselines: (i) CanICA is run on smoothed data while DL methods are not—this mixes algorithm and preprocessing effects. Showing CanICA with/without smoothing and thresholding would reduce ambiguity. (ii) No comparison to structured NMF, GraphNet-regularized PCA/DL, or TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning (even on a smaller subset) weakens the significance claim. (iii) Only one HCP contrast is used; more tasks or a resting-state dataset would improve generality.
- Modeling choices and constraints: Using a simplex (non-negative atoms) for Z-score maps (which can contain positive and negative effects) is a strong modeling assumption that may bias decompositions. Please justify positivity or show results with ℓ1-ball (allowing signed atoms). Similarly, the choice of ridge vs ℓ1 codes is consequential; an ablation would help.
- Evaluation rigor: Stability and reproducibility metrics are reported without confidence intervals; details of the behavior prediction pipeline are underspecified (regressor type, nested CV, feature selection performed within folds to avoid leakage for RAW). Given that prediction is a key claim, more methodological detail and uncertainty estimates are needed.
- Clarity/notation: A few notational inconsistencies and typos (e.g., scaling in Eq. 7 and the definition of F_{γ̃}, factor t vs A_t[j,j], clarity on boundary conditions for ∆ and λ_max(∆)=4D). The “global optimum” claim should be restricted to the convex per-atom subproblem.

Significance
The contribution is practically useful for large neuroimaging studies: it provides a scalable way to enforce spatially coherent, sparse atoms with modest overhead and improved stability/interpretability. While the methodological advance is incremental, the empirical evidence suggests it is a good engineering solution likely to be adopted, especially if released in Nilearn.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a brief theoretical note or empirical justification for time-varying γt and its effect on convergence and the limiting objective.
- Clarify the inner solver: initialization, stopping, typical iteration counts, and measured objective decrease.
- Add at least one structured baseline (e.g., TV-ℓ1 DL or GraphNet-regularized PCA/DL) on a smaller subset to quantify the interpretability vs runtime trade-off.
- Report sensitivity to using ℓ1-ball vs simplex constraints and to allowing signed atoms; include an ablation with ℓ1 codes.
- Ensure fair baselines: CanICA with/without smoothing and thresholding; optionally MELODIC.
- Expand evaluation: more tasks or resting-state; error bars/CI for stability and prediction; full details of predictive modeling and CV to rule out leakage.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important practical gap with a method that is simple, fast, and effective.
- Improves interpretability and stability over standard online DL with minimal computational cost.
- Solid empirical study on a large public dataset with useful analysis (learning curves, stability).

Cons for acceptance
- Methodological novelty is modest; missing comparisons to the most relevant structured baselines.
- Some theoretical and algorithmic details are underspecified; fairness and rigor of certain evaluations could be improved.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a practical and scalable contribution with clear utility for neuroimaging, but it would benefit from stronger comparisons and clarifications. If space permits addressing the key concerns above, it would be a good addition to the program.
