

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes an extension to online dictionary learning (SODL) for brain imaging applications, introducing a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially smooth and sparse dictionary atoms. The authors argue that this "Smooth-SODL" approach better captures interpretable brain regions compared to standard dictionary learning or ICA-based methods.

**Summary of contributions:** The paper builds on the SODL framework [16] by adding a Laplacian regularization term to promote spatial structure in the dictionary atoms. The key claim is that this approach produces more interpretable brain regions (as opposed to scattered activation patterns) while maintaining computational efficiency. The authors test their method on HCP fMRI data, comparing against SODL and CanICA.

**Strengths:**
- The paper identifies a genuine problem in neuroimaging: the lack of principled methods for defining brain regions (ROIs) that capture inter-subject variability.
- The technical approach is well-motivated, with a clear explanation of how the Laplacian penalty enforces spatial smoothness.
- The implementation details are thorough, and the authors provide a clear description of the algorithmic modifications.
- The experimental setup is reasonable, using a large public dataset (HCP) with appropriate metrics for comparison.
- The paper acknowledges limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for careful hyperparameter tuning).

**Weaknesses and concerns:**
1. **Questionable novelty:** The core contribution is a straightforward extension of existing dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty. The paper fails to clearly articulate how this differs from prior work on structured dictionary learning for brain imaging [1, 25]. The authors cite [1] but don't adequately address why their approach is superior to the TV-`1 minimization used there, especially since they acknowledge that alternated minimization (used in [1]) is slow but their method is only "slightly less than 3 times slower" than SODL.

2. **Inadequate justification for spatial regularization:** The authors acknowledge that "spatial regularization isn't needed if data are abundant" (as in HCP), yet they present their method as a major improvement. Their learning curve experiments (Table 1) show that the performance gap between Smooth-SODL and SODL diminishes as more data is used, suggesting the spatial regularization is most beneficial only in small data regimes. This undermines their main claim that the method is universally superior.

3. **Unfair comparison:** The paper compares Smooth-SODL to CanICA but pre-smooths CanICA (with 6mm FWHM) while not pre-smoothing SODL or Smooth-SODL. This is a significant methodological inconsistency that likely disadvantages their method. CanICA is designed to work with smoothed data, while their method is not, making the comparison invalid.

4. **Lack of convincing evidence for practical utility:** The paper claims their method "better captures inter-subject variability" but provides only qualitative evidence (Fig. 2a). The quantitative metrics (explained variance, reproducibility) show modest improvements (e.g., 0.4 vs. 0.1 for reproducibility), which may not justify the additional complexity. The behavioral prediction results (Fig. 2c) show only marginal gains over SODL.

5. **Overstated claims:** The paper states "the resultant model extracts structured and denoised dictionaries that are more interpretable" but the evidence for interpretability is weak - the figures show similar brain networks across methods, with only minor differences in blob structure. The "denoised" claim is also questionable given the Laplacian penalty may simply smooth out noise but also potentially smooth out real signal.

6. **Lack of comparison to established methods:** The paper compares to SODL and CanICA but doesn't compare to established ROI definition methods like anatomical atlases or task-based activation maps, which are the current standard in the field.

**Recommendation:**
I cannot recommend acceptance. While the paper presents a technically sound extension to dictionary learning with reasonable experimental validation, the novelty is marginal, the evidence for practical superiority is insufficient, and the comparison to existing methods is flawed. The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method offers substantial advantages over established approaches for the problem it addresses.

The authors should reconsider their claims about the necessity of spatial regularization, provide a fair comparison to standard methods, and demonstrate more compelling evidence of improved utility for neuroimaging analysis. Without these improvements, the paper does not advance the field sufficiently to merit publication at NIPS. The method is an incremental contribution to an already well-studied problem, with results that are largely consistent with what would be expected from adding a spatial penalty to dictionary learning.

The paper would be better suited for a specialized neuroimaging venue rather than NIPS, where the focus should be on significant methodological advances with clear evidence of impact on the field.
