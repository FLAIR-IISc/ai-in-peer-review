**Summary**

This paper proposes an online dictionary learning algorithm, "Smooth-SODL," designed to extract spatially coherent brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging datasets. The core idea is to extend the online sparse dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. [16] by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty promotes spatial smoothness, resulting in components that are both sparse and structured as contiguous "blobs," which are more neuroscientifically interpretable than scattered voxels. The authors derive the necessary algorithmic updates, demonstrate that the additional computational cost is modest (a factor of 2-3x), and present experiments on a large fMRI dataset (HCP) showing that their method produces more interpretable and stable components than standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and more structured components than ICA-based methods (CanICA), with particular advantages in lower sample-size regimes.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important problem in neuroimaging: the principled, data-driven definition of functional regions of interest (ROIs). Developing scalable methods for this task is highly relevant as datasets continue to grow in size (e.g., HCP, UK Biobank).
2.  **Originality:** While structured penalties on dictionary atoms are not new [1, 14, 25], combining a Laplacian penalty with the highly scalable *online* learning framework of [16] is a novel and practical contribution. It successfully bridges the gap between computationally expensive batch methods for structured decomposition and fast but unstructured online methods.
3.  **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is relatively thorough. The authors compare their method against relevant baselines (SODL, CanICA) on a large, modern dataset. The inclusion of multiple evaluation criteria (visual quality, explained variance, stability, and predictive power for behavioral scores) provides a multi-faceted view of the algorithm's performance. The learning curve analysis in Table 1 is particularly compelling, as it clearly demonstrates the benefit of the spatial regularization in data-limited scenarios.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising direction, the paper is marred by a significant number of issues in its presentation, technical clarity, and experimental rigor. These issues, while individually addressable, collectively undermine the quality of the submission.

1.  **Lack of Technical and Notational Rigor:**
    *   The objective function in Equation (2) includes a `lim n→∞`. This is unconventional for an empirical objective function. The online setting typically frames this as minimizing an expectation over the data distribution, which is then approximated by the empirical sum. The current notation is confusing and likely incorrect.
    *   The derivation of the dictionary update in Equation (7) is opaque. The use of a large underbrace with the comment "refer to [16] for the details" is not acceptable for a self-contained conference paper. The notation `Fγ̃(At[j,j]/t)−1(v, a)` is ambiguous; it is unclear if the inverse applies to the function `F` or its first argument. A clearer formulation, perhaps defining `γ̃_t,j = γ / (At[j, j] / t)`, would be necessary.
    *   The justification for the choice of `αt ∼ t⁻¹/²` in Equation (10) is absent. This is presented as an empirical finding without any theoretical motivation, which is insufficient for a NIPS paper. Similarly, the condition on `γ` in Equation (9) is stated in big-O notation without a clear derivation.
    *   The convergence claim is hand-wavy. The paper states that the hypotheses of [16] are satisfied but provides minimal detail, delegating the full justification to supplementary materials.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The reference list is exceptionally poorly formatted and contains errors. It lacks a consistent citation style, with missing years, page numbers, and journal volumes. Crucially, reference [12] is cited for the "HCP wiki" but actually points to a paper on the Smooth-Lasso. This is a serious oversight.
    *   The writing style is often too informal for a top-tier scientific publication (e.g., "One may then ask... YES!:", "N.B.:", "futile attempt to remedy the situation").
    *   Figure 2 is poorly presented. The y-axis label in 2(b) is written vertically and is difficult to read. The x-axis labels in 2(c) are awkwardly abbreviated with extra spaces ("Pictu re vo cab."). There are no sub-captions (a), (b), (c) to clearly link the text to the different parts of the figure.
    *   The algorithm descriptions are not self-contained. Algorithm 2's main step refers to Equation (8), which itself provides no detail, creating a circular and unhelpful dependency.

3.  **Experimental Design Flaws:**
    *   The comparison to CanICA is potentially confounded. The paper states that CanICA was run on data smoothed with a 6mm FWHM kernel, while SODL and Smooth-SODL were not. This is presented as a strength, but it means the algorithms are not being compared on identical inputs, making it difficult to attribute performance differences solely to the models themselves.
    *   The "RAW" baseline in the behavioral prediction task (Figure 2(c)) is a weak one. Using the top 2000 most correlated voxels is a form of feature selection known to be prone to overfitting, which is exactly what the results show. A more standard and robust baseline, such as Ridge or Lasso regression on the full set of voxels (or a PCA-reduced set), would provide a much more meaningful comparison.
    *   Key metrics are not fully defined. The "stability of the dictionaries" is mentioned, but the procedure for calculating the mean correlation (including the matching of atoms, which is only mentioned in a figure caption) is not described in the main text.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a computationally efficient method for a significant problem in a high-impact domain.
*   The core idea of making structured dictionary learning online and scalable is a valuable contribution.
*   The experimental results, particularly the advantage in the low-data regime, are promising.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper suffers from numerous and substantial presentation flaws, including an erroneous and poorly formatted bibliography, which suggest a lack of care.
*   Key technical details regarding the model formulation, algorithmic updates, and hyper-parameter selection are either unclear, poorly justified, or missing.
*   The experimental design has notable weaknesses (confounded comparison with CanICA, weak "RAW" baseline) that temper the strength of the conclusions.

**Conclusion:**

While the core idea of this paper is valuable and addresses a relevant problem with a sensible approach, the execution and presentation fall significantly short of the standards expected for NIPS. The manuscript is marred by a large number of issues, ranging from minor formatting inconsistencies to more serious problems of technical ambiguity and questionable experimental choices. These collective issues prevent a confident assessment of the work's soundness and reproducibility. Therefore, despite its potential, I recommend **rejection** for this paper in its current form. The authors are encouraged to address the detailed critiques to substantially improve the manuscript for a future submission.
