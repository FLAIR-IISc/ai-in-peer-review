

# Peer Review: "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online sparse dictionary learning (SODL) for neuroimaging data that incorporates spatial smoothness via Laplacian regularization. The authors address the problem of principled ROI definition by leveraging inter-subject variability in brain imaging data rather than relying on anatomical definitions or standard clustering approaches. The method builds upon SODL [16] by adding a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms (representing brain regions), while maintaining the computational efficiency of online learning. The work is positioned as a solution to the limitations of existing approaches like CanICA [22] (which produces dense, non-sparse maps) and standard SODL (which produces spatially unstructured maps).

The paper makes a reasonable connection to related work, citing key contributions in structured dictionary learning [1, 14, 25] and online learning [16, 17]. However, it could better distinguish its specific contribution from prior work on spatially structured dictionary learning, particularly the TV-ℓ1 minimization approach in [1] which also addresses brain atlas learning.

## Strengths

1. **Problem Significance**: The paper correctly identifies a meaningful challenge in neuroimaging—ROI definition is often heuristic and lacks principled statistical foundations. The authors convincingly argue that inter-subject variability contains important information that should be leveraged.

2. **Algorithmic Innovation**: The extension of SODL with Laplacian regularization is well-motivated and implemented efficiently. The authors demonstrate that the additional computational cost is minimal (only 2-3x slower than SODL), making it practical for large-scale neuroimaging datasets.

3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The experiments on HCP fMRI data are comprehensive, comparing against relevant baselines (SODL, CanICA) across multiple metrics: visual quality, explained variance, stability, and predictive performance for behavioral variables. The learning curve analysis (Fig. 2b) effectively demonstrates the advantage of spatial regularization for smaller datasets.

4. **Clarity and Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and the mathematical formulation is precise. The visual comparisons in Fig. 2(a) effectively demonstrate the qualitative advantage of their method over baselines.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification**: While the authors provide convergence guarantees for their algorithm, they lack a theoretical analysis of *why* the Laplacian penalty improves performance. Specifically, they don't address the bias-variance tradeoff introduced by the spatial regularization, nor how it affects the statistical properties of the learned dictionary. This is particularly important given their claim that "spatial regularization is needed" for capturing inter-subject variability.

2. **Overstated Necessity of Spatial Regularization**: The paper claims spatial regularization is crucial, yet their own results (Fig. 2b) show that for the HCP dataset (n=500), the gap between Smooth-SODL and SODL narrows significantly as more data is used. For large datasets like HCP, the advantage of spatial regularization appears marginal, which undermines their main claim. The learning curve analysis in Table 1 shows that with sufficient data, the performance gap closes, suggesting the regularization might be less critical than implied.

3. **Superficial Hyperparameter Analysis**: The hyperparameter section (Section 3.2) is underdeveloped. The authors mention using k-fold cross-validation with explained variance but provide no details on how the hyperparameters were selected or how sensitive the results are to their choices. This is particularly concerning given the known difficulty of hyperparameter selection in dictionary learning.

4. **Limited Discussion of Biological Interpretability**: While the paper claims their method produces more interpretable brain regions, it doesn't provide concrete evidence of *biological* significance. For instance, they don't show that the learned regions correspond to known functional networks (beyond general visual inspection) or that they lead to new neuroscientific insights. The comparison with known networks (e.g., executive control networks) is somewhat superficial.

5. **Comparative Analysis Gap**: The paper compares against CanICA and SODL, but doesn't address more recent structured dictionary learning approaches that might be relevant. For instance, the work by [1] (which they cite) uses TV-ℓ1 minimization for brain atlases, and the authors don't clearly differentiate their approach from this prior work.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of dictionary learning for neuroimaging, with a practical algorithm that demonstrates clear advantages in terms of interpretability and stability. The method is well-motivated and the experiments are thorough. However, the claims about the necessity of spatial regularization are overstated, particularly for large-scale neuroimaging datasets like HCP where the advantage diminishes with more data.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. A more nuanced discussion of when spatial regularization is truly beneficial (e.g., for small datasets, sparse data, or noisy measurements)
2. A theoretical analysis of the bias-variance tradeoff introduced by the Laplacian penalty
3. More detailed hyperparameter analysis
4. Stronger evidence of biological significance beyond visual inspection

## Recommendation

**Accept with minor revisions**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, but the current version overstates the necessity of spatial regularization and lacks sufficient theoretical grounding for the proposed approach. With the revisions suggested above, this would be a strong contribution to NeurIPS. The method is well-motivated, the experiments are well-executed, and the computational efficiency is a practical advantage for large-scale neuroimaging applications. The paper would benefit from a more balanced discussion of the conditions under which spatial regularization is truly beneficial, rather than presenting it as universally necessary.

The authors have made a solid contribution to the literature on structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging, and with minor revisions, this paper would be a valuable addition to NeurIPS.
