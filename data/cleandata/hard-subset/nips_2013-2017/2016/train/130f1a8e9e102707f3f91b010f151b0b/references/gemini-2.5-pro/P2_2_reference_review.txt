**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL), a method for discovering structured brain regions (or "atoms") from large-scale neuroimaging data. The work addresses the challenge of identifying meaningful, data-driven Regions of Interest (ROIs) that capture inter-subject variability, which is crucial for statistical power and interpretability in brain imaging studies. The proposed method extends the well-known online sparse dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) [16] by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty promotes spatial smoothness and compactness, encouraging the resulting atoms to be "blob-like" rather than scattered sets of voxels. The authors present an efficient online algorithm based on Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) that retains much of the scalability of the original method. The approach is validated on a large dataset of 500 subjects from the Human Connectome Project (HCP), where it is compared against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a group Independent Component Analysis (ICA) method (CanICA). Results demonstrate that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable and stable brain maps while maintaining strong performance in terms of explained variance and predictive power for behavioral traits.

**Review and Justification**

This is a strong paper that presents a well-motivated and technically sound contribution to an important problem at the intersection of machine learning and neuroimaging. The evaluation is thorough and provides convincing evidence for the utility of the proposed method.

**Quality: High**
The paper is technically sound. The formulation in Equation (2) is a principled extension of existing dictionary learning frameworks, combining a data-fidelity term with penalties for code complexity, atom sparsity (via the constraint set C), and spatial structure (via the Laplacian term). The derivation of the BCD algorithm is a logical adaptation of the approach in [16], and the analysis of the dictionary update subproblem (Problem 7) is correct. The authors rightly identify it as a GraphNet-like denoising problem solvable with fast first-order methods like FISTA and astutely discuss the computational complexity and the practical implications of the hyperparameter γ on convergence speed.

The experimental validation is a key strength. Using a large, public, and challenging dataset like the HCP (500 subjects) lends significant credibility to the findings. The choice of baselines is appropriate, pitting the proposed method against its direct predecessor (SODL) and a widely used alternative for this task (CanICA). The evaluation is multi-faceted, covering qualitative visual assessment, quantitative metrics like explained variance and stability, and a practical application in predicting behavioral scores. The learning curve analysis in Table 1 is particularly effective, clearly demonstrating the benefit of the spatial regularization, especially in lower data regimes.

**Clarity: Good**
The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, and the model and algorithm are described with sufficient detail for an expert to understand the core mechanics. The figures and tables are informative and support the paper's claims effectively. Figure 2, in particular, provides a compelling and comprehensive summary of the results.

A few areas could be slightly improved for clarity:
1.  **Hyper-parameter Selection:** While the authors provide a concise study in Section 3.2 and Figure 1, the discussion on the data-adaptive choice for α (`αt ∼ t−1/2`) is very brief. A short justification or a reference for this choice would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Predictive Model:** In the "Prediction of behavioral variables" section, the "RAW" baseline is described as using the top 2000 most correlated voxels. However, the specific predictive model used (e.g., Ridge Regression, Linear Regression) is not mentioned. Clarifying this would improve reproducibility.
3.  **Atom Matching:** The caption for Figure 2a mentions that dictionary atoms were matched via a Hungarian algorithm. This is an important methodological detail that should ideally be mentioned in the main text of the experimental setup.

These are minor points, and the overall clarity of the paper is high.

**Originality: Good**
The core components of the model—online dictionary learning, L1 sparsity, and Laplacian regularization—are not new in isolation. However, their synthesis into a single, scalable, online learning framework for large-scale neuroimaging data is novel and constitutes a significant contribution. Previous work on structured decompositions (e.g., [1, 25]) often relied on slower batch optimization schemes that do not scale to datasets of this magnitude. This paper successfully bridges the gap between the scalability of online methods like [16] and the superior interpretability of spatially structured models. The work is a non-trivial engineering and modeling achievement that directly addresses a known limitation of unstructured online methods, which often produce noisy, scattered components. The paper does a good job of positioning its contribution relative to related work.

**Significance: High**
The significance of this work is substantial. The neuroimaging community is increasingly dealing with massive datasets (e.g., HCP, UK Biobank), making scalable analysis methods essential. This paper provides a practical and powerful tool for data-driven discovery of functional brain atlases. Such atlases are fundamental for a wide range of downstream analyses, from testing scientific hypotheses to building predictive biomarkers.

The results show that Smooth-SODL offers a better trade-off between model interpretability, stability, and performance compared to existing methods. SODL produces overly sparse maps, while ICA produces dense, noisy maps that require arbitrary thresholding. Smooth-SODL finds a principled middle ground, yielding clean, interpretable regions without post-hoc manipulation. The fact that the implementation is slated for inclusion in a widely used open-source library (Nilearn) further enhances its potential impact, making it accessible to a broad audience of researchers and practitioners. This work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a tool that others are very likely to use and build upon.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and practical problem in a large and active research community (neuroimaging).
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated combination of existing techniques to create a scalable and effective solution.
*   The technical contribution is sound, with a principled model and an efficient algorithm.
*   The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing, using a large-scale, real-world dataset and relevant baselines.
*   The results clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method in terms of interpretability, stability, and performance, especially in data-limited regimes.
*   The potential impact is high, particularly with its planned release in an open-source package.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The novelty could be viewed as incremental, as it combines known components. However, the synthesis is non-trivial and solves a real-world scalability problem that hindered previous structured methods.
*   The predictive performance on behavioral scores is slightly lower than the dense CanICA model, highlighting a trade-off between interpretability and raw predictive power. This is not a major weakness, as interpretability is a primary goal of the method, but it is a point worth noting.

In conclusion, this is a very solid paper that makes a valuable contribution. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are largely related to clarifying small details. I recommend acceptance.
