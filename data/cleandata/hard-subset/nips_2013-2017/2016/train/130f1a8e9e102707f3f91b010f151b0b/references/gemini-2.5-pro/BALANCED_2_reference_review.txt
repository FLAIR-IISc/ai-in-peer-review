### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a method, "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL), for identifying spatially coherent brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data. The work addresses the challenge of modeling inter-subject variability in a principled way to define regions of interest (ROIs). The core contribution is an extension of the widely-used online sparse dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) to incorporate spatial structure. This is achieved by adding a Laplacian penalty to the objective function, which encourages the learned dictionary atoms (the brain regions) to be spatially smooth and blob-like, a desirable property for neuroanatomical interpretability. This is combined with a standard L1-norm constraint to ensure sparsity. The authors derive a block-coordinate descent algorithm for this new objective, demonstrating that the additional computational cost over the unstructured baseline is modest (a factor of 2-3). The method is validated on a large task-fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (n=500), showing that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable, stable, and reproducible components than standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA (CanICA), particularly in scenarios with fewer subjects.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem in neuroimaging: data-driven discovery of functional brain parcels from population data. While structured dictionary learning has been explored before (e.g., using Total Variation), this work makes a valuable contribution by integrating a spatial smoothness prior into a scalable *online* learning framework. This is critical for handling the massive datasets becoming common in the field (e.g., HCP, UK Biobank). The proposed model is an elegant and well-motivated combination of existing techniques that yields a practical and powerful tool.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The formulation is clear, and the derivation of the online algorithm is a logical extension of prior work. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors use a large, modern, and publicly available dataset (HCP), compare against appropriate and strong baselines (SODL, CanICA), and use a comprehensive set of metrics, including qualitative visual inspection, quantitative measures of explained variance and stability, and a downstream task of predicting behavioral variables. The learning curve experiment (described in Table 1) provides particularly strong evidence for the utility of the Laplacian regularizer, showing its significant advantage in lower-sample-size regimes.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for structured, sparse components is clearly articulated in the introduction. The model and algorithm are presented with sufficient detail. The results are clearly presented and visualized, and the figures effectively support the authors' claims. The discussion of practical aspects like hyper-parameter selection is also a welcome inclusion.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Comparison to Other Spatial Priors:** The paper positions itself against unstructured methods (SODL, CanICA) and briefly mentions TV-L1 models as being computationally slow. However, a more detailed discussion of the choice of a Laplacian penalty versus other spatial regularizers would strengthen the paper. For instance, Total Variation encourages piecewise-constant regions with sharp boundaries, while the Laplacian penalty encourages smooth variations. A discussion of these different modeling assumptions and their respective suitability for neuroimaging data would be insightful. Is the choice of the Laplacian purely for computational convenience (as it leads to a smooth objective), or are there also neurobiological reasons to prefer it over TV in this context?

2.  **Analysis of the Dictionary Update:** The paper states empirically that 1-2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient for the dictionary update sub-problem. While plausible, this is a key part of the claim to computational efficiency. It would be beneficial to provide more intuition or analysis on this point. For example, does the quality of the final dictionary degrade significantly if only a single FISTA step is performed? How does the required number of inner iterations relate to the regularization parameter `gamma` and the state of the sufficient statistics `A_t`?

3.  **Interpretation of Prediction Results:** In the behavioral prediction experiment (Fig. 2c), the CanICA baseline achieves the best performance on the test set. While the paper correctly highlights that Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL and thresholded CanICA, this result somewhat tempers the claims of the proposed method's superiority. A more nuanced discussion would be beneficial. It could be that the dense, non-sparse components from ICA, while less interpretable, retain more subtle variance that is useful for prediction. Acknowledging this trade-off between interpretability/stability and raw predictive power would provide a more balanced perspective.

4.  **Minor Details:**
    *   The choice of the constraint set `C` as a simplex `Sp(τ)` forces the dictionary atoms to be non-negative. While this may be reasonable for Z-maps representing positive activation, these maps can also have negative values (deactivations). A brief justification for this choice over a standard L1-ball (which would allow negative values) would be helpful.
    *   The number of components `k` was fixed. Some discussion on the sensitivity to this parameter or potential strategies for its selection would be valuable for practitioners seeking to apply this method.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important, practical problem in a large scientific domain (neuroimaging).
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated extension of a state-of-the-art online learning algorithm.
*   The proposed method is scalable and computationally efficient.
*   The experimental validation is extensive, using a large real-world dataset and multiple relevant metrics.
*   The results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed spatial regularization, especially in terms of interpretability, stability, and performance in low-data regimes.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The discussion of related work on structured priors could be more comprehensive.
*   The predictive performance is surpassed by a baseline method, which requires a more nuanced discussion.
*   Some minor technical and modeling choices (e.g., simplex constraint, inner loop convergence) could be justified in more detail.

### Decision

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a valuable and timely contribution. The strengths—a novel, scalable, and well-validated method for an important problem—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are largely related to the discussion and could be addressed in a final version. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and the results are significant for the neuroimaging community and potentially for other fields dealing with structured spatial data. I recommend acceptance.
