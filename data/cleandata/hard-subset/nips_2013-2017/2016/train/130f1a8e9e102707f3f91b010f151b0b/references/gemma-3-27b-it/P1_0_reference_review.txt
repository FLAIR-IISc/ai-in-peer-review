## Peer Review for "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), for automatically discovering brain regions directly from population-level fMRI data.  The authors address the challenge of inter-subject variability by framing region definition as a decomposition problem, learning a dictionary of spatially localized and sparse components (atoms) that capture shared patterns of brain activity.  The key contribution lies in extending online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatial smoothness in the learned dictionary, improving interpretability and performance compared to standard sparse dictionary learning. The method is demonstrated on a large HCP dataset, showing improvements in dictionary quality, reproducibility, and predictive power compared to ICA-based approaches and standard SODL.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader context of brain region definition, acknowledging the limitations of both univariate methods and traditional ROI-based approaches. It builds upon prior work in dictionary learning for neuroimaging (e.g., [16]) and spatial regularization techniques (e.g., Total Variation minimization [1, 25]).  The authors appropriately cite relevant work in clustering-based methods [14, 21], PCA/ICA [5], and online learning [16, 17]. The connection to GraphNet [11, 13] for solving the dictionary update problem is a valuable observation.  However, a more detailed discussion of recent advances in *sparse* online learning approaches, particularly those addressing scalability, would be beneficial.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for spatial smoothness is a novel and compelling approach. It effectively bridges the gap between scalability (online learning) and interpretability (spatial regularization).
* **Scalability:** The online learning framework allows the method to handle the large-scale datasets common in neuroimaging, a significant advantage over batch methods like ICA.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on the HCP dataset are well-designed, comparing the proposed method to relevant baselines (CanICA, SODL). The results demonstrate consistent improvements in terms of dictionary quality (visual inspection), stability, and prediction performance. The ablation study (varying γ) provides valuable insights into the impact of the spatial regularization.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The authors provide a thoughtful discussion of hyperparameter tuning and initialization strategies, offering practical guidance for applying the method. The availability of an implementation in Nilearn [2] is a major plus.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and its underlying principles.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors mention the convergence properties of BCD, a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the impact of the Laplacian penalty on the convergence rate or solution quality would strengthen the paper.  
* **Comparison to Related Work:**  While the authors cite relevant work, the comparison feels somewhat superficial. For example, they mention the limitations of CanICA but don’t fully explore more modern sparse ICA variants. A more in-depth comparison to recent methods incorporating spatial priors within ICA or other dimensionality reduction frameworks would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The parameter selection discussion (Section 3.2) hints at the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters (α, γ, τ).  The provided guidance (e.g., time-varying α) is helpful, but a more systematic sensitivity analysis – perhaps visualizing the impact of each parameter on the learned dictionaries – would be beneficial.
* **Interpretation of Atoms:** While the authors show that the learned atoms are interpretable, a more quantitative analysis of their relationship to known brain networks or anatomical structures would be compelling.  Are the learned regions consistently associated with specific cognitive functions?
* **Limited Behavioral Prediction:** While the behavioral prediction results are encouraging, the reported R<sup>2</sup> values are relatively modest. Further investigation into the relationship between the learned dictionary components and specific behavioral traits could provide stronger evidence for the method’s utility.



**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The method is novel and addresses an important problem in neuroimaging.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.
* The paper is well-written and provides practical guidance for implementation.
* The scalability of the online learning framework is a significant advantage.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is limited.
* The comparison to related work could be more thorough.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity warrants further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential to impact the field of neuroimaging. The proposed Smooth-SODL method offers a principled and scalable approach to automated brain region definition.  While some areas could be strengthened with further analysis and comparison, the novelty, empirical validation, and practical considerations make this a worthwhile contribution to NIPS.

**Rating:**  **Strong Accept** 

**Recommendations:**

*  I recommend accepting this paper.
*  The authors should consider addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the theoretical analysis and the comparison to related work, in a revised version.
*  Adding a more detailed visualization and quantitative analysis of the relationship between the learned dictionary components and known brain networks would strengthen the paper considerably.
