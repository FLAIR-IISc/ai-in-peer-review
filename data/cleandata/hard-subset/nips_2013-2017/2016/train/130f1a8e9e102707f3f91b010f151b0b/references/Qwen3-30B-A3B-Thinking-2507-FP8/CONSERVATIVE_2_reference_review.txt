

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning that incorporates Laplacian regularization to produce spatially structured, sparse brain regions from fMRI data. The authors claim this method better captures inter-subject variability than existing approaches while maintaining computational efficiency. The paper builds on the online dictionary learning framework [16] by adding a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness in the dictionary atoms, with experiments on HCP fMRI data showing improved interpretability and stability over SODL and CanICA.

## Strengths
The paper presents a clear and well-motivated problem in neuroimaging: the need for principled ROI definition that captures inter-subject variability. The mathematical formulation of Smooth-SODL is technically sound, and the extension to spatial regularization is a logical step from existing dictionary learning approaches. The experimental validation on a large public dataset is commendable, and the comparison to SODL and CanICA provides useful context. The computational efficiency claim (3x slower than SODL) is supported by empirical results, and the small-data regime analysis (Fig. 2, Table 1) is particularly interesting.

## Weaknesses
The paper's main weakness is a lack of sufficient evidence for its key claims. While the method appears to be a reasonable extension of existing work, the novelty is overstated. The Laplacian regularization for spatial structure is not new to neuroimaging or image processing (see [1, 3, 25]), and the paper fails to sufficiently differentiate its approach from these prior works. The claim that "spatial regularization is particularly useful when data is scarce" is supported only by a single dataset and task, with no theoretical justification for why this should generalize. The paper also doesn't address whether the Laplacian penalty might be detrimental when the true brain regions aren't spatially smooth.

The experimental validation is insufficiently thorough. The comparison to CanICA is problematic because CanICA is an ICA method that doesn't enforce sparsity, making it an unfair comparison. The paper claims CanICA produces "dense and noisy" maps, but provides no quantitative metrics for this claim beyond visual inspection. The "reproducibility" metric (mean correlation between corresponding atoms across splits) is used to claim superiority for Smooth-SODL over SODL (0.4 vs 0.1), but the authors don't explain why this metric is appropriate or how it relates to scientific interpretability.

The computational complexity claim (only 3x slower than SODL) lacks sufficient detail. The paper states that "1 or 2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient," but doesn't provide timing details for these iterations relative to the SODL updates. Without this information, it's difficult to assess the practical significance of the claimed efficiency.

The hyperparameter tuning section mentions "a safe range" for (γ, τ) but provides no principled method for selecting these parameters, which is a known challenge in dictionary learning. The paper also doesn't discuss potential pitfalls of the method, such as how the Laplacian penalty might interact with the L1 sparsity constraint in non-ideal scenarios.

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a technically sound extension of online dictionary learning with spatial regularization, but the evidence supporting its claims is insufficient. The novelty is overstated, the experimental validation is limited in scope, and the comparison to prior work is not sufficiently rigorous. While the method may be useful for the specific problem domain, the paper fails to demonstrate that it represents a meaningful advancement over existing approaches that already incorporate spatial structure (such as [1, 3, 25]).

The paper would benefit from:
1. A more rigorous comparison with existing spatially-structured dictionary learning methods
2. Quantitative metrics for "interpretability" and "spatial structure"
3. Analysis of the method's performance on datasets with known non-spatial brain regions
4. A more detailed discussion of the theoretical justification for the Laplacian penalty's benefits

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound and addresses a meaningful problem in neuroimaging, the evidence provided for its claims is insufficient to justify the novelty and significance it claims. The method appears to be a minor extension of existing work without sufficient validation against the most relevant baselines. The paper would require substantial additional experiments and a more careful discussion of how it differs from prior work to be considered for publication at NIPS.
