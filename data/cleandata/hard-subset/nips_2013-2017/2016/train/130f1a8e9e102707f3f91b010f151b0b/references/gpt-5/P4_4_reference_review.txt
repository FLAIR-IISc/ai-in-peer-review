Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for large-scale neuroimaging that learns sparse and spatially smooth dictionary atoms to define brain regions of interest. The method builds on sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) [16] by adding a Laplacian penalty on atoms to encourage compact, blob-like spatial structure, while constraining atoms in an ℓ1 ball or simplex for sparsity. Codes are obtained by ridge regression (with an option for ℓ1 coding). The dictionary update for each atom reduces to a strongly convex quadratic denoising problem with Laplacian regularization, solved efficiently with FISTA; the full algorithm is a block-coordinate descent with standard convergence guarantees to a stationary point. On HCP task fMRI (n=500; p≈2.6e5; k=40), Smooth-SODL is compared to SODL [16] and CanICA [22]. It yields more interpretable, spatially structured atoms and better stability than SODL, with smaller loss in explained variance relative to ICA. Learning curves show advantages of spatial regularization in low-data regimes; prediction of behavioral variables shows competitive or better performance than SODL and thresholded ICA.

The work relates to: (i) online matrix factorization/dictionary learning [16,17]; (ii) structured sparsity and spatial priors (GraphNet) [11,12], structured sparse PCA [14], and TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning in neuroimaging [1,8,23,24]; and (iii) group PCA/ICA for multi-subject fMRI [22,19]. The novelty is integrating a Laplacian atom prior into the online SODL framework with near-constant overhead and demonstrating practical scalability on a large dataset.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Formulation and optimization: The per-atom update is minv∈C 1/2||v−a||^2 + (γ̃/2)v^TΔv, a strongly convex problem over a convex set; FISTA with projection onto C is appropriate, and the Lipschitz bound using ||Δ||≤4D is reasonable for grid Laplacians. The use of ridge coding ensures unique code solutions.
- Convergence: Standard block coordinate descent arguments as in [16] apply given strong convexity of subproblems and compact constraints. The claim that each atom update attains the global optimum of its subproblem is valid; global optimality for the joint problem is not claimed.
- Practical details: The discussion on step size conditioning (Lt=1+4D γ (At[j,j]/t)−1) is important. However, the proposed schedule γt=O(maxj At[j,j])/t appears inconsistent with the goal of keeping γ̃=γ·(t/At[j,j]) roughly constant; more precise guidance (e.g., set γt proportional to At[j,j]/t or to its running average) would improve rigor. The statement that 1–2 FISTA iterations “reach 10−6 accuracy” is unlikely in general; clarifying that few iterations suffice for adequate progress in practice would avoid overclaiming.
- Model specification: The text alternates between “ℓ1 penalty on atoms” and “atom constraints via ℓ1 ball or simplex.” The implementation relies on constraints; this should be made consistent throughout. The choice of ridge vs ℓ1 codes is discussed qualitatively; an ablation would strengthen claims about stability/performance trade-offs.
- Experiments: The dataset and setup are appropriate and scaled. Metrics include explained variance, stability across splits, interpretability, and behavioral prediction. However, details are missing on cross-validation procedures for prediction (e.g., nested CV, model class, leakage control), target preprocessing, and selection of k. Baseline choices could be stronger: TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning [1,23,24] or structured SPCA [14] are relevant comparators, even if run on smaller subsets due to computational cost. Smoothing applied only to CanICA introduces a confounder; results with/without smoothing for each method would improve fairness.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, with algorithms and main ideas clearly stated. Some notational inconsistencies and typos exist (e.g., switching between penalized vs constrained atoms; minor grammar). Key implementation details (projection onto C, stopping criteria for FISTA, FFT option) are referenced but not fully specified; the supplementary is said to contain more. The hyperparameter strategy (αt∝t−1/2, CV for γ and τ) is sensible but could use clearer justification and ranges. Figures (referred to but not included here) are central to interpretability claims.

Originality
The integration of a Laplacian atom prior into an online dictionary-learning scheme with modest overhead is a useful and nontrivial engineering contribution. Related structured decompositions exist (GraphNet [11], structured SPCA [14], TV-ℓ1 [1,23,24]), but prior works either use slower alternating minimization or do not address online large-scale settings. The contribution is incremental relative to [16] but practically meaningful for neuroimaging.

Significance
The method addresses a relevant need in neuroimaging: learning stable, interpretable ROIs from large cohorts without heavy pre-smoothing. The empirical gains in interpretability and stability, modest runtime overhead, and competitive predictive performance suggest utility. Broader impact depends on availability of code (promised in Nilearn) and broader comparisons. Significance is moderate to high for the neuroimaging community; for NeurIPS, the contribution is an applied methodological extension rather than a theoretical advance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, scalable extension of online dictionary learning with spatial regularization; solid optimization design.
- Empirical benefits on a large, public dataset: more structured atoms, improved stability, competitive explained variance and prediction.
- Practical guidance on hyperparameters and runtime; modest computational overhead.
- Relevant to data-driven ROI learning and large-scale neuroimaging analysis.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited comparisons to alternative structured methods (TV-ℓ1, structured SPCA); smoothing asymmetry across baselines.
- Some technical claims need clarification (γ schedule, FISTA iterations, penalty vs constraint).
- Incomplete details on predictive modeling pipeline and cross-validation, leaving potential for leakage.
- Clarity issues and typos; code not yet available.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and correct the γt scheduling derivation; report the actual schedule used and its effect on runtime and quality.
- Make the atom sparsity treatment consistent (constraint vs penalty); justify choice of simplex vs ℓ1 ball and nonnegativity.
- Add or discuss results with TV-ℓ1 [1,23,24] and/or structured SPCA [14] on a subset to contextualize gains; or provide strong rationale if infeasible.
- Ensure fairness in preprocessing across methods; report CanICA without smoothing and SODL/Smooth-SODL with smoothing for completeness.
- Detail the prediction pipeline: model class (e.g., ridge), nested CV, feature selection procedures, and statistical tests; report confidence intervals.
- Provide an ablation of ridge vs ℓ1 codes and of the Laplacian weight (including γ=0) on stability/EV/prediction.
- Include memory footprint and scalability discussion; release code and scripts.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and practically useful extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging, with convincing—though not exhaustive—empirical evidence. Strengthening comparisons and clarifying technical details would further support its contributions.
