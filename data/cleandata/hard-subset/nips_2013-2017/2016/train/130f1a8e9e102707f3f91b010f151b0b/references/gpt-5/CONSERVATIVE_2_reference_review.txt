Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging in which dictionary atoms are constrained to be sparse (via an ℓ1-ball/simplex constraint) and spatially structured via a quadratic Laplacian penalty. Codes are obtained by ridge regression. The dictionary is updated with block-coordinate descent; the atom subproblem reduces to a convex quadratic with an indicator constraint, solved with FISTA using the Laplacian’s gradient. The authors argue this extends Mairal et al.’s online sparse coding with minimal extra cost while yielding smoother, more interpretable spatial “blobs.” Experiments on HCP task fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to vanilla online dictionary learning (SODL) and CanICA, evaluating explained variance, stability/reproducibility, predictive performance on behavioral measures, and runtime.

Relation to prior work
The formulation is a straightforward specialization of structured sparse decomposition (e.g., GraphNet, TV-ℓ1, structured SPCA) to online dictionary learning: Laplacian regularization on atoms has been widely used in neuroimaging for denoising and spatial smoothness, and online matrix factorization with convex atom constraints is standard. The work claims improved scalability over earlier structured methods (e.g., TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning) that rely on slow alternating minimization; this is plausible but not demonstrated against strong structured baselines.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical need in neuroimaging: deriving interpretable, spatially coherent ROIs/components at scale, beyond unstructured ℓ1.
- Methodologically simple and compatible with established online dictionary learning; the Laplacian penalty is convex, computationally tractable, and easier than TV.
- Experiments use a large, relevant public dataset (HCP) at realistic scale, and include multiple metrics (EV, stability, behavior prediction, runtime).
- Reported runtime overhead (~3x vs SODL) is modest and could make structured decompositions more usable at scale.
- The stability and qualitative interpretability gains over vanilla SODL are plausible and of interest to practitioners.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/soundness:
- The convergence discussion leans on [16], but the algorithm uses time-varying αt and γt. Changing hyperparameters online generally breaks the standard convergence guarantees; this needs justification or removal. The suggested γ schedule (Eq. 9) is ad hoc and not theoretically motivated.
- The claim that “1 or 2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient to reach an accuracy of 1e-6” is not credible in general and depends on conditioning and γ; please report actual iteration counts and stopping rules.
- The Fourier-domain update suggestion is questionable on brain masks with irregular support and non-periodic boundary conditions.
- The use of the simplex (nonnegativity) constraint is not justified for signed fMRI Z-maps; this can bias atoms. Please compare ℓ1-ball vs simplex, and signed vs nonnegative atoms.
- Stability metric is under-specified (how many splits, matching details, variance). No confidence intervals or statistical tests are reported for EV, stability, or prediction.

Experimental rigor:
- Missing strong structured baselines: TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning (e.g., Abraham MICCAI; modern TV solvers), GraphNet/structured SPCA with Laplacian penalties, and spatially constrained ICA. Even on a subset, these are essential for validating the claimed advantage of Laplacian-regularized online learning.
- Pre-smoothing is applied only to CanICA, which biases comparisons; please include “SODL + pre-smoothing” and “Smooth-SODL vs pre-smoothing” ablations to isolate the effect of the Laplacian vs standard preprocessing.
- Predictive performance: CanICA appears to be best on test R2, which undercuts the main claim unless interpretability is quantitatively established. Potential leakage in the RAW baseline (voxel selection) and unclear whether dictionaries/hyperparameters are trained with nested CV; please clarify and correct if needed.
- Reproducibility: hyperparameter search spaces, selection criteria, and number of passes/iterations are not fully described; code is “coming soon.”

Clarity:
- Numerous typos and notation errors (e.g., “maxUi,” missing constants, ambiguous Lipschitz scaling), incomplete citations, and placeholder figure annotations reduce readability. Theorems/lemmas are not provided where claimed. The paper should be thoroughly proofread and reorganized for clarity.

Originality
The technical novelty is incremental: online dictionary learning with a quadratic Laplacian regularizer on atoms is a natural, known variant of GraphNet-style structured penalties adapted to the online setting. The contribution is more on engineering/scale than new theory or models.

Significance
If validated against strong structured baselines and made reproducible, the approach could be useful to practitioners for large-scale neuroimaging and atlas construction. However, the current experimental evidence is insufficiently rigorous, and the strongest predictive baseline (CanICA) outperforms Smooth-SODL on test R2. The interpretability gains are qualitative and not quantified against existing structured methods (TV-ℓ1/GraphNet).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide rigorous convergence statements for fixed α, γ, or remove time-varying schedules. If schedules are retained, justify them theoretically and empirically.
- Report exact FISTA stopping criteria, iteration counts, and sensitivity to γ and conditioning; avoid unrealistic accuracy claims.
- Add strong structured baselines (TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning, GraphNet/structured SPCA, spatially constrained ICA) and a “SODL + smoothing” control. Include ablations: γ=0 vs γ>0; simplex vs ℓ1-ball; nonnegativity vs signed; ridge vs ℓ1 codes.
- Ensure fair evaluation: nested CV for hyperparameters and feature selection; multiple random splits; confidence intervals and statistical tests; clear stability protocol.
- Quantify interpretability (e.g., sparsity ratio, smoothness, overlap with known atlases; human rater studies).
- Clarify implementation details (masking/boundaries, Fourier approach applicability) and release code.

Pros
- Practical problem; scalable implementation; plausible interpretability/stability gains; modest runtime overhead; large real dataset.

Cons
- Incremental novelty; theoretical gaps with time-varying hyperparameters; questionable optimization claims; missing strong baselines; potential evaluation biases; clarity/reproducibility issues.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is reasonable and potentially impactful for practitioners, but the novelty is modest and the empirical/theoretical validation is not yet at NeurIPS standards. Strengthening baselines, rigor, and clarity could make a future version competitive.

Confidence
High.
