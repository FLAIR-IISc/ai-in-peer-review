

# Peer Review: "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel method called Smooth-SODL (Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning) for learning structured brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors address a critical problem in neuroimaging: the lack of principled methods for defining regions of interest (ROIs) that capture meaningful inter-subject variability rather than relying on anatomical definitions or segmentation. The core idea is to use the variability across subjects to define brain regions, with the key innovation being the incorporation of a Laplacian penalty into online dictionary learning to enforce spatial smoothness on the dictionary atoms (representing brain regions), while maintaining sparsity for interpretability.

The paper builds on the online dictionary learning framework of [16], adding a spatial regularization term that encourages "blob-like" structures rather than scattered patterns. The authors demonstrate their method on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (500 subjects), comparing against SODL (sparse online dictionary learning) and CanICA (a standard ICA method). Their results show that Smooth-SODL produces more spatially structured and interpretable brain regions, with better stability and comparable or improved prediction of behavioral variables.

## Critical Assessment

### Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method, with clear optimization procedures and convergence guarantees. The authors successfully extend the online dictionary learning framework [16] with spatial regularization while maintaining computational efficiency.
- The experimental design is comprehensive, evaluating multiple metrics: visual inspection of the dictionary atoms, explained variance, stability (measured by mean correlation across data splits), and prediction of behavioral variables.
- The authors conduct a thorough hyperparameter analysis, providing valuable insights into the trade-offs between sparsity and reconstruction quality.

**Weaknesses:**
- The claim that "spatial regularization is needed" when data is abundant (like in the HCP dataset) is not fully supported. While the authors show that Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL with limited data (241 subjects vs. 17 subjects), the main experiments are on the full 500-subject dataset where the difference between the methods is smaller. The paper should more clearly acknowledge that spatial regularization is most beneficial when data is limited.
- The comparison with CanICA is problematic because CanICA was run on smoothed data (FWHM 6mm) while Smooth-SODL and SODL were not. This creates an unfair comparison, as smoothing is a standard preprocessing step for ICA methods but not for dictionary learning methods. The authors should either run CanICA on unsmoothed data or run their methods on smoothed data for a fair comparison.
- The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach. For instance, how sensitive is the method to the choice of Laplacian parameter γ? What happens if γ is chosen poorly? The authors mention that "taking it too large can lead to degenerate maps," but don't provide guidance on how to select γ in practice.
- The explanation of why the Laplacian penalty produces "blob-like" structures is insufficient. The paper states that ΩLap(v) = 1/2 v^TΔv, but doesn't clearly explain how this leads to spatially compact structures rather than other spatial patterns (e.g., elongated structures).

### Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-structured and logically organized, with a clear progression from problem statement to methodology to experiments.
- The mathematical formulation is precise and well-explained, with good use of figures to illustrate results.
- The authors provide sufficient detail for reproduction, including algorithm pseudocode and implementation details.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't clearly define what "structured" means in the context of brain regions. The authors say atoms should be "piece-wise smooth and compact," but this is not sufficiently explained or distinguished from other spatial regularization methods.
- The relationship between the Laplacian penalty and the resulting spatial structure needs more clarification. For example, the paper refers to "blobs" but doesn't explain why the Laplacian penalty would produce this specific structure rather than other patterns.
- The explanation of the Laplacian operator (Equation 4) could be clearer. The authors state "∇x being the discrete spatial gradient operator along the x-axis," but don't specify how this is implemented for 3D brain images (e.g., whether it's a finite difference approximation, and how it handles boundary conditions).

### Originality

**Strengths:**
- The paper's core contribution—incorporating a Laplacian penalty into online dictionary learning for neuroimaging—represents a meaningful extension of existing work. The authors successfully integrate spatial structure into a scalable online framework, which is novel in this context.
- The authors correctly position their work as extending [16] while addressing a key limitation (lack of spatial structure) that has been noted in the literature (e.g., [1, 25]).

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from related work. For example, they cite [1] as using "TV-`1 minimization" to extract brain atlases, but don't clearly explain how their Laplacian-based approach differs from total variation regularization.
- The paper could better acknowledge the limitations of their approach relative to other methods. For instance, they claim their method "extracts structured and denoised dictionaries that are more interpretable," but don't acknowledge that some brain regions might not be well-represented by blob-like structures (e.g., long fiber tracts).
- The paper doesn't adequately discuss how their approach compares to more recent structured dictionary learning methods beyond [16], such as those in [17, 23].

### Significance

**Strengths:**
- The problem of principled ROI definition is important in neuroimaging, and the paper provides a promising solution that could have significant impact on the field.
- The method has potential to be widely adopted in neuroimaging research, as it provides a principled way to define ROIs that are both interpretable and capture meaningful variability.
- The demonstration of improved prediction of behavioral variables (Fig. 2c) shows practical utility beyond just visualization.

**Weaknesses:**
- The significance of the method is somewhat overstated. The improvement over SODL in prediction tasks is modest (5-10% on most variables), and the comparison with CanICA is problematic as noted above.
- The paper doesn't provide a clear path to adoption in the neuroimaging community. For example, it doesn't address how a neuroimaging researcher would choose hyperparameters for their specific dataset, or how the method would integrate into existing neuroimaging pipelines.
- The paper doesn't provide evidence that the learned regions correspond to known functional networks beyond visual inspection. For instance, do the regions correspond to the default mode network, executive control network, etc., in a quantifiable way?

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. **Re-evaluate the comparison with CanICA**: Either run CanICA on unsmoothed data or run Smooth-SODL and SODL on smoothed data to create a fair comparison.

2. **Clarify the spatial regularization**: Provide a more detailed explanation of why the Laplacian penalty produces blob-like structures, and how this differs from other spatial regularization methods (e.g., total variation).

3. **Address the data size question**: Conduct additional experiments showing the performance of Smooth-SODL vs. SODL on varying dataset sizes, and provide a clearer discussion of when spatial regularization is most beneficial.

4. **Improve the hyperparameter discussion**: Provide more practical guidance on selecting γ and τ, including how to determine a "safe range" for these parameters in practice.

5. **Add biological validation**: Include quantitative comparisons with known functional networks to demonstrate that the learned regions correspond to biologically meaningful brain regions.

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-written paper that addresses an important problem in neuroimaging with a technically sound approach. The method is a meaningful extension of existing online dictionary learning frameworks, and the experimental results are promising. However, the paper has several weaknesses that need to be addressed before it can be considered for publication at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Major Revision**

The paper has significant potential to contribute to the field, but it requires substantial revisions to address the weaknesses identified above. Specifically, the authors need to:

1. Provide a fair comparison with CanICA by using consistent preprocessing.
2. Clarify the relationship between the Laplacian penalty and the resulting spatial structure.
3. Better explain the practical considerations for using the method in real-world neuroimaging applications.
4. More thoroughly address the limitations of their approach.

With these revisions, the paper would be a strong contribution to the field of machine learning for neuroimaging.

## Final Assessment

**Quality**: 7/10 (Solid methodology but needs more thorough evaluation of limitations)
**Clarity**: 8/10 (Well-written but could be clearer on key technical points)
**Originality**: 7/10 (Novel extension of existing work but needs better differentiation from related approaches)
**Significance**: 7/10 (Important problem but impact overstated)

**Overall**: 7.25/10 (Strong potential but needs significant revisions before acceptance)
