### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a method called "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL) for extracting spatially structured and sparse component maps from large-scale neuroimaging data. The goal is to define meaningful brain regions of interest (ROIs) by modeling inter-subject variability in a population. The work extends the well-known online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010), which imposes sparsity on dictionary atoms, by adding a Laplacian regularization term. This new term encourages the learned atoms to be spatially smooth and contiguous, making them more interpretable as anatomical or functional brain parcels.

The proposed optimization problem is solved using an online Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) algorithm. For each incoming data sample (or mini-batch), the algorithm alternates between updating the subject-specific codes (via a closed-form ridge regression solution) and updating the dictionary atoms. The dictionary update step, which incorporates the new Laplacian penalty, becomes a constrained quadratic minimization problem that is efficiently solved using a first-order method like FISTA. The authors argue that this modification adds only a minor computational overhead (a factor of 2-3) compared to the original online dictionary learning algorithm, thus preserving its scalability to massive datasets.

The method is evaluated on a large task-fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (500 subjects). It is compared against the standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a popular group-level ICA method, CanICA. The evaluation uses multiple criteria: computational time, qualitative assessment of the learned maps, explained variance on training and test data, stability of the components across data splits, and performance in a downstream task of predicting behavioral scores. The results demonstrate that Smooth-SODL produces more structured and interpretable brain maps than its competitors while maintaining competitive performance on quantitative metrics.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The model is a well-justified extension of a classic dictionary learning framework. The derivation of the online BCD algorithm is clear and appears correct. The authors properly discuss the convergence properties of their algorithm, noting that it inherits the guarantees of the original work by Mairal et al. The experimental evaluation is a significant strength. The use of a large, publicly available dataset (HCP) and a comprehensive set of evaluation metrics (qualitative, quantitative, stability, prediction) provides strong evidence for the claims made. The learning curve analysis presented in Table 1 is particularly effective at demonstrating the value of the proposed spatial regularization, especially in lower sample size regimes, which is a common scenario in clinical studies.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The motivation for structured, sparse decompositions in neuroimaging is clearly articulated in the introduction. The mathematical formulation of the Smooth-SODL model in Section 2 is precise. The algorithmic details in Section 3 are presented logically, making the procedure understandable. The figures, particularly Figure 2, are informative and effectively summarize the main comparative results. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert to understand the approach, although some specifics for full reproducibility (e.g., precise FISTA parameters) are brief.

**Originality:** The core ideas—online dictionary learning, `l1`-based sparsity, and Laplacian smoothness regularization—are not new in themselves. The primary novelty of this work lies in the successful integration of a spatial structure penalty (Laplacian) into a large-scale *online* dictionary learning framework. Previous works that impose spatial structure on brain decompositions, such as those using Total Variation (e.g., Abraham et al., 2014) or other structured penalties (e.g., Varoquaux et al., 2011), have typically relied on slower batch optimization schemes that do not scale well to datasets with a very large number of subjects. This paper presents a practical, scalable algorithm for this task, which is a valuable contribution. The work is adequately situated within the relevant literature, citing both the online learning and structured decomposition predecessors.

**Significance:** The problem of identifying meaningful brain parcels from population data is of high importance to the neuroimaging community for improving statistical power, interpretability, and understanding brain organization. The proposed method provides a scalable, principled tool for this task. Given the increasing size of neuroimaging cohorts (e.g., UK Biobank, HCP), online algorithms like the one proposed are critical. The results suggest that the method produces more stable and interpretable results than standard techniques without a significant loss in explanatory power, making it likely to be adopted by researchers in the field. The planned integration into a widely used open-source library (Nilearn) further increases its potential impact.

A minor weakness is the hyper-parameter tuning, which remains a challenging aspect of dictionary learning models. While the authors provide an empirical study (Fig. 1) and suggest cross-validation, a more automated or robust procedure for setting the regularization parameters (`γ`, `τ`) would enhance the method's practicality. Additionally, while the comparison to CanICA is relevant, CanICA is known to produce dense maps requiring ad-hoc thresholding. A comparison against other structured decomposition methods, even on a smaller dataset where they are computationally feasible, could have provided a more direct benchmark for the quality of the learned structured components.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Addresses a key challenge:** The paper provides a scalable solution to the important and challenging problem of learning structured brain representations from large-scale population data.
2.  **Technically sound contribution:** The work successfully combines established machine learning techniques into a novel, efficient online algorithm with clear benefits for the target application.
3.  **Thorough empirical validation:** The experiments are comprehensive, using a large, modern dataset and multiple relevant metrics that convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method over unstructured dictionary learning and standard ICA-based approaches.
4.  **High potential impact:** The method is directly applicable to a wide range of neuroimaging studies, and its demonstrated scalability makes it suitable for the very large datasets that are becoming the norm in the field.

### Arguments against Acceptance:

1.  **Incremental novelty:** The methodological contribution consists of combining existing components (online dictionary learning and Laplacian smoothing). It is more of an effective engineering contribution than a fundamental theoretical advance.
2.  **Practicality of hyper-parameter selection:** The paper acknowledges that parameter selection is difficult but does not offer a complete solution, which may limit ease of use for practitioners.

Overall, the strengths of the paper, particularly its technical soundness, thorough evaluation, and potential for significant impact in its domain, outweigh the weaknesses. It represents a solid and valuable contribution to the field of machine learning for neuroimaging.
