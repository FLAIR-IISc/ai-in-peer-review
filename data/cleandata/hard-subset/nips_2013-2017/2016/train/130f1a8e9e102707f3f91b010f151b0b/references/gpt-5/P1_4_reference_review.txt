Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method for neuroimaging that constrains dictionary atoms to be sparse and spatially smooth. The model augments Mairal et al.’s online sparse dictionary learning with a Laplacian (quadratic) penalty on each atom to encourage compact, blob-like spatial structure, while keeping sparsity via a constraint (e.g., l1-ball or simplex). Codes are computed per sample by ridge regression (with a note that l1 codes are also possible). The dictionary is updated by block coordinate descent using historical sufficient statistics; each atom update reduces to a constrained quadratic smoothing/denoising problem (GraphNet-like) solved by projected first-order methods (FISTA). The authors discuss Lipschitz constants and a schedule to avoid vanishing step sizes. They evaluate on 500 HCP subjects’ task-fMRI GLM Z-maps (Math vs Story), comparing against vanilla online DL (SODL) and CanICA. Smooth-SODL yields more spatially structured and interpretable atoms, improves stability across splits, is 2–3x slower than SODL but faster than CanICA, shows better explained variance in low-data regimes than SODL, and competitive explained variance asymptotically. For behavioral prediction, CanICA performs best overall, though Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL and thresholded CanICA.

Relation to prior work
The paper sits at the intersection of online dictionary learning (Mairal et al. JMLR 2010; Mensch et al. ICML 2016) and structured sparsity for neuroimaging (TV-l1, GraphNet, structured PCA/ICA; Abraham et al., Varoquaux et al., Jenatton et al., Grosenick et al.). Prior structured DL methods in neuroimaging (e.g., TV-l1 atlas learning) have used slow alternating optimization; the contribution here is to integrate a simple Laplacian prior into an online framework with low overhead. This is close in spirit to GraphNet but applied to atoms within an online DL framework.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is reasonable: population risk with ridge codes and Laplacian-regularized atoms under sparsity constraints. The online BCD with sufficient statistics mirrors [16]. The atom subproblem is a convex quadratic with simple constraints; projected gradient/FISTA is appropriate. However:
  - Convergence claims rely on [16], which assumes exact solutions (or controlled inexactness) in the block updates; here only “1–2 FISTA iterations” are used. The paper should clarify whether they enforce the residual summability conditions required for convergence with inexact updates, or provide empirical evidence that a small, fixed number of inner iterations suffices without harming convergence.
  - The statement that 1–2 FISTA iterations “reach 1e-6 accuracy” is implausible for large p; likely they meant sufficient decrease for good performance. Please quantify stopping criteria and actual inner accuracy.
  - The Lipschitz constant discussion mixes the atom-specific scaling via At[j,j]/t; the proposed γt = O(||A||∞,∞/t) schedule is sensible but in experiments γ is fixed (1e3–1e4). Reconcile theory vs. practice (did step sizes shrink materially?).
  - The Laplacian penalty ΩLap(v) = 0.5 v^T Δ v: clarify Δ’s spectrum and boundary conditions; LΩLap = 4D assumes a specific normalized grid; otherwise the Lipschitz bound should reference the operator norm of Δ.
  - The constraint set: using the simplex (nonnegativity + l1) is a strong modeling choice for fMRI maps that can have positive/negative effects; justify this choice, and report whether similar results hold with signed l1-balls.

- Empirical evaluation: The HCP experiment is valuable and large-scale. You evaluate EV, stability, runtime, and prediction—good. Yet:
  - Baselines are limited: no comparison to any spatially regularized methods (TV-l1 DL, GraphNet-regularized DL) even on a subset to show quality/runtime trade-offs.
  - SODL is typically presented with l1 codes; here codes are ridge. Please ensure fairness by evaluating SODL with its standard coding (and yours as well), or justify why ridge is preferable.
  - For prediction, details are sparse (regression model, regularization, CV protocol). The RAW baseline uses top-2000 voxels by univariate correlation (risk of leakage if feature selection peeks at test folds); ensure feature selection is nested within CV.
  - Stability metric and matching procedure are briefly described; provide statistical significance and full protocol. Include the number of splits/repeats and variability.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, with a clear algorithmic description. Some issues:
- Several typos/grammatical errors; add careful proofreading.
- The objective writes lim n→∞ E-style risk but the algorithm uses finite-sample sufficient statistics; either state empirical risk or justify the population formulation.
- “No step size to tune” is true for the outer BCD but the inner FISTA still requires a Lipschitz-based step size; clarify.
- Figures/Tables referenced (Fig. 1, 2, Table 1) are central; ensure they are legible and include more quantitative summaries (e.g., sparsity vs. EV curves, ablations).

Originality
Moderately original. The combination—online DL + Laplacian-regularized atoms—seems new in this exact form and practical for large data. The idea of imposing smoothness on dictionary elements is not new, but bringing it into the online DL framework with small overhead is a useful engineering contribution.

Significance
For neuroimaging practitioners, this is likely useful: better interpretability and stability with modest runtime overhead, operating at HCP scale, and potentially deployable in open-source software (Nilearn). Methodologically, the step from unstructured to Laplacian-structured online DL is incremental but impactful. The downside is that on some quantitative metrics (EV on large data, behavioral prediction), the method is not best.

Pros
- Practical, scalable algorithm with small overhead vs. SODL; clear implementation path.
- Improves spatial structure and stability of components; compelling qualitative maps.
- Careful runtime reporting; HCP-scale experiment.
- Useful analysis of hyperparameters and learning curves in low-/mid-data regimes.

Cons
- Convergence justification with inexact inner solves is weak; some theoretical/practical gaps (γ schedule vs. fixed γ).
- Baselines omit other structured DL methods; SODL coding choice may not be fair.
- Limited diversity of datasets/tasks; predictive performance lags CanICA.
- Modeling choices (simplex/nonnegativity) not justified; lack of ablations (e.g., l1 vs ridge codes).

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide convergence guarantees under inexact atom updates (e.g., summable errors), or empirical convergence plots of objective vs. passes for different numbers of inner iterations.
- Compare against at least one spatially-regularized DL baseline (TV-l1) on a smaller subset to position your method.
- Report results with signed l1-ball constraints and with l1-coded SODL to assess robustness and fairness.
- Clarify prediction pipeline and ensure fully nested CV for feature selection and hyperparameter tuning.
- Discuss sensitivity to k and grid resolution; do blobs fragment/merge?
- Release code at submission, not “soon,” to support reproducibility.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper provides a solid, practical contribution with clear benefits for large-scale neuroimaging and likely community impact via open-source. However, I urge the authors to strengthen convergence justification and broaden the baselines; addressing these in the final version would solidify the case for acceptance.
