Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning framework for neuroimaging that enforces sparsity and spatial structure on dictionary atoms. Each subject image Xi is approximated as VUi; codes Ui are obtained via ridge regression (optionally L1), while dictionary atoms Vj are constrained to lie in an L1 ball or simplex and penalized by a Laplacian term ΩLap(Vj) to encourage compact, smooth “blobs.” The optimization is handled by block-coordinate descent: code updates via SVD-based ridge regression; atom updates via proximal steps on a quadratic + Laplacian penalty (solved with 1–2 iterations of FISTA and projection onto the constraint set). The method targets the large n, large p regime with mini-batches and maintains the computational profile of Mairal et al.’s online sparse dictionary learning [16], with a reported 2–3× overhead for the Laplacian term.

Empirically, the method is evaluated on HCP task fMRI Z-maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40). It is compared to SODL [16], PCA, and CanICA [22] on explained variance (EV), qualitative interpretability, atom stability across data splits, and behavioral prediction (R2 on several HCP scores). Smooth-SODL achieves more spatially coherent atoms than SODL, better atom stability than CanICA, competitive EV (slightly below PCA/CanICA as expected), and generally better behavioral prediction than SODL and thresholded-CanICA. The implementation will be integrated in Nilearn [2].

The work connects to: online dictionary learning [16, 17], structured sparsity/GraphNet [11, 13], spatially structured decompositions in neuroimaging (e.g., TV-L1/GraphNet-based atlases [1, 3, 8, 23, 24]). The main claimed novelty is bringing spatially structured penalties into an online framework while preserving scalability and convergence guarantees.

Strengths
- Practical contribution: a simple, scalable extension of online dictionary learning that yields spatially structured, interpretable components with modest overhead, suitable for large neuroimaging datasets.
- Sound use of convex subproblems and well-understood proximal methods; clear mapping to GraphNet denoising for atom updates.
- Empirical evidence that Laplacian regularization improves qualitative interpretability and split-half stability, especially in low-data regimes (learning-curve analysis).
- Integration into an established toolbox (Nilearn) enhances potential impact and reproducibility for the community.
- Clear positioning relative to PCA/ICA: acknowledges their EV optimality while demonstrating drawbacks for interpretability/stability.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Several claims are under-justified empirically or theoretically. The assertion that 1–2 FISTA iterations achieve 1e-6 accuracy is implausible for large p; at minimum, report actual primal/dual residuals vs iterations and a stopping criterion. Similarly, the “provably” slower behavior for large γ is argued via a loose Lipschitz bound; a more principled schedule or analysis would help.
- Hyperparameter selection is not rigorously documented. Fig. 1 (referenced repeatedly) is missing; cross-validation details (splits, grids, selection criteria) are sparse. The time-varying αt ~ t^{-1/2} is proposed without ablation against fixed α or L1 coding.
- Behavioral prediction baselines are weak: “RAW” uses 2000 most correlated voxels, which overfits. Stronger baselines (ridge/elastic-net on all voxels, PCA+ridge, GraphNet regression) are standard in HCP benchmarks.
- Fairness of preprocessing: CanICA is run on smoothed data while SODL/Smooth-SODL are not; this confounds EV and interpretability comparisons. Either smooth all inputs or none.
- Experimental breadth is limited: one task contrast, one k (40), no results on resting-state, no ablation on constraint choice (simplex vs L1, nonnegativity), or on code penalty (ridge vs L1). Reported runtimes (minutes on CPU for n=500, p=2.6e5) seem surprisingly low; provide per-iteration costs, number of passes T, and memory usage.

Clarity
- Numerous typos/grammar issues, and some notational inconsistencies (e.g., missing braces, “simple (mainly in the sense that the Euclidean projection onto C should be easy to comput)”). Several referenced items (Alg. numbers, Fig. 1, supplementary derivations/FFT route) are critical but absent.
- The stability metric and matching (Hungarian) are only briefly described; number of splits and variance/error bars are not reported.

Originality
- Adding a Laplacian penalty on dictionary atoms is not new in batch settings (GraphNet/TV-regularized decompositions). The novelty lies in adapting it to an online scheme with minimal overhead. This is incremental but practically useful.

Significance
- If validated and robust, this approach could become a default for data-driven ROI extraction at scale, given its usability and integration in Nilearn. However, the current empirical evidence (single dataset, limited baselines) falls short of a strong NeurIPS-level claim.

Questions/clarifications for authors
- Provide the exact stopping criteria and iterations for FISTA per atom update; report convergence diagnostics.
- Detail the hyperparameter search spaces and selection procedure for γ, τ, α (and αt schedule), and the number of CV folds.
- How many passes over data (T), mini-batch size, and wall-clock per pass? What is the memory footprint for A_t, B_t at p≈2.6e5, k=40?
- Why the simplex (nonnegativity) constraint for atoms given that Z-maps can have positive and negative lobes? Did you try signed atoms (L1-ball) and how did that affect results?
- How many splits for stability; include means and standard deviations?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen baselines: PCA+ridge, whole-brain ridge/elastic-net, GraphNet regression for behavior; TV-regularized batch dictionary learning (e.g., FAASTA [24]/Abraham MICCAI) as a structured baseline.
- Ensure preprocessing parity across methods (either smooth all or none) and add results across multiple k values and additional HCP tasks/resting-state.
- Include ablations: code penalty (ridge vs L1), constraint (simplex vs L1), effect of γ and τ with error bars.
- Provide a more careful complexity analysis (per-iteration costs) and realistic convergence plots; avoid over-precise numerical accuracy claims.
- Improve presentation: fix typos, include missing figures/supplementary, and better justify the αt and γt schedules (or keep them fixed with CV).

Ethics/reproducibility
- Positive: publicly available data (HCP) and planned open-source implementation in Nilearn.
- Please include exact random seeds, splits, and scripts to reproduce figures and numbers.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline)
Pros for acceptance:
- Practical, scalable extension with clear utility in neuroimaging.
- Improved interpretability and stability with modest computational overhead.
- Integration into community tools.

Cons for acceptance:
- Modest methodological novelty relative to prior structured sparse decompositions.
- Insufficient experimental rigor: limited datasets, weak baselines, missing details/figures, questionable convergence claims.
- Fairness and reproducibility issues (preprocessing differences, incomplete reporting).

With stronger, more comprehensive experiments, clearer analysis of optimization behavior, and improved baselines, this work could be a solid contribution in a future submission.
