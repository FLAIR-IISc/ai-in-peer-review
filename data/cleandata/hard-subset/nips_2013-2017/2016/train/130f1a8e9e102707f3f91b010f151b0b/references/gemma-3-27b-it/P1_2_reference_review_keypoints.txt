- Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online dictionary learning method with L1 sparsity and Laplacian spatial smoothness to discover brain regions from fMRI.  
- Leverages inter-subject variability to define spatially coherent regions, moving beyond ROI-based approaches.  
- Extends online dictionary learning for scalability to large datasets like HCP.  
- Provides a clear mathematical formulation and convergence discussion based on block coordinate descent.  
- Empirical validation on HCP shows improved interpretability, stability, and predictive power versus standard SODL and ICA.  
- Positions the work relative to PCA/ICA, CanICA, TV-l1, and structured sparsity literature, emphasizing the online framework advantage.  
- Novelty: combines online dictionary learning with spatial regularization for automated ROI discovery.  
- Scalability: suitable for large neuroimaging datasets due to online optimization.  
- Theoretical foundation: convergence guarantees and clear model description.  
- Empirical strengths: multi-metric evaluation (explained variance, stability, behavior prediction) with relevant comparisons.  
- Practical guidance: discusses hyperparameter tuning (adaptive α) and computational cost trade-offs.  
- Paper clarity: generally well-written and organized.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: tuning remains complex and needs more systematic sensitivity analysis and a practical recipe.  
- γ (Laplacian penalty) selection: explanation and intuition for constraint on γ (eq.9) are unclear and need clarification.  
- Limited comparisons: lacks benchmarking against traditional segmentation methods (e.g., FreeSurfer).  
- Visualizations: Fig.2(a) should better overlay regions on a standard brain template for intuitive localization.  
- Choice of k: the selection of k=40 is insufficiently justified; impact of varying k should be explored.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel, and significant; recommendation for acceptance with minor revisions.  
- For acceptance: addresses automatic ROI definition, is novel and scalable, and shows strong empirical results.  
- Against acceptance: hyperparameter tuning issues, limited traditional segmentation comparisons, and clarifications needed in some sections.
