This paper introduces a novel approach to functional region of interest (ROI) definition in neuroimaging by leveraging population variability via structured sparse dictionary learning (Smooth-SODL). The core idea—using inter-subject differences to *define* regions rather than as noise—is a compelling and potentially significant shift in perspective. The authors cleverly combine sparsity-inducing regularization with a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially coherent, “blob-like” structures, directly addressing a key limitation of standard clustering or ICA-based methods. The connection to existing work in online dictionary learning is well-established, and the extension appears non-trivial.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of defining ROIs based on population variability is fresh and potentially impactful. The combination of sparsity and spatial regularization in this context is also a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The mathematical formulation is clear and builds logically upon established principles of dictionary learning and regularization. The algorithm, while building on [16], appropriately addresses the challenge of incorporating spatial structure.
*   **Experimental validation:** The experiments on the large HCP dataset demonstrate practical improvements over both standard SODL and CanICA, particularly in terms of interpretability and predictive power. The learning curve analysis is insightful, showcasing the benefit of the spatial regularization, especially with limited data.
*   **Practical relevance:** The potential impact on fMRI analysis (and other neuroimaging modalities) is high, as principled ROI definition is a long-standing challenge.
*   **Clear writing:** The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow, despite the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** While the paper acknowledges hyperparameter tuning is crucial and provides some guidance, the optimal regime feels somewhat ad-hoc. The relationship between γ, τ, and data characteristics could be explored more thoroughly. A more systematic ablation study varying these parameters would be beneficial.
*   **Computational cost:** The method is more computationally expensive than vanilla SODL, by a factor of 2-3. While this isn't prohibitive, it's a consideration for very large datasets and could be a barrier to widespread adoption. Further optimization, possibly through leveraging the FFT-based update mentioned, would be valuable.
*   **Limited comparison:** The comparison to CanICA, while demonstrating improvement, is somewhat limited. CanICA is often used with significant preprocessing (smoothing, dimensionality reduction), which may not be a fair comparison to the more raw data approach of Smooth-SODL. A comparison against more recent or state-of-the-art ROI definition methods (e.g., methods based on deep learning) would be stronger.
*   **Interpretation of 'blobs':** The authors emphasize the "blob-like" structure as desirable, aligning with known functional organization. However, the justification for this prior isn’t fully explored. It’s possible that more complex or irregularly shaped regions are also functionally meaningful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to ROI definition.
*   The theoretical framework is solid, and the algorithmic extensions appear well-justified.
*   The experimental results on a large, publicly available dataset are promising.
*   The paper addresses a significant problem in neuroimaging and has the potential to impact the field.
*   The minor weaknesses do not fundamentally undermine the contributions of the paper.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The hyperparameter tuning process could be more rigorous.
*   The computational cost is higher than simpler methods.
*   The comparison to other ROI definition methods is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially significant contribution. The authors have successfully extended online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial structure, resulting in a method that effectively learns interpretable brain regions from population data. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and I believe it is worthy of acceptance. I am willing to overlook the minor flaws and incomplete validations due to the novelty of the approach and the potential for significant impact.  I encourage the authors to address the hyperparameter sensitivity issue and consider a broader comparison to existing ROI definition methods in future work.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
