- Proposes Smooth-SODL: online dictionary learning with a Laplacian smoothness penalty on atoms and ridge (dense) codes to learn localized, interpretable neuroimaging components.  
- Uses online block-coordinate descent: SVD-based ridge coding, sufficient statistics (A,B), and per-atom convex updates solved by projected FISTA.  
- Claims online convergence extends from SODL given bounded data, unique codes, and strong convexity with the Laplacian term.  
- Implementation details include Lipschitz constant estimation (L = 1 + γ̃·λmax(Δ)), projection cost analysis, and practical γ guidance.  
- On HCP task-fMRI (n=500, p≈260k, k=40), Smooth-SODL produces more structured, sparser, and visually cleaner atoms than SODL and CanICA.  
- Smooth-SODL shows higher stability/reproducibility across splits and better early-sample explained variance than SODL.  
- Predictive performance is competitive but CanICA achieves the best test R^2; Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL and tCanICA.  
- Runtime is about 3× SODL and faster than CanICA; code release planned for Nilearn.  
- Positioning: bridges slow spatially-structured methods (TV/GraphNet) and scalable SODL by integrating Laplacian regularization with low per-atom cost.  
- Technical soundness: formulation and convex per-block subproblems are reasonable; ridge codes ensure uniqueness/stability of U.  
- Convergence claim is plausible but needs a formal adaptation/sketch of the proof to include the Laplacian term.  
- The FISTA inner-loop claim (“1–2 iterations to 1e-6”) is unrealistic in general, especially for large γ; inner-loop stopping should be reported.  
- Recommendations on γ are useful, but authors should consider time-varying γt schedules or report actual inner iteration counts.  
- Experiments are well-chosen and metrics appropriate, but some preprocessing choices may bias baselines (e.g., smoothing only for CanICA).  
- Missing statistical reporting: no confidence intervals, significance tests, or complete tables/figure details shown.  
- Clarity issues: typos, formatting glitches, incomplete references, and under-specified inner-loop composite objective and projection details.  
- Originality is incremental: Laplacian smoothness is standard, novelty lies in integrating it efficiently into online SODL with preserved convergence.  
- Related work coverage is adequate but lacks deeper comparisons to structured sparse PCA/ICA and modern fast TV solvers.  
- Significance: important practical contribution for scalable, interpretable, and stable population-level components in neuroimaging.  
- Limitation: primary gains are interpretability and stability rather than consistent predictive superiority over CanICA.  
- Pros: relevant large-scale problem, practical efficient algorithm, scalability demonstrated, planned open-source code, improved stability/early performance.  
- Cons: incremental novelty, light theoretical development, incomplete experimental reporting and baselines, preprocessing asymmetry, and questionable FISTA accuracy claims.  
- Suggested improvements: add statistical uncertainty and multiple splits, ablations over γ/τ/α and k, compare to TV-ℓ1 and structured PCA baselines, justify preprocessing choices, provide convergence sketch and inner-loop counts, and release code at submission.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — incremental but useful and likely impactful for practitioners; moderate confidence.
