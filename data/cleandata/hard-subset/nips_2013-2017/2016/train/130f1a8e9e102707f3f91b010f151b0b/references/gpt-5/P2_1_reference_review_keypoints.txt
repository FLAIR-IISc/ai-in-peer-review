- Proposal: Smooth-SODL augments online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty to produce sparse, spatially smooth atoms for neuroimaging.
- Algorithm: Uses ridge regression for codes and per-atom convex quadratic updates solved with accelerated projected gradient (FISTA-style) under simplex/ℓ1 constraints.
- Claim: Authors report more structured, reproducible atoms than SODL and more interpretable components than CanICA, at modest extra runtime.
- Technical soundness: Optimization subproblems and use of projected accelerated gradient are standard and correct; per-atom problems are convex with global optima if solved accurately.
- Convergence issue 1: Time-varying hyperparameters (αt ~ t−1/2 and adaptive γt scaling) change the objective and invalidate standard convergence guarantees cited.
- Convergence issue 2: Using 1–2 inexact FISTA iterations conflicts with claims of reaching the global optimum per iteration; error bounds or summable-error conditions should be discussed.
- Lipschitz/scale: The Lipschitz constant derivation (1 + 4D·γ̃) is plausible but depends on Laplacian boundary conditions and normalization; scaling γt by ‖At‖∞,∞/t is heuristic and needs theoretical clarification.
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments are well-designed (comparisons, reproducibility, predictive tasks, runtimes) and show claimed 2–3x overhead.
- Missing baselines: No comparison to prior structured-sparse methods (e.g., TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning, structured sparse PCA); a small-scale comparison is recommended.
- Fairness/control: CanICA is run on smoothed inputs while SODL variants are not, biasing results; preprocessing should be harmonized or justified.
- Code/constraints: Unclear whether codes are constrained nonnegative; relationship between simplex atoms and unconstrained ridge codes should be stated.
- Writing clarity: Generally clear and organized but contains typos/misstatements (e.g., "max_U" vs "min_U", misleading "parameter free", conflicting global-optimum claims).
- Notation issues: Eq. (7) and related notation are hard to parse; explicitly define γ̃ and the projected-gradient gradient used.
- Laplacian details: Boundary conditions and grid normalization for the Laplacian (and the 4D bound) need clarification.
- Hyperparameter guidance: Hyperparameter tuning references figures but lacks concrete numerical ranges to aid reproduction.
- Reproducibility: Open-source plan is good; include seeds, exact preprocessing (masking, normalization), and train/test split protocol.
- Originality: Incremental but useful extension—online Laplacian-regularized per-atom projected updates are practical though not a theoretical breakthrough.
- Significance: Valuable for neuroimaging and other spatial data due to scalable, interpretable, reproducible atoms; impact tempered by theoretical gaps.
- Pros for acceptance: Practical, scalable algorithm with strong application-driven evaluation and imminent open-source release.
- Cons for acceptance: Overstated theoretical claims, missing structured-sparse baselines, and some experimental fairness/notation issues.
- Suggested fixes: Either fix α,γ or prove convergence under annealing; quantify effect of inexact updates; add TV-ℓ1/structured-SPCA comparison; harmonize preprocessing; clarify Laplacian/projection/code sign details; fix typos and notation.
