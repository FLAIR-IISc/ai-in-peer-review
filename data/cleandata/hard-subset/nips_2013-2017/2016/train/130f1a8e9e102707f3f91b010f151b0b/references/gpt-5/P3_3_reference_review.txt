Summary
The paper proposes Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), an online matrix factorization method tailored to neuroimaging. The key idea is to learn a dictionary of spatially sparse and spatially smooth atoms V with ridge-penalized codes U, by extending the online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. [16] with a Laplacian (ℓ2) penalty on atoms. At each online step, codes are updated by ridge regression; dictionary atoms are updated via a block-coordinate scheme that, for each atom, solves a convex quadratic with Laplacian regularization under an ℓ1-ball or simplex constraint (projected accelerated gradient/FISTA-like update). The method aims to yield interpretable, blob-like ROIs while scaling to large n and p. Experiments on HCP task fMRI Z-maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to standard online DL (SODL) and CanICA/PCA, assessing explained variance, spatial interpretability, stability/reproducibility, runtime, and prediction of behavioral scores.

Relation to prior work
- Online DL: builds directly on [16] (and mentions [17]).
- Structured decompositions in neuroimaging: TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning and atlas extraction (Abraham et al. [1], Varoquaux et al. [24]) and structured sparse PCA (Jenatton et al.) and GraphNet [11]. Prior structured approaches are typically batch/slow; this work claims to retain online scalability with an ℓ2 Laplacian prior on atoms.
- Group ICA/CanICA [22] and related PCA/ICA methods are strong baselines for variance capture but lack sparsity/localization.

Strengths
- Practical contribution: an online algorithm that incorporates spatial structure on atoms with minimal overhead (≈3× SODL), which is important for large neuroimaging cohorts.
- Sensible modeling choice for interpretability: enforce sparsity on atoms (via ℓ1-ball/simplex) and smoothness (Laplacian), while keeping codes stable via ridge. This flips the usual sparsity-on-codes paradigm and is well-motivated for ROI learning.
- Clear computational strategy: sufficient statistics (A,B) updates, separable atom updates with convex quadratic+constraint, and use of fast projections onto ℓ1/simplex. Convergence of subproblems is straightforward.
- Empirical evidence: improved qualitative interpretability, higher split-half stability than SODL and tCanICA, and better early data efficiency (learning curves), with competitive explained variance on held-out data. Runtime comparisons are favorable.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Quality/technical soundness
- Theoretical details are underdeveloped/inconsistent:
  - The objective is written as a lim n→∞ expected cost but the algorithm uses finite-sample sufficient statistics; the stochastic approximation framing should be formalized as in [16].
  - Inner dictionary updates are said to reach 1e-6 accuracy with 1–2 FISTA iterations; this is not credible for generic Laplacian-regularized quadratics. Please report actual iteration counts/tolerances and stopping criteria.
  - Convergence claims rely on exact minimization of block subproblems; with inexact inner solves and time-varying αt, γt, a reference or error control is needed.
  - The Lipschitz-constant argument suggests γt should scale like O(‖At‖∞,∞/t) to avoid vanishing steps, yet experiments use fixed γ=10^3–10^4. This contradicts the earlier guidance—please reconcile.
- Modeling choices need justification/ablation:
  - Nonnegativity/simplex constraints on atoms for Z-maps that can have positive/negative effects may bias solutions. How do results change with ℓ1-ball without nonnegativity?
  - Why ridge codes instead of sparse codes? A comparison to ℓ1-coded variants (with the same atom regularization) would strengthen claims about stability and interpretability trade-offs.
  - Laplacian (ℓ2) smoothness vs TV (edge-preserving) priors: given [1,24], a comparison would be very informative. Laplacian may oversmooth and blur boundaries.
- Evaluation fairness and completeness:
  - CanICA is run on pre-smoothed data while SODL/Smooth-SODL are not; this confounds comparisons in explained variance and interpretability. Either smooth all methods equally or analyze the effect explicitly.
  - Reproducibility/stability: details about split protocol, atom matching, and statistics (mean±std correlations) are insufficient. Include error bars and multiple random initializations.
  - Prediction: CanICA outperforms Smooth-SODL on test R2, and only positively predicted traits are reported. Please report full distribution (including negatives), statistical significance, and confound control.
  - Only one HCP task and one choice of k are shown; generalization to other tasks/rest fMRI or other ks would improve significance.

Clarity
- The writing is uneven, with typos and formatting issues. Several references are incomplete. Important implementation details are missing: boundary conditions for ∆ on brain masks, use of FFT on irregular domains, preprocessing (masking, standardization), and hyperparameter selection ranges. The “global optimum at each iteration” statement should be clarified as “global optimum of the convex subproblem.”

Originality
- The core idea—adding an ℓ2 Laplacian penalty on atoms within online DL—is a modest extension of [16] and closely related to GraphNet/structured sparse PCA ideas applied to atoms rather than coefficients. The novelty lies primarily in integrating spatially structured atom updates into an online DL pipeline with good practical performance. Given existing structured DL for fMRI (TV-ℓ1) and structured PCA, the conceptual advance is incremental but practically relevant.

Significance
- For neuroimaging practitioners, a fast, online, spatially structured DL method that runs on a laptop and yields interpretable components is useful. However, the gains over SODL are mostly qualitative/stability; prediction performance does not improve over CanICA; and comparisons to TV-based methods are missing. Broader ML impact is limited by the domain-specific nature and incremental methodology.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a precise optimization description: projected accelerated gradient vs FISTA, proximal operator used, stopping criteria, typical inner iterations.
- Clarify and justify fixed vs time-varying γ and α; provide sensitivity plots and theoretical rationale.
- Report ablations: with/without nonnegativity; ℓ1-coded variant; TV vs Laplacian atom priors.
- Equalize smoothing across methods or justify why it is appropriate only for CanICA.
- Expand stability evaluation with multiple splits, confidence intervals, and show dictionary variability across runs.
- Detail preprocessing and boundary conditions for ∆ on the brain mask. FFT-based updates are not applicable on irregular masks; explain when used.
- Release code to ensure reproducibility.

Pros
- Practical, scalable algorithm; easy to integrate in existing toolchains.
- Improved interpretability and stability of components with small computational cost.
- Solid large-scale experiment on HCP; reasonable baselines.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to prior structured DL/SPCA/GraphNet work.
- Some theoretical and methodological inconsistencies; insufficient detail on optimization and hyperparameters.
- Evaluation has fairness issues (smoothing) and lacks comparisons to TV-based methods; behavior prediction not improved.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is practically valuable for neuroimaging, but the contribution is incremental and the paper needs stronger theoretical grounding, fairer and broader evaluation (including TV-based baselines), and clearer exposition. With these improvements, this could be a solid contribution for a domain-focused venue or a future NeurIPS resubmission.
