Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework tailored to neuroimaging that enforces both sparsity and spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms. Each subject image is modeled as a linear combination of k atoms; codes are obtained via ridge regression, and atoms are constrained to a simplex/ℓ1 ball and penalized by a Laplacian (GraphNet-like) term to yield compact, blob-like components. The algorithm extends sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) of Mairal et al. [16] by incorporating the Laplacian penalty into the BCD dictionary update, solved efficiently via a few FISTA iterations. Experiments on HCP task-fMRI maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to vanilla SODL and CanICA [22], reporting improved interpretability and stability at modest computational overhead, with competitive explained variance and behavioral prediction.

This sits at the intersection of online matrix factorization [16,17], structured sparsity and GraphNet [11,13], and prior structured/TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning in neuroimaging [1,24]. The main novelty is integrating Laplacian-regularized atoms into an online dictionary-learning loop with practical speed and stability on large volumes.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Learning spatially coherent population-level components is important for robust ROI definition and inter-subject variability modeling in neuroimaging.
- Methodological contribution: A pragmatic and scalable way to integrate Laplacian structure on atoms into online dictionary learning with sufficient statistics (A_t, B_t). The subproblem reduces to a convex denoising (GraphNet) proximal step, amenable to FISTA.
- Empirical scale: Experiments on 500-subject HCP task fMRI with k=40 components and p≈260k voxels; runtime overhead is only ~3× SODL on a single CPU, still faster than CanICA.
- Interpretability and stability: Qualitative maps look cleaner and more compact than SODL, and stability across splits (Hungarian-matched atom correlations) improves substantially (0.4 vs 0.1).
- Sensible exploration of hyperparameters: Practical guidance on τ and γ and a time-varying α_t, with cross-validation on explained variance.

Weaknesses
Quality/technical soundness:
- Convergence and parameterization details are underspecified or inconsistent. The discussion suggests γ must scale like O(||A_t||∞,∞/t) to avoid vanishing step sizes, yet experiments use fixed γ (10^3–10^4). A clear statement of the algorithm actually used (fixed γ vs γ_t) and the associated convergence guarantees is missing.
- The claim that “1 or 2 iterations of FISTA” reach 1e-6 accuracy is implausible for general Laplacian-regularized problems; provide stopping criteria, residuals, and sensitivity to early stopping.
- The code update uses ridge rather than sparse codes; the trade-offs vs ℓ1 codes (as in [16]) are not quantified, and stability is asserted but not ablated.
- Evaluation of behavioral prediction lacks methodological detail (e.g., nested CV, standard pipelines like ridge/elastic net on the learned codes vs voxel-space baselines). The RAW baseline with preselection of 2000 voxels is prone to optimistic bias; stronger baselines (e.g., whole-brain ridge with nested CV) should be included.

Clarity:
- Several typos, missing reference years, and notation slips make reading harder. Some derivations (e.g., Eq. 7 and the mapping to the proximal problem with constraints) are terse; the role of the constraint set vs Laplacian penalty could be clarified.

Originality:
- The idea of spatially structured dictionary atoms is not new ([1,24], structured SPCA [14], GraphNet [11,13]). The main novelty is bringing Laplacian-regularized atoms into an online setting with small overhead. This is incremental but practically useful.

Significance:
- Gains are clear for interpretability and stability; however, CanICA performs best for behavioral prediction and explained variance, weakening claims of superiority beyond interpretability. Additional baselines (e.g., TV-regularized offline DL [1], structured SPCA [14], or modern online variants [17]) and broader datasets (resting-state, other tasks) would strengthen impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, scalable algorithm addressing a real need in neuroimaging: structured, reproducible components at population scale.
- Empirical evidence of improved stability/interpretability with modest computational cost.
- Integration into a widely used toolbox (Nilearn) could facilitate adoption.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical clarity on convergence with fixed vs time-varying γ and the actual optimization accuracy.
- Novelty is incremental relative to existing structured DL and GraphNet literature.
- Behavioral prediction and EV benchmarks favor baselines; comparisons and baselines are not fully convincing.
- Clarity and presentation issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a precise algorithmic statement: fixed γ vs γ_t, stopping criteria for the inner FISTA, and a proposition establishing convergence of the overall BCD with the chosen schedule and inexact updates (e.g., bounded errors).
- Report sensitivity analyses: vary number of FISTA iterations, γ, τ, α_t schedule; show how stability, EV, and runtime trade off.
- Compare against stronger baselines: (i) offline TV-ℓ1 DL [1], (ii) structured SPCA [14], (iii) online DL with elastic-net atoms or group structure, (iv) whole-brain ridge/elastic net for prediction with nested CV.
- Include an ablation with ℓ1 codes vs ridge codes to substantiate stability claims.
- Add quantitative interpretability metrics (e.g., compactness, spatial smoothness, overlap with known atlases) beyond visuals.
- Expand datasets (another HCP task, resting-state), and report absolute EV and R^2 numbers with confidence intervals.
- Improve exposition: fix typos, complete references, clarify Eq. (7–9), and better motivate the choice of simplex constraint vs ℓ1 ball.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The paper addresses an important problem and presents a practical extension with promising empirical properties, but the methodological novelty is modest, theoretical justifications are incomplete/inconsistent, and empirical comparisons do not clearly demonstrate superiority beyond interpretability. With stronger theoretical clarity, more rigorous baselines, and expanded evaluation, this work could become a solid contribution.
