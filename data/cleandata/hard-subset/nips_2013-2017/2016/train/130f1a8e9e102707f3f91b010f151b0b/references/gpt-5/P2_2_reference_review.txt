Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning method for neuroimaging that enforces both sparsity and spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms. The model retains the standard online block-coordinate descent framework of Mairal et al. (JMLR 2010) for large-scale settings: codes are updated by ridge regression, and atoms are updated one-by-one. The main novelty is adding a Laplacian penalty on dictionary atoms to promote compact, blob-like spatial structure, while constraining atoms to an L1 ball or simplex to induce sparsity/nonnegativity. The atom update becomes a quadratic denoising problem with Laplacian regularization under a convex constraint; the authors solve it efficiently via projected proximal-gradient (FISTA) with small extra cost compared to vanilla online DL. Experiments on HCP task fMRI maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA. Results suggest improved interpretability and split-half stability over SODL, substantially better small-sample explained variance (EV), and competitive predictive performance on behavioral variables (though CanICA performs best on prediction).

Relative to prior work, the paper builds directly on online dictionary learning (Mairal) and connects to structured penalties used in neuroimaging (TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning: Abraham; GraphNet: Grosenick; structured sparse PCA: Jenatton). Prior structured decompositions have been slower or batch; this paper targets scalable online learning with spatial structure at minor overhead.

Evaluation by NIPS criteria

Quality
- Technical soundness: The objective is separately convex, and the Laplacian penalty preserves strong convexity of the atom subproblem. Using projected proximal-gradient for atom updates is appropriate, and the Lipschitz constant can be computed from the quadratic form. However:
  - Convergence claims are not fully justified under the proposed inexact updates. The algorithm uses a small fixed number of FISTA steps (often 1–2) and time-varying αt and γt. Mairal’s convergence analysis assumes exact minimization of block subproblems or carefully controlled inexactness for a fixed objective; changing α, γ over time and early-stopping FISTA should be discussed rigorously (or the claims softened).
  - The statement that 1–2 FISTA iterations suffice to reach 1e−6 accuracy is implausible for general γ and p and contradicts the later warning about large Lipschitz constants. Please report actual duality gaps or objective decreases versus iterations.
  - The scaling in Eq. (7) and in the Lipschitz constant Lt = 1 + 4D γ (At[j,j]/t)−1 is confusing; the derivation of the γ̃ factor and its dependence on At[j,j]/t should be made explicit.
  - Some notational slips (max vs min in Eq. (2), missing details in Alg. 2) and typos detract from clarity.
- Empirics: The dataset is appropriate and sizable; the runtime comparison is informative; the stability and EV analyses are useful. However, baselines are limited (no TV-ℓ1/GraphNet-based decompositions, no comparison to ℓ1-coded DL), and statistical uncertainty (error bars, variability across splits) is largely absent. The behavioral prediction evaluation is honest (Smooth-SODL not best), but more details on model selection, cross-validation, and voxel selection for RAW would strengthen conclusions.

Clarity
- The high-level idea and algorithmic sketch are clear. The connection to GraphNet-style denoising is helpful. That said, the presentation of the atom update is terse; the precise proximal-gradient step (gradient, projection operator for the chosen C) and the role of the At, Bt sufficient statistics deserve a complete derivation. The discussion of tuning (αt ∝ t−1/2, γt bounds) mixes practical heuristics with theoretical remarks without clearly separating them. There are several typos and minor inconsistencies. Overall, an expert can reproduce the method, but the paper would benefit from tighter exposition and more precise algorithmic statements.

Originality
- The contribution is an incremental but useful extension: integrating a Laplacian smoothness prior into online dictionary learning for large-scale neuroimaging with minimal computational overhead. While spatially structured decompositions are not new, doing so in an online framework with careful engineering is not, to my knowledge, widely established in the literature cited. The idea is a natural combination rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic paradigm.

Significance
- For neuroimaging, the method addresses a practical need: interpretable, spatially localized components learned at scale, with better small-sample behavior and improved stability. The proposed approach is likely to be adopted by practitioners, especially if integrated into Nilearn. For the broader ML audience, the advance is modest but relevant: a practical recipe for adding quadratic structured priors to online DL with small overhead and maintaining most of the scalability benefits. The empirical evidence is promising but would be more compelling with broader datasets, additional baselines, and stronger statistical analysis.

Pros
- Simple, scalable extension of online DL to incorporate spatial smoothness with modest overhead (~2–3x SODL).
- Large-scale experiment on HCP data; sensible metrics (EV, stability, prediction) and honest reporting.
- Clear practical benefit: markedly improved interpretability and split-half stability; strong small-sample EV gains.
- Implementation plan in a widely used library (Nilearn) supports impact.

Cons
- Convergence guarantees are not fully supported under inexact atom updates and time-varying regularization; theoretical claims need tightening.
- Limited baselines (no TV-ℓ1 online variants, no ℓ1-coded DL or Elastic-Net codes, no nonnegative matrix factorization).
- Narrow experimental scope (one task/dataset, k fixed), limited statistical analysis (no confidence intervals), and incomplete description of stability matching/metrics.
- Clarity issues and some questionable numerical claims (FISTA iteration counts, Lipschitz scaling).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a precise atom-update derivation, explicit proximal step, and correct Lipschitz scaling; report actual per-iteration residuals/gaps.
- Either fix α, γ (and tune via CV) or state and justify an inexact BCD convergence result for time-varying regularization and early stopping.
- Add ablations (ℓ1 codes vs ridge; L1 ball vs simplex) and additional baselines (TV-ℓ1 DL, NMF).
- Expand experiments to multiple tasks/datasets and k values; include error bars and statistical tests; detail the stability matching procedure.

Overall assessment
The paper makes a practical and computationally relevant contribution with clear utility in neuroimaging. While originality is moderate and some theoretical and clarity issues remain, the empirical benefits and scalability are convincing. With the suggested clarifications and expanded comparisons, this work would be a solid fit.

Recommendation: Weak Accept.
