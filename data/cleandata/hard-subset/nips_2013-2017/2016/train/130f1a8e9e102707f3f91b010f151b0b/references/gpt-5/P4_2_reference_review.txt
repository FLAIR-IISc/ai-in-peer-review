Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning framework for neuroimaging in which dictionary atoms (spatial maps) are constrained to be sparse and spatially smooth. The model decomposes each high-dimensional brain image Xi as Xi ≈ VUi with a shared dictionary V and sample-specific codes Ui. Sparsity is enforced on atoms by constraining each atom to an ℓ1-ball or simplex, and spatial structure is imposed by adding a Laplacian (GraphNet-style) quadratic penalty ΩLap(Vj) to each atom. Codes are obtained via ridge regression; the dictionary is updated by block-coordinate descent, with each atom updated by solving a convex quadratic problem with a Laplacian penalty and projection onto the constraint set using a few iterations of FISTA. The algorithm extends Mairal et al.’s online sparse dictionary learning (SODL) to incorporate spatial structure at small additional computational cost and retains similar convergence guarantees (to a stationary point). The paper studies hyperparameter effects and reports empirical gains in interpretability, stability, and small-sample explained variance on a large HCP fMRI dataset, compared against SODL and CanICA.

This work sits at the intersection of online dictionary learning [Mairal et al., JMLR 2010], structured sparsity/GraphNet [e.g., Grosenick et al., NeuroImage], TV-ℓ1 decompositions for neuroimaging atlases [Abraham et al., MICCAI], and multi-subject ICA (e.g., CanICA). Unlike prior structured decompositions typically solved by slow alternating optimization, the contribution is an online, scalable algorithm that integrates a Laplacian prior into the dictionary update.

Quality
- Modeling/algorithmic soundness: The objective is standard for online factorization with an added Laplacian penalty on atoms and a convex constraint set to induce sparsity. The code update via ridge regression is correct and stable. The atom update subproblem is a strongly convex quadratic with a convex constraint, so global optimality of each sub-update holds. Using FISTA with a projection onto C (prox of indicator) is appropriate since the Laplacian term is smooth and the constraint is handled by projection.
- Theoretical claims: Convergence to a stationary point under the hypotheses of [16] is plausible; however, the paper could more clearly state assumptions (e.g., bounded data, uniqueness of codes with ridge) and how the Laplacian term preserves the online convergence arguments. The Lipschitz bound for the gradient with Laplacian (1 + γ̃‖∆‖2) is reasonable; the bound 1 + 4Dγ̃ matches the spectral norm upper bound for the standard discrete Laplacian. The recommendation γt = O(‖At‖∞,∞/t) to prevent vanishing steps is heuristic; no proof is provided that this schedule preserves convergence.
- Some statements are inaccurate or imprecise: “1–2 iterations of FISTA suffice to reach 1e-6 accuracy” is not credible in general; a more careful stopping criterion or tolerance discussion is needed. The rate for FISTA is misstated (the iteration complexity is O(√(L/ε)), not O(L/√ε)). The objective includes a “lim n→∞” expected risk form but is instantiated empirically with finite t; the connection should be clarified.
- Experimental design: Experiments on 500 HCP subjects with p ≈ 2.6×10^5 and k=40 are appropriate. Comparisons include SODL and CanICA, with explained variance, stability (mean atom correlation across splits), qualitative interpretability, and behavioral prediction R^2. Runtime comparisons show a ≈3× overhead vs SODL and faster than CanICA on a single CPU. Positive: analysis of small-sample regime where Smooth-SODL substantially outperforms SODL in test EV, and stability improvements. Limitations: lack of comparison to TV-ℓ1/GraphNet-based atlas learning baselines (even on a subset) weakens empirical claims about structured priors; details of behavioral prediction pipeline (regressor type, cross-validation protocol, target preprocessing) are insufficient for reproduction; hyperparameter choices (τ, γ) and cross-validation splits are not fully specified.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-structured, with algorithms outlined and core choices motivated. There are typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., “max” vs “min” in the code objective; “1-strongly-convex”; using “ℓ1 penalty” in text but implementing sparsity via constraint C). The derivation of the atom update (Eq. 7–8) is sketched but not fully shown; providing the gradient and projection step would improve clarity. Figures are informative, but details of matching (Hungarian algorithm parameters), thresholds for tCanICA, and stability computation should be made explicit. Reproducibility would benefit from exact hyperparameters and released code (currently “will soon be available”).

Originality
The core elements (online DL, Laplacian/GraphNet smoothing, ℓ1/simplex constraints, FISTA) are known. The paper’s novelty lies in integrating a Laplacian prior into the online SODL framework with minimal overhead and demonstrating its practical benefits at HCP scale. Methodologically, this is an incremental but nontrivial extension that addresses a real computational gap (scalable structured dictionary learning).

Significance
For neuroimaging applications requiring interpretable, spatially localized components learned at scale, the contribution is valuable. The observed gains in atom stability and early-sample generalization, while maintaining competitive explained variance and modest runtime overhead, are practically significant. Broader ML impact is modest, as the advances are primarily engineering/integration rather than new theory, but the approach may be adopted in other high-dimensional spatial domains.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear problem motivation: interpretable, spatially structured components for population neuroimaging.
- Scalable algorithm that integrates Laplacian smoothing into online DL with small overhead.
- Empirical evidence of improved stability and small-sample test EV over SODL; better interpretability than ICA.
- Comprehensive evaluation on a large public dataset; analyses of hyperparameters and runtime.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited methodological novelty beyond combining known components; no new convergence theory.
- Some technical imprecisions (FISTA rate, claimed iteration accuracy) and unclear gamma/alpha schedules.
- Missing comparisons to other structured baselines (TV-ℓ1/GraphNet decompositions) on even a smaller setting.
- Reproducibility details (hyperparameters, prediction pipeline) and code availability are incomplete at submission time.

Questions/requests for authors
- Provide full details for the behavioral prediction setup (features, model, CV protocol, confound handling).
- Report τ, γ (and schedules) used in main results; clarify how cross-validation was performed.
- Include at least a small-scale comparison to a TV-ℓ1 or GraphNet-based atlas-learning baseline.
- Correct the FISTA rate statement and temper the “1–2 iterations to 1e-6” claim; define stopping criteria.
- Clarify the “lim n→∞” objective vs finite-sample online updates.

Recommendation: Weak accept
