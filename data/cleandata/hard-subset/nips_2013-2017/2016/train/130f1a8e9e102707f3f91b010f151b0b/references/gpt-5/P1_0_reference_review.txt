Summary
This paper introduces Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method for neuroimaging that augments standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL; Mairal et al.) with a Laplacian penalty on dictionary atoms to encourage spatially compact, smooth “blobs,” while also enforcing sparsity via an ℓ1-type constraint (simplex or ℓ1-ball). Codes are computed by ridge regression (with an option for ℓ1 codes). The dictionary update becomes a constrained quadratic denoising step akin to GraphNet; the authors solve it by a few projected-FISTA iterations within the standard online BCD framework using sufficient statistics (A, B). They target large-scale regimes (p ~ 10^5–10^6 voxels; n up to 10^5). On HCP task fMRI (500 subjects; k=40), Smooth-SODL yields more structured, interpretable atoms and higher stability across data splits than SODL, with modest runtime overhead (≈3×). It achieves explained variance close to CanICA and substantially better early-sample performance than SODL; for behavioral prediction, CanICA performs best, but Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL and thresholded CanICA.

Relation to prior work
The method builds directly on online dictionary learning [Mairal et al., Mensch et al.] and connects to spatially structured penalties used in neuroimaging (GraphNet; TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning and structured sparse PCA: Abraham et al., Varoquaux et al., Jenatton et al.). Prior structured decompositions often rely on slow alternating minimization; the paper’s contribution is to integrate spatial structure into an efficient online framework with convergence guarantees similar to SODL. The work cites these lines appropriately, though experimental comparison to TV/GraphNet-based structured DL is missing.

Quality
- Algorithmic soundness: The formulation is reasonable; the per-atom subproblem is strongly convex and amenable to projected first-order updates. The sufficient-statistics update and mini-batch coding are standard and scalable. However:
  - The convergence discussion relies on [16], but the dictionary update is solved inexactly. Convergence of inexact BCD/proximal steps typically requires either summable errors or sufficient decrease; this is not established. The statement that 1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e-6 accuracy is implausible unless heavily warm-started; please clarify the stopping rule and provide a bounded inexactness argument.
  - The Lipschitz/bandwidth condition (Eq. 9) suggests γ should scale with t to avoid vanishing steps, but experiments use fixed γ (10^3–10^4). Reconcile theory and practice (e.g., A_t[j,j]/t stabilizes; warm starts; small k; practical step sizes).
  - The Laplacian denoising via FFT is only valid for full regular grids with periodic BC; for brain masks this is generally inapplicable. Please clarify boundary conditions, handling of brain masks, and whether FFT is actually used.
  - The choice of constraint set as a simplex (non-negativity + ℓ1 budget) is consequential for fMRI contrasts that have both positive and negative values. Justify non-negativity or report results with an ℓ1-ball to decouple the effects of positivity vs Laplacian.
- Empirics: The HCP experiment is relevant and large. Metrics include explained variance, visual quality, stability across splits, and predictive R^2. However, evaluation breadth could be improved:
  - No comparison to TV-ℓ1/GraphNet-based structured DL (even on a smaller subset) to isolate the benefits vs computational cost.
  - Stability is reported as mean correlation across “different splits,” but the number of splits, matching protocol (Hungarian? absolute correlation?), and variance are not specified.
  - Behavioral prediction: ensure feature selection for RAW is performed within folds. Report cross-validation protocol, error bars, and whether hyperparameters for all baselines are tuned fairly.
  - The visualization appears to show selected “most interpretable” atoms; quantitative measures of spatial compactness (e.g., Gini coefficient, cluster size, perimeter/area) would strengthen claims.
- Reproducibility: Many implementation details are missing or terse (mask and Laplacian construction, voxel spacing, projection algorithm onto simplex, warm-start strategy). Code availability in Nilearn is promised, which would be valuable.

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable, with clear high-level motivation and algorithmic flow, but there are issues:
- Several typos/grammar and incomplete references (years missing). Figures are referenced but not fully legible in the text provided.
- Notation: C alternates between ℓ1-ball and simplex; make explicit which is used in which experiment. Define the full objective for the dictionary update including the indicator of C to clarify the proximal step (smooth + projection).
- Provide the precise definition of explained variance and stability metrics, and details of train/test splits and hyperparameter selection (you mention K-fold CV for γ, τ).

Originality
Moderately original. The combination of an online SODL framework with a Laplacian penalty on atoms and efficient projected first-order updates is an incremental but useful advance enabling large-scale structured decompositions in neuroimaging. The idea of Laplacian/GraphNet smoothing is not new; the novelty is primarily in integrating it into the online DL loop with small overhead.

Significance
For practitioners working with population-level neuroimaging maps, Smooth-SODL offers a pragmatic tool that improves interpretability and stability without sacrificing much fidelity and with reasonable compute. If released in Nilearn, adoption potential is high. From a core ML perspective, the methodological advance is incremental; significance hinges on the empirical support and accessibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real need: scalable, structured, interpretable decompositions for massive neuroimaging data.
- Elegant integration of Laplacian regularization into online DL with modest overhead.
- Strong large-scale experiment; improved stability and early-sample performance; clear qualitative gains.
- Potential impact via open-source integration.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical gaps regarding inexact updates and γ scheduling; some questionable claims (1–2 FISTA iterations to 1e-6).
- Limited comparisons to stronger structured baselines (TV-ℓ1, GraphNet DL).
- Evaluation limited to one task/dataset and partly qualitative; stability and prediction analyses need more rigor and detail.
- Clarity/precision issues and incomplete references.

Questions for the authors
1) Do you warm-start each per-atom FISTA from the previous iterate? What stopping criterion do you use, and what is the typical number of iterations? Please correct the 1e-6 claim or provide evidence.  
2) How is the Laplacian defined on the brain mask (adjacency, spacing, boundary conditions)? Is FFT ever used in practice here?  
3) How do you reconcile Eq. (9) with using fixed γ in experiments? Does A_t[j,j]/t stabilize so that L remains bounded?  
4) Why adopt non-negativity (simplex) for atoms in fMRI contrasts? Results with an ℓ1-ball would help interpretability of the ablation.  
5) Detail the stability computation: number of splits, matching procedure, use of absolute correlations, and variability.  
6) Confirm that RAW feature selection and all hyperparameter tuning are done within the training folds.  
7) Can you add a small-scale comparison with TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning to demonstrate similar interpretability at much lower cost?

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a practical and scalable contribution with clear utility in neuroimaging and promising empirical results, but would benefit from clarifying theoretical aspects of the inexact updates, strengthening baselines and evaluation rigor, and improving clarity. If the authors address the questions above (especially convergence/stopping, γ scheduling, and evaluation details), this would make a solid NeurIPS contribution.
