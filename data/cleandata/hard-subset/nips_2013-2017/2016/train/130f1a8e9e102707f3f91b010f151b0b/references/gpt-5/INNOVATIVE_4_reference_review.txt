Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging that augments standard sparse online dictionary learning (Mairal et al.) with a spatial Laplacian penalty on dictionary atoms to enforce localized, blob-like, interpretable regions. Codes are estimated via ridge regression (or optionally L1), while atoms are updated by solving a convex denoising subproblem with Laplacian regularization using fast first-order methods (FISTA), embedded in a block-coordinate descent loop. The authors argue that this yields spatially structured factors at only a 2–3× runtime overhead relative to vanilla SODL, retaining online scalability. They provide experiments on HCP task fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) comparing Smooth-SODL against SODL and CanICA, with metrics including explained variance, stability across splits, interpretability, and prediction of behavioral scores. They report improved spatial structure and stability over SODL, competitive explained variance, and competitive-to-best predictive performance (with CanICA best on average) while being faster than CanICA.

Relation to prior work
There is a substantial literature on structured sparse decompositions for neuroimaging (e.g., TV-ℓ1/GraphNet and structured sparse PCA) and on online dictionary learning for large-scale settings. Prior structured atlas-learning methods have relied on expensive alternating minimization. The novelty here is the integration of a graph/Laplacian prior on atoms directly into an online dictionary learning loop with per-atom convex updates and practical speed, coupled with a large-scale empirical study on HCP. The approach resembles GraphNet regularization but is applied in an online factorization context with explicit analysis of step sizes and simple parameterization.

Strengths
- Idea and scope: Pushing structured spatial regularization into an online dictionary learning solver that scales to hundreds of subjects and hundreds of thousands of voxels is a useful and timely direction for population neuroimaging and ROI discovery.
- Practical impact: The claimed 2–3× overhead vs SODL is compelling; the reported runtimes on CPU are reasonable and better than CanICA. The approach is simple, uses standard primitives (ridge codes + Laplacian-prox), and is suitable for deployment (Nilearn).
- Empirical evidence: Experiments on a large public dataset, with multiple evaluation criteria (EV, stability, prediction, qualitative interpretability). The learning-curve analysis showing much higher early EV with spatial regularization is insightful.
- Clarity of algorithmic choices: The subproblem is clearly stated; the Lipschitz constant and the impact of γ on step sizes are discussed; practical schedules for αt and γt are suggested.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate: Using a Laplacian/graph-regularizer on factors is well-known; the primary contribution is making it work online with minimal overhead. This is valuable but not a conceptual leap beyond existing pieces (SODL + GraphNet).
- Theoretical guarantees are light: Convergence “inherits” from [16], but αt and γt are time-varying and the subproblem involves a projection on an ℓ1/simplex set with a Laplacian penalty. A more careful statement of conditions under which the BCD converges (with time-varying parameters and inexact inner solves) would strengthen the paper.
- Empirical comparisons: 
  - CanICA is run on smoothed data while SODL/Smooth-SODL are not; this makes comparisons harder to interpret. A consistent preprocessing setting would be fairer, or include both.
  - There is no direct comparison to TV-regularized atlas learning on the same data (e.g., Abraham MICCAI; GraphNet-based approaches), even if slower; a small-scale subset comparison would be informative to isolate the effect of Laplacian vs TV.
  - The claim “1–2 FISTA iterations suffice to reach 1e-6 accuracy” is not credible without heavy warm-starting; please clarify stopping criteria and whether warm-starts are used.
- Modeling choices need clearer motivation:
  - Non-negativity/simplex constraints vs ℓ1-ball: the experiments use positivity; please justify (interpretability? identifiability?) and show ablations with/without non-negativity.
  - Ridge vs L1 codes: the paper mentions both; the main experiments use ridge. An ablation comparing ridge and L1 codes would help understand stability/performance trade-offs.
- Reporting:
  - Quantitative interpretability/stability metrics are sparse. Include sparsity measures, spatial compactness (e.g., number/size of connected components), and cross-split matching scores for all methods.
  - EV and prediction: CanICA often wins on EV and test R2, which undercuts the claim of strictly better downstream utility. Emphasize where Smooth-SODL is distinct (stability, interpretability) with stronger quantitative evidence.

Questions for the authors
- Can you formalize convergence with time-varying αt and γt and inexact inner solves (e.g., using block coordinate descent with errors)? What are practical tolerances?
- How sensitive are results to γ and τ? Fig. 1 is mentioned but not shown; please include full grids and failure modes.
- How does Smooth-SODL compare to TV-ℓ1 atoms at equal runtime (on a subset) and in terms of spatial compactness?
- Do results generalize to other modalities (rest fMRI time series, diffusion, PET), and to surface meshes where the Laplacian differs?
- Can you release code and exact hyperparameters for reproducibility at camera-ready?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem (defining ROIs from population variability) with a scalable, simple, and useful method.
- Empirically strong on stability/interpretability with minimal computational overhead; solid large-scale application.
- Likely to see adoption (Nilearn integration), thus real impact.

Cons for acceptance
- Conceptual novelty is incremental (graph-regularized atoms in online dictionary learning).
- Missing key baselines and ablations; some evaluation choices complicate fairness.
- Limited theoretical development beyond adapting [16].

Recommendation
Weak accept. While the technical novelty is moderate, the paper presents a practical and scalable approach that advances structured decomposition for massive neuroimaging, with evidence of improved interpretability and stability at reasonable cost. With tightened evaluations and clarifications, this work can catalyze broader use of online structured factorization in neuroscience.

Confidence
High for assessing methodological contributions and experimental design; moderate regarding neuroimaging domain nuances.
