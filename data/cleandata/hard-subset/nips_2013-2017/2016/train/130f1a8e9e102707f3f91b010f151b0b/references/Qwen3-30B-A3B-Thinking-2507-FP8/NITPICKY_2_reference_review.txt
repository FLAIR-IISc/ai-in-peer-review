

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization to learn spatially structured brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors argue that traditional ROI definition methods have limitations and propose using inter-subject variability to define regions. The method combines sparsity (L1 penalty) and spatial smoothness (Laplacian penalty) to obtain interpretable brain regions. The authors claim their method is only 3x slower than standard SODL and demonstrate improvements in interpretability over competing methods.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in neuroimaging where region-of-interest (ROI) definition is crucial but often ad hoc. The idea of using variability across subjects to define regions is well-motivated, and the method has potential practical value for brain imaging analysis. The experimental results show that the spatial regularization leads to more interpretable brain maps compared to standard dictionary learning. The computational efficiency claim (3x slowdown) is reasonable for the added complexity.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Inconsistencies
The most concerning issue is a mathematical inconsistency in equation (4), where the authors define the Laplacian penalty as:
ΩLap(v) := 1/p ∑(∇xv)²k + (∇yv)²k + (∇zv)²k = 1/2 v^TΔv

This equality is mathematically incorrect. The left-hand side (with 1/p factor) does not equal the right-hand side (1/2 v^TΔv) for any reasonable definition of the Laplacian. The 1/p factor should not be present in the standard Laplacian penalty formulation. This error undermines the technical credibility of the method.

### Overstated Computational Complexity
The paper claims "only a factor of 2 or 3 on the running time" compared to SODL, but the results in Section 6 show Smooth-SODL takes 340s vs SODL's 110s, which is approximately 3.1x slower, not "only a factor of 2 or 3." This is a significant overstatement that misrepresents the computational cost.

### Inconsistent Terminology and Definitions
The paper uses inconsistent terminology throughout (e.g., "dictionary atoms," "component maps," "latent factors"). More importantly, the definition of "structured" atoms is vague: "piece-wise smooth and compact, thus making up blobs." This lacks mathematical precision and doesn't distinguish from other regularization approaches. The paper also fails to define "spatially-localized" or how to quantify it.

### Implementation Details
The paper claims "a single iteration is sufficient for the dictionary update sub-routine," but provides no evidence or justification for this claim. The convergence analysis is superficial and doesn't address whether the single iteration is sufficient for theoretical convergence guarantees.

### Experimental Methodology
The authors compare against CanICA with a key difference: they applied smoothing to CanICA data but not to SODL/Smooth-SODL. This creates an unfair comparison, as smoothing would affect both methods similarly. The paper should have either applied smoothing to all methods or explained why it's necessary only for CanICA.

### Parameter Selection
The paper mentions "care must be taken in selecting the Laplacian regularization parameter γ" but provides no clear guidance on how to choose it in practice. The hyperparameter tuning section lacks sufficient detail about the cross-validation procedure and the search space.

### Formatting Issues
The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies:
- Inconsistent use of "L1" vs "l1" and "L2" vs "l2"
- Incomplete references (e.g., "MICCAI, , , " with missing year)
- Redundant phrasing ("Nilearn open-source library Python library")
- Mathematical expressions with inconsistent spacing and formatting

## Recommendation
While the paper addresses a meaningful problem and contains some valuable insights, the technical inconsistencies (particularly the mathematical error in equation 4) and overstated computational claims are serious concerns. The lack of precise definitions for key concepts like "structured" atoms and the inconsistent experimental methodology further undermine the paper's credibility. The formatting issues, while minor, indicate a lack of attention to detail that is inconsistent with the high standards expected at NIPS.

Given the paper's technical flaws and the fact that the core contribution (adding Laplacian regularization to dictionary learning) is a relatively straightforward extension of existing work, I recommend rejection. The authors should address these technical issues and provide a more rigorous mathematical foundation before considering resubmission to NIPS.
