## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel method, Smooth-SODL, for automatically discovering brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data (fMRI in this case). The core idea is to leverage inter-subject variability to define spatially localized and sparse regions, extending the online dictionary learning (SODL) framework. The key contribution lies in incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness alongside sparsity in the learned dictionary atoms, aiming for more interpretable and biologically plausible regions than standard dictionary learning or ICA-based approaches. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of Smooth-SODL on a large HCP dataset, showing improvements in explained variance, stability, and predictive performance compared to existing methods like CanICA and standard SODL.

**Related Work:**

The paper effectively situates itself within the context of brain region definition, contrasting its approach with traditional ROI-based methods and clustering techniques. It correctly highlights the limitations of univariate analyses and the challenges of cross-modality segmentation. The connection to existing dictionary learning and sparse coding methods in neuroimaging is well-established, particularly referencing [16] as a foundational work. The authors also accurately position their contribution relative to ICA/PCA-based approaches and the more recent work leveraging structured sparsity, acknowledging the computational challenges of prior methods.  They demonstrate awareness of relevant work on online learning and graph-based regularization techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for spatial regularization is a valuable and novel contribution. This addresses a key limitation of standard dictionary learning in neuroimaging – the tendency to produce spatially fragmented and less interpretable components.
* **Scalability:**  The online learning approach is crucial for handling the large-scale datasets common in neuroimaging (high voxel count and large subject numbers).  The authors successfully adapt the existing SODL framework while maintaining computational efficiency.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the Smooth-SODL model and justifies the choice of penalties (L1 for sparsity, Laplacian for smoothness). The discussion of convergence and the relationship to block-coordinate descent is sound.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The comparisons with CanICA and SODL are well-designed, and the results across multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, prediction) are compelling. The analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and the learning curves provide useful insights.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The technical details are presented clearly, and the motivation for the approach is well-explained. The inclusion of implementation details and planned availability of code (via Nilearn) is a positive aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter tuning:** While the authors provide guidance on hyperparameter selection (α, γ, τ), the process still appears somewhat ad-hoc.  A more systematic exploration of the parameter space and potentially an automated tuning strategy could strengthen the results. The impact of different constraint sets 'C' beyond the simplex is not explored.
* **Comparison to state-of-the-art:** While comparisons to CanICA and SODL are valuable, a comparison to more recent deep learning-based approaches for brain region discovery or segmentation (e.g., autoencoders, variational autoencoders) would be beneficial. Though the authors rightfully state these methods have different objectives, a discussion of the relative strengths and weaknesses would strengthen the paper.
* **Biological validation:** The paper focuses primarily on statistical performance. While the qualitative assessment of the dictionaries reveals some plausible brain regions, a more rigorous biological validation (e.g., comparing the discovered regions to known anatomical structures or functional networks) would add significant value.
* **Computational cost:** While the authors address computational efficiency, the 3x slowdown compared to vanilla SODL is significant. Further optimization or exploration of alternative solvers could be worthwhile. A more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of each step would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in neuroimaging.
* The method is scalable and demonstrates strong empirical performance on a large, publicly available dataset.
* The writing is clear, and the paper is well-organized.
* The potential impact on the field of neuroimaging is significant, offering a data-driven approach to brain region discovery.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The hyperparameter tuning process could be more systematic.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art methods could be more comprehensive.
* The lack of rigorous biological validation limits the interpretability of the results.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a **strong paper** with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed Smooth-SODL method is a valuable addition to the toolkit for automated brain region discovery. While there are some areas for improvement (particularly regarding hyperparameter tuning and biological validation), the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. I recommend that the authors address the points raised regarding hyperparameter tuning, state-of-the-art comparisons, and biological validation. Including a discussion of these points in a revised manuscript would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
