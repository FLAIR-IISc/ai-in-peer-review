This paper tackles a crucial problem in neuroimaging: leveraging inter-subject variability to define meaningful brain regions, moving beyond purely data-driven or anatomy-based ROI definitions. The core idea – using structured sparse dictionary learning to uncover these regions – is genuinely novel and potentially impactful. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches (univariate methods, standard ROI definitions, clustering) and position their work as a principled alternative rooted in signal processing principles. The extension of online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization is a clever way to enforce spatial coherence, which is critical for neuroimaging applications.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the mathematical formulation of Smooth-SODL. The connection to related work, including PCA/ICA, clustering, and existing dictionary learning methods, is well-established. The experimental setup using the HCP dataset is appropriate, and the comparison to CanICA and standard SODL provides valuable insights.  The discussion of hyperparameter selection and the convergence analysis are also commendable.

However, while the *idea* is strong, the validation feels somewhat incomplete. The quantitative results (Table 1, Fig. 2) show improvements, but some are marginal. The discussion acknowledges overfitting in the RAW model, but a deeper analysis of the trade-offs between sparsity (γ) and explained variance would be helpful. While the authors demonstrate that Smooth-SODL begins with substantially higher explained variance, the asymptotic convergence to similar performance as SODL is a concern – does the spatial regularization ultimately provide a lasting benefit, or does it primarily aid initial convergence?

A minor weakness is the somewhat verbose presentation of the algorithms. Alg. 1 and Alg. 2 could be more concise, and the details of the FISTA implementation, while mentioned, could be elaborated upon. The explanation of why the Lipschitz constant might cause issues if γ is too large, while correct, requires a deeper understanding of optimization and might be difficult for some readers to grasp.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of combining structured sparsity with online dictionary learning for ROI definition is genuinely new and represents a significant departure from traditional methods.
*   **Potential Impact:** If successful, this method could automate and improve the process of defining brain regions, leading to more robust and interpretable neuroimaging analyses.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is well-defined and builds upon established signal processing principles.
*   **Demonstrated Improvements:** The experiments show consistent, though sometimes modest, improvements over existing methods.
*   **Open-source commitment:** Planned release of the implementation is a great plus.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Incomplete Validation:** The quantitative results, while positive, are not overwhelmingly convincing. More extensive validation, perhaps with different datasets or cognitive tasks, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Marginal Gains:** Some of the performance improvements are relatively small, raising questions about the practical significance of the method.
*   **Computational Cost:** The increase in running time (3x slower than SODL) is not insignificant, and the authors should discuss strategies for further optimization.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While they address it, the sensitivity to hyperparameter choices requires careful consideration, and a more automated method for parameter selection would be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor flaws and incomplete validation, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach and its potential impact outweigh the limitations. The authors have tackled a challenging problem with a creative and theoretically sound solution. I lean towards **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors should consider addressing the concerns raised above in a future iteration of the work or a response to reviewers. The boldness of the approach and the potential for significant impact are key factors in my decision. The paper introduces a fresh perspective on ROI definition and provides a strong foundation for future research.
