

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning that incorporates spatial structure via Laplacian regularization to learn interpretable brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors address the challenge of defining brain regions in a principled way by leveraging inter-subject variability in statistical maps, rather than relying on anatomical segmentation or predefined ROIs. The approach extends the online dictionary learning framework [16] by adding a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms while maintaining sparsity through an L1 penalty. The authors demonstrate their method on HCP task fMRI data (500 subjects, 260k voxels) and compare against SODL [16] and CanICA [22].

**Strengths:**
1. The problem of principled brain region definition is important and well-motivated, with clear relevance to neuroimaging practice. The paper effectively contextualizes the work within previous approaches to ROI definition in brain imaging [18, 21, 14].

2. The technical contribution is well-executed and clearly articulated. The extension of online dictionary learning with spatial structure via Laplacian regularization is novel and properly positioned relative to prior work on structured dictionary learning [1, 25] and brain imaging [23].

3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple dimensions of performance: visual quality of dictionaries, explained variance, stability across data splits, and predictive performance for behavioral variables. The learning curve analysis (Table 1) provides strong evidence for the value of spatial regularization in data-scarce regimes.

4. The computational efficiency claim (2-3x slower than standard SODL) is well-supported by empirical results, making the method practical for large-scale neuroimaging applications.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper's claim of "large-scale" applicability is not sufficiently demonstrated. The experiments are limited to the HCP dataset (500 subjects, 260k voxels), and the paper doesn't show how the method scales to larger datasets (e.g., 10,000+ subjects or higher-resolution images) that would truly test the "large-scale" claim.

2. The comparison with CanICA is potentially misleading. CanICA requires pre-smoothing (isotropic FWHM of 6mm) as a necessary preprocessing step, while the authors didn't apply this to their methods. This creates an uneven playing field, as the spatial structure in the data is effectively introduced to CanICA but not to the other methods.

3. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the method generalizes beyond the specific fMRI task used in the experiments. The authors claim the approach could be applied to other neuroimaging modalities, but provide no evidence or discussion of this.

4. The hyperparameter selection discussion is somewhat superficial. While the authors acknowledge the difficulty of parameter selection, they don't provide a principled method for choosing these parameters in practice beyond k-fold cross-validation with explained variance, which may not be feasible in all applications.

5. The paper's claim that spatial regularization isn't needed when data is abundant (HCP) contradicts the learning curve results (Table 1), which show a clear advantage for Smooth-SODL even as data size increases. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of this apparent contradiction.

**Overall assessment:**
This is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of brain region definition in neuroimaging. The technical extension to incorporate spatial structure into online dictionary learning is sound, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the value of the approach, particularly in data-scarce regimes. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and properly contextualized within the literature.

The main weaknesses are related to the scope of the experiments (not demonstrating true large-scale performance) and the potentially misleading comparison with CanICA. However, these are addressable with additional experiments and clarifications.

**Recommendation:**
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to a well-motivated problem in neuroimaging, with a technically sound approach that is well-supported by experiments. The weaknesses identified are not fundamental to the contribution but could be addressed in a revised version to strengthen the paper further. The method has clear potential for impact in the neuroimaging community and aligns well with NIPS' interest in scalable machine learning for scientific applications.

**Suggestions for improvement:**
1. Include additional experiments demonstrating scalability to larger datasets (e.g., 10,000+ subjects) to better support the "large-scale" claim.
2. Clarify the comparison with CanICA by either applying the same pre-processing to all methods or explaining why the pre-processing is necessary only for CanICA.
3. Discuss how the method might be adapted for other neuroimaging modalities beyond fMRI.
4. Provide more concrete guidance for hyperparameter selection in practice, beyond k-fold cross-validation.
5. Clarify the relationship between data abundance and the benefit of spatial regularization, addressing the apparent contradiction in the learning curve results.
