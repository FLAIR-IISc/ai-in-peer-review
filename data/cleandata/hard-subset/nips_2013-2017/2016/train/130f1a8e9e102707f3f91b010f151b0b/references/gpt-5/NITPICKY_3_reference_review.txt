Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning framework for neuroimaging that augments Mairal et al.’s Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (SODL) with a Laplacian smoothness penalty on the atoms and an ℓ1 (or simplex) constraint to induce sparsity/positivity. The goal is to learn spatially localized “blob-like” components from large-scale multi-subject brain maps that capture inter-subject variability. The optimization is tackled by block-coordinate descent: per-sample ridge-coded updates for U and per-atom constrained proximal updates for V. The atom subproblem reduces to a GraphNet-style denoising proximal step solved with FISTA. Experiments on HCP task fMRI (n=500, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA on explained variance, visual quality, stability, runtime, and behavioral prediction.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant problem: learning structured, interpretable brain regions from large-scale data.
- Clear conceptual extension: combining ℓ1/positivity and Laplacian smoothness on atoms within an online dictionary-learning scheme.
- Reasonable computational story: per-atom proximal updates with a bounded Lipschitz constant and costs roughly 2–3× SODL in wall time (on the reported setup).
- Empirical evaluation touches multiple axes (reconstruction, stability, prediction) on a public high-quality dataset.
- Practical insights on hyperparameters and qualitative benefits of spatial regularization.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and algorithmic details
- Inconsistency about time-varying hyperparameters: the algorithmic convergence claims are for a fixed objective (as in [16]), yet α is later chosen time-varying (αt ∼ t−1/2) and γ is suggested to be time-varying via Eq. (9). Optimizing a moving target invalidates the stated convergence to a local optimum of (2). Either analyze the continuation scheme or fix the parameters during optimization.
- Lipschitz constant and “blow-up” claim: For Fγ̃(v,a)=1/2||v−a||^2+1/2 γ̃ v^T Δ v, ∇F= (I+γ̃Δ)v − a, so L = 1 + γ̃ λmax(Δ). You set γ̃ = γ (At[j,j]/t)−1. If At[j,j]/t converges to a positive constant (as expected), L remains bounded; a blow-up is not inevitable. The recommendation γt = O(∥At∥∞,∞/t) is not justified and conflicts with optimizing a fixed objective.
- “Parameter free” claim for BCD is misleading: the atom subproblem is solved by FISTA, which needs a stepsize 1/L (or equivalent). Also, the inner loop’s stopping criteria are unspecified yet runtime comparisons depend on it.
- “1–2 FISTA iterations reach accuracy 10^-6”: not credible for first-order methods on large p unless L and conditioning are extraordinarily favorable. Elsewhere, you say a single iteration is “sufficient” qualitatively. These statements conflict and need careful empirical or theoretical backup.
- Ridge coding via SVD of V: For k=40, a Cholesky of (V^T V+αI) is simpler/cheaper than repeated SVDs. Also, complexity details are missing (per-iteration flops, memory).
- FFT acceleration: briefly mentioned but not developed. With positivity/simplex constraints and non-circulant boundary conditions, FFT-based diagonalization of Δ does not directly yield a closed-form proximal; clarifying assumptions is necessary.

Modeling choices
- Laplacian promotes smoothness but not compact support. Claiming it “imposes blobs” is only true in conjunction with sparsity and nonnegativity; otherwise Laplacian alone can diffuse mass. A comparison to TV-based penalties (which more directly encourage spatial compactness) would strengthen claims. You cite TV-ℓ1 works but do not compare experimentally.
- Using simplex Sp(τ) (nonnegativity + ℓ1 bound) for V but comparing against CanICA (which permits signed maps) confounds interpretability and fairness. Consider SODL/Smooth-SODL with and without nonnegativity, and a nonnegative ICA baseline.

Experimental design and reporting
- Pre-smoothing only for CanICA (6mm FWHM) but not for SODL/Smooth-SODL biases comparisons. Either apply the same preprocessing or demonstrate robustness of each method under matched conditions.
- Explained variance on test data: clarify whether codes for test subjects were refit using ridge coding with hyperparameters fixed from training only. Specify normalization/centering steps.
- Stability metric: define precisely (spatial Pearson correlation? sign and permutation handling? thresholding?). The reported numbers (0.4 vs 0.1) lack error bars and methodological detail.
- Behavioral prediction: potential leakage. RAW baseline “top 2000 voxels most correlated with the target” must be selected on training folds only; the text does not state this. Hyperparameters for each method (α, γ, τ) are tuned via K-fold CV on explained variance, not on prediction performance; ensure a properly nested CV for prediction.
- Learning curves and Table 1 are referenced, but Table 1 is missing. Figures are not included in the submission; the text descriptions are insufficient to assess results (no standard deviations, CIs, or statistical tests).
- Runtime comparisons lack clear stopping criteria for the inner atom updates and equalization of passes T across methods.

Clarity, notation, and formatting
- Numerous typos and notation inconsistencies hinder readability: “intepretable,” “comput,” missing spaces, V j vs Vj, Xt vs Xi, “max Ui” (should be min), “jth” vs “j th,” “At[j, j] −1,” etc.
- Equation (4): ΩLap has both a componentwise sum and “= 1/2 v^T Δ v”; typically ||∇v||^2 = v^T Δ v without the 1/2; define Δ and boundary conditions precisely.
- Algorithm 2 uses r in the for-loop though k is the number of atoms. Stopping criteria unspecified.
- Section numbering skips “3.2” and has “.2 Practical considerations.” References lack years/venues and are inconsistently formatted. Some attributions are incorrect (“Human Connectome Projection”).

Originality and related work
- While integrating Laplacian smoothness into online dictionary learning for neuroimaging is useful, graph/Laplacian-regularized dictionary learning exists in ML/CV literature and is not cited. Position your contribution relative to general graph-regularized DL and to structured sparsity beyond TV (e.g., group/graph sparsity on atoms).
- The connection to GraphNet is noted but not fully exploited (e.g., no comparison to GraphNet for predictive tasks).

Pros for acceptance
- Addressed a relevant, large-scale neuroimaging problem with a scalable method.
- Empirical evidence suggests improved interpretability and stability at moderate computational overhead.
- Potentially useful to practitioners; integration into Nilearn would enhance impact.

Cons for acceptance
- Multiple technical inconsistencies (time-varying hyperparameters vs convergence claims; Lipschitz/blow-up argument; FISTA iteration claims).
- Experimental comparisons have fairness and leakage concerns; missing details, figures, and statistics.
- Significant clarity, notation, and formatting issues; incomplete references.
- Limited novelty beyond known graph-regularized DL; insufficient positioning versus related general ML work.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is promising and likely useful, but the paper in its current form has too many methodological inconsistencies, unclear algorithmic details, and presentation issues to merit acceptance. A revised version addressing the above concerns, with rigorous and fair experiments and improved clarity, could be strong.

Confidence
High.
