Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning method for neuroimaging that adds a Laplacian smoothness penalty on dictionary atoms to the sparse-atom setting of Mairal et al. (SODL) while keeping codes dense via ridge regression. The goal is to learn spatially localized, blob-like, interpretable components (putative ROIs) that capture inter-subject variability in statistical maps (e.g., task fMRI GLM Z-maps). The algorithm uses standard online block-coordinate descent: ridge coding via SVD of the current dictionary; maintenance of sufficient statistics (A, B); and per-atom convex updates that solve a smooth quadratic with a convex constraint (simplex or ℓ1-ball) using FISTA with projection. The authors argue the online convergence results of [16] extend, and they analyze implementation details (Lipschitz constants, the role of γ, projection cost). On HCP task-fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40), they compare Smooth-SODL against SODL and CanICA. Smooth-SODL yields more structured, sparser, visually cleaner atoms, higher stability/reproducibility across splits than CanICA and SODL, better early-sample explained variance than SODL, and competitive predictive performance on behavioral targets. Runtime is ≈3× SODL and faster than CanICA.

Relative to prior work, this sits between spatially structured decompositions with TV/GraphNet penalties [1,11,23,25], which have been slow in practice, and scalable online sparse dictionary learning [16,17] that typically lacks spatial structure. The key contribution is integrating a Laplacian penalty into the online SODL framework while keeping per-atom updates computationally light (projected FISTA) and retaining online convergence guarantees.

Assessment by NIPS criteria

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is reasonable and the per-block subproblems are convex, so per-atom updates achieve global optima. The use of ridge codes ensures uniqueness and stability of U. The extension of online convergence claims from [16] is plausible given bounded data, unique code solutions, and strongly convex surrogate with the Laplacian term, although a formal adaptation of the proof (with the additional smooth regularizer) should be sketched in the main text or supplement.
- Algorithmic details: The FISTA-based atom update with projection onto C is standard; computing L=1+γ̃·λmax(Δ) is correct (their bound 1+4Dγ̃ is reasonable for grid Laplacians). However, the claim that “1–2 iterations of FISTA suffice to reach 1e-6 accuracy” is not credible in general, especially when γ is large and L is high; in practice early stopping may work, but the stated numerical accuracy seems unrealistic. The guidance on γ to avoid vanishing step sizes is helpful, but it would be better to either (a) adopt an explicit time-varying γt schedule and evaluate it, or (b) fix γ and report actual inner iteration counts.
- Experiments: The HCP experiment is well chosen and demonstrates scalability. The suite of metrics (visual quality, explained variance on train/test, stability via atom matching, behavioral prediction) is appropriate. Some evaluation choices could bias baselines (e.g., smoothing only for CanICA), and statistical significance (CIs or tests) is missing. Table 1 and some details alluded to in text are not visible; more complete reporting is needed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable with a clear high-level story and algorithm description. There are several typos/formatting glitches, incomplete references (missing years/venues), and missing figure/table details. The derivation of the online surrogate and the exact constrained FISTA step are sketched but could use a concise, explicit statement of the composite objective used in the inner loop (smooth quadratic + indicator of C) and the projection operator for the chosen C (simplex vs ℓ1-ball).
- The fairness choices in preprocessing (smoothing for CanICA only) should be justified more explicitly. Reporting hyperparameter ranges and selection procedures (for α, γ, τ) would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The idea of imposing spatial structure on dictionary atoms is not new (TV, GraphNet, structured sparsity PCA/ICA), and using Laplacian smoothness is standard. The main novelty is integrating this penalty into the scalable online SODL framework with minimal additional cost and preserving convergence behavior. This is an incremental but practically meaningful contribution, particularly for large neuroimaging datasets.
- Related work is discussed, though some closely related structured sparse PCA/ICA and modern fast TV solvers are only briefly mentioned. A clearer positioning against [1,14,23,25] with quantitative comparisons would strengthen claims.

Significance
- The problem—learning interpretable, stable population-level spatial components at scale—is important in neuroimaging. The method appears to provide better interpretability and stability than SODL and CanICA at comparable accuracy, and to scale to hundreds of subjects and hundreds of thousands of voxels. If released in Nilearn, it will likely be adopted by practitioners.
- However, the strongest metric (behavioral prediction) shows CanICA best on test R2; Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL and tCanICA but not CanICA. This tempers the claim of overall superiority; the primary benefit appears to be interpretability and stability rather than pure predictive performance.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant, large-scale problem with a practical, efficient algorithm and clear benefits in interpretability and stability.
- Solid integration of a spatial smoothness prior into an online framework with reasonable convergence justification.
- Demonstrates scalability on HCP; runtime overhead over SODL is modest; code slated for open-source release.
- Empirical results show improved early-sample performance and better stability/reproducibility.

Cons for acceptance
- Methodological novelty is incremental; Laplacian regularization is standard, and theoretical development is light.
- Some experimental choices and reporting are incomplete: lack of statistical tests/CIs, missing ablations, limited baselines (no TV-ℓ1 or structured sparse PCA), preprocessing asymmetry (smoothing for ICA only).
- Claims about FISTA inner-loop accuracy and γ tuning are not fully convincing; more rigorous or empirical support is needed.
- Clarity/polish issues (typos, missing details, incomplete references).

Suggestions to improve
- Add statistical uncertainty on EV and prediction metrics; report multiple splits; include ablations over γ, τ, α schedules; analyze effect of k.
- Compare to a TV-ℓ1 decomposition (even if slower) on a subset, and to structured sparse PCA/GraphNet; include MELODIC with standard thresholding pipelines.
- Justify preprocessing choices; where possible, equalize smoothing across methods.
- Provide a short convergence argument specialized to the added Laplacian term; report actual inner-loop iteration counts.
- Release code at submission time for reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The contribution is incremental but useful, technically sound overall, and likely to be impactful for practitioners in neuroimaging. Strengths in scalability and interpretability outweigh the weaknesses in theoretical novelty and evaluation completeness. Confidence: moderate.
