Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework tailored to neuroimaging that enforces both sparsity and spatial structure on dictionary atoms. Building on the online sparse dictionary learning of Mairal et al. (SODL), the authors add a Laplacian penalty on atoms to promote smooth, blob-like, spatially localized components while retaining an online algorithm that scales to massive datasets. Codes are obtained via ridge regression (with an option for L1), atoms are constrained within an L1-type set (simplex), and atom updates are solved by a few FISTA iterations with warm starts inside a block-coordinate descent loop. The approach is positioned as a fast, scalable alternative to prior structured decompositions (e.g., TV-ℓ1/GraphNet, structured sparse PCA) that are typically solved by slow alternating minimization. Experiments on HCP task fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to vanilla SODL and CanICA, reporting improved interpretability/structure, higher split-half stability, competitive explained variance, reasonable runtime (≈3× SODL, faster than CanICA), and competitive prediction of behavioral variables. A study of hyperparameters and learning curves suggests that spatial regularization provides clear benefits, especially with limited data.

Relation to prior work
The paper sits at the intersection of online matrix factorization (Mairal; Mensch), structured sparsity for imaging (GraphNet; TV-ℓ1), and neuroimaging decompositions (CanICA/MELODIC; data-driven atlases). Prior structured DL for brain maps (e.g., TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning) typically relied on slow alternating solvers; Smooth-SODL contributes an online, scalable variant by embedding a Laplacian penalty into the ODL framework with efficient per-atom updates. Compared to structured sparse PCA (Jenatton) and GraphNet regression (Grosenick), the contribution targets dictionary atoms directly with Laplacian smoothing and demonstrates scale on HCP.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and pragmatic impact: Integrating spatial smoothness into large-scale online dictionary learning is a valuable direction for neuroimaging where interpretability and ROI-like atoms matter. The proposed formulation is simple, principled, and likely to be adopted (Nilearn integration).
- Scalability: The approach remains only 2–3× slower than SODL in wall-clock, and faster than a batch method (CanICA) on the reported setup. The mini-batch coding and per-atom FISTA updates are practical.
- Empirical validation at scale: Experiments on 500 subjects with comprehensive metrics (explained variance, stability across splits via Hungarian matching, behavioral prediction) are appreciated. Learning curves nicely illustrate benefits of spatial regularization in low/medium data regimes.
- Interpretability and stability: Qualitative maps look cleaner and more localized than SODL; quantitative stability (mean atom correlation ≈0.4 vs 0.1 for SODL) is a meaningful gain.
- Transparent discussion of hyperparameters and practicalities (αt schedule, safe γ, τ trade-offs).

Weaknesses and questions
- Theoretical details light/possibly imprecise:
  - The Lipschitz constant of the atom subproblem is stated as 1+4Dγ̃; in general it should be 1+γ̃λmax(Δ), which depends on boundary conditions, voxel spacing, and grid size. Please clarify assumptions and tighten the bound.
  - The guideline γt=O(||A||∞,∞/t) to prevent vanishing steps is heuristic; can you provide a derivation or at least empirical sensitivity plots?
  - Claim that “1–2 iterations of FISTA reach 1e-6 accuracy” is hard to believe; perhaps due to warm starts and small γ. Please either report the actual suboptimality vs iterations or rephrase to emphasize practical sufficiency rather than numerical tolerance.
- Comparisons: Missing baselines that are closer in spirit:
  - TV-regularized dictionary learning for fMRI (e.g., Abraham et al., MICCAI) and GraphNet-like decompositions.
  - Structured sparse PCA/NMF with Laplacian/TV regularization.
  - Recent online matrix factorization variants (e.g., Mensch) as a runtime/accuracy reference.
  Including at least one TV-ℓ1 baseline would strengthen the claim that Laplacian is a sweet-spot between structure and speed.
- Evaluation scope and fairness:
  - Only a single HCP task contrast is shown. A resting-state or a second task would help assess robustness.
  - The CanICA comparison relies on thresholded maps (tCanICA) for interpretability; the choice of threshold can strongly affect stability. Please standardize thresholds (e.g., mixture-model or fixed z-score) and report sensitivity.
  - Interpretability is largely qualitative. Consider simple quantitative proxies (e.g., spatial compactness, number/size of connected components, Gini index).
- Reproducibility: Parameters (τ, γ ranges, final selected values), stopping criteria for inner FISTA and outer BCD, and exact preprocessing would be helpful. Code is “soon” to be released; a link at submission time would improve reproducibility.

Quality
The method is technically sound at a high level; the optimization is convex per block and the overall scheme mirrors ODL. Some formulae and convergence statements need tightening, and a few claims appear overly strong. The empirical study is solid, but could be broadened and include more appropriate baselines.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. There are typos and minor notation issues (e.g., inconsistent use of max/min in code updates, missing subscripts/superscripts in places). The derivation of Eq. (7) could be expanded in the main text. Please clarify constraints C and projection details, and specify boundary conditions for ∆.

Originality
Moderately high. While Laplacian/TV-regularized decompositions are known, bringing a Laplacian prior into online DL with efficient atom updates and demonstrating scalability and stability on large neuroimaging data is a useful step forward.

Significance
High for neuroimaging practitioners. A fast, structured, online decomposition that yields interpretable, stable maps at HCP scale is valuable. The ideas may carry to other high-dimensional spatial domains.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, useful idea bridging structured regularization and online DL at scale
- Empirical gains in stability/interpretability with modest runtime overhead
- Practical guidance and planned open-source release

Cons against acceptance
- Missing key structured baselines (TV-ℓ1/GraphNet/structured SPCA)
- Some theoretical/algorithmic claims need correction or evidence
- Evaluation on a single dataset/contrast and interpretability quantified mostly qualitatively

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a timely and practically impactful variant of online dictionary learning that meaningfully improves interpretability and stability for large-scale neuroimaging, with reasonable computational cost. Addressing the theoretical clarifications and adding at least one structured baseline would solidify the contribution.
