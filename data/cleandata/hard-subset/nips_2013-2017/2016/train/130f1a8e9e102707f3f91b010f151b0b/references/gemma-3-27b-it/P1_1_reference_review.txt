## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), for automatically discovering spatially coherent brain regions from fMRI data. The core idea is to leverage population variability to define regions, extending online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization to encourage spatially smooth and sparse dictionary atoms ("blobs").  The authors motivate this approach by highlighting the limitations of traditional ROI definition methods and the need for data-driven approaches that account for inter-subject variability. They demonstrate the method on a large HCP dataset, comparing it to ICA-based approaches (CanICA) and standard SODL, and provide insights into hyperparameter selection.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The work builds upon the existing literature on dictionary learning for neuroimaging, specifically extending the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2012). It connects to research on clustering-based region definition (e.g., Baldassarre et al., 2011) and ICA/PCA-based methods for functional decomposition (Beckmann & Smith, 2009). However, it distinguishes itself by incorporating spatial regularization within an online learning framework to handle large datasets efficiently. The authors correctly position their work as bridging the gap between scalable online learning and the need for spatially structured decompositions, a combination previously hindered by computational costs. The related work section adequately summarizes the field.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization for spatial structure is a significant contribution. This allows for scalable learning of spatially coherent features directly from large-scale neuroimaging data.
*   **Scalability:** The online learning approach is crucial for handling the high dimensionality and large sample sizes common in neuroimaging. The authors demonstrate that while it introduces a computational overhead compared to vanilla SODL, it remains significantly faster than alternative methods like CanICA.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the problem and discuss the convergence properties of their algorithm.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improved performance in terms of explained variance, stability, and predictive power compared to standard SODL and CanICA.  The ablation study investigating the impact of the regularization parameter γ is particularly insightful.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper provides valuable practical guidance on hyperparameter selection and initialization, addressing a common challenge in dictionary learning.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Exploration of Alternative Spatial Regularization:** While the Laplacian penalty effectively promotes smoothness, the authors do not explore other possible spatial regularizers (e.g., total variation) or compare their performance.
*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** Despite providing guidance, selecting the optimal hyperparameters (α, γ, τ) still appears to require careful tuning and potentially cross-validation. A more automated hyperparameter optimization strategy could be beneficial.
*   **Interpretability of Dictionary Atoms:** While the authors mention the interpretability of the learned regions, a more in-depth qualitative analysis of the functional significance of specific dictionary atoms would strengthen the paper.  Connecting the atoms to known brain networks in more detail is needed.
*   **Minor Clarity Issue:** The transition between theoretical description and algorithmic details could be smoother. Alg. 1 and Alg. 2 are presented without sufficient explanation of the roles of the historical statistics A and B.
*   **Discussion of Assumptions:** The paper assumes that data is compactly supported, but doesn't elaborate on the impact of violations of this assumption in the context of fMRI data.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis is adequate, and the experimental results are convincing. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their work.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, though some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations.
*   **Originality:** The combination of online dictionary learning and Laplacian regularization is novel and represents a significant advance in the field.
*   **Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to impact the way brain regions are defined and analyzed in neuroimaging research. The method offers a promising alternative to traditional ROI-based approaches.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

*   Addresses a challenging problem in neuroimaging (data-driven ROI definition).
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound method.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical results on a large, publicly available dataset.
*   Provides valuable practical guidance.

**Against:**

*   Limited exploration of alternative spatial regularization techniques.
*   Hyperparameter tuning can be challenging.
*   Further exploration of the functional significance of the learned regions is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed method is novel, scalable, and performs well on a challenging dataset. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS.



**Recommendation:**  **Accept**



**Confidence:** 8/10



**Additional Comments:**

I recommend the authors consider addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above in a revised version of the paper.  Specifically, a more detailed discussion of the functional significance of the learned dictionary atoms and a brief exploration of alternative spatial regularizers would further strengthen the work. Providing more detail on the roles of A and B in the algorithms would also improve clarity.
