Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging that enforces spatially localized, “blob-like” atoms by adding a Laplacian (graph-smoothness) penalty on dictionary atoms and an l1-type sparsity-inducing constraint (l1-ball or simplex). Codes are updated via ridge regression; atoms are updated by block coordinate descent with an inner projected proximal gradient (FISTA) step to handle the Laplacian-regularized quadratic plus simple constraint. The method is positioned as a scalable extension of Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (SODL, Mairal et al. 2010), with modest computational overhead. Experiments on HCP task-fMRI (n=500, one contrast) compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA, reporting improved spatial structure and stability, similar explained variance to SODL (below CanICA), and modest advantages in some behavioral predictions.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on well-known online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., JMLR 2010) and matrix factorization (Mensch et al., ICML 2016).
- Uses Laplacian/GraphNet-style smoothness, related to GraphNet (Grosenick et al.), structured sparse PCA (Jenatton et al.), and prior neuroimaging decompositions with TV-l1/GraphNet (Abraham; Varoquaux et al.; Dohmatob).
- The idea of graph/Laplacian-regularized sparse coding/dictionary learning has a broader ML/signal processing literature beyond neuroimaging (graph-regularized sparse coding/dictionary learning), which should be cited and contrasted; the paper currently under-cites this area.

Strengths
- Practical, scalable extension: integrates a spatial regularizer into an online DL routine with modest overhead (2–3x SODL).
- Addresses a real need in neuroimaging: obtaining spatially coherent, interpretable group-level components without ad hoc pre-smoothing.
- Uses a large public dataset (HCP), and evaluates multiple criteria (explained variance, stability, prediction).
- Implementation intent in Nilearn enhances potential impact and reproducibility (if actually released).

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological clarity and rigor
- The optimization section has inconsistencies and overstatements. Claiming “parameter-free” BCD (no step size) ignores the fact that the atom update uses FISTA, which requires a step-size tied to a Lipschitz constant; a projection-based proximal gradient still depends on this choice. The statement that “1 or 2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient to reach 1e-6 accuracy” is not credible in general and suggests either a near-closed-form solution or an overly loose notion of accuracy.
- The Lipschitz constant for the Laplacian term is stated as 1+4Dγ̃; this bound assumes a regular grid with specific boundary conditions. In practice, brain masks are irregular; the spectral norm of Δ depends on the adjacency on the mask. If the step-size is set from an underestimated bound, convergence guarantees may not hold. Please specify the graph, boundary conditions, and how L is computed robustly.
- Convergence claims are tied to [16], but the proposed method departs in key ways (structured penalty, constrained projected inner loop, possibly time-varying αt and γt). The text alternates between constant γ (as used in experiments) and a time-varying γt to avoid vanishing step sizes. The theoretical section should clearly state the assumptions under which the online algorithm converges and whether these match the experimental setting.
- The constraint set choices (simplex vs l1-ball) change the problem materially (nonnegativity vs signed atoms). The paper does not motivate the choice (why nonnegativity?), nor quantify its effect on performance or interpretability.

Novelty and positioning
- Adding a Laplacian penalty to dictionary atoms is a natural and known extension; similar structured penalties have been widely used (GraphNet/TV for brain imaging; graph-regularized sparse coding in ML). The main claim to novelty is the efficient online integration. This is incremental; to be suitable for NeurIPS, the work should thoroughly compare and position against graph-regularized/TV dictionary learning baselines and relevant ML literature, not only SODL and CanICA.

Empirical evaluation
- Baselines are limited. Missing are structured/graph-regularized/TV dictionary learning baselines (e.g., TV-l1 DL; GraphNet-based decompositions; structured sparse PCA), as well as clustering-based ROI definitions (Ward, spectral clustering), which are widely used in neuroimaging.
- Generalization is tested on a single task contrast (Math vs Story). Given the claims about robustness and interpretability, additional tasks or datasets would strengthen the case. Also, only k=40 is reported; sensitivity to k is important in practice.
- Hyperparameter selection is under-specified. The paper suggests αt ∼ t^{-1/2} and cautions about γ, but the experiments appear to use fixed γ values (1e3, 1e4). Provide a principled selection protocol (CV across subjects) and quantify sensitivity. The proposed “optimality regime” is only qualitatively described.
- Stability/reproducibility: details on the split protocol, matching (Hungarian), and the exact reproducibility metric are sparse. Report distributions, not only means. The absolute stability gains reported (e.g., 0.4 vs 0.1) need error bars and statistical tests.
- Behavioral prediction: gains over SODL/tCanICA are modest and CanICA often performs best on test R2. This undercuts the central claim that the proposed structure better captures meaningful inter-subject variability. More careful model selection and proper nested CV are needed to ensure fairness; also report confidence intervals.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typos and grammar issues, and some confusing notation (e.g., mixing min and max in the code update bullet; unclear definition lines in Eq. 7). Important algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement, which is essential to assess correctness. The paper needs editing for clarity and completeness (precise definitions of Δ, C, projections, stopping criteria, batch size, number of passes T, memory footprint).

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Practical, scalable method addressing a relevant problem.
- Some evidence of improved spatial structure and stability at modest computational cost.
- Public data and intent to release code.

Cons:
- Incremental novelty; insufficient engagement with established structured DL literature and baselines.
- Theoretical claims are loosely argued; optimization details and convergence conditions are not rigorously established for the proposed setting.
- Empirical validation is limited (single task, limited baselines), and predictive performance does not clearly improve over strong baselines.
- Clarity and reproducibility concerns.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is reasonable and potentially useful, but the paper in its current form lacks rigorous theoretical grounding for the online structured setting, omits key baselines, and provides limited, partly contradictory empirical evidence. Strengthening the theory, expanding baselines (including TV/GraphNet/graph-regularized DL and clustering ROIs), adding multiple tasks, and releasing anonymized code would significantly improve the paper. Confidence: moderate.
