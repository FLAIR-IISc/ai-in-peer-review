- Proposal: Introduces Smooth-SODL — SODL extended with a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness while preserving sparsity for ROI discovery in neuroimaging.

- Key empirical claim: Smooth-SODL is computationally efficient (≈2–3× slower than SODL), yields more structured/ interpretable regions, and improves explained variance, stability, and behavioral prediction, especially on small datasets.

- Technical merit: Well-motivated mathematical formulation with convergence guarantees, careful complexity analysis, and a concrete algorithm.

- Novelty: Meaningful extension of online dictionary learning by adding spatial regularization, faster than prior structured dictionary approaches while maintaining guarantees.

- Experimental rigor: Thorough evaluation across visual quality, explained variance, stability, and behavioral prediction, with compelling learning-curve analyses.

- Practical impact: Method is efficient and planned for release in Nilearn, addressing an important ROI-definition bottleneck in neuroimaging.

- Scope limitation: Paper focuses on neuroimaging and does not assess applicability to other image domains, limiting broader NIPS impact.

- Spatial-scale issue: Laplacian smoothness does not directly control region size; no discussion of handling multi-scale or variable-sized regions.

- Comparison fairness: Comparison with CanICA is potentially unfair since CanICA is a batch method; a batch version of the authors’ method would be a more balanced baseline.

- Theoretical gap: No new theoretical analysis of how the Laplacian penalty affects optimization landscape or statistical properties beyond existing convergence guarantees.

- Hyperparameter sensitivity: No principled method provided for selecting the Laplacian regularization γ; reliance on standard cross-validation is insufficient.

- Recommendation: Strong accept — meets NIPS criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance) and likely to be adopted by neuroimaging researchers.

- Suggested improvements: discuss applicability to other image types; address spatial-scale selection (adaptive or multi-scale); provide theoretical analysis of the Laplacian effect; compare to a batch variant; and give guidance for choosing γ.
