Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning framework for neuroimaging that enforces spatially sparse and smooth atoms by combining an L1-type constraint on atoms with a Laplacian (GraphNet-style) penalty, while using ridge codes for stability. The key claim is that this structured prior can be integrated into the efficient online block-coordinate descent scheme of Mairal et al. (2010) with only a 2–3x runtime overhead, making spatially structured multivariate decompositions feasible at HCP scale. The atom updates reduce to denoising subproblems with Laplacian regularization solvable by projected FISTA; practical guidance for hyperparameters and a schedule to control the Lipschitz constant are discussed. On HCP task fMRI (500 subjects), Smooth-SODL yields more interpretable and more stable components than SODL and CanICA, competitive explained variance, better behavior prediction than SODL and thresholded CanICA, and strong data-efficiency early in the learning curve.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on online dictionary learning (Mairal ’10) and structured penalties for brain imaging (TV-ℓ1, GraphNet). Prior structured decompositions (e.g., Abraham MICCAI ’13; Varoquaux et al.) achieved spatial structure but were slow due to alternating minimization and nonseparable penalties. Here, a Laplacian penalty is chosen for computational tractability in an online setting, with separable atom updates. This bridges a gap between scalable online DL and spatially structured priors. The idea is related to GraphNet denoising (Grosenick) and structured sparsity (Jenatton et al.), but the contribution is the specific online integration and practical recipe for large 3D images.

Strengths
- Conceptual: A clear and pragmatic formulation that combines sparsity and spatial smoothness on atoms, enabling data-driven ROIs that look like “blobs” rather than scattered patterns.
- Algorithmic: Integrates a Laplacian penalty into an online BCD scheme with per-atom projected FISTA, maintaining scalability. The discussion of Lipschitz control and γt scaling is thoughtful.
- Empirical: Solid large-scale experiment on HCP task fMRI with multiple metrics (interpretability, stability, explained variance, prediction, learning curves). The early data-efficiency advantage is compelling. Runtime overhead appears modest.
- Practicality: Reasonable hyperparameter guidance (τ, γ, αt ~ t^-1/2) and promises of a Nilearn implementation increase potential impact.

Weaknesses
- Incrementality: The core idea—Laplacian/GraphNet regularization on atoms—is known; the novelty lies in its efficient online integration. While useful, the conceptual leap is moderate.
- Theoretical gaps: Convergence is argued by analogy to Mairal ’10 under exact per-atom solves, but the implementation uses 1–2 FISTA iterations, which is unlikely to reach 1e-6 accuracy in general. Claims of “global optimum at each iteration” need to be qualified for inexact updates. A formal inexact BCD analysis or empirical sensitivity to inner-loop tolerance would strengthen the work.
- Fairness of baselines: CanICA is reported slower than Smooth-SODL; this likely depends on implementation and preprocessing choices. Also, TV-ℓ1 dictionary-learning or GraphNet-based batch decompositions on a subset would be informative, even if slower, to isolate the benefit of the Laplacian vs. the online scheme.
- Limited task diversity: Results focus on a single HCP contrast; additional tasks or resting-state would test generality. For behavior prediction, more details on the regression pipeline, cross-validation, and leakage control are needed.
- Clarity/polish: Some typos and missing citation details; a few claims (e.g., “1–2 FISTA iterations suffice for 1e-6”) are not credible as stated and should be rephrased.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with reasonable algorithmic choices and good empirical validation. The theoretical discussion is mostly heuristic where it matters (inexact inner solves, time-varying γ and α), but acceptable for an engineering paper if claims are toned down.

Clarity
Readable and well-organized, but could use tighter exposition of Algorithm 2, exact projected FISTA steps, and the γt schedule actually used in experiments. Figures reportedly show good qualitative effects; ensure self-contained captions and report the exact hyperparameters.

Originality
Moderate. The main originality is the efficient online integration of a Laplacian prior for spatially structured atoms at scale. The components (online DL, GraphNet) are known.

Significance
High for neuroimaging practitioners: a scalable, interpretable alternative to ICA/PCA and unstructured DL, with immediate utility and likely adoption (Nilearn). For the broader ML audience, significance is moderate: a strong systems/practice contribution with limited theoretical novelty.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method that enables structured DL at HCP scale.
- Clear gains in interpretability and stability with modest computational overhead.
- Useful guidance and impending open-source release.

Cons for acceptance
- Conceptual novelty is limited; primarily an engineering integration.
- Theoretical claims around convergence/inexactness need clarification.
- Baseline and evaluation breadth could be improved.

Suggestions
- Provide an inexact BCD convergence reference or empirical study varying inner-loop iterations.
- Clarify the actual γt/αt schedules used and report sensitivity.
- Add at least one structured baseline (e.g., TV-ℓ1 DL on a subset) to isolate the role of the Laplacian.
- Expand to another task/rest or provide cross-task reproducibility.
- Tighten statements about accuracy and “global optimum.”

Overall recommendation: Weak accept (6). This is a solid, practically impactful contribution that moves structured DL into the large-scale online regime for neuroimaging. While the novelty is not profound, the paper advances the state of practice in a way likely to be used and built upon.

Confidence: 4/5.
