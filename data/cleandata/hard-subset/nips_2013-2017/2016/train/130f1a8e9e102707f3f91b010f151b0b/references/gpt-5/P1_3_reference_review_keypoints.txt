- Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online dictionary learning model enforcing sparsity and Laplacian smoothness to learn ROI-like atoms for neuroimaging (Xi ≈ VUi) with ridge-coded stability.  
- Uses online block-coordinate updates (Mairal et al. 2010), ridge regression for codes, and proximal/FISTA updates for atoms with discussed Lipschitz constants and mini-batching.  
- Experiments on HCP task-fMRI (n=500, p≈260k, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA, showing more spatially coherent, sparser atoms and better stability/prediction for behavioral tasks.  
- Smooth-SODL incurs ~3× runtime over SODL but is faster than CanICA on a single CPU.  
- Strength: addresses an important practical need for scalable, interpretable brain decompositions respecting spatial structure and inter-subject variability.  
- Strength: solid engineering by integrating a Laplacian penalty into an online DL framework without breaking convergence guarantees.  
- Strength: empirical evidence that Laplacian regularization improves spatial coherence and atom stability, especially at small-to-medium sample sizes.  
- Strength: sensible hyperparameter discussion and planned Nilearn code release increase practical impact.  
- Strength: large-scale, public-dataset evaluation with qualitative and quantitative metrics.  
- Weakness: novelty is incremental—Laplacian/GraphNet-style regularization is known; main novelty is online integration and empirical demonstration.  
- Weakness: baselines are limited—missing principled spatial competitors (e.g., TV-ℓ1 or GraphNet dictionary learning); tCanICA is an inadequate comparator.  
- Weakness: statistical reporting lacks uncertainty estimates, significance testing, and detailed stability/prediction protocols (splits, matching, nested CV).  
- Weakness: some technical claims imprecise or optimistic (e.g., 1–2 FISTA iterations to 1e-6), and Lipschitz bound assumptions need explicit operator/boundary definitions.  
- Weakness: hyperparameter schedules (αt, γt) are heuristic and need ablation/sensitivity analyses to justify choices.  
- Weakness: modeling choices (positivity/simplex, choice of k) require more discussion and ablation to disentangle effects.  
- Weakness: scope limited to one task contrast and k=40; robustness across tasks and k should be shown or justified.  
- Quality: technically sound and plausible convergence, but experiments need stronger baselines, clearer protocols, and more rigorous reporting.  
- Clarity: generally clear and organized but has typos and omits critical implementation details that should be moved from supplement to main text.  
- Originality: moderate—combines known ideas in an online DL context with efficiency gains.  
- Significance: high practical value for neuroimaging adoption, lower conceptual novelty for core ML venues.  
- Arguments for acceptance: practical, scalable method with clear interpretability and stability gains and likely high adoption.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, missing key baselines, heuristic hyperparameters, and insufficient statistical rigor.  
- Questions for authors: specify Laplacian operator and spectral norm, outer passes/mini-batch counts and runtime comparability, γ/τ selection protocol and sensitivity plots, exact stability protocol, rationale for nonnegativity on signed Z-maps, and results’ dependence on k.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept (lean weak accept); strengthen baselines, statistical reporting, and ablation evidence to improve the case.
