## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth-SODL, for learning spatially coherent brain regions from population fMRI data by extending online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization. The core idea – leveraging inter-subject variability to define meaningful ROIs – is conceptually sound and addresses a key challenge in neuroimaging: principled ROI definition. The authors connect their work to existing approaches like clustering and PCA/ICA, framing Smooth-SODL as a structured sparse decomposition with desirable properties for functional brain mapping. 

While the idea is promising and the technical execution appears generally solid, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and a lack of compelling demonstration of superiority, particularly given the computational cost. The sheer volume of these issues, despite the novelty of the approach, leads me to recommend rejection.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization for spatially structured decomposition is a genuinely novel contribution.
* **Relevance:** The problem addressed – principled ROI definition – is a crucial one in neuroimaging.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The mathematical formulation of Smooth-SODL is clear and well-motivated, drawing connections to established optimization techniques.
* **Scalability:** The authors correctly identify the importance of online learning for handling large-scale neuroimaging datasets.
* **Implementation Detail:** The discussion of algorithmic complexities and practical considerations (e.g., hyperparameter tuning, initialization) is appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Baseline Clarity:** The comparison to CanICA is weakened by the necessary pre-smoothing step. The impact of this smoothing isn’t sufficiently discussed. Furthermore, comparing to a thresholded CanICA (tCanICA) feels ad-hoc and doesn’t represent a fair comparison to the method in its original form.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Tuning:** While the authors acknowledge the difficulty of hyperparameter tuning, the provided guidance (e.g., time-varying α) feels somewhat heuristic. The interplay between α, γ, and τ is not fully elucidated, and a more systematic sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. Figure 1 is helpful but lacks quantitative metrics.
* **Computational Cost:** The 2.6x increase in runtime compared to standard SODL is substantial. The justification that this is “only a factor of 2 or 3” feels dismissive.  A more thorough discussion of computational trade-offs is needed, especially given the marginal improvements in some experiments. The claim that the algorithm is "fast and scalable" feels overstated.
* **Overstated Claims:** Statements like “Smooth-SODL extracts structured denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability” lack sufficient quantitative support. The qualitative assessment in Figure 2(a) is subjective. While the Smooth-SODL maps *appear* cleaner, this is not rigorously demonstrated.
* **Minor Technical Inconsistencies:**  Several minor issues detract from the paper’s polish. For example, the use of “A0 ← 0 ∈ Rk×k, B0 ← 0 ∈ Rp×k” and then “At = ∑t i=1 UiUTt” implies a time-dependent A0 which is incorrect. This, and similar inconsistencies, suggest a lack of careful editing. The notation for the Laplacian operator (∆) is unclear, and the spatial gradient operators are not fully defined.
* **Redundancy and Verbosity:** The introduction is repetitive and could be significantly condensed. The description of Algorithm 1 is verbose and could be streamlined.
* **Referencing:** While thorough, the referencing is not always precise.  Some citations are quite old (e.g., [6] Bertsekas, 1999), and more recent work on online dictionary learning should be considered.
* **Figure Quality:** Figure 2(c) lacks appropriate axis labels (e.g. units of R2 score).

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel approach to ROI definition with theoretical guarantees.
* Potential for improved interpretability of brain imaging data.
* Scalability to large datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Marginal improvements over existing methods, given the increased computational cost.
* Lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation of the benefits of spatial regularization.
* Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies detracting from the paper’s overall polish.
* Overstated claims and a lack of critical self-assessment.
* Hyperparameter tuning remains largely heuristic.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the proposed method, the numerous minor flaws, lack of compelling quantitative results, and overstated claims outweigh the strengths. The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a fully polished and rigorously validated contribution. While the idea holds promise, it requires further development and more robust evaluation before it can be considered a significant advancement in the field. I recommend rejection. A major revision addressing the points raised above, with a focus on rigorous evaluation and a more balanced discussion of limitations, might be reconsidered in the future.
