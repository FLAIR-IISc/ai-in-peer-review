Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework tailored for neuroimaging that encourages atoms (spatial maps) to be both sparse and spatially smooth. It augments the standard online dictionary learning of Mairal et al. with a Laplacian penalty on atoms and retains an online block-coordinate descent scheme: codes are obtained by ridge regression; dictionary atoms are updated one at a time by solving a convex, constrained quadratic program equivalent to a Laplacian-regularized denoising step with a simplex or ℓ1-ball constraint. The authors use accelerated projected gradient (FISTA-style) for these updates and argue the added cost is modest. On 500-subject HCP task fMRI maps (k=40), the method yields more structured and reproducible atoms than SODL, and better interpretability than CanICA, while remaining significantly faster than CanICA. Quantitative evaluations include explained variance, atom stability across splits, predictive performance on behavioral scores, learning curves, and ablations over hyperparameters.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The optimization subproblems (ridge-coded updates and per-atom convex updates with Laplacian) are standard and correct. Using projected accelerated gradient on the smooth quadratic with simple convex constraints is appropriate; projections onto the simplex/ℓ1-ball are efficient. The per-atom subproblem is convex; if solved accurately it has a global optimum.
- Convergence: The paper asserts convergence by appealing to [16]. However, two aspects weaken this claim:
  1) Time-varying hyperparameters: αt ∼ t−1/2 and the adaptive scaling of γt tied to At[j,j]/t change the objective over iterations. Convergence guarantees in [16] assume fixed regularization. If the authors intend annealing, they should either prove convergence to a stationary point of a limiting objective or clarify they fix α,γ in the reported experiments.
  2) Inexact dictionary updates: The text states that 1–2 FISTA iterations suffice in practice, yet also claims “guaranteed to converge to a global optimum at each iteration.” Inexact BCD can still converge under summable errors; this should be discussed or bounded.
- Lipschitz constant and scaling: The derivation that the Lipschitz constant is 1 + 4D·γ̃ (with γ̃ incorporating the At[j,j]/t scaling) is plausible for a standard discrete Laplacian, but depends on boundary conditions and normalization. The recommendation to scale γt with ‖At‖∞,∞/t to avoid vanishing step sizes is sensible heuristically but again needs theoretical qualification.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are well-designed for the target application: comparisons to SODL and (thresholded) CanICA, reproducibility via Hungarian matching, and behavioral prediction. Runtime measurements are informative and show the claimed 2–3x overhead. A limitation is the lack of comparison to prior structured-sparse methods (e.g., TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning [Abraham]; structured sparse PCA [Jenatton et al.]) even on a smaller subset to quantify the speed/quality trade-off.
- Fairness/controls: CanICA runs on smoothed inputs; SODL and Smooth-SODL do not. This favors CanICA for explained variance and stability. Either apply consistent pre-smoothing across methods or justify asymmetry more carefully. Also, report whether codes are constrained to be nonnegative; with simplex atoms and unconstrained ridge codes negative loadings can capture sign, but this should be stated.

Clarity
- Writing is generally clear and well-organized, with a good balance between methods and experiments. However:
  - There are several typos/misstatements (e.g., “max_U” should be “min_U”; “parameter free” is misleading since the FISTA step requires L; “global optimum at each iteration” conflicts with using 1–2 iterations).
  - The use of lim n→∞ in the objective is unnecessary; this is an empirical risk minimization over a finite dataset in practice.
  - Notation in Eq. (7) is hard to parse; explicitly define γ̃ = γ/(At[j,j]/t) and the gradient used in the projected updates.
  - Clarify boundary conditions for the Laplacian and the grid normalization that underpin the 4D bound.
  - The hyperparameter tuning section references Fig. 1 but more concrete guidance (numerical ranges) would help reproduction.
- Reproducibility: Planning an open-source release in Nilearn is excellent. Please include seeds, preprocessing details (masking, normalization), and the exact train/test split protocol.

Originality
- The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian-regularized, constrained update for spatially-structured atoms is a reasonable and useful extension, with emphasis on scalability. Prior work has explored spatially-structured decompositions (TV-ℓ1, GraphNet, structured SPCA), but online variants with Laplacian priors and explicit per-atom projected accelerated updates appear less developed. The contribution is more incremental/engineering than fundamentally new theory, but it fills an important practical gap.
- Related work is broadly cited; however, a more explicit differentiation from [Varoquaux et al. 2010/2011], [Abraham MICCAI], and recent online matrix factorization with structure would strengthen the originality narrative.

Significance
- For neuroimaging, the ability to learn interpretable, reproducible, and compact ROIs at scale is valuable. The method demonstrates improved interpretability and stability over SODL with modest cost, and competitive predictive performance versus CanICA while avoiding dense, hard-to-interpret components.
- Beyond neuroimaging, the technique is applicable to any high-dimensional spatial data where atoms should be smooth and sparse.
- The main limitations for impact are the relatively modest novelty and the theoretical gaps around adaptive regularization and inexact updates. Still, the demonstrated speed/quality trade-off and plan for open-source release make it likely others will use/build on this work.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, practical algorithm addressing a real scaling bottleneck for structured decompositions.
- Strong application-driven evaluation on a large public dataset; improved interpretability and stability with small overhead.
- Simple implementation with standard components; imminent open-source release.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical analysis is thin and some claims (convergence with time-varying α,γ; “global optimum” per iteration) are overstated.
- Missing comparisons to prior structured-sparse baselines (even on smaller data).
- Some fairness issues in experimental setup (pre-smoothing for only one method) and minor clarity/notation issues.

Suggestions to improve
- Either fix α,γ or justify/derive convergence under the proposed annealing; quantify the effect of using only 1–2 projected gradient steps.
- Add a small-scale comparison to a TV-ℓ1 or structured SPCA baseline to contextualize the Laplacian choice (quality vs speed).
- Harmonize preprocessing across methods or add a control showing the effect of smoothing on SODL/Smooth-SODL.
- Clarify Laplacian details (boundary conditions), projection sets (ℓ1-ball vs simplex), and sign constraints on codes.
- Correct typos and notation around Eq. (2), (5), and (7).

Overall: The paper meets the NeurIPS criteria reasonably well on quality (algorithmic soundness, strong experiments) and clarity (with fixable issues), is moderately original, and is practically significant. With minor revisions, it would make a solid contribution.
