

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning for brain imaging that incorporates both sparsity (via L1 penalty) and spatial smoothness (via Laplacian penalty) to define interpretable brain regions. The authors claim this approach improves upon traditional dictionary learning by producing spatially structured, denoised components that better capture inter-subject variability in neuroimaging data. The method is evaluated on HCP task fMRI data, with comparisons against SODL, CanICA, and PCA.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging - the principled definition of brain regions (ROIs) - and presents a scalable solution that extends existing online dictionary learning methods. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including visual inspection of dictionary atoms, explained variance, stability metrics, and prediction of behavioral variables. The authors provide a thoughtful analysis of hyperparameter selection and demonstrate the method's scalability.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Errors and Inconsistencies
1. **Critical Mathematical Error**: In Equation (4), the authors define ΩLap(v) = 1/p ∑(∇xv)²k + (∇yv)²k + (∇zv)²k = 1/2 v^TΔv. This is incorrect - the standard discrete Laplacian regularization should be ΩLap(v) = 1/2 v^TΔv (without the 1/p factor), and the gradient squared terms should be summed before dividing by p. This error fundamentally affects the interpretation of the regularization strength.

2. **Inconsistent Notation**: In Equation (7), the authors write "At[j,j]/t" but later refer to "At[j,j]/t" as a scalar quantity. At is k×k, so At[j,j] is a scalar, but the notation is inconsistent with standard matrix indexing conventions.

3. **Misleading Convergence Claim**: The paper claims "the BCD dictionary-update algorithm Alg. 2 is guaranteed to converge to a global optimum" citing [6,16]. However, the problem is not jointly convex, so this claim is incorrect. The paper should clarify that convergence is to a local optimum.

4. **Grammatical Errors**: "We typically use we use mini-batches of size η = 20" (Section 3.1) contains a clear grammatical error.

5. **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper uses "tCanICA" for the hard-thresholded CanICA results in Figure 2 but refers to "hard-thresholded CanICA" in the text, creating confusion.

### Experimental and Methodological Concerns
1. **Learning Curves Contradict Key Claim**: The paper claims spatial regularization is "really needed" based on learning curves (Table 1), but the curves show the gap between Smooth-SODL and SODL narrows as more data is added, contradicting the claim that spatial regularization is universally beneficial.

2. **Insufficient Implementation Details**: The paper states "the Euclidean projections onto the `1 ball C can be computed exactly in linear-time O(p)" but provides no reference for this claim (beyond citing [7] which doesn't appear to support it). This is critical for reproducibility.

3. **Hardware Specification Missing**: The paper claims "Smooth-SODL took 340s (≈ 5.6 minutes)" but doesn't specify the hardware used for these timing measurements, making it impossible to verify the "slightly less than 3 times slower" claim.

4. **Incomplete Comparison**: The paper compares against CanICA and SODL but fails to include comparisons against other structured dictionary learning methods like [1, 25], which are directly relevant to the problem.

5. **Unclear Hyperparameter Selection**: While the paper discusses hyperparameter tuning, it doesn't provide sufficient detail about how the "safe range" of (γ, τ) was determined, making it difficult for others to replicate the results.

## Overall Assessment
This paper addresses an important problem with a technically interesting extension to online dictionary learning. However, the paper contains several critical technical errors (particularly in Equation 4) that undermine the theoretical foundation of the proposed method. The claims about convergence and the interpretation of the Laplacian regularization are misleading due to mathematical inaccuracies. The experimental results are promising but suffer from insufficient detail about implementation and hardware specifications.

The paper would benefit from a complete mathematical revision, particularly correcting the Laplacian formulation and clarifying the convergence properties. The experimental section should provide more details about hardware specifications and include comparisons against relevant structured dictionary learning methods.

## Recommendation
**Reject**

While the problem addressed is important and the approach shows promise, the paper's technical errors and methodological inconsistencies are too substantial to overlook. The mathematical errors in the formulation of the Laplacian regularization fundamentally affect the interpretation of the method and its performance. The claims about convergence are misleading, and the lack of sufficient implementation details for reproducibility is problematic for a NIPS submission. The authors should address these issues before the paper can be considered for publication.
