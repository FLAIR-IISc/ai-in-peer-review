**Summary:**
This paper presents a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), for identifying spatially structured brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging datasets. The core problem is to find a set of basis functions (a "dictionary" of brain maps or ROIs) that can represent the brain activity of a large population, where these basis functions are both sparse and spatially coherent (i.e., forming smooth "blobs" rather than scattered voxels). The authors build upon the well-established framework of online dictionary learning (SODL) by Mairal et al., which is known for its scalability. The key contribution is the elegant integration of a Laplacian penalty into the online optimization objective. This penalty enforces spatial smoothness on the dictionary atoms. The authors derive the necessary updates for their block-coordinate descent algorithm, showing that the increased complexity is minimal, and demonstrate their method's effectiveness on a large dataset of 500 subjects from the Human Connectome Project.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Originality and Significance:**
The paper's primary strength lies in its originality and significance. While both online dictionary learning and structured sparsity (e.g., using Laplacian or Total Variation penalties) are established concepts, their combination in a scalable online framework for a challenging real-world problem like whole-brain analysis is a significant and bold step forward. Previous methods for learning spatially-structured brain atlases were typically batch algorithms, which are computationally infeasible for modern, large-scale datasets. This work breaks that barrier, making structured decomposition practical at scale. By providing a fast and principled way to extract interpretable, structured patterns of inter-subject variability, the paper addresses a critical need in the neuroimaging community. The potential impact is high, as such data-driven atlases can improve statistical power and interpretability in a wide range of brain mapping studies. The promise to release the code in a major open-source library (Nilearn) further amplifies its potential impact.

**Quality:**
The technical quality of the paper is high. The model is a well-motivated extension of a classic machine learning algorithm. The derivation of the dictionary update step is clear, and the authors correctly identify its connection to GraphNet-style problems, allowing for efficient solution via standard convex optimization tools like FISTA. The empirical evaluation is strong and convincing. The authors use a large, relevant, and publicly available dataset (HCP). The choice of baseline methods—unstructured sparse dictionary learning (SODL) and a popular dense decomposition (CanICA)—is appropriate and allows for a clear demonstration of the proposed method's benefits. The evaluation is comprehensive, covering computational time, qualitative interpretability, quantitative measures of explained variance and stability, and downstream performance on a prediction task. The analysis in Table 1, showing the method's pronounced advantage in lower-data regimes, is particularly insightful and highlights the real-world value of the spatial regularizer when data is not abundant.

The paper is candid about its limitations, particularly the difficulty of hyperparameter tuning. While a more automated procedure for setting parameters would be ideal, the authors provide a reasonable empirical exploration and practical advice. Given the non-convex nature of the overall problem, this is an acceptable weakness.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, connecting the technical problem to a tangible scientific need. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the algorithmic details are explained sufficiently for an expert to understand the implementation. The figures are clear and effectively communicate the main results. Figure 2, in particular, provides a compelling side-by-side comparison of the outputs from different methods, immediately highlighting the superior spatial structure of the maps produced by Smooth-SODL.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The paper successfully makes structured dictionary learning scalable, a significant achievement that opens up new possibilities for analyzing massive datasets in neuroimaging and potentially other fields with spatio-temporal data.
2.  **Strong and Bold Idea:** Instead of a minor tweak, this work tackles a fundamental limitation of existing methods by integrating a structural prior directly into a scalable online learning framework.
3.  **Convincing Empirical Evidence:** The experiments are thorough, well-designed, and demonstrate clear advantages over relevant baselines in terms of interpretability, stability, and performance, especially in data-limited scenarios.
4.  **Principled Approach:** The method is a well-founded extension of a powerful and theoretically grounded algorithm (SODL).

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Incremental Contribution:** A critical view might label the work as simply adding a known regularizer (Laplacian) to a known algorithm (SODL). However, I believe the non-trivial algorithmic integration and the demonstrated impact on scalability and solution quality elevate it far beyond a simple incremental step.
2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method introduces additional hyperparameters, and their selection remains a somewhat open problem, relying on cross-validation. This is a common issue in the field and does not detract significantly from the core contribution.

**Recommendation:**
I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a bold, novel, and highly significant contribution that solves a real-world problem with an elegant and scalable solution. The work combines methodological rigor with a clear, high-impact application. It is precisely the kind of research that pushes the field forward by making a powerful class of models computationally tractable for modern large-scale problems. The minor weaknesses are far outweighed by the novelty and potential impact of the contribution.
