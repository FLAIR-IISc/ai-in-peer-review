Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging that augments standard sparse online dictionary learning (Mairal et al.) with a Laplacian (graph-smoothing) penalty on dictionary atoms to encourage spatially smooth, “blob-like” components. The optimization is handled via block coordinate descent: ridge-coded updates for subjects and per-atom constrained quadratic updates solved with a few iterations of FISTA, projecting onto an ℓ1-ball or simplex to induce sparsity and, optionally, non-negativity. Experiments on HCP task fMRI GLM maps (n=500, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL against vanilla SODL and CanICA. The authors report improved spatial structure and stability, similar or slightly lower explained variance than PCA/ICA, and modest behavioral prediction performance; runtime is 2–3x that of SODL.

Relation to prior work: The approach sits between unstructured online sparse coding (Mairal 2010; Mensch 2016) and spatially-structured decompositions such as TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning (Abraham 2013), GraphNet-style smoothness penalties (Grosenick), and structured sparse PCA (Jenatton et al.). The novelty is primarily the integration of a Laplacian penalty into the online DL framework with practical efficiency.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and practical need in neuroimaging: spatially coherent, interpretable multivariate decompositions at scale.
- Algorithmic formulation is sensible: per-atom convex subproblems with a smooth quadratic + Laplacian term and a simple convex constraint set; using FISTA with projections is standard and scalable.
- Empirical results suggest improved spatial coherence and stability over unstructured SODL, with only a modest runtime penalty.
- The paper acknowledges hyperparameter sensitivity and proposes practical heuristics; the focus on reproducibility/stability is appropriate for this domain.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Incremental novelty: Adding a Laplacian/Tikhonov smoothness to atoms within online DL is a relatively modest extension. Closely related prior methods (TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning; GraphNet; structured sparse PCA) are cited but not compared empirically. Without such baselines, the claimed advantages of Laplacian-regularized online DL are not convincingly established.
- Convergence claims are not fully justified. The per-atom subproblem is convex and solvable to global optimality, but the overall nonconvex online scheme inherits guarantees from Mairal (2010) under assumptions that seem altered here:
  - The paper suggests time-varying αt ∼ t^-1/2 and a schedule for γt to avoid very small steps. Changing the objective during learning undermines standard convergence results; this needs theoretical treatment or at least careful empirical convergence diagnostics.
  - Statements such as “1–2 FISTA iterations are sufficient to reach 10^-6 accuracy” are not credible; either the tolerance is misstated, or the per-atom inner problems are not actually being solved to that precision. Please clarify stopping criteria and actual tolerances used.
- Baselines and fairness:
  - CanICA is pre-smoothed (6 mm) while SODL/Smooth-SODL are not; this asymmetry can affect explained variance and stability. If smoothing is deemed necessary for ICA, the impact should be quantified (smoothed vs unsmoothed for all methods).
  - The use of hard-thresholded CanICA (tCanICA) as an “interpretability” baseline is reasonable, but measuring explained variance on thresholded maps is not meaningful; report EV for the unthresholded model (which you do) and use separate interpretability metrics for thresholded versions, but do not mix them.
  - Missing baselines: TV-ℓ1 DL (e.g., Abraham 2013; Varoquaux et al. 2011/2014), GraphNet-like decompositions, structured SPCA. These are important for assessing whether Laplacian is the right structural prior (TV often yields more compact blobs).
- Methodological choices need stronger justification:
  - Positivity/simplex constraint on atoms: GLM Z-maps contain positive and negative effects; enforcing non-negativity biases components and may partly explain improved “blob” appearance. Please justify and ablate (ℓ1-ball without non-negativity).
  - Claim that Laplacian penalty “imposes blobs”: L2-Laplacian enforces smoothness but tends to spread support; compactness typically requires TV or explicit compactness priors. The paper should temper this claim or empirically compare to TV.
  - Hyperparameter selection: γ and τ are reportedly set via K-fold CV on explained variance, but details are missing (folds, grids, nested CV in prediction tasks, computational cost). For behavioral prediction, nested CV is essential to avoid leakage; your “RAW” baseline with voxel selection risks leakage unless selection is done within folds (not stated).
- Evaluation gaps:
  - Stability/reproducibility: Only a single number (mean correlation) is provided, without confidence intervals or split details. Please provide variance across splits and matching procedure details.
  - Generality: All results are on a single task contrast and one choice of k=40 with n=500. Claims of “massive-data setting” are not fully substantiated; no results on time-series DL, different k, different modalities, or larger n.
  - Scalability: Runtimes on a laptop are helpful but insufficient; memory usage, per-iteration costs, and scaling with p, k, and batch size should be profiled. The FFT-based update suggestion is likely impractical with brain masks; clarify whether it was used.
- Clarity and presentation: Many typos/grammar issues, some equations are hard to parse, and figures/tables are referenced but not shown. The derivation around Eq. (7) is terse; define all symbols clearly. The condition on γ in Eq. (9) is nonstandard and needs derivation or citation.

Questions for the authors
1. Do you use nested CV for setting γ, τ, and α in the behavioral prediction experiments? How are features standardized and regressors regularized?
2. How sensitive are results to the positivity/simplex constraint? Please report variants with ℓ1-ball (signed atoms).
3. Can you include empirical comparisons to TV-ℓ1 DL and/or GraphNet-based decompositions on the same data?
4. Provide learning curves (objective value vs iterations), and ablate the time-varying αt and γt. Do fixed α, γ yield similar performance and preserve convergence guarantees?
5. How are stability scores computed (splits, Hungarian matching details, number of seeds), and what are confidence intervals?
6. What is the actual stopping criterion and tolerance for the FISTA inner loop? How many iterations on average per atom update?

Pros
- Practical extension of a widely used framework with attention to scalability.
- Produces visually cleaner, more stable components than unstructured SODL.
- Reasonable runtime overhead; addresses a relevant application.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to established structured DL/SPCA literature.
- Convergence guarantees overstated given time-varying regularization and loose inner solves.
- Incomplete and partially unfair baseline comparisons; missing key structured baselines.
- Evaluation restricted in scope; some methodological choices insufficiently justified.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues; code not yet available.

Recommendation
I lean toward rejection in the current form. The idea is promising and practically useful, but the paper needs stronger and fairer empirical validation against established structured methods, clearer and correct convergence/optimization details, ablations (positivity, hyperparameters), and more rigorous predictive/stability evaluations. With these additions and improved clarity, this could become a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
