This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), for learning brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data. The core idea is to leverage inter-subject variability to define regions, extending online dictionary learning with both sparsity and spatial smoothness constraints imposed via a Laplacian penalty. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the limitations of existing ROI definition methods (manual segmentation, cross-modality alignment) and the shortcomings of univariate and clustering-based approaches. They demonstrate the method on a large HCP dataset, comparing it to CanICA and standard SODL in terms of reconstruction quality, stability, and predictive power for behavioral variables.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important problem in neuroimaging. The proposed method appears technically sound, and the extension of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty is a reasonable approach to encourage spatially localized and smooth regions. The experiments are comprehensive, utilizing a large, publicly available dataset (HCP) and including comparisons with relevant baselines. The results presented demonstrate the benefits of the spatial regularization, particularly in terms of interpretability and predictive performance. The authors also thoughtfully address hyperparameter tuning and discuss practical considerations, providing useful insights for potential users. The connection to related work is generally good, citing relevant publications in dictionary learning, neuroimaging, and spatial regularization.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for spatial regularization is a novel contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is well-defined and based on established optimization techniques (block coordinate descent, FISTA).
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The experiments are thorough, using a large dataset and comparing against relevant baselines (CanICA, SODL).
*   **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning and the implementation details (Nilearn integration) are valuable for potential users.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and the limitations of existing approaches, providing a strong rationale for the proposed method.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The results demonstrate the benefits of the spatial regularization, particularly in scenarios with limited data.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:**  The method is significantly slower than standard SODL (approximately 3x slower), which could be a limitation for extremely large datasets. While still faster than CanICA, the overhead should be acknowledged.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors address hyperparameter tuning, the optimal values seem to be quite specific to the dataset and task. More guidance on selecting these parameters for new applications would be helpful.  The practical consideration section, while present, feels somewhat light given the complexity of tuning.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art ROI methods:** While CanICA and SODL are good baselines, the paper doesn't compare against more recent, highly-optimized ROI definition techniques commonly used in the field (e.g., multi-level kernel regression, spatial clustering with advanced regularization). This limits the assessment of the practical significance of the improvements.
*   **Interpretation of Dictionary Atoms:**  While the authors emphasize the improved interpretability of the learned regions, a more detailed qualitative analysis of the atoms, perhaps with specific brain region labels, would strengthen this claim. Showing how well the learned regions align with known neuroanatomical structures could be beneficial.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** While convergence is mentioned, there's limited discussion of theoretical guarantees regarding the recovery of the true underlying brain regions. A deeper analysis of the conditions under which the method is likely to succeed would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for learning brain regions.
*   The experiments are comprehensive and demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.
*   The method addresses an important problem in neuroimaging with practical relevance.
*   The code will be publicly available, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost is higher than standard SODL.
*   The hyperparameter tuning can be challenging.
*   The comparison to state-of-the-art ROI methods is limited.
*   A deeper theoretical analysis would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of neuroimaging. While there are some weaknesses, the novelty, technical soundness, and comprehensive evaluation outweigh these concerns.  The method offers a principled and data-driven approach to ROI definition that has the potential to improve the accuracy and interpretability of neuroimaging studies.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Expand the discussion of hyperparameter tuning, providing more practical guidance for users.
2.  Include a comparison to more recent and widely-used ROI definition methods.
3.  Provide a more detailed qualitative analysis of the learned dictionary atoms, perhaps with anatomical labels.
4.  Clarify the computational trade-offs between Smooth-SODL and standard SODL, and suggest strategies for mitigating the increased cost.
