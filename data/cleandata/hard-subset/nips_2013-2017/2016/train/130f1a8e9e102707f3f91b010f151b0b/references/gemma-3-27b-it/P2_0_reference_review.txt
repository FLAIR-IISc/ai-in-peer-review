## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth-SODL, for learning spatially localized brain regions directly from inter-subject variability in neuroimaging data.  It extends existing online dictionary learning techniques by incorporating both sparsity and smoothness constraints on the learned dictionary atoms using an L1 penalty for sparsity and a Laplacian penalty for smoothness. The motivation is strong: existing methods for region-of-interest (ROI) definition are often ad-hoc or rely on prior knowledge. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a large fMRI dataset (HCP), showing improvements in dictionary interpretability, stability, and predictive power compared to standard SODL and ICA-based methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of current ROI definition methods in neuroimaging and establishes a compelling need for a data-driven, principled approach.
* **Novelty:**  Combining online dictionary learning with both sparsity *and* spatial smoothness constraints is a genuinely novel contribution. Existing work often tackles sparsity or smoothness in isolation or relies on offline methods.
* **Scalability:**  The online learning framework is well-suited to large-scale neuroimaging datasets, a significant practical advantage.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation on a large, publicly available dataset (HCP) using a variety of metrics, including visual inspection, explained variance, stability, and prediction of behavioral variables.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the problem and discusses convergence properties, referencing relevant theoretical work.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors address important practical aspects, such as hyperparameter tuning and initialization, providing guidance for practitioners.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the online learning framework is scalable, the addition of the Laplacian penalty does introduce a significant computational overhead (approximately 3x slower than standard SODL).  A more detailed analysis of the trade-off between computational cost and performance would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The results demonstrate sensitivity to the choice of hyperparameters (α, γ, τ). While the authors offer some guidance, a more systematic exploration of the hyperparameter space and a discussion of methods for robust hyperparameter selection would be helpful.  The explanation of Eq. 9 is a bit hand-wavy and could benefit from more rigorous justification.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the comparison to CanICA and SODL is valuable, the authors could strengthen their claims by comparing Smooth-SODL to more recent or advanced methods for spatial decomposition in neuroimaging, such as those based on deep learning.
* **Interpretation of "Blobs":** The notion of "blobs" as the desired spatial structure feels somewhat intuitive but lacks a more rigorous connection to neuroanatomical principles.  Discussing how the learned regions relate to known brain structures would enhance the interpretability of the results.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the comparison between SODL and Smooth-SODL is insightful, a more detailed ablation study systematically removing each regularization term (sparsity, smoothness) would further clarify their individual contributions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant and well-executed contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed Smooth-SODL method offers a principled and scalable approach to learning brain regions directly from data, addressing a long-standing challenge in the field. While there are some weaknesses related to computational cost, hyperparameter sensitivity, and comparison to more recent methods, the strengths of the paper, particularly its novelty, strong empirical validation, and clear presentation, outweigh these concerns.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* Strong empirical results demonstrating improvements over existing methods.
* Scalable online learning framework suitable for large-scale datasets.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Potential for significant impact on neuroimaging research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Increased computational cost compared to standard SODL.
* Hyperparameter sensitivity requires careful tuning.
* Comparison to a broader range of state-of-the-art methods would be beneficial.

**NIPS Criteria Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  Strong. The paper is technically sound, claims are well-supported by experiments, and the authors are careful in their evaluation.
* **Clarity:**  Good. The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
* **Originality:**  High. The combination of online learning, sparsity, and spatial smoothness is novel and represents a significant advance.
* **Significance:**  High. The results are likely to be of interest to a broad audience of neuroimaging researchers and could lead to new insights into brain function and organization.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  I recommend accepting this paper for presentation at NIPS 2016. The work is of high quality, originality, and significance and has the potential to make a lasting impact on the field of neuroimaging. I encourage the authors to address the minor weaknesses identified in the review in a revised version of the manuscript.
