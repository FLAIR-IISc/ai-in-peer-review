## Review of “Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning”

This paper proposes a novel approach to defining brain regions by leveraging inter-subject variability through a structured sparse online dictionary learning (Smooth-SODL) method. The core idea – using population-level data to *define* regions, rather than relying on pre-defined ROIs or purely data-driven clustering – is potentially interesting, aiming to bridge a gap between hypothesis-driven and data-driven neuroimaging analyses. The authors position their work as an extension of existing online dictionary learning methods ([16]) incorporating Laplacian regularization to encourage spatially coherent dictionary atoms. 

However, while the motivation is sound, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence demonstrating a substantial advantage over established methods, and the presentation lacks sufficient rigor in establishing the novelty and benefits of Smooth-SODL.

**Strengths:**

* **Relevant Problem:** The challenge of principled ROI definition in neuroimaging is well-recognized. Automatically discovering meaningful brain regions is a valuable goal.
* **Clear Motivation:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods (univariate approaches, purely data-driven clustering, and reliance on pre-defined ROIs) and frame their work as a solution.
* **Online Learning:** The use of online learning is pragmatic, particularly for large-scale neuroimaging datasets where memory constraints are significant.
* **Laplacian Regularization:** The inclusion of a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness is a reasonable and potentially impactful addition to standard dictionary learning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Advance:** The core contribution – adding a Laplacian penalty to online dictionary learning – feels incremental. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate that this extension yields a *significant* improvement in performance or interpretability compared to well-established techniques, particularly given the computational cost increase.
* **Limited Baselines:** The comparison to CanICA, while included, feels somewhat weak.  CanICA, especially in its thresholded form (tCanICA), is often a baseline for exploratory analysis, but not necessarily a strong competitor when aiming for interpretable and spatially coherent regions. Crucially missing are comparisons to more established, spatially-informed methods like independent components analysis with spatial PCA or group ICA, or even simple k-means clustering with spatial constraints. The lack of comparison to more directly competing methods raises concerns about the significance of the reported improvements.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors acknowledge hyperparameter sensitivity, the discussion feels superficial. Fig. 1 provides a limited view and doesn’t adequately guide readers on *how* to choose optimal parameters in practice. The adaptive α selection rule (eq. 10) seems somewhat ad-hoc and lacks strong justification.
* **Computational Cost:** The 3x increase in runtime compared to standard SODL is mentioned but not thoroughly justified.  The authors claim a “minor cost,” but this needs further qualification. For large datasets, even a factor of 3 can be substantial.
* **Overstated Claims:** Claims about “data-driven atlases” and the potential to “revolutionize” ROI extraction seem overly enthusiastic given the presented results. The improvements demonstrated, while potentially useful, don’t warrant such strong statements.
* **Lack of Reproducibility Details:** While the authors state the implementation will be released, crucial details regarding preprocessing steps (beyond smoothing for CanICA) and specific parameter settings are lacking, hindering immediate reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound, but ultimately incremental, contribution. The idea of incorporating spatial regularization into online dictionary learning is reasonable, but the paper fails to demonstrate a compelling advantage over existing methods. The comparisons are limited, the discussion of hyperparameters is superficial, and the claims are overstated. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging.
* Presents a technically correct and well-motivated approach.
* Demonstrates some improvement in performance compared to standard SODL.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Incremental contribution with limited novelty.
* Insufficient comparison to strong baselines.
* Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Computational cost is not adequately justified.
* Overstated claims about significance.



**Recommendation:** **Reject.** 

The paper, in its current form, does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above – particularly performing more rigorous comparisons to established methods and providing a more thorough analysis of hyperparameter selection – and resubmit to a future conference. The idea has potential, but requires more substantial validation and justification.
