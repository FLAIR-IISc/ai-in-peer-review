

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online sparse dictionary learning (SODL) [16] that incorporates spatial smoothness constraints through a Laplacian penalty to learn interpretable brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors address the problem of defining meaningful regions of interest (ROIs) in brain imaging, arguing that inter-subject variability contains important information that is often discarded in standard approaches. They extend SODL by adding a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms (representing brain regions), while maintaining the computational efficiency of online learning. The method is evaluated on HCP task fMRI data, comparing against SODL [16] and CanICA [22].

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear mathematical formulation. The optimization framework (Algorithm 1) is well-justified, building on established online dictionary learning while adding a Laplacian penalty for spatial structure. The authors provide convergence guarantees and demonstrate empirical performance across multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, prediction of behavioral variables). The experimental design is appropriate, with thorough hyperparameter analysis and comparison to strong baselines. The paper adequately addresses limitations, noting that spatial regularization is most beneficial with limited data. The main technical contribution is the integration of spatial structure into online dictionary learning, which the authors convincingly argue is computationally feasible (only ~3x slower than SODL).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, methodology, and experimental results are presented in a logical flow. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The figures effectively illustrate the qualitative differences between methods (Fig. 2), particularly showing how Smooth-SODL produces more spatially structured and interpretable brain regions compared to SODL and CanICA. The supplementary materials provide additional implementation details. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the Laplacian penalty's implementation in the Fourier domain (mentioned in supplementary materials but not in main text), but this is a minor point.

**Novelty:** The core idea of incorporating spatial smoothness constraints into online dictionary learning for neuroimaging is novel in this context. While structured dictionary learning has been explored in neuroimaging [1, 25], these approaches typically use slow alternating optimization methods. The authors correctly position their work as extending [16] with a spatial regularization that maintains computational efficiency. The novelty is significant because it enables structured decomposition at scale, which has been a bottleneck for previous approaches. The comparison to related work (Section 4) is thorough and properly contextualizes the contribution relative to NIPS proceedings.

**Significance:** The problem of defining meaningful ROIs is important in neuroimaging, with direct applications to better understanding brain function and pathology. The authors demonstrate that Smooth-SODL provides more interpretable brain regions that better capture inter-subject variability, especially in small data regimes (14x better explained variance with only 17 subjects). The method's computational efficiency (only ~3x slower than SODL) makes it practical for large-scale neuroimaging studies. The paper also shows improved performance in predicting behavioral variables, suggesting the learned representations have practical utility. The planned integration into Nilearn will facilitate adoption in the neuroimaging community.

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a meaningful problem in neuroimaging with clear motivation
- Well-motivated extension of existing online dictionary learning
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation with multiple metrics
- Demonstrates significant gains in small-data regimes (14x explained variance gain)
- Maintains computational efficiency (only 3x slower than SODL)
- Thorough hyperparameter analysis and implementation details
- Clear presentation with effective figures

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty is somewhat incremental (extension of [16] with Laplacian penalty), though well-justified
- Limited theoretical analysis of how the Laplacian penalty affects the quality of learned regions
- Comparison with CanICA may be slightly unfair due to CanICA's lack of sparsity enforcement
- Limited discussion of generalizability to other neuroimaging modalities (beyond fMRI)
- The paper could more explicitly discuss the relationship between the Laplacian penalty and known brain organization principles

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
- **Pro:** The method addresses a significant problem in neuroimaging with a well-executed solution that extends existing work in a meaningful way. The computational efficiency and demonstrated gains in small-data regimes are compelling. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides a valuable contribution to the field.
- **Con:** The novelty is incremental rather than transformative, and the theoretical analysis of the spatial regularization's effect could be strengthened.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a high-quality paper that makes a solid contribution to the intersection of neuroimaging and machine learning. The proposed Smooth-SODL method is technically sound, well-justified, and demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches, particularly in small-data regimes. The paper is clearly written and makes a strong case for the value of spatial structure in dictionary learning for neuroimaging. While the novelty is incremental, the practical impact and computational efficiency make this a valuable contribution to the field. The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS.
