

# Peer Review: "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging data that incorporates spatial smoothness constraints to learn interpretable brain regions. The authors address a key limitation in neuroimaging analysis: the lack of principled methods for defining regions of interest (ROIs) that capture meaningful inter-subject variability. Unlike traditional approaches that rely on anatomical segmentation or predefined ROIs, the authors model brain activity patterns as linear combinations of spatially smooth and sparse dictionary atoms, where each atom represents a brain region. The key innovation is adding a Laplacian penalty to the standard online dictionary learning framework [16] to enforce spatial structure on the atoms, while maintaining computational efficiency. The method is evaluated on the Human Connectome Project (HCP) dataset, demonstrating improved interpretability and better capture of inter-subject variability compared to state-of-the-art methods like SODL [16] and CanICA [22].

The work builds on several strands of previous research: the online dictionary learning framework [16], spatially structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging [1, 25], and the application of sparse coding to brain imaging [14, 21]. The authors properly situate their contribution within this literature, acknowledging the limitations of existing approaches and positioning their method as a scalable solution that integrates spatial structure into online learning.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a technically sound extension of online dictionary learning with clear theoretical justification for the Laplacian penalty.
- The algorithmic details are well-described, including the BCD updates, convergence guarantees, and practical implementation considerations.
- The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, reproducibility, predictive performance) and a large-scale dataset (500 subjects).
- The authors carefully analyze hyperparameter effects (Fig. 1) and provide practical guidance for implementation.
- The comparison with relevant baselines (SODL, CanICA) is appropriate, and the authors acknowledge limitations of each approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The claim that "spatial regularization is needed" is somewhat overstated. The learning curves (Table 1) show that as data size increases, the performance gap between Smooth-SODL and SODL diminishes, suggesting that spatial regularization is most beneficial in data-limited scenarios. The paper could better clarify the conditions under which spatial regularization provides significant benefits.
- The paper states that "Smooth-SODL extracts structured denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability," but doesn't provide formal statistical tests for these improvements (beyond visual comparisons and explained variance metrics).
- The reproducibility metric (mean correlation between corresponding atoms) is reported, but the paper lacks detailed analysis of how the method performs across different random seeds or data splits (e.g., standard deviations across multiple runs).
- The computational complexity analysis is somewhat superficial. While the paper claims the method is "only 2-3x slower" than SODL, it doesn't provide detailed complexity analysis or scalability experiments with larger datasets.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to method to evaluation.
- The mathematical formulation is precise, and the key concepts (sparsity, spatial smoothness, online learning) are well-explained.
- The figures (especially Fig. 2) effectively illustrate the qualitative differences between methods, with clear visual comparisons of dictionary atoms.
- The supplementary materials (mentioned but not included in the submission) appear to provide additional technical details that would be valuable for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The explanation of how the Laplacian penalty enforces "blobs" (as opposed to scattered patterns) could be strengthened with more intuitive geometric or visual examples.
- The paper mentions "the safe range of the parameter pair (γ, τ)" but doesn't provide a clear, practical guideline for selecting these parameters in real-world applications.
- The comparison with CanICA could be more nuanced. CanICA is an ICA-based method that explicitly does not enforce sparsity, so it's not surprising that it produces dense atoms. A more informative comparison would be with a sparse ICA variant.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The paper successfully extends the online dictionary learning framework [16] to incorporate spatial structure via a Laplacian penalty, which is novel in the context of large-scale neuroimaging analysis.
- The combination of online learning with spatial constraints is not common in the literature, and the authors provide a clear justification for why this approach is both feasible and beneficial.
- The application to brain region discovery is novel in the sense that it leverages inter-subject variability directly, rather than relying on anatomical priors or fixed ROIs.

**Weaknesses:**
- While the spatially structured dictionary learning is novel for online learning in neuroimaging, the Laplacian penalty for spatial smoothness has been used in other contexts (e.g., [3, 8, 25]). The paper could better clarify how this specific implementation differs from prior work.
- The paper mentions related work [23] but doesn't explicitly contrast their approach with the proposed method, which could help clarify the novelty.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The problem of defining meaningful ROIs in neuroimaging is important and has significant practical implications for brain imaging analysis.
- The proposed method addresses a real limitation in current neuroimaging analysis pipelines by providing a principled, data-driven approach to ROI definition.
- The scalability of the method to large datasets (HCP with 500 subjects) is a significant contribution, as many existing methods become computationally intractable at this scale.
- The results demonstrate clear advantages in interpretability and capturing inter-subject variability, which are crucial for clinical and cognitive neuroscience applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper claims the method "shall become the de facto way to do dimensionality reduction and ROI extraction," but this is somewhat overstated. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the method is broadly applicable to different neuroimaging modalities or tasks beyond the specific HCP task used in the experiments.
- While the method improves on CanICA in terms of interpretability, the predictive performance on behavioral variables (Fig. 2c) shows that CanICA still outperforms Smooth-SODL in some metrics. The paper doesn't adequately address why this is the case.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of machine learning and neuroimaging. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and evaluated thoroughly on a large-scale dataset. The primary strengths are the principled extension of online dictionary learning with spatial constraints, the clear demonstration of improved interpretability, and the scalability to large neuroimaging datasets.

While there are some minor weaknesses in the presentation and analysis (particularly regarding the claimed necessity of spatial regularization and the comparison with CanICA), these do not significantly undermine the paper's contributions. The work advances the state of the art in brain region discovery by providing a scalable, interpretable method that leverages inter-subject variability.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Novel extension of online dictionary learning with spatial constraints for neuroimaging
- Comprehensive evaluation on a large-scale dataset with multiple metrics
- Clear demonstration of improved interpretability and capture of inter-subject variability
- Practical implementation details and hyperparameter guidance
- Strong connection to real-world neuroimaging problems

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- Overstated claims about the necessity of spatial regularization (the benefit diminishes with more data)
- Comparison with CanICA is somewhat unfair due to fundamental differences in the methods (sparsity vs. non-sparsity)
- Limited analysis of reproducibility across multiple runs or data splits
- The method's applicability beyond the specific HCP task used in the experiments is not thoroughly established

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The method is innovative, well-executed, and addresses a meaningful problem in neuroimaging. While there are minor weaknesses in the presentation and analysis, they do not detract significantly from the paper's contribution. The work provides a valuable tool for neuroimaging analysis that is both theoretically sound and practically useful.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Clarify the conditions under which spatial regularization provides significant benefits (not just "always needed"). The learning curves in Table 1 suggest it's most beneficial in data-limited scenarios.

2. Provide more detailed analysis of reproducibility across multiple random seeds or data splits, not just the mean correlation metric.

3. Include a brief discussion of how the method might be extended to other neuroimaging modalities (e.g., diffusion MRI, structural MRI) beyond fMRI.

4. Consider adding a comparison with a sparse ICA variant to provide a fairer comparison to CanICA, as the current comparison is between a sparse method (Smooth-SODL) and a non-sparse method (CanICA).

5. Add a short discussion of limitations regarding the choice of Laplacian operator (e.g., how it handles different spatial resolutions or coordinate systems).

This is a strong contribution to the field that will likely be valuable to both machine learning researchers and neuroimaging scientists. The authors have successfully bridged a gap between theoretical advances in dictionary learning and practical applications in brain imaging.
