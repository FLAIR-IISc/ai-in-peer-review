

# Peer Review: "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning that incorporates spatial smoothness constraints to learn brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors address a significant challenge in neuroimaging: principled definition of brain regions (ROIs) from inter-subject variability rather than relying on anatomical segmentation or manual ROI selection. The method combines L1 sparsity for sparse representations with a Laplacian penalty for spatial smoothness, producing more interpretable, blob-like brain regions. The authors demonstrate their approach on HCP fMRI data, showing that spatial regularization significantly improves interpretability and stability compared to standard sparse dictionary learning (SODL) and ICA-based methods like CanICA. The work extends the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) while maintaining computational efficiency.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The problem formulation is well-motivated and clearly articulated, building on a solid foundation of dictionary learning and spatial regularization. The theoretical analysis of the optimization problem is sound, with convergence guarantees established through references to standard results. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple baselines (SODL, CanICA) across multiple metrics (visual quality, explained variance, stability, predictive performance on behavioral variables). The authors carefully acknowledge limitations (e.g., parameter tuning challenges, the fact that spatial regularization becomes less critical with very large datasets). The implementation details and computational efficiency (only ~3x slower than SODL) are particularly strong points. The paper presents a complete, well-executed study rather than a position paper.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively frames the problem and motivates the approach. The technical sections are logically structured and provide sufficient detail for reproducibility (including algorithm pseudocode and implementation details). The figures (particularly Figure 2) effectively communicate the main findings. The authors make a strong effort to clarify technical concepts (e.g., explaining the Laplacian penalty in the context of brain imaging). The writing is clear and accessible to a broad ML audience, though some neuroimaging-specific terminology could be slightly more explained for non-specialists.

## Originality
The core contribution—combining L1 sparsity with Laplacian-based spatial smoothness in an online dictionary learning framework—is novel in the context of neuroimaging. While both sparse dictionary learning and spatial regularization have been studied separately, this specific combination for brain region discovery represents a meaningful extension. The paper clearly differentiates from related work: it's more efficient than previous spatially-structured approaches (which relied on slow alternating optimization), and it provides a principled method with convergence guarantees unlike heuristic approaches like [1]. The authors appropriately cite relevant prior work, including recent NIPS contributions on dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010; Mensch et al., 2016).

## Significance
This work addresses a significant practical problem in neuroimaging with clear potential impact. The ability to automatically learn interpretable brain regions from inter-subject variability could improve the statistical power and interpretability of neuroimaging studies. The method's scalability to large datasets (HCP with 500 subjects) makes it practically relevant. The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of interpretability and stability—key concerns in neuroimaging where ROI selection is often subjective. The demonstration of improved predictive performance on behavioral variables further underscores the method's utility. The planned integration into Nilearn suggests strong potential for adoption by the neuroimaging community.

## Strengths
- Addresses a meaningful problem in neuroimaging with clear practical impact
- Well-motivated extension of existing dictionary learning framework
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple metrics
- Excellent computational efficiency (only 3x slower than SODL)
- Strong results showing spatial regularization significantly improves interpretability
- Clear connection between method and brain imaging domain
- Plan for open-source implementation in Nilearn

## Weaknesses
- The theoretical analysis of the Laplacian penalty's impact on the optimization is somewhat superficial
- The comparison to other spatially-regularized approaches (e.g., TV-regularized dictionary learning) could be more thorough
- Some claims about the necessity of spatial regularization could benefit from more nuanced discussion (e.g., how it might interact with different types of neuroimaging data)
- The hyperparameter selection procedure, while described, could be more detailed for reproducibility

## Overall Assessment
This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to both machine learning and neuroimaging. The method is novel, well-motivated, and rigorously evaluated. The practical significance for neuroimaging is clear, and the computational efficiency makes it applicable to large-scale studies. The paper is clearly written and technically sound.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound and well-executed approach that advances the state of the art in brain region discovery. The method's practical utility and scalability to large datasets represent significant contributions to the field. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the paper's overall quality and impact.

## Pro/Con for Acceptance

**Pros:**
- Novel combination of sparse and spatially-structured dictionary learning
- Strong empirical validation on large-scale neuroimaging data
- Clear demonstration of improved interpretability and stability
- Practical computational efficiency (3x slower than SODL)
- Well-motivated problem with clear neuroimaging impact
- Plans for open-source implementation

**Cons:**
- Theoretical analysis of spatial regularization could be deeper
- Comparison to alternative spatial regularization methods could be more comprehensive
- Some hyperparameter selection details could be more specific

The paper represents a significant contribution that would be valuable to both the ML and neuroimaging communities. It advances the state of the art in a meaningful way with clear practical applications.
