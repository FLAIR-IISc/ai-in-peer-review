## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a novel extension to online dictionary learning (ODL) for identifying brain regions from fMRI data, incorporating both sparsity and spatial smoothness constraints. The core idea of leveraging population variability to define ROIs is a sensible one, building on existing clustering and decomposition approaches like PCA/ICA. The motivation, clearly outlining the limitations of univariate models and the challenges in defining ROIs, is well-presented. The authors aim to address a significant problem – principled ROI definition – and position their work as a bridge between signal processing techniques and neuroimaging analysis.

However, while the idea is potentially valuable, the execution and presentation suffer from numerous issues, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to a lack of rigor in the experimental validation. The level of detail devoted to implementing seemingly trivial aspects of the algorithm overshadows a lack of robust justification for key methodological choices. Overall, the paper feels underdeveloped and lacking the polish expected of a NIPS submission.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** The problem of defining meaningful ROIs in neuroimaging is a long-standing challenge.
*   **Principled Approach:** Extending ODL with spatial regularization offers a theoretically sound framework for incorporating prior knowledge about brain structure.
*   **Scalability:** The use of online learning is crucial for handling the large datasets common in neuroimaging.
*   **Initial Results:** The experiments show promising qualitative results and some quantitative gains over standard ODL, particularly in capturing structured patterns.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigor in Hyperparameter Selection:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning (α, γ, τ) is superficial. While the authors mention K-fold cross-validation, the specifics are missing. Figure 1 presents a vague visual assessment without any quantitative metrics or justification for the chosen values in the main experiments. The claim of a “safe range” is unsubstantiated.
*   **Computational Cost Concerns:** While claiming only a "factor of 2 or 3" increase in running time compared to standard ODL, the experiment section demonstrates a 3x increase. The authors dismiss this as minor, yet given the scale of neuroimaging data, even a modest increase is significant. Furthermore, the details on computational scaling with dataset size (n & p) are absent.
*   **Insufficient Comparison to Established Methods:** The comparison to CanICA is hampered by the application of smoothing to CanICA data, creating an unfair comparison. The authors acknowledge this but do not fully justify the choice, nor explore the performance of Smooth-SODL on pre-smoothed data for a fair comparison. The rationale for using only *one* ICA implementation is also unclear.
*   **Overly Detailed Algorithm Description, Underdeveloped Theoretical Justification:** The paper spends an inordinate amount of space detailing the BCD algorithm, including step-by-step derivations of standard results (e.g., the ridge regression update). This level of detail is unnecessary for a high-level NIPS paper, especially given the lack of novel algorithmic contributions. Conversely, the theoretical justification for the Laplacian penalty – why it’s the *right* way to enforce smoothness – is weak.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** Several minor notational issues detract from clarity. For example, the definition of ΩLap(v) includes both summation indices `k` and `p` which seems redundant and confusing. The meaning of 't' changes several times throughout the text.
*   **Vague Language & Unsubstantiated Claims:** Phrases like "good grounds to assume" and "safe range" lack precision. The claim that the algorithm is "parameter-free" is misleading, as α, γ, and τ are clearly hyperparameters that require tuning.
*   **Missing Error Analysis:** There's no discussion of failure cases or limitations of the method. Do certain brain regions pose challenges for this approach? Are there specific dataset characteristics that impact performance?
*   **Missing Supplementary Material:** Despite mention in the text, the supplementary material was not provided. This is crucial given the volume of detail.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Addresses an important problem in neuroimaging.
*   Offers a theoretically grounded approach that builds on existing ODL methods.
*   Demonstrates initial promising results on a large dataset.

**Con:**

*   The experimental validation is weak and lacks rigor.
*   Hyperparameter selection is not adequately addressed.
*   The paper is overly detailed in some areas and underdeveloped in others.
*   Numerous minor flaws in notation and presentation.
*   The computational overhead, although modest, is not properly justified.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting idea, but the lack of rigor in the experimental setup, the superficial discussion of hyperparameters, and the numerous minor flaws significantly detract from its overall quality. While the authors demonstrate some improvements over standard ODL, the evidence is not compelling enough to justify acceptance at NIPS. The current version reads more like a preliminary study than a fully developed research contribution.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. The authors should significantly strengthen the experimental validation, provide a more thorough justification for their methodological choices, address the notational inconsistencies, and refine the presentation before resubmitting. A more focused paper, prioritizing theoretical contributions and rigorous evaluation, would be more suitable for NIPS.
