

# Peer Review: "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel method called Smooth-SODL (Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning) for learning brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors address the problem of principled ROI (region of interest) definition in neuroimaging, which is typically handled through anatomical segmentation or cross-modality correspondence, both of which have limitations. Instead, they propose to use inter-subject variability in statistical maps to define brain regions, leveraging the spatial structure of brain activity patterns.

The core idea extends the online dictionary-learning framework from [16] by adding a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on the dictionary atoms (which represent brain regions). This results in sparse, spatially localized patterns that better match known functional brain organization. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves a 2-3x computational overhead compared to standard SODL while providing significantly more interpretable and spatially structured brain regions.

The paper evaluates their method on 500 subjects from the Human Connectome Project dataset, comparing against SODL and CanICA. Results show that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable brain regions, better captures inter-subject variability (especially in small data regimes), and achieves better performance in predicting behavioral variables compared to SODL and thresholded CanICA.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound approach. The formulation of the Smooth-SODL model (equation 2) is clear and properly grounded in the literature on sparse dictionary learning and spatial regularization. The theoretical analysis of the optimization problem, including the convergence guarantees, is rigorous and well-supported.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experimental section is thorough, comparing the method across multiple metrics: visual quality of dictionaries, explained variance, stability, reproducibility, and prediction of behavioral variables. The learning curves showing the advantage of spatial regularization in small data regimes (Table 1) are particularly compelling.

3. **Practical Significance**: The method addresses a real problem in neuroimaging with clear practical implications. The authors provide evidence that their approach outperforms existing methods, especially in small data regimes where spatial regularization provides a 14-fold improvement in explained variance. The implementation as part of the Nilearn library (mentioned in the conclusion) will facilitate adoption by the neuroimaging community.

4. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper demonstrates that the spatial regularization can be incorporated with only a 2-3x computational overhead compared to standard SODL, which is impressive given that previous structured approaches were much slower. This makes the method suitable for large-scale neuroimaging datasets.

5. **Careful Discussion of Limitations**: The authors acknowledge the challenges in hyperparameter selection and provide a detailed discussion of the trade-offs between sparsity, interpretability, and explained variance (Figure 1). They also note the limitations of their approach, particularly the need for careful selection of γ to avoid degenerate maps.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Recent Structured Methods**: While the paper mentions related work [1, 25, 23] that use structured approaches, it doesn't fully contrast their method with more recent structured dictionary learning approaches. For example, the paper could have compared with methods like those in [25] that use TV-`1 regularization, explaining why their Laplacian approach is preferable.

2. **Insufficient Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: While the paper discusses hyperparameter selection, it could have provided more concrete guidance on how to select γ and τ in practice. The "safe range" mentioned in Figure 1 is qualitative; a more quantitative analysis of how sensitive the results are to these parameters would strengthen the paper.

3. **Limited Analysis of Spatial Structure**: The paper claims that the Laplacian penalty creates "blobs" rather than scattered patterns, but doesn't provide quantitative evidence of the spatial structure of the learned atoms. Metrics like "blobiness" or spatial compactness could have been computed to better support this claim.

4. **Comparison with Other Structured Methods**: The comparison with CanICA is good, but the paper doesn't compare with other state-of-the-art structured methods for brain imaging. For example, how does Smooth-SODL compare with the "structured sparse PCA" approach in [14] or the "multi-subject dictionary learning" in [25]?

5. **Reproducibility Concerns**: The paper mentions that the implementation will be available in Nilearn, but doesn't provide a DOI or link to the code repository. While this is a future work item, it would be helpful to know when this will be available for reproducibility.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neuroimaging analysis. The problem of ROI definition is important and well-motivated, and the authors provide a principled solution that is both theoretically sound and practically useful. The method addresses a real limitation in current neuroimaging analysis pipelines and provides clear evidence of its advantages over existing approaches.

The paper is well-written, technically rigorous, and provides comprehensive experimental validation. The computational efficiency of the method (only 2-3x slower than standard SODL) makes it particularly appealing for large-scale neuroimaging studies, which is a significant advantage over previous structured approaches.

While the paper has some minor weaknesses in terms of comparison with recent structured methods and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, these do not detract from the overall contribution. The method represents a meaningful advance in the application of structured dictionary learning to neuroimaging.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper for NIPS. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The method has practical significance for neuroimaging analysis and is well-evaluated with appropriate metrics.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the related work section, provide a more detailed comparison with recent structured dictionary learning methods in neuroimaging, particularly those using spatial constraints.

2. Include quantitative metrics for the spatial structure of the learned atoms (e.g., spatial compactness, blobiness) to better support the claim that the Laplacian penalty creates "blobs" rather than scattered patterns.

3. Provide more concrete guidance on hyperparameter selection, perhaps including a recommended range for γ and τ based on the data characteristics.

4. In the experiments, consider including a comparison with the TV-`1 method from [1] to directly demonstrate the advantages of the Laplacian approach.

5. Add a note about the availability of the code in the final version of the paper, including a link to the Nilearn repository once it's available.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Addresses a significant problem in neuroimaging with a well-motivated solution
- Provides clear evidence of advantages over existing methods, especially in small data regimes
- Maintains computational efficiency (2-3x overhead) while adding spatial structure
- Well-written, technically sound, and thoroughly evaluated
- Implementation will be made available in Nilearn, ensuring accessibility to the community

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Limited comparison with recent structured methods in neuroimaging
- Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis could be more quantitative
- Limited quantitative evidence for the spatial structure of learned atoms
- Code availability is mentioned but not yet implemented

The pro-acceptance arguments clearly outweigh the con-acceptance arguments, and the paper makes a significant contribution to the field that aligns well with NIPS's focus on machine learning approaches to complex data analysis.
