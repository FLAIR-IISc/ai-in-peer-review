Summary
The paper proposes Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), an online matrix factorization framework tailored to neuroimaging that augments sparse dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty on dictionary atoms to induce spatially localized, smooth “blobs.” Codes are obtained via ridge regression (with the option for l1), and dictionary updates are solved by a convex denoising subproblem with Laplacian regularization and simple convex constraints (l1 ball or simplex), handled efficiently with a few FISTA iterations within a block-coordinate descent scheme. The authors argue that this preserves the scalability and convergence properties of online DL (Mairal et al.), while producing interpretable, structured components suitable for ROI definition. On HCP task fMRI maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40), they compare Smooth-SODL to vanilla SODL and CanICA, showing: improved interpretability and stability, competitive explained variance (EV), better early-sample learning curves, modest computational overhead (~3x SODL) and faster than CanICA, and competitive predictive performance on behavioral variables (below CanICA but above SODL and thresholded CanICA). They also discuss hyperparameter effects and provide a heuristic time-varying schedule for α and guidance for γ.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on online dictionary learning (Mairal JMLR’10; Mensch ICML’16) and structured priors used in neuroimaging (GraphNet, TV-ℓ1; Abraham MICCAI’13; Jenatton et al. AISTATS’10). TV- and GraphNet-style regularization have been used to impose spatial structure in atlas learning, but previous structured methods are largely offline and slow; online methods typically omit spatial structure. This paper’s novelty is integrating a Laplacian penalty into an online DL framework with minimal computational overhead and demonstrating large-scale feasibility and practical benefits in neuroimaging.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a simple, scalable way to inject spatial structure (via Laplacian) into online DL. This is a meaningful step for population neuroimaging where interpretability and reproducibility matter.
- Algorithmic clarity and practicality: the dictionary update reduces to a convex denoising step with a simple projection (l1 ball or simplex). Using FISTA and mini-batches yields good speed; careful discussion of Lipschitz constants and step sizes for stability.
- Empirical scale and relevance: evaluated on large HCP fMRI maps; comparisons to commonly used baselines (CanICA, SODL); comprehensive qualitative and quantitative metrics (interpretability, EV, stability, prediction).
- Early-data regime advantage: strong improvement over SODL with few subjects, which is valuable in many neuroimaging contexts with limited cohorts.
- Interpretability/stability: smoother, more localized atoms and improved split-half stability over SODL; better interpretability than CanICA without post hoc thresholding.

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity and notation: Several typos and confusing expressions (e.g., “max” instead of “min” in Section 2 bullets; Eq. (7) notation Fγ(At[j,j]/t)−1(…) is hard to parse). Please clean up the derivations, especially the dependence of the FISTA Lipschitz constant on At[j,j]/t and γ, and the precise optimization being solved (smooth + indicator of C with projected gradient).
- Convergence under time-varying hyperparameters: The theoretical convergence discussion references [16], but αt ~ t−1/2 and γt scaling are introduced. Do the convergence guarantees still hold with these time-varying parameters? A brief justification would strengthen the paper.
- Choice of Laplacian vs TV: TV-ℓ1 yields sharper, compact ROIs than Laplacian smoothing. The paper cites TV work but does not empirically compare (even on a smaller subset) to show the trade-off between speed and spatial specificity. A small ablation (TV vs Laplacian vs none) would be informative.
- Codes: Experiments appear to use ridge codes; in settings where each subject should load on a few components, l1 codes may be more appropriate. An ablation (ridge vs l1 codes) would clarify when each is preferable.
- Positivity/constraint set: Using the simplex Sp(τ) imposes positivity and l1 norm. This is a design choice with strong effects on interpretability and identifiability; please justify and report if nonnegativity is essential to the observed gains.
- Metrics and statistics: Stability is reported as mean correlation; please add confidence intervals or statistical tests. Similarly, provide details for cross-validation used to tune τ and γ, and for predictive models (regularization, CV splits).
- Comparators: Beyond CanICA and SODL, structured NMF or GraphNet-based matrix factorization, and recent scalable structured SPCA could be relevant baselines. Even if not fully scalable, a downsampled comparison would position the contribution more clearly.
- Claims of “1–2 FISTA iterations to 1e-6 accuracy” seem optimistic; likely due to warm starts and small effective condition number. Please clarify stopping criteria and practical tolerances used.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the subproblems are convex, the BCD scheme is standard, and the computational story is credible. However, the presentation needs tightening (notation/typos), and the theoretical guarantees under time-varying α, γ should be stated precisely or limited to empirical observations.

Clarity
Generally well organized, but the optimization section needs clearer notation and a more explicit pseudo-code for the FISTA-with-projection step. A figure illustrating the effect of τ and γ on atom structure would help. Fix typos and minor grammatical issues throughout.

Originality
Moderate but meaningful. While Laplacian/GraphNet/TV penalties are known, bringing spatial structure into large-scale online DL with near-vanilla complexity and demonstrating its benefits on massive neuroimaging data is a useful and nontrivial advance. The paper is more than incremental for the neuroimaging DL community, though methodologically it is an elegant combination rather than a fundamentally new optimization technique.

Significance
High for practitioners of neuroimaging ML: this approach can become a default for learning interpretable ROIs at scale. The early-sample advantage and stability gains are valuable. Broader ML significance is moderate, as the core ideas (proximal updates with Laplacian) are established; the contribution is in scalable integration and compelling application.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method enabling structured online DL at neuroimaging scale
- Clear gains in interpretability and stability with modest runtime overhead
- Solid empirical evidence on a large, relevant dataset
- Useful guidance on hyperparameters and implementation

Cons for acceptance
- Somewhat incremental algorithmically; novelty is integrative
- Clarity/notation issues, and limited theoretical discussion for time-varying schedules
- Missing comparisons to TV-based and other structured baselines
- Limited statistical rigor in some metrics

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a practically important and timely idea for large-scale, interpretable brain mapping with online DL. With improved clarity and a couple of targeted ablations or clarifications (TV vs Laplacian; ridge vs l1 codes; convergence under schedules), this will be a valuable contribution.
