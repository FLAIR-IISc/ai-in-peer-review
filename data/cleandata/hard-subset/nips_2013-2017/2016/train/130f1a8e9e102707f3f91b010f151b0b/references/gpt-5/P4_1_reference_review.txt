Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method for neuroimaging that enforces sparse and spatially smooth dictionary atoms. The formulation extends standard online dictionary learning (Mairal et al., JMLR 2010) by adding a Laplacian penalty on each atom to promote blob-like, spatially localized components, while keeping a sparsity-inducing constraint (e.g., simplex or ℓ1-ball) on atoms. Codes are obtained via ridge regression per sample, and the dictionary is updated via block-coordinate descent; each atom update solves a convex quadratic with Laplacian regularization and projection onto the constraint set using FISTA. The approach connects to GraphNet-style smoothness (Grosenick et al., NeuroImage 2013) and contrasts with prior structured decompositions using TV-ℓ1 penalties (e.g., Abraham MICCAI; Varoquaux IPMI; Dohmatob PRNI) that rely on slow alternating minimization. Experiments on HCP task-fMRI activation maps (n=500, p≈2.6×10^5) compare Smooth-SODL to standard SODL and CanICA (Varoquaux et al., NeuroImage) on interpretability, explained variance, stability/reproducibility, and behavioral prediction. The method reportedly runs within a 2–3× overhead relative to vanilla online dictionary learning and yields more structured atoms and better stability, especially in small-data regimes.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Formulation: The objective is a standard sum of reconstruction + ridge code penalty, with per-atom Laplacian regularization and atom-wise sparsity constraints. The per-atom subproblem is convex and handled by projected gradient (FISTA) with projection onto ℓ1-ball/simplex (for which linear-time routines exist).
- Algorithm: The block-coordinate online scheme mirrors Mairal et al. The stated Lipschitz bound for the Laplacian term uses λmax(Δ) ≤ 4D on a regular grid, which is a reasonable upper bound, though boundary conditions and discretization should be specified. The claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations suffice to reach 1e−6 accuracy is unlikely in general; more realistic is that warm starts yield adequate approximate solutions in practice. The suggested time-varying γt to control the effective smoothness weight γ̃ = γ/(At[j,j]/t) is plausible for numerical stability but alters the underlying objective; convergence guarantees under changing γt are not provided.
- Convergence: The paper argues that standard assumptions for convergence of the online alternating scheme are satisfied, but since the per-atom subproblems are not necessarily solved to optimality and γ is adapted over time, the claim should be qualified. Nonetheless, the approach is consistent with established practice for online dictionary learning with approximate inner solves.
- Experiments: The HCP-based experiments are large scale and evaluate multiple criteria (qualitative maps, explained variance, stability across splits, behavioral prediction). However, key details are missing for full reproducibility: exact hyperparameter grids, chosen τ, the γ schedule, stopping criteria for FISTA/BCD, and the regression protocol for behavioral prediction (regularizer, cross-validation). Confidence intervals or variance across splits are not reported.

Clarity
The manuscript communicates the high-level idea and algorithmic structure, but presentation quality needs improvement:
- Several typographical issues and incomplete references (missing years/pages) make it difficult to trace related work precisely.
- Some equations contain minor inconsistencies (e.g., notation for Fγ, definition of LF). Clarify Δ and boundary conditions, and explicitly write the FISTA update used (gradient step + projection).
- Figures and tables are referenced (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Table 1), but the text includes only partial descriptions; quantitative results should be fully reported in-text or with clear captions.
- The comparison setup (e.g., smoothing for CanICA, thresholding for tCanICA) should be justified and standardized across methods.

Originality
The novelty is incremental but nontrivial: integrating a Laplacian prior into an online dictionary-learning framework with efficient per-atom projected gradient updates, and demonstrating that this yields structured atoms at modest computational cost. Spatial regularization of factors (GraphNet/TV) and structured sparse decompositions are well known; the contribution is primarily an efficient online instantiation for high-dimensional neuroimaging. Prior structured methods cited (e.g., TV-ℓ1 constrained dictionary learning) are slower; however, comparison to a strong structured baseline (TV or GraphNet in a batch setting) is missing.

Significance
For neuroimaging applications requiring interpretable, spatially coherent components at scale, the method is practically useful. The empirical results suggest improved stability and early-sample efficiency, with similar asymptotic performance to SODL, and modest runtime overhead. The significance would be strengthened by broader validation (rest fMRI, other tasks/datasets), comparisons to additional structured baselines, and more rigorous quantification of stability and prediction performance.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important practical need: scalable estimation of spatially structured, sparse components from large neuroimaging cohorts.
- Simple algorithmic extension with modest overhead; leverages standard tools (FISTA, fast projections) and fits well in existing pipelines (Nilearn).
- Empirical evidence of improved interpretability and stability, and better sample efficiency than unstructured SODL in small-data regimes.
- Clear connection to established models (Mairal online DL, GraphNet), facilitating adoption.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty; spatial smoothness penalties are standard, and the main contribution is engineering the online update.
- Theoretical treatment is light; the time-varying γ schedule and approximate inner solves call for clearer convergence guarantees or ablations.
- Experimental comparisons are limited: no direct comparison to TV-regularized or GraphNet baselines; only one dataset/task; limited reporting of uncertainty.
- Presentation issues (typos, incomplete references, figure clarity) and incomplete methodological details hinder reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a precise description of the per-atom update (gradient expression, step size using λmax(Δ), projection operator), stopping criteria, and the γ/α schedules; report the actual values used (e.g., τ, γ=1e3/1e4).
- Include ablations on γ, τ, and the number of FISTA iterations; quantify the impact on runtime and performance.
- Add comparisons to a strong structured baseline (e.g., TV-regularized dictionary learning or GraphNet factorization), even if in smaller-scale settings.
- Report stability and prediction results with confidence intervals across multiple splits; standardize preprocessing across methods (justify smoothing for CanICA) and clarify the prediction pipeline.
- Fix references and clarify relations to Mensch et al. (ICML) and structured sparse PCA (Jenatton et al.) in terms of scalability and structure.

Overall, this is a solid, practically motivated extension with encouraging empirical results, but with incremental novelty and presentation gaps that should be addressed.
