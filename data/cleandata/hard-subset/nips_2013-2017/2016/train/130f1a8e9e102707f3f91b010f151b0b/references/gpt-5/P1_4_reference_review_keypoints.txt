- Proposes Smooth-SODL: online dictionary learning for neuroimaging that enforces spatial smoothness via a Laplacian penalty and sparsity via l1-ball/simplex constraints.
- Codes are computed by ridge regression (authors note l1 codes are possible); dictionary updated by online block-coordinate descent using sufficient statistics.
- Atom updates reduce to constrained quadratic (GraphNet-like) denoising solved by projected first-order methods (FISTA).
- Authors discuss Lipschitz constants and a step-size schedule to avoid vanishing step sizes, but experiments use fixed γ (1e3–1e4).
- Evaluated on 500 HCP task-fMRI GLM Z-maps (Math vs Story) and compared to vanilla online DL (SODL) and CanICA.
- Smooth-SODL yields more spatially structured, interpretable atoms and improved stability across splits.
- Runtime: Smooth-SODL is 2–3× slower than SODL but faster than CanICA.
- Explained variance: better than SODL in low-data regimes, competitive asymptotically; behavioral prediction: CanICA best overall, Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL and thresholded CanICA.
- Relation to prior work: integrates Laplacian smoothness into online DL, close to GraphNet and prior structured DL but novel in online, scalable form.
- Technical soundness: overall reasonable formulation; projected gradient/FISTA appropriate for convex atom subproblem.
- Major theoretical concern: convergence claims rely on exact/controlled inexact block updates from [16], but authors use only 1–2 FISTA iterations without quantifying inexactness or summable errors.
- Implausible claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e-6 accuracy for high-dimensional atoms; stopping criteria and actual inner-loop accuracy must be quantified.
- Lipschitz/step-size issues: mixing of atom-specific scaling and proposed γt schedule vs. fixed γ in experiments needs reconciliation.
- Laplacian penalty: Δ’s spectrum and boundary conditions (and correct Lipschitz bounds) are not clearly specified.
- Constraint choice: using simplex (nonnegativity + l1) is strong for fMRI which may have signed effects; need justification and results for signed l1-balls.
- Empirical baselines are limited: no comparisons to other spatially regularized DL methods (e.g., TV-l1 DL, GraphNet DL).
- Coding fairness: SODL evaluated with ridge codes while SODL typically uses l1 codes—need fair comparisons or justification for ridge.
- Prediction protocol lacks detail (model, regularization, nested CV); RAW baseline feature selection could leak if not nested.
- Stability metric and matching procedure need fuller description, number of splits/repeats, and statistical significance.
- Clarity issues: typos, population vs empirical risk notation mismatch, and ambiguous claim “no step size to tune” since inner FISTA needs step size.
- Originality: moderately original—bringing Laplacian smoothness into online DL is a useful, practical incremental contribution.
- Significance: useful for neuroimaging practitioners due to interpretability and scalability, but quantitative gains are mixed and predictive performance trails CanICA.
- Pros: scalable implementation, improved spatial structure and stability, comprehensive HCP-scale experiments, careful runtime reporting.
- Cons: weak convergence justification for inexact inner solves, missing structured baselines, modeling choices not justified, limited dataset/task diversity, predictive performance lags.
- Suggestions: provide convergence guarantees or empirical objective plots for different inner iterations; compare to at least one spatially-regularized DL baseline; report signed l1 constraints and l1-coded SODL; clarify prediction CV and feature selection; release code at submission.
- Recommendation: borderline accept contingent on strengthening convergence arguments and broadening baselines.
