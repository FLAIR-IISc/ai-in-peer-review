### Summary of the paper

This paper introduces "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning" (Smooth-SODL), a method for learning spatially coherent brain regions of interest (ROIs) from large-scale neuroimaging data. The central idea is to model the brain maps of a population of subjects as linear combinations of a shared set of basis functions, or "atoms," which represent the ROIs. The method extends the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) [16] by incorporating a novel regularization term. Specifically, in addition to an `l1`-norm constraint on the dictionary atoms to enforce sparsity, the authors add a Laplacian penalty to promote spatial smoothness and compactness, resulting in more anatomically plausible, "blob-like" regions. The online nature of the algorithm makes it scalable to datasets with a very large number of subjects (large `n`) and voxels (large `p`), a setting where traditional batch methods are computationally infeasible. The authors derive a block-coordinate descent (BCD) algorithm to solve the optimization problem and demonstrate its effectiveness on a large task-fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (HCP). They compare their method against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical ICA (CanICA), evaluating the results on qualitative interpretability, explained variance, stability, and a downstream task of predicting behavioral scores.

The work positions itself as a bridge between two lines of research. On one hand, there are methods that use structured penalties (e.g., Total Variation) to learn brain atlases [1, 25], but these typically rely on slow, alternating batch optimization schemes. On the other hand, there are highly scalable online dictionary learning methods [16, 17], which have been applied to neuroimaging but lack explicit spatial structure in their priors. This paper's primary contribution is to integrate a spatial smoothness prior into a scalable online framework, preserving computational efficiency while improving the neuroscientific plausibility of the learned components.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The model is a well-motivated extension of an established dictionary learning framework. The optimization procedure, based on Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) with FISTA for the dictionary update step, is appropriate and correctly derived. The authors provide a sound analysis of the computational complexity, noting the dependence of the FISTA step size on the Laplacian regularization parameter `gamma` (Eq. 9), which is an important practical consideration. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments on a large, public dataset (HCP). The authors are reasonably careful in their evaluation, reporting performance on both training and test data and using multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, prediction accuracy) to assess the model. The inclusion of learning curves (Table 1) effectively demonstrates the value of the spatial regularizer, especially in lower-data regimes, which is a key strength of the work.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem statement, proposed model, and algorithm are presented logically and are easy to follow. The motivation for combining sparsity with a smoothness prior is well-articulated in the context of neuroimaging. The figures, particularly the qualitative comparison of dictionary atoms (Fig. 2a), are effective at illustrating the benefits of the proposed approach over the baselines. The experimental setup and results are described with sufficient detail for an expert to understand the main findings. However, some details regarding hyper-parameter tuning could be more explicit. While the paper discusses the trade-offs and suggests cross-validation, the exact protocol and the use of the adaptive `alpha` from Eq. 10 in the final experiments are not fully specified, which could present a minor hurdle for exact reproduction.

**Originality:** The core algorithmic components (online learning, BCD, `l1` sparsity, Laplacian smoothing) are not new in themselves. The novelty lies in their specific combination and application to solve a significant problem in computational neuroscience. The work successfully extends the online framework of [16] to handle a structured spatial prior, which previous work had primarily tackled with slower batch algorithms [1, 25]. This is a non-trivial engineering contribution that makes structured decomposition methods practical for massive datasets. The paper adequately references related work and clearly situates its contribution within the existing literature.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is highly significant for the neuroimaging community. As datasets like the HCP and UK Biobank become standard, the need for scalable analysis methods that can extract meaningful, structured patterns is paramount. The proposed Smooth-SODL method directly addresses this need. It provides a principled, computationally efficient way to perform data-driven ROI definition, which is a fundamental task in brain mapping. The improved interpretability and stability of the learned components compared to unstructured SODL and the noisy maps from ICA are substantial benefits. The fact that the implementation is planned for inclusion in a widely used open-source library (Nilearn) further enhances its potential impact, making it likely that researchers and practitioners will use and build upon these ideas.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Practical Contribution:** The paper presents a novel and highly practical method that successfully combines the scalability of online dictionary learning with the desirable properties of structured spatial priors. This addresses a clear gap in the existing literature.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The method is validated on a large-scale, challenging, and relevant real-world dataset (HCP). The experiments are comprehensive and the results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed model over strong baselines in terms of interpretability, stability, and data efficiency.
3.  **High Potential Impact:** The work tackles a fundamental problem in neuroimaging analysis. Given its computational efficiency and the quality of the results, the method is likely to be widely adopted by the community, especially with its planned integration into the Nilearn library.
4.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is well-written, the method is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by the evidence provided.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Incremental Methodological Novelty:** The core optimization techniques are standard. The main contribution is the application-driven integration of a Laplacian penalty into an existing online framework, which could be viewed as an incremental advance from a pure machine learning perspective.
2.  **Predictive Performance:** In the behavioral prediction task (Fig. 2c), the proposed method is outperformed by the dense CanICA model on test data. While the authors rightly emphasize the interpretability benefits of Smooth-SODL, this result highlights a potential trade-off between interpretability and raw predictive power that could have been discussed in more detail.
3.  **Missing Direct Comparison:** The paper motivates its online approach by contrasting it with slow, structured batch methods [1, 25]. However, a direct computational and qualitative comparison with one of these methods on a smaller dataset would have provided a more concrete benchmark to quantify the speed/quality trade-off that this work addresses.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its practical significance, strong empirical results, and the successful integration of structured priors into a scalable framework, far outweigh the weaknesses. It represents a solid and valuable contribution to the field of machine learning for neuroimaging.
