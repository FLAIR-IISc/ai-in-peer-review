

# Peer Review: "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, a novel extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging data that incorporates spatial structure into the learned brain regions (dictionary atoms). The authors address the problem of principled ROI definition by leveraging inter-subject variability in brain imaging data, rather than relying on anatomical segmentation or standard ICA/PCA approaches. The key innovation is the incorporation of a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on the dictionary atoms, while maintaining the computational efficiency of online dictionary learning. The method is evaluated on HCP fMRI data (500 subjects, 260k voxels) and compared against SODL and CanICA. The results demonstrate that Smooth-SODL produces more spatially structured, interpretable brain regions that better capture inter-subject variability, especially with limited data.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and relevance**: The paper clearly articulates the limitations of current ROI definition methods (anatomical segmentation, ICA-based approaches) and makes a compelling case for leveraging inter-subject variability to define brain regions. This is a significant challenge in neuroimaging, and the authors position their work well within this context.

2. **Technical soundness**: The mathematical formulation is well-defined, and the algorithmic extensions to incorporate spatial structure are carefully derived. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the optimization problem, including convergence properties and computational complexity considerations.

3. **Computational efficiency**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that the spatial regularization adds only a factor of 2-3 to the computational cost (compared to 5x for CanICA), which is crucial for large-scale neuroimaging applications where n and p are both large.

4. **Thorough experimental validation**: The experiments are comprehensive, comparing against multiple baselines (SODL and CanICA) across multiple metrics (visual quality, explained variance, reproducibility, predictive performance). The learning curve analysis (Table 1) particularly demonstrates the value of spatial regularization with limited data.

5. **Practical implementation**: The authors plan to integrate their method into Nilearn, which will make it accessible to the neuroimaging community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with structured dictionary learning methods**: While the paper positions itself against standard SODL and CanICA, it fails to compare against other structured dictionary learning approaches like TV-L1 regularization [1, 25] that have been specifically applied to brain imaging. This is a significant omission since these methods directly address the same problem of spatial structure. For example, [1] uses TV-L1 for brain atlas extraction, and the authors should explicitly discuss how their Laplacian approach differs and why it's superior for their application.

2. **Insufficient theoretical analysis of spatial regularization**: The paper claims that "spatial regularization is particularly beneficial when data are limited," but provides no theoretical justification for this claim. The learning curve results in Table 1 support this empirically, but a theoretical analysis (e.g., relating the Laplacian penalty to the effective number of parameters) would strengthen the paper significantly.

3. **Ambiguous definition of "structured"**: The paper uses the term "structured" frequently but doesn't provide a precise mathematical definition of what this means in the context of brain regions. Is it related to connectivity patterns, functional networks, or just spatial smoothness? Clarifying this would help readers understand the specific type of structure being imposed.

4. **Hyperparameter selection lacks principled justification**: While the paper discusses the impact of hyperparameters (γ, τ, α), it relies on k-fold cross-validation for selection without discussing the computational cost of this approach for large-scale neuroimaging data. The authors mention an adaptive choice for α (equation 10), but don't explain why this works well theoretically.

5. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't address potential limitations of the Laplacian approach, such as:
   - How the method might perform on highly heterogeneous populations
   - Whether the Laplacian penalty favors certain spatial patterns (e.g., circular vs. elongated regions)
   - How the method would scale to even larger datasets (10k+ subjects, which is becoming common)

## Critical Evaluation Against Review Criteria

### Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The methodology is carefully derived, and the experiments are well-designed. However, the paper would be stronger with more rigorous theoretical analysis of the spatial regularization and comparisons with other structured approaches. The claim that "spatial regularization is particularly beneficial with limited data" needs stronger theoretical support beyond the empirical learning curve results.

### Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the algorithm descriptions are detailed. However, the explanation of the Laplacian penalty (Equation 4) could be clearer, and the paper would benefit from a more precise definition of "structured" in the context of brain regions. The choice of the simplex constraint (Sp(τ)) over other sparsity-inducing constraints (like the L1-ball) is not adequately justified.

### Originality

The extension of online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial structure is novel for neuroimaging applications. However, the use of Laplacian regularization for spatial smoothness is not entirely new in imaging applications (as cited in [1, 25]). The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how their approach differs from these prior works and why their specific formulation is better suited for brain imaging data. The claim that their method "enjoys the same convergence guarantees as the basic unstructured online dictionary-learning" is somewhat vague and could be clarified.

### Significance

The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging (principled ROI definition) with significant potential impact. The method could help researchers better understand inter-subject variability, which is crucial for personalized medicine and understanding neurological disorders. The demonstration that the method works well with limited data (14x better explained variance with 17 subjects) is particularly valuable for studies with small sample sizes. However, the significance would be enhanced by discussing concrete applications in clinical or research settings.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a comparison against TV-L1 structured dictionary learning methods (e.g., [1]) to better position the contribution.

2. Provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis of why spatial regularization helps with limited data, perhaps by relating the Laplacian penalty to model complexity.

3. Clarify the definition of "structured" in the context of brain regions and discuss how it relates to known functional organization.

4. Discuss the computational cost of hyperparameter selection (k-fold cross-validation) for large-scale neuroimaging data.

5. Add a paragraph discussing the limitations of the Laplacian approach and potential alternatives.

6. Consider adding a visualization of the Laplacian penalty's effect on the dictionary atoms to better illustrate the spatial smoothness.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper with minor revisions. The work is technically sound, addresses an important problem in neuroimaging, and provides a valuable contribution to the field of structured dictionary learning. The method is well-motivated, the experiments are comprehensive, and the computational efficiency is impressive for a structured approach.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly the comparison with other structured dictionary learning methods and providing more theoretical justification for the spatial regularization. These revisions would help position the work more clearly within the existing literature and demonstrate a deeper understanding of the problem.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant problem in neuroimaging (principled ROI definition)
- Presents a computationally efficient method that extends online dictionary learning
- Demonstrates clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods in multiple metrics
- Provides a valuable tool for the neuroimaging community (will be integrated into Nilearn)
- Well-executed experiments on a large public dataset

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited comparison with other structured dictionary learning methods
- Insufficient theoretical analysis of spatial regularization
- Ambiguous definition of "structured" in the context of brain regions
- Limited discussion of the method's limitations
- Hyperparameter selection lacks principled justification

## Final Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging and structured dictionary learning. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and practically useful. With the suggested revisions addressing the comparison with other structured methods and providing more theoretical justification, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
