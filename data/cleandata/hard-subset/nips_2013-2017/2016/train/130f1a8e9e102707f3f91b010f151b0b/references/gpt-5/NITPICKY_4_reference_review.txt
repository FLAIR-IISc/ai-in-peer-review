Summary
This paper proposes Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), an online matrix factorization method for neuroimaging that augments Mairal et al.’s online dictionary learning (SODL) with an additional Laplacian (GraphNet-like) penalty on the dictionary atoms to encourage spatial smoothness (“blobs”) while maintaining sparsity via an ℓ1-type constraint (simplex or ℓ1-ball). The codes are obtained via ridge regression (or optionally ℓ1-coded) and the dictionary is updated by block-coordinate descent (BCD), where each atom update solves a convex denoising problem using FISTA. The authors evaluate on HCP task fMRI maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40), reporting improved interpretability and stability over SODL, and competitive reconstruction and prediction performance versus CanICA.

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on online matrix factorization (Mairal et al., JMLR 2010; Mensch et al., ICML 2016) and on structured penalties in neuroimaging (GraphNet; TV-ℓ1 atlasing). Prior structured dictionary-learning methods exist (e.g., Abraham et al., MICCAI; Varoquaux et al., IPMI/PRNI), though often slow. The novelty here is integrating a Laplacian prior into the online SODL framework to retain scalability.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant problem: deriving interpretable, spatially coherent components across subjects at scale.
- Reasonable modeling choice: Laplacian smoothness plus sparsity improves spatial coherence over pure ℓ1.
- Online algorithm with per-atom convex subproblems; use of historical sufficient statistics At, Bt is standard and effective.
- Empirical results suggest improved qualitative interpretability and higher stability than SODL; reasonable runtime (≈3x SODL) and faster than CanICA in the reported setup.
- Comparison includes reconstruction (explained variance), predictive utility, and stability with Hungarian matching.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological and theoretical:
- Objective formulation: Eq. (2) uses lim n→∞ (1/n) Σ, but experiments optimize a finite-sample online surrogate. If the intent is an expected risk E[·], say so; otherwise remove the limit and clarify the online empirical objective. As written, it is misleading.
- Inconsistent/incorrect assertions:
  - “Each of the terms maxUi … measures how well …” should be min, not max.
  - “BCD is parameter-free; there is no step size to tune” conflicts with the need to choose FISTA step sizes (1/L) and the time-varying schedules αt, γt you propose. Please correct this.
  - You claim each BCD atom subproblem reaches the global optimum, yet later state that “1 or 2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient” and are used in practice. These are incompatible: early stopping does not guarantee optimality.
- Notation issues around Eq. (7): Fγ(At[j,j]/t)−1(·,·) is unclear. You define Fγ̃(v,a)=1/2||v−a||^2 + 1/2 γ̃ v^TΔv; then in (7) γ̃ seems to be γ(At[j,j]/t)−1 (or γ t / At[j,j]?). The factors of t and the role of At[j,j] need to be derived carefully; currently it is opaque and error-prone.
- Lipschitz constant: You state LFγ̃=1+4Dγ̃. This depends on boundary conditions and masking; for a brain mask (irregular domain), the spectral norm of Δ is not trivially 4D nor FFT-diagonalizable. The later claim that updates can be done in the Fourier domain needs strong qualification (regular grid, periodic/Neumann BC, no mask). As written, it is not applicable to typical brain-masked data.
- γ schedule: You suggest γt=O(‖At‖∞,∞/t) to avoid vanishing step sizes. This makes the regularization time-varying, changing the objective during training and breaking the convergence guarantees of [16], which assume a fixed cost (or a specific surrogate sequence). The convergence section does not address this discrepancy. If you keep γt, provide a proof (or cite one) that convergence still holds.
- Choice of constraints: You claim “ℓ1 penalty on the atoms” yet implement a hard constraint C (ℓ1-ball or simplex). Penalty vs constraint are not interchangeable; please be precise. The simplex enforces nonnegativity: discuss implications (e.g., inability to represent deactivations, interaction with possibly negative codes due to ridge).
- Ridge-coded stability: The claim that ridge codes are “stable” relative to ℓ1 is asserted without empirical or theoretical support (conditioning, sensitivity analysis). Provide evidence or a citation that matches this setting.

Experimental design and reporting:
- Missing details for reproducibility: preprocessing (masking, normalization, boundary conditions for Δ), solver tolerances, number of FISTA iterations per atom, number of BCD passes, hardware specs beyond “a single CPU of laptop,” seeds, precise CV protocol. “Code will be available” is insufficient.
- Predictions: The RAW baseline uses top-2000 features “most correlated with the target.” Was feature selection nested within CV folds? If not, there is leakage. Similarly, clarify whether dictionary learning and hyperparameter tuning (γ, τ) were nested within CV. You also switch between R2 and “R-score”; be consistent and define the metric.
- Stability metric: Report means only; no variance/CI across splits. Matching via Hungarian is fine, but provide the number of runs, split strategy, and variability.
- Learning curves (Table 1) and hyperparameter sweeps (Fig. 1) are referenced but not shown in the submission. This seriously limits evaluation.
- Comparators: No direct comparison to TV-ℓ1/GraphNet atlas learning (e.g., Abraham et al.; Varoquaux et al.) in either quality or runtime, which would be the most relevant baseline for spatially structured decompositions. Smoothing CanICA but not SODL/Smooth-SODL also introduces asymmetry.
- Interpretability claims are largely qualitative. Provide quantitative spatial coherence metrics (e.g., cluster sizes, perimeter/area ratios, TV norms) and sparsity levels.
- Under “Convergence,” the arguments from [16] are invoked, but the key assumptions (fixed objective; exact solution of subproblems) are violated by γt and early-stopped FISTA. This section is not convincing.

Presentation and formatting:
- Numerous typos and grammatical errors: “intepretable,” “there is not step size,” “comput,” “volumic,” inconsistent capitalization (e.g., “th Conference”), section numbering (“.2 Practical considerations”), inconsistent notation (V j vs Vj; At[j,j] vs Aj; Bt vs Bjt). Algorithm 2 lists A=[A1|…|Ak]∈Rk×k which is nonsensical; Aj seems to denote columns, but A is k×k.
- References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted (missing years/venues/pages). Several in-text citations do not match the provided entries, and some entries are placeholders.
- Figures and tables (Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Tab. 1) are essential but absent in the text as submitted.

Pros
- Practical and timely problem; plausible approach that improves spatial coherence.
- Scalable online scheme with reasonable runtime.
- Evaluations across reconstruction, stability, and behavioral prediction are welcome.

Cons
- Multiple methodological inconsistencies (objective, convergence, γ schedule, optimality claims).
- Critical experimental and implementation details missing; potential leakage in prediction analysis.
- Key baselines (TV-ℓ1/GraphNet atlas learning) omitted; some comparator choices asymmetric.
- Significant clarity/notation/format issues; missing figures/tables; incomplete references.

Recommendation: Reject

While the core idea is promising and likely useful, the current submission has too many methodological ambiguities, overstatements, and presentation issues to be reliable. I encourage the authors to: (1) correct the objective and convergence arguments; (2) provide full algorithmic details and ablations (including boundary conditions and feasibility of FFT), with fixed γ (or a justified varying scheme) and clear stopping criteria; (3) add rigorous baselines and quantitative interpretability metrics; (4) clean up notation/typos and complete references; and (5) release code and include all figures/tables. With these fixes, the work could be a strong contribution.
