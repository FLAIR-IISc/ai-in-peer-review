

# NIPS 2016 Peer Review

## Summary

This paper proposes "Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning" (Smooth-SODL), a novel method for learning structured brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors address a critical problem in neuroimaging: the lack of principled approaches for defining regions of interest (ROIs) that capture meaningful inter-subject variability. Rather than relying on anatomical segmentation or predefined ROIs, they propose using the variability in statistical maps across a population to define brain regions. Their key innovation is extending the online dictionary learning framework [16] with a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on the dictionary atoms (representing brain regions), while maintaining sparsity.

This work builds on several important threads in the literature: online dictionary learning for large-scale problems [16], structured sparsity for brain imaging [25, 1], and the use of dictionary learning for brain region discovery [23]. The paper makes a compelling case for why structured dictionary learning is needed, as traditional approaches either produce noisy, unstructured regions (ICA/PCA) or require slow optimization (structured dictionary learning).

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation and Problem Definition**: The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing ROI definition methods in neuroimaging and establishes a compelling need for a principled approach. The authors effectively argue that inter-subject variability contains valuable information that is currently underutilized.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The formulation of the Smooth-SODL model is mathematically sound, building logically on the online dictionary learning framework. The authors provide a clear derivation of their BCD algorithm with Laplacian regularization, and the implementation details are well-considered.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The experiments on HCP data (500 subjects, 2.6×10^5 voxels) are thorough and well-designed. The authors compare against relevant baselines (SODL, CanICA) across multiple metrics: visual quality, explained variance, stability, reproducibility, and prediction performance on behavioral variables.

4. **Practical Impact**: The authors demonstrate that their method provides a 3x speedup over existing structured dictionary learning approaches while achieving better interpretability. The planned integration with Nilearn (a widely used neuroimaging library) will facilitate adoption in the field.

5. **Careful Analysis of Hyperparameters**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of the impact of hyperparameters (α, γ, τ), which is crucial for practitioners. The authors identify a "safe range" for these parameters and provide practical guidance for their selection.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification for Spatial Regularization**: While the Laplacian penalty is commonly used for spatial smoothness, the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why it's particularly appropriate for brain imaging data. It would be valuable to compare with other spatial regularizers (e.g., total variation, graph-based smoothness) to establish why Laplacian is the right choice for this problem.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with Structured Dictionary Learning Methods**: The paper claims that "structured decompositions have been limited by computational cost," but the comparison with other structured methods is limited. The authors should directly compare with methods like [25, 1] (which use TV-`1 minimization) to quantify the computational advantage of their approach.

3. **Superficial Treatment of Hyperparameter Selection**: The paper mentions that "a safe range" exists for (γ, τ), but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of how this range varies with different datasets or problem characteristics. The connection between the theoretical requirement for γ (Equation 9) and the practical choice of γ needs more elaboration.

4. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper reports mean explained variance and prediction performance, but doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the differences between methods. For example, the 31% vs 2% explained variance at small sample sizes needs to be validated as statistically significant.

5. **Overlooking Alternative Interpretations of Results**: The paper claims that "Smooth-SODL extracts structured denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability," but doesn't fully address the possibility that the spatial regularization might be capturing noise patterns rather than meaningful structure. A deeper analysis of what the dictionary atoms actually represent (e.g., through meta-analysis with known functional networks) would strengthen the claims.

## Specific Technical Concerns

1. **Convergence Analysis**: While the paper claims convergence to a local optimum, it would be valuable to provide empirical evidence of convergence rates compared to other methods, especially given the increased complexity from the Laplacian penalty.

2. **Comparison with Other Structured Methods**: The paper states "structured priors such as TV-`1 [3] minimization, were used by [1] to extract data-driven state-of-the-art atlases of brain function," but doesn't provide a quantitative comparison of performance between Smooth-SODL and TV-`1 methods.

3. **Algorithm Complexity**: The paper mentions that Smooth-SODL is "slightly less than 3 times slower than SODL," but doesn't provide a detailed complexity analysis. It would be helpful to understand how the complexity scales with the Laplacian penalty.

4. **Data Preprocessing**: The paper mentions that "we did not perform pre-smoothing for the SODL and Smooth-SODL models," but doesn't discuss how this might affect the comparison with CanICA (which required pre-smoothing). This could introduce a bias in the comparison.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of neuroimaging analysis. The authors successfully extend the online dictionary learning framework to incorporate spatial structure with minimal computational overhead, providing a principled approach for ROI definition that better captures inter-subject variability. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods.

The paper's main weaknesses are the limited theoretical justification for the spatial regularization choice, the superficial treatment of hyperparameter selection, and the lack of comprehensive comparison with other structured dictionary learning methods. However, these are relatively minor concerns given the paper's overall quality and significance.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a computationally efficient method for learning interpretable brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data. The methodology is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches. The planned integration with Nilearn will facilitate adoption in the neuroimaging community.

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a significant contribution to the problem of ROI definition in neuroimaging. While there are some areas for improvement (as noted above), these do not undermine the paper's overall contribution.

## Additional Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a comparison with TV-`1 dictionary learning methods [1] in the experiments to directly quantify the computational advantage of Smooth-SODL.

2. Provide more detailed analysis of the statistical significance of the performance differences between methods.

3. Include a discussion of when the spatial smoothness assumption might not hold (e.g., for certain pathological conditions), and how the method might be adapted in those cases.

4. Consider adding a qualitative analysis of the dictionary atoms (e.g., comparing to known functional networks from the literature) to strengthen the interpretability claims.

5. Provide a more detailed explanation of the connection between the theoretical requirement for γ (Equation 9) and the practical choice of γ values in the experiments.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS 2016 as it presents a novel algorithmic approach to a significant problem in a domain that is increasingly important in machine learning applications. The work bridges machine learning and neuroimaging in a meaningful way that will be of interest to both communities.
