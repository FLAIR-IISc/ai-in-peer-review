Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for large-scale neuroimaging that enforces spatially structured and sparse dictionary atoms. Each subject’s image Xi is approximated as VUi with k atoms Vj. The method extends Mairal et al.’s online dictionary learning by (i) using a sparsity-inducing constraint on atoms (e.g., L1-ball or simplex) and (ii) adding a Laplacian quadratic penalty ΩLap(Vj) to promote spatial smoothness/compact “blobs.” Codes are updated via ridge regression; atoms are updated by block coordinate descent (BCD), solving at each atom a denoising-type quadratic with Laplacian using FISTA with a projection onto the atom constraint set. The authors discuss Lipschitz constants and a heuristic to avoid slow updates by adapting γ relative to sufficient statistics At. Experiments on HCP task fMRI (Math vs Story Z-maps; n=500; p≈2.6e5; k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to standard sparse online DL (SODL) and multi-subject ICA (CanICA), evaluating explained variance, spatial interpretability, stability across splits, behavioral prediction, and runtime. Smooth-SODL yields more localized/denoised atoms, higher stability than SODL, better small-n generalization, modest EV relative to ICA, and competitive behavioral prediction. Runtime is ≈3× SODL and faster than CanICA in their setup.

Relation to prior work
The formulation connects to online DL (Mairal JMLR’10), large-scale factorization (Mensch ICML’16), structured sparsity/SPCA (Jenatton AISTATS’10), GraphNet (Grosenick NeuroImage’13), and TV-regularized DL for fMRI (Abraham MICCAI’13). The novelty lies in embedding a smoothness prior on atoms into the online DL update with minimal overhead while retaining BCD-style updates and simple projections. Compared to prior structured decompositions that rely on slow alternating minimization, the proposed approach targets scalability.

Quality
- Algorithmic soundness: The BCD framework with ridge codes and projected FISTA for atom updates is reasonable. Identifying the atom update with a Laplacian-regularized denoising plus projection is correct; the gradient is linear ((I+γ̃Δ)v−a), and using projections onto L1-ball/simplex is standard.
- Convergence claims: The paper cites Mairal’s assumptions to argue convergence, but two issues need clarification. First, the use of time-varying αt and especially γt (suggested to scale with At to avoid tiny steps) changes the objective over time, so the convergence theory of [16] does not directly apply; either provide a justification for time-varying regularization within the online DL convergence framework or stick to fixed α, γ in the theoretical section. Second, the claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations suffice to reach 1e−6 accuracy is implausible; more likely, a few iterations give adequate progress for the outer BCD loop. Please temper this claim and report actual inner-iteration counts and tolerances.
- Computational aspects: The Lipschitz bound L = 1 + 4Dγ̃ assumes a particular scaling/bound on the Laplacian spectrum; this should be stated (boundary conditions, grid spacing). The FFT option is mentioned but not evaluated.
- Experiments: The HCP setup is compelling and large-scale. The qualitative differences are convincing, and the learning-curve analysis is insightful. However, several experimental details are missing:
  - Stability metric: how atoms are matched (Hungarian?) for the stability computation, number of splits, and error bars.
  - Prediction: model class and regularization (e.g., ridge), nested CV, exact feature construction (codes vs raw voxels), and whether variable selection on RAW was nested to avoid leakage.
  - Hyperparameters: how τ and γ were selected (CV folds, search space), whether positivity/simplex was used for all models, and sensitivity to k.
  - Fairness of preprocessing: CanICA is run on smoothed data (FWHM 6mm) while others are not. Smoothing benefits ICA but also could be applied to SODL; please justify or include matched preprocessing comparisons.
- Results: EV and R2 differences are moderate; stability improvements are meaningful. Lack of statistical uncertainty (bootstrap/CIs) weakens the strength of claims. Not comparing to TV-ℓ1 DL (e.g., Abraham) or GraphNet-based factorizations leaves open whether Laplacian is the best spatial prior here.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable with useful algorithm boxes. There are notational glitches (e.g., “for j=1 to r” likely k), minor typos, and some confusing statements (e.g., FISTA iterations/accuracy). The derivation of the scaled γ̃ in the atom update is sketched but should be made explicit. The choice of simplex (nonnegativity) for atoms is not motivated; Z-maps can be signed—please justify or compare with L1-ball without positivity.

Originality
Incremental but useful. Spatial Laplacian/GrafNet-type penalties are well known, as is online DL; the contribution is their practical integration for atom structure in an online setting with modest overhead and evidence that this improves interpretability/stability at scale. Relative to TV-ℓ1 DL, the novelty is the faster online implementation rather than a new objective.

Significance
For neuroimaging practice, a fast, online, structured decomposition that yields reproducible, interpretable ROIs from large cohorts is valuable, and the empirical study on HCP is relevant. Methodologically, the step from unstructured to Laplacian-structured atoms in online DL is not a major theoretical advance but is meaningful for applications. With stronger experimental rigor and clearer theory around time-varying regularization, the impact would be higher.

Pros
- Addresses a timely large-scale neuroimaging problem with a scalable method.
- Simple, implementable extension of online DL; practical insights on hyperparameters.
- Produces more interpretable and more stable components than SODL; better small-n performance.
- Reasonable runtime overhead and public code intent (Nilearn).

Cons
- Convergence claims do not account for time-varying αt, γt; FISTA accuracy claim is unrealistic.
- Missing experimental details, uncertainty estimates, and some fairness issues in baselines.
- Limited baseline coverage (no TV-ℓ1 DL/GraphNet factorizations); modest gains on prediction/EV.
- Motivation for nonnegativity/simplex constraint and its effect not discussed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a precise derivation of the atom update, including the γ̃ scaling, Laplacian spectral bound, and boundary conditions.
- Either fix α, γ for the convergence discussion or add a justification for time-varying regularization in the online DL framework; temper FISTA convergence claims and report inner-loop iterations/tolerances.
- Add methodological details: matching procedure and number of splits for stability; predictive model (and nested CV); hyperparameter search; error bars for EV/R2/stability.
- Include ablations: simplex vs L1-ball; effect of k; sensitivity to γ, τ; pre-smoothing vs Laplacian regularization.
- Compare (even on smaller subsets) to a TV-ℓ1 DL baseline and/or structured SPCA/GraphNet factorization.
- Clarify the fairness of preprocessing choices across methods.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper addresses an important application with a practical, scalable method and provides compelling qualitative/stability improvements. However, theoretical claims around convergence with time-varying regularization and some experimental reporting need tightening. With revisions addressing these points, the paper would make a solid contribution to NIPS.
