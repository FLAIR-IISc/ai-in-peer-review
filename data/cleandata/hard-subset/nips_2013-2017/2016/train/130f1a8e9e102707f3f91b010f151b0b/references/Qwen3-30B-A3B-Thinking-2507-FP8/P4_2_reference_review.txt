

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging data that incorporates spatial structure into the learned dictionary atoms. The authors address the problem of defining meaningful brain regions (ROIs) from inter-subject variability in brain imaging data, rather than relying on anatomical segmentation or predefined ROIs. Their key insight is to use the variability across subjects to learn structured, sparse dictionary atoms that represent functional brain organization, with spatial smoothness enforced via a Laplacian penalty. The method extends the online dictionary-learning framework of Mairal et al. [16] by adding a Laplacian penalty to the dictionary atoms, maintaining computational efficiency while producing spatially coherent regions.

The paper builds on prior work in structured dictionary learning [1, 25] and online dictionary learning [16, 17], but makes a novel contribution by integrating spatial structure into the online learning framework with minimal computational overhead. The authors compare against state-of-the-art methods including CanICA [22] and standard SODL [16] on HCP task fMRI data (500 subjects, 260k voxels), demonstrating that Smooth-SODL produces more spatially structured and interpretable dictionaries while maintaining good predictive performance.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The authors provide a clear optimization problem formulation with appropriate constraints (sparsity via L1 penalty and spatial structure via Laplacian penalty), along with detailed algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2) that extend the online dictionary-learning framework. The convergence analysis is based on established optimization theory. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with multiple metrics including visual assessment, explained variance, stability, and prediction of behavioral variables. The computational efficiency claim (3x slower than SODL) is supported by empirical results.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The motivation for the problem is strong, and the technical contributions are well-explained. The algorithms are described precisely, and the experimental setup is clearly documented. Minor clarity issues include the somewhat abrupt introduction of the Laplacian penalty (ΩLap) without sufficient justification for why it's particularly suited to brain imaging, and some experimental details (like specific hyperparameter values) are not fully elaborated in the main text.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by extending online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial structure with minimal computational overhead. While structured dictionary learning has been explored before [1, 25], the authors' adaptation to the online framework (which is crucial for large-scale neuroimaging data) appears novel. The paper effectively distinguishes their approach from prior work, particularly noting that previous structured methods were computationally prohibitive for large-scale neuroimaging.

**Significance:** The work addresses a significant challenge in neuroimaging analysis: the lack of principled, data-driven methods for ROI definition. The proposed method enables the extraction of structured, interpretable brain regions that better capture inter-subject variability, with implications for both basic neuroscience research and clinical applications. The scalability to large datasets (like HCP) makes the approach highly relevant for current neuroimaging practice.

**Strengths:**
- Strong motivation for the problem of data-driven ROI definition in neuroimaging
- Clear extension of online dictionary learning with spatial structure
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on a large-scale dataset
- Demonstrated computational efficiency (3x slower than SODL)
- Good analysis of trade-offs between sparsity, spatial structure, and predictive performance
- Practical implementation as part of Nilearn library

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited theoretical analysis of how the Laplacian penalty affects the learning properties
- Some experimental details (hyperparameter values) not provided in main text
- Superficial connection between learned atoms and known brain networks
- Does not sufficiently address potential limitations of the approach
- The "structured" aspect could be more rigorously justified compared to alternative spatial regularization methods

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:* The paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound method that addresses a significant problem in neuroimaging. The extension to structured dictionary learning is novel and the computational efficiency claim is well-supported. The experimental results demonstrate clear advantages in interpretability and stability over competing methods.

*Con:* The theoretical justification for the Laplacian penalty could be stronger, and the connection to neuroscience could be more deeply explored. Some experimental details are missing from the main text.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to both machine learning (for large-scale structured dictionary learning) and neuroimaging (for principled ROI definition). The method is technically sound, well-validated, and has clear significance for the field. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission.
