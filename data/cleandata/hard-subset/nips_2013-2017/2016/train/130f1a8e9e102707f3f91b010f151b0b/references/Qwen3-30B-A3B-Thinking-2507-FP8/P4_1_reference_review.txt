

# Review for "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning that incorporates spatial structure through a Laplacian penalty, to learn interpretable brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors address the challenge of defining meaningful regions of interest (ROIs) in brain imaging, arguing that existing approaches either rely on anatomical segmentation (with cross-modality issues) or predefined ROIs that may not match spatial patterns of interest. Their method learns spatially structured, sparse dictionary atoms that represent functional brain regions directly from inter-subject variability in statistical maps, extending the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) with spatial constraints. This work builds on prior structured dictionary learning approaches like those by Abraham (2013) and Varoquaux (2012), but significantly advances the field by enabling large-scale application through online learning with minimal computational overhead.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear theoretical foundations. The authors properly formalize the problem as a structured dictionary learning task with both sparsity (L1) and spatial smoothness (Laplacian) constraints, and carefully derive the optimization algorithm. The convergence guarantees are well-established by showing the problem satisfies the conditions of Mairal et al. (2010). The experimental validation on HCP data (500 subjects, ~260k voxels) is comprehensive, including multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, reproducibility, behavioral prediction). The ablation study on hyperparameters provides valuable practical guidance. The computational efficiency claim (2-3x slower than SODL) is well-supported by runtime measurements.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, motivation, and technical approach are logically presented with appropriate mathematical notation. The algorithmic details are precise, and the supplementary materials provide additional implementation details. The experimental results are effectively presented with informative figures (Fig. 2) that clearly illustrate the qualitative and quantitative advantages of the proposed method. The paper adequately informs readers about the technical content, with sufficient detail for reproduction.

## Originality
The paper makes a clear and significant contribution by extending online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial structure with minimal computational cost. While structured dictionary learning has been explored before (e.g., [1, 25]), this is the first to successfully adapt it to the online setting for massive-scale neuroimaging data. The key insight is that the Laplacian penalty can be efficiently integrated into the online framework without substantially increasing computational complexity (only 2-3x slower than SODL), making structured dictionary learning feasible for large neuroimaging datasets. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work, particularly noting that previous structured approaches were too slow for large-scale applications.

## Significance
The problem of defining meaningful ROIs is fundamental to neuroimaging analysis, and the authors demonstrate that their method produces more interpretable brain maps that better capture inter-subject variability, especially in small-to-medium data regimes. The results show that spatial regularization provides significant benefits when data is limited (31% explained variance vs. 2% for SODL with only 17 subjects). The method's ability to extract structured, denoised dictionaries that outperform competing methods in predicting behavioral variables (e.g., picture vocabulary, reading proficiency) demonstrates practical utility. The implementation will be made available in Nilearn, facilitating adoption by the neuroimaging community.

## Strengths
1. Well-motivated problem addressing a significant challenge in neuroimaging
2. Sound technical extension of online dictionary learning with spatial structure
3. Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple metrics
4. Practical implementation with clear hyperparameter guidance
5. Clear demonstration of computational efficiency (2-3x slower than SODL)
6. Strong theoretical grounding with convergence guarantees

## Weaknesses
1. The comparison with CanICA is somewhat unfair as CanICA doesn't enforce sparsity, while the authors' method does. A more nuanced comparison would be valuable.
2. The paper could better demonstrate how the learned regions relate to established neuroanatomical knowledge beyond mentioning "executive control networks."
3. The theoretical analysis of how the Laplacian penalty specifically affects the learned regions could be more detailed.
4. The significance of the 31% vs 2% explained variance advantage in practical neuroscience terms could be more clearly articulated.

## Pro/Con for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Clear technical contribution that extends online dictionary learning for large-scale neuroimaging
- Strong experimental validation demonstrating both theoretical and practical benefits
- Well-written with clear organization and sufficient detail for reproduction
- Addresses a significant problem in neuroimaging with practical impact
- Implementation will be made available in Nilearn, enhancing reproducibility and adoption

**Con:**
- The comparison with CanICA is not entirely fair due to fundamental differences in methodology
- The connection to established neuroanatomical knowledge could be more explicit
- The practical significance of the explained variance differences could be better contextualized

Overall, this paper makes a solid technical contribution that advances the state of the art in structured dictionary learning for neuroimaging. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and practically useful. While the comparison with CanICA could be more nuanced, the paper's strengths outweigh this weakness. The work represents a significant step toward scalable, interpretable brain region extraction that should be valuable to the neuroimaging community.
