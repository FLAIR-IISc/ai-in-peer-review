This paper proposes a novel approach to defining brain regions by leveraging inter-subject variability through structured sparse dictionary learning (Smooth-SODL). The core idea – using population-level differences to *define* ROIs, rather than pre-defining them – is a compelling and potentially impactful shift in perspective. This contrasts nicely with existing ROI-based methods which often rely on anatomical priors or task-specific activations, and offers a data-driven alternative. The authors successfully extend online dictionary learning, incorporating a Laplacian penalty to promote spatially localized, “blob-like” structures, crucial for interpretable brain regions. The experiments on the HCP dataset demonstrate the efficacy of the method, particularly in capturing variability and predicting behavioral scores, showing a clear advantage over standard SODL and, surprisingly, traditional ICA-based approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central idea of deriving regions *from* population variability, rather than imposing them, is genuinely novel and addresses a long-standing problem in neuroimaging. The combination of sparsity and spatial structure via the Laplacian penalty is also a valuable contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is clear and well-motivated. The adaptation of online dictionary learning with the Laplacian penalty seems principled and the algorithmic details are reasonably well-described, referencing relevant prior work. The convergence guarantees inherited from SODL are reassuring.
* **Empirical Results:** The results on the HCP dataset are promising. The observed improvements in explained variance, particularly in the early stages of data accumulation, and the predictive performance on behavioral variables, suggest that the learned regions capture meaningful information. The comparison against CanICA is particularly strong, highlighting the benefits of the sparsity and structure enforced by Smooth-SODL.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to the neuroimaging community. The ability to automatically and data-drivenly define ROIs could significantly simplify and improve the analysis of fMRI, PET, and other brain imaging data.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning is adequate but could be more in-depth. While the authors offer some guidance (e.g., the time-varying alpha), a more systematic investigation of the impact of different parameter settings would be beneficial. The reliance on cross-validation feels somewhat ad-hoc.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the increased computational cost compared to SODL (3x slower), this is a non-negligible drawback, especially given the size of typical neuroimaging datasets. A more thorough analysis of the scaling behavior of the algorithm with dataset size would be valuable. It's not clear how this would perform on even larger, whole-brain datasets.
* **Interpretability:** While the authors emphasize the spatial structure of the learned regions, further qualitative analysis of the resulting “atlases” would be helpful. Connecting the learned regions to known anatomical or functional structures in greater detail would strengthen the paper. Visualizations beyond those provided in Fig. 2 are needed to fully assess the interpretability.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison is limited to CanICA and SODL. While these are relevant baselines, it would be valuable to compare against other recent ROI definition methods, potentially including spectral clustering approaches or more sophisticated segmentation techniques.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is highly novel and addresses a fundamental challenge in neuroimaging.
* The technical approach is sound and well-motivated.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods, particularly in capturing inter-subject variability.
* The potential impact on the field is significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost is a concern.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity requires further investigation.
* The interpretability of the learned regions could be improved with more detailed analysis.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling idea and promising results. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh these concerns. The authors successfully extend online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial structure, resulting in a method that can automatically and data-drivenly define brain regions. I believe this work represents a significant contribution to the field and warrants acceptance. I am willing to overlook the slightly incomplete validation and increased computational cost, given the novelty and potential of the approach. I would encourage the authors to address the hyperparameter sensitivity and interpretability concerns in future work.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
