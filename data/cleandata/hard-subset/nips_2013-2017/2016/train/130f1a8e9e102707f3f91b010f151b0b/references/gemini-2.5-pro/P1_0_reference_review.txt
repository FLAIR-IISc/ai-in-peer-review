### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the problem of identifying meaningful brain regions of interest (ROIs) from large neuroimaging datasets by modeling inter-subject variability. The authors propose a novel method called Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL). The core idea is to extend the scalable online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) [16] by incorporating a spatial smoothness prior. Specifically, they add a Laplacian regularization term to the dictionary learning objective function. This encourages the learned dictionary atoms (the ROIs) to be not only sparse (containing mostly zeros, via an L1-ball constraint) but also spatially structured and smooth (forming contiguous "blobs"). The optimization is performed using an online block-coordinate descent algorithm, which the authors show remains computationally efficient, adding only a factor of 2-3 in runtime compared to the unstructured version. The method is validated on a large fMRI dataset (500 subjects from the Human Connectome Project), where it is compared against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a popular group-ICA method (CanICA). The results demonstrate that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable, stable, and less noisy brain maps, with a particularly strong advantage in low-data regimes, while maintaining good predictive power for behavioral variables.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed model is a well-motivated extension of a widely-used algorithm. The authors correctly identify the key algorithmic challenge—solving the dictionary update sub-problem with the added Laplacian penalty—and propose an efficient solution using FISTA, noting its connection to GraphNet. The empirical evaluation is strong and convincing.
*   The use of a large, public dataset (HCP) allows for robust evaluation and reproducibility.
*   The choice of baselines (unstructured SODL and group-ICA) is appropriate and represents the state-of-the-art in scalable and standard fMRI decomposition methods, respectively.
*   The evaluation metrics are comprehensive, covering qualitative visual assessment, quantitative measures like explained variance and stability, and relevance to a downstream task (predicting behavioral scores).
*   The learning curve analysis in Table 1 is a particularly strong piece of evidence. It clearly demonstrates the value of the spatial prior, showing a massive 14-fold improvement in explained variance in the low-data regime (N=17), which is a common scenario in clinical studies. This highlights the method’s broad applicability.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation for structured, data-driven ROIs is clearly laid out in the introduction. The model in Section 2 is presented formally and its components are explained well. The algorithm is described in sufficient detail for an expert to understand the implementation. The figures are excellent; Figure 2, in particular, effectively summarizes the main contributions and results in a single, clear presentation. The qualitative comparison in Fig 2(a) provides compelling visual evidence for the superiority of the proposed method in generating interpretable maps.

**Originality:** The core components of the method—online dictionary learning and Laplacian regularization—are not new in isolation. However, their combination into a single, scalable framework for large-scale neuroimaging analysis is novel and significant. Previous works on structured dictionary learning for brain imaging (e.g., Abraham et al. [1] using Total Variation) have relied on slower, batch-mode alternating optimization schemes that do not scale to datasets with hundreds or thousands of subjects. This paper successfully bridges the gap between scalability (from online learning) and interpretability (from structured priors). The contribution is a pragmatic but important one: making structured decompositions computationally feasible for the massive datasets that are becoming the norm in neuroscience.

**Significance:** The work has high potential significance for the neuroimaging community.
*   It provides a principled and computationally efficient tool for deriving data-driven brain atlases or ROIs that reflect population variability. This is a fundamental problem in the field.
*   The demonstrated stability and interpretability of the resulting maps are crucial for scientific discovery, as researchers need to trust that the components they analyze are meaningful and reproducible.
*   The method's strong performance in low-data settings makes it highly relevant for clinical research, where sample sizes are often limited.
*   The authors' commitment to releasing the code in a major open-source library (Nilearn) will greatly facilitate its adoption and impact.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Hyperparameter Discussion:** The discussion on hyperparameter selection is somewhat brief. While the paper provides a good empirical study (Fig. 1) and a useful heuristic for `alpha`, the link between the adaptive `gamma` rule proposed in Eq. (9) and the fixed values used in the experiments (`gamma = 10^3, 10^4`) is unclear. A more detailed discussion on how to practically set `gamma` in relation to the data or other parameters would strengthen the paper. For instance, is the rule in Eq. (9) used in the implementation, or is it just a theoretical guideline for avoiding blow-up?

2.  **Comparison to Other Structured Methods:** The paper positions itself against slow structured methods like [1] but does not provide a direct comparison. While running such a baseline on 500 subjects might be infeasible (which would itself be a strong result), a comparison on a smaller subset of the data could have more directly quantified the trade-off between the Laplacian penalty and a more sophisticated one like Total Variation in terms of both map quality and runtime.

3.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** The derivation of the dictionary update in Eq. (7) is abbreviated, pointing the reader to [16] "for the details". While this is understandable due to space constraints, it makes the step slightly opaque. A brief sentence outlining how the `B` and `A` matrices are rearranged to isolate `v` before introducing the Laplacian term would improve readability.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and practical problem in a large scientific community (neuroimaging).
*   Presents a novel and effective combination of existing techniques to create a scalable solution.
*   The experimental validation is thorough, using a large modern dataset and comprehensive metrics.
*   The results clearly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method (improved interpretability, stability, and small-sample performance) at a modest computational cost.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and the planned open-source release promises high impact.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The core algorithmic components are not fundamentally new; the novelty lies in the combination and application.
*   The practical guidance on setting the crucial regularization parameter `gamma` could be clearer.
*   A direct comparison to other, albeit slower, structured decomposition methods is missing.

### Recommendation

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a well-executed, high-quality piece of research that provides a valuable tool for a wide range of researchers. The work is a significant step forward in making principled, structured analysis of large-scale neuroimaging data practical. I strongly recommend acceptance.
