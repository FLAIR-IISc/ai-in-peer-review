- Summary: Proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning extension with a Laplacian penalty to extract spatially structured brain regions from large-scale fMRI, validated on 500 HCP subjects.  
- Motivation: Addresses the need for principled ROI definitions capturing inter-subject variability beyond anatomical segmentation or heuristic clustering.  
- Novelty: Introduces spatial smoothness via a Laplacian penalty into efficient online dictionary learning, enabling scalable structured decomposition.  
- Empirical results: Shows more interpretable, stable, and predictive brain regions than SODL and CanICA, with modest computational overhead (~3x SODL).  
- Theoretical foundation: Provides convergence guarantees, optimization details, and links to GraphNet literature.  
- Practical impact: Implementation planned for Nilearn, increasing accessibility and potential adoption in neuroscience.  
- Weakness 1: Limited theoretical justification for choosing the Laplacian penalty over alternatives like total variation.  
- Weakness 2: Comparison to CanICA is unfair because CanICA lacks sparsity; a sparse-ICA baseline would be more appropriate.  
- Weakness 3: Limited neuroscience interpretation — the paper doesn't deeply link learned regions to specific cognitive processes.  
- Weakness 4: Scalability beyond 500 subjects or to higher-resolution data is not evaluated or discussed in depth.  
- Weakness 5: Hyperparameter selection guidance is insufficient for practitioners applying the method to new datasets.  
- Overall assessment: Strong, well-motivated, technically sound contribution with thorough experiments and high practical relevance.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Suggestion 1: Add theoretical or empirical comparisons between Laplacian and other spatial regularizers (e.g., total variation).  
- Suggestion 2: Compare against sparse ICA baselines to fairly isolate the benefit of spatial regularization.  
- Suggestion 3: Include analysis linking discovered regions to cognitive functions to strengthen neuroscience relevance.  
- Suggestion 4: Provide concrete hyperparameter selection heuristics or a case study for new datasets.  
- Suggestion 5: Discuss scalability limits and potential bottlenecks for much larger datasets (e.g., 10k+ subjects).
