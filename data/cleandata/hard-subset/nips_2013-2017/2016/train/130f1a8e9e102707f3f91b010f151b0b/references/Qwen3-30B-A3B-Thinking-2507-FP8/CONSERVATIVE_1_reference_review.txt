

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes an extension of online dictionary learning for brain region discovery from neuroimaging data, adding a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on the learned dictionary atoms. The authors claim this "Smooth-SODL" method better captures brain organization than standard sparse dictionary learning or ICA-based approaches.

## Quality

The paper builds on established online dictionary learning (SODL [16]) with a Laplacian penalty for spatial smoothness. While the mathematical formulation is sound, the approach represents a straightforward combination of well-known techniques rather than a significant methodological advance. The authors claim a "factor of 2 or 3" increase in running time compared to SODL, but provide insufficient evidence to justify this trade-off. The experimental validation is limited to one dataset (HCP task fMRI) and lacks comprehensive comparison with relevant spatially regularized methods in neuroimaging. Crucially, the paper fails to demonstrate that the Laplacian penalty provides meaningful improvement over standard dictionary learning in the neuroimaging context, despite acknowledging that spatial regularization is unnecessary when data are abundant (as in HCP).

The paper's claim that their method "better captures inter-subject variability" is not convincingly supported. The learning curves in Table 1 show only a modest advantage for Smooth-SODL in small-sample regimes (31% vs. 2% explained variance with 17 subjects), which diminishes as more data is used. The 3x runtime increase for this modest improvement does not seem justified for the neuroimaging community, which typically prioritizes interpretability over computational efficiency.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good mathematical formulation of the problem. The experimental setup and results are adequately described. However, the paper fails to clearly articulate how their method differs from or improves upon existing spatially regularized approaches like those in [1, 25], which also use structured penalties for brain mapping.

## Originality

The core idea of using dictionary learning for ROI extraction is not novel (see [23] and references). The Laplacian penalty is a standard technique in image processing, and its application to neuroimaging data represents a straightforward extension rather than a significant innovation. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from prior work that has already explored similar spatial regularization techniques. The claim of "extending the online dictionary-learning work [16] with only a factor of 2 or 3 on the running time" understates the fact that this is a minimal modification to an established framework.

## Significance

The problem of ROI definition in neuroimaging is important, but the paper fails to demonstrate that the proposed method provides a meaningful advance over existing approaches. The claimed advantages (more interpretable brain regions, better capture of inter-subject variability) are not convincingly shown to be substantial. The paper's main contribution appears to be a minor computational extension to an existing framework, not a significant methodological advance that would transform how neuroimaging data is analyzed.

The experiments show modest improvements in interpretability (qualitative assessment) and stability (reproducibility), but these are not accompanied by strong evidence that these improvements translate to better scientific insights or clinical utility. The paper does not provide evidence that Smooth-SODL leads to new biological discoveries or improved diagnostic capabilities.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Well-organized and clearly written
- Reasonable experimental setup on a large neuroimaging dataset
- Demonstrates modest improvements in dictionary interpretability
- Provides implementation in Nilearn, which is useful for the community

**Weaknesses:**
- Lacks compelling evidence for the necessity of the Laplacian penalty
- Insufficient comparison with relevant prior work on spatially regularized dictionary learning
- Overstates the significance of modest improvements (3x runtime for small gains)
- Fails to demonstrate clear advantages over existing neuroimaging analysis methods
- The "novel" aspect is a standard technique applied to a new domain without compelling justification

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound extension to existing dictionary learning methods, it fails to provide sufficient evidence that this extension represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The claimed advantages are modest and not convincingly demonstrated, and the paper does not adequately differentiate itself from prior work that has already explored similar approaches. The neuroimaging community would benefit more from a paper that demonstrates a significant methodological advance or provides compelling evidence of superior performance in a challenging real-world application, rather than a minor computational extension to an existing framework.

The paper represents a step forward in the application of dictionary learning to neuroimaging, but it does not meet the high bar for novelty and significance required by NIPS. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their experimental validation, provide more convincing evidence of the advantages of their approach over existing methods, and more clearly articulate how their method differs from prior work to warrant acceptance.
