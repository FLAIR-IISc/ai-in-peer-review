- Proposes Smooth-SODL: an online dictionary learning method enforcing sparsity and spatial smoothness via Laplacian penalization and ℓ1-ball/simplex constraints on atoms.
- Codes are estimated with ridge regression (optionally ℓ1), and per-atom updates use proximal gradient (FISTA) within an online block-coordinate descent framework similar to Mairal et al.
- Targets large-scale neuroimaging data, claiming only a 2–3× runtime overhead versus unstructured online DL.
- Empirical evaluation on HCP task fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) versus SODL and CanICA.
- Reports more interpretable, spatially coherent atoms than SODL (fewer spurious fringes).
- Shows improved split-to-split stability compared to SODL.
- Demonstrates superior early learning curves (test explained variance) when sample size is small.
- Achieves competitive behavioral prediction (below CanICA, above SODL and thresholded CanICA).
- Strength: addresses a timely, practical problem for large neuroimaging cohorts.
- Strength: clean algorithmic integration of Laplacian prior with known Lipschitz constants and preserved memory footprint.
- Strength: convincing qualitative and stability empirical evidence plus reasonable runtime overhead.
- Strength: clear emphasis on interpretability and reproducibility.
- Weakness: novelty is incremental—Laplacian/GraphNet smoothness is well-established; main novelty is online integration.
- Weakness: lacks direct comparisons to structured baselines (TV-ℓ1, GraphNet, structured NMF) to quantify trade-offs.
- Weakness: time-varying regularization (γt scaling) alters the objective; unclear if Mairal-style convergence guarantees hold or what the limiting objective is.
- Weakness: implausible claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e-6 without clarifying warm-starting, stopping rules, or inner objective gaps.
- Weakness: fairness concerns—CanICA was run on smoothed data while DL methods were not, confounding preprocessing and algorithm effects.
- Weakness: modeling choices (simplex/non-negativity for Z-score maps and ridge vs ℓ1 codes) may bias results; ablation missing.
- Weakness: evaluation lacks confidence intervals and detailed description of the behavioral prediction pipeline (possible leakage concerns).
- Weakness: notational inconsistencies and minor typos; some optimization claims need tightening (e.g., “global optimum” scope).
- Significance: practically useful, scalable method likely to be adopted, but methodologically incremental.
- Suggestion: provide theory or empirical justification for time-varying γt and its convergence/limiting objective.
- Suggestion: clarify inner solver details—initialization, stopping criteria, typical iteration counts, and objective decrease per inner update.
- Suggestion: add at least one structured baseline (TV-ℓ1 or GraphNet) on a smaller subset to quantify interpretability vs runtime.
- Suggestion: report sensitivity to ℓ1-ball vs simplex and to using ℓ1 codes; include ablations for signed atoms.
- Suggestion: ensure fair baselines (CanICA with/without smoothing/thresholding, possibly MELODIC) and expand tasks/resting-state datasets plus error bars for metrics.
- Pros for acceptance: fills practical gap, improves interpretability/stability with minimal cost, solid empirical analysis on public data.
- Cons for acceptance: modest novelty and underspecified theoretical/algorithmic and evaluation details.
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept, contingent on stronger comparisons and clarifications where possible.
