## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth-SODL, for learning spatially coherent brain regions directly from population-level fMRI data, extending the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2012). The core idea – leveraging inter-subject variability to define ROIs – is a promising direction, addressing a fundamental challenge in neuroimaging: principled ROI definition. The authors attempt to bridge the gap between clustering approaches and sparse decomposition methods like PCA/ICA, introducing a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially smooth and compact dictionary atoms. The paper details the algorithm, discusses practical considerations (hyperparameter tuning), and provides experimental results on the HCP dataset, comparing Smooth-SODL to CanICA and standard SODL.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for spatial smoothness represents a genuine contribution. While online dictionary learning exists, the extension to spatially structured decompositions is new and addresses a specific need within the neuroimaging community.
* **Potential Significance:** The proposed method could provide a more automated and data-driven approach to ROI definition, potentially improving statistical power and interpretability in fMRI analysis. The ability to learn these regions *directly* from the data, rather than relying on predefined atlases or task-based activation, is a key advantage.
* **Scalability:** The online learning framework is well-suited for large-scale datasets like the HCP, a crucial consideration in modern neuroimaging. The authors demonstrate a reasonable runtime, though it's slower than SODL.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset provide a preliminary validation of the method, demonstrating comparable performance to existing techniques (and improvements in specific aspects like spatial coherence) and showing predictive power on behavioral variables.
* **Detailed Algorithm Description:** The paper provides a thorough mathematical description of the Smooth-SODL algorithm, including derivations and justifications for design choices.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Improvement, Not Transformative:** While novel, the improvements over existing methods (particularly SODL) appear incremental rather than transformative. The gains in explained variance, while present, are not dramatic, and the qualitative improvements in dictionary atom structure (Figure 2(a)) could be subjective. The authors acknowledge this point themselves.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper discusses hyperparameter tuning but provides limited guidance on optimal parameter selection. The explanation of the relationship between γ, τ, and the resulting dictionary characteristics is somewhat hand-wavy.  Figure 1 is helpful, but lacks quantitative metrics to support the qualitative observations. The time-varying α parameter also feels *ad hoc*.
* **Lack of Robustness Analysis:** The authors mention random initialization having limited impact, but a more thorough robustness analysis (e.g., multiple random initializations with different seeds, evaluating the variance in resulting dictionaries) would be beneficial.
* **Minor Technical Inconsistencies/Imprecisions:**
    * Equation (2): The 'lim n→∞' is unnecessary and slightly confusing. It doesn’t fundamentally change the equation but adds visual clutter.
    * Algorithm 2: The statement “Problem (7) is the compactly-constrained minimization…” is somewhat inaccurate. The constraint is *convex*, but not necessarily *compactly* constrained in the sense of having a finite bound.
    * The discussion of the Lipschitz constant and γ in the final paragraph of Section 3 is difficult to follow and feels rushed. A more detailed explanation or a reference to supporting material would be helpful.
* **Limited Comparison to Established ROI Definition Methods:** The comparison is largely limited to CanICA and SODL. A comparison to more traditional ROI definition techniques (e.g., anatomical atlases, task-based activation maps) would provide a stronger benchmark.  How does Smooth-SODL perform when used as input to a standard GLM analysis?
* **Figure 2(c) Interpretation:** The claim that Smooth-SODL *outperforms* tCanICA in predicting behavioral variables requires closer scrutiny. The difference in R<sup>2</sup> scores appears minimal, and the error bars (if present) are not shown.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* Presents a novel and technically sound method for learning spatially coherent brain regions.
* Addresses an important problem in neuroimaging with potential practical impact.
* Demonstrates promising results on a large, publicly available dataset.
* The detailed algorithm description and clear writing style make the paper relatively easy to follow.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* The improvements over existing methods are incremental, and the overall significance of the work may be limited.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity and lack of a robust tuning strategy are concerning.
* Minor technical inconsistencies and imprecisions detract from the overall quality of the paper.
* A more comprehensive comparison to established ROI definition methods is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a well-executed and technically sound contribution, but its impact feels limited by the incremental nature of the improvements and the lack of a truly compelling demonstration of superiority over existing methods. The hyperparameter sensitivity and minor technical issues further detract from the paper’s overall quality. While the idea is promising, and the method could be useful to researchers in the field, I lean towards **rejection**. The authors would significantly benefit from addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly focusing on a more rigorous hyperparameter optimization strategy, a more thorough robustness analysis, and a more comprehensive comparison to established ROI definition techniques.  A clearer articulation of the benefits and limitations of the method would also be helpful.
