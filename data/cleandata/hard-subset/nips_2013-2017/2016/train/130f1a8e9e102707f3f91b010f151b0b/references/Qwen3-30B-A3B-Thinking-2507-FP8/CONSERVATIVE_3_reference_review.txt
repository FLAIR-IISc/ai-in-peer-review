

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online sparse dictionary learning (SODL) with a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms, for identifying brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors argue that traditional approaches to defining brain regions (ROIs) rely on anatomical segmentation or predefined regions, which may not capture meaningful functional organization. Instead, they propose using inter-subject variability in statistical maps to define regions, with the key innovation being the addition of spatial regularization to the dictionary learning framework. The method is evaluated on HCP task fMRI data, comparing against SODL and CanICA.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging - the need for principled approaches to define brain regions. The methodology builds logically on established work (SODL [16]), and the implementation is well-documented with plans for public release in Nilearn. The experimental setup on a large-scale dataset (500 subjects) is appropriate, and the authors provide a thoughtful discussion of hyper-parameter selection and computational considerations. The qualitative comparison in Figure 2 demonstrates visually that the proposed method produces more spatially coherent regions than standard SODL.

## Weaknesses

**1. Incremental contribution without sufficient justification:** The paper's core innovation - adding a Laplacian penalty to SODL - is a minor modification to an existing framework. The authors claim this "extends" the online dictionary learning work [16], but fail to convincingly demonstrate why this specific penalty is superior to other spatial regularization approaches (e.g., total variation, graph-based methods). The paper lacks a thorough comparison with other spatial regularization techniques that have been used in neuroimaging (e.g., [1, 25, 3]).

**2. Insufficient evidence for claimed advantages:** The paper asserts that Smooth-SODL "better captures inter-subject variability" but provides only modest improvements in explained variance (55% vs. 50% for SODL on test data in Fig. 2b) and weak evidence for improved interpretability. The qualitative assessment in Figure 2a is subjective - the "structured" nature of the regions is apparent to the authors but not rigorously quantified. The paper does not demonstrate that these regions correspond to biologically meaningful brain networks or lead to better scientific insights.

**3. Lack of statistical validation:** The paper fails to provide statistical tests for the differences between methods. For instance, the claim that Smooth-SODL has higher reproducibility (0.4 vs. 0.1 for SODL) should be supported by p-values or confidence intervals. The paper also doesn't establish whether the observed improvements are statistically significant given the variability in neuroimaging data.

**4. Overstated significance:** The paper claims that their method "shall become the de facto way to do dimensionality reduction and ROI extraction" based on limited evidence. The improvement in behavioral prediction (Fig. 2c) is modest and not substantially better than CanICA, which the authors admit performs best for this task. The paper doesn't convincingly argue that the method's advantages outweigh the added complexity.

**5. Inadequate comparison with relevant work:** The paper dismisses [1] as "mostly heuristic" but doesn't compare against its structured dictionary learning approach, which has been used for brain atlas creation. The paper also doesn't address how their method compares to more recent approaches like [23], which also addresses population-level functional mapping.

## Critical Evaluation

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the methodological contribution of this paper to be incremental rather than transformative. The Laplacian penalty is a standard technique in image processing, and its application to dictionary learning is not novel. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that this specific implementation offers meaningful advantages over existing methods for the neuroscience community.

The experimental validation is limited to a single dataset (HCP) and doesn't demonstrate how the method performs on other imaging modalities or in different neuroimaging contexts. The paper also fails to address whether the "spatially structured" regions they identify correspond to known functional networks or provide new insights into brain organization.

While the computational efficiency claim (3x slower than SODL) is reasonable for the added complexity, the paper doesn't establish that this trade-off is justified by significant improvements in scientific output.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a relevant problem and presents a well-executed extension of existing work, the contribution is incremental and lacks sufficient evidence to support the claims of improved interpretability and capture of inter-subject variability. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with other spatial regularization methods, rigorous statistical validation of results, and stronger evidence that the proposed method leads to scientifically meaningful advances rather than just visual improvements.

The field of neuroimaging would benefit more from a paper that demonstrates how structured dictionary learning leads to new biological insights, rather than one that makes modest improvements to an existing algorithm without clear scientific impact.
