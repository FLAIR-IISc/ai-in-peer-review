## Peer Review: Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), for automatically discovering brain regions from fMRI data. The core idea is to leverage inter-subject variability to define spatially coherent regions, moving beyond traditional ROI-based approaches.  The method extends online dictionary learning by incorporating both sparsity (using an L1 penalty) and spatial smoothness (using a Laplacian penalty) into the dictionary learning objective. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a large-scale HCP dataset, showing improvements over standard dictionary learning and ICA-based methods in terms of interpretability, stability, and predictive power.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds on a strong foundation of existing research in neuroimaging and machine learning. It appropriately acknowledges the limitations of univariate approaches and the challenges of ROI definition.  The paper draws connections to clustering methods used in neuroimaging, but distinguishes itself by framing the problem as a signal decomposition rather than a clustering task. It correctly positions its contribution relative to existing dictionary learning techniques (specifically [16]) and relates to broader methods like PCA and ICA used for functional unit extraction. The authors acknowledge and compare to related methods like CanICA and TV-l1 regularization, referencing relevant literature. They are thorough in relating their approach to existing structured sparsity methods, but highlight the advantage of their online learning framework for scalability.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with spatial regularization is a significant contribution. It addresses a key challenge in neuroimaging – defining meaningful ROIs without relying on a priori knowledge – in a scalable and computationally efficient manner.
* **Scalability:** The online learning framework addresses the major bottleneck of applying dictionary learning to large neuroimaging datasets, making it suitable for datasets like the HCP.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the Smooth-SODL model and discusses convergence guarantees based on previous work on block coordinate descent.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset are well-designed and demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method across multiple metrics: explained variance, stability, and prediction of behavioral variables. The comparison to established techniques like CanICA and standard SODL is crucial.
* **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning (particularly the adaptive α parameter) and the analysis of computational cost are valuable for potential users of the method. The authors openly acknowledge the trade-offs involved.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors provide guidance on hyperparameter selection, the process still appears complex.  A more systematic investigation of the sensitivity of the results to different parameter settings (beyond the single experiment shown in Fig. 1) would be beneficial.  A practical "recipe" for setting parameters for new datasets would be useful.
* **Laplacian Penalty Parameter Selection:** The discussion around the constraint on γ (eq. 9) is somewhat opaque and difficult to grasp without a deeper understanding of the underlying theory. Clarifying this point and providing more intuitive guidance would be helpful.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Segmentation Methods:** While comparisons to CanICA and standard SODL are appropriate, the paper could benefit from a brief discussion of how the discovered regions compare to results obtained from more traditional segmentation techniques (e.g., FreeSurfer).
* **Visualizations:** The visualization in Fig. 2(a) could be improved.  Overlaying the resulting regions on a standard brain template would provide a more intuitive understanding of the spatial localization of the extracted components.
* **Justification of k:** The choice of k=40 is not thoroughly justified. Exploring a range of k values and assessing the impact on the results would be beneficial.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and provides a well-supported methodology. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.  The authors are appropriately cautious in their claims.
* **Clarity:** The paper is largely clear and well-organized. A few sections (e.g., the discussion of γ selection) could benefit from further clarification.
* **Originality:** The combination of online dictionary learning with spatial regularization is a novel contribution. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work and provides a valuable extension to existing techniques.
* **Significance:**  The results are significant. The proposed method offers a promising approach for automated ROI definition, which could have a broad impact on neuroimaging research. The scalability of the method is particularly valuable for analyzing large-scale datasets.



**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Addresses a challenging problem in neuroimaging (automatic ROI definition).
* Presents a novel and technically sound method.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on a large-scale dataset.
* Offers a scalable solution suitable for large-scale neuroimaging data.

**Against:**

* Hyperparameter tuning remains a challenge.
* Limited comparison to traditional segmentation methods.
* Some sections could benefit from further clarification.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging.  The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and demonstrates promising results. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above (particularly clarifying the hyperparameter selection process and providing more intuitive visualizations) would further strengthen the paper.  This work has the potential to significantly impact how brain regions are defined and analyzed in neuroimaging research.
