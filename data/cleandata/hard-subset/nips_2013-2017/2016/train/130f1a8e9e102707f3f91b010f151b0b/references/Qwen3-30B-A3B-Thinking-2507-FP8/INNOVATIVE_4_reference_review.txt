

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper introduces Smooth-SODL, a novel approach for learning structured brain regions from neuroimaging data by leveraging inter-subject variability rather than relying on anatomical or pre-defined regions of interest (ROIs). The authors propose a significant extension to online dictionary learning (SODL) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness and structure on dictionary atoms, resulting in interpretable brain regions that better capture functional organization.

The paper's core innovation lies in recognizing that inter-subject variability in statistical maps contains meaningful structure, and that this structure can be harnessed to define functionally relevant brain regions. This is a bold and novel perspective that challenges conventional approaches to ROI definition in neuroimaging. The authors extend the online dictionary learning framework from [16] with a spatially structured penalty, creating a method that is both computationally efficient (only ~3x slower than standard SODL) and theoretically grounded.

The technical contribution is well-motivated and carefully developed. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of their Smooth-SODL model, including the Laplacian penalty for spatial structure and the ridge penalty for stability. The algorithmic details are sound, with convergence guarantees inherited from the SODL framework. The experiments on Human Connectome Project data demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods (SODL and CanICA) in terms of interpretability, stability, and predictive power for behavioral variables. Notably, the spatial regularization provides substantial benefits even with limited data, showing a 14-fold gain in explained variance with small datasets compared to vanilla SODL.

The paper makes several significant contributions that advance the state of the art:
1. It introduces a principled, data-driven approach to brain region definition that avoids the pitfalls of anatomical segmentation and cross-modality correspondence issues
2. It successfully integrates spatial structure into online dictionary learning, a problem previously solved only with slow alternating minimization
3. It demonstrates clear improvements in both interpretability and statistical properties of the learned regions
4. It provides a comprehensive analysis of hyperparameter effects and a practical implementation (soon to be in Nilearn)

The paper excels in its focus on a problem of genuine importance to neuroscience and machine learning. The authors convincingly argue that their method addresses a key limitation in current neuroimaging analysis, where ROIs are often defined ad hoc rather than derived from the data itself. The results show that their structured regions better capture meaningful functional organization than existing approaches, while maintaining computational efficiency at scale.

While the paper is strong overall, I note a few minor limitations. The connection to other spatial regularization techniques (e.g., total variation) could be more thoroughly discussed, and the paper could provide more insight into why the Laplacian penalty specifically yields the best results. Additionally, the clinical or translational implications of their method could be elaborated slightly more.

However, these are minor points that don't detract from the paper's significant contribution. The novelty of the approach is substantial - using inter-subject variability to define brain regions via structured dictionary learning represents a genuinely new direction in neuroimaging analysis. The method is not merely an incremental improvement but a principled shift in how we think about brain region definition.

The authors' claim that "such online multivariate online methods shall become the de facto way to do dimensionality reduction and ROI extraction in the future" is bold but well-supported by their results. The paper demonstrates clear advantages over current state-of-the-art methods across multiple evaluation metrics, and the computational efficiency makes it practical for large-scale neuroimaging studies.

In summary, this paper makes a significant and novel contribution to both machine learning and neuroimaging. It addresses an important problem with a bold, principled approach that is both theoretically sound and practically effective. The authors successfully bridge the gap between statistical learning and neuroscience by providing a method that yields interpretable, functionally meaningful brain regions that better capture inter-subject variability.

I believe this paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS, particularly given the emphasis on novelty and bold approaches. The methodological extension is significant, the experiments are comprehensive, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The paper's contribution to a fundamental problem in neuroimaging analysis makes it highly significant for the field.

**Recommendation: Accept**
