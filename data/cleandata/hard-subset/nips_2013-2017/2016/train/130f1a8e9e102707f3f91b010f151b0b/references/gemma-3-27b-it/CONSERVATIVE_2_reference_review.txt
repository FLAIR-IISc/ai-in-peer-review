This paper proposes a novel online dictionary learning method (Smooth-SODL) incorporating Laplacian regularization to induce spatial smoothness in learned brain regions, aiming to address the challenges of inter-subject variability in neuroimaging. The motivation, rooted in the limitations of traditional ROI-based analyses and the shortcomings of purely data-driven approaches like ICA, is sound. The authors correctly identify the need for principled, data-driven region definition. The method extends existing online dictionary learning work and attempts to bridge the gap between sparse coding and spatially coherent brain parcellation.

However, despite a clear presentation and detailed methodological description, I find the paper ultimately lacking in compelling evidence to support its claims of significant advancement over established techniques. My primary concern is the incremental nature of the contribution alongside the considerable computational cost. While the authors acknowledge a 3x slowdown compared to standard SODL, the tangible benefits demonstrated – primarily in the prediction of behavioral variables – aren’t substantial enough to justify this overhead, especially given the existing arsenal of effective methods in neuroimaging. 

The experimental validation, while utilizing a large and respected dataset (HCP), feels insufficiently rigorous. The comparison to CanICA is somewhat unfair; the authors themselves acknowledge the need for hard-thresholding to make CanICA interpretable, yet then use this as a point of criticism. A more direct comparison against established spatially regularized methods (e.g., methods utilizing total variation regularization directly on the spatial maps, or graph-based parcellation approaches) is crucial. Furthermore, the chosen hyperparameter tuning strategy, while discussed, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The learning curves presented (Fig. 2b) are informative, but lack a thorough quantitative analysis of the trade-offs between sparsity and explained variance for different parameter settings. The claim of a 14-fold gain in explained variance *early* in the learning process is intriguing, but not explored with enough depth – what does this practically mean for reducing the amount of data required for reliable region identification?

The clarity of the paper is high; the mathematical formulation is well-presented and the algorithmic details are adequately described. However, the extensive use of algorithmic notation can make certain sections dense. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, but could benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of existing methods and a clearer positioning of the proposed approach within the broader landscape of brain parcellation techniques. The authors adequately address the convergence properties of their algorithm, drawing on existing results from online learning theory.

The originality lies in the combination of online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization for brain region identification. However, the core ideas – sparse coding and spatial regularization – are not novel in themselves. The novelty resides in the specific implementation and adaptation to the large-scale neuroimaging setting. 

The significance of the work is questionable. While the method shows some improvement in predictive performance compared to standard SODL and CanICA, the gains are modest. The claim of a method “that shall become the de facto way to do dimensionality reduction and ROI extraction” is overly optimistic and unsupported by the presented evidence. The computational cost and the lack of a clear advantage over established, often simpler, techniques limit the potential impact of this work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in neuroimaging.
*   Presents a technically sound method with clear mathematical formulation.
*   Demonstrates improvements over standard SODL in some evaluation metrics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution with limited practical advantage.
*   Insufficiently rigorous comparison to established spatially regularized methods.
*   Computational cost is a significant drawback.
*   Overstated claims of significance.
*   Hyperparameter tuning feels ad-hoc, missing a deeper analysis.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. While the paper is technically sound and clearly written, the limited novelty and modest improvements do not justify acceptance to NIPS. The authors need to demonstrate a more substantial advantage over existing methods – either in terms of accuracy, computational efficiency, or robustness – to warrant publication at a conference of this caliber. A more thorough experimental validation, including a comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art spatially regularized methods, is essential.
