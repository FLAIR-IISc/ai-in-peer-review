Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging that enforces both sparsity and spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms to learn interpretable, ROI-like “blobs” that capture inter-subject variability in brain maps. The model decomposes images Xi ≈ VUi with a compact constraint on atoms (e.g., l1-ball or simplex) to induce sparsity and nonnegativity, a Laplacian penalty ΩLap(Vj) for spatial smoothness, and ridge regularization on codes Ui for stability. The algorithm follows the online block-coordinate descent scheme of Mairal et al. (JMLR 2010), updating codes by ridge regression and atoms via a proximal update solved with a few FISTA iterations. The authors discuss Lipschitz constants for the proximal step, time-varying regularization choices, and practicalities like mini-batching.

Experiments on HCP task-fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and CanICA (group ICA/PCA). Smooth-SODL yields more spatially structured, sparser atoms than SODL, with about 3× runtime overhead relative to SODL but faster than CanICA on a single CPU. Quantitatively, CanICA explains the most variance (as expected), while Smooth-SODL improves atom stability/reproducibility and outperforms SODL in behavioral prediction and early data regimes (learning curves). The paper situates the approach relative to structured sparsity methods (GraphNet, TV-ℓ1) and recent online factorization work, arguing the Laplacian prior offers a good speed/structure trade-off.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical need in neuroimaging: scalable, interpretable decompositions that respect brain spatial structure and inter-subject variability.
- Solid engineering contribution: integrates a Laplacian penalty into the online dictionary learning framework with modest computational overhead and without compromising convergence guarantees of the block-coordinate scheme.
- Empirically convincing that the Laplacian prior improves spatial coherence and stability of atoms over SODL, especially at small-to-medium sample sizes; qualitative maps are clearly more ROI-like.
- Sensible discussion of hyperparameter effects and practical implementation choices; code planned for release in Nilearn increases impact.
- Large-scale evaluation on a widely used public dataset (HCP) with both qualitative and quantitative metrics (explained variance, stability, prediction).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Novelty is incremental: Laplacian/GraphNet-style spatial regularization and structured sparse dictionary learning exist; the main novelty is the online integration and empirical demonstration. Explicitly clarifying what is theoretically new beyond adapting [16] with a proximal update would help.
- Baselines are limited. A strong spatial baseline (e.g., TV-ℓ1/GraphNet-based dictionary learning as in Abraham et al., or other structured sparse PCA/DL) is missing; tCanICA is a post hoc threshold that is not a principled comparator. Including at least one TV-regularized or GraphNet-regularized atlas method would strengthen claims.
- Statistical rigor: many reported metrics lack uncertainty estimates (error bars, confidence intervals) and significance testing; the stability measure (mean correlation across splits) lacks details on splitting, matching protocol, and variability. Behavioral prediction results would benefit from a clear cross-validation protocol, nested model selection, and error bars.
- Some technical statements are either imprecise or optimistic. The claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations suffice to reach 1e-6 accuracy is unlikely; better to report achieved suboptimality or stopping tolerance and show that few iterations suffice empirically for good outer-loop progress. The bound L=1+4Dγ̃ assumes a particular Laplacian normalization/boundary condition; please state the exact operator and spectral bound used.
- Hyperparameter schedules (αt∼t−1/2, γ scaling with At) are heuristic; provide ablations showing sensitivity and whether constant choices perform similarly. The suggested γt=O(‖At‖∞,∞/t) rationale deserves more justification or empirical support.
- Modeling choices need more discussion: the impact of positivity/simplex constraints on interpretability and reconstruction (fMRI Z-maps can have mixed signs), choice of k, and robustness across tasks. An ablation of (i) Laplacian only, (ii) positivity only, (iii) both would disentangle contributions.
- Scope of evaluation: results are for one task contrast and k=40; it would help to show robustness across multiple tasks/contrasts and different k, or to justify choices.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the optimization scheme is appropriate, and convergence of the outer loop under inexact proximal updates is plausible in practice. Experimental evaluation is substantial but could be strengthened with stronger baselines, proper uncertainty reporting, and clearer stability/prediction protocols. Some derivations and parameterization details should be clarified.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized, but there are typos and a few incomplete sentences. Algorithmic details are mostly provided; moving critical implementation details (e.g., Laplacian operator definition, boundary conditions, stopping criteria) from the supplement into the main text would improve readability. Please clarify data splits, matching procedure for stability, and CV setup.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of combining sparsity and Laplacian smoothness is established; the main contribution is integrating it into an online DL framework with efficient proximal updates and demonstrating scalability.

Significance
For the neuroimaging community, this is a useful and practical advance likely to be adopted (especially with Nilearn integration). From a core NeurIPS perspective, the contribution is more engineering than conceptual, but the application scale and impact are persuasive.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method addressing a real need; demonstrated on large data.
- Clear gains in interpretability and stability with modest runtime overhead.
- Reproducible and likely to be used by practitioners.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental novelty; missing strong spatial regularization baselines and statistical rigor.
- Some technical claims lack precision; hyperparameter choices are heuristic without ablation support.
- Predictive performance does not surpass CanICA, somewhat weakening the “captures variability” claim.

Questions for the authors
- Please detail the Laplacian operator (grid, spacing, boundary conditions) and its spectral norm used in Lipschitz constants.
- How many outer passes T and mini-batches were used to reach reported runtimes? Are times comparable across methods (same k, same number of components)?
- How were γ and τ selected (grid, folds, criterion)? Provide sensitivity plots and error bars.
- What is the exact stability protocol (number of splits/runs, matching, statistics)?
- Why enforce nonnegativity/simplex on atoms for signed Z-maps? Did you test without positivity?
- How do results vary with k?

Recommendation
Borderline accept (leaning weak accept). The paper offers a solid, scalable, and useful extension with convincing empirical benefits in interpretability/stability on large neuroimaging data. Strengthening baselines and statistical reporting would make the case clearer. My confidence is medium.
