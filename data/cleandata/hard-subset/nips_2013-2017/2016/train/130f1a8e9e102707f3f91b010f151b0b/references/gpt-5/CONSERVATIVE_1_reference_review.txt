Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method for neuroimaging that adds a Laplacian (GraphNet-style) smoothness penalty on dictionary atoms, combined with sparsity via an l1-ball/simplex constraint. Codes are estimated with ridge regression. The dictionary is updated by block-coordinate descent; each atom subproblem is a convex quadratic with Laplacian smoothness and a convex constraint, solved by FISTA with projection onto the constraint set. The authors claim comparable complexity to Mairal’s online sparse dictionary learning, with only a 2–3x slowdown. On HCP task fMRI Z-maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40), Smooth-SODL is reported to produce more spatially coherent and reproducible components than SODL, and better early-sample explained variance (EV). It does not outperform ICA-like baselines on EV or prediction of behavioral measures but claims advantages in interpretability and stability.

Relation to prior work
The method sits at the intersection of online dictionary learning (Mairal et al.) and structured sparsity for images (GraphNet, structured sparse PCA; TV–l1 dictionary learning in neuroimaging). The idea of imposing Laplacian/TV smoothness on spatial maps is established, as are online dictionary-learning frameworks. The contribution is essentially the integration of a Laplacian-regularized atom update within the online BCD loop. The paper cites relevant work (e.g., Abraham MICCAI for TV–l1 DL, GraphNet, Jenatton et al. structured SPCA), but does not empirically compare to structured alternatives.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant problem in neuroimaging: learning spatially localized, interpretable population components at scale.
- Algorithmic formulation is straightforward: smooth convex per-atom subproblems with simple projections, amenable to accelerated proximal methods.
- Experiments use a large public dataset (HCP), include comparisons to SODL and CanICA, and evaluate EV, stability, and downstream prediction.
- Reported runtime overhead relative to SODL is modest.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological clarity and rigor
- The novelty appears incremental: Laplacian smoothing on atoms within online DL is a relatively straightforward adaptation of GraphNet/structured SPCA ideas. A key missing piece is a comparison with TV-regularized dictionary learning (Abraham; Varoquaux; FAASTA) or structured SPCA baselines. Without this, it is hard to assess the significance of choosing Laplacian vs TV/fused penalties.
- Convergence claims are overstated. The per-atom subproblem is strongly convex and has a unique minimizer, but the paper reports using only 1–2 FISTA iterations and still asserts “guaranteed to converge to a global optimum at each iteration.” That guarantee requires solving the subproblem to optimality. The effect of inexact updates on the overall algorithm is not analyzed.
- The time-varying γ discussion (Eq. 9) is ad hoc. The dependence of the effective Lipschitz constant on At[j,j]/t is plausible, but the proposed γt scaling is not derived from a stability proof; it reads as a practical heuristic. There is no analysis of how this impacts convergence or solution bias.
- The choice of constraint set C as a simplex (non-negativity + l1-bound) for task fMRI contrasts is not motivated. Negative effects are common in GLM contrasts; forcing non-negativity may distort components. An ablation contrasting simplex vs l1-ball vs elastic net would be informative.

Experimental design and baselines
- The comparison to CanICA is not entirely fair: CanICA is run on smoothed data (6mm FWHM), whereas SODL/Smooth-SODL are not. Pre-smoothing changes the effective signal/noise structure and EV; either smooth all methods to a common preprocessing standard or none.
- No comparisons to other structured decompositions (TV–l1 DL, GraphNet/SPCA) despite positioning the work as solving their scalability limitation. Without these, it is unclear that Laplacian regularization is the right trade-off for “blobby” ROIs vs TV (which is more standard for compactness).
- Results lack statistical testing (e.g., confidence intervals/variance across splits) for EV, stability, and prediction. Reported stability improvements are modest (0.4 vs 0.1 correlation) and should be quantified with significance.
- The predictive utility is mixed: CanICA performs best on behavioral prediction; Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL but not the strongest baseline. This weakens claims of practical impact.
- The runtime claims (5.6 minutes total on a laptop CPU for n=500, p=2.6e5, k=40) seem optimistic given repeated large-scale FISTA updates per atom; details are missing (number of passes T, number of FISTA iterations per atom, memory footprint). More thorough complexity reporting is needed.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typos and notational issues (e.g., inconsistent factor 1/2 in v^TΔv; Algorithm 2 uses “for j=1..r” where r is undefined). Some equations are under-explained (derivation of Eq. 7).
- The Fourier-domain acceleration is mentioned but not described.
- Figures and hyperparameter selection details (Fig. 1) are not fully provided; “safe range” statements are qualitative.

Qualitative evaluation per NIPS criteria
- Quality: The approach is technically plausible but lacks rigorous convergence analysis under inexact updates and lacks strong empirical baselines and statistical validation.
- Clarity: Generally understandable but needs careful editing and more algorithmic detail to be reproducible.
- Originality: Incremental; the integration of Laplacian smoothing into online DL is not a substantial conceptual advance.
- Significance: Potentially useful in practice for interpretable, scalable decompositions, but current evidence is limited and does not outperform strong baselines on key metrics.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide explicit FISTA updates (gradient and projection) for Eq. (7), and quantify how many iterations are used per atom, with ablation on accuracy vs speed.
2. Justify the simplex constraint for task contrast maps and report ablations vs l1-ball and Elastic Net constraints.
3. Clarify the γ schedule rationale and provide a stability/convergence result (even under inexact subproblem solves), or at least empirical sensitivity curves.
4. Ensure fair preprocessing across baselines; add TV–l1 DL and structured SPCA/GraphNet baselines.
5. Report T (number of passes), mini-batch size, total epochs, and wall-clock per pass to substantiate runtime claims; include memory usage.
6. Add statistical tests/intervals for EV, stability, and prediction; evaluate multiple tasks/modalities to assess generality.

Pros
- Addresses an important application; scalable implementation.
- Reasonable improvement in interpretability/stability over SODL.
- Clear link to established structured regularization (GraphNet).

Cons
- Limited novelty; missing key structured baselines.
- Overstated convergence claims; heuristic γ scheduling.
- Mixed downstream performance; lack of statistical rigor and reproducibility details.
- Presentation issues and some methodological ambiguities.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). With stronger baselines (TV/GraphNet), fair preprocessing, statistical validation, clearer convergence/complexity analysis, and improved clarity, this could become a solid application paper. As is, the contribution is incremental and insufficiently validated for NeurIPS.
