Summary
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning approach that augments Mairal et al.’s Sparse Online Dictionary Learning [16] with a Laplacian (graph Laplacian/roughness) penalty on the dictionary atoms and an L1/simplex constraint to encourage sparsity and nonnegativity. Codes are estimated via ridge regression; dictionary atoms are updated by block coordinate descent with per-atom convex subproblems solved by projected FISTA on a quadratic objective with Laplacian smoothing. The authors argue this imposes spatially localized, smooth “blobby” functional units suitable for ROI definition. Experiments on HCP task fMRI Z-maps (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to vanilla SODL and CanICA. Reported results show: (i) improved qualitative interpretability; (ii) somewhat higher stability/reproducibility than SODL and thresholded CanICA; (iii) reduced explained variance relative to CanICA but competitive generalization; (iv) modest behavioral prediction improvements over SODL and thresholded CanICA. Runtime is ≈3× SODL and faster than CanICA.

Relation to prior work
The formulation is closely related to GraphNet-style penalties [11] and to structured sparse decompositions in neuroimaging using TV/L1 or Laplacian regularization (e.g., Abraham et al. [1], Varoquaux et al. [23, 25]) as well as structured sparse PCA [14]. The novelty claimed is to integrate a Laplacian prior into an online dictionary learning framework with low additional cost, preserving convergence properties of [16].

Strengths
- Addresses a real neuroimaging need: stable, interpretable, spatially coherent group-level components/ROIs learned from large cohorts without heavy preprocessing.
- Method is simple to implement atop established SODL: code update via ridge; atom update via projected FISTA with known Lipschitz constant; projection onto simplex/L1-ball is standard and fast.
- Per-atom subproblems are convex; the block separability and convergence arguments largely mirror [16].
- Empirical results suggest improved qualitative spatial coherence and higher split-half stability than SODL; sensible trade-off between interpretability and fidelity.
- Scalability appears reasonable; reported runtimes are modest.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/rigor
- Limited and potentially unbalanced baselines. The most relevant structured methods (TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning [1], GraphNet decompositions [11], structured sparse PCA [14]) are cited but not compared experimentally. Claiming “minor cost” vs TV/structured approaches is not supported by runtime/quality comparisons.
- Hyperparameter selection is largely heuristic. αt ∼ t^(-1/2) and γ scheduling to avoid blow-up are suggested without principled justification or ablation. The “safe range” of (γ, τ) is discussed qualitatively; details relegated to figures (not accessible here) and no systematic selection protocol beyond “K-fold CV on explained variance.” The risk of over-smoothing or degeneracy is acknowledged but not characterized.
- The statement that “1–2 iterations of FISTA suffice to reach 1e-6 accuracy” is implausible for ill-conditioned Laplacian-regularized problems unless there is strong warm-starting; no convergence traces or stopping criteria are shown. If 1–2 iterations are used in practice, this is an inexact BCD and calls for analysis or at least empirical convergence diagnostics.
- Prediction experiments lack methodological detail. The “RAW” baseline uses univariate voxel selection; it is unclear whether feature selection is nested within cross-validation. The regression model for all methods (ridge? lasso?) and hyperparameter tuning protocol are not specified. This opens the possibility of leakage or unfair comparisons.
- Fairness of preprocessing choices: CanICA is run on smoothed data while SODL/Smooth-SODL are not; this may confound conclusions about EV and stability. A control where all methods share the same smoothing would strengthen claims.

Originality
- The technical contribution is incremental: adding a Laplacian quadratic to the SODL atom-update and solving per-atom convex problems with projected FISTA is a straightforward adaptation of known components (GraphNet penalty, online DL). The paper does not introduce new optimization techniques or theory beyond [16]; convergence guarantees are inherited qualitatively but not extended.
- The idea of spatially structured sparse decompositions for brain maps is well-trodden. The main novelty is the online instantiation and empirical study at HCP scale.

Significance
- Potentially useful for practitioners seeking fast, interpretable components; however, the paper does not show clear advancement on key quantitative axes. Smooth-SODL underperforms CanICA on explained variance and on most behavioral prediction tasks; gains over SODL are primarily in qualitative interpretability and stability, which, while important, are not rigorously quantified beyond a single stability metric.
- Without comparisons to other structured methods and with limited ablations, it is hard to assess whether Smooth-SODL is the preferable point on the quality/speed/interpretability Pareto front.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but the presentation has typos, inconsistent notation, and missing details (exact proximal/projection operator in FISTA loop; number of passes T; minibatch protocol; stopping criteria for inner/outer loops). The “lim n→∞ 1/n” objective is unusual for finite datasets—clarify how it is instantiated.
- Experimental details (CV splits, matching of atoms, stability computations, leakage prevention) need to be specified to ensure reproducibility. Code is “to be released,” which reduces reproducibility at review time.

Pros
- Simple, scalable extension of established SODL accommodating spatial structure.
- Empirical evidence of improved interpretability and stability.
- Useful practical guidance (even if heuristic) on parameter regimes and runtime/accuracy trade-offs.

Cons
- Incremental technical novelty with limited theoretical contribution.
- Missing strong structured baselines and key ablations.
- Incomplete methodological details for prediction and hyperparameter tuning; questionable claims on FISTA iterations/accuracy.
- Mixed quantitative performance relative to established ICA baselines.

Questions for authors
1. Provide exact algorithms for the per-atom update: is FISTA used with projection onto the simplex/L1 ball as the proximal operator? What are stopping criteria and typical iteration counts with/without warm starts?
2. How are α, γ, τ selected in practice? Any nested CV? Please report sensitivity curves and a principled schedule for γt if it varies with t.
3. Are the prediction pipelines fully nested to avoid leakage (feature selection and model tuning within folds)?
4. Can you include comparisons to TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning and structured sparse PCA at comparable runtimes?
5. Why smooth only ICA? Please include a control where all methods share the same preprocessing.

Recommendation
Reject. The work is a pragmatic and potentially useful application-oriented extension of [16], but the technical novelty is limited, key baselines/ablations are missing, and parts of the methodology and evaluation lack rigor. Strengthening the experimental comparisons (especially to structured methods), clarifying optimization details, and releasing code would make this more compelling.
