This paper tackles a crucial problem in neuroimaging: leveraging inter-subject variability to define meaningful brain regions, moving beyond the limitations of purely data-driven or anatomy-based ROI definitions. The core idea – using structured sparse dictionary learning to uncover shared patterns of variability – is genuinely novel and represents a promising direction. While building on online dictionary learning, the introduction of a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness is a significant extension, addressing a key limitation of standard approaches. The authors correctly identify the connections to clustering, PCA/ICA, and existing ROI-based methods, situating their work within a broader context.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The proposed Smooth-SODL algorithm offers a principled way to define ROIs that are both data-driven and spatially coherent. The experimental validation on the large HCP dataset is a major plus, and the comparison to CanICA and standard SODL provides valuable insights. The results, particularly the demonstration of improved performance in predicting behavioral variables, are encouraging. The authors acknowledge and address the computational cost increase introduced by the Laplacian penalty, and their discussion of hyperparameter tuning (α, γ, τ) is insightful. The inclusion of implementation details (planned Nilearn integration) is also a positive aspect.

However, there are some weaknesses. While the authors demonstrate improved performance over SODL, the gains aren’t always overwhelming, and the practical differences in the resulting ROIs aren't always visually striking. The explanation of why the spatial regularization is *needed* isn't fully convincing, especially given the large sample size of the HCP dataset.  The discussion of the optimality regime (Eq. 9) is rather dense and might benefit from more intuitive explanation. The claim of convergence guarantees relies on the properties of the underlying BCD scheme from [16], which needs to be referenced more explicitly in the convergence discussion. Although the authors touch on initialization, a more thorough investigation of its impact would be beneficial. Finally, while the prediction results are promising, a comparison to established ROI-based methods using *predefined* ROIs (e.g., from the AAL atlas) would provide a stronger baseline. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of structured sparsity and online dictionary learning for ROI definition is a genuinely novel approach.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is important, and the proposed method has the potential to improve the analysis of neuroimaging data.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical foundation is solid, and the experimental validation is robust.
*   **Potential Impact:** This work could influence how ROIs are defined in future neuroimaging studies.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Incremental Improvement:** While the improvements over existing methods are present, they are not always dramatic.
*   **Complexity:** The addition of the Laplacian penalty increases the computational cost and introduces additional hyperparameters to tune.
*   **Limited Comparison:**  A comparison to standard ROI-based methods using established atlases is missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. While not without its minor flaws, the strengths of the work – particularly the novelty of the approach and the robust experimental validation – outweigh the weaknesses.  I am willing to overlook the incremental nature of the improvement and the relatively minor limitations in the evaluation. I believe this paper introduces a significant new direction for ROI definition in neuroimaging and warrants acceptance.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
