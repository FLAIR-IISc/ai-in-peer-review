## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth-SODL, for learning spatially coherent brain regions directly from population-level fMRI data variability. The core idea is to leverage online dictionary learning with both sparsity and smoothness constraints to decompose fMRI maps into a set of interpretable, spatially localized components (dictionary atoms).  This approach aims to address the limitations of traditional ROI definition methods (anatomical priors, activation-based approaches) and the challenges of directly applying clustering or dimensionality reduction techniques to high-dimensional neuroimaging data. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on a large HCP dataset, comparing it to standard SODL and CanICA, showcasing improved performance in terms of dictionary interpretability, stability, and predictive power.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Significant Problem:**  The paper tackles a fundamental challenge in neuroimaging – defining meaningful regions of interest (ROIs) – in a principled, data-driven manner.  Traditional methods are often ad-hoc or rely on potentially biased priors.
* **Novel Approach:** Combining online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization to enforce spatial smoothness is a clever and novel extension of existing techniques.  This tackles the inherent high dimensionality and noisy nature of fMRI data effectively.
* **Scalability:** The online learning approach is crucial for handling the massive datasets common in modern neuroimaging, which is clearly articulated and a key contribution.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on the HCP dataset are well-designed and demonstrate consistent improvements over competing methods. The comparison to CanICA, a strong baseline, is particularly valuable.  The analysis of learning curves provides insights into the benefits of spatial regularization.
* **Clear Writing and Structure:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The mathematical formulation is clear, and the algorithmic details are provided sufficiently for reproducibility. The practical considerations section is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  While the paper discusses hyperparameter tuning, the sensitivity to parameters (α, γ, τ) appears considerable.  The described heuristics are helpful, but a more systematic investigation or automated tuning procedure would strengthen the work.  The optimal regime described feels a bit hand-wavy.
* **Computational Cost:** The running time of Smooth-SODL (340s) is significantly longer than standard SODL (110s), and while still reasonable, this difference might become a concern with even larger datasets or more complex models. The authors acknowledge this, but further optimization or parallelization strategies could be explored.
* **Limited Scope of Behavioral Prediction:** The behavioral prediction results are promising, but the specific variables chosen for prediction (IQ, reading proficiency) are somewhat limited.  Exploring a wider range of cognitive and behavioral measures would strengthen the impact of this finding.
* **CanICA Implementation:** The description of the CanICA implementation, specifically the thresholding step ("tCanICA"), feels somewhat rushed and lacks detail. A more thorough comparison, including a discussion of the sensitivity of tCanICA to the threshold value, would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Segmentation/Clustering Methods:** While the paper motivates itself by contrasting with ROI definition, it doesn't directly compare against more recent and sophisticated segmentation or clustering algorithms tailored to neuroimaging data (e.g., those employing deep learning).

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are reasonably well-supported by the experimental results.  The method appears to be a complete and well-defined piece of work, though further investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational scalability would be valuable. The authors are careful to evaluate both strengths and weaknesses.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the algorithmic description is understandable.

**Originality:** The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for spatial smoothness is novel and represents a meaningful contribution to the field.

**Significance:** The results are potentially significant. A data-driven method for defining brain regions that is scalable and yields interpretable results could have a substantial impact on neuroimaging research.  The potential to improve predictive power for cognitive and behavioral measures further enhances the significance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed method, Smooth-SODL, addresses a significant problem in a principled and scalable manner.  While there are some minor weaknesses related to hyperparameter sensitivity, computational cost, and the scope of the behavioral prediction analysis, these do not detract substantially from the overall quality of the work.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and well-motivated approach to ROI definition.
* Scalable to large datasets.
* Demonstrably superior performance compared to existing methods.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Potential for significant impact on neuroimaging research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Increased computational cost compared to standard SODL.
* Limited scope of behavioral prediction experiments.



**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above). I recommend the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process and potentially explore strategies for improving computational efficiency.  A more comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art segmentation methods would also be beneficial.
