Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), an online matrix factorization method for neuroimaging that learns spatially localized, sparse, and smooth brain maps (dictionary atoms) and per-subject loadings (codes). Each brain image Xi is approximated by VUi, with a dictionary V ∈ Rp×k subject to sparsity constraints (e.g., simplex or ℓ1-ball) and a Laplacian regularization ΩLap(Vj) to enforce piecewise-smooth, blob-like structure. Codes are learned via ridge regression (with an option for ℓ1 penalties), and the dictionary is updated by block coordinate descent using sufficient statistics At and Bt as in [16] (Mairal et al.), with per-atom proximal optimization of a quadratic + Laplacian + constraint subproblem (GraphNet-like) solved by FISTA.

This extends the SODL framework [16] by adding a structured spatial prior on the atoms while keeping online scalability. Related structured decompositions using TV or structured sparsity (e.g., [1, 3, 11, 14, 23, 24]) exist, but have typically relied on slower alternating optimization and batch settings; the contribution here is to integrate spatial regularization efficiently into an online BCD scheme using proximal methods. Comparisons are made to SODL [16] and CanICA [22]. Experiments on HCP task-fMRI Z-maps (n=500, p≈2.6×10^5, k=40) evaluate explained variance, stability/reproducibility, interpretability, runtime, and behavioral prediction.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Formulation: The objective is well-defined and convex in each block (codes/dictionary), with compact convex constraints on atoms and a Laplacian quadratic penalty. The code update is standard ridge regression; the dictionary subproblem per atom is a strongly convex proximal problem with a known Lipschitz constant for its smooth part, making FISTA appropriate.
- Online algorithm: The use of sufficient statistics At, Bt and BCD follows [16] and should inherit convergence guarantees under similar assumptions (compact support, unique code solution). However, the paper alternates approximate solutions of the per-atom proximal subproblems (via a few FISTA steps), which weakens exact per-iteration optimality claims.
- Convergence claims: It is stated that the BCD dictionary update “is guaranteed to converge to a global optimum at each iteration” and the overall algorithm converges under [16]. This is correct for the convex subproblem if solved to optimality; with early-stopped FISTA (1–2 iterations as claimed), this becomes an inexact BCD and should be discussed with appropriate conditions (e.g., summable errors) to ensure convergence. The discussion around the Lipschitz constant and the suggestion of time-varying γt is useful, but the interplay between fixed γ (as a tunable hyperparameter) and the adaptive γt required to avoid ill-conditioning is not completely resolved.
- Computational aspects: The proximal step requires eigenvalue bounds of the Laplacian for L, which are provided. Claims that “1 or 2 iterations of FISTA reach 1e−6 accuracy” appear implausible for general settings; a more cautious statement about practical sufficiency of few iterations would be preferable. Runtime comparisons are informative but lack hardware details (CPU model, BLAS/MKL, threading), making reproducibility more difficult.
- Experiments: The setup is reasonable and metrics are standard (explained variance, stability via atom matching, predictive R2). Statistical uncertainty (variance or confidence intervals) is not reported, and cross-validation details for hyperparameters (α, γ, τ) are brief. The comparison does not include a structured TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning baseline (e.g., [1]) at smaller scale; this limits conclusions about the structured prior beyond Laplacian vs ℓ1-only.

Clarity
The core ideas are understandable, and algorithmic steps are described with helpful pointers to [16]. However, clarity is hindered by numerous typos and formatting issues (e.g., equation numbering, “.2 Practical considerations,” inconsistent notation), incomplete citations (missing years/pages), and some imprecise statements (FISTA complexity, “parameter-free” BCD while FISTA uses L). A clearer, self-contained derivation of the per-atom update, exact complexity, memory usage, and a precise convergence proposition (with inexact updates) would improve readability.

Originality
The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian prior on atoms for large-scale neuroimaging is a meaningful but incremental contribution. Spatially structured sparse decompositions are known (TV/GraphNet/structured sparsity) and online dictionary learning is established; the novelty lies in showing that a Laplacian-regularized atom update can be integrated into the [16] framework with modest computational overhead and practical benefit. The paper positions itself appropriately relative to prior work but would benefit from stronger empirical comparison to other structured regularizers.

Significance
The method addresses a practical need in large-scale neuroimaging: learning reproducible, interpretable ROIs at population level with tractable runtime. The empirical findings—better interpretability and stability than SODL, competitive explained variance, improved early-data efficiency, and reasonable predictive performance—suggest utility for practitioners. The impact is likely moderate: the algorithm is a practical refinement of existing tools rather than a conceptual breakthrough. Inclusion in widely used software (Nilearn) would increase adoption.

Strengths
- Integrates a spatially structured prior into online dictionary learning with limited runtime overhead; scalable to p≈10^5–10^6.
- Solid formulation using proximal methods; subproblem is strongly convex and well-understood (GraphNet).
- Empirical evidence of improved interpretability and stability over SODL, with competitive explained variance and prediction performance.
- Sensible analysis of hyperparameter regimes and learning curves demonstrating benefits in low-data regimes.
- Clear connection to prior online DL [16] and neuroimaging literature.

Weaknesses
- Overstated convergence and accuracy claims for per-atom updates; insufficient treatment of inexact BCD convergence.
- Clarity issues: typos, formatting, incomplete references, imprecise complexity statements.
- Missing comparisons to other structured baselines (e.g., TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning) and lack of statistical reporting (variability/CI).
- Potential fairness concern: CanICA is run with smoothing and optionally hard-thresholded, while proposed methods are not smoothed; effect of smoothing on SODL/Smooth-SODL is not reported.
- Limited ablations: no systematic study of k, code penalty (ridge vs ℓ1), or different Laplacian weights beyond a couple of settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide rigorous convergence discussion for inexact per-atom updates; specify stopping criteria and error control.
- Correct FISTA complexity statements and quantify iteration counts empirically vs target accuracy.
- Report hardware/software details, memory footprint, and complexity per iteration.
- Add comparisons to a TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning baseline on a smaller subset to isolate the contribution of the Laplacian prior.
- Include variability (e.g., mean±std over splits) and significance tests where applicable; expand stability metric details.
- Explore ablations on k, α (ridge vs ℓ1 codes), and smoothing choices across methods; quantify spatial smoothness/compactness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method that improves interpretability and stability with modest overhead.
- Addresses a relevant problem in large-scale neuroimaging with convincing empirical benefits.
- Methodological integration (Laplacian + online DL) is useful and likely to be adopted.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to existing structured and online DL methods.
- Convergence/accuracy claims need tightening; clarity and presentation issues.
- Missing key baselines and statistical rigor limit the strength of empirical conclusions.

Overall, the work presents a useful applied contribution with solid potential impact in neuroimaging, but would benefit from stronger theoretical precision and more comprehensive empirical evaluation.
