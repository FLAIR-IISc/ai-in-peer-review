Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method for neuroimaging that learns population-level spatial components (atoms) that are both sparse and spatially structured. The approach models each subject image Xi as a linear combination of k atoms V with subject-specific codes U, akin to PCA/ICA but with spatial sparsity and smoothness on atoms. Sparsity is induced via a convex constraint on each atom (either an l1-ball or simplex), and spatial structure via a Laplacian penalty on atoms. Code updates use ridge regression; dictionary updates are solved per-atom by a convex quadratic with Laplacian term, tackled via FISTA with projection onto the chosen constraint set. The method extends the online framework of Mairal et al. (JMLR 2010) to include spatial structure at low overhead. Experiments on HCP task-fMRI maps (n=500, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA (multi-subject PCA/ICA), examining explained variance, stability/reproducibility, qualitative interpretability, behavioral prediction, and runtime.

Prior work on structured decompositions in neuroimaging includes TV- and GraphNet-regularized dictionary learning and regression (e.g., Abraham MICCAI; Grosenick NeuroImage; Jenatton et al. AISTATS), and online dictionary learning (Mairal JMLR; Mensch ICML). The paper positions itself as bringing spatial regularization into large-scale online DL with guarantees and minimal computational overhead, and compares to SODL and CanICA.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Formulation: The objective combines a ridge-penalized coding step with constrained atoms and a Laplacian penalty, leading to per-atom convex subproblems. The per-atom update is correctly identified as a strongly convex quadratic with a Laplacian smoothness term plus projection onto a convex set. Solving these via FISTA with known Lipschitz constant of the gradient is appropriate.
- Optimization: The overall algorithm follows block coordinate descent in an online setting using sufficient statistics (A, B), as in Mairal. Convergence to a stationary point for the nonconvex global problem is standard under their assumptions; per-atom subproblems are convex, so each atom update reaches a global optimum if solved to convergence.
- Practical details: The paper proposes time-varying αt ∝ t−1/2 and suggests adapting γ to control the Lipschitz constant: γt = O(‖At‖∞,∞/t). This stabilizes step sizes but effectively changes the objective over time; no convergence analysis is provided for time-varying γ, which weakens theoretical guarantees. The claim that “1–2 iterations of FISTA reach 1e−6 accuracy” is unlikely in general without warm starts and should be qualified or empirically substantiated.
- Constraints: Experiments use a simplex constraint (nonnegativity + l1-radius) on atoms. For signed statistical maps (Z-maps), this disallows negative patterns within an atom. The paper should justify this choice or report results with l1-ball allowing signed atoms.
- Evaluation: The experimental protocol includes train/test EV, stability across data splits via Hungarian matching, behavioral prediction, and runtime. However, statistical uncertainty (e.g., confidence intervals) is not reported, and only one task contrast and k=40 are tested.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well structured. The algorithmic steps are clear, with references to derivations and supplementary details. There are noticeable typos/grammar issues and occasional informal claims; tightening language and adding precise statements where claims are made (e.g., FISTA iterations, convergence with time-varying γ) would improve clarity. Explicitly defining the Laplacian operator (boundary conditions) and its spectral norm for the chosen grid would also help reproducibility.

Originality
The main novelty lies in integrating a Laplacian-regularized per-atom update within the online DL framework with minimal additional cost and maintaining the standard sufficient-statistics scheme. While spatially-structured DL in neuroimaging is not new (TV- and GraphNet-based methods exist), those have typically used batch/alternating solvers. The proposed online approach with Laplacian smoothness appears to be a practical and incremental contribution rather than a fundamentally new model.

Significance
The method addresses a relevant problem—learning interpretable, spatially structured population-level components at scale. Empirically, Smooth-SODL:
- Improves stability/reproducibility over SODL and CanICA on HCP task data.
- Matches or approaches SODL’s explained variance while producing more spatially coherent atoms.
- Outperforms SODL and thresholded CanICA in behavioral prediction; CanICA often achieves the best EV and prediction but yields dense, less interpretable components requiring ad hoc thresholding.
- Incurs ~3x runtime over SODL and is faster than CanICA in the reported setup.

These results suggest practical utility, especially in low/medium data regimes (learning curves indicate gains over SODL with few subjects). Broader significance would be strengthened by evaluation on other datasets (e.g., resting-state, multiple tasks), exploring different k, and comparisons to other structured DL baselines (e.g., TV-regularized online methods, Mensch 2016).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, practical extension of online DL to include spatial structure with small computational overhead.
- Convex per-atom updates with standard first-order solvers; straightforward to implement and scale.
- Empirical gains in reproducibility and qualitative interpretability, with competitive EV and prediction.
- Addresses a common need in neuroimaging: data-driven ROI extraction with better spatial coherence.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical scope: single task contrast, single k, no uncertainty estimates; no comparison to other structured DL formulations beyond CanICA and unstructured SODL.
- Theoretical aspects under-specified: adaptation of γ over time lacks convergence analysis; optimistic claims about FISTA iterations lack evidence.
- Methodological choices (simplex constraint) may preclude modeling negative patterns; justification or alternatives are not explored.
- Code not yet released; reproducibility depends on upcoming availability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations for l1-ball vs simplex constraints and report signed atoms when appropriate.
- Include additional datasets (resting-state, other tasks) and vary k; report confidence intervals and statistical tests.
- Compare to TV-regularized DL with modern fast solvers (e.g., FAESTA/FAASTA) and to recent scalable DL (e.g., Mensch 2016).
- Either fix γ and analyze step sizes, or provide convergence arguments for time-varying γ; temper or justify the FISTA iteration claim.
- Release code and scripts, and detail hyperparameter selection (CV folds, grids, stopping criteria).

Overall assessment
Technically sound and practically useful incremental contribution that brings spatially structured atoms into an online DL framework with compelling empirical benefits in interpretability and stability. The paper would benefit from broader validation and stronger theoretical clarity around time-varying regularization, but the approach is relevant and likely to be adopted.
