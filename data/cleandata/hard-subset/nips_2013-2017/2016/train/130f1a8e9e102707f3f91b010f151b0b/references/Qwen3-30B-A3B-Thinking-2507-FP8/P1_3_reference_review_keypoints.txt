- Summary: Proposes Smooth-SODL—online dictionary learning with L1 sparsity and Laplacian spatial smoothness—to learn interpretable brain regions from fMRI, outperforming SODL and CanICA on HCP data.  
- Quality: Technically strong with sound optimization analysis, convergence guarantees, comprehensive experiments, and efficient implementation (~3x SODL runtime).  
- Clarity: Exceptionally well-written and organized with reproducible details and effective figures; some neuroimaging jargon could be clarified for non-specialists.  
- Originality: Novel combination of sparse and Laplacian-regularized online dictionary learning for brain-region discovery, more efficient and principled than prior spatial methods.  
- Significance: Addresses an important neuroimaging problem, scales to large datasets (500 subjects), improves interpretability/stability and predictive performance, and targets adoption via Nilearn.  
- Strength — Practical impact: Tackles meaningful ROI definition problem likely to aid neuroimaging analyses.  
- Strength — Methodology: Well-motivated extension of existing dictionary learning with principled spatial regularization.  
- Strength — Evaluation: Comprehensive empirical validation across visual quality, explained variance, stability, and predictive tasks.  
- Strength — Efficiency: Computationally efficient and feasible for large-scale studies.  
- Strength — Interpretability: Spatial regularization produces blob-like, more interpretable brain regions.  
- Strength — Reproducibility: Includes algorithm pseudocode, implementation details, and plan for open-source release.  
- Weakness — Theory depth: Analysis of the Laplacian penalty’s impact on optimization is somewhat superficial.  
- Weakness — Baseline comparisons: Could compare more thoroughly to other spatial regularizers (e.g., TV-regularized dictionary learning).  
- Weakness — Claims nuance: Some claims about necessity of spatial regularization need more nuanced discussion across data types.  
- Weakness — Hyperparameters: Hyperparameter selection procedure could be described in greater detail for reproducibility.  
- Overall assessment: High-quality, well-executed, and impactful work that meaningfully advances ML and neuroimaging.  
- Recommendation: Accept — minor weaknesses do not outweigh the paper’s contributions and practical significance.
