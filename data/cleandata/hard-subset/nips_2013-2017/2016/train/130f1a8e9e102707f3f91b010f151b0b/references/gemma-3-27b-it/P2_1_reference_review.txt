## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth-SODL, for learning spatially localized and sparse brain regions directly from population-level fMRI data variability. The authors address the challenge of defining Regions of Interest (ROIs) without relying on prior knowledge or cross-modal registration.  They extend existing online dictionary learning (SODL) techniques by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness alongside sparsity. The method is presented with theoretical justification and evaluated on a large HCP dataset, comparing its performance to standard SODL and ICA-based methods (CanICA) in terms of runtime, dictionary interpretability, and prediction of behavioral variables.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Defining ROIs is a critical step in many neuroimaging analyses, and the proposed method offers a data-driven, principled alternative to existing approaches.
* **Novel approach:** Combining online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for structured sparsity is a creative idea, offering a balance between computational efficiency and spatial coherence.
* **Scalability:** The online learning framework makes the method suitable for large-scale datasets, a significant advantage in neuroimaging.
* **Comprehensive evaluation:** The authors perform a thorough evaluation on a large, publicly available dataset (HCP), comparing their method to relevant baselines (SODL, CanICA) across multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, behavioral prediction).
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and provides a clear explanation of the method, its theoretical underpinnings, and experimental setup. The inclusion of equations and algorithms enhances reproducibility.
* **Practical considerations:** The paper discusses hyperparameter tuning and initialization, addressing practical concerns for implementation.  The acknowledgment of computational cost and trade-offs (sparsity vs. smoothness) is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Improvement, Not a Paradigm Shift:** While the results show improvements over standard SODL, the gains aren’t overwhelmingly large, particularly in the final explained variance performance compared to CanICA. The authors acknowledge CanICA’s superior performance in explained variance, which somewhat diminishes the impact of their contribution.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The parameter selection process (α, γ, τ) appears complex and requires careful tuning. While the paper provides guidance, it doesn't offer a fully automated approach.  The data-adaptive choice for α is a good start, but the optimal range for γ and τ still requires cross-validation.
* **Computational Cost:** While scalable, Smooth-SODL is noticeably slower than SODL, requiring significantly more computational resources. The authors discuss this trade-off, but it's a practical consideration for users with limited computing power.
* **Limited Novelty in Spatial Regularization:** The use of Laplacian regularization for spatial smoothness is not entirely novel in the context of image processing. The core contribution lies in its successful integration into the online dictionary learning framework.
* **Interpretation of "Blobs":**  The authors emphasize that the learned components represent “blobs.” While visually plausible, it would be beneficial to explore how these blobs align with known anatomical structures or functional networks beyond the qualitative assessment provided.  A more quantitative validation against established brain atlases would strengthen the paper.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations and algorithmic descriptions are clear and seem correct. The experimental validation is well-designed, and the results are presented in a convincing manner.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but some sections (e.g., the discussion of convergence) could benefit from further simplification. The mathematical notation is appropriate for the target audience.
* **Originality:** The combination of online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization is a novel contribution. However, the individual components (online learning, sparse coding, Laplacian regularization) are well-established techniques. The originality lies in the integration and application to the specific problem of ROI definition in neuroimaging.
* **Significance:** The paper addresses a practically important problem in neuroimaging and provides a potentially valuable tool for researchers. While not a revolutionary breakthrough, the method offers a robust and scalable alternative to existing ROI definition approaches.  The impact hinges on its adoption by the neuroimaging community and its ability to facilitate more insightful analyses.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Solves a relevant problem in neuroimaging with a novel and technically sound approach.
* Demonstrates empirically significant improvements over a closely related baseline (SODL).
* Offers a scalable solution for large-scale fMRI data analysis.
*  Is clearly written and well-organized, with a thorough experimental evaluation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The improvements over standard SODL are incremental, and CanICA demonstrates superior explained variance.
*  The method requires careful hyperparameter tuning, which can be computationally expensive.
* The computational cost of Smooth-SODL is higher than SODL.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While it doesn’t represent a radical departure from existing techniques, it offers a valuable extension to online dictionary learning with clear benefits for neuroimaging applications. The strengths of the paper – its technical soundness, thorough evaluation, and practical relevance – outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  Elaborate on the alignment of the learned "blobs" with known anatomical structures or functional networks, potentially through quantitative comparisons with established brain atlases.
2. Provide further discussion on methods to automate or simplify hyperparameter selection.
3.  Clarify the trade-offs between computational cost and performance gains in the discussion section.
