### **Summary**

This paper addresses the important problem of defining brain Regions of Interest (ROIs) from population-level neuroimaging data. The authors propose a new method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), to decompose a set of subject-level statistical maps into a shared dictionary of spatially coherent "atoms" (the ROIs) and subject-specific loading coefficients. The core technical contribution is the extension of the scalable Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (SODL) framework by Mairal et al. (2010) to include a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty promotes spatial smoothness, encouraging the learned atoms to be contiguous, blob-like regions, which is neuroanatomically plausible. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a large fMRI dataset (500 subjects from the Human Connectome Project), where it is shown to produce more interpretable, stable, and reproducible brain maps compared to standard SODL and a popular PCA/ICA-based method (CanICA), while remaining computationally efficient.

### **Evaluation**

#### **Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed model is a well-motivated and sound extension of an established dictionary learning framework. The optimization problem is clearly formulated, and the proposed Block-Coordinate Descent (BCD) algorithm is appropriate for its structure. The authors correctly identify that the dictionary update sub-problem is a standard denoising instance for which fast solvers exist, and they astutely analyze the computational complexity, noting the potential for blow-up if the regularization parameter `γ` is not chosen carefully.

The experimental validation is thorough and compelling. The authors use a large, modern, and publicly available dataset (HCP), which lends credibility to the results. The choice of baselines is appropriate, including the unstructured version of their own algorithm (SODL) and a widely used alternative approach (CanICA). The evaluation metrics are comprehensive, covering not just data-fit (explained variance) but also crucial qualitative aspects for this application domain, such as interpretability (visual inspection), stability across data splits, and utility for downstream tasks (predicting behavioral scores). The authors are also commendably honest about the trade-offs; for instance, they show that CanICA achieves higher explained variance but at the cost of interpretability and stability, which is a key justification for their method. The learning curve analysis in Table 1 is particularly effective, demonstrating a clear advantage for the spatial regularization in lower data regimes.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem of data-driven ROI definition and situates the work within the existing literature. The model (Equation 2) is broken down into its constituent parts, making it easy to understand the role of each term. The algorithmic details are presented logically, building upon the original SODL algorithm.

The figures and tables are informative and support the main claims of the paper. Figure 2 provides a concise and powerful summary of the main results, comparing all methods across multiple criteria. The qualitative comparison in Fig. 2(a) is particularly illustrative of the benefits of the proposed smoothness penalty. A minor suggestion for improvement would be to provide slightly more intuition behind the BCD dictionary update in Equation 7, as simply referring to a previous paper might be a hurdle for non-expert readers. However, given the space constraints of a conference paper, this is a minor point.

#### **Originality**

The originality of the paper lies in the novel and effective combination of existing techniques to solve a significant applied problem. The core components—online dictionary learning, l1-sparsity, and Laplacian regularization—are not new in isolation. However, their integration into a single, scalable, online framework for discovering structured brain maps is a novel contribution. Previous works on structured sparse decompositions for neuroimaging have typically relied on slower, batch-optimization methods. This work successfully bridges the gap by adapting a fast online algorithm to incorporate spatial priors, at only a minor computational cost (a 2-3x increase in runtime). This is a significant practical achievement. The paper does an excellent job of citing and differentiating its contributions from related work, such as the batch TV-l1 methods and the unstructured online methods.

#### **Significance**

The significance of this work is high. The need for robust, scalable, and principled methods for analyzing large-scale neuroimaging data is ever-increasing. This paper provides a practical and powerful tool for a fundamental task: identifying functionally relevant brain networks that exhibit inter-subject variability. The method produces maps that are not only statistically sound but also more interpretable and stable than those from competing state-of-the-art methods.

The scalability of the online approach is a major advantage that makes it applicable to the massive datasets that are becoming the norm in neuroscience (e.g., UK Biobank, ABCD). Furthermore, the authors' commitment to releasing the implementation in a widely used open-source library (Nilearn) will greatly facilitate its adoption and impact on the neuroimaging community. Researchers are highly likely to use and build upon this work for data exploration, dimensionality reduction, and feature engineering for predictive modeling.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
1.  **High practical relevance:** Addresses a key challenge in computational neuroscience with a practical, scalable solution.
2.  **Strong empirical validation:** The experiments are well-designed, conducted on a large-scale real-world dataset, and use a comprehensive set of relevant metrics.
3.  **Well-motivated technical contribution:** The addition of a Laplacian penalty to an online dictionary learning framework is a simple but highly effective idea for this application.
4.  **Computational efficiency:** The method remains fast and scalable, only incurring a small computational overhead compared to its unstructured counterpart.
5.  **Clarity and presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper acknowledges that hyperparameter tuning (especially for the sparsity `τ` and smoothness `γ` parameters) is a difficult issue, relying on cross-validation. While this is a common problem, the work would be even stronger with more guidance or a more automated approach to setting these parameters.
2.  **Limited discussion of alternative priors:** The Laplacian penalty is well-justified for smoothness, but a brief discussion of its trade-offs compared to other structure-inducing priors, like Total Variation (which promotes piece-wise constant regions with sharp edges), would have been welcome.

### **Overall Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that presents a methodologically sound, practically useful, and thoroughly validated contribution to the field. It combines machine learning techniques in a novel way to address a significant problem in neuroimaging. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
