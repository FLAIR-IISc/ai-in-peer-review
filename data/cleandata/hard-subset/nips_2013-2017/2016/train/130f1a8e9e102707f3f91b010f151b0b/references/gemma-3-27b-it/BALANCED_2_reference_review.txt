This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), for learning brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data. The core idea is to leverage inter-subject variability to define spatially localized and sparse regions, extending existing online dictionary learning techniques with Laplacian regularization to enforce spatial smoothness. The authors motivate the approach by highlighting the limitations of univariate analyses and the challenges of defining Regions of Interest (ROIs) in a principled manner. They present a detailed algorithmic description, discuss practical considerations like hyperparameter tuning, and demonstrate the method’s performance on a large HCP dataset, comparing it to ICA-based methods (CanICA) and standard SODL.

The paper is well-written and tackles an important problem in neuroimaging. The motivation is clear, and the introduction effectively establishes the context and limitations of existing approaches. The proposed Smooth-SODL method represents a logical extension of prior work on online dictionary learning, and the addition of Laplacian regularization seems well-justified for enhancing the interpretability of the learned regions. The theoretical formulation appears sound, and the authors acknowledge the connection to related work like graph-based methods. The experimental results are comprehensive, including comparisons with relevant baselines, analyses of running time, and assessments of dictionary quality, stability, and predictive performance. The inclusion of a discussion on hyperparameter selection and the provided guidelines are valuable for practitioners. The implementation being made available as part of Nilearn further strengthens the paper’s impact.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. Firstly, while the improvement over standard SODL is demonstrated, the gains, especially in terms of predictive power, aren't always dramatic. The reported R2 scores for behavioral prediction, although positive, are not exceptionally high, and the improvement over CanICA is marginal in some cases. A more nuanced discussion of the situations where Smooth-SODL truly excels would be beneficial.  Secondly, the connection to existing work on spatially regularized decomposition methods (e.g., TV-L1 minimization [1, 23, 25] as cited) could be strengthened. The authors position their work as offering a computationally efficient alternative to these methods, but a more detailed comparison of computational complexity and scalability, particularly in the context of large datasets, would be valuable. The claim of retaining algorithmic simplicity is somewhat weakened by the need for multiple iterations of FISTA for the dictionary update.  Thirdly, the sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters, while present, feels somewhat limited. The impact of different regularization strengths (α and γ) on the resulting regions and their interpretability could be explored more thoroughly. The reported optimal ranges in Fig. 1 are useful but a more systematic analysis with varying combinations of α and γ might reveal further insights. Fourthly, the discussion of the limitations of CanICA feels somewhat one-sided. While it’s true that the hard-thresholding step is necessary, a more balanced discussion acknowledging the strengths of ICA, particularly its ability to capture independent components, would be appropriate. Finally, the paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of why the Laplacian penalty specifically leads to "blobs" and how this corresponds to the expected structure of brain regions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of online dictionary learning with spatially regularizing Laplacian penalty.
*   Well-motivated and tackles an important problem in neuroimaging.
*   Comprehensive experimental validation on a large, publicly available dataset.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Implementation available to the community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The improvements over standard SODL and CanICA are sometimes marginal.
*   Limited comparison to existing spatially regularized decomposition methods.
*   Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis could be more thorough.
*   Discussion of CanICA limitations feels biased.
*   Lack of detailed explanation regarding the formation of "blobs" with the Laplacian penalty.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field, offering a practical and scalable method for learning brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data. While there are some weaknesses, they are not severe enough to warrant rejection. The strengths of the paper – its novelty, clear presentation, and thorough experimental validation – outweigh the limitations. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly strengthening the comparison to related work and providing a more nuanced discussion of the limitations and benefits of the proposed method. Specifically, a more detailed algorithmic complexity analysis compared to TV-L1 methods would be valuable.
