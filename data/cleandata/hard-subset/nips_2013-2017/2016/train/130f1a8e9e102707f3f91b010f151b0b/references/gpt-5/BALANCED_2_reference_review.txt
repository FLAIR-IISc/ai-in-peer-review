Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method for neuroimaging that enforces both sparsity and spatial structure on dictionary atoms. Each subject’s brain image is modeled as a linear mixture of k atoms; codes are obtained via ridge regression (with optional ℓ1 coding), while atoms are constrained to an ℓ1 ball or simplex (for sparsity and optional nonnegativity) and penalized by a Laplacian quadratic (for spatial smoothness/compactness). The atom update reduces to a constrained GraphNet-type denoising subproblem solved with FISTA; the overall solver follows the online framework of Mairal et al. [16]. Experiments on HCP task fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to standard SODL [16] and CanICA [22], claiming improved interpretability and stability with small runtime overhead (~3× SODL) and competitive explained variance and prediction of behavioral measures.

This work sits at the intersection of online dictionary learning [16,17], structured sparsity (e.g., TV–ℓ1 and GraphNet in neuroimaging [1,8,11]), and group ICA [22,19]. Prior structured decompositions with TV or other spatial priors have typically used slow alternating minimization [1,25]; here the authors argue a Laplacian penalty integrates cleanly into the online updates, preserving scalability.

Strengths
- Problem importance: Learning spatially coherent, interpretable ROIs from population data is central in neuroimaging, with clear benefits for statistical power and downstream prediction.
- Methodological simplicity and scalability: The per-atom update is a convex quadratic with an ℓ1/simplex constraint, enabling an efficient projected gradient/FISTA update with cheap ℓ1-ball projection. The integration into the Mairal online framework is natural.
- Practical relevance: Uses a large, public HCP dataset; compares runtime, stability, explained variance, and prediction performance; discusses hyperparameter effects and provides reasonable guidance (e.g., trade-offs for τ and γ).
- Empirical findings: Smoother, more compact atoms than SODL; improved stability across splits; competitive explained variance relative to PCA/ICA while preserving interpretability; reasonable behavioral prediction compared to raw features and CanICA/tCanICA.
- Potential for impact: If integrated into Nilearn, the method would be widely accessible to the community.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Quality/technical correctness
- Theoretical claims around the Lipschitz constant and γ: The statement that a fixed γ “provably” causes the dictionary updates to “take forever” because Lt = 1 + 4Dγ(At[j,j]/t)−1 would blow up is questionable. Under standard conditions At[j,j]/t converges to a positive constant (E[Uj^2]), so Lt stays bounded for fixed γ. If there are regimes where At[j,j]/t→0 (e.g., due to vanishing codes from a time-varying αt), this should be analyzed and evidenced. The proposed schedule γt = O(||At||∞,∞/t) needs justification and a convergence statement.
- “1 or 2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient to reach an accuracy of 1e−6” seems implausible in general. If the warm start from the previous iterate explains this, quantify typical iteration counts and tolerances actually used.
- The paper claims each per-atom subproblem reaches the “global optimum” (true), but the overall problem remains nonconvex; be careful not to overstate convergence guarantees. Similarly, calling the approach “parameter free” is misleading since FISTA step sizes depend on L; please clarify how L is set in practice.
- Objective formulation with lim n→∞ is nonstandard in empirical risk settings; clarify whether you optimize empirical averages over a finite dataset, or an expectation over an underlying distribution.

Clarity
- Some notation and derivations (Eq. 7–8) are terse; expanding the derivation of the atom update (how a is formed and how γ̃ depends on At[j,j]/t) would aid reproducibility.
- There are typos and grammar issues (“intepretable”, “no step size to tune”, etc.). A careful edit would improve readability.

Experimental rigor and ablations
- Important details are missing: number of passes T, mini-batch size used in all runs, initialization, stopping criteria for the inner FISTA loop, precise CV protocol for γ and τ, and whether positivity constraints were used (simplex vs ℓ1-ball).
- Baselines are limited. It would strengthen the case to include: (i) a TV–ℓ1 structured DL baseline on a subset (e.g., Abraham/Varoquaux [1,24]) to quantify the interpretability/quality gap, (ii) nonnegative matrix factorization (with/without Laplacian), and (iii) SODL with nonnegativity only (to isolate the value of Laplacian vs sparsity/positivity).
- Provide quantitative measures of spatial compactness/smoothness (e.g., Laplacian energy, connected component counts, blob size distributions) in addition to visuals.
- The stability metric is described but exact numbers, variance across runs, and matching protocol details (Hungarian algorithm configuration) should be reported. Statistical tests on EV and prediction R^2 would help.
- The behavioral prediction setup needs more detail: feature construction (codes vs atom projections), model class, cross-validation splits, and whether target leakage is prevented at all stages.

Originality
- The idea of adding a Laplacian penalty on dictionary atoms within an online DL framework is a sensible extension rather than a conceptual breakthrough. Prior structured factorizations explored TV/GraphNet-like penalties, while online DL is well-established; the contribution is an efficient synthesis tailored to neuroimaging. This is valuable but incremental.

Significance
- If the reported stability/interpretability gains hold broadly and code is released in Nilearn, adoption in neuroimaging workflows seems likely. However, the absence of a released implementation and some missing experimental details reduce immediate impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a principled analysis of the step size/Lipschitz constants and the effect of time-varying αt and γt on convergence, or fix α,γ and report robust performance ranges.
- Add ablations disentangling the roles of Laplacian, sparsity, and nonnegativity constraints.
- Include at least a small-scale comparison to a TV–ℓ1 DL solver to position Smooth-SODL’s quality vs speed trade-off.
- Report quantitative spatial structure metrics and statistical tests for EV and prediction.
- Expand experimental details (hyperparameter grids, T, batch size, stopping, seeds) and release code at submission time if possible.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important neuroimaging problem with a scalable, easy-to-use method.
- Empirically improves interpretability and stability with modest runtime overhead.
- Solid integration into established online DL with a practical optimization scheme.
- Large, public dataset evaluation; potential wide impact via Nilearn.

Arguments against acceptance
- Methodological novelty is modest; largely an engineering integration of known pieces.
- Some technical claims are weakly justified or potentially incorrect (γ schedule, FISTA iterations).
- Missing experimental details, limited baselines, and lack of released code hinder reproducibility.
- Clarity/typo issues reduce polish.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper is promising and practically useful, but to meet NeurIPS standards it needs stronger theoretical justification of the optimization choices, broader and more rigorous empirical validation (including structured DL baselines and quantitative spatial metrics), and improved clarity and reproducibility. I encourage a revision addressing these points.
