

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging that incorporates spatial smoothness via a Laplacian penalty while maintaining sparsity. The authors address the problem of defining meaningful brain regions (ROIs) from population-level brain imaging data by leveraging inter-subject variability, rather than relying on anatomical segmentation or predefined regions. The method extends the SODL framework [16] by adding a spatial regularization term, enabling the extraction of spatially compact, interpretable brain regions. The authors demonstrate their approach on HCP task fMRI data (500 subjects, 260k voxels), comparing against SODL and CanICA, and show improvements in interpretability, stability, and performance in capturing inter-subject variability.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound extension of existing dictionary learning methods. The theoretical formulation is well-motivated, with clear justification for the Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness. The algorithmic approach (BCD with FISTA for dictionary updates) is well-justified and the computational complexity analysis is thorough. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including quantitative metrics (explained variance, stability, prediction performance) and qualitative visual comparisons. The authors carefully address hyperparameter selection and provide empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness across different data regimes.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, motivation, and methodological contributions are presented logically. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the experimental setup is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The figures effectively illustrate the key results, particularly the qualitative comparison of dictionary atoms across methods.

**Originality:** The paper makes a meaningful contribution by adapting online dictionary learning to incorporate spatial structure, addressing a gap in the literature where structured dictionary learning has been computationally prohibitive for large-scale neuroimaging. While structured dictionary learning for brain imaging has been explored [1, 25], this is the first to combine it with online learning in a computationally efficient manner, making it applicable to massive datasets. The authors properly contextualize their work against related literature in Section 4.

**Significance:** The problem of defining meaningful ROIs is critical in neuroimaging, and the authors demonstrate that their approach produces more interpretable brain regions that better capture inter-subject variability. The computational efficiency (only 3x slower than SODL) makes this method practically applicable to large-scale datasets, which is a significant advantage over previous structured approaches. The method's potential to become the "de facto way" for ROI extraction in neuroimaging is well-supported by the results.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical depth:** While the algorithmic convergence is established, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees about the properties of the learned dictionaries. It would strengthen the contribution to provide analysis showing how the Laplacian penalty affects the statistical properties of the learned regions, such as their ability to capture true underlying brain organization.

**Comparison with related work:** The authors claim their method is the first to combine online learning with spatial regularization, but the comparison with [25, 1] could be more detailed. It's not entirely clear how their Laplacian-based approach differs from the TV-`1 minimization in [1], and the authors should clarify why their approach is more suitable for online learning.

**Significance of results:** While the paper demonstrates improved interpretability and stability, the significance could be further strengthened by showing more concrete applications. For instance, how do these regions improve downstream analyses beyond just being "more interpretable"? A specific example of how Smooth-SODL could be used in a neuroscience study would better demonstrate the method's impact.

**Hyperparameter selection:** The paper mentions that hyperparameter selection is challenging but doesn't provide enough guidance for practitioners. The authors could include more detailed recommendations for choosing γ and τ based on their extensive experiments.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to neuroimaging analysis by providing an efficient method for extracting spatially structured brain regions from population data. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches. The paper is well-written and the experimental evaluation is thorough.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should strengthen the theoretical discussion, provide more detailed comparison with related work (particularly [1, 25]), and elaborate on the practical significance of their results with concrete examples of how these regions could be used in neuroscience research.

The paper addresses an important problem in neuroimaging with a method that is both theoretically grounded and practically applicable to large-scale datasets. The proposed Smooth-SODL framework represents a significant step forward in the field of brain region extraction and could become a standard tool for neuroimaging researchers.
