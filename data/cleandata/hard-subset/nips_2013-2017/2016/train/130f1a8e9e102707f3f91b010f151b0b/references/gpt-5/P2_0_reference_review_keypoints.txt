- Paper proposes Smooth-SODL: online dictionary learning with sparse, Laplacian-smoothed atoms and ridge-coded codes, adapting Mairal et al.’s online BCD and using FISTA for atom updates.  
- Atom subproblem is convex and well-posed, but claims of global convergence are misleading if FISTA is only run for a few iterations.  
- The statement that “1–2 iterations of FISTA reach 1e−6” is implausible; if inner solves are inexact, convergence claims must cite inexact BCD theory or be empirically validated.  
- Time-varying γt/αt schedules change the objective over time and weaken links to existing convergence proofs; authors should clarify theory vs. heuristic.  
- The “lim n→∞” notation in the objective is nonstandard for finite-sample work and should be explained as expected risk or rephrased.  
- Baseline fairness concerns: SODL uses ridge codes here (not typical l1) which may disadvantage it; CanICA preprocessing differs (pre-smoothing) — justify and add controlled baselines.  
- Recommend adding SODL with l1 codes and a structured baseline (e.g., TV-l1) on a feasible subset for proper comparison.  
- Statistical reporting is weak: stability metrics lack number of splits, error bars, and significance tests; behavioral prediction CV, feature selection, and hyperparameter tuning are under-specified.  
- Empirics are promising: valuable HCP experiment, strong early-learning benefits from spatial regularization, and improved interpretability/stability, but quantitative gaps remain (no error bars, limited ablations).  
- Runtime claims: method is ~3x slower than SODL but faster than CanICA — report runtime/accuracy trade-offs and sensitivity to number of FISTA iterations.  
- Clarity issues: notation errors (e.g., “maxUi” vs “minUi”), inconsistent factors in Laplacian, misnumbered steps, overstated algorithmic guarantees, and typos in figures/references.  
- Originality is incremental: natural extension of Mairal et al. and GraphNet-style regularization, but a meaningful practical contribution in scaling structured decompositions.  
- Significance is moderate: useful for neuroimaging practice with potential adoption (e.g., Nilearn), but methodological novelty is limited for a top-tier venue.  
- Pros: clear motivation, simple scalable modification, convex per-atom updates with standard tools, solid dataset and relevant metrics, improved interpretability and stability.  
- Cons: overstated convergence/theory, inexact inner solves and time-varying hyperparameters, incomplete baselines/protocols, and limited statistical reporting.  
- Suggested fixes: clarify CV protocol and online adaptation of α/γ, report sensitivity to FISTA iterations and runtime/accuracy trade-offs, add SODL-l1 and TV-l1 baselines, provide confidence intervals and Hungarian matching details, and ensure nested CV to prevent leakage.  
- Overall assessment: useful, scalable method with credible empirical benefits but overstated theory and insufficiently rigorous evaluation; borderline paper, leaning weak accept if issues are addressed.
