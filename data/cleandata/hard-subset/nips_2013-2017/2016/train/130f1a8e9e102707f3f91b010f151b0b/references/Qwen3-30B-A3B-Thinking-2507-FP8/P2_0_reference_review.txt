

# Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes an extension of online dictionary learning for neuroimaging analysis called Smooth-SODL, which incorporates a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure on the dictionary atoms. The authors argue that traditional approaches to defining brain regions (ROIs) in neuroimaging either rely on anatomical segmentation (with cross-modality correspondence issues) or statistical clustering (which doesn't account for the signal structure). Instead, they propose to use the inter-subject variability in statistical maps to define regions, modeling brain activity as a linear mixture of spatially structured components.

The method extends the SODL framework (Mairal et al., 2010) by adding a Laplacian regularization term to the dictionary learning objective, which enforces spatial smoothness and compactness on the dictionary atoms. The authors provide a detailed algorithm (Algorithm 1) for the Smooth-SODL method, demonstrating that the computational cost increases by only about 3x compared to the standard SODL. They validate their approach on HCP task fMRI data, comparing against SODL and CanICA (a standard ICA-based method), using metrics including explained variance, stability, reproducibility, and prediction of behavioral variables.

## Evaluation

### Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-constructed. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of their problem (Equation 2), a detailed algorithm (Algorithm 1), and a convergence analysis based on standard optimization theory. The experimental design is appropriate for the problem, using a standard neuroimaging dataset (HCP) with a clear comparison against relevant baselines (SODL and CanICA).

The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and provide a nuanced discussion of hyperparameter selection (Section 3.2), which is particularly important for dictionary learning. They demonstrate that their method achieves a better trade-off between interpretability (sparsity) and data fidelity than the baselines. The quantitative results (Fig. 2, Table 1) provide strong evidence for their claims.

However, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with other structured dictionary learning approaches for neuroimaging. The authors cite [1] (which uses TV-`1 minimization) as related work but don't provide a detailed comparison of their method with this approach. Given the importance of spatial structure in neuroimaging, a more thorough comparison with other spatially structured methods would strengthen the quality assessment.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to experimental validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the algorithms are clearly presented. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce their results, including implementation details about the Nilearn library.

The paper could be improved by more clearly explaining the relationship between the Laplacian penalty and the spatial structure of brain regions. The authors acknowledge that spatial regularization might not be as critical with abundant data (HCP), but they don't thoroughly explore this nuance in the paper. A more detailed explanation of how the Laplacian penalty enforces spatial structure would enhance clarity.

### Originality

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by extending online dictionary learning with spatial regularization for neuroimaging. While the technical approach (adding a Laplacian penalty) is a natural extension of existing work, the application to neuroimaging is novel and addresses a significant gap in the literature.

The authors cite relevant prior work (SODL, CanICA, structured dictionary learning methods), but they could better articulate how their method differs from the TV-`1 approach used in [1] (which they reference but don't deeply compare with). The paper's originality would be strengthened by a more detailed comparison with this related approach.

### Significance

The problem of identifying interpretable brain regions is important in neuroimaging, and the paper addresses this problem in a meaningful way. The results suggest that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable brain regions with better stability (mean correlation between atoms across data splits = 0.4 for Smooth-SODL vs. 0.1 for SODL and 0.1 for CanICA) while maintaining good predictive performance for behavioral variables.

The significance is somewhat limited by the focus on a single dataset (HCP) and a single task (language vs. math). While the authors claim the method works well in "small, medium, and large-scale regimes," the experimental validation is limited to one dataset and one task. Broader validation across multiple datasets and tasks would significantly strengthen the significance claim.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Clear and well-motivated problem statement
- Sound technical approach with appropriate mathematical foundation
- Good experimental validation on a standard neuroimaging dataset
- Practical implementation details and computational efficiency (3x slowdown vs SODL)
- Careful analysis of hyperparameter selection
- Good comparison against relevant baselines (SODL and CanICA)

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited experimental validation (single dataset, single task)
- Insufficient comparison with other structured dictionary learning methods for neuroimaging (e.g., [1])
- Inadequate explanation of why spatial regularization is needed beyond the HCP case
- Overstated significance claim given the limited experimental scope

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging analysis by providing a method that produces interpretable, spatially structured brain regions with minimal computational overhead. The technical approach is sound, the experimental validation is appropriate for the problem, and the paper is well-written.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Add a more detailed comparison with other spatially structured dictionary learning methods (e.g., [1])
2. Provide more thorough explanation of why spatial regularization is beneficial, especially in the context of data abundance
3. Consider adding experiments on additional datasets or tasks to strengthen the significance claim

The paper aligns well with NIPS' focus on machine learning for scientific applications, and the proposed method has clear potential for impact in neuroimaging analysis. The minor weaknesses identified are easily addressable and do not undermine the overall contribution of the work.

## Final Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions (or Accept as is, with the minor revisions suggested above). The paper is a strong contribution to the field of machine learning for neuroimaging, with a well-motivated problem, sound technical approach, and meaningful experimental validation.
