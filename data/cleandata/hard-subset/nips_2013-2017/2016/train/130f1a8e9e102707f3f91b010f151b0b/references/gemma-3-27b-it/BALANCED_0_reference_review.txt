This paper proposes a novel online dictionary learning method, Smooth-SODL, for identifying brain regions from neuroimaging data, specifically leveraging inter-subject variability. The core idea is to learn spatially smooth and sparse dictionary atoms representing underlying brain regions, addressing the limitations of univariate models and the challenges of defining Regions of Interest (ROIs). The method extends existing online dictionary learning techniques by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness alongside sparsity. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (HCP), showing improvements in dictionary interpretability, reproducibility, and predictive power compared to standard SODL and ICA-based methods.

The paper is well-motivated. The problem of principled ROI definition in neuroimaging is a significant one, and the authors clearly articulate the shortcomings of existing approaches. The proposed method logically addresses these shortcomings by directly learning spatially coherent patterns from population-level data. The connection to existing work, particularly online dictionary learning [16], is well established, and the extension with the Laplacian penalty is presented in a clear and mathematically sound manner.  The experimental evaluation using the HCP dataset is a strong point, providing a rigorous benchmark against established methods. The inclusion of both qualitative assessment (visual inspection of learned dictionaries) and quantitative metrics (explained variance, prediction accuracy) strengthens the evaluation. The practical considerations section, detailing hyperparameter tuning and initialization, adds to the paper’s completeness.  The related work section is comprehensive and accurately positions the contribution within the existing literature.  The authors’ willingness to share their implementation through Nilearn is commendable.

However, the paper isn’t without its weaknesses. While the theoretical formulation is solid, the justification for the specific choice of the Laplacian penalty could be strengthened. A more detailed discussion of alternative spatial regularization techniques and why the Laplacian was preferred would be beneficial.  The comparison to CanICA, while valuable, feels slightly imbalanced. The authors rightly point out the need for post-processing (thresholding) in CanICA to achieve interpretability, but they don’t fully explore the implications of this post-processing step on the comparison.  A more thorough analysis of the impact of different thresholding strategies on CanICA's performance would be useful.  The convergence analysis relies heavily on citing [16]. While this is reasonable given the extension, a more self-contained discussion of convergence properties for Smooth-SODL specifically would be preferable. The discussion of the hyperparameter α and its time-varying adaptive choice feels slightly hand-wavy. More justification for this specific choice, perhaps with a brief ablation study comparing it to other strategies, would increase confidence in the results. Finally, while the experiments are comprehensive, the analysis of the prediction results (Fig. 2(c)) could be more nuanced. Simply stating that Smooth-SODL outperforms SODL and tCanICA is insufficient; a statistical significance test would strengthen this claim. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed method offers a novel approach to ROI definition by learning spatially regularized dictionaries from inter-subject variability.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical formulation is well-grounded and builds upon established techniques in dictionary learning and spatial regularization.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on the HCP dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of Smooth-SODL compared to existing methods.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The method addresses a significant challenge in neuroimaging and has the potential to improve the analysis of brain data.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors' commitment to releasing the implementation through Nilearn promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Justification for Laplacian Penalty:** The rationale for choosing the Laplacian penalty could be more thoroughly explained.
*   **Imbalanced Comparison to CanICA:** The comparison to CanICA doesn’t fully account for the impact of post-processing.
*   **Convergence Analysis:** The convergence analysis relies heavily on a cited work rather than a self-contained discussion.
*   **Hyperparameter Justification:** The adaptive choice of α lacks detailed justification.
*   **Statistical Significance:**  The prediction results lack statistical significance testing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed method is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some weaknesses, they are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is **accept-worthy**, but with the caveat that the authors address the points raised above, particularly concerning the justification for the Laplacian penalty, a more balanced comparison to CanICA, and providing statistical support for the prediction results. Addressing these points would elevate the paper from good to excellent.
