This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), for learning brain regions from fMRI data by leveraging inter-subject variability. The core idea is to decompose statistical parametric maps across a population of subjects into a sparse and spatially smooth dictionary, effectively defining regions of interest (ROIs). The method extends existing online dictionary learning techniques by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness, aiming to overcome limitations of both univariate models and traditional ROI definition approaches.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a critical challenge in neuroimaging – the principled definition of ROIs. The authors clearly articulate the problem and position their work within the context of related approaches like clustering, PCA/ICA, and existing dictionary learning methods. The proposed method is a logical extension of prior work (SODL [16]) and attempts to address a known limitation – the lack of spatial structure in learned components. The experiments on the large-scale HCP dataset are a significant strength, and the comparisons to CanICA and SODL are relevant. The thorough investigation of hyperparameter selection and the discussion of the optimality regime are commendable. The code availability (planned via Nilearn) further enhances the paper’s impact.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty for spatial smoothness is a novel contribution.
*   **Relevance:** The problem addressed – automated and principled ROI definition – is a long-standing challenge in neuroimaging with significant practical implications.
*   **Scalability:** The online learning approach makes the method applicable to large datasets, such as the HCP dataset used in the experiments.
*   **Thoroughness:** The paper includes a detailed analysis of the method, including hyperparameter tuning, convergence guarantees, and computational complexity.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to existing approaches, showing improvements in both interpretability and predictive performance.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Theoretical Justification for Laplacian Penalty:** While the Laplacian penalty intuitively promotes spatial smoothness, the justification for its specific form and the choice of the regularization parameter (γ) feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more rigorous theoretical analysis of the impact of the Laplacian penalty on the properties of the learned dictionary would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:**  The method is reported to be approximately 3x slower than the baseline SODL. While the authors acknowledge this, a deeper analysis of the computational bottlenecks and potential optimization strategies could be valuable. It’s not clear if this increase is acceptable for many real-world applications.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art Segmentation Methods:** The comparison is primarily against other dictionary learning and ICA-based methods.  It would be valuable to compare Smooth-SODL to more traditional segmentation algorithms (e.g., those based on graph cuts or level sets) and assess its performance in terms of segmentation accuracy and robustness.
*   **Impact of Sparsity Parameter (τ):** The discussion regarding the sparsity parameter (τ) is insightful, but the optimal selection criteria remain somewhat vague. More guidance on choosing an appropriate value for τ, possibly based on the data characteristics, would be helpful.
*   **Visualization:** While Fig. 2 (a) provides a qualitative comparison, the matching of atoms between models appears to be based on a Hungarian algorithm. Further detail on the matching criterion would be beneficial. Are the matched atoms truly comparable, or is this merely the best possible alignment?

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in neuroimaging with a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   The proposed method demonstrates promising results on a large-scale dataset, outperforming existing techniques in terms of interpretability and predictive performance.
*   The paper is thorough, well-written, and includes a detailed analysis of the method and its performance.
*   The planned code release will facilitate the adoption and further development of the method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical justification for the Laplacian penalty is somewhat limited.
*   The increased computational cost compared to SODL might be a barrier to adoption in some applications.
*   The comparison to traditional segmentation methods is lacking.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed Smooth-SODL method offers a promising approach to automated ROI definition, leveraging the power of online dictionary learning with a novel spatial regularization technique. While some minor weaknesses remain, the strengths of the paper, particularly its novelty, empirical validation, and thoroughness, outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

*   Provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for the choice of the Laplacian penalty and the regularization parameter γ.
*   Discuss potential optimizations to reduce the computational cost of the method.
*   Include a comparison to traditional segmentation algorithms.
*   Provide more concrete guidance on choosing the sparsity parameter τ.
*   Clarify the atom matching criterion in Fig. 2 (a).
