

# Peer Review: "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning that incorporates Laplacian regularization to learn spatially structured, sparse brain regions from neuroimaging data. The authors address a significant limitation in current neuroimaging analysis: the lack of principled methods for defining regions of interest (ROIs) that leverage inter-subject variability. While prior work has used clustering or ICA for ROI definition, these approaches either fail to capture spatial structure or are computationally intractable for large datasets. The authors extend the online dictionary learning framework of [16] with a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness in the learned dictionary atoms, enabling scalable computation while maintaining interpretability.

**Strengths:**

The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of spatially structured ROIs in neuroimaging, well-motivated by the limitations of standard approaches. The technical contribution is significant: the integration of Laplacian regularization into online dictionary learning is novel and well-executed. The authors provide a clear algorithmic adaptation of the online framework to handle the structured penalty, with appropriate convergence guarantees.

The experimental evaluation on HCP fMRI data is comprehensive and convincing. The comparison against SODL and CanICA demonstrates clear advantages in interpretability (Fig. 2a), reproducibility (Table 1), and performance on prediction tasks (Fig. 2c). The learning curve analysis (Fig. 2b) is particularly compelling, showing that Smooth-SODL provides a 14-fold gain over SODL when using only 17 subjects, with the gap diminishing as more data is added.

The computational analysis is strong, showing that Smooth-SODL is only 3x slower than vanilla SODL (340s vs. 110s) while offering significant improvements in interpretability and reproducibility. This makes the method practical for large-scale neuroimaging datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

The most significant limitation is the lack of comparison with recent related work. The paper mentions [23] ("online group-level functional brain mapping") as related work but fails to include it in the comparison. Given that [23] also proposes an online method for functional brain mapping, this omission is substantial and weakens the novelty claim. The authors should directly compare against this method to establish their contribution more clearly.

The paper discusses hyperparameter tuning but lacks a thorough analysis of how sensitive the results are to the choice of α, γ, and τ. A more detailed exploration of this would strengthen the paper. For instance, the authors should show how performance varies across the parameter space rather than just presenting the "safe range" they found.

The paper claims that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable results but primarily supports this with visual inspection. While the visual comparison in Fig. 2a is convincing, the authors should provide more quantitative metrics for interpretability (e.g., how well the learned regions align with known anatomical structures).

The theoretical justification for the Laplacian penalty could be strengthened. While the paper provides convergence guarantees, it lacks analysis of how the spatial regularization affects the statistical properties of the learned representations or the bias-variance trade-off.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of neuroimaging analysis. The problem is significant, the method is well-executed, and the experiments are thorough and convincing. The paper addresses a real need in the field and provides a practical tool for the neuroimaging community.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Including a direct comparison with [23] (the online group-level functional brain mapping method)
2. Providing a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity
3. Adding quantitative metrics for interpretability

With these revisions, the paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. As it stands, it's a high-quality contribution that addresses a significant problem in a well-executed way. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary of pros and cons:**

*Pros:*
- Addresses a significant problem in neuroimaging with practical value
- Well-executed technical extension of online dictionary learning
- Comprehensive experiments with clear advantages over baselines
- Strong scalability analysis (only 3x slower than SODL)
- Clear writing and organization

*Cons:*
- Limited comparison with recent related work (specifically [23])
- Insufficient analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity
- Lack of quantitative interpretability metrics
- Limited theoretical justification for spatial regularization

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The method has clear potential to become the de facto approach for ROI extraction in neuroimaging, and the authors have provided a strong foundation for this.
