## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a novel method, Smooth-SODL, for learning spatially coherent brain regions from fMRI data by extending online dictionary learning with Laplacian regularization. The core idea – leveraging inter-subject variability to define meaningful regions – builds upon existing clustering and dictionary learning approaches in neuroimaging (e.g., [21, 14, 5]), but introduces a principled way to enforce spatial smoothness alongside sparsity. The method is presented as a solution to the limitations of both univariate models and traditional ROI-based analysis, offering a scalable and data-driven approach to identifying brain regions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears mathematically well-formulated, building upon existing online dictionary learning techniques [16] with a clearly defined Laplacian penalty. The derivation of the BCD update rules and discussion of convergence are reasonably thorough. The analysis regarding the impact of hyperparameter selection, particularly the relationship between γ and the dictionary update speed (Equation 9), demonstrates a good understanding of the algorithm’s behavior.
*   **Scalability:** The online learning approach is crucial for handling the large-scale nature of fMRI datasets (high voxel count and subject number), as explicitly stated and justified. This addresses a key limitation of batch-processing methods.
*   **Novelty:** While dictionary learning and spatial regularization are individually known techniques, their combination within an *online* framework specifically tailored for large-scale neuroimaging data appears novel. The ability to perform structured decomposition with minimal computational overhead, as claimed, is a significant contribution.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset provide a reasonably comprehensive evaluation, comparing Smooth-SODL against established methods like CanICA and SODL. The reported results demonstrate improvements in dictionary interpretability, stability, and predictive performance on behavioral variables. The learning curve analysis (Table 1) effectively highlights the benefits of spatial regularization, especially with limited data.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** While the mathematical formulation is clear, the prose can be dense and assumes a high level of prior knowledge. Some explanations are abrupt (e.g., the transition from constraint set C to its specific forms in Equation 3). Providing more intuitive explanations for key concepts, such as the Laplacian penalty, would improve accessibility.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The discussion of hyperparameter tuning is comprehensive but could benefit from more practical guidance. While the authors identify a “safe range” for (γ, τ), a more systematic exploration of these parameters and their impact on the results (perhaps using sensitivity analysis) would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While CanICA is included as a baseline, it's presented in a somewhat limited manner (hard-thresholded version, tCanICA). A more thorough comparison with contemporary, high-performing methods for spatial pattern discovery in fMRI (e.g., multi-layer perceptrons, convolutional neural networks) would be valuable. The claim that Smooth-SODL outperforms CanICA on behavioral prediction needs to be carefully considered in the context of the preprocessing and parameter tuning applied to each method.
*    **Limited Biological Interpretation:** The paper focuses primarily on technical aspects and performance metrics. While dictionary interpretability is mentioned, a deeper exploration of the *biological* significance of the learned regions, possibly linking them to known brain networks or cognitive functions, would significantly enhance the impact of the work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for learning brain regions from large-scale fMRI data.
*   The online learning approach addresses a critical scalability issue in neuroimaging.
*   The empirical results demonstrate improvements over existing methods in terms of dictionary quality, stability, and predictive performance.
*   The detailed analysis of the algorithm's behavior and hyperparameter sensitivity provides valuable insights for practitioners.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The clarity of the writing could be improved to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience.
*   The comparison to state-of-the-art methods is not fully comprehensive.
*   The paper lacks a deeper biological interpretation of the learned brain regions.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper represents a significant contribution to the field of neuroimaging data analysis. While some improvements in clarity and more comprehensive benchmarking would be beneficial, the novelty, technical soundness, and empirical validation of the proposed method are compelling. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the clarity and comparison points. The authors should also consider expanding the discussion of the biological relevance of the identified brain regions.
