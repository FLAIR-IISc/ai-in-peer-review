Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL) for neuroimaging. Building on online sparse dictionary learning (Mairal et al., JMLR 2009), the authors add spatial structure by penalizing the 3D Laplacian of dictionary atoms, while enforcing sparsity via an ℓ1-ball or simplex constraint on the atoms. Codes are obtained by ridge regression (optionally ℓ1). The overall problem is solved with online block coordinate descent using sufficient statistics, with per-atom updates reduced to constrained GraphNet-like denoising subproblems solved by FISTA. The method aims to yield spatially localized, blob-like components that better reflect neuroanatomy than unstructured dictionary learning, while retaining scalability.

The paper situates itself against: (i) unstructured online dictionary learning (Mairal 2009; Mensch 2016), (ii) PCA/ICA-based group models (e.g., CanICA), and (iii) structured sparse decompositions (TV–ℓ1, GraphNet) that have been effective but computationally heavy in neuroimaging (e.g., Abraham MICCAI; Varoquaux IPMI; Grosenick NeuroImage). The contribution is an online, scalable approach that integrates a simple Laplacian prior on atoms.

Experiments on HCP task fMRI Z-maps (n=500, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA on explained variance, qualitative interpretability, stability/reproducibility across splits, runtime, and prediction of behavioral variables.

Strengths
- Clear and practically motivated objective: sparse, spatially smooth atoms are desirable for ROI-like factors in brain maps. The combination of Laplacian smoothness on atoms and an ℓ1 or simplex constraint is simple and sensible.
- Scalable algorithmic design: reuses online sufficient statistics; the per-atom subproblem is convex and efficiently solvable with first-order methods. Empirical runtime is only ~3× SODL and faster than CanICA, on realistic whole-brain data.
- Useful practical guidance: discussion of the impact of γ and τ, an αt ∝ t−1/2 schedule, and a condition on γ to avoid vanishing step sizes. These are valuable to practitioners.
- Empirical evidence: on HCP, Smooth-SODL yields more structured, interpretable components than SODL; improved early-sample explained variance; better stability across splits than SODL and thresholded CanICA; competitive explained variance and behavior-prediction performance relative to baselines.
- Code release (via Nilearn) is promised, which would enhance impact.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Incremental novelty: The main novelty is the addition of a Laplacian penalty on atoms within the online BCD framework. While useful, this is a relatively small extension of established methods (online DL, GraphNet/TV regularization on spatial coefficients). The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical or empirical differentiators.
- Algorithmic claims need tightening:
  - The statement that “1 or 2 iterations of FISTA are sufficient to reach an accuracy of 1e−6” is not credible given known convergence rates; better to argue that very few iterations suffice empirically for good outer-loop progress, and quantify this.
  - “Guaranteed to converge to a global optimum, at each iteration” is only true for the convex per-atom subproblem; globally the method converges to a stationary point. Please clarify wording.
  - Convergence under time-varying αt and γt is asserted but not justified. If schedules are used, conditions ensuring convergence (e.g., boundedness, diminishing changes) should be stated or cited.
  - The “lim n→∞” in the objective is unusual for the finite-sample experiments; either remove or clearly connect to the online expectation setting in Mairal (2009).
- Experimental design and baselines:
  - For behavioral prediction, the RAW baseline uses a correlation filter and seems under-regularized; a fairer, strong baseline is ridge/elastic net on the full voxel set or PCA features, with proper CV. As shown, CanICA outperforms all on test R2, which weakens the claim that the learned representations are better for prediction.
  - Structured baselines are missing. Please compare to TV–ℓ1 or GraphNet-based decompositions solved with modern solvers (e.g., FAASTA, proximal splitting), even if at smaller scale, to support the “minor cost” claim and interpretability gains.
  - Only one task contrast is used. Results on additional tasks or on resting-state validate generality.
  - The positivity/simplex constraint on atoms is not discussed. Z-maps have positive and negative effects; nonnegativity may discard relevant patterns. Provide ablations with ℓ1-ball (signed) vs simplex (nonnegative).
  - Fairness of pre-smoothing: CanICA is smoothed but SODL/Smooth-SODL are not. Either smooth all inputs or justify why this favors the comparator.
  - Stability/reproducibility metrics (how atoms are matched, spatial vs correlation measures) need more detail; currently only a mean correlation is reported.
- Clarity/typos: several typos and grammar issues; some derivations are sketched with pointers to supplement. The paper would benefit from tightening notation (e.g., At[j,j]/t roles), and being explicit about the projection operator used in the constrained FISTA step.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The method is technically sound and the per-block optimization is convex; however, some claims (FISTA accuracy, convergence under schedules) are overstated or insufficiently justified. Experimental comparisons are informative but could be stronger with better baselines and broader datasets.
- Clarity: Generally understandable, but organization and precision can be improved; several confusing statements should be corrected.
- Originality: Moderate; a pragmatic extension of online DL with a standard spatial prior. Not a fundamentally new model or theory.
- Significance: Moderate within neuroimaging practice; the approach is likely useful for ROI learning at scale. Broader ML impact is limited without stronger theory or generality beyond the specific Laplacian-on-atom design.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method that many neuroimaging practitioners could use.
- Demonstrated gains in interpretability and stability with small runtime overhead.
- Clear, implementable regularization that integrates smoothly into online DL.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental contribution relative to existing online DL and structured sparse modeling.
- Some methodological claims need correction/justification.
- Experimental baselines and breadth are insufficient given NeurIPS standards; behavior prediction results do not show clear advantage.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). With stronger baselines, clearer theory (or better-justified heuristics), ablations (positivity vs signed atoms, ℓ1 codes), broader datasets, and tightened exposition, this could be a solid submission in the future.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
