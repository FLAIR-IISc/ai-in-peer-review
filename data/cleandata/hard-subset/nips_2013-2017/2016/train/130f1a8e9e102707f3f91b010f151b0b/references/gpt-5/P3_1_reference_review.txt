Summary
This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning framework for neuroimaging that enforces sparsity and spatial smoothness on dictionary atoms to learn interpretable, blob-like brain regions. The method augments Mairal’s online dictionary learning (SODL) with a Laplacian penalty on atoms and keeps the codes dense via a ridge penalty. The dictionary update per atom reduces to a convex quadratic with a Laplacian term under an l1 or simplex constraint, solved efficiently with a few FISTA iterations within a block-coordinate descent loop. Experiments on HCP task fMRI Z-maps (n=500, p≈2.6×10^5, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to standard SODL and CanICA on explained variance, atom stability, qualitative interpretability, and behavioral prediction. Smooth-SODL yields more spatially coherent components than SODL at a modest extra cost (≈3×), better stability, competitive explained variance (especially in low-data regimes), and better behavioral prediction than SODL and thresholded CanICA.

Relation to prior work
The approach is related to structured sparse decomposition methods (GraphNet, structured sparse PCA, TV-regularized dictionary learning) and multi-subject ICA (CanICA). Prior spatially structured decompositions in neuroimaging (e.g., TV-l1 dictionary learning for functional atlases) have been computationally heavy and batch; this work adapts an online scheme with a Laplacian smoothness prior, akin to GraphNet on the dictionary atoms. The design choice to make atoms sparse and smooth (rather than making codes sparse) is well-motivated for defining ROIs.

Strengths
- Clear practical motivation: learning reproducible, spatially compact ROIs from population variability without pre-smoothing.
- Methodological contribution: integrates Laplacian regularization into online dictionary learning with a simple per-atom convex subproblem and practical updates. Good discussion of complexity and Lipschitz constants.
- Scalability: works on n=500, p=260k with runtime only ≈3× SODL, faster than CanICA in the reported setup.
- Empirical evidence: improved spatial structure/interpretability, higher atom stability across splits, and superior low-data explained variance vs. SODL. Behavioral prediction competitive, surpassing SODL and thresholded CanICA.
- Sensible ablations/hyperparameter discussion (αt ~ t^(-1/2), impact of γ and τ), and atom matching with Hungarian algorithm for stability.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
Quality/technical soundness
- Inexact updates and convergence: The algorithm uses 1–2 FISTA iterations per atom and claims 1e-6 accuracy. This seems unrealistic unless warm-started and with very loose tolerances. Convergence of the overall scheme with inexact inner solves should be justified (e.g., via inexact BCD conditions guaranteeing sufficient decrease).
- γ scheduling inconsistency: The paper states γ must scale as O(‖At‖∞,∞/t) to avoid vanishing step sizes when At[j,j]/t is small, yet experiments use fixed γ (10^3–10^4). Please clarify whether code uses a fixed γ, a schedule γt, or atom-specific γj,t, and how numerical stability is ensured when some atoms are rarely used (At[j,j]/t ≈ 0).
- Modeling choice (sparse atoms, dense codes): This is a non-standard reversal compared to classical dictionary learning; while well-motivated for ROI discovery, the paper should include an ablation comparing (i) sparse codes + Laplacian atoms vs (ii) dense codes + Laplacian atoms (current) vs (iii) both sparse, to quantify the trade-offs in reconstruction, interpretability, and stability.
- Baselines: No direct comparison to TV-regularized dictionary learning (e.g., Abraham et al., MICCAI; Varoquaux et al., IPMI/PRNI) or structured sparse PCA on graphs (Jenatton et al.). Even on a smaller subset, such a comparison would strengthen claims of efficiency and quality versus spatially structured alternatives.
- Fairness of preprocessing: CanICA is run on pre-smoothed data, but SODL and Smooth-SODL are not. Either report SODL/Smooth-SODL with the same pre-smoothing, or report CanICA without smoothing, to isolate the effect of the modeling choice rather than preprocessing.

Clarity/reproducibility
- Missing details hinder reproducibility: number of passes/epochs T, stopping criteria for FISTA/BCD, CPU model, memory footprint, and the exact CV protocol for (γ, τ, k) selection. The learning-curve setup (how many passes per “amount of data seen”) and variance across resamples should be specified.
- The description of the Laplacian Lipschitz constant (1+4Dγ̃) is fine for a regular grid with typical boundary conditions, but stating discretization/boundary conditions would improve clarity.
- Notation and typos: some inconsistencies (max vs min over codes, V j vs Vj). The claim “converge to a global optimum at each iteration” is true only for the convex per-atom subproblem, not the overall nonconvex objective—phrase carefully.

Originality
- Combining online dictionary learning with a Laplacian prior on atoms and l1/simplex constraints is a reasonable, incremental idea rather than a fundamentally novel model. The novelty lies mainly in making spatially structured dictionary learning online and fast, with a careful engineering of the per-atom update. This is still valuable for practice in neuroimaging.

Significance
- The method addresses a real gap: scalable, structured decompositions for large neuroimaging cohorts. The improved stability and interpretability are useful for ROI definition and downstream analysis. On prediction, Smooth-SODL does not outperform CanICA, but it narrows the gap and improves over SODL and thresholded CanICA while producing more interpretable maps.
- Potentially impactful for practitioners, especially if integrated into Nilearn.

Questions for the authors
- Do you use nonnegativity on atoms (simplex) in all experiments? Have you evaluated allowing negative atoms (pure l1-ball) and its effect on interpretability and EV?
- How many FISTA iterations per atom do you actually run on average, and what stopping criterion is used? Any warm-start strategy?
- How many epochs T over the dataset? What is the final explained variance and stability as a function of T?
- Could you include a small-scale comparison to a TV-regularized dictionary learning baseline to substantiate the “minor extra cost” claim?

Pros for acceptance
- Well-motivated problem; practical, scalable method with demonstrable benefits in interpretability and stability.
- Careful algorithmic adaptation with attention to computational issues.
- Solid empirical evaluation on a large, relevant dataset.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical justification for inexact inner solves and γ scheduling; some inconsistencies.
- Missing key structured baselines; preprocessing mismatch with CanICA.
- Reproducibility details (hyperparameter selection, number of epochs, stopping) need tightening.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper makes a useful, practically significant step by bringing spatially structured dictionary learning to the online/large-scale regime with good empirical gains in stability and interpretability at modest cost. Addressing the noted methodological clarifications and adding at least a small-scale comparison to TV/structured baselines would strengthen the contribution.
