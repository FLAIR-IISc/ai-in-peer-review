### **Summary**

This paper proposes a new method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), for identifying brain regions from large-scale neuroimaging data. The goal is to decompose a collection of subject-level brain maps (e.g., fMRI statistical maps) into a set of shared, spatially coherent basis functions (dictionary atoms) and subject-specific loading coefficients. This problem is framed as a dictionary learning task where the atoms are constrained to be both sparse (via an `l1`-ball constraint) and spatially smooth (via a Laplacian penalty). The key contribution is the development of a scalable online algorithm, based on the framework of Mairal et al. (2010), that can efficiently incorporate the Laplacian smoothness prior. This extends previous online dictionary learning methods, which typically only enforce sparsity, and offers a significant computational speed-up over batch-based methods for structured decomposition. The authors validate their method on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (n=500), comparing it against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and Canonical Independent Component Analysis (CanICA). The results demonstrate that Smooth-SODL produces more interpretable and stable brain networks, captures inter-subject variability effectively (especially in low-data regimes), and achieves competitive performance in predicting behavioral scores, all with a modest increase in computational cost.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and Significant Problem:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of defining meaningful regions of interest (ROIs) or functional networks from population-level neuroimaging data. The introduction clearly lays out the limitations of existing methods, such as the lack of spatial structure in standard sparse models and the computational burden of batch-based structured models, providing strong motivation for the proposed approach.

2.  **Sound and Scalable Method:** The core technical contribution—integrating a Laplacian penalty into an online dictionary learning framework—is well-executed. The formulation is principled, and the resulting block-coordinate descent algorithm is a natural and effective extension of prior work. Crucially, the authors show that the dictionary update step, while more complex than in standard SODL, remains computationally tractable using established solvers like FISTA. The online nature of the algorithm makes it highly suitable for modern, large-scale neuroimaging datasets.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is a major strength of this work. The authors compare their method against strong and relevant baselines (SODL and CanICA) on a large, real-world dataset. The evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple criteria:
    *   **Qualitative:** Visual inspection (Fig. 2a) clearly shows the benefit of the smoothness prior, yielding cleaner and more blob-like components than SODL.
    *   **Quantitative:** The paper analyzes explained variance, stability, and predictive power. The results present a nuanced and honest picture of the trade-offs involved (e.g., CanICA has higher explained variance but poor interpretability and stability).
    *   **Ablation/Analysis:** The learning curve analysis (Table 1) provides compelling evidence for the value of the spatial prior, showing that its benefits are most pronounced when data is limited, which is a very common scenario in clinical studies.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The model is presented formally, the algorithm is described in sufficient detail, and the experimental setup and results are easy to understand. The figures and tables effectively communicate the main findings.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** While the engineering and application of the method are novel and valuable, the core components—online dictionary learning and Laplacian regularization—are well-established techniques. The primary contribution lies in their effective combination into a scalable framework for neuroimaging. This makes the work more of a strong methodological advancement than a fundamentally new theoretical contribution.

2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model introduces several key hyperparameters (α, γ, τ), and performance is sensitive to their selection, as shown in the study in Figure 1. While the paper provides some practical guidance and suggests cross-validation, the need to tune these parameters, particularly the smoothness parameter γ, could be a practical hurdle for users and may require computationally expensive grid searches.

3.  **Predictive Performance Trade-off:** In the behavioral prediction task, the dense CanICA model achieves the highest performance on the test set. While Smooth-SODL outperforms other interpretable models (SODL, tCanICA), this result highlights a potential trade-off between model interpretability/structure and raw predictive power. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of why this might be the case and the implications for using such decompositions in predictive modeling versus descriptive analysis.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Contextualizing the Choice of Regularizer:** The Laplacian penalty is a good choice for enforcing smoothness, but it is not the only option. Total Variation (TV) regularization is another popular choice for promoting piecewise-constant regions, which might be neurobiologically plausible. A brief discussion comparing the implicit assumptions of a Laplacian (L2-norm on the gradient) versus a TV (L1-norm on the gradient) penalty would strengthen the paper by justifying the design choice more thoroughly.

*   **Clarification on the "RAW" Baseline:** The baseline for the prediction experiment ("RAW") uses the top 2000 voxels most correlated with the target variable. It is crucial to clarify whether this feature selection was performed strictly within the training fold of the cross-validation loop. If the selection was based on the entire dataset (including test subjects), the reported performance would be optimistically biased due to information leakage. Please clarify this point.

*   **FFT-based Update:** The paper mentions in passing that the dictionary update can be performed in the Fourier domain via FFT. This is an interesting and potentially very efficient alternative. If space permits, adding a sentence or two in the main text to explain the conditions under which this is applicable (e.g., assuming periodic boundary conditions) and its potential speed benefits would be valuable for the reader.

### **Decision**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a well-motivated, technically sound, and thoroughly evaluated method for a significant problem in computational neuroscience. The work successfully combines existing machine learning tools to create a scalable and effective algorithm that advances the state-of-the-art in data-driven functional brain parcellation. The strengths, particularly the strong empirical results and the scalability of the approach, clearly outweigh the minor weaknesses related to incremental novelty and hyperparameter tuning. The paper is a solid contribution to the field and would be of interest to both machine learning and neuroimaging researchers at NeurIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept**
