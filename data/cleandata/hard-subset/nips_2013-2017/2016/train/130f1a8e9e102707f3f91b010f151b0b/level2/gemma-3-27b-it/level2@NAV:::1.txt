## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a novel multivariate online dictionary learning method, Smooth-SODL, for decomposing brain images into structured and sparse components. The key contribution lies in extending the existing online dictionary learning framework (Mairal et al., 2010) by incorporating a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to encourage spatially smooth, “blob-like” dictionary atoms, representing interpretable brain regions. The authors motivate this work by highlighting the limitations of existing methods in handling inter-subject variability and the need for principled, data-driven region-of-interest (ROI) definition in neuroimaging.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly articulates the problem. The proposed method appears to be a logical extension of existing online dictionary learning techniques, addressing a significant challenge in neuroimaging. The addition of the Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure is a sensible approach. The authors demonstrate the scalability of their algorithm, a crucial aspect for large-scale brain imaging datasets. Empirical results on the Human Connectome Project data show that Smooth-SODL extracts more interpretable dictionaries and captures inter-subject variability better than state-of-the-art methods like ICA and the original SODL.  Specifically, the performance gains in predicting behavioral variables are noteworthy.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge a 3x increase in runtime compared to SODL, a more detailed computational analysis would be beneficial. Furthermore, the hyperparameter selection process is briefly described, and a more in-depth analysis of sensitivity to these parameters would strengthen the results. Although comparisons are made against CanICA, the limitations of CanICA (lack of sparsity) are readily known, and a comparison against more recent, spatially-aware dictionary learning methods would provide a more compelling benchmark.  The discussion of related work could also be more extensive; for instance, the connection to total variation regularization and its application in neuroimaging warrants further elaboration.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of online dictionary learning with a principled approach to incorporating spatial structure.
*   Scalability to large-scale neuroimaging datasets.
*   Demonstrated improvement in dictionary interpretability and capture of inter-subject variability.
*   Promising results in predicting behavioral variables.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of computational complexity and runtime analysis.
*   Superficial analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   Comparisons to limited, potentially outdated, baseline methods.
*   Could benefit from more in-depth related work discussion.

**Overall:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed Smooth-SODL method offers a promising approach for extracting meaningful brain regions from large-scale data.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above, particularly a more detailed runtime analysis and a broader comparison to relevant state-of-the-art methods. The potential impact of this work on ROI definition and understanding brain organization is significant.
