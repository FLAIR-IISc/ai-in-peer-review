Summary
The paper presents Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), an extension of online dictionary learning [16] for large-scale neuroimaging. The goal is to learn spatially localized, sparse, and piecewise-smooth “atoms” (dictionary components) that define regions of interest (ROIs) across subjects. Sparsity is enforced by constraining atoms in an ℓ1 ball or simplex, while spatial structure is encouraged via a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty on each atom. Codes are estimated via ridge regression; dictionary updates are performed with block coordinate descent, solving each atom subproblem with a proximal-gradient method (FISTA). The approach retains the scalability of [16], adding only a 2–3× runtime factor. Experiments on HCP task fMRI Z-maps (n=500, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to standard SODL [16] and CanICA [22] using explained variance, stability across splits, runtime, qualitative interpretability, and prediction of behavioral scores. Smooth-SODL yields cleaner, more compact atoms and higher stability than SODL and CanICA, competitive explained variance (especially in small-data regimes), and prediction performance below CanICA but above SODL and thresholded CanICA.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on online sparse coding/dictionary learning [16,17] and leverages ideas from GraphNet-style smoothness penalties [11] and structured sparsity/SPCA [14]. It contrasts with spatially-structured decompositions using TV-ℓ1 penalties [1,25], which are typically solved by slow alternating minimization; Smooth-SODL aims to preserve online scalability while adding spatial structure. The neuroimaging baselines (CanICA [22] and SODL [16]) are appropriate; discussion of MELODIC [19] and earlier multi-subject dictionary learning [24] provides context.

Strengths
- Problem relevance and scope: addresses scalable, interpretable ROI learning from large neuroimaging cohorts—highly relevant to both ML and neuroimaging communities.
- Algorithmic contribution: integrates a Laplacian prior on atoms into an online BCD framework with efficient per-atom updates. The subproblem is strongly convex and amenable to fast proximal methods; implementation details (At, Bt updates; mini-batching; projections onto ℓ1/simplex) are standard and sound.
- Empirical findings: clearer, more compact atoms than SODL; improved stability (mean atom-to-atom correlation across splits); strong performance in the small-data regime where structure acts as an effective prior; acceptable runtime overhead relative to SODL and faster than CanICA.
- Practical guidance: discussion of hyperparameter effects (γ, τ, α), adaptive αt ∝ t−1/2, and the impact of over/under-regularization is useful.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Experimental scope: results focus on a single HCP contrast (Math vs Story). Additional tasks or resting-state would better support generality. Competing structured baselines (e.g., TV-ℓ1 dictionary learning [1,25]) are not included, even on a subset, which weakens comparative claims about structure vs speed.
- Quantifying interpretability: visual assessments are persuasive, but interpretability is not quantified beyond stability. Compactness/contiguity (e.g., cluster count, spatial dispersion, Gini index) or overlap with known atlases would strengthen claims about “blobs” and ROI delineation.
- Details on stability metric: the matching procedure (Hungarian algorithm) is mentioned, but specifics (e.g., number of splits, thresholds, handling sign/scale) and statistical variability are not reported.
- Methodological details: 
  - The statement that 1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e−6 accuracy is surprising for Laplacian-regularized problems; reporting iteration counts and convergence curves would be more convincing.
  - The guidance on γ (Eq. 9) and its interaction with At should be made more concrete; the current bound is asymptotic and leaves practical selection to cross-validation.
  - Boundary conditions and implementation of the discrete Laplacian (and whether FFT acceleration was used) are deferred to the supplement; a brief in-paper summary would help reproducibility.
  - The choice of ridge codes vs ℓ1 codes is motivated by stability, but an ablation would clarify trade-offs in EV, stability, and speed.
- Evaluation of k: k=40 is fixed; sensitivity analysis over k would be informative for practitioners.
- Clarity and presentation: several typos and minor inconsistencies (e.g., “xperiments”, “intepretable”). Equation (7) derivation is deferred; a concise derivation sketch would aid readability.

Quality
The optimization scheme is technically sound: block separability and strong convexity of the per-atom subproblem justify proximal-gradient updates with convergence to the global minimizer of each subproblem; overall nonconvexity implies convergence to a stationary point, as in [16]. Runtime/memory claims are plausible. Empirical evaluation is careful within the chosen setup, but broader baselines and datasets would strengthen the case.

Clarity
Overall organization is good. The algorithmic flow (codes via ridge; At/Bt updates; per-atom proximal updates under ℓ1/simplex) is clear. Some derivations and practical details are deferred or stated informally; tightening these would improve clarity. Fixing typos and standardizing notation would help.

Originality
The contribution is an incremental but non-trivial extension of [16], enabling spatially structured atoms in an online framework with modest computational overhead. While Laplacian/GraphNet-style regularization is known, bringing it into an online dictionary-learning solver for neuroimaging at this scale appears novel relative to [1,25], which use slower alternations.

Significance
The approach provides a scalable tool to learn interpretable ROIs from large cohorts, likely of interest to both ML and neuroimaging practitioners. If released in Nilearn, adoption potential is high. The current empirical scope limits claims of broad superiority, but the demonstrated stability/interpretability-speed trade-off is valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential (feasible for camera-ready):
- Add quantitative interpretability metrics (e.g., spatial compactness/contiguity) and report stability with error bars across multiple splits.
- Include convergence/iteration plots for the per-atom update and the overall objective; report typical FISTA iterations and step sizes.
- Clarify implementation details: Laplacian boundary conditions, whether FFT acceleration was used, and the exact constraint set used (ℓ1 ball vs simplex) and its effect (nonnegativity).
- Expand γ/τ selection guidance: provide practical default ranges and a brief ablation; report the selected values for the main experiments.

Nice-to-have:
- Add at least one additional dataset/contrast or resting-state analysis.
- Include a TV-ℓ1 baseline [1,25] on a subset to contextualize the structure vs speed trade-off.
- Ablate ridge vs ℓ1 codes and k sensitivity.

Pros for acceptance
- Scalable, well-motivated method with clear practical impact.
- Solid integration of spatial structure into online dictionary learning.
- Improved stability and interpretability with modest overhead.
- Clear relation to and extension of established work [16].

Cons for acceptance
- Limited experimental breadth; missing structured baselines.
- Interpretability claims largely qualitative.
- Some methodological details under-specified; surprising optimization claims need evidence.

Overall, the work is a useful and technically sound contribution with clear practical value; bolstering empirical breadth and quantitative interpretability would substantially strengthen it.
