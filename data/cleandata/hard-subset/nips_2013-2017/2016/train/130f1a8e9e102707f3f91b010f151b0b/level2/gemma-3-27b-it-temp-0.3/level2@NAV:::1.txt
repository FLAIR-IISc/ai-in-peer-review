## Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"

This paper proposes a novel multivariate online dictionary learning method, Smooth-SODL, for decomposing brain images into structured and sparse components. The core idea is to combine sparsity-inducing L1 regularization with a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially smooth, blob-like dictionary atoms, thereby facilitating the identification of brain regions. The method extends existing online dictionary learning work (Mairal et al., 2010) and is designed to scale to large datasets.

The paper effectively motivates the problem by highlighting the limitations of existing approaches in handling inter-subject variability and the need for principled ROI definition in neuroimaging. The proposed method addresses these issues by learning data-driven, spatially coherent representations. The technical contribution – integrating a Laplacian penalty into an online learning framework – is a reasonable extension of existing work and addresses a practical challenge in brain image analysis. The authors demonstrate the method's performance on a large HCP dataset, comparing it to CanICA and standard SODL, showing improvements in interpretability, stability, and predictive power for behavioral variables. 

A key strength is the scalability of the proposed approach, leveraging online learning to handle large datasets. The authors also acknowledge the computational cost of the Laplacian penalty, reporting a runtime increase of approximately 3x compared to standard SODL, which is reasonable. The discussion of hyperparameter tuning and the provided insights into the optimal regime are valuable.

However, the paper lacks a deeper exploration of the theoretical properties of the proposed method. While convergence is mentioned, a more rigorous analysis of the impact of the Laplacian penalty on the learned representations would be beneficial. The experiments, while comprehensive, primarily focus on a single dataset (HCP). Evaluating the method on diverse neuroimaging modalities (e.g., PET, diffusion MRI) and datasets would strengthen the claims of generalizability. The comparison to CanICA feels somewhat unfair, as the authors acknowledge the need for post-processing (thresholding) to make the CanICA results interpretable, yet still present the thresholded version as a competitor. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of sparsity and smoothness constraints in an online learning framework.
*   Scalability to large datasets, addressing a key challenge in neuroimaging.
*   Promising empirical results on a large, publicly available dataset.
*   Clear motivation and well-defined problem statement.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited theoretical analysis of the method's properties.
*   Evaluation primarily on a single dataset.
*   Potentially unfair comparison to CanICA.



Overall, this is a solid contribution with a clear potential impact on the field of neuroimaging. While some theoretical and experimental refinements would be beneficial, the paper presents a valuable new method for learning interpretable brain representations. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
