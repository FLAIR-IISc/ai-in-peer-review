This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, aiming to extract spatially structured and sparse components representing brain regions. The core contribution lies in extending existing online dictionary learning techniques with a Laplacian penalty to enforce smoothness, leading to more interpretable and spatially localized decompositions. The method is evaluated on fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project, demonstrating improved performance compared to ICA and standard sparse dictionary learning, particularly in capturing inter-subject variability and predicting behavioral scores.

The strengths of this work are numerous. First, the problem addressed – principled ROI definition in neuroimaging – is important and well-motivated. The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods (univariate approaches, reliance on pre-defined ROIs) and positions the proposed approach as a data-driven alternative. Second, the technical contribution of incorporating a Laplacian penalty into an online dictionary learning framework is sound and appears to be a non-trivial extension of existing work (Mairal et al., 2010). The justification for this extension, based on the expectation of spatially coherent brain structures, is logical. Third, the experimental evaluation on a large, publicly available dataset (HCP) is a significant positive. The inclusion of comparisons to relevant baselines (CanICA, SODL) and the exploration of prediction tasks strengthen the empirical validation. The ablation studies related to the hyperparameter selection and the discussion of the optimality regime is useful and adds to the robustness of the method. Finally, the paper is generally well-written and clear, providing sufficient details for reproducibility, including planned release of code in Nilearn.

However, there are areas where the work could be improved. First, while the online learning aspect is highlighted, the practical benefits in this specific neuroimaging context are not fully elaborated. It would be beneficial to explicitly discuss the memory requirements and scalability advantages of the online approach compared to batch methods, particularly given the large dataset sizes typical in neuroimaging. While the running time is reported, a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost (e.g., time spent in dictionary updates vs. coding) would be helpful. Second, the choice of the Laplacian penalty could benefit from further justification. While smoothness is a reasonable prior, alternative spatial regularization techniques (e.g., total variation) might be considered and their trade-offs discussed. A brief comparison, even qualitative, would strengthen the rationale for the chosen approach. Third, the comparison to CanICA is somewhat limited. The thresholding applied to CanICA (“tCanICA”) is mentioned as a necessary step to improve interpretability, but the thresholding procedure itself is not described in detail. Providing more specifics on the thresholding parameters and their influence on the results would improve the fairness of the comparison. Fourth, the quantitative results, while encouraging, could be presented with more statistical rigor. Reporting p-values or confidence intervals alongside the R-squared scores for the behavioral prediction task would increase confidence in the observed differences. Fifth, the discussion on the convergence of the overall algorithm can be expanded by giving some empirical assessment of the convergence properties.

Arguments for acceptance:

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in neuroimaging.
*   Presents a novel and technically sound extension of existing online dictionary learning techniques.
*   Demonstrates promising results on a large, publicly available dataset.
*   Provides a clear and well-written description of the method and experiments.

Arguments against acceptance:

*   The practical benefits of the online learning approach could be more explicitly discussed.
*   The justification for the choice of the Laplacian penalty could be strengthened.
*   The comparison to CanICA could be more thorough and transparent.
*   The quantitative results could benefit from more statistical rigor.



Overall, this is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of neuroimaging. Addressing the points outlined above would further enhance the quality of the work and increase its impact. The suggested revisions are mostly minor and reasonable to implement within a reasonable timeframe.
