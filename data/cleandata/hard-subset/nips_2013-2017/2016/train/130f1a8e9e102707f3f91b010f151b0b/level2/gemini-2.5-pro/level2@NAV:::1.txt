This paper presents Smooth-SODL, a scalable online dictionary learning algorithm for decomposing large-scale neuroimaging data. The method extends the well-known Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (SODL) framework by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This novel addition aims to produce components that are not only sparse but also spatially smooth and compact, making them more interpretable as functional brain regions and improving on prior work that was either computationally expensive (batch methods with structured priors) or produced unstructured results (standard SODL).

The paper’s strengths lie in its technical quality, originality, and significance for the neuroimaging community. The extension to the online framework is non-trivial and well-motivated. The experimental validation on a large Human Connectome Project dataset is thorough, with compelling comparisons against relevant baselines like SODL and CanICA. The results clearly demonstrate the main claims: the proposed method yields dictionary atoms that are qualitatively more interpretable and quantitatively more stable across data splits, at a modest computational cost (a 3x increase over SODL). This is a significant contribution, as scalable methods that produce interpretable representations are highly sought after.

The work also has some minor weaknesses that could be addressed. While the clarity is generally high, the derivation of the dictionary update in Equation 7 relies heavily on the reader’s familiarity with prior work. A more self-contained explanation would improve reproducibility. Furthermore, the discussion on hyperparameter selection is brief; providing more robust guidance or an automated strategy for setting the regularization parameters (γ, τ) would increase the method’s practical utility. Finally, while the stability and interpretability gains are clear, the authors correctly note that the explained variance advantage over standard SODL diminishes in very large-data regimes, a limitation that tempers the method’s superiority for purely predictive tasks on massive datasets.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant, practical problem in large-scale scientific data analysis.
*   Presents a novel and computationally efficient integration of a structured prior into an online learning framework.
*   Provides strong empirical evidence of improved component interpretability and stability.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The contribution is an incremental, though valuable, extension of an existing algorithm.
*   The performance benefits for some metrics (explained variance) are less pronounced in the large-data limit.

Overall, the paper is a strong contribution that successfully advances the state of the art in scalable, interpretable matrix factorization for neuroimaging. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
