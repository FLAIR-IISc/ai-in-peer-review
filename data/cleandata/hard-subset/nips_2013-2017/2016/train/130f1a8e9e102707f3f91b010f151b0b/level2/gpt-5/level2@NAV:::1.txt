Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary-learning framework that augments Mairal et al. (2010) with a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty on dictionary atoms and a sparsity-inducing constraint (ℓ1-ball or simplex), aiming to produce sparse, piecewise-smooth, spatially localized components from large neuroimaging datasets. The approach connects to GraphNet-style spatial smoothing and to structured sparsity works (e.g., TV-ℓ1 atlases and structured sparse PCA), while retaining online scalability. Experiments on HCP task fMRI (n=500; k=40) compare against SODL and CanICA, reporting improved interpretability/stability and competitive explained variance and downstream prediction.

Strengths:
- Clear claim: online, scalable structured dictionary learning with Laplacian-regularized atoms at modest extra cost (≈2–3× SODL).
- Technically sensible: block coordinate descent with ridge coding and FISTA-based atom updates; discussion of Lipschitz constants and safe γ schedules.
- Empirical value: better qualitative spatial structure, higher atom stability than CanICA/SODL, earlier data-regime gains in explained variance, and competitive behavioral prediction versus dense baselines.
- Practical relevance: aligns with ROI discovery and inter-subject variability modeling; integrates naturally with neuroimaging pipelines.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Support and statistical rigor: Results lack uncertainty estimates and statistical tests (e.g., confidence intervals, permutation baselines for R2, stability significance). Please quantify reproducibility with split-half/bootstraps and report variance across seeds/splits.
- Ablations: Isolate the contribution of Laplacian vs sparsity vs positivity (simplex) constraints; compare ridge vs ℓ1 codes; include “smooth-only” and “sparse-only” settings.
- Baselines: Add TV-ℓ1/GraphNet-based decompositions and recent online matrix factorization (e.g., Mensch et al.) for fairness; include nonnegative matrix factorization where positivity is used.
- Completeness: Specify Laplacian boundary conditions, normalization of atoms, exact τ/γ/α schedules, number of passes, stopping criteria, and memory footprint. The claim that 1–2 FISTA iterations reach 1e-6 tolerance seems optimistic—report actual residuals vs runtime trade-offs.
- Generalization: Evaluate on additional tasks or resting-state data, and test cross-dataset transfer to assess robustness.
- Usefulness: Provide quantitative interpretability metrics (e.g., compactness, blob count, smoothness norms) and a small user study or expert ratings.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance:
- Quality: Method is technically sound; more rigorous evaluation would strengthen claims.
- Clarity: Generally well written; tighten the algorithmic description (distinguish constraints vs penalties; clarify “parameter free” vs FISTA step sizes).
- Originality: Solid extension—online structured atoms with Laplacian regularization is novel in this scalable setting.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for data-driven atlases and population analyses.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Scalable, interpretable decomposition with clear practical relevance.
- Empirical advantages in stability and early data regimes.
- Methodological clarity and potential for adoption.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Insufficient statistical validation and ablations.
- Limited baselines and generalization tests.
- Some implementation details missing; code not yet available.
