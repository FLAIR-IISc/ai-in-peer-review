**Review of "Learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning"**

**Summary of the paper**

The authors propose a multivariate online dictionary-learning method, called Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), for obtaining decompositions of brain images with structured and sparse components. The method combines a Laplacian penalty with an `1-norm constraint to impose sparsity and spatial smoothness on the dictionary atoms. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on a large public dataset, showing that it extracts structured and denoised dictionaries that better capture inter-subject variability compared to state-of-the-art models.

**Claims and Contributions**

The authors claim that their proposed method:

1. Extends the online dictionary-learning framework to learn structured regions representative of brain organization.
2. Successfully augments the online dictionary-learning framework with a Laplacian penalty on the component maps, while conserving the low numerical complexity of the latter.
3. Extracts structured and denoised dictionaries that are more interpretable and better capture inter-subject variability in small, medium, and large-scale regimes alike, compared to state-of-the-art models.

**Support for Claims**

The authors provide experimental results on a large public dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach. They compare their method with state-of-the-art models, including Canonical ICA (CanICA) and sparse online dictionary-learning (SODL), and show that it outperforms these methods in terms of explained variance, stability, and interpretability.

**Usefulness**

The proposed method has the potential to be useful in various applications, including:

1. Dimensionality reduction: The method can be used to reduce the dimensionality of high-dimensional brain imaging data, while preserving the most relevant information.
2. ROI extraction: The method can be used to extract regions of interest (ROIs) from brain imaging data, which can be used for further analysis or as features for machine learning models.

**Field Knowledge**

The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the field of brain imaging and dictionary learning. They provide a clear and concise overview of the related work and position their contribution within the existing literature.

**Novelty**

The authors propose a novel approach that combines a Laplacian penalty with an `1-norm constraint to impose sparsity and spatial smoothness on the dictionary atoms. This approach is an extension of the online dictionary-learning framework, which is a well-established method in the field.

**Completeness**

The authors provide a detailed description of their method, including the optimization algorithm and the experimental setup. They also provide a comprehensive evaluation of their method, including quantitative and qualitative results.

**Limitations**

The authors acknowledge that their method has some limitations, including:

1. Computational cost: The method is computationally expensive, especially for large-scale datasets.
2. Hyper-parameter tuning: The method requires careful tuning of hyper-parameters, which can be time-consuming.

**Constructive Criticism**

The authors could improve their paper by:

1. Providing more details on the optimization algorithm and the computational complexity of the method.
2. Exploring the use of other regularization techniques, such as total variation or wavelet-based regularization.
3. Evaluating the method on a wider range of datasets and applications.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

Pro:

* The method is novel and extends the online dictionary-learning framework in a meaningful way.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in extracting structured and denoised dictionaries.
* The method has the potential to be useful in various applications, including dimensionality reduction and ROI extraction.

Con:

* The method is computationally expensive and requires careful tuning of hyper-parameters.
* The evaluation of the method is limited to a single dataset and application.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, as it presents a novel and effective method for learning brain regions via large-scale online structured sparse dictionary learning. However, the authors should address the limitations and criticisms mentioned above to improve the paper.
