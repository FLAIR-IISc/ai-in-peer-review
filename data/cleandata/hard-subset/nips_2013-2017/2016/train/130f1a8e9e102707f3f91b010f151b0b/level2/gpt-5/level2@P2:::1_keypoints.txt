- Proposal: Smooth-SODL extends online dictionary learning with a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty to produce spatially sparse, smooth “blobby” atoms.
- Algorithm: Uses ridge-coded representations, block-coordinate descent with sufficient-statistics (A,B), and atom updates solving a quadratic+Laplacian via projected accelerated methods or FFT.
- Novelty: Integrates spatial regularization into an online DL framework with claimed convergence guarantees and practical scalability.
- Technical soundness: Method is a natural extension of Mairal et al., but inexact inner updates raise convergence questions.
- Inner-loop concern: Clarify stopping criteria and relate to inexact block-coordinate descent guarantees; the claim “1–2 FISTA iterations to 1e-6” is implausible and must be corrected.
- Lipschitz/steps: L = 1 + γ̃ λmax(Δ) derivation is reasonable; using time-varying γt changes the objective and may affect convergence—specify the schedule used.
- Experimental setup: Evaluated on HCP task fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6e5, k=40) against SODL and CanICA using EV, stability, interpretability, prediction, and runtime.
- Preprocessing fairness: CanICA was run on 6mm-smoothed data while SODL/Smooth-SODL used raw inputs—need to run matched preprocessing (SODL on smoothed, CanICA on raw) or justify mismatch.
- Behavioral prediction details: Must specify the predictive model, CV protocol, feature selection procedure (avoid leakage), and report confidence intervals.
- Stability details: Define Hungarian matching procedure, sign handling, and whether correlations were computed on whole volume or masked regions.
- Learning curves/details: Report exact train/test splits, whether dictionaries were trained only on training set, and how EV on held-out subjects was computed.
- Empirical findings: Smooth-SODL improves interpretability and stability (stability ~0.4 vs 0.1–0.2), has modest runtime overhead (~3x), higher EV than SODL but lower than CanICA, and better behavioral prediction than SODL/tCanICA.
- Small-sample advantage: Smooth-SODL shows clear benefits in small-sample regimes.
- Clarity issues: Paper has numerous typos and notational inconsistencies; objective should use an expectation or empirical average instead of lim n→∞.
- Laplacian specifics: Specify boundary conditions, normalization, masking, and image standardization used for the Laplacian.
- Constraint clarity: State whether C is an l1-ball or a simplex and discuss implications for sign/non-negativity constraints.
- Pseudocode: Provide explicit pseudo-code showing the projection operator used for C in presence of the Laplacian.
- Accuracy claim revision: Remove or correct the “1–2 FISTA iterations to 1e-6” statement.
- Relation to prior work: Contribution is modest but meaningful compared to batch methods (TV-l1, GraphNet, structured sparse PCA); discuss relation to Mensch et al. (2016) more deeply.
- Significance: Practical and useful for neuroimaging practitioners; offers interpretability–fidelity trade-offs and Nilearn integration increases impact.
- Strengths: Clear modeling, scalable algorithm with practical runtimes, comprehensive evaluation, and empirical evidence that spatial regularization improves stability.
- Weaknesses/suggestions: Tighten convergence theory or run inner loops to tolerance; ensure fair, reproducible experiments; add ablations (γ, τ, k) and sensitivity plots; detail prediction/CV; discuss time-varying αt/γt effects; fix typos and definitions.
- Pros for acceptance: Practical, scalable, and empirically validated method likely to be adopted.
- Cons for acceptance: Theoretical/convergence claims need tightening, experimental fairness/reproducibility issues, and the contribution is somewhat incremental.
- Recommendation: Weak accept contingent on clarifying inner-loop convergence, improving experimental fairness/details, and revising clarity/typos.
