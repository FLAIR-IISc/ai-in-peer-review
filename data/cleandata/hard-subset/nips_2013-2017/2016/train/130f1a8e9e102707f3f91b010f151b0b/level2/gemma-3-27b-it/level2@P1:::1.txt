This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images into structured and sparse components. The key idea is to combine an L1 penalty for sparsity with a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially smooth, blob-like dictionary atoms. The method extends previous online dictionary learning work (Mairal et al., 2010) and is shown to scale well to large datasets. Experiments on HCP data demonstrate that the proposed method extracts more interpretable dictionaries and better captures inter-subject variability compared to ICA and standard online dictionary learning.

**Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles a relevant problem in neuroimaging: identifying meaningful spatial patterns of brain activity while accounting for inter-subject variability. Existing approaches often rely on predefined regions of interest (ROIs) or unsupervised clustering, each with limitations. The authors position their work as an alternative that learns these regions directly from the data, leveraging the strengths of dictionary learning with added structural priors. The connection to Mairal et al. (2010) is clearly stated, and the paper appropriately frames its contribution as an extension of that work. The authors also acknowledge related approaches like ICA and spatially regularized methods (e.g., TV-L1), accurately placing their method within the broader landscape of neuroimaging analysis techniques. Notably, the paper correctly identifies that prior spatially-regularized methods often suffer from computational scaling issues, which their online approach addresses.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of L1 and Laplacian penalties within an *online* dictionary learning framework is a valuable contribution. The online aspect is crucial for handling the massive datasets common in neuroimaging.
* **Scalability:** The authors emphasize and demonstrate the scalability of their approach, which is a significant advantage for real-world applications. The running time comparison with CanICA and SODL provides a good benchmark.
* **Interpretability:** The visually more structured dictionary atoms produced by the method, as shown in Fig. 2(a), are a compelling result. The authors make a strong case that this structure leads to more interpretable representations of brain activity.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the HCP dataset are well-designed, including comparisons to established methods and a thorough analysis of the impact of hyperparameters. The predictive performance on behavioral variables adds further support for the effectiveness of the method.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical formulation and algorithmic details. The inclusion of algorithmic pseudocode (Alg. 1 & 2) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors discuss hyperparameter tuning, the process still appears somewhat ad-hoc.  The reliance on cross-validation and the observed sensitivity to γ and τ could be a practical challenge for users. A more principled method for hyperparameter selection would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** While the method scales well, the running time is still significantly longer than the vanilla SODL (3x slower). This might be a concern for very large datasets or real-time applications. The authors note this, but further optimization could be considered.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to CanICA is valuable, but it might be beneficial to also compare against more recent deep learning-based approaches for brain decoding and representation learning.
* **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on empirical results.  A more rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties and statistical guarantees of the proposed method would strengthen the contribution.
* **Redundancy:** Some sections (e.g., the introduction) contain repetitive statements.  Conciseness could improve clarity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for learning brain regions.
* The online learning framework addresses a critical scalability issue in neuroimaging.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method and its advantages over existing approaches.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The hyperparameter sensitivity could limit the practical usability of the method.
* The computational cost is higher than simpler alternatives.
* The lack of theoretical analysis weakens the scientific rigor.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed method is novel, scalable, and performs well in practice. While there are some weaknesses, they are not major enough to warrant rejection. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**

* Provide a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter tuning process and explore more principled methods for selecting γ and τ.
* Investigate potential optimizations to reduce the computational cost of the Laplacian penalty.
* Consider including a comparison to recent deep learning-based approaches.
* Briefly address the theoretical limitations in the discussion section.
* Streamline the introduction and remove redundant statements.
