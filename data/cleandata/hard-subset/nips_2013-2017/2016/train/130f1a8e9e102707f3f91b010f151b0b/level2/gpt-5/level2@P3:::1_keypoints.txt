- Paper proposes Smooth-SODL: an online dictionary learning extension for neuroimaging that yields spatially localized, sparse, piecewise-smooth atoms.
- Sparsity enforced via ℓ1-ball or simplex constraints; spatial structure via a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty on each atom.
- Codes estimated with ridge regression; dictionary updated with block coordinate descent and per-atom FISTA proximal-gradient steps.
- Method preserves scalability of prior online DL [16] with ~2–3× runtime overhead.
- Experiments on HCP task fMRI (n=500, k=40, Math vs Story) compare Smooth-SODL to SODL and CanICA on EV, stability, runtime, interpretability, and behavioral prediction.
- Results: smoother, more compact atoms and higher stability than SODL and CanICA; competitive EV (especially with small data); prediction performance below CanICA but above SODL and thresholded CanICA.
- Builds on online sparse coding [16,17] and GraphNet/structured sparsity ideas [11,14]; contrasts with TV-ℓ1 approaches [1,25] that are slower.
- Baselines (CanICA, SODL) are appropriate, but broader structured baselines and MELODIC context are noted.
- Strength: tackles a relevant scalable, interpretable ROI learning problem for ML and neuroimaging communities.
- Strength: sound algorithmic integration of Laplacian regularization into an online BCD framework with strong convex per-atom subproblems.
- Strength: empirical evidence of improved atom compactness and split stability, with acceptable runtime trade-offs.
- Strength: practical hyperparameter guidance (γ, τ, α) and adaptive αt schedule discussed.
- Weakness: experiments limited to a single contrast; generality to other tasks/resting-state not shown.
- Weakness: missing comparisons to structured baselines (e.g., TV-ℓ1) weakens claims about structure vs speed.
- Weakness: interpretability claims largely qualitative; no quantitative metrics for compactness/contiguity or atlas overlap.
- Weakness: stability metric details (number of splits, sign/scale handling, statistical variability) are under-specified.
- Weakness: methodological details need clarification—FISTA iteration counts/convergence, practical γ guidance, Laplacian boundary conditions and FFT use, and exact constraint set implementation.
- Weakness: choice of ridge codes vs ℓ1 codes lacks ablation showing EV/stability/speed trade-offs.
- Weakness: sensitivity to dictionary size k not studied.
- Quality: optimization scheme is technically sound; runtime/memory claims plausible; empirical evaluation careful but narrow in scope.
- Clarity: overall organization is good, but some derivations and implementation details are deferred; typos and notation inconsistencies present.
- Originality: incremental but non-trivial extension of [16], novel in bringing Laplacian regularization into an online DL solver at scale.
- Significance: provides a scalable tool for interpretable ROI learning with potential for adoption (e.g., Nilearn), though broader empirical support would strengthen impact.
- Essential suggestions: add quantitative interpretability metrics, report stability with error bars, include convergence/iteration plots, and clarify Laplacian/constraint/γ implementation and chosen hyperparameters.
- Nice-to-have suggestions: additional datasets/contrasts or resting-state, include TV-ℓ1 baseline on a subset, ablate ridge vs ℓ1 codes and k sensitivity.
- Pros for acceptance: scalable, well-motivated method with practical impact, solid integration of spatial structure, improved stability/interpretability with modest overhead.
- Cons for acceptance: limited experimental breadth, interpretability mostly qualitative, and some optimization/implementation claims under-specified.
