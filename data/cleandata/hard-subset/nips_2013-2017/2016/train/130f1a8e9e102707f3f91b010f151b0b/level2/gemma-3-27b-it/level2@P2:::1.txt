This paper proposes a multivariate online dictionary learning method for decomposing brain images, aiming to extract structured and sparse components representing brain regions. The key contribution lies in extending the online dictionary learning work of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty to encourage spatially smooth, blob-like dictionary atoms alongside the standard L1 sparsity constraint. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project, showing improved interpretability and capture of inter-subject variability compared to ICA and standard online dictionary learning.

The paper is well-motivated. The problem of defining regions of interest (ROIs) in neuroimaging is a crucial one, and the idea of learning these ROIs from the data, rather than relying on predefined anatomical or functional criteria, is compelling. The authors appropriately situate their work within the context of existing methods like PCA, ICA, and clustering, highlighting the limitations of these approaches and positioning their dictionary learning approach as a potentially superior alternative. The extension of online dictionary learning with a Laplacian penalty seems like a sensible approach to enforce spatial structure, and the theoretical justification connecting it to Sobolev spaces is sound. 

The paper's strengths lie in its clear exposition of the method, its theoretical grounding, and its experimental validation on a large dataset. The inclusion of comparisons to both CanICA and the original SODL is valuable. The discussion of hyperparameter tuning, particularly the data-adaptive choice for α, is a practical contribution. The running time comparisons are also useful, showing a reasonable trade-off between computational cost and the benefits of the spatial regularization. The experiments demonstrate clear qualitative improvements in the structure of the learned dictionaries. The quantitative results are also compelling, with the Smooth-SODL model demonstrating superior performance in predicting behavioral variables.

However, several weaknesses require addressing. First, while the authors mention the factor of 2-3 increase in running time compared to standard SODL, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost would be beneficial. A breakdown of the time spent in different parts of the algorithm (e.g., code updates, dictionary updates, Laplacian regularization) would help understand the bottleneck.  The discussion regarding the convergence criterion and the choice of FISTA parameters could be expanded. The claim of convergence “to a local optimum” feels weak – providing stronger guarantees, or at least a more thorough discussion of the limitations, would be valuable.

Second, the justification for the choice of the Laplacian penalty could be strengthened. While the authors relate it to piece-wise smooth and compact structures, a more detailed explanation of why the Laplacian is a suitable choice, compared to other spatial regularizers (e.g., Total Variation), would be helpful.  Similarly, the choice of the simplex constraint for sparsity could benefit from further justification. 

Third, while the authors acknowledge that CanICA performs best in terms of explained variance, they downplay this somewhat by focusing on interpretability. A more nuanced discussion of the trade-off between explained variance and interpretability is needed.  Is it possible to combine the strengths of both approaches—for example, by applying the Smooth-SODL method to the residuals after applying CanICA?

Finally, there are some minor issues with presentation. Some of the equations are difficult to follow due to the dense notation. More careful formatting and explanation of the variables would improve clarity. 



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in neuroimaging with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   Demonstrates clear improvements in interpretability and the capture of inter-subject variability compared to existing methods.
*   Scales well to large datasets due to the online learning framework.
*   Includes a thorough experimental evaluation on a publicly available dataset.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational cost analysis is insufficient.
*   The justification for the choice of Laplacian penalty and simplex constraint could be strengthened.
*   The trade-off between explained variance and interpretability requires further discussion.
*   Some equations are difficult to follow.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of neuroimaging. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated. While there are some weaknesses that need to be addressed, they are not critical. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns raised above. A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity, a stronger justification for the chosen regularization techniques, and a more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs involved would substantially strengthen the paper.
