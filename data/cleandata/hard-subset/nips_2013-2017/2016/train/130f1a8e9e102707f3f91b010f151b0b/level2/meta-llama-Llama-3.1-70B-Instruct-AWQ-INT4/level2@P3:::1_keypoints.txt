- Proposes Smooth-SODL, an online multivariate dictionary-learning method combining L1 sparsity and a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure in brain-image decompositions.
- Demonstrated effectiveness on a large public dataset, extracting structured, denoised dictionaries that capture inter-subject variability across scales.

Strengths:
- Novel approach combining sparsity and spatial structure for improved interpretability of learned dictionaries.
- Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for the algorithm.
- Thorough experimental evaluation comparing to state-of-the-art methods on a large dataset.
- Produces more interpretable dictionaries for brain imaging than competing methods.

Weaknesses:
- Lacks a detailed analysis of computational complexity and scalability to very large datasets.
- Does not provide practical guidance or detailed analysis for hyper-parameter tuning.
- Comparison set is limited; missing comparisons to other potentially relevant brain-imaging methods.

Originality and Significance:
- Novel combination of online dictionary learning with spatial-structure regularization.
- Significant empirical demonstration on a large public dataset relevant to brain imaging.

Quality of the Paper:
- Well-written and clear explanations of methods and theory.
- Well-organized with a clear structure.
- Effective use of figures and tables to illustrate results.

Recommendations for Improvement:
- Add a detailed computational-complexity analysis showing how the method scales.
- Provide practical guidance and empirical analysis for hyper-parameter selection and sensitivity.
- Expand comparisons to additional relevant methods in brain imaging.

Arguments for Acceptance:
- Introduces a novel, interpretable approach to online dictionary learning.
- Offers theoretical convergence guarantees.
- Strong experimental results on a large public dataset.

Arguments for Rejection:
- Insufficient analysis of computational complexity for large-scale use.
- Limited practical guidance on hyper-parameter tuning.
- Incomplete comparison to other relevant methods, limiting evaluation scope.
