This paper proposes a new method, Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary-Learning (Smooth-SODL), for decomposing large-scale neuroimaging datasets into a dictionary of spatially structured, sparse components (or atoms). The goal is to learn a set of interpretable brain regions that capture inter-subject variability. The method extends the well-known online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by introducing a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages the atoms to be spatially smooth, forming coherent "blobs" rather than scattered patterns, which is desirable for defining brain regions. The resulting optimization problem is solved efficiently using a block-coordinate descent scheme, where the key dictionary update step is handled by a fast first-order solver (FISTA). The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on a large task-fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project (n=500), comparing it against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a popular neuroimaging method, Canonical ICA (CanICA).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses an important and timely problem in neuroimaging: the scalable, data-driven discovery of functional brain atlases from massive datasets. While sparse dictionary learning has been applied to this problem, the resulting components often lack spatial coherence. The key contribution—integrating a spatial smoothness prior via a Laplacian penalty directly into the online learning framework—is a novel and well-motivated extension. It provides a principled way to obtain more interpretable and neuroscientifically plausible results.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The method is technically sound. The formulation is a clear and sensible generalization of previous work. The algorithmic solution, which adapts the block-coordinate descent framework and solves the structured dictionary update sub-problem with FISTA, is appropriate and efficient. The authors correctly identify the computational trade-offs, noting that the Lipschitz constant of the sub-problem depends on the regularization parameter γ. The experimental evaluation is strong: it uses a large, public dataset (HCP), compares against highly relevant baselines (SODL and CanICA), and employs a comprehensive set of metrics, including visual quality, explained variance, stability, and performance on a downstream prediction task.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for structured sparsity is well-explained in the introduction. The model and algorithm are presented with sufficient mathematical detail. The figures, particularly Figure 2, are very effective at summarizing the main experimental results and illustrating the qualitative differences between the outputs of the compared models. The finding that the added structure is especially beneficial in the low-data regime (Table 1) is a compelling result that is clearly presented.

4.  **Practical Impact:** The proposed method is not only theoretically sound but also practical. The authors report that Smooth-SODL is only about 3 times slower than the unstructured SODL, while being faster than CanICA. This makes it a viable tool for large-scale analysis. The commitment to release the implementation in the popular Nilearn library is a significant contribution to the community and greatly increases the potential impact of this work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Comparison with other Structure-Inducing Priors:** The paper uses a Laplacian penalty (squared `l2`-norm of the gradient) to induce smoothness. Another very common and powerful regularizer for enforcing spatial structure is the Total Variation (TV) norm (`l1`-norm of the gradient), which promotes piece-wise constant solutions. The authors cite one work using TV but do not discuss the relative merits of their choice. A brief discussion on why the Laplacian was chosen over TV would be beneficial. While the quadratic nature of the Laplacian penalty likely leads to a simpler and faster optimization sub-problem than the non-smooth TV penalty, explicitly stating this trade-off between model assumption (smooth vs. piece-wise constant) and computational efficiency would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Guidance on Hyperparameter Selection:** The authors acknowledge that hyperparameter selection for `γ` (smoothness) and `τ` (sparsity) is a difficult problem. While Figure 1 provides a useful characterization of the parameter space, the final recommendation of using cross-validation is standard but not very insightful. It would be valuable to provide more practical guidance. For example, are there data-driven heuristics for setting a reasonable range for `γ`? How sensitive are the qualitative results to the choice of these parameters within the "good" regime identified in Figure 1?

3.  **Discussion of Prediction Results:** In the behavioral prediction experiment (Figure 2(c)), the dense CanICA model appears to achieve the best predictive performance on the test set, outperforming Smooth-SODL. This is an important finding that deserves more discussion. It suggests a potential trade-off between model interpretability (where Smooth-SODL excels) and raw predictive power (where dense features might retain more information). Acknowledging and briefly discussing this trade-off would provide a more nuanced conclusion.

4.  **Minor Details for Reproducibility:** In the definition of the Laplacian operator (Equation 4), it would be helpful to specify the neighborhood system used to define the discrete gradients on the 3D voxel grid (e.g., 6-connectivity, 26-connectivity). This is a minor detail but is important for full reproducibility. Additionally, some citations in the reference list appear to be incomplete (e.g., [1], [2]).

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable and well-executed contribution to the field. It successfully combines the scalability of online dictionary learning with the interpretability of structured models for a challenging and important application in neuroimaging. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach over existing methods. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to opportunities for deeper discussion and clarification, which can be addressed in a final version. I recommend acceptance.
