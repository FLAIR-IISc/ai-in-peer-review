

This paper proposes an extension to online dictionary learning called Smooth-SODL, which introduces a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial structure in dictionary atoms for brain imaging applications. The authors argue that structured, blob-like brain regions are more interpretable than scattered patterns, and they extend Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty to achieve this. The method is designed to scale to large datasets while producing structured, sparse representations of brain activity patterns.

The paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness on HCP fMRI data, comparing it against SODL (sparse online dictionary learning) and CanICA. The authors show that their method produces more interpretable dictionaries with better spatial structure, improved stability across data splits (measured by mean correlation between corresponding atoms), and competitive performance in predicting behavioral variables. They also demonstrate that the method maintains computational efficiency (only 2-3x slower than SODL) and performs well across small, medium, and large datasets.

Strengths:
1. The motivation for structured brain regions is well-justified, addressing a real problem in neuroimaging where inter-subject variability contains important information.
2. The theoretical extension to the online dictionary learning framework is sound, with proper handling of the optimization problem using BCD and FISTA.
3. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, behavioral prediction) and a learning curve analysis demonstrating scalability across dataset sizes.
4. The paper provides valuable insights into hyperparameter selection (particularly the trade-off between γ and τ) and shows that their method produces more interpretable results without sacrificing predictive performance.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks a clear justification for using the simplex constraint Sp(τ) instead of the more common L1-ball constraint Bp,`1(τ) used in the original SODL. This choice significantly impacts the results but isn't properly motivated or compared.
2. The comparison with other spatial regularization approaches (like TV-regularization mentioned in related work) is limited to a brief mention rather than direct experimental comparison. The paper claims their method is "non-trivially" extending Mairal et al. but doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how their Laplacian penalty compares to alternatives.
3. The paper claims the method "better captures inter-subject variability" but doesn't provide quantitative measures of this improvement beyond stability metrics. It would be valuable to see how the structured atoms relate to known brain networks in more detail.
4. The paper states that CanICA is "dense and noisy" but doesn't provide quantitative measures of this (e.g., average non-zero values per atom, smoothness metrics). The visual comparison in Figure 2(a) is helpful, but a quantitative analysis would strengthen the claim.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from motivation to method to evaluation. The experiments are appropriate for the claimed contribution, and the implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The authors provide a thoughtful discussion of the hyperparameter trade-offs, which is valuable for practitioners.

The main concern is the lack of proper comparison with alternative spatial regularization techniques, which is critical for establishing the novelty and superiority of their approach. While the Laplacian penalty is a reasonable choice, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why it's better than other spatial regularization methods commonly used in neuroimaging (like total variation). The authors should include a brief comparison with a TV-based approach to strengthen their claim of "structured" versus "scattered" patterns.

The significance of this work is substantial for neuroimaging, where interpretable brain regions are crucial for understanding brain function. The method's computational efficiency makes it practical for large-scale applications, and the improved stability across data splits addresses a real limitation of current approaches. The paper successfully demonstrates that structured dictionary learning can produce more interpretable brain maps without sacrificing predictive power.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of neuroimaging and dictionary learning. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and the experiments appropriately support the claims. The main weakness is the insufficient comparison with alternative spatial regularization methods, which should be addressed to strengthen the novelty argument. With minor revisions to clarify the constraint choice and add a brief comparison with TV-regularization, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
