

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning for brain imaging that incorporates spatial structure via a Laplacian penalty while maintaining sparsity. The authors address a significant challenge in neuroimaging: extracting interpretable brain regions from large-scale functional data. Their method builds on Mairal et al.'s (2010) online dictionary learning framework, adding a Laplacian penalty to enforce spatial smoothness on the dictionary atoms, which represent brain regions.

The paper demonstrates that their approach yields more spatially structured, interpretable brain maps compared to standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and CanICA. Experiments on HCP task fMRI data show that Smooth-SODL achieves better stability (0.4 vs. 0.1 for SODL and 0.1 for CanICA in dictionary atom reproducibility), while maintaining good explanatory power (55% vs. 49% test explained variance). The method is only 2-3x slower than SODL, making it practical for large-scale neuroimaging applications.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in neuroimaging with clear practical impact. Extracting interpretable brain regions from large datasets is crucial for understanding functional organization.
2. The method is well-motivated and theoretically grounded. The Laplacian penalty effectively enforces the spatial smoothness constraint that leads to blob-like brain regions, which aligns with known brain organization.
3. The experimental validation is comprehensive, comparing against relevant baselines (SODL and CanICA) across multiple metrics (explained variance, stability, reproducibility, predictive performance for behavioral variables).
4. The paper provides valuable practical guidance on hyperparameter selection, particularly the time-varying α parameter and the safe range for γ.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty claim could be strengthened. While the paper extends existing work, it doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from prior structured dictionary learning approaches like [1] (Varoquaux et al., 2012) which also uses spatial regularization. The authors mention that their method "non-trivially extends" Mairal et al. but don't clearly articulate how their approach differs from the structured dictionary learning methods already used in neuroimaging.
2. The justification for spatial regularization when data is abundant (HCP dataset) is somewhat superficial. The paper shows that Smooth-SODL has advantages with small datasets but doesn't thoroughly address why spatial regularization is beneficial even with large datasets, which is a key claim of the paper.
3. The paper doesn't discuss limitations of the Laplacian penalty (e.g., it might not capture certain brain structures that aren't smooth or have complex boundaries).
4. The connection to previous NIPS work on structured sparsity (e.g., Jenatton et al., 2011) could be more explicit.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with clear mathematical formulation and appropriate experimental validation. The algorithmic details are presented with sufficient precision for reproducibility. The authors have made a thoughtful effort to address computational constraints for large-scale neuroimaging, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The significance of the work is clear: it provides a practical tool for neuroimaging researchers to extract interpretable brain regions from large datasets, addressing a key limitation of current approaches. The method has potential to influence how brain imaging data is analyzed in future studies.

Recommendations for improvement:
1. Strengthen the comparison with prior work on structured dictionary learning in neuroimaging. Clearly articulate how Smooth-SODL differs from and improves upon existing methods.
2. Provide a more thorough discussion of when spatial regularization is most beneficial, especially given the HCP dataset's size.
3. Consider adding a brief discussion of limitations of the Laplacian penalty and potential alternatives for capturing different types of brain structure.
4. Clarify the relationship with recent structured sparsity work in machine learning (e.g., Jenatton et al., 2011) to better position the contribution within the broader machine learning literature.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of machine learning and neuroimaging. The method is practical, well-validated, and addresses an important problem with clear impact on the field. With minor revisions to strengthen the novelty claim and address the limitations of the spatial regularization approach, this work would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
