

This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an extension of online dictionary learning for brain imaging that imposes both sparsity (via L1-norm) and spatial structure (via Laplacian penalty) on dictionary atoms. The authors claim this approach extracts more interpretable, structured brain regions that better capture inter-subject variability compared to state-of-the-art methods, while maintaining scalability with only 2-3x computational overhead.

The main strengths are well-documented: the method effectively addresses a significant gap in neuroimaging analysis by providing a principled way to define brain regions without segmentation or cross-modality alignment. The experiments on HCP data (500 subjects) demonstrate clear advantages in interpretability (Fig. 2a), stability (0.4 correlation vs. CanICA's 0.1), and performance on behavioral prediction (Fig. 2c). The computational efficiency claim (3x slowdown vs. SODL) is well-supported with runtime measurements. The paper also properly acknowledges limitations, noting spatial regularization is most beneficial with limited data.

However, the novelty claim requires refinement. While the Laplacian penalty for spatial structure is well-motivated, the authors don't sufficiently distinguish their approach from prior work like Varoquaux et al. (IPMI 2015) that also used structured dictionary learning for brain imaging. The comparison to this work is insufficient, and the paper doesn't clearly establish how their "non-trivial extension" differs from existing structured approaches in terms of mathematical formulation or computational guarantees.

The methodological details are generally sound, but the hyper-parameter selection discussion (Section 3.2) is somewhat superficial. The authors claim "a safe range" for (γ, τ) but provide limited guidance on how to practically determine these values for new datasets beyond the HCP example. A more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity would strengthen the contribution.

Overall, this is a solid technical contribution with clear practical value for neuroimaging. The paper would be significantly improved by better contextualizing the novelty against prior structured dictionary learning work and providing more concrete guidance for parameter selection. With these revisions, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
