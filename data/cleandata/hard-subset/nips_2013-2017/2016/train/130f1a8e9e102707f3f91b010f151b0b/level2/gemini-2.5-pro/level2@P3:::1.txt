This paper proposes a large-scale online dictionary learning method, termed Smooth-SODL, for decomposing brain images into a set of components that are both sparse and spatially structured. The method extends the well-known online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by incorporating a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. This penalty encourages the resulting components to be piece-wise smooth and form compact regions ("blobs"), which is a desirable property for defining brain regions of interest (ROIs). The work is motivated by the need for scalable analysis methods that can handle massive neuroimaging datasets while producing interpretable and stable results. The proposed online algorithm retains the computational benefits of its predecessor, enabling application to datasets with a very large number of subjects, while adding the spatial structure at a modest computational cost. This work positions itself as a scalable and principled alternative to slower batch methods for structured sparse decomposition and as an improvement over methods like ICA that do not enforce structure.

**Strengths of the work:**

*   **Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound extension of an established algorithm. The optimization problem is well-formulated, and the use of a Block-Coordinate Descent scheme is appropriate and clearly described. The experimental evaluation is a major strength of this work. It is performed on a large, contemporary dataset (HCP) and includes a comprehensive comparison against relevant state-of-the-art methods (SODL, CanICA). The evaluation uses a thoughtful combination of qualitative (visual inspection) and quantitative metrics, including explained variance, stability, and predictive performance on behavioral variables. The learning curve analysis presented in Table 1 is particularly compelling, as it provides strong evidence for the benefit of the spatial regularizer, especially in the common small-to-medium sample size regime.

*   **Originality:** The primary contribution is the novel and effective integration of a spatial smoothness prior (Laplacian penalty) into a scalable online dictionary learning framework. While structured sparse models have been explored in neuroimaging before (e.g., using Total Variation), this work appears to be the first to develop a truly online, large-scale solver for this class of problems. This successfully bridges the gap between fast but unstructured methods and more structured but computationally expensive batch algorithms, which is a significant and timely contribution.

*   **Significance:** The proposed method addresses a critical need in the neuroimaging community for tools that can extract meaningful, data-driven ROIs from increasingly large datasets. The results demonstrate that the method produces components that are more interpretable and reproducible than existing methods, which is of high practical value for researchers. The scalability of the algorithm makes it applicable to massive consortia-level data. Furthermore, the plan to release the implementation as part of the open-source Nilearn library greatly increases the potential for this work to have a broad and lasting impact on the field.

**Suggestions for improvement:**

While this is a strong paper, several aspects could be clarified or strengthened to further improve the contribution.

*   **Clarity of the Dictionary Update:** The dictionary update step (Algorithm 2) involves solving a subproblem with FISTA. The paper states that 1-2 iterations are empirically sufficient for convergence. This is a critical factor for the overall efficiency of the algorithm. It would be beneficial to add a brief discussion or empirical analysis showing how the solution quality and overall algorithm performance are affected by the number of inner-loop FISTA iterations. This would provide a more robust justification for this important implementation choice.

*   **Discussion of Predictive Performance:** The results in Figure 2(c) show that while Smooth-SODL outperforms several baselines in predicting behavioral scores, the non-sparse CanICA method achieves the highest predictive accuracy. This suggests a potential trade-off between component interpretability/stability and raw predictive power. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of this trade-off. For instance, it could be that the dense components from ICA retain subtle, distributed patterns of variance that are useful for prediction but are smoothed over by the spatial regularization. Acknowledging and interpreting this result would add valuable nuance.

*   **Hyperparameter Selection:** The paper provides a good empirical study of the model's hyperparameters in Figure 1. The data-adaptive choice for the code regularization parameter `alpha` is a nice practical contribution. It might be helpful to provide a slightly more explicit recommendation or strategy for setting the key parameters `gamma` (Laplacian weight) and `tau` (sparsity) in practice, beyond the use of cross-validation on explained variance, which can be computationally intensive.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and computationally efficient method for a significant problem in computational neuroscience. The experiments are thorough and provide convincing evidence for the utility of the proposed approach. The minor suggestions above are intended to further strengthen an already excellent contribution and could be addressed in a final version. The work is a clear advance for the field and is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.
