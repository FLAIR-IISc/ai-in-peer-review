This paper proposes Smooth-SODL, a method for learning structured and sparse decompositions of large-scale neuroimaging data. The work extends the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by introducing a Laplacian penalty on the dictionary atoms. The goal is to learn a dictionary of components, or "brain regions," that are not only sparse (containing mostly zeros) but also spatially structured (i.e., smooth, compact blobs). This is achieved by combining an `l1`-ball constraint with a Sobolev (Laplacian) penalty in the objective function. The online nature of the algorithm allows it to scale to massive datasets, which is a critical need in modern neuroimaging. The authors demonstrate their method on a large fMRI dataset from the Human Connectome Project, comparing it against standard sparse online dictionary learning (SODL) and a popular neuroimaging-specific method, Canonical ICA (CanICA).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem in computational neuroscience: the data-driven discovery of functional brain regions from large population datasets. The proposed combination of a scalable online learning algorithm with a spatial structure prior is a valuable and novel contribution. It effectively bridges the gap between fast but unstructured decomposition methods (like standard SODL) and structured but computationally expensive batch methods (e.g., those using Total Variation penalties).

2.  **Technical Quality:** The method is technically sound, presenting a well-motivated extension of an established algorithm. The derivation of the block-coordinate descent update rules is clear, and the authors correctly identify the dictionary update subproblem as a constrained quadratic minimization solvable with fast first-order methods. The discussion of convergence and the practical considerations for choosing hyperparameters are handled well.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength of this work.
    *   The use of a large, challenging, and publicly available dataset (HCP) makes the results relevant and credible.
    *   The choice of comparison methods (SODL and CanICA) is appropriate, representing both a direct baseline and a domain-specific state-of-the-art method.
    *   The evaluation metrics are comprehensive, covering computational performance, qualitative interpretability, explained variance, dictionary stability, and predictive power for external behavioral variables.
    *   The learning curve analysis in Table 1 is particularly compelling. It clearly demonstrates that the spatial regularizer provides a substantial benefit in low-to-medium data regimes, which is a very common scenario in neuroimaging studies, while remaining competitive in the large-data limit. This provides a strong justification for the added complexity of the model.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The problem statement is clearly motivated in the introduction, the model is formally defined, and the experimental results are presented effectively in figures and tables.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Justification of Laplacian vs. Total Variation (TV):** The authors mention TV-regularized methods as related work but primarily dismiss them on computational grounds. While the computational efficiency of the Laplacian penalty is a clear advantage, a brief discussion on the qualitative differences between the solutions produced by a Laplacian (L2-norm on the gradient) versus a TV (L1-norm on the gradient) penalty would be insightful. TV is known to promote piece-wise constant regions with sharp boundaries, which may be more anatomically plausible for delineating distinct brain areas than the smooth solutions encouraged by the Laplacian. Is the choice of the Laplacian purely for computational convenience, or is there a neuroscientific reason to prefer smoother regions?

2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper rightly notes that hyperparameter selection is a difficult problem. Figure 1 illustrates the trade-offs, and the authors suggest k-fold cross-validation. This is a reasonable approach, but it can be very computationally demanding. It would strengthen the paper to provide more practical guidance on setting the parameters `γ` and `τ`. For example, could the optimal range for `γ` be related to image properties like voxel size or the intrinsic smoothness of the data? Adding more intuition here would increase the practical utility of the method.

3.  **Penalty on Codes (`U`):** The model uses a Ridge (`l2`) penalty on the codes, noting that this leads to stability, in contrast to the `l1` penalty used in the original SODL work by Mairal et al. (2010). This choice is reasonable, but the paper would benefit from a more explicit justification. For neuroimaging data, what is the practical implication of using dense (`l2`) versus sparse (`l1`) subject-level codes? Does assuming each subject's brain map is a sparse combination of dictionary atoms provide any benefit or drawback in this context? A short discussion or a small comparative experiment would be helpful.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a solid contribution to both the machine learning and computational neuroscience fields. It presents a novel, scalable, and effective algorithm for a significant real-world problem. The strengths—a sound technical approach, strong and thorough empirical validation, and clear presentation—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The work is a valuable extension of an important line of research in online matrix factorization and is likely to be impactful and widely used by neuroimaging researchers.

I recommend acceptance.
