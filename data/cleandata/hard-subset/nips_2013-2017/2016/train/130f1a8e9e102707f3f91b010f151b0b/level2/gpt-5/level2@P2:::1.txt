Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth Sparse Online Dictionary Learning (Smooth-SODL), an online matrix factorization method for decomposing high-dimensional brain images into a small number of spatially sparse and smooth dictionary atoms. The method extends the online dictionary learning framework of Mairal et al. (2010) by adding a Laplacian (Sobolev) penalty on atoms to promote piecewise smooth, compact “blobby” patterns while retaining sparsity via an l1-type constraint (e.g., simplex). Codes are obtained by ridge regression (with an option for l1), and the algorithm performs block-coordinate descent with sufficient-statistics updates (A, B). Each atom update solves a strongly convex quadratic with a Laplacian term under a convex constraint, using projected accelerated first-order methods (e.g., FISTA) or FFT in special cases. The authors discuss hyperparameter choices and provide empirical runtime.

Experiments on HCP task fMRI Z-maps (n=500, p≈2.6×10^5, k=40) compare Smooth-SODL to standard sparse online DL (SODL; γ=0) and CanICA. Metrics include explained variance (train/test), spatial stability (mean inter-split atom correlation with Hungarian matching), qualitative interpretability, prediction of behavioral variables, and runtime. Results suggest Smooth-SODL improves interpretability and stability over SODL with modest runtime overhead (~3x), yields higher explained variance than SODL and below CanICA, performs better than SODL and thresholded CanICA on behavioral prediction, and provides advantages in small-sample regimes. Prior work on structured decompositions (TV-l1, GraphNet, structured sparse PCA) and online factorization is discussed; the novelty is integrating spatial regularization into an online DL algorithm with convergence guarantees and practical scalability.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The model and online updates are natural extensions of Mairal et al. with a Laplacian regularizer on atoms. The atom update reduces to minimizing a quadratic with Laplacian over a convex set, for which projected accelerated methods are appropriate. The use of ridge codes stabilizes online updates. Convergence claims rest on assumptions of [16], but the paper performs inexact inner updates (“1–2 iterations suffice”), which warrants more care: Mairal’s analysis typically assumes exact minimization of the surrogate or bounded errors satisfying summability conditions. Please clarify whether your inner solver is run to a prescribed accuracy, and relate to inexact block coordinate descent guarantees; as stated, the “1–2 iterations to 1e-6 accuracy” claim is not credible for general FISTA and should be corrected.
- Algorithmic details: The Lipschitz constant derivation L = 1 + γ̃ λmax(Δ) ≈ 1 + 4D γ̃ is reasonable for standard grid Laplacians. The discussion that a fixed γ may lead to very small steps late (via At[j,j]/t) is insightful; however, using time-varying γt to compensate changes the objective and may affect convergence/interpretability. Empirically, what schedule was used? Did you keep γ fixed in the reported experiments?
- Experiments: The evaluation suite is thoughtful and largely supports claims on interpretability, stability, and small-sample performance. However, several design choices need clarification to ensure fairness and reproducibility:
  - CanICA is run on 6mm-smoothed data, whereas SODL/Smooth-SODL use raw data, but Smooth-SODL includes an implicit spatial smoother. For fairness, please also report SODL on 6mm-smoothed inputs, and CanICA on raw inputs, or justify the preprocessing mismatch.
  - Behavioral prediction: specify the predictive model (linear/ridge/elastic net?), CV protocol, and how feature selection for RAW (top 2000 voxels) was performed to avoid leakage (selection must be inside training folds). Include confidence intervals.
  - Stability: define how atoms were matched (you mention Hungarian), whether signs were handled, and how correlations were computed (whole volume vs. masked).
  - Learning curves: provide the exact training/test split, how dictionaries are trained (train only), and how EV is computed on held-out subjects.
- Empirical support: Reported EV (train/test) and stability numbers (Smooth-SODL ~0.4 vs SODL/CanICA ~0.1–0.2) support the claim that spatial regularization improves reproducibility. The small-sample advantage is compelling. Behavioral prediction shows CanICA best overall, while Smooth-SODL > SODL/tCanICA; this nuance is fairly acknowledged.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-structured but contains numerous typos (e.g., “intepretable,” “xperiments”), and some notational/terminological inconsistencies:
- The objective uses lim n→∞; more standard is an expectation over the data distribution or an empirical average up to time t. Rewrite for clarity.
- Specify boundary conditions and normalization for the Laplacian, and whether images are masked/standardized.
- Clarify whether C is an l1 ball or a simplex; discuss effect on sign constraints (simplex implies non-negativity).
- Provide pseudo-code indicating the projection operator used for C in the presence of the Laplacian (projected gradient).
- The claim “1–2 FISTA iterations suffice to reach 1e-6” should be revised.

Originality
The combination of spatial Laplacian regularization on atoms with online dictionary learning and efficient atom updates is a modest but meaningful contribution. Prior structured decompositions (TV-l1, GraphNet, structured sparse PCA) have addressed spatial structure but generally in batch or slow alternating frameworks; bringing spatially structured atom learning into an online regime with small overhead is valuable. The relation to Mensch et al. (2016) and whether their massive matrix factorization techniques could incorporate similar structure could be discussed more deeply.

Significance
The approach addresses a practical need in neuroimaging: interpretable, stable, and scalable decompositions for ROI/atlas learning across subjects. The gains in stability and small-sample performance, and the qualitative improvements in spatial coherence, are likely to be useful to practitioners. While CanICA still wins on explained variance and some prediction tasks, Smooth-SODL offers a better interpretability–fidelity trade-off with competitive test EV and prediction. The implementation plan in Nilearn further increases impact.

Strengths
- Clear and sensible modeling: sparse, smooth atoms; stable ridge codes.
- Scalable online algorithm with modest overhead; practical runtimes on large p, n.
- Comprehensive evaluation including stability, interpretability, EV, prediction, and runtimes.
- Empirical evidence that spatial regularization improves stability and small-sample performance.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Convergence/inexact updates: formalize the inexact inner-loop guarantees or run atoms to a prescribed tolerance; correct the accuracy claim.
- Fairness in comparisons: include SODL on smoothed inputs; provide CanICA on raw as an ablation.
- More ablations: γ=0 vs γ>0, effect of τ and k; show sensitivity plots (you mention Fig. 1 but details are sparse).
- Behavioral prediction: detail the model and CV; provide CIs; consider additional targets and nuisance regression.
- Minor theory: discuss the effect of time-varying αt and γt on the limiting objective and convergence.
- Clarity and typos: clean up language; define all operators and constraints precisely.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Practical, scalable method that improves interpretability and stability with modest cost.
- Solid empirical evaluation on a large, relevant dataset.
- Likely to be adopted (Nilearn integration).

Cons:
- Theoretical/convergence claims need tightening given inexact updates.
- Some fairness and reproducibility issues in experiments (preprocessing mismatch, limited details).
- Incremental contribution relative to existing DL frameworks.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying/constraining the inner-loop convergence claims, improving experimental fairness/details, and revising clarity/typos.
