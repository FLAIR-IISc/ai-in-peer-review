Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Smooth-SODL, an online dictionary learning method for multivariate brain images that enforces two properties on dictionary atoms: sparsity (via an ℓ1-type constraint on each atom) and spatial structure (via a Sobolev/Laplacian penalty). The codes are obtained by ridge regression given the current dictionary, while dictionary updates are performed per-atom using block coordinate descent with a projected (F)ISTA step for a constrained quadratic with Laplacian smoothing. The approach extends online sparse dictionary learning (Mairal et al., 2010) by adding spatial regularization directly on atoms while keeping scalability. The authors relate the per-atom update to GraphNet-style quadratic smoothness and discuss FFT-based acceleration. Empirically, on HCP task fMRI (n=500, p≈2.6×10^5), Smooth-SODL yields more interpretable, blob-like atoms, improves stability and early-regime generalization over vanilla SODL, and competes with ICA/PCA on explained variance and behavior prediction. The work is positioned against TV-ℓ1 decompositions for atlases (e.g., Abraham et al.; Varoquaux et al.), structured sparsity/PCA (Jenatton et al.), and massive-scale DL (Mensch et al.). It argues that prior structured decompositions were slow batch methods, whereas Smooth-SODL integrates spatial structure into an online solver with modest extra cost.

Strengths
- Addresses a practical and important need in neuroimaging: data-driven regions/atoms that are sparse, smooth, and reproducible, suitable for population-level analyses.
- Clear algorithmic integration of Laplacian smoothing into the online DL framework with per-atom BCD and projected accelerated gradient; convergence properties rely on known assumptions and are discussed.
- Sensible discussion of hyperparameters, especially interaction between Laplacian weight γ and stability/complexity; provides a heuristic schedule for α and guidance on τ and γ.
- Empirical evidence on a large, public dataset; compares to relevant baselines (SODL, CanICA), reports runtime, EV, stability, and downstream prediction.
- The “small data” learning curve analysis is insightful: spatial regularization improves sample efficiency markedly before asymptotic parity.
- Implementation plans in Nilearn increase impact and reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty is incremental: adding a Laplacian quadratic to atoms and solving with projected gradient is a straightforward extension; the key contribution is engineering and scalability rather than new theory.
- The constrained update (quadratic + Laplacian subject to ℓ1-ball/simplex) is described at a high level. Details of the projected FISTA variant (choice of projection, backtracking, stopping) and boundary conditions/masking for ∆ in 3D brains are deferred or implicit. Since masks break FFT assumptions, please clarify when FFT acceleration is valid and what is used in the main experiments.
- The claim that “1–2 FISTA iterations suffice to reach 1e-6 accuracy” seems contradictory; 1–2 iterations typically cannot yield such small subproblem optimality gaps in p≈10^5. Perhaps the intended meaning is that very few iterations suffice for good downstream performance. Please quantify the residuals and the effect of inexactness on overall objective decrease.
- γ scheduling: Eq. (9) proposes γt scaling to avoid vanishing step sizes, yet the experiments use fixed γ=10^3/10^4. Please reconcile: is γ fixed in practice? If so, under what data/code energy ranges is the Lipschitz blow-up not a concern?
- Baseline fairness: CanICA was run with 6mm smoothing and both raw and hard-thresholded variants are reported, but SODL and Smooth-SODL were not given the option of (pre-)smoothing; conversely, CanICA could benefit from the same spatial Laplacian post-hoc regularization. A stronger baseline would be a batch TV-ℓ1 or GraphNet-regularized decomposition (even at smaller scale) and/or spatial ICA variants.
- Quantitative reporting is incomplete in places (e.g., the referenced Table 1 is not included; precise stability numbers beyond mean correlations would help). Significance testing for differences in EV and R^2 would strengthen claims.
- The code learning uses ridge (ℓ2) rather than sparse codes; this choice is reasonable for stability, but an ablation comparing ℓ1 vs ℓ2 codes (and mixed elastic nets) for the same spatially-regularized atoms would isolate where gains come from.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with algorithmic choices backed by known convex optimization results. Some theoretical claims (convergence under inexact updates, γ scheduling) would benefit from tighter statements or empirical ablations. Experimental design is generally solid but could include additional structured baselines and statistical tests.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well-organized, with helpful motivations and practical guidance. A few derivations are compact; adding explicit projected FISTA pseudocode for the atom update, and clarifying Laplacian implementation (masking, boundary conditions, normalization) would improve reproducibility. Please proof-read for style and minor typographical issues.

Originality
Moderate. The integration of Laplacian regularization on atoms into an online DL framework is useful and, to my knowledge, not previously demonstrated at this scale in neuroimaging. However, the ingredients (online DL, Laplacian/GraphNet smoothing, projected first-order methods) are established.

Significance
High practical significance for neuroimaging workflows seeking interpretable, scalable decompositions; likely to be adopted (especially via Nilearn). Scientific novelty is moderate, but the demonstrated gains in interpretability, stability, and sample efficiency are valuable.

Questions for the authors
- What boundary conditions and brain mask handling are used for ∆? Is ∆ normalized (e.g., scaled Laplacian)? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Do you use projected FISTA with fixed step size 1/L or backtracking? What stopping criterion and typical iteration counts are used per atom update?
- How were γ and τ selected in the main experiments (CV grid? ranges)? Can you report sensitivity curves and stability/EV trade-offs?
- Could you provide results with ℓ1 codes (and/or elastic net) to quantify the impact of code sparsity vs atom smoothness?
- What is the memory footprint as k, p, and batch size increase? Any issues for p>10^6?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a structured baseline (e.g., batch TV-ℓ1 DL on a subset; GraphNet-regularized PCA/ICA) and/or spatial ICA for completeness.
- Report statistical significance and confidence intervals for EV, stability, and behavior prediction.
- Include ablations: effect of k, γ, τ, α schedule; number of FISTA iterations; with/without FFT; with/without pre-smoothing.
- Clarify and, if possible, justify theoretically the proposed γ schedule vs fixed γ; provide a practical recipe.
- Provide details sufficient to reproduce the Laplacian operator on masked 3D volumes.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and timely problem in a scalable, practical way.
- Demonstrated improvements in interpretability and stability with modest computational overhead.
- Solid empirical validation on a large public dataset; open-source implementation promised.

Arguments against acceptance
- Methodological novelty is limited; key components are known.
- Some implementation and evaluation details are missing; baselines could be stronger; incomplete statistical analysis.

Overall, I view this as a valuable, well-motivated engineering contribution with clear utility for the community, provided the authors strengthen clarity on the Laplacian update, parameterization, and baseline comparisons.
