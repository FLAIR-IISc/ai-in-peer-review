This paper studies the expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the set of mean parameters in a Gaussian mixture model. The number of Gaussian components, the mixture weights, and the covariance matrices are assumed known and fixed. The work is theoretical and establishes three negative results for EM: 1) Even with large sample sizes, the likelihood can have local maxima whose values are arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum. 2) With random initialization, EM converges with high probability to a critical point whose likelihood is arbitrarily below the maximal likelihood. 3) An analogous negative result holds for the gradient EM algorithm.

I find the question addressed by the paper interesting and worthwhile. To obtain rigorous proofs, the paper focuses on a simplified Gaussian mixture setting: the number of components is known, the weights are known and uniform, and the covariances equal the identity. I do not view these restrictions as problematic, given the technical nature of the arguments and the informativeness of the resulting conclusions. The results are, in my opinion, very interesting and useful for improving our practical understanding of EM.

It is worth noting that the paper is presented as covering general dimension d, while the proofs are carried out for d = 1. It would be helpful if the authors could clarify whether similar proofs are feasible for arbitrary d and, if not, briefly outline the additional challenges that arise when d > 1.

My main concern pertains to the proof of Theorem 1 (I did not read the supplementary proofs). I find this proof too brief and would have preferred a longer treatment, possibly at the expense of condensing some discussion elsewhere. The segment I believe needs more detail is lines 288–306. The authors take limits (as gamma or R tends to infinity) of suprema of likelihoods over certain domains. I have no objection to limits of the likelihood evaluated at fixed parameter values, so I accept that the three equations on line 288 should be correct. However, passing to the limit of a maximum is not straightforward when the domain is unbounded or depends on parameters that themselves diverge. Similarly, while the likelihood is continuous, this alone does not, in my view, justify the convergence statements made. More detail—and thus a longer proof—would be needed to fully convince the reader of the validity of Theorem 1.

Finally, I have a minor concern with the section on Gradient EM (lines 162–172). I found this part harder to follow than the rest. In particular, it would help if the authors could provide more explanation of how Equation (5) is derived.
