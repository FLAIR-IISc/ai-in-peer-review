The paper studies the convergence of the EM algorithm and iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation in learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, presenting several key results that demonstrate non-convergence to global maxima. Specifically, the authors show that: (1) even with infinite samples and well-separated clusters (consisting of three components), algorithms like EM can still get stuck in local optima; (2) for mixtures of k Gaussians, random initialization for EM only succeeds with at most exp(-Ω(k)) probability; and (3) Gradient EM will not converge to strict saddle points generically, implying that bad local maxima are typically the issue. The paper presents a coherent set of results that demonstrate the failure of EM to converge to global maxima, even with infinite samples.

However, I found the results to be somewhat intuitive, and the techniques employed follow expected lines. The main counterexample presented is a mixture with three components, where two are closer to each other, and the third is far away from both. The authors show that for the log-likelihood objective, there exists a local maximum with one center near the first two components and two centers near the far-off cluster. This behavior is fairly expected, particularly when the centers are separated by a distance significantly greater than √d. Under such separation, learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians becomes similar in nature to k-means. Similar examples have been used to demonstrate bad instances of Lloyd's algorithm for k-means clustering. The authors extend this example to k components using a recursive construction, which, although requiring some effort, is somewhat expected. Similarly, the failure of random initialization can be attributed to similar reasons as in k-means clustering, with the same failure probability. This has motivated the use of alternative initialization methods, such as choosing k log k centers or distance-squared sampling.

In conclusion, while the results present a coherent story about the non-convergence to global optima, I feel that they are largely along expected lines. 

Comments: 
1. A comparison with k-means (and Lloyd's heuristic) would be useful, as Kumar-Kannan provides convergence guarantees for Lloyd's heuristic for k-means clustering (and hence mixtures of Gaussians under sufficient separation conditions). Although EM is different from Lloyd's heuristic when the Gaussians are not sufficiently separated (as in Balakrishnan-Wainwright-Yu), they seem equivalent when there is large separation (which is true of the bad examples presented here).
