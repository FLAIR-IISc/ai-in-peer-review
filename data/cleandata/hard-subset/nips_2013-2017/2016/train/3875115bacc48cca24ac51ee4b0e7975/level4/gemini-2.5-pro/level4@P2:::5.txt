This paper addresses a significant open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of spurious local maxima in the population log-likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The authors provide a definitive negative answer by constructing a class of counterexamples. The core findings are: (1) for any number of components M ≥ 3, the population log-likelihood of a well-separated, spherical, uniform GMM can possess local maxima; (2) the log-likelihood values at these local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than that of the global maximum; and (3) standard algorithms like Expectation-Maximization (EM) and a first-order variant, when started from a common random initialization scheme, will converge to these suboptimal critical points with a probability that approaches 1 exponentially fast in M. The paper also establishes that for the first-order variant of EM, this failure is attributable to bad local maxima rather than strict saddle points.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is resolving a long-standing and important open question in the theory of mixture models and non-convex optimization. This finding is significant as it formally challenges the optimistic view that the population likelihood for even simple, well-behaved GMMs might be free of problematic local optima. The results have broad implications, highlighting the fundamental necessity of careful initialization for local search algorithms like EM, even in idealized, infinite-sample settings.

*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results appear sound and are rigorously presented. The construction of the counterexample is insightful. Crucially, the authors go beyond a mere existence proof to analyze the algorithmic consequences for EM, which is the de-facto standard for fitting GMMs. Showing that a natural random initialization scheme fails with exponentially high probability is a powerful and impactful result.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating their work within the existing literature, and clearly stating their main contributions. The intuitive explanation of the construction for the M=3 case is particularly helpful for conveying the core idea behind the main theorem.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper's main theoretical results are asymptotic in the number of mixture components, M. While powerful, it is unclear from the current analysis at what scale of M these guarantees translate into observable phenomena in practice, where M is often small or moderate. The practical impact of the paper could be strengthened by addressing this point.

*   The probability of converging to a bad critical point is given as being at least 1 - e⁻ᶜᴹ. It would be highly valuable if the authors could provide some analysis or estimation of the universal constant 'c'. This would offer insight into the regime (or "phase transition") where random initializations begin to fail with high probability. For instance, is failure a concern for M=5 or M=50? Quantifying this would help practitioners understand the practical relevance of this failure mode.

*   If an analytical treatment of the constants is too complex, the paper would be significantly strengthened by including simulation results. An experiment demonstrating the empirical frequency of convergence to suboptimal maxima for the constructed GMM family as M increases would provide valuable validation for the theory. Such a simulation could powerfully illustrate that this is not just a theoretical curiosity but a phenomenon that can be observed in practice.

**Summary:**

This is a strong and impactful paper that makes a fundamental contribution to our understanding of the GMM likelihood landscape and the behavior of the EM algorithm. The results are novel, significant, and clearly presented. The suggestions above are intended to further strengthen the paper's connection to practical scenarios but do not detract from the core theoretical achievements. I strongly recommend acceptance.
