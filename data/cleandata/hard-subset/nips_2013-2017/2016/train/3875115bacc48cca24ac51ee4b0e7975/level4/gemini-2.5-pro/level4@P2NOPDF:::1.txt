This paper studies the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians. The authors present a series of results demonstrating that EM and related iterative heuristics are not guaranteed to converge to the global maximum of the likelihood function, even in idealized settings. The key findings are:

1.  In the infinite sample setting, even for a simple mixture of three well-separated clusters, algorithms like EM can converge to a suboptimal local maximum.
2.  For mixtures of k Gaussians, a standard random initialization strategy for EM will find the global optimum with a probability that is exponentially small in k, succeeding with at most `exp(-\Omega(k))` probability.
3.  The analysis suggests that gradient-based EM variants will generically avoid strict saddle points, implying that convergence to poor local maxima, rather than getting stuck at saddle points, is the primary failure mode.

The paper presents a coherent and self-contained set of results that highlight fundamental limitations of EM. However, the primary weakness of the work is that the core findings, while rigorously proven, may not be entirely surprising to the community. The analysis and the failure modes identified largely follow expected lines and mirror well-known issues in the related problem of k-means clustering.

The central counterexample involves a mixture of three components where two are relatively close to each other and a third is very distant. The authors demonstrate the existence of a local maximum where one learned center is placed near the two close true components, while two learned centers are assigned to the single distant true component. This behavior is intuitively expected in settings with large component separation (i.e., when inter-center distances are significantly larger than `\sqrt{d}`). Under such conditions, the mixture components have minimal overlap, and the maximum likelihood estimation problem for spherical Gaussians behaves almost identically to the k-means clustering objective. Consequently, this failure mode is analogous to known difficult instances for Lloyd's algorithm for k-means. The paper's extension of this example to k components via a recursive construction is technically sound, but since the underlying principle remains the same, the result is similarly foreseeable.

Likewise, the analysis showing that random initialization fails with high probability aligns with established results for k-means clustering. The derived failure probability is analogous to that for k-means, which historically motivated the development of more sophisticated initialization schemes like k-means++ (distance-squared sampling) or the strategy of selecting `O(k log k)` initial centers.

In summary, while the paper provides rigorous proofs and a clear narrative about the non-convergence of EM to global optima, the insights feel incremental. The failure modes demonstrated are well-understood in the context of k-means, a problem that becomes almost indistinguishable from learning well-separated spherical Gaussian mixtures.

**Comments for the Authors:**

The connection to k-means and Lloyd's algorithm is a recurring theme, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing this relationship more explicitly.

Under the large separation conditions assumed in the paper's negative examples, the EM algorithm for spherical Gaussians closely resembles Lloyd's heuristic. This is a different regime from settings with smaller separation, where the algorithms are known to behave differently (as explored in work by Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu). Given this similarity in the high-separation case, a discussion of the existing literature on k-means convergence is essential for context. For instance, the work by Kumar and Kannan provides convergence guarantees for Lloyd's heuristic. A detailed comparison that contrasts your results with the findings for k-means would help clarify the specific contributions of this paper beyond what is already understood from the clustering literature.
