This paper studies the existence and impact of bad local maxima in the (population) log-likelihood of well-specified Gaussian mixture models with isotropic components and known variance. The authors construct configurations of true centers for which the likelihood has suboptimal local maxima separated from the global optimum by an arbitrarily large gap, and they show that EM with random initialization will, with high probability, converge to a bad local maximum as the number of components grows. The results are presented for carefully designed cluster configurations (e.g., centers grouped into widely separated clusters).

Strengths
- Clear theoretical focus on the population (infinite-sample) likelihood, removing finite-sample noise as a confounder.
- Crisp negative results: arbitrarily bad local maxima exist even under the simplest, well-specified settings.
- Theorems provide sharp intuition about how grouping of centers into well-separated clusters induces spurious attraction basins for EM.
- Writing is generally clear and proofs seem careful; the paper highlights an important limitation of widely used methods.

Primary concerns about practical relevance
1) Likelihood of “bad” center configurations. Theorem 1 establishes that for a particular arrangement of true centers, the population likelihood has a bad local mode. However, it is not clear how likely such arrangements are under realistic generative assumptions. To strengthen the practical relevance, I would encourage:
- Stating a model for the random placement of the true centers (e.g., i.i.d. from a distribution with bounded covariance, or from a mixture with well-separated cluster means), and quantifying the probability that a “bad” configuration occurs as k or d grows.
- Alternatively, a smoothed analysis viewpoint: how robust are the constructions to small random perturbations of the true centers?
- A formal statement clarifying the measure (or asymptotic frequency) of these “bad” configurations under specified assumptions. Lines 210–218 indicate Theorem 2 addresses this, but I do not see a clear probabilistic bound on the occurrence of the problematic true-center configurations (as opposed to bad initializations).

2) Practicality of random initialization. Theorem 2 analyzes EM initialized by sampling centers from the data distribution, and shows high failure probability under certain center configurations. I worry this understates how practitioners initialize mixture models when the data are clearly clustered. For example, in a setting with three Gaussians, two close and one far, and with balanced sample sizes N per component, it seems unlikely in practice to place two initial centers in the sparse, distant cluster and only one in the denser merged region. Common heuristics such as k-means++, farthest-first, or multiple restarts tend to allocate more initial centers to denser or larger regions, especially when clusters are well-separated. It would help to:
- Discuss whether the negative result persists with k-means++ or farthest-first initialization, or with a modest number of random restarts.
- Clarify whether the failure is driven by a mismatch between the number of true subclusters within a well-separated group and the number of initial centers seeded there, and whether simple density-aware initializations avoid this pathology.

Specific questions and requests for clarification
- L74–81: What is the purpose of this example? It does not convincingly demonstrate Srebro’s conjecture for k=2. If it serves a different purpose (e.g., illustrating a subtlety of EM dynamics), please state that explicitly or streamline the example.
- L233–235: I understand requirement (2), but the necessity of requirement (1) is not clear. Is (1) used to prevent an initial center from drifting between clusters or to avoid symmetry-induced ambiguity? A brief lemma or counterexample explaining why (1) is needed would help.
- L267–273: You show EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point. Why is this practically important relative to the risk of converging to a bad local maximum? If almost all failures are due to attraction to suboptimal local maxima, this distinction could be stated clearly and its implications discussed.

Additional suggestions
- Scope and robustness. Please comment on extensions: unknown variance, anisotropic covariances, non-uniform mixing weights, or mild model misspecification. Do similar negative phenomena persist, or are they artifacts of the simplest isotropic-known-variance setting?
- Quantitative statements. Wherever possible, make failure probabilities explicit as functions of k, d, separation, and sample size (when moving from population to finite-sample EM).
- Empirical illustration. A small synthetic experiment measuring the frequency of bad configurations and EM failure under random placements of true centers would complement the theory.

Overall assessment
The paper delivers sharp impossibility results for EM (and for the likelihood landscape itself) in well-specified Gaussian mixtures, highlighting that even in extremely favorable settings, optimization can be treacherous. The main limitation is the gap between the worst-case constructions and typical practice. Providing probabilistic statements about the prevalence of bad true-center configurations, and discussing the robustness of the negative results to practical initializations, would significantly strengthen the paper’s practical relevance without diluting its theoretical contributions.
