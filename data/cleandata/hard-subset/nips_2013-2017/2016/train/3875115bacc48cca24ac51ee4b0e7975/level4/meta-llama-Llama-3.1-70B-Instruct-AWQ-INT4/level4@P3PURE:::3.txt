The paper explores the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models, focusing on the simplest possible mixture model: isotropic Gaussian components with known scale, where the model is well-specified. The authors demonstrate that local modes exist, even with infinite data, and that these modes can be arbitrarily bad in terms of the difference in log-likelihood between the local and global optima. They concentrate on particular configurations, specifically cases where the mixture centers are divided between well-spaced clusters. Under such configurations, the authors show that random initialization will cause the EM algorithm to miss the global optima almost certainly, as the number of components increases.

The paper is well-written, with occasional typos. However, I have two main concerns regarding the practical relevance of the paper. Firstly, Theorem 1 shows that the log-likelihood has local modes, even with infinite data, when a particular configuration of the centers is chosen. My question is: how likely is this configuration to occur in practice? I would feel more confident about the practical relevance of the paper if the authors made some assumptions about the distribution of the centers and then demonstrated that "bad" configurations become increasingly likely as the number of components (k) or the dimensionality of the problem increases. Lines 210 to 218 of the paper suggest that Theorem 2 addresses these concerns, but I am not convinced that it does. It would be beneficial to have a clear statement about the likelihood of "bad" configurations under some assumptions. Note that I am referring to "bad" configurations (positions of the true centers), not "bad" initializations.

Secondly, in Theorem 2, the initialization of the EM algorithm plays a crucial role. The authors consider initializing the centers of the components by randomly sampling the mixture. With this initialization, and under specific configurations of the centers, the EM algorithm often fails to converge to the global optimum. However, let us consider the case where the data is generated using three Gaussians, two of which are close and one far apart. If we have the same number N of data-points from each Gaussian, then 2N points will fall close to the first cluster, and N will fall close to the third density. It seems unusual that someone would initialize the EM algorithm by placing two centers where there are only N points and one center where there are N observations, especially given that the two clusters are well-separated from each other. Is random initialization practically relevant when the data is clearly clustered?

I also have some minor issues with specific lines in the paper. On lines 74-81, I am not sure what the example is meant to convey. It does not convince me that the Srebro conjecture holds when k = 2, so I question its purpose. On lines 233-235, I understand requirement (2), but I do not see why requirement (1) is necessary. It appears that initializing a center between two clusters does not guarantee that it will move toward the correct cluster. On lines 267-273, the authors prove that the EM algorithm is unlikely to converge to a saddle point. From a practical perspective, I am not sure why this result is important. Is converging to a saddle point more or less problematic than converging to a local optimum?
