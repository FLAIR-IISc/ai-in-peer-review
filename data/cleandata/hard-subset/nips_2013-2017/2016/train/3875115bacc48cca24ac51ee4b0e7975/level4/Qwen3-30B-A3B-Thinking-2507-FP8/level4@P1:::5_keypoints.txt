- Resolves Srebro (2007) open question by constructing counterexamples showing bad local maxima exist in the population likelihood for GMMs when M ≥ 3.  
- Constructs well-separated, equal-weighted spherical Gaussian mixtures whose population log-likelihood has local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.  
- Two main contributions: (1) structural proof of existence of bad local maxima under favorable conditions; (2) algorithmic consequence that EM often converges to suboptimal points.  
- Shows EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 - e^{-cM}.  
- Proves failure is due to bad local maxima (not saddle points) by showing first-order EM avoids strict saddles almost surely (Theorem 4).  
- Provides clear, well-structured presentation with concise overview and precise statements of results.  
- Effective proof intuition for M=3 (d=1), illustrating "collapsed" initializations that trap EM in suboptimal maxima.  
- Technical arguments are rigorous and avoid unnecessary smoothness assumptions via tailored analysis of first-order EM updates.  
- Practical implication: random initialization fails exponentially often in M, necessitating careful initialization strategies (e.g., method-of-moments).  
- Advances understanding beyond prior M=2 local convergence results, revealing an inherent limitation of local search for M ≥ 3.  
- Aligns with recent non-convex optimization theory by identifying poor local optima as the main obstacle rather than saddles.  
- Meets NIPS quality criteria: sound proofs, clarity, originality, and significance; recommends acceptance.  
- Minor suggestion: explicitly discuss the computational cost of repeated random restarts needed to obtain constant success probability (e^{Ω(M)}).
