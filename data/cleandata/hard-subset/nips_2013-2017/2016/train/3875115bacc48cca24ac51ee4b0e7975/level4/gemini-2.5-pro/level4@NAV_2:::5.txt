This paper investigates the structure of the population log-likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), directly addressing an open question posed by Srebro [2007] regarding the existence of spurious local maxima. The authors provide a definitive negative answer by constructing a class of counterexamples. The paper's first main contribution is to show that for any number of components M ≥ 3, the population log-likelihood of a well-separated, spherical, and uniformly-weighted GMM can possess local maxima whose values are arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. The second key contribution explores the algorithmic consequences of this structural property. The authors prove that the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and a first-order variant, when initialized with a standard random sampling scheme, will converge to a suboptimal critical point with a probability that is exponentially high in M (at least 1 - e⁻ᶜᴹ). Finally, the paper establishes that the first-order EM variant will almost surely not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that bad local maxima, rather than saddle points, are the primary cause of algorithmic failure in this setting.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper resolves a notable and long-standing open problem in the theoretical analysis of GMMs. The finding that even idealized GMM likelihood landscapes possess bad local maxima is a fundamental result with significant implications for the broader field of non-convex optimization in statistical learning.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical results are strong and the connection between the structural properties of the likelihood function and the algorithmic consequences for EM is a major strength. The analysis goes beyond a mere existence proof for bad local maxima by quantifying the high probability with which a standard algorithm like EM fails, making the result particularly impactful. The additional result isolating local maxima as the primary obstacle over strict saddle points adds further depth.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly presents the main theorems and provides excellent intuition for the underlying proofs, for instance by discussing the geometric construction for the M=3 case. This makes a set of complex theoretical results accessible and compelling.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper's theoretical results are powerful, but their direct implications are primarily asymptotic in M, the number of mixture components. To better contextualize the work for practitioners, who often use GMMs with moderate values of M, it would be valuable to provide more insight into the non-asymptotic regime.
*   Could the authors provide an estimate or analysis of the constant `c` in the failure probability term 1 - e⁻ᶜᴹ? This would give a clearer picture of the scale of M at which the high probability of failure becomes a practical concern.
*   If a precise analysis of the constants is too complex, the inclusion of a targeted simulation study would greatly strengthen the paper. Empirically demonstrating that randomly initialized EM fails on the constructed GMM instances for moderate M would provide concrete evidence for the practical relevance of the theoretical findings and could help visualize the "phase transition" where failure becomes likely.
