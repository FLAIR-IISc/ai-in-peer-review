This paper studies the expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the set of mean parameters in a mixture of Gaussian variables. The number of components, the mixture weights, and the covariance matrices are known and fixed; in fact, the analysis specializes to the case of uniform weights and identity covariances. The paper is theoretical and establishes three negative results for EM: (1) even with large sample sizes, the likelihood can have local maxima whose values are arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum; (2) with random initialization, EM converges with high probability to a critical point whose likelihood is arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum; and (3) the same negative phenomenon occurs for gradient EM. The question addressed is important and deserves attention. Given the technical nature of the arguments, I find the simplifying assumptions reasonable, and the results themselves are informative and practically relevant for understanding EM.

One point of scope: the paper is framed for general dimension d, whereas the proofs focus on d = 1. It would help if the authors clarified whether analogous proofs extend to arbitrary dimension, and if not, briefly described the additional challenges for d > 1.

My main concern is the proof of Theorem 1 (I did not consult the supplementary proofs). The proof as written feels too compressed; I would prefer a more detailed argument, even at the expense of shortening some of the surrounding discussion. In particular, the steps from lines 288 to 306 require more justification. The authors take limits (as gamma or R tend to infinity) of suprema of likelihoods over certain domains. I have no issue with pointwise limits of the likelihood evaluated at fixed parameter values; for example, the three equations on line 288 appear correct. However, passing to limits of maxima (or suprema) is nontrivial when the optimization domains are unbounded or themselves depend on the diverging parameters. Similarly, continuity of the likelihood, while true, does not by itself yield the types of convergence claims made. To fully convince the reader of Theorem 1, I recommend expanding this part to justify the interchanges of limit and supremum, the behavior on unbounded/expanding domains, and any needed compactness or tightness arguments.

A minor concern is the section on gradient EM (lines 162–172), which I found harder to follow than the rest. It would help to provide more explanation on how Equation (5) is derived and to clarify any assumptions used in that step.

Overall, despite these issues, I find the results interesting and potentially impactful for our understanding of EM’s behavior in practice. With a strengthened proof of Theorem 1 and some added clarity around gradient EM, the paper would be significantly improved.
