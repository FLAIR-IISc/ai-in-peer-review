This paper investigates the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm in the context of estimating the mean parameters of a Gaussian mixture model. Critically, the paper assumes that the number of Gaussian components, the mixture weights, and the covariance matrices are known and fixed, allowing for a focused theoretical analysis. The authors present three significant negative results concerning the EM algorithm: first, that local maxima can exist for the likelihood function even with large sample sizes, and these maxima can have arbitrarily low likelihood values; second, that a randomly initialized EM algorithm converges with high probability to a critical point yielding a suboptimal likelihood; and third, that these results also hold for the gradient EM algorithm. 

I find the question addressed by this paper to be both interesting and important. Understanding the limitations of the EM algorithm, even in simplified settings, is crucial for its practical application. The paper’s rigorous approach, while necessitating simplifications to the model – specifically, a known number of components, uniform mixture weights, and identity covariance matrices – is reasonable. Given the technical complexity of the proofs, these simplifications do not detract significantly from the value of the results, which remain informative about the fundamental behavior of EM. These findings have the potential to improve our understanding of EM's practical performance and inform the development of more robust optimization strategies.

However, the paper has areas that would benefit from clarification. While presented as generally applicable to dimension *d*, the proofs are confined to the case *d* = 1. The authors should briefly discuss the feasibility of extending their proofs to arbitrary dimension *d*. If such an extension is not possible, a concise explanation of the additional difficulties encountered in higher dimensions would be valuable.

My primary concern lies with the proof of Theorem 1. While I did not examine the supplementary material in detail, the proof presented in the main text (lines 288-306) feels somewhat condensed. Specifically, the justification for taking the supremum *after* taking the limit requires more careful explanation. While the calculations of likelihood function limits at fixed parameters appear correct (e.g., the three equations on line 288), establishing the limit of a supremum is not trivial, especially when dealing with unbounded domains or parameters approaching infinity. The authors state the likelihood function is continuous, but this alone does not guarantee the convergence properties claimed. A more detailed and expansive proof, potentially at the cost of shortening some of the introductory discussion, would greatly enhance the reader’s confidence in the theorem’s validity.

Finally, I found the discussion of the Gradient EM algorithm (lines 162-172) comparatively difficult to follow. Greater clarity in this section would be beneficial. In particular, a more detailed derivation or explanation of how Equation (5) is obtained would be helpful. The connection between the steps leading to this equation isn't immediately apparent, and expanding on this would improve the overall accessibility of the paper.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable, albeit technically challenging, contribution to the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding the proof of Theorem 1 and the Gradient EM discussion, would strengthen the paper considerably and increase its impact. Despite these concerns, I believe the core results are significant and worthy of publication.
