The paper studies the convergence of the EM algorithm for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, presenting several results that demonstrate non-convergence to global maxima. These findings include: (1) the EM algorithm can become stuck in local optima even with infinite samples when the mixture has three clusters that are very far apart; (2) for mixtures of k Gaussians, random initialization for EM only succeeds with a probability of at most exp(-Ω(k)); and (3) Gradient EM will not converge to strict saddle points generically, indicating that bad local maxima are typically the issue.

The paper presents a coherent set of results that show the EM algorithm does not converge to global maxima, even with infinite samples. However, the reviewer feels that these results are not particularly surprising, and the techniques used to obtain them follow expected lines. The main example used to demonstrate this is a mixture with three components, where two are closer to each other and the third is very far from both. The authors show that for the log-likelihood objective, there is a local maxima with one center near the first two components and two centers near the far-off cluster. This behavior is fairly expected, particularly when the centers are separated by a distance much greater than √d. Under this amount of separation, learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians becomes very similar to k-means clustering.

Similar examples have been used to demonstrate bad instances of Lloyd's algorithm for k-means clustering. The authors extend this example for k components using a recursive construction, which requires some effort but is again somewhat expected. Similarly, the failure of random initialization can be attributed to similar reasons as for k-means clustering, with the same failure probability. This serves as the motivation to pick k log k centers or use distance-squared sampling in k-means clustering.

In conclusion, while the results presented in the paper tell a coherent story about the non-convergence to global optima, the reviewer feels that this is along somewhat expected lines. A comparison with k-means clustering (and Lloyd's heuristic) that contrasts this result would be very useful. For instance, Kumar and Kannan provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd's heuristic for k-means clustering (and hence mixtures of Gaussians under sufficient separation conditions). While EM is different from Lloyd's heuristic when the Gaussians are not sufficiently separated, they seem essentially equivalent when there is large separation, as in the bad examples presented in the paper.

Comments:

1. A comparison with k-means clustering (and Lloyd's heuristic) would provide a more complete understanding of the results presented in the paper. This could include a discussion of how the EM algorithm differs from Lloyd's heuristic when the Gaussians are not sufficiently separated, and how the results presented in the paper relate to existing convergence guarantees for k-means clustering.

2. The authors may want to consider discussing the implications of their results for practical applications of the EM algorithm. For instance, how can the algorithm be modified or initialized to avoid getting stuck in local optima?

3. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work, particularly in the context of k-means clustering and Lloyd's heuristic. This would help to situate the results presented in the paper within the broader context of clustering algorithms and provide a clearer understanding of the contributions of the paper.
