This paper investigates the existence of spurious local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The analysis is focused on a simplified but important setting: a well-specified model with isotropic Gaussian components and known, shared covariance. The authors demonstrate that spurious local modes exist even in the infinite data limit (i.e., on the population log-likelihood) and that these modes can be arbitrarily poor, with log-likelihood values significantly different from the global optimum. The paper focuses on particular configurations where the true mixture centers are themselves grouped into well-separated clusters. Under these conditions, the authors show that a random initialization strategy for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm will almost certainly fail to find the global optimum as the number of components increases.

The paper is well-written and addresses a fundamentally important problem in mixture model estimation. However, I have two main reservations regarding the practical implications of the results.

1.  **On the Likelihood of "Bad" Parameter Configurations:**
    Theorem 1 shows that the population log-likelihood has spurious local modes when the true mixture centers are arranged in a specific configuration. My primary question is: how likely is this type of configuration to occur in practice? The paper's practical relevance would be significantly strengthened if the authors could provide an analysis based on some distributional assumptions over the true centers. For instance, could it be shown that the probability of encountering such "bad" configurations increases with the number of components (k) or the data dimensionality (d)? The discussion on lines L210-L218 suggests that Theorem 2 addresses these concerns, but that theorem seems to focus on the algorithm's initialization rather than the underlying data-generating process. A clear statement on the likelihood of these problematic parameter settings, under reasonable assumptions, would make the theoretical contribution much more compelling.

2.  **On the Relevance of the Initialization Scheme:**
    Theorem 2 demonstrates that under the previously mentioned "bad" configurations, EM often fails when its centers are initialized by randomly sampling from the mixture. While this is a standard initialization method to analyze, its practical relevance is questionable in the very settings the paper studies. Consider a scenario where data is generated by three Gaussians: two are close together, and one is far apart. If each component contributes an equal number of data points (N), the dataset will exhibit a large cluster of 2N points and a smaller, well-separated cluster of N points. In this case, it seems highly counter-intuitive that a practitioner would initialize two centers in the sparse region of N points and only one center in the dense region of 2N points, an outcome that is possible under the random sampling scheme. When data is clearly clustered, more sophisticated initializations (e.g., k-means++) are often employed. It would be helpful if the authors could discuss whether the identified failure mode is specific to this simple random initialization or if it extends to other, more practical initialization strategies.

**Questions and Clarifications:**

*   **L74-81:** The purpose of the example presented here is not entirely clear. It does not appear to fully convince the reader that Srebro's conjecture holds for k=2, so what is its main intended takeaway?
*   **L233-235:** I understand the need for requirement (2) for a "good" initialization (at least one initialized center per true cluster). However, I do not see why requirement (1) (no more than one initialized center per true component) is necessary. Could you please clarify this? Is the concern that if a center is initialized between two true components within the same cluster, it may not converge correctly?
*   **L267-273:** The proof that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point is a nice theoretical result. From a practical standpoint, could you elaborate on its importance? Is convergence to a saddle point considered more or less problematic than convergence to a poor local optimum?
