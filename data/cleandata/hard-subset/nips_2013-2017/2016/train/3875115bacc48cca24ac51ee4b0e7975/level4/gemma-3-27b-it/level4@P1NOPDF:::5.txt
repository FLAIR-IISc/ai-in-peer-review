The paper addresses the open question posed by Srebro in his 2007 work, “Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?” and provides a definitive negative answer through the construction of a general class of counterexamples. The authors begin by outlining standard methods for maximizing the likelihood in Gaussian Mixture Models, including the Expectation-Maximization (EM) and gradient-based EM algorithms. They also discuss the important consideration of initialization, providing a common and practical choice. The core of the paper lies in the authors’ primary theorems, which demonstrably refute Srebro’s conjecture [2007]. Furthermore, they demonstrate that even with this common random initialization strategy, both the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to non-optimal maxima with exponentially high probability. To aid intuition, the authors present a simplified case proof (k=3, d=1) illustrating the core concepts.

This paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, successfully balancing the constraints of space with the need to provide intuition for its theoretical results. The question addressed by the authors is fundamentally important for advancing our understanding of the challenges inherent in non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic limit and for relatively simple distributions. 

I did not perform a detailed review of the supplementary material, but providing estimates for the constants within the probability bounds would be valuable. This is particularly relevant because, in practice, the number of mixture components (k) is unlikely to be excessively large. Estimates of these constants would help determine when convergence to suboptimal solutions becomes significant in the asymptotic limit – essentially, revealing a phase transition. If obtaining closed-form expressions for these constants proves difficult or yields impractical results, supporting simulations demonstrating the observed phenomenon would be beneficial.

However, this point does not diminish the novelty or overall impact of the paper.
