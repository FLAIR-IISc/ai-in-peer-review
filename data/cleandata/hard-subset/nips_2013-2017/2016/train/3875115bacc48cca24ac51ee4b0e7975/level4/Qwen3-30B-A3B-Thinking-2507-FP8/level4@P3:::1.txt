

The paper addresses a fundamental open question regarding the likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), resolving Srebro’s conjecture (2007) that the population likelihood for well-separated GMMs has no bad local maxima. The authors establish three key results: (1) For M ≥ 3 equally-weighted, well-separated spherical Gaussians, the population log-likelihood contains local maxima with arbitrarily worse log-likelihood than the global optimum; (2) The EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to such suboptimal points with probability at least 1 − e^{−Ω(M)}; (3) First-order EM avoids strict saddle points almost surely, confirming that bad local maxima—not saddle points—are the primary cause of failure. These results rigorously demonstrate that careful initialization remains essential even in idealized settings (infinite samples, spherical Gaussians, strong separation).

**Strengths**  
The paper makes a significant contribution by resolving a long-standing open problem. Srebro’s conjecture has been widely cited in the literature (e.g., in Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and this work definitively shows its falsity. The structural result (Theorem 1) is elegant: the authors construct a counterexample where two close centers are collapsed to a single location while two centers occupy the third cluster, creating a local maximum with arbitrarily large loss relative to the global optimum. The algorithmic results (Theorems 2–4) are tightly argued, with the recursive construction for M components providing a compelling extension of the M=3 case. The proof that first-order EM avoids strict saddle points (Theorem 4) leverages recent advances in non-convex optimization (Lee et al., 2016) and adds theoretical depth by distinguishing between local maxima and saddle points as failure modes. The analysis is technically sound, with clear connections to prior work on GMM estimation (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Hsu & Kakade, 2013) and k-means clustering (e.g., Kumar & Kannan, 2004). The paper’s clarity is excellent: the problem setup, intuition, and proofs are well-motivated, and the recursive construction for Theorem 2 is particularly accessible.

**Weaknesses**  
While the results are technically rigorous, their novelty is somewhat limited by the context of existing literature. The counterexample for M=3 (two close centers, one far away) is intuitively expected under strong separation, as it mirrors known failure modes in k-means clustering (e.g., Lloyd’s algorithm can collapse clusters when centers are too close). The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion but could strengthen the contribution by more explicitly contrasting their results with k-means theory. For instance, Kumar & Kannan (2004) show that k-means with random initialization succeeds with high probability when centers are separated by Ω(√d), but their analysis assumes *fixed* initialization (e.g., k random points). Here, the failure probability scales exponentially in M, whereas k-means typically requires O(k log k) points for success (e.g., via distance-squared sampling). A direct comparison would clarify whether the exponential failure is unique to likelihood-based methods or a general feature of non-convex optimization under strong separation. Additionally, the paper’s practical implications could be emphasized more: while the results hold for infinite samples, they extend to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments (as noted in Section 4), yet the discussion of real-world relevance remains brief.

**Overall Assessment**  
This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that resolves an important open problem in GMM theory. The results are well-supported, the writing is clear, and the analysis advances our understanding of non-convex optimization in mixture models. While the core intuition is not entirely unexpected (given parallels to k-means), the paper provides the first rigorous proof for the population likelihood and establishes tight failure probabilities for EM. The work aligns with NIPS’s emphasis on sound theoretical contributions, and the significance is clear: it demonstrates that random initialization is insufficient for GMMs even under ideal conditions, necessitating careful design of initialization schemes (e.g., method-of-moments-based approaches like Moitra & Valiant, 2010). The paper’s technical depth and clarity make it suitable for NIPS, though the novelty is incremental rather than transformative.  

**Recommendation**  
Accept. The paper’s resolution of Srebro’s conjecture and its rigorous analysis of EM’s failure modes constitute a meaningful advance. The minor weakness in novelty is outweighed by the paper’s technical rigor, clarity, and direct relevance to a core problem in statistical learning. The authors’ suggestion to compare with k-means clustering would further strengthen the narrative, but the current work stands as a solid contribution to the literature.
