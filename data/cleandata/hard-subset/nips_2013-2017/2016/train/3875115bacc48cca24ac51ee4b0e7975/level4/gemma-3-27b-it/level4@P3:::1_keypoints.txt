- Studies EM convergence for GMMs and shows EM can converge to suboptimal solutions even with well-separated components and infinite samples.  
- Shows existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood for mixtures of three or more Gaussians, resolving Srebro (2007).  
- Proves the probability of successful convergence from random initialization vanishes exponentially with number of components M.  
- Demonstrates that bad local maxima, not saddle points, are the primary obstacle for first-order EM methods.  
- Paper is well-structured, cites relevant foundational and recent work, and places results in broader non-convex optimization context.  
- Mathematical exposition and proofs are rigorous and appear sound.  
- Strength: provides strong theoretical guarantees and resolves an open conjecture.  
- Strength: combines multiple complementary results (local maxima, initialization failure, role of saddles) for a comprehensive picture.  
- Strength: clear presentation makes technical content accessible.  
- Strength: results are relevant to many applications using GMMs and highlight the need for careful initialization.  
- Weakness: core findings (EM gets stuck in local optima, worse with more components) are not wholly surprising to experts.  
- Weakness: fails to explicitly connect to k-means/Lloyd’s heuristic and related convergence literature (e.g., Kumar & Kannan).  
- Weakness: analysis is limited to equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians, restricting generalizability to anisotropic or unequal-weight models.  
- Weakness: identifies problems but offers no concrete algorithmic remedies or novel initialization schemes.  
- Arguments for acceptance: mathematically sound, resolves an open problem, comprehensive and clearly presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty to specialists, narrow scope, and lack of mitigation strategies.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—add discussion of k-means connections, potential extensions to general GMMs, and avenues for improved initialization.
