- Main result: Population log-likelihood of GMMs with M ≥ 3 has arbitrarily bad local maxima even for uniformly weighted, spherical, well-separated mixtures, refuting Srebro (2007).
- Algorithmic consequence: EM and first‑order EM with random initialization from the mixture converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{-Ω(M)}, so exponentially many restarts are needed.
- First‑order EM property: It almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, implying failures arise from poor local maxima rather than saddles.
- Complementarity: Results sharpen earlier local-convergence analyses (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman; Balakrishnan et al.) and contrast with positive algorithmic approaches (clustering, method-of-moments).
- Consistency with finite-sample work: Matches empirical findings of many local maxima by establishing analogous negative behavior at the population level.
- Construction strengths: Simple, powerful constructions (often one-dimensional, well-separated mixtures) make the negative result clear and impactful.
- Clarity strength: Clear resolution of a long-standing conjecture and conceptually clarifying the maxima vs. saddle-point issue.
- Technical soundness: Overall appears strong, contingent on rigorous handling of limit/suprema arguments in Theorem 1.
- Scope/dimension issue: Core constructions and proofs are in d = 1, but the exposition often speaks to general d; this should be stated early and discussed.
- Theorem 1 concern: Steps relying on limits of suprema over unbounded/moving domains (lines 288–306) are nontrivial and need rigorous justification (uniform convergence/compactness/tightness or epi-/Gamma-convergence).
- Requested fixes for Theorem 1: Precisely define neighborhoods/boundaries, justify exchanging limits and suprema, and provide explicit inequalities controlling boundary likelihoods and error terms.
- First‑order EM derivation issue: Transition from the Q-function lower bound to the gradient update (Eq. (5)) needs a brief derivation showing equivalence to gradient ascent and the role of stepsize s.
- Assumption clarity: Make assumptions explicit (equal weights, identity covariance, known M) and discuss extension plausibility to non-uniform weights or unknown/heterogeneous covariances.
- Population vs. sample: Make the “population vs sample” scope explicit early and briefly describe how population results transfer to large finite samples.
- Suggested improvements: State d = 1 focus, expand Theorem 1 proof details, elaborate first‑order EM derivation and stepsize ranges, and comment on extensions to other weight/covariance settings.
- Significance: High — changes understanding of EM for GMMs even in benign regimes and highlights need for careful initialization.
- Originality: Novel structural and algorithmic negative results, particularly the exponential failure probability and strict-saddle avoidance.
- Pros summary: Resolves an open conjecture, gives strong practical relevance, and provides conceptual insight on local maxima vs. saddles.
- Cons summary: Key technical step in Theorem 1 is terse, d = 1 scope not emphasized, and first‑order EM section needs clearer derivation and assumptions.
