

This paper presents a theoretically rigorous analysis of the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for Gaussian mixture models under specific simplifications: known component count, uniform weights, and identity covariance matrices. The authors establish three key negative results: (1) the likelihood function may exhibit local maxima with arbitrarily lower values than the global maximum, even for large sample sizes; (2) random initialization of EM converges with high probability to such suboptimal critical points; and (3) these results persist for the gradient EM variant. These findings are significant for understanding the practical limitations of EM, particularly in high-dimensional settings where global convergence is often assumed.

The paper’s theoretical contribution is compelling, and the simplifications are justified given the technical depth required for the proofs. Restricting to uniform weights and identity covariances allows the authors to isolate core challenges in EM convergence without unnecessary complexity. The results remain highly informative for practitioners, as they reveal fundamental instabilities that could manifest in real-world applications even with "well-specified" models.

A minor point concerns the dimensionality claim. The paper states results for general dimension $d$, yet Theorem 1’s proof is confined to $d=1$. The authors should clarify whether the core arguments extend to $d > 1$ and, if not, briefly discuss the additional technical hurdles (e.g., geometric complexities in higher dimensions or interactions between parameters). This would strengthen the paper’s scope and prevent potential misinterpretation.

The primary concern pertains to Theorem 1’s proof (lines 288–306 in the main text), which appears insufficiently detailed. The authors compute limits of likelihood suprema over parameter domains as $\gamma, R \to \infty$, but the justification for interchanging limits and suprema over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains requires rigorous elaboration. While the pointwise limit computations (e.g., the three equations at line 288) seem plausible, the transition from continuity of the likelihood to the claimed convergence of suprema is non-trivial. Specifically, continuity alone does not guarantee that $\lim_{R \to \infty} \sup_{\theta \in \mathcal{D}_R} \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \sup_{\theta \in \mathcal{D}} \lim_{R \to \infty} \mathcal{L}(\theta)$ for unbounded $\mathcal{D}_R$. The proof would benefit from explicit justification of this step, such as uniform convergence arguments or dominated convergence, to fully convince the reader. Given the theorem’s centrality to the paper’s claims, a more detailed proof—possibly in the supplement—would significantly enhance the paper’s technical soundness.

Additionally, the Gradient EM analysis (lines 162–172) requires clearer exposition. The derivation of Equation (5) is particularly opaque; the authors should explicitly state the gradient computation steps, including how the EM update is transformed into the gradient form. A brief intermediate derivation would improve readability and ensure the argument’s validity is transparent to readers.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution by rigorously demonstrating EM’s potential for suboptimal convergence under controlled conditions. The results are theoretically robust and practically relevant, offering critical insights for researchers relying on EM in mixture modeling. The minor concerns raised above are not structural flaws but opportunities to strengthen an otherwise strong submission. With the suggested clarifications—particularly for Theorem 1’s proof and the Gradient EM derivation—the paper would be well-suited for publication at NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.
