This paper investigates the existence of spurious local modes in the log-likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The analysis is focused on a simplified yet fundamental setting: a well-specified model with isotropic Gaussian components of a known, uniform scale. The authors demonstrate that even with access to infinite data (i.e., analyzing the population log-likelihood), local modes can exist. Furthermore, these local modes can be arbitrarily poor, exhibiting a significant difference in log-likelihood compared to the global optimum.

The paper's core results are built upon analyzing particular configurations of the true mixture centers, specifically where the centers themselves form well-separated clusters. In such scenarios, the authors prove that the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, when initialized by randomly sampling from the mixture distribution, will almost certainly fail to find the global optimum as the number of components increases. The paper is well-written and the theoretical contributions are insightful, though a few minor typos are present.

While the theoretical analysis is sound, I have two primary concerns regarding the practical implications of the findings.

1.  **Practical Likelihood of "Bad" Center Configurations:** Theorem 1 establishes the existence of a poor local mode for a specific configuration of the true component centers. A critical question for the practical relevance of this result is: how frequently are such "bad" configurations encountered in real-world problems? The paper's impact would be significantly strengthened if the authors could provide an analysis on the prevalence of these problematic configurations. For example, by assuming a distribution over the true centers, could the authors show that the probability of encountering such a configuration increases with the number of components (k) or the data dimensionality (d)? The text on lines 210-218 suggests that Theorem 2 addresses these concerns, but it primarily focuses on the consequences of a bad configuration for EM, rather than the likelihood of the configuration itself. A clear statement on the probability of these "bad" ground-truth parameter settings, under reasonable assumptions, would be highly valuable.

2.  **Relevance of the Random Initialization Scheme:** Theorem 2's negative result hinges on initializing the EM algorithm by randomly sampling component centers from the true mixture distribution. While this is a common initialization method in theoretical analyses, its practical relevance is debatable, especially in cases where the data is clearly clustered, which is the exact setting considered.
    
    Consider a scenario with data generated from three Gaussian components, where two are very close to each other and the third is far apart. If an equal number of data points (N) is sampled from each component, the first cluster will contain approximately 2N points, while the second will contain N points. In this well-separated case, it seems counter-intuitive that a practitioner would initialize two centers in the sparse region (with N points) and only one center in the dense region (with 2N points), which is a possible outcome of the random sampling procedure. When visible clusters exist, initialization strategies are often guided by data density (e.g., k-means++). It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss how their results hold under more sophisticated, and arguably more practical, initialization schemes.

**Questions for Clarification:**

*   **L74-81:** The purpose of the example presented in these lines is unclear. It does not appear to resolve the Srebro conjecture for k=2, so its intended contribution to the paper's argument would benefit from clarification.
*   **L233-235:** In the requirements for Lemma 3, the necessity of condition (1) ("at most one of the initial centers falls in B(μj, R) for any j") is not immediately obvious when condition (2) already ensures that centers are initialized near their "correct" cluster. Could the authors elaborate on why condition (2) alone is insufficient? Is the concern that a center initialized between two clusters is not guaranteed to converge to the globally optimal one?
*   **L267-273:** The proof that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point is an interesting technical result. From a practical standpoint, what is the significance of this finding? Is convergence to a saddle point considered more or less problematic than convergence to a poor local optimum?
