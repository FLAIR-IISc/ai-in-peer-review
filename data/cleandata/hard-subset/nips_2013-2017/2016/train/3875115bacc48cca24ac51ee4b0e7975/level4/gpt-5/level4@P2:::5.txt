This paper resolves Srebro’s 2007 open question on the population (infinite-sample) likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors construct counterexamples showing that even for equally weighted, well-separated, spherical Gaussians with M ≥ 3 components, the population log-likelihood admits spurious local maxima whose values can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. They further analyze the EM and first-order (gradient) EM algorithms under a standard random initialization scheme and prove that, with probability at least 1 − e^{-Ω(M)}, these methods converge to suboptimal critical points; consequently, achieving a good solution by random restarts requires exponentially many trials. To build intuition, they present the one-dimensional case with three components (k = 3, d = 1) and provide geometric insight into their construction. The paper also clearly outlines the algorithms considered (EM and first-order EM) and the initialization strategy under study.

Strengths:
- Resolves a notable open problem by demonstrating the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood of GMMs, even in a highly favorable setting.
- Provides clean algorithmic consequences: EM and gradient EM with random initialization fail with high probability, highlighting the necessity of careful initialization.
- The presentation is clear and well organized, balancing rigorous results with intuitive explanations (e.g., the k = 3, d = 1 illustration).
- The results are relevant to the broader NIPS community interested in non-convex optimization and the practical behavior of EM-like methods.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The results hinge on asymptotic, population-level analysis; while compelling, readers would benefit from quantitative guidance on finite-sample behavior.
- The probability bounds are stated in asymptotic form (1 − e^{-Ω(M)}). Estimates of the hidden constants would help assess when failures become likely in practice, especially since many applications do not involve large M.
- If constant estimates are intractable or unwieldy, small-scale simulations illustrating the onset of failure (a “phase transition”) under random initialization would strengthen the practical message.
- I did not verify all details in the supplementary proofs; a brief summary of key lemmas/assumptions enabling the exponential-failure bound would aid readability.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound with clear statements and proofs at the population level. The counterexample construction and the probabilistic analysis of random initialization are convincing.
- Clarity: Very well written and well structured. The intuition for the low-dimensional, three-component case is particularly helpful.
- Originality: High. The paper settles an open problem and provides new structural insights into GMM likelihood landscapes along with sharp algorithmic implications.
- Significance: High. The results temper optimistic folklore about EM in benign mixture settings and underscore the central role of initialization, influencing both theoretical work and practical methodology.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves a long-standing theoretical question with elegant constructions and clear implications.
- Delivers strong, general insights about the limitations of EM/gradient EM under natural random initializations.
- Well written and accessible to the NIPS audience.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of explicit constants and empirical corroboration may leave practitioners uncertain about when the negative results manifest at realistic scales.
- Focus on the population setting may limit immediate applicability without finite-sample calibration.

Overall, this is a clear and impactful paper that advances our understanding of non-convex likelihood landscapes for GMMs and the behavior of EM-style algorithms. I lean toward acceptance, with the suggestion to add constant estimates or illustrative experiments to contextualize the asymptotic results.
