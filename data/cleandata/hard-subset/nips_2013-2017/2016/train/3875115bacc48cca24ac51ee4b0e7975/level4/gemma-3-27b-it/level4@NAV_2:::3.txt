The paper investigates the existence of local modes in the likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), focusing on the idealized scenario of isotropic Gaussian components with known scale and a well-specified model. The authors demonstrate that local maxima exist even with infinite data, and that these can be arbitrarily suboptimal in terms of log-likelihood. The analysis centers on specific configurations, particularly those with well-separated clusters of mixture centers. Under these configurations, the authors prove that random initialization of the EM algorithm leads to a high probability of failing to converge to the global optimum as the number of components increases. The paper is generally well-written, though minor typographical errors are present.

My primary concern lies with the practical relevance of the findings. While Theorem 1 establishes the existence of local modes for a specific configuration of mixture centers, its applicability to real-world scenarios is unclear. How likely is such a configuration to arise in practice? A stronger argument for practical relevance would involve assumptions about the distribution of the true centers and a demonstration that “bad” configurations become more probable as the number of components (k) or dimensionality increases. Although lines 210-218 suggest Theorem 2 addresses this, it doesn’t fully convince me. A clear statement quantifying the likelihood of “bad” configurations under certain assumptions would be valuable. Regarding Theorem 2, the initialization scheme—randomly sampling the mixture—seems potentially unrealistic. Consider a dataset generated from three Gaussians, with two close together and one distant, and an equal number of samples from each. Initializing EM with two centers in the sparsely populated region and one in the dense region seems counterintuitive. Is random initialization a practically relevant initialization strategy when data clearly exhibits clustering? 

The example on lines 74-81, relating to the case k=2, is unclear in its purpose. It doesn't demonstrate the truth of Srebro’s conjecture for k=2, so its inclusion feels misplaced. Lines 233-235 clarify requirement (2), but the necessity of requirement (1) remains unclear. Does initializing a center between two clusters preclude it from converging to the correct cluster? Finally, the significance of Theorem 4—proving EM is unlikely to converge to a saddlepoint—is not fully explained. Is converging to a saddlepoint more or less problematic than converging to a local optimum from a practical perspective?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper rigorously addresses an important theoretical question regarding the landscape of the likelihood function for GMMs, resolving a long-standing conjecture.
*   The mathematical results are technically sound and well-proven, demonstrating a deep understanding of the problem.
*   The paper identifies a critical issue with the EM algorithm – its susceptibility to poor local optima under random initialization – highlighting the need for careful initialization strategies.
*   The results contribute to a broader understanding of non-convex optimization challenges in statistical estimation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical relevance of the findings is questionable, as the analysis focuses on specific, potentially unrealistic configurations of mixture centers.
*   The assumptions underlying the results (e.g., random initialization) may not hold in many real-world scenarios.
*   The paper lacks a clear discussion of the probability of encountering the problematic configurations in practice.
*   The connection between the theoretical findings and practical algorithmic improvements is not fully explored.



I believe the paper presents solid theoretical work but requires further justification of its practical impact to warrant acceptance to NIPS. A strong revision addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding the likelihood of the analyzed configurations occurring in practice, would significantly strengthen the paper.
