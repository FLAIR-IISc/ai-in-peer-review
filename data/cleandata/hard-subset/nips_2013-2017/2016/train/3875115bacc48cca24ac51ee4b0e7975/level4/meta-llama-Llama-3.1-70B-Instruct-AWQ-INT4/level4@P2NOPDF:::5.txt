The paper provides a definitive answer to the open question posed by Srebro in his 2007 paper, "Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?" The authors demonstrate that the answer is indeed negative, presenting a general class of counterexamples to support their claim. The paper begins by outlining the methods used to maximize the likelihood in the context of Gaussian Mixture Models, including the EM and gradient EM algorithms. The authors also address the issue of initialization, discussing a common choice used in practice.

The authors then present their primary theorems, which provide a counterexample to Srebro's question. Furthermore, they show that even with a common random initialization, the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to non-optimal maxima with exponentially high probability. To provide intuition for their proofs, the authors present a simple case (k=3, d=1) and walk the reader through the key steps.

The paper is well-written and clear, striking a balance between the need for technical details and the importance of providing intuition for the theoretical results. The question addressed by the authors is crucial for understanding the challenges associated with non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic limit for simple distributions.

While I did not thoroughly review the supplementary material, I believe it would be beneficial to include estimates of the constants in the probability term. This would provide insight into when we can expect to observe convergence to suboptimal solutions in the asymptotic limit, effectively identifying the phase transition. If the constants are too complex or do not lend themselves to meaningful interpretation, supporting simulations would be a valuable addition to demonstrate the practical implications of this phenomenon. However, this omission does not detract from the novelty and impact of the paper.
