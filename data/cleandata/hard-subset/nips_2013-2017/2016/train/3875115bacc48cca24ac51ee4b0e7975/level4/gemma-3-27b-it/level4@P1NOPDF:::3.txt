The paper investigates the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models. It considers the simplest case: isotropic Gaussian components with known scale, and assumes a well-specified model. The authors demonstrate that local modes exist even with infinite data, and that the difference in log-likelihood between these local modes and the global optimum can be arbitrarily large. The paper focuses on specific configurations, particularly those where mixture centers are well-spaced. Under these configurations, the authors show that random initialization will likely cause the EM algorithm to miss the global optimum as the number of components increases. The paper is generally well-written, with only minor typos. I have two main concerns regarding the practical relevance of this work.

1. Theorem 1 demonstrates the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood even with infinite data, but this is shown for a particular configuration of the centers. My question is: how frequently does this configuration occur in practice? I would be more convinced of the paper’s practical relevance if the authors made assumptions about the distribution of the centers and then showed that “bad” configurations become increasingly likely as the number of components (k) or the dimensionality of the problem increases. Lines 210-218 state that Theorem 2 addresses these concerns, but I am not convinced it fully does. A clearer statement quantifying the likelihood of these “bad” configurations, under some defined assumptions, would be valuable. Note that I am referring to the likelihood of “bad” configurations of the *true centers*, not “bad” initializations.

2. Theorem 2 considers the impact of the EM algorithm’s initialization. The authors examine initializing the component centers by randomly sampling from the mixture. Under specific configurations of the centers, the EM algorithm frequently fails to converge to the global optimum with this initialization strategy. However, consider a scenario where data is generated from three Gaussians, two of which are closely clustered and one is isolated. If an equal number N of data points are generated from each Gaussian, then 2N points will fall near the first cluster, and N points near the third. It seems counterintuitive to initialize EM by placing two centers where only N points exist, and one center where N points are observed, particularly given the clear separation between the clusters. Is random initialization a practically relevant approach when the data exhibits such clear clustering?

Lines 74-81 include an example that I find unpersuasive. It doesn't convincingly demonstrate the validity of Sresbro’s conjecture when k = 2, so its purpose is unclear.

Lines 233-235: I understand requirement (2), but I don't understand the need for requirement (1). Does this mean that initializing a center between two clusters does not guarantee it will converge towards the correct cluster?

Lines 267-273: The authors prove that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point. From a practical perspective, why is this result important? Is converging to a saddle point more or less problematic than converging to a local optimum?
