This paper studies local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models under the simplest setting: isotropic Gaussians with known variance and a well-specified model. The authors prove that local maxima exist even with infinite data and that they can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum in terms of log-likelihood gap. The analysis focuses on specific geometric arrangements of the true component means, notably when means are grouped into well-separated clusters. In these settings, the paper shows that random initialization causes EM to miss the global optimum with high probability as the number of components grows. The paper is clearly written overall, with a few typos.

I have two main concerns about practical relevance.

1) Likelihood of “bad” configurations. Theorem 1 establishes that the population log-likelihood admits a spurious local optimum given a particular configuration of the true centers. How likely is such a configuration to arise in practice? The results would be more compelling if the authors posited a distribution over the true centers and then quantified the probability that “bad” configurations occur, e.g., showing that their likelihood increases with the number of components k or with dimension. Lines 210–218 suggest that Theorem 2 addresses this point, but I do not see a direct statement that bounds or characterizes the probability of these configurations under explicit assumptions. A clear theorem quantifying the prevalence of “bad” center configurations (as opposed to “bad” initializations) would strengthen the practical message.

2) Practicality of random initialization. Theorem 2 considers EM initialized by sampling centers from the mixture distribution, and shows that, for certain center configurations, EM frequently fails to reach the global optimum. Consider a simple case with three Gaussians: two close together and one far apart, with an equal number N of samples from each component. Then roughly 2N points lie near the close pair and N near the distant component. It seems implausible in practice that an initialization would place two centers in the sparse region and one in the dense region when clusters are clearly separated. Is random seeding from the mixture a realistic model of how practitioners initialize EM in such clearly clustered data? Some discussion or experiments comparing random seeding to standard heuristics (e.g., k-means++ or multiple restarts) would help contextualize the relevance of the negative result.

Additional questions and clarifications:
- L74–81: I am unsure what the example is intended to demonstrate. It does not convince me that Srebro’s conjecture holds for k = 2. Please clarify the purpose of this example and what precise claim it supports.
- L233–235: I understand requirement (2), but why is requirement (1) necessary? Are you asserting that if a center is initialized between two clusters, you cannot guarantee it will move toward the intended cluster?
- L267–273: You show EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point. Why is this practically important? Is converging to a saddle point better or worse than converging to a suboptimal local maximum in the contexts you study? Some interpretation would help.

Overall, the theoretical results are clean and the negative examples are interesting. To bolster practical relevance, I recommend:
- Providing probabilistic statements about the frequency of “bad” true-center configurations under natural generative models, as k or d grow.
- Justifying the random-seeding model for EM (or contrasting it with common practical initializations) and, if possible, extending the analysis to more realistic initialization schemes.
