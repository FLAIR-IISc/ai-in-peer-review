This paper analyzes the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters of a Gaussian mixture model. The analysis is performed under the assumption that the number of components, the mixture weights, and the covariance matrices are known and fixed. The paper is theoretical in nature and presents three main negative results for the EM algorithm: 1) even with a large sample size, the likelihood function can have local maxima with likelihood values that are arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum; 2) when randomly initialized, the EM algorithm converges with high probability to a critical point whose likelihood is arbitrarily smaller than the maximum likelihood; and 3) this same negative result also applies to the gradient EM algorithm.

The research question is both interesting and important. To facilitate a rigorous analysis, the authors restrict their focus to a simplified setting where the number of components is known, the weights are uniform, and the covariance matrices are the identity matrix. These simplifications are well-justified, as the proofs are already quite technical and the results remain highly informative. In my view, these are significant findings that contribute to a deeper practical understanding of the EM algorithm.

A point for clarification regards the dimensionality of the problem. While the paper is framed for a general dimension `d`, the proofs are presented only for the one-dimensional case (`d=1`). The authors should clarify whether these results extend to an arbitrary dimension `d` and briefly discuss any additional technical challenges that arise for `d > 1`.

My main concern with the paper lies in the proof of Theorem 1. (Note: I have not reviewed the proofs in the supplementary material). The proof presented in the main text is overly condensed. I would recommend expanding it, even if it requires shortening other discursive sections. Specifically, the reasoning between lines 288 and 306 requires more detail. While the limits of the likelihood function for fixed parameters (as in the equations on line 288) seem plausible, the subsequent step of taking the limit of a supremum over a domain that is unbounded or dependent on parameters approaching infinity is non-trivial and requires rigorous justification. The authors' appeal to the continuity of the likelihood function is, by itself, insufficient to establish the claimed convergence of the suprema. To make the proof of Theorem 1 fully convincing, a more detailed and expanded derivation is necessary.

Finally, I have a minor concern regarding the section on Gradient EM (lines 162-172), which I found more difficult to follow than the rest of the paper. In particular, the authors should provide a more detailed explanation of how Equation (5) is derived.
