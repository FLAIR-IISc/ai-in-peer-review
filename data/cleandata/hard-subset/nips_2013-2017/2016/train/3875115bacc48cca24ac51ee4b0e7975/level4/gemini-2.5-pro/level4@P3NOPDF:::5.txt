This paper provides a definitive negative answer to the long-standing open question posed by Srebro (2007): "Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?". The authors achieve this by constructing a general class of counterexamples, demonstrating the existence of spurious, non-optimal local maxima even in the asymptotic, infinite-sample limit.

The paper begins by outlining the standard optimization schemes for Gaussian Mixture Models, namely the Expectation-Maximization (EM) and gradient EM algorithms, and discusses common initialization strategies. The core contribution lies in the main theorems, which formally establish the counterexample to Srebro's question. Crucially, the paper goes beyond a mere existence proof. It demonstrates that for a common random initialization scheme, both the EM and gradient EM algorithms will converge to these spurious maxima with exponentially high probability. To build intuition for their proofs, the authors skillfully walk through a simpler case (k=3, d=1), which effectively illustrates the mechanism behind their more general result.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The authors strike an excellent balance between presenting rigorous theoretical results and providing accessible intuition, making a complex topic understandable. This clarity is a significant strength. The contribution of this work is highly significant, as it settles an important open question and deepens our understanding of the fundamental challenges associated with non-convex optimization. The findings highlight that even for a widely used model like the Gaussian Mixture Model, the optimization landscape can possess misleading local optima in the ideal, infinite-data setting—a result with broad implications for machine learning theory and practice.

While the paper's theoretical contribution is self-contained and impactful, its practical relevance could be further illuminated with some additional analysis. The high-probability convergence result depends on constants related to the model parameters (e.g., the number of components, k). In practice, k is often not excessively large, so understanding the threshold at which these spurious maxima begin to dominate the optimization landscape—a sort of "phase transition"—would be of great interest. Therefore, providing estimates for the constants in the probability term would be a valuable addition.

If an analytical treatment of these constants is intractable, supporting simulations demonstrating the phenomenon for moderate values of k would also be very effective. Such experiments would provide empirical evidence for the theory and give readers a more concrete sense of when to expect this behavior.

It should be emphasized, however, that this is a suggestion for strengthening the paper's practical connection and does not diminish the core theoretical novelty or importance of the work. The paper is a substantial contribution to the field, and its central result is both surprising and important.
