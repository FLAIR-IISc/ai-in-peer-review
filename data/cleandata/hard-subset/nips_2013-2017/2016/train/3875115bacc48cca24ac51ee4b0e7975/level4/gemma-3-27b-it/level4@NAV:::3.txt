The paper investigates the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models. Specifically, it analyzes the simplest case of isotropic Gaussian components with known scale, assuming a well-specified model. The authors demonstrate that local modes persist even with infinite data and can be arbitrarily distant from the global optimum in terms of log-likelihood. Focusing on configurations with well-spaced mixture centers, they prove that, as the number of components increases, random initialization of the EM algorithm will almost certainly lead to convergence to a suboptimal local mode. The paper is generally well-written, although some minor typos are present. 

I have two primary concerns regarding the practical implications of this work. First, while Theorem 1 establishes the existence of local modes given a specific configuration of centers, its practical relevance hinges on the likelihood of observing such configurations in real-world data. I would be more convinced if the authors explored this further, perhaps by introducing distributional assumptions on the center locations and demonstrating how the probability of encountering these “bad” configurations grows with the number of components (k) or the dimensionality of the problem. The discussion on lines 210-218 suggests Theorem 2 addresses this point, but I find the connection unclear. A more explicit statement quantifying the likelihood of “bad” configurations – defined by the positions of the true centers – under plausible assumptions would be valuable.

Second, Theorem 2 examines the impact of EM algorithm initialization. The authors consider random initialization by sampling from the mixture, and show that, under particular center configurations, EM frequently fails to find the global optimum. However, this initialization strategy seems questionable in scenarios where data exhibits clear clustering. For example, consider a dataset generated from three Gaussians, with two clusters closely grouped and a third isolated. If each Gaussian contributes an equal number of data points, we would expect twice as many points near the closer clusters as near the distant one. It appears counterintuitive to initialize EM with two centers placed in the sparsely populated region and only one in the denser cluster, especially given the separation between groups. This raises questions about the practical relevance of random initialization in clearly clustered data. 

Finally, I have a few specific points of clarification. The example on lines 74-81 does not effectively illustrate the Sresbro conjecture for k=2, and its purpose is unclear. Regarding lines 233-235, while I understand requirement (2), the necessity of requirement (1) is not apparent – is the concern that initializing a center between two clusters does not guarantee convergence to the correct cluster? The practical significance of proving EM is unlikely to converge to a saddlepoint (lines 267-273) is also unclear; is convergence to a saddlepoint more or less problematic than convergence to a local optimum?
