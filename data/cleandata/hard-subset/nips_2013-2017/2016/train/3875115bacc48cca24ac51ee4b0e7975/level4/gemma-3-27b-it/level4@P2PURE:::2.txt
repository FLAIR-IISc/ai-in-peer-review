This paper investigates the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters of a Gaussian mixture model. Crucially, the number of Gaussian components, mixture weights, and covariance matrices are assumed known and fixed. The work is theoretical, presenting three negative results concerning the EM algorithm’s performance. First, the likelihood function can possess local maxima even with large sample sizes, with arbitrarily low likelihood values compared to the global maximum. Second, the EM algorithm, when initialized randomly, converges with high probability to a critical point yielding a sub-optimal likelihood. Third, the gradient EM algorithm exhibits the same negative behavior. I find the question addressed by this paper interesting and significant. To achieve rigorous proofs, the authors focus on a simplified Gaussian mixture model where the number of components is known, the weights are uniform, and the covariance matrices are identity. While these simplifications exist, I believe they are reasonable given the technical complexity of the proofs and the informative nature of the results. These findings are highly interesting and potentially valuable for understanding the practical behavior of the EM algorithm. 

It is important to note that the paper presents results for general dimension *d*, while the proofs are specifically for *d*=1. The authors should discuss the potential for extending the proofs to arbitrary dimensions, and if that is not feasible, briefly outline the additional difficulties that arise in higher dimensions. 

My primary concern lies with the proof of Theorem 1 (I have not examined the supplementary material proofs in detail). I found this proof to be concise, and would have preferred a more expanded presentation, potentially at the expense of some of the surrounding discussion. Specifically, the section from line 288 to 306 requires more elaboration. The authors provide limits (as gamma or R approaches infinity) of suprema of likelihood functions over certain domains. I do not question the correctness of the likelihood function evaluations for fixed parameters, and thus find the three equations on line 288 plausible. However, determining the limit of a maximum is not straightforward when dealing with unbounded domains or parameters that tend to infinity. Similarly, while the authors state the likelihood function is continuous, this property does not automatically guarantee the stated convergence properties. Consequently, I believe a more detailed and longer proof is necessary to fully convince the reader of its validity.

Finally, I have a minor concern regarding the discussion of the Gradient EM algorithm from line 162 to 172. I found this section more challenging to follow than the rest of the paper. In particular, the derivation of Equation (5) could benefit from further explanation.
