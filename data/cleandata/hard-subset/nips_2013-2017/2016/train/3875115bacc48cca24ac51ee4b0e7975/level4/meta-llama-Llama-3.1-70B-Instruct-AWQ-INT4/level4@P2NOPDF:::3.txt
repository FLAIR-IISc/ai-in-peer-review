The paper explores the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models, specifically focusing on the simplest possible mixture model with isotropic Gaussian components of known scale. The authors demonstrate that local modes can exist even with infinite data, and these modes can be arbitrarily bad in terms of log-likelihood differences between local and global optima. They concentrate on particular configurations where mixture centers are divided among well-spaced clusters, showing that random initialization often causes the EM algorithm to miss the global optimum as the number of components increases.

The paper is well-written, with occasional typos. However, I have two main concerns regarding the practical relevance of the paper. Firstly, Theorem 1 shows that the log-likelihood has local modes even with infinite data, but it relies on a specific configuration of centers. I question how likely this configuration is to occur in practice. To strengthen the paper's practical relevance, it would be beneficial to make assumptions about the distribution of centers and demonstrate that "bad" configurations become increasingly likely as the number of components or dimensionality increases. The authors claim that Theorem 2 addresses these concerns (L210-L218), but I am not convinced. A clear statement on the likelihood of "bad" configurations under certain assumptions would be helpful.

Secondly, Theorem 2 involves the initialization of the EM algorithm, which considers initializing component centers by randomly sampling the mixture. Under specific center configurations, the EM algorithm often fails to converge to the global optimum with this initialization. However, I find it unlikely that someone would initialize EM by placing two centers where there are only N points and one center where there are N observations, especially when the two clusters are well-separated. I question the practical relevance of random initialization when the data is clearly clustered.

Additionally, I am unsure about the purpose of the example on lines 74-81, as it does not convince me that Srebro's conjecture holds when k = 2. I also do not understand the necessity of requirement (1) in L233-235, as initializing a center between two clusters should guarantee movement toward the correct cluster.

The authors prove that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddlepoint (L267-273), but I am unsure why this result is important from a practical perspective. Is converging to a saddlepoint more or less problematic than converging to a local optimum?
