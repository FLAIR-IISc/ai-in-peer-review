

This paper investigates the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models under the simplest setting: isotropic components with known covariance, and a well-specified model. The authors demonstrate that local modes can exist even with infinite data, and that these modes can yield arbitrarily poor log-likelihood values relative to the global optimum. Focusing on specific configurations—particularly when true cluster centers form well-separated groups—the paper shows that random initialization causes EM to miss the global optimum almost surely as the number of components increases. The writing is generally clear, though occasional typos require attention.

I have two primary concerns regarding the paper’s practical relevance. First, Theorem 1 establishes that local modes exist under a specific configuration of the true cluster centers. However, it remains unclear how likely such a configuration is to arise in practice. To strengthen the practical argument, the authors should specify assumptions about the distribution of true centers (e.g., uniform over a domain) and demonstrate that "bad" configurations (i.e., those inducing spurious local modes) become increasingly probable as the number of components \(k\) or dimensionality grows. While the paper states in Lines 210–218 that Theorem 2 addresses this, it does not explicitly quantify the likelihood of such configurations. Clarifying this—particularly distinguishing "bad configurations" of the *true centers* from "bad initializations"—would significantly bolster the paper’s relevance.  

Second, Theorem 2 analyzes EM’s failure under random initialization when centers are arranged in specific ways. Consider a scenario where data comes from three Gaussians, two of which are close together and one well-separated. With equal-sized clusters (\(N\) points per Gaussian), 2\(N\) points concentrate near the first cluster and \(N\) near the third. Under such clear separation, it is highly implausible that a practitioner would initialize EM with two centers in the dense cluster (2\(N\) points) and one in the sparse cluster (\(N\) points). The paper’s assumption of random initialization appears unrealistic for clearly clustered data, undermining the practical significance of Theorem 2. The example in Lines 74–81 fails to convince me that the Sresbro conjecture holds for \(k=2\); its purpose remains unclear.  

Additional technical points:  
- L233–235: Requirement (1) is unclear. If initializing a center between two clusters prevents guaranteed convergence to the correct cluster, this should be explicitly justified.  
- L267–273: The result that EM rarely converges to saddle points is stated, but its practical importance is unexplained. Is saddle-point convergence more or less problematic than local optima convergence? This context is essential.  
- Minor corrections:  
  - L18: Add "that the" before "gradient" (i.e., "that the gradient EM algorithm").  
  - L38: "quality of the estimate" → "quality of the estimates" (plural).  
  - L60: "characterize when it is that efficient" → "characterize under which conditions efficient."  
  - L138: Clarify "O(\(d^{1/2}\))-separated" means all densities are \(c\)-separated with \(c = \Theta(d^{1/2})\).  
  - L172: The sentence is redundant; either reformulate or omit.  
  - L177: "equivalent of" → "equivalent to."  
  - L218: Define "well-separated constant" (e.g., "a constant \(\delta\) defining well-separatedness").  
  - L220: "initialized by the the random" → "initialized by the random."  
  - L225: \(\Omega(k)\) is standard asymptotic notation; retain but ensure context is clear.  
  - L245: "unfortunate coincident" → "unfortunate coincidence."  
  - L249: "Recall for uniform weighted" → "Recall that uniform weights."  
  - L308: "And claim when" → "And claim that."  

Overall, the paper makes a technically sound contribution to understanding EM’s limitations in Gaussian mixtures. However, the practical relevance hinges on demonstrating that the critical configurations (for Theorems 1 and 2) are not pathological but arise naturally under plausible data-generating assumptions. Addressing the concerns above would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.
