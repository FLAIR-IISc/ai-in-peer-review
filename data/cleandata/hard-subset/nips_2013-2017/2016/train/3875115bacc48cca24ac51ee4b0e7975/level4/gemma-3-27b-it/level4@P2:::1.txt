This paper investigates the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors demonstrate that, even in well-separated mixtures with an infinite number of samples, EM can converge to suboptimal local maxima. Specifically, they prove: (1) a non-trivial local maximum exists for a 3-component GMM with well-separated components; (2) random initialization of EM leads to convergence to a suboptimal solution with a probability of at least 1 – exp(-Ω(M)), where M is the number of components; and (3) first-order EM methods are unlikely to converge to strict saddle points, indicating that bad local maxima are the primary cause of performance issues. The paper presents a consistent set of findings illustrating the challenges of achieving global optimality with EM for GMMs.

While the results are coherent and contribute to a better understanding of EM’s limitations, they are not entirely surprising. The presented counterexample, involving a 3-component mixture where two components are closely clustered and the third is distant, intuitively leads to the identified local maximum. This behavior echoes known challenges in k-means clustering, particularly when clusters exhibit significant separation. The extension to k components utilizes a recursive construction, which, while requiring effort, also follows a reasonably anticipated approach. Similarly, the failure of random initialization with a probability of exp(-Ω(M)) is analogous to the difficulties encountered in k-means clustering, where improved initialization strategies (like k-log(k) sampling) are often employed. 

The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison to k-means clustering and Lloyd’s heuristic. Kumar and Kannan’s work provides convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic under sufficient separation conditions. While EM and Lloyd’s heuristic differ when Gaussian components are not well-separated (as explored by Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu), they become increasingly similar as separation increases, which is the case in the constructed counterexamples. Contrasting the results in this context would provide valuable insight and contextualize the contributions of this work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear presentation of challenging scenarios:** The paper clearly identifies and rigorously analyzes problematic configurations for EM estimation of GMMs.
*   **Rigorous theoretical analysis:** The proofs provide solid mathematical foundations for the claimed results.
*   **Coherent narrative:** The findings are presented in a logical progression, building a compelling case for the limitations of EM.
*   **Identification of key issue:** Establishing that saddle points are not the primary reason for EM’s failure is a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited novelty:** The results, while technically sound, are not radically surprising and build upon existing knowledge of the challenges in clustering and mixture modeling.
*   **Lack of comparison to related work:** A more detailed discussion of k-means and Lloyd’s heuristic would enhance the paper's impact and contextualize the findings.
*   **Practical implications somewhat understated:** While the paper highlights the need for careful initialization, further discussion of practical strategies to mitigate these issues would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a rigorous mathematical analysis of the convergence properties of EM, addressing a fundamental question in statistical estimation.
*   The results contribute to a deeper understanding of the challenges inherent in non-convex optimization problems.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents complex ideas.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core findings are not particularly novel and are somewhat predictable based on prior work in clustering.
*   The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to related techniques and algorithms.
*   The practical implications of the results could be more thoroughly explored.

**Overall assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical paper that contributes to the understanding of the limitations of EM for GMM estimation. However, the results are not groundbreaking, and the lack of comparison to related work weakens the overall impact. A revised version addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend a borderline accept, contingent on the authors addressing the suggested improvements, particularly by providing a more thorough discussion of the connection to k-means and existing initialization strategies.
