This paper provides a definitive negative answer to the open question posed by Srebro (2007) in his paper, "Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?". The authors resolve this question by constructing a general class of counterexamples.

The paper begins by outlining the standard algorithms used to maximize the likelihood for Gaussian Mixture Models, namely the EM and gradient EM algorithms. The authors also discuss the role of initialization and describe a common random initialization strategy. They then present their primary theorems, which formalize the counterexample to the question posed by Srebro. Furthermore, the authors show that even when using a common random initialization scheme, both the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to these sub-optimal local maxima with exponentially high probability. To build intuition for their theoretical results, the authors include a proof for the simple case of k=3 components in d=1 dimension.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. It strikes an excellent balance between the formal presentation of its theoretical results and the need to provide intuition, all within the constraints of the page limit. The question answered by the authors is of significant importance for advancing our understanding of the challenges that arise in non-convex optimization, showing that such issues can persist even in the asymptotic, infinite-sample limit for relatively simple distributional models.

While I did not have the opportunity to check the supplementary material in detail, the paper's analysis could be strengthened by providing estimates for the constants in the stated probability bounds. This is important because, in many practical applications, the number of mixture components, k, is not expected to be excessively large. Explicit estimates for these constants would help clarify the practical regime in which convergence to sub-optimal solutions becomes a concern (i.e., where a phase transition might occur). If the constants are too complex to be insightful, supporting simulations would be a valuable alternative to demonstrate that this is an empirically observable phenomenon.

However, this is a minor suggestion and does not detract from the novelty or significant impact of this work.
