This paper addresses a fundamental open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood function for Gaussian mixture estimation. The authors provide a definitive negative answer through the construction of a general class of counterexamples. 

The paper begins with a concise overview of standard techniques used to maximize the likelihood in Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), including the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and gradient-based EM approaches. The authors appropriately discuss the role of initialization, outlining a common and practical initialization strategy. The core contribution lies in their primary theorems, which demonstrably construct these counterexamples, resolving Srebro’s question.

Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that even when employing the widely used random initialization scheme, the EM and gradient EM algorithms are highly likely – with exponentially high probability – to converge to suboptimal local maxima. To bolster intuition, they present a simplified proof for the case of k=3 and d=1, providing a concrete illustration of the underlying mechanisms.

This work is exceptionally clear and well-written. The authors strike an excellent balance between providing rigorous theoretical results and offering intuitive explanations, all within the constraints of a conference paper format. The question they address is of significant importance to the broader field of non-convex optimization, particularly in understanding the challenges that persist even in the asymptotic limit for relatively simple distributions like GMMs.

I did not perform an in-depth review of the supplementary material. However, providing estimates for the constants within the probability bounds presented would be valuable. Specifically, understanding the magnitude of these constants is crucial for assessing the practical relevance of the findings. While the theoretical result is strong, it’s important to consider scenarios where *k* (the number of mixture components) might be large.  Estimates would help pinpoint a potential "phase transition" – the point at which convergence to suboptimal solutions becomes increasingly probable as the sample size grows. If deriving such estimates proves analytically challenging, supporting simulations demonstrating the observed phenomenon would be beneficial.

Despite this minor point, the novelty and impact of this paper are substantial. The authors’ work provides a significant contribution to our understanding of the limitations of commonly used optimization algorithms in the context of Gaussian mixture models and, more generally, in non-convex optimization landscapes.
