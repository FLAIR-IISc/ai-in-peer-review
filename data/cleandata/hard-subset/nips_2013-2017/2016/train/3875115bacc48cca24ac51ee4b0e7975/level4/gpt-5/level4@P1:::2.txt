This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components and analyzes the behavior of EM and first-order EM under random initialization. The main structural result is negative: even for uniformly weighted, spherical, well-separated mixtures, the population log-likelihood admits bad local maxima with values that can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, refuting Srebro’s 2007 conjecture. Algorithmically, the paper shows that EM and a first-order EM variant, when randomly initialized by sampling from the underlying mixture, converge to such suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-Ω(M)}, implying that exponentially many restarts are needed to succeed. Finally, the first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, suggesting that failure is due to poor local maxima rather than saddles. These results complement and sharpen earlier analyses of EM’s local convergence near good basins (e.g., Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and contrast with algorithmic approaches based on clustering or method-of-moments (Arora et al., 2005; Vempala and Wang, 2002; Hsu and Kakade, 2013; Moitra and Valiant, 2010). They are also consistent with finite-sample phenomena showing many local maxima (Améndola et al., 2015), by establishing analogous negative behavior at the population level.

Strengths
- Clear, impactful resolution of a long-standing conjecture: the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in highly favorable GMM settings.
- Sharp algorithmic consequences: random initialization plus local search (EM or gradient ascent) fails with high probability; success requires exponentially many restarts.
- Conceptual clarity on the role of local maxima versus saddle points via the first-order EM result.
- The constructions are simple to state (e.g., one-dimensional, well-separated mixtures) yet powerful in implication.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Scope versus proofs: While the exposition often speaks to general dimension d, the core constructions and proofs are in d = 1. It would help to explicitly state this early and to discuss whether the arguments can be extended to higher dimensions, and what obstacles arise for d > 1 (e.g., coupling between coordinates, geometry of attraction basins, or technicalities in controlling likelihood boundaries).
- Theorem 1 proof details (lines 288–306): The argument relies on limits of suprema of likelihoods over domains that are unbounded and/or depend on diverging parameters (γ, R). While pointwise limits of the likelihood at fixed parameters are plausible, passing to limits of suprema over moving/unbounded sets is nontrivial. Continuity alone does not justify these steps. Please expand this part:
  - Precisely define the neighborhoods and boundaries and verify that global maxima are excluded.
  - Justify exchanging limits and suprema, e.g., via uniform convergence on compacta, tightness/compactness arguments, bounding the contribution from tails, or epi-/Gamma-convergence.
  - Provide explicit inequalities showing the boundary likelihood is uniformly below the interior configuration for large γ, R, including control of all error terms.
  A longer, more self-contained proof sketch in the main text (or a dedicated lemma with full details) would substantially improve readability and confidence.
- First-order EM derivation (lines 162–172): The transition from the Q-function lower bound to the gradient update (Eq. (5) in your numbering) could be clarified. A brief derivation showing that the first-order update equals gradient ascent on the population likelihood L, including the role of stepsize s and the evaluation at θ′ = θ, would help readers unfamiliar with Balakrishnan et al. (2015).

Quality
- Technical soundness appears strong, contingent on the rigorous handling of the limit/suprema arguments in Theorem 1. The algorithmic probability bounds are well-motivated via the multilevel clustering construction and match the claimed exponential decay.
- The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of assumptions (equal weights, identity covariance, known M) and whether the constructions and probability bounds plausibly extend to non-uniform weights or shared/unknown covariances.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and well-organized, with useful intuition for the M = 3 case. However, the two areas noted above (Theorem 1 details; first-order EM derivation) are harder to follow than the rest.
- Consider making the “population vs sample” scope explicit early on and adding a short paragraph describing how the population results transfer to large finite samples (e.g., via standard empirical process arguments).

Originality
- The structural and algorithmic negative results are novel and important, especially the exponential failure probability under random initialization and the strict-saddle avoidance result for first-order EM.

Significance
- High. The work changes how we think about EM for GMMs even in benign regimes and underscores the necessity of careful initialization. It will likely influence both theoretical analyses and practical algorithm design.

Suggestions for improvement
- State explicitly that the main constructions and proofs are in d = 1; discuss prospects and obstacles for d > 1.
- Expand the proof of Theorem 1 around lines 288–306 with rigorous justification for limits of suprema over moving/unbounded domains.
- Elaborate the derivation and interpretation of the first-order EM update (Eq. (5)), including the connection to gradient ascent on L and acceptable stepsize ranges.
- Briefly comment on extensions to non-uniform weights or unknown/heterogeneous covariances, and on whether the multilevel construction can be adapted.

Pros
- Resolves an open conjecture; clear negative structural and algorithmic results.
- Strong relevance to practice: highlights the limits of random restarts and the value of better initialization.
- Conceptual insight on local maxima versus saddles.

Cons
- Proof of Theorem 1 in the main text is too terse at the critical technical step.
- Scope (d = 1) not emphasized; limited discussion of higher-dimensional extensions.
- The first-order EM section could be clearer in derivation and assumptions.
