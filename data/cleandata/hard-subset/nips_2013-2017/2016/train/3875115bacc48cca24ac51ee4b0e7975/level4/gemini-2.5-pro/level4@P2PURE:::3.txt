This paper investigates the existence of spurious local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The analysis is focused on the simplest case: a well-specified mixture of isotropic Gaussian components with a known, shared scale. The authors demonstrate that even with infinite data, the log-likelihood surface possesses local modes that can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. The paper concentrates on specific configurations where the true mixture centers are grouped into well-separated clusters. For such configurations, it is shown that with random initialization, the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm will almost certainly fail to find the global optimum as the number of components increases. The paper is well-written, despite occasional typos.

I have two main concerns regarding the practical relevance of the findings:

1.  Theorem 1 establishes the existence of spurious local modes for a particular configuration of the true component centers, even in the infinite data limit. A key question is how likely these specific "bad" configurations are to occur in practice. The paper's practical implications would be significantly strengthened if the authors could, for instance, assume a distribution over the true centers and show that the probability of encountering such "bad" configurations increases with the number of components (k) or the data dimensionality. While lines 210-218 suggest that Theorem 2 addresses this concern, it is not immediately clear that it does. A clear statement on the likelihood of these "bad" configurations, under some reasonable assumptions, would be beneficial. Note that this concern is about the configuration of the *true* centers, not the initialization of the algorithm.

2.  Theorem 2 introduces the role of EM algorithm initialization. The authors analyze a scheme where component centers are initialized by randomly sampling from the mixture distribution. They show that for specific true center configurations, this leads to EM frequently converging to a suboptimal solution. However, consider a case where data is generated from three Gaussians: two that are close together and one that is far apart, with each component contributing N data points. The data would exhibit one dense cluster of 2N points and another sparse cluster of N points. In such a clearly clustered scenario, an initialization scheme that places, for example, two centers in the sparse region (with N points) and only one center in the dense region (with 2N points) seems counter-intuitive. Is random sampling from the mixture a practically relevant initialization strategy when the data has a clear clustered structure?

Specific Questions and Comments:

*   **L74-81:** The purpose of the example on these lines is unclear. It does not seem to convincingly argue that Srebro's conjecture holds for k=2. What is this example intended to convey?
*   **L233-235:** While requirement (2) is understandable, the necessity of requirement (1) is not obvious. Is the point that if a center is initialized between two clusters, it is not guaranteed to move toward the correct one?
*   **L267-273:** From a practical perspective, what is the significance of proving that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point? Is convergence to a saddle point more or less problematic than convergence to a local optimum?
