

This paper addresses a fundamental theoretical question about Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically whether the population likelihood function can have "bad" local maxima (i.e., local maxima with log-likelihood values arbitrarily worse than global maxima) when the mixture components are well-separated. The authors resolve an open question posed by Srebro (2007), who conjectured that for well-separated mixtures with M ≥ 3 components, all local maxima would be global maxima in the population setting. Previous work has established that for M = 2, empirical evidence supports Srebro's conjecture, though a formal proof remains open. The paper makes three main contributions: (1) construction of a GMM with M ≥ 3 components where the population likelihood has arbitrarily bad local maxima; (2) proof that EM with random initialization converges to these bad local maxima with probability at least 1−e^−Ω(M); and (3) demonstration that a first-order variant of EM also fails to find global maxima with high probability under random initialization.

**Strengths:**
1. **Originality and Significance:** The paper resolves a long-standing open question in GMM theory with significant implications for practitioners. The results fundamentally challenge the theoretical justification for EM in GMM estimation, showing that even in highly favorable settings (uniform weights, spherical Gaussians, well-separated components), local search methods cannot guarantee convergence to global optima. This insight is crucial for understanding the limitations of EM and motivates the development of better initialization schemes, which aligns with recent method-of-moments approaches (Hsu & Kakade, 2013; Moitra & Valiant, 2010).

2. **Quality:** The theoretical results are technically sound and well-justified. The authors carefully distinguish between population and sample likelihoods, and the proofs establish precise probabilistic guarantees. The paper demonstrates that the failure of EM is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points, which is an important theoretical refinement. The results extend beyond the specific case of d=1 to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments, as noted in the conclusion.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem statement to results. The geometric intuition for the construction (Section 3.1) is particularly effective in illustrating why bad local maxima exist. The distinction between the EM algorithm and its first-order variant is clearly explained, and the practical implications are well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Proof of Theorem 1:** While the main results are compelling, the proof of Theorem 1 (existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima) is somewhat brief. The authors provide limits of supremums of likelihood functions over unbounded domains (lines 288-306), but the justification for these limits is not sufficiently detailed. Specifically, the transition from pointwise convergence to convergence of suprema over domains that depend on parameters going to infinity requires more rigorous treatment. For readers without deep familiarity with likelihood theory, this section would benefit from additional technical details to fully convince them of the proof's validity.

2. **Dimensional Generalization:** The paper claims to address the general case of dimension d but restricts proofs to d=1. While the authors note that results extend to finite samples, they do not explicitly discuss whether the core theoretical results (particularly Theorem 1) hold for d > 1. The proof techniques for d=1 may not directly generalize to higher dimensions, and the authors should clarify whether the construction can be extended or what additional challenges arise in higher dimensions.

3. **Gradient EM Explanation:** The discussion of the first-order EM algorithm (lines 162-172) is somewhat difficult to follow. The derivation of Equation (5) could be clarified with a brief derivation showing how the first-order EM update relates to gradient ascent on the population likelihood.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Resolves a significant open question in GMM theory with important theoretical and practical implications.
- Provides rigorous guarantees about the failure of EM under random initialization, which is highly relevant for practitioners.
- The results are technically sound and well-motivated, with clear connections to prior work.
- The simplifications (uniform weights, spherical Gaussians) are justified as necessary for obtaining the rigorous results, and the authors appropriately acknowledge these limitations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The proof of Theorem 1 lacks sufficient detail for full verification, which is a concern for a theoretical paper.
- The paper's focus on d=1 without sufficient discussion of generalization to higher dimensions limits the scope of the theoretical contribution.
- The explanation of the first-order EM algorithm could be clearer, potentially confusing readers unfamiliar with the relationship between EM variants and gradient methods.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding the limitations of the EM algorithm for Gaussian mixture models. The results are significant, original, and well-supported by rigorous analysis. While the proof of Theorem 1 would benefit from additional detail and the paper should clarify the extension to higher dimensions, these are relatively minor concerns for a theoretical paper of this caliber. The authors have made a strong case for why careful initialization is essential for EM in GMM estimation, which will inform future work on algorithm design. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and addresses a fundamental question in the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the proof details of Theorem 1 and to clarify the dimensional generalization. The paper's insights are important for both theoretical understanding and practical implementation of GMM estimation methods.
