This paper investigates the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm in the context of estimating mean parameters for a mixture of Gaussian variables. The model assumes fixed and known mixture weights and covariance matrices, focusing on the theoretical properties of the algorithm. The authors present three key negative results: first, the likelihood function can possess local maxima even with large sample sizes, potentially yielding arbitrarily suboptimal likelihood values; second, the EM algorithm, when initialized randomly, is likely to converge to a critical point with a similarly suboptimal likelihood; and third, these results extend to the gradient EM algorithm. 

The research question is both interesting and relevant. To achieve rigorous proofs, the authors appropriately constrain their analysis to a simplified Gaussian mixture model – a known number of components, uniform mixture weights, and identity covariance matrices. While these simplifications are present, they do not detract from the value of the results, particularly given the inherent technical complexity of the proofs. The findings offer valuable insight into the behavior of the EM algorithm in practice.

It is important to note that the paper presents results for the general dimension *d*, but the proofs are specifically for *d*=1. Addressing the potential for extending these proofs to arbitrary dimensions, or acknowledging the challenges associated with higher dimensions, would strengthen the work. 

My primary concern lies with the proof of Theorem 1. While I did not examine the supplementary material, I found the presented proof to be concise, perhaps to a fault. Expanding upon this proof, even at the expense of some discussion elsewhere, would be beneficial. Specifically, the section from line 288 to 306 requires further elaboration. The authors present limits of suprema of likelihood functions, and while the individual likelihood calculations appear correct, establishing the limit of a supremum over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains is not immediately obvious. Furthermore, the assertion of continuity of the likelihood function does not automatically guarantee the stated convergence properties. Increased detail and a more extended proof would enhance reader confidence.

Finally, I found the section concerning the Gradient EM algorithm (lines 162-172) somewhat challenging to follow. Providing additional explanation regarding the derivation of Equation (5) would improve clarity.
