This paper explores the existence of local maxima in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models, focusing on the simplest possible mixture model with isotropic Gaussian components and known scale. The authors demonstrate that local maxima exist even with infinite data and are arbitrarily bad in terms of differences in log-likelihood between local and global optima. They concentrate on specific configurations where the mixture centers are divided between well-spaced clusters. Under these configurations, the authors show that random initialization makes the EM algorithm miss the global optima almost certainly as the number of components increases.

The paper is well-written, with occasional typos. However, I have two main concerns regarding the practical relevance of the paper. Firstly, Theorem 1 shows that the log-likelihood has local modes even with infinite data, but it only considers a specific configuration of the centers. I would like to see an analysis of how likely this configuration is to occur in practice, perhaps by making assumptions about the distribution of the centers and showing that "bad" configurations become increasingly likely as the number of components or dimensionality increases. Theorem 2 partially addresses these concerns but does not provide a clear statement on the likelihood of "bad" configurations.

Secondly, Theorem 2 involves initializing the EM algorithm by randomly sampling the mixture. Under specific configurations of the centers, the EM algorithm often fails to converge to the global optimum. However, I question the practical relevance of random initialization when the data is clearly clustered. It seems unlikely that someone would initialize EM by placing centers in areas with fewer data points.

Additionally, I have some minor points and questions. On lines 74-81, I am unsure what the example is meant to convey, as it does not convince me that Srebro's conjecture holds when k = 2. I also do not understand the requirement on lines 233-235, specifically why initializing a center between two clusters is problematic. Furthermore, I am unclear on the practical importance of the result on lines 267-273, which proves that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point. Is converging to a saddle point more or less problematic than converging to a local optimum?

Minor points include: adding "that the" before "gradient" on line 18, changing "quality of the estimate" to "estimates" on line 38, rephrasing the sentence on line 60 to "to characterize under which conditions efficient algorithms...", clarifying the meaning of "O(d^1/2)-separated" on line 138, removing or rephrasing the redundant sentence on line 172, changing "equivalent of" to "equivalent to" on line 177, clarifying the meaning of "well-separated" constant on line 218, correcting the typo on line 220, explaining the meaning of "\Omega (k)" on line 225, correcting the typo on line 245, adding "that" after "recall" on line 249, and adding "that" after "claim" on line 308.

Strengths of the paper include its clear writing style and the significance of its results. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the existence of local maxima in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models and demonstrate the limitations of the EM algorithm in converging to the global optimum. However, the paper could benefit from further discussion on the practical relevance of the results and the likelihood of "bad" configurations occurring in practice.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the existence of local maxima in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models.
* The authors demonstrate the limitations of the EM algorithm in converging to the global optimum.
* The paper is well-written and clear in its presentation.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The practical relevance of the results is unclear, particularly with regards to the likelihood of "bad" configurations occurring in practice.
* The paper could benefit from further discussion on the implications of the results for real-world applications.
* Some minor points and typos need to be addressed.
