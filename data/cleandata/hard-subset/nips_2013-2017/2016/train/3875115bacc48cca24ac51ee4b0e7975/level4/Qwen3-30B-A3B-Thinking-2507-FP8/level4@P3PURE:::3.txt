

The paper investigates the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models under the simplest setting: isotropic Gaussian components with known scale and a well-specified model. The authors demonstrate that local modes exist even with infinite data, and that these modes can be arbitrarily poor in terms of log-likelihood difference from the global optimum. The analysis focuses on specific configurations where mixture centers are partitioned into well-spaced clusters. Under such configurations, the authors show that random initialization causes the EM algorithm to miss the global optimum almost surely as the number of components increases. The paper is well-written but contains occasional typos.  

I have two primary concerns regarding practical relevance. First, Theorem 1 establishes local modes under a specific center configuration, but the paper does not address how likely this configuration is to occur in practice. To strengthen the practical relevance, the authors should specify assumptions about the distribution of true centers and demonstrate that "bad" configurations become increasingly probable as the number of components \(k\) or dimensionality grows. While lines L210–L218 claim Theorem 2 addresses this, it does not provide a clear statement on the likelihood of such configurations under reasonable assumptions. Crucially, this pertains to the *positions of the true centers* (not initializations).  

Second, Theorem 2 examines EM initialization via random sampling of the mixture. The authors show that under specific center configurations, EM often fails to converge to the global optimum. However, consider a scenario with three Gaussians: two closely spaced clusters (each with \(N\) points) and one well-separated cluster (with \(N\) points). Here, \(2N\) points cluster near the first two Gaussians, while \(N\) points cluster near the third. It seems implausible that a practitioner would initialize EM with two centers in the \(2N\)-point region and one center in the \(N\)-point region, especially given the clear separation between clusters. The relevance of random initialization in such clearly clustered data warrants justification.  

Additionally:  
- L74–L81: The example does not convincingly support the Sresbro conjecture for \(k=2\); its purpose is unclear.  
- L233–L235: Requirement (1) is not justified. The authors state that initializing a center between two clusters prevents guaranteeing movement toward the correct cluster, but the necessity of Requirement (1) is unclear.  
- L267–L273: The result on EM avoiding saddle points is stated, but its practical significance is unexplained. Why is avoiding saddle points more or less problematic than converging to a local optimum?
