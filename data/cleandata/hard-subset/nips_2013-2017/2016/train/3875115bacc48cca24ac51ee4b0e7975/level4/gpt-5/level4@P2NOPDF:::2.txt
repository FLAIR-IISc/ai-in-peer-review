This paper studies the expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the component means in a Gaussian mixture model where the number of components, the mixture weights, and the covariance matrices are known and fixed. The contribution is theoretical and establishes three negative results:
1) Even with large sample sizes, the likelihood can have local maxima whose values are arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum.
2) With random initialization, EM converges with high probability to a critical point whose likelihood is arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum.
3) The same phenomenon occurs for gradient EM.

The problem is important and the results are thought-provoking. To obtain rigorous proofs, the paper focuses on a simplified setting: known number of components, uniform weights, and identity covariances. I view these simplifications as reasonable given the technical difficulty of the analysis, and the results are already informative for understanding EM in practice.

One point that merits clarification is dimensionality. The paper is written as if results apply to general dimension d, yet the proofs address d=1. It would help if the authors could either indicate how the arguments extend to arbitrary d, or briefly explain the obstacles in higher dimensions.

My main concern is the proof of Theorem 1 (I have not yet read the supplementary material). The current proof is too terse; I would prefer a more detailed argument, even if that requires shortening some discussion elsewhere. In particular, the steps around lines 288–306 require more justification. The authors take limits (as γ or R → ∞) of suprema of the likelihood over domains that either expand or are unbounded, and they rely on continuity of the likelihood. While limits of the likelihood at fixed parameter values seem straightforward (e.g., the three equations around line 288 are plausible), passing limits through suprema over changing or unbounded domains is not immediate. Continuity alone does not guarantee the stated convergences. It would strengthen the proof to:
- Specify the domains precisely and state whether they are compact or how non-compactness is controlled.
- Justify interchanging limits and suprema (e.g., via uniform convergence, equicontinuity, monotone convergence of sets, or explicit ε–δ/ε–M arguments).
- Provide bounding lemmas that show the contribution outside large compact sets is negligible.
These additions would make the argument fully convincing.

A secondary concern is the presentation of the gradient EM results (lines 162–172). This section was harder to follow than the rest. It would be helpful to expand the derivation leading to Equation (5), making explicit any assumptions (e.g., step sizes, projections, or surrogate function definitions) and the relationship between gradient EM and the standard EM updates.

Overall, I find the question addressed by the paper interesting and the negative results valuable. With a clearer and more detailed proof of Theorem 1 and a more transparent derivation for gradient EM (especially Equation (5)), the paper would make a strong theoretical contribution to our understanding of EM’s behavior in mixture models.
