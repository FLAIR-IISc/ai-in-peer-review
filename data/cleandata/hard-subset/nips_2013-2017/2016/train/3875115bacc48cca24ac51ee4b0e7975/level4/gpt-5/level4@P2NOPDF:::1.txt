This paper analyzes the convergence behavior of EM and related iterative maximum-likelihood heuristics for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, and establishes a set of negative results showing failure to reach the global maximum, even in the population setting. The main findings are:

- Even with three well-separated clusters and infinite data, algorithms such as EM can get trapped in suboptimal local maxima.
- For mixtures with k components, random initialization leads EM to the correct solution with probability at most exponentially small in k.
- Gradient EM generically avoids strict saddle points, indicating that failures are primarily due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

Overall, the results present a coherent narrative that EM need not converge to the global optimum, including in the idealized noise-free (population) regime. However, I found the outcomes largely unsurprising, and the techniques proceed along expected lines.

The central counterexample involves a 3-component mixture where two components are relatively close to each other while the third is very far from both. The paper shows that, for the log-likelihood objective, there exists a local maximum placing one center near the two close components and two centers near the far-away component. This behavior is intuitive, especially when the separation between true centers is much larger than sqrt(d): in that regime, cluster overlap is negligible, and learning spherical Gaussian mixtures resembles k-means clustering. Analogous constructions are known to create bad instances for Lloyd’s algorithm in k-means.

The paper extends this construction to k components through a recursive design. While this extension requires nontrivial work, the qualitative outcome again aligns with expectations from the k-means literature. Similarly, the negative result for random initialization mirrors what is known for k-means: with naive random seeds, success probability can be exponentially small in k. This echo of k-means failures also suggests standard mitigations, such as selecting on the order of k log k initial centers or using distance-squared (k-means++) sampling.

The third result, that gradient EM does not converge to strict saddle points generically, complements the above by clarifying the nature of the failure modes: one should expect suboptimal local maxima, not saddle-point stagnation. This is consistent with modern nonconvex optimization insights and helps isolate the source of EM’s difficulties in these settings.

In sum, the paper provides a clear set of impossibility results for EM under high separation, population-level conditions. While the exposition is coherent and the constructions are carefully executed, the phenomena they illustrate are, in my view, anticipated from the close analogy to k-means in this regime.

Comments and suggestions:
- Prior work by Kumar and Kannan gives convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic for k-means (and thus for well-separated spherical Gaussian mixtures under suitable conditions). Although EM and Lloyd’s method behave differently when components are not well separated (as highlighted, for example, by Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu), they are essentially similar under large separation—the exact regime of the paper’s counterexamples. A direct, systematic comparison with k-means and Lloyd’s algorithm would strengthen the paper. It would help clarify which negative results are inherently about EM versus those that arise from k-means-like behavior under large separation, and would position the contributions relative to known guarantees and standard remedies (e.g., k-means++ initialization).

Overall assessment: The paper offers a unified and well-argued account of EM’s failure to find global optima in separated Gaussian mixtures, but the results and techniques largely corroborate expectations drawn from the k-means literature. A deeper comparative discussion with Lloyd’s algorithm and related guarantees would improve the paper’s clarity and contextual significance.
