

This paper addresses an open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the absence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors establish three key results: (1) They construct a counterexample showing that for M ≥ 3 well-separated, equally-weighted, unit-variance spherical Gaussians in d=1, the population log-likelihood contains local maxima whose values can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum—directly resolving Srebro's conjecture. (2) They prove that with random initialization (drawing centers uniformly from the underlying mixture), the EM algorithm and its first-order variant converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1−e^−Ω(M), demonstrating exponentially small success probability in M. (3) They further show that the first-order EM algorithm avoids strict saddle points almost surely, indicating that poor convergence is attributable to bad local maxima rather than saddle points. These results extend prior work on EM convergence (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015; Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007) by characterizing the structural limitations of local search in GMMs even under idealized settings.

Quality: The proofs for Theorem 1 are technically sound and provide a rigorous construction of bad local maxima. The argument for Theorem 2 logically extends Theorem 1 via a hierarchical grouping strategy, and the avoidance of saddle points (Theorem 4) is supported by recent advances in non-convex optimization (Lee et al., 2016). The analysis is thorough and appropriately contextualizes the population setting versus finite-sample behavior.

Clarity: The paper excels in exposition, clearly motivating the problem via Srebro's conjecture and prior EM literature. The intuitive geometric explanation for the M=3 case (Figure 1 in the paper) effectively conveys the core insight. The formal statements of results are precise, and the connection between the theoretical results and practical EM limitations is well-articulated.

Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by resolving a long-standing open question about GMM likelihood surfaces. The novel construction of bad local maxima (Theorem 1) and the hierarchical initialization analysis (Theorem 2) represent a clever synthesis of structural and algorithmic insights not previously explored in NIPS proceedings (e.g., distinct from Arora et al., 2005; Moitra and Valiant, 2010).

Significance: The findings have immediate practical implications: they rigorously justify the necessity of careful initialization for EM even in well-separated settings, challenging the common heuristic of random initialization. Theoretically, the results open new avenues for studying non-convex optimization landscapes in clustering (e.g., k-means) and motivate future work on initialization schemes beyond random sampling. The paper’s focus on population likelihood also provides a clean theoretical foundation for understanding finite-sample behavior via standard empirical process arguments.
