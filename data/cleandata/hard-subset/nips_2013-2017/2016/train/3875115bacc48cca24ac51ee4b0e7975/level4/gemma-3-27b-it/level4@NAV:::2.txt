This paper investigates the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm in the context of estimating mean parameters for a Gaussian mixture model. The study focuses on a scenario where the number of Gaussian components, mixture weights, and covariance matrices are pre-defined and fixed. The work presents a theoretical analysis, demonstrating three negative results concerning the EM algorithm’s performance. First, the likelihood function can exhibit local maxima even with large sample sizes, potentially yielding likelihood values significantly lower than the global maximum. Second, randomly initialized EM algorithms are shown to converge with high probability to critical points with suboptimal likelihood values. Third, these negative results extend to the gradient EM algorithm as well. 

The research question is both interesting and relevant. The authors appropriately restrict their analysis to a simplified Gaussian mixture model – known number of components, uniform mixture weights, and identity covariance matrices – to facilitate rigorous proofs. While these simplifications are present, I believe they are justifiable given the inherent technical challenges of the proofs and the informative nature of the findings. The results provide valuable insight into the practical behavior of the EM algorithm.

It is important to note that the presented results are established for the one-dimensional case (d=1), despite the paper generally framing the problem in arbitrary dimension *d*. The authors should address the generalizability of their proofs to higher dimensions, either by discussing the feasibility of extending the proof techniques or by acknowledging the additional complexities that arise when *d* > 1.

My primary concern lies with the proof of Theorem 1. While I did not examine the supplementary material, I found the presented proof to be concise to a fault. Expanding on the proof, potentially at the cost of reducing some of the paper’s discussions, would be beneficial. Specifically, the reasoning between lines 288 and 306 warrants greater detail. While the individual likelihood function evaluations appear correct, establishing the limit of a supremum over unbounded domains or domains dependent on parameters approaching infinity is not immediately obvious.  Furthermore, the authors’ claims regarding the continuity of the likelihood function do not automatically imply the stated convergence properties, and more justification is needed to support these conclusions. A more detailed proof would strengthen the reader’s confidence in the theorem’s validity.

Finally, I found the discussion of the Gradient EM algorithm (lines 162-172) comparatively difficult to follow.  Providing further explanation regarding the derivation of Equation (5) would improve clarity.
