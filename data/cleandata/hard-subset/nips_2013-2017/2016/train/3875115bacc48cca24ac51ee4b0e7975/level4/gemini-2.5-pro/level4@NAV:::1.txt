This work investigates the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians. The authors establish several key results demonstrating the algorithm's failure to converge to the global maximum likelihood solution:

1.  They demonstrate that even in an idealized setting with infinite samples and a three-component mixture with widely separated centers, EM can be trapped in suboptimal local maxima.
2.  For a mixture of *k* components, the probability of success for EM with a random initialization is shown to be exponentially small, decaying at a rate of exp(-Ω(k)).
3.  The paper argues that the primary obstacle to convergence is the presence of poor local maxima, as Gradient EM is known to avoid strict saddle points under generic conditions.

Collectively, these findings provide a consistent narrative about EM's non-convergence to global optima. Nevertheless, the central conclusions and the techniques employed to derive them feel largely anticipated. The primary negative example is constructed from a three-component mixture where two components are proximal and a third is distant. The analysis reveals a local maximum where one center is positioned between the two nearby components while two centers cluster near the distant one. This outcome is quite intuitive, especially in the high-separation regime (center separation >> √d) considered.

In such a high-separation scenario, learning a Gaussian mixture model closely resembles k-means clustering. Indeed, analogous counterexamples are well-documented for Lloyd's algorithm in the k-means literature. The extension of this example to *k* components, while involving an elegant recursive construction, follows a predictable path. Similarly, the result on the failure of random initialization mirrors established findings for k-means, where the same failure probability motivates more sophisticated initialization schemes (e.g., k-means++ or distance-squared sampling).

In summary, although the paper effectively communicates a unified message regarding the failure of EM to find global optima, the contributions seem to confirm existing intuition rather than providing fundamentally new insights.

**Comments:**

1.  A more thorough discussion of the relationship with k-means clustering would strengthen the paper. For instance, works like Kumar-Kannan offer convergence guarantees for Lloyd's algorithm. While EM and Lloyd's heuristic behave differently when Gaussian components overlap (as noted by Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu), they become nearly identical under the large separation conditions that are central to this paper's negative examples. Therefore, a direct comparison with Lloyd's heuristic for k-means, explicitly contrasting the convergence properties and failure modes, would provide valuable context for the presented results.
