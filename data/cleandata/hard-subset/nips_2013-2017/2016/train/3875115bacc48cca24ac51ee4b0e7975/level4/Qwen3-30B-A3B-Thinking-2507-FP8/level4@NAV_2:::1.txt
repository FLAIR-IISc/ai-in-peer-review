

The paper addresses an important open question regarding the convergence properties of the EM algorithm for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically focusing on the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function. The authors provide two key results: (1) they construct an example of a well-separated, equally-weighted mixture of three spherical Gaussians with arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood, thereby resolving Srebro's [2007] conjecture that no such bad local maxima exist; and (2) they demonstrate that the EM algorithm (and a first-order variant) with random initialization converges to such bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^{-Ω(M)} for M ≥ 3 components. This work builds upon previous NIPS contributions on GMMs, including Balakrishnan et al. [2015] on local convergence guarantees for two-component mixtures, and Dasgupta and Schulman [2007] on two-round EM variants, while addressing a fundamental gap in understanding the behavior of EM in the population setting.

Quality: The technical contributions are sound, with rigorous proofs establishing the existence of bad local maxima and the failure of random initialization for the EM algorithm. The paper carefully addresses the relationship between the population likelihood and finite-sample behavior, and the construction of bad local maxima is well-justified. The authors properly acknowledge the limitations of their results (e.g., restricted to M ≥ 3 components) and provide clear connections to the broader literature.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions of the problem setting, the EM algorithm, and the first-order variant. The main results are stated precisely, and the geometric intuition behind the construction of bad local maxima is well-explained. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications for practitioners, particularly regarding initialization strategies beyond random sampling.

Originality: The paper resolves a longstanding open question in the GMM literature, providing a negative answer to Srebro's conjecture. While the construction of bad local maxima for M=3 components may seem intuitive in hindsight (two components close together, one far away), the extension to general M via a recursive construction is novel and non-trivial. The connection to the failure of random initialization for the EM algorithm, and the distinction between bad local maxima and strict saddle points, represents a significant contribution that advances our understanding of non-convex optimization in GMMs.

Significance: The results have important practical implications for practitioners using EM in GMM estimation, demonstrating that even in favorable settings (well-separated, equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians), random initialization can fail with high probability. This work provides theoretical justification for the need for careful initialization schemes in practice, supporting recent algorithmic advances based on the method of moments [Hsu and Kakade, 2013, Moitra and Valiant, 2010]. While the negative result may not be entirely surprising to experts, the rigorous characterization of the failure probability (1 - e^{-Ω(M)}) and the distinction between local maxima and saddle points significantly advance our understanding.

Strengths: The paper provides a complete and rigorous resolution of an important open problem in GMM theory. The theoretical results are well-motivated and supported by careful analysis. The connection between the population likelihood behavior and practical algorithmic consequences is compelling. The distinction between local maxima and saddle points via Theorem 4 is particularly valuable.

Weaknesses: The construction of bad local maxima for M=3 components may seem somewhat expected to experts in mixture models, as it mirrors the behavior of k-means clustering with insufficient separation. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with k-means clustering literature (e.g., Kumar and Kannan [2004] on Lloyd's algorithm), which provides similar failure modes for well-separated clusters. While the recursive construction for general M is novel, the techniques used to establish the failure probability follow expected lines in non-convex optimization theory.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper resolves a significant open question in the GMM literature with rigorous theoretical results that have clear practical implications. The connection between population likelihood behavior and algorithmic performance is well-established, and the distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points provides important theoretical insights.

Arguments against acceptance: The results, while technically sound, may not represent a substantial advance in the state of the art for experts familiar with the GMM literature. The negative result regarding bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 components is largely expected in the context of non-convex optimization, and the techniques employed follow standard approaches for analyzing local search algorithms.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the limitations of the EM algorithm for GMMs, with implications for both theoretical and practical work. While the results may not be entirely surprising to experts, the rigorous characterization of the failure probability and the distinction between local maxima and saddle points represent meaningful advances in the field. The paper should be accepted with minor revisions to strengthen the comparison with k-means clustering literature and to elaborate on practical implications.
