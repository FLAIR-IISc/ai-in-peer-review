This paper addresses the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters of a Gaussian mixture model. The number of components, mixture weights, and covariance matrices are assumed known and fixed. The paper presents three negative results regarding the EM algorithm's performance: 1) even with infinite sample size, the likelihood function can exhibit local maxima with arbitrarily worse values than the global maximum; 2) the EM algorithm, when initialized randomly, converges with high probability to a suboptimal critical point; and 3) the same holds for a first-order variant of the EM algorithm. I find the addressed question interesting and significant. To achieve rigorous proofs, the authors focus on a simplified model – known number of components, uniform mixture weights, and identity covariance matrices. I do not believe these simplifications detract from the results, given the inherent technicality of the proofs and the informative nature of the findings. These results are insightful and may be valuable for a deeper understanding of the EM algorithm’s practical behavior. It’s worth noting that the paper presents results for a general dimension *d*, but the proofs are primarily for *d*=1. The authors might benefit from discussing whether similar proofs are feasible for arbitrary *d* and, if not, outlining the additional challenges posed by higher dimensions. My primary concern lies with the proof of Theorem 1 (and I haven't examined the supplementary materials). I feel the proof is somewhat condensed and could benefit from greater detail, even at the expense of shortening other discussions. Specifically, the section from line 288 to 306 requires more elaboration. While I accept the computations of limits of likelihood functions evaluated at fixed parameters, obtaining the limit of a supremum over an unbounded domain, or a domain dependent on parameters approaching infinity, is not immediately obvious. Furthermore, the authors’ assertion of continuity of the likelihood function doesn’t directly imply the stated convergence properties. Increased detail and a more extended proof would enhance the reader’s confidence in the theorem’s validity. Finally, I found the section discussing the first-order EM algorithm (lines 162-172) somewhat challenging to follow. Providing more explanation regarding the derivation of Equation (5) could improve clarity.
