The paper investigates the presence of local modes in the log-likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models, focusing on the simplest setting: isotropic Gaussian components with known variance and a correctly specified model. The authors prove that local modes persist even in the infinite-sample regime and that they can be arbitrarily suboptimal relative to the global maximum in terms of log-likelihood gap. The analysis concentrates on specific geometric arrangements, especially when component means are grouped into well-separated clusters. In these settings, the authors show that with random initialization, EM will almost surely fail to reach the global optimum as the number of components grows. The manuscript is generally well written, with a few typographical issues.

I have two primary concerns regarding practical relevance.
1. Theorem 1 establishes that the log-likelihood admits local modes even with infinite data, demonstrated via a specific configuration of the component means. How likely is such a configuration in practice? I would be more persuaded of the practical impact if the authors posited a distribution over the true centers and then showed that “bad” configurations become increasingly probable as the number of components k or the ambient dimension increases. Lines 210–218 state that Theorem 2 addresses this, but I remain unconvinced. A clear statement quantifying the likelihood of “bad” configurations under explicit assumptions would be very helpful. To be clear, I am asking about the prevalence of “bad” configurations (i.e., the true mean locations), not “bad” initializations.
2. Theorem 2 brings EM initialization into play. The authors consider initializing centers by random samples from the mixture. Under certain center configurations, this leads EM to miss the global optimum with high probability. Consider three Gaussians, two close together and one far away, with N samples from each component. Then roughly 2N points lie near the first cluster and N near the distant one. It seems odd that one would initialize EM by placing two centers where there are only N points and one center where there are N points, particularly when the two clusters are clearly well separated. Is random initialization a realistic choice when the data exhibit obvious clustering structure?

L74–81 I’m unsure what this example is intended to demonstrate. It does not persuade me that Srebro’s conjecture holds for k = 2; what is its role?

L233–235 I understand condition (2), but I do not see why condition (1) is necessary. Are you saying that if a center is initialized between two clusters, you cannot ensure it drifts toward the correct one?

L267–273 You show EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point. From a practical standpoint, why does this matter? Is convergence to a saddle point more or less problematic than convergence to a local optimum?
