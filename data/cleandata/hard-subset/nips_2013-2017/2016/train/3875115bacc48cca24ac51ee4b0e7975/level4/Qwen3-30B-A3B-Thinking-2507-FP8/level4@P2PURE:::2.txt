

This paper investigates the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters in a Gaussian mixture model, where the number of components, mixture weights, and covariance matrices are known and fixed. The work presents three theoretical negative results regarding EM. First, the likelihood function may exhibit local maxima with values arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum, even for large sample sizes. Second, when randomly initialized, the EM algorithm converges with high probability to a critical point with a likelihood value arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum. Third, analogous negative results hold for the gradient EM algorithm. The question addressed is interesting and merits attention. The proofs are restricted to a simplified model—specifically, a known number of components, uniform mixture weights, and identity covariance matrices—to maintain rigor given the technical complexity. These simplifications are justified, as the results remain informative despite the restricted setting. The findings are valuable for understanding EM's behavior in practice. The paper claims to address general dimension $d$, yet the proofs focus solely on $d=1$. The authors should clarify whether similar results extend to arbitrary $d$, and if not, briefly discuss the additional challenges for $d > 1$. My primary concern is the proof of Theorem 1 (I did not review the supplementary material). The proof is too brief; it would benefit from greater detail, particularly in the segment from line 288 to 306. The authors compute limits (as $\gamma$ or $R \to \infty$) of supremums of the likelihood over certain domains. While I accept the correctness of the limit computations for fixed parameters (e.g., the three equations on line 288), the convergence of suprema over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains is non-trivial. The assertion of likelihood continuity does not directly imply the stated convergence types. Additional detail is required to fully convince the reader of Theorem 1's validity. A secondary minor concern pertains to the Gradient EM section (lines 162–172), which is less clear than the rest of the paper. The derivation of Equation (5) warrants further explanation.
