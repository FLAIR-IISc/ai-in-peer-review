This paper investigates the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters of a Gaussian mixture model. Importantly, the number of Gaussian components, the mixture weights, and the covariance matrices are assumed to be known and fixed. The paper presents a theoretical analysis, proving three negative results concerning the EM algorithm. First, it demonstrates that even with large sample sizes, the likelihood function can possess local maxima with arbitrarily lower likelihood values than the global maximum. Second, the paper shows that, when randomly initialized, the EM algorithm converges with high probability to a critical point yielding a likelihood value arbitrarily smaller than the maximum possible. Third, the same negative outcome holds for the gradient EM algorithm. 

I find the question addressed by this paper interesting and worthy of investigation. To achieve rigorous proofs, the authors necessarily restrict their analysis to a simplified Gaussian mixture model: the number of components is known, the weights are uniform, and the covariance matrices are set to the identity matrix. I do not view these simplifications as a significant limitation, given the inherent technical complexity of the proofs and the informative nature of the resulting conclusions. These results are valuable and offer potential insights into the practical behavior of the EM algorithm.

It’s worth noting that the paper frames its analysis in general dimension *d*, while the proofs are presented specifically for *d*=1. It would strengthen the paper if the authors commented on the possibility of extending the proofs to arbitrary dimensions. If such an extension is not feasible, a brief discussion of the additional difficulties encountered in higher dimensions would be beneficial.

My primary concern lies with the proof of Theorem 1 (I have not examined the proofs in the supplementary material). I believe the proof is somewhat condensed and would benefit from greater detail, even at the expense of shortening some of the surrounding discussions. Specifically, the section from line 288 to 306 requires further elaboration. The authors present limits of suprema of likelihood functions over certain domains as gamma or R approaches infinity. I do not question the computation of these limits for fixed parameters; for instance, I accept that the three equations on line 288 are likely correct. However, determining the limit of a maximum is not straightforward when the domain is unbounded or depends on parameters tending to infinity. Similarly, while the authors state the continuity of the likelihood function in several places, this does not directly imply the types of convergence they claim. I believe a more detailed and extended proof is needed to fully convince the reader of its validity. 

Finally, I have a minor concern regarding the section on the Gradient EM algorithm, from line 162 to 172. I found this portion of the paper more challenging to follow than the rest. In particular, the authors could provide additional explanation regarding the derivation of Equation (5).
