

The paper investigates the convergence properties of the EM algorithm and related iterative methods for maximum likelihood estimation in spherical Gaussian mixture models, establishing three key results demonstrating non-convergence to global optima: (1) Even for mixtures with three well-separated components under infinite data, EM can converge to local maxima; (2) For mixtures of $k$ Gaussians, random initialization succeeds with probability at most $\exp(-\Omega(k))$; (3) Gradient EM typically avoids strict saddle points, indicating that poor local maxima—not saddle points—are the primary obstacle. While the results form a coherent narrative about EM’s failure to reach global optima, they largely align with established expectations rather than introducing novel insights.  

The central counterexample involves a three-component mixture: two clusters are close together, while the third is sufficiently isolated (separated by more than $\Theta(\sqrt{d})$). The authors demonstrate a local maximum where one center aligns near the two proximate clusters, and two centers localize near the isolated component. This behavior is intuitive under strong separation, as the problem reduces to a k-means-like clustering task where clusters are non-overlapping. Similar counterexamples for k-means, such as those illustrating Lloyd’s algorithm failures, provide direct precedent. The extension to $k$ components via a recursive construction is technically sound but follows naturally from known k-means failure modes. Similarly, the random initialization failure probability mirrors k-means results, where techniques like distance-squared sampling are adopted to mitigate such issues.  

The main contribution—highlighting EM’s non-convergence under standard conditions—does not significantly advance beyond what is already understood from k-means analysis. Specifically, the paper’s bad examples and failure modes for EM under large separation are nearly identical to those for Lloyd’s heuristic, as noted in Balakrishnan et al. and Kumar & Kannan. Kumar & Kannan establish convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s algorithm under sufficient separation, and while EM differs when clusters overlap (as in Balakrishnan et al.), it behaves analogously to Lloyd’s under the large-separation regime used in the paper’s counterexamples. The authors should explicitly contrast their results with Kumar & Kannan’s guarantees, clarifying the relationship between EM and Lloyd’s algorithm under separation conditions. This comparison would strengthen the paper’s positioning relative to existing literature on mixture model optimization.  

Overall, the technical arguments are sound and well-structured, but the novelty is limited. The results reinforce rather than challenge the prevailing view that EM’s convergence behavior mirrors k-means under strong separation, where non-convergence to global optima is expected. The recursive construction for $k$ components is a minor technical extension, not a conceptual leap. For a venue like NIPS, the work would benefit from either: (i) identifying a regime where EM’s behavior fundamentally differs from k-means (e.g., under moderate separation), or (ii) demonstrating that the observed non-convergence persists under conditions where k-means succeeds. Without such a distinction, the paper’s contribution appears incremental.  

The review is well-written technically but would be significantly strengthened by a clearer articulation of the gap relative to k-means literature. The proposed comparison with Kumar & Kannan’s work is essential to contextualize the findings and justify their novelty. As presented, the paper describes a known phenomenon with a technically correct but unsurprising analysis.
