This paper investigates the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, and more broadly iterative Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) heuristics, when applied to learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians. The authors demonstrate scenarios where these algorithms fail to converge to a global optimum, presenting three key results: (1) even with well-separated clusters and an infinite number of samples, EM can become trapped in local optima; (2) the probability of successful random initialization for EM with *k* Gaussians is bounded above by exp(-Ω(*k*)); and (3) gradient-based EM generally does not converge to strict saddle points, indicating that poor local maxima are the primary obstacle to optimization.  The paper’s strength lies in its cohesive presentation of these results, collectively illustrating EM's susceptibility to non-global optima even in the limit of infinite data.

However, I found the findings to be largely unsurprising and the methodologies employed fairly standard. The central illustrative example features a mixture of three Gaussians: two closely positioned components and a third significantly distant. The authors demonstrate the existence of a local maximum where one center converges near the pair of closer components and two centers cluster around the isolated Gaussian. This behavior is readily anticipated, particularly when the distance between clusters significantly exceeds the square root of the dimensionality (*d*).  Under such separation, cluster overlap is minimized, and the problem effectively reduces to a k-means-like scenario.  Analogous instances are known to cause difficulties for Lloyd’s algorithm in k-means clustering. While extending this example to *k* components via a recursive construction represents a worthwhile effort, the outcome feels predictable. 

The analysis of random initialization also reveals a failure probability mirroring that observed in k-means clustering, for similar reasons.  This parallels the motivation in k-means for employing initialization strategies such as selecting *k* log *k* centers or utilizing distance-squared sampling.  

In summary, the paper presents a logically sound argument regarding EM’s non-convergence to global optima. Nevertheless, the overall narrative feels somewhat incremental and follows established lines of reasoning.

A particularly useful addition would be a more detailed comparison with k-means clustering and Lloyd’s heuristic. While EM and Lloyd’s differ when Gaussian clusters are not well-separated (as highlighted in prior work, such as Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu), they appear to behave similarly when separation is substantial – a condition present in the paper’s key examples. Contrasting the results in this paper with existing convergence guarantees for k-means (e.g., those provided by Kumar and Kannan) would strengthen the analysis and better position the contribution within the broader literature on clustering and MLE.
