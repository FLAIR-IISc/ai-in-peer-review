- Paper settles Srebro’s conjecture by constructing counterexamples for M ≥ 3 showing population GMM likelihoods can have arbitrarily bad spurious local maxima.  
- Counterexamples hold even for well-separated, equally weighted, spherical components.  
- With random i.i.d. initialization from the mixture, EM and first-order EM converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e−Ω(M).  
- First-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, indicating failures arise from bad local maxima rather than saddle points.  
- Builds on and contrasts prior EM and mixture-learning work (clustering/moments) and recent optimization landscape results.  
- Technical arguments are precise and the geometric M=3, d=1 construction is intuitive; recursive construction for the failure probability is plausible.  
- The structural counterexample directly refutes the long-standing conjecture and algorithmic consequences align with practical initialization schemes.  
- Analysis is at the population level; finite-sample extensions are claimed but not explicitly quantified.  
- Paper is clearly written, well organized, and balances intuition with proofs; minor typos and bibliography issues remain.  
- Definition of “well-separated” is provided but explicit constants in key statements are lacking.  
- Original and significant: provides explicit counterexamples and quantifies exponential failure probability for common algorithms.  
- Results imply practitioners need informed initialization (e.g., moment- or clustering-based) since random restarts can be exponentially ineffective.  
- Strength: resolves an open problem with a clean constructive example and clear algorithmic implications.  
- Strength: distinguishes bad local maxima from saddles via dynamical-systems-based strict-saddle avoidance for first-order EM.  
- Weakness: missing explicit constants for separation, gap, and the universal constant in the e−cM bound.  
- Weakness: lacks a finite-sample corollary detailing required sample sizes and stability to noise.  
- Weakness: no empirical simulations to illustrate bad local maxima or the dependence on M.  
- Weakness: strict-saddle avoidance proved only for first-order EM; EM proper lacks an analogous result or discussion of technical obstacles.  
- Weakness: unclear whether constructions extend beyond equal weights/unit variances; extensions should be discussed.  
- Suggestion: add formal statement/context for the M = 2 case to delineate the boundary of positive results.  
- Minor comments: fix typos, bibliography formatting, notation consistency (E/M-step indices), clarify Figure 1, and state precise stepsize conditions for first-order EM.  
- Arguments for acceptance: resolves a notable open question, has high theoretical and practical impact, and is well written.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks empirical illustration, explicit constants, and finite-sample exploration; strict-saddle result limited to first-order EM.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution worthy of acceptance with minor revisions addressing constants, finite-sample discussion, and polishing references.
