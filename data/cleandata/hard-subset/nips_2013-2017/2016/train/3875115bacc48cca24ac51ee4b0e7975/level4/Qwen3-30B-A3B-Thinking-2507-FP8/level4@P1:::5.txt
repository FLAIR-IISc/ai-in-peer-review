

This paper resolves an important open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors provide a negative answer by constructing a counterexample: for any M ≥ 3, they exhibit a well-separated, equally-weighted mixture of M spherical Gaussians (with unit variance) whose population log-likelihood contains local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. This directly refutes Srebro’s conjecture that such bad local maxima do not exist in the infinite-sample limit for well-behaved GMMs.

The paper makes two key contributions. First, it establishes the structural result that bad local maxima exist in the population likelihood under favorable conditions (well-separated, spherical, equal-weighted Gaussians). Second, it demonstrates the algorithmic consequence: the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 - e^{-cM} for a universal constant c > 0. Crucially, the authors prove that this failure is due to bad local maxima—not saddle points—by showing the first-order EM algorithm avoids strict saddle points almost surely (Theorem 4). This distinction is critical, as it clarifies that the core issue is the presence of poor local optima, not the typical saddle-point challenges in non-convex optimization.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-structured. The authors begin with a concise overview of GMMs, EM, and initialization schemes, then precisely state their results. The proof intuition for M=3 (d=1) is particularly effective, illustrating how a "collapsed" initialization (e.g., two centers assigned to one true component) can trap the algorithm in a suboptimal local maximum. The technical arguments are rigorous, with proofs avoiding unnecessary smoothness assumptions via tailored properties of the first-order EM update. The implications for practice are stark: even in idealized settings, random initialization fails exponentially often in M, necessitating careful initialization strategies (e.g., method-of-moments pre-estimators) for reliable GMM estimation.

Regarding significance, this work addresses a fundamental gap in understanding non-convex optimization for GMMs. While prior work (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) studied local convergence for M=2, this paper establishes the *inherent limitation* of local search for M ≥ 3 in the population setting. This advances the state of the art by revealing a previously unknown barrier to global convergence in a canonical statistical model, with implications for the design of robust algorithms beyond GMMs. The results also align with recent theoretical work on non-convex optimization (e.g., Lee et al., 2016), showing that for GMMs, the primary obstacle is bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria. The technical arguments are sound, with theorems well-supported by rigorous proofs (deferred to the appendix but summarized clearly). The clarity is outstanding—readers can reconstruct the intuition and key steps without consulting supplementary material. Originality is high: the counterexample is novel, and the connection between structural properties and algorithmic failure is fresh. The significance is demonstrable, as the findings directly impact practical GMM estimation and theoretical understanding of non-convex likelihoods.

Minor suggestions: The paper could briefly discuss the computational cost of repeated random initialization (e.g., "to achieve constant success probability, one must repeat EM e^{Ω(M)} times"), though this is implicit in Theorem 2. The focus on theoretical guarantees is appropriate for NIPS, and empirical validation is unnecessary here given the asymptotic nature of the results.

In summary, this is a strong, impactful contribution that resolves a long-standing open question. The results are technically sound, clearly presented, and carry significant implications for both theory and practice. The paper should be accepted.
