This paper addresses the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the set of mean parameters in a model of mixture of Gaussian variables. The number of Gaussian variables, the weights of the mixture, and the covariance matrices of the Gaussian variables are known and fixed. The paper is theoretical and proves three negative results for the EM algorithm. Firstly, even for large sample sizes, local maxima can exist in the likelihood function with likelihood values arbitrarily smaller than the maximal likelihood value. Secondly, the EM algorithm, when randomly initiated, converges with high probability to a critical point with a likelihood value arbitrarily smaller than the maximal likelihood value. Thirdly, the same negative result holds for the gradient EM algorithm. I find the question addressed by the paper to be interesting and worthy of attention. To obtain rigorous proofs, the paper restricts itself to a simplified mixture of Gaussian variable model, where the number of components is known, the weights are known and uniform, and the covariance matrices are equal to identity. I do not think these simplifications are problematic, given that the proofs are already technical and the obtained results are informative. These results are very interesting in my opinion and can be useful in understanding the EM algorithm in practice.

It should be noted that the paper is written to address a case of general dimension d, while the proofs address the case d=1. Perhaps the authors could discuss whether similar proofs would be possible for arbitrary dimension d and, if not, briefly mention the additional difficulties of the case d larger than 1. My only concern about the paper is the proof of Theorem 1 (unfortunately, I did not read the proofs in the supplementary material). I think this proof is too concise, and I would have preferred it to be longer, even at the expense of shortening some of the discussions in the paper. The part of the proof that I think requires more details is from line 288 to 306. The authors provide limits (as gamma or R goes to infinity) of supremums of likelihood functions over some domains. While I do not have a problem with the computation of limits of likelihood functions evaluated at fixed parameters, obtaining the limit of a maximum is not entirely obvious to me, especially when the domain is unbounded or depends on the parameters that go to infinity. Furthermore, the authors mention at some places that the likelihood function is continuous, but this does not directly imply the types of convergences stated in the paper, in my opinion. I think that more details, and thus a longer proof, are needed to convince the reader of the validity of the proof of Theorem 1.

Finally, I have another minor concern with the part of the paper addressing the Gradient EM, from line 162 to line 172. I found this part more difficult to follow than the rest of the paper. In particular, perhaps the authors could provide more explanation on how Equation (5) is obtained.
