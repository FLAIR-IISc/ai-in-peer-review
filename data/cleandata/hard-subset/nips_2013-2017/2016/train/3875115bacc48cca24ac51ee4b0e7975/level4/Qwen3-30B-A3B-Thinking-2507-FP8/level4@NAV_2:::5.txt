

The paper addresses a long-standing open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding whether Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components can have bad local maxima in their population likelihood function. The authors provide a negative answer to this question through the construction of a general class of counterexamples, demonstrating that bad local maxima exist even for equally-weighted, well-separated, and spherical Gaussians. The authors establish two main results: first, that the log-likelihood value of these bad local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than that of global optima, resolving Srebro's open question; second, that the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to bad critical points with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)). They further show that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, indicating that poor performance stems from bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper is technically sound (Quality) with rigorous theoretical proofs that establish the existence of bad local maxima and the convergence properties of EM variants. The authors properly contextualize their work against previous NIPS and related literature, including Srebro (2007), Balakrishnan et al. (2015), and Dasgupta and Schulman (2007), while clearly differentiating their contributions. The writing is exceptionally clear (Clarity) and well-organized, with a concise yet comprehensive presentation that effectively balances theoretical depth with intuitive explanations, including a helpful geometric description for the M=3, d=1 case. The work demonstrates high originality (Originality) by resolving an important open question in the theoretical understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces, which previous work had only partially addressed. The significance (Significance) of this contribution is substantial, as it fundamentally changes our understanding of the convergence behavior of EM for GMMs, with important implications for practitioners who rely on random initialization schemes.

The paper's main weakness is the lack of concrete estimates for the constants in the probability bound (1 - e^(-cM)), which would help practitioners understand when the exponential failure probability becomes relevant. While the authors show that convergence to bad solutions occurs with exponentially high probability in M, they do not provide specific values for c or discuss the practical threshold where this phenomenon would manifest. This would be valuable for understanding the phase transition point where the probability of failure becomes significant. However, this limitation does not undermine the paper's core theoretical contribution, which remains novel, impactful, and well-supported.

Pro: The paper resolves an important open question in a theoretically rigorous manner, provides significant theoretical insights into the behavior of EM for GMMs, and has clear implications for practitioners. The writing is exceptionally clear and well-structured.

Con: The lack of concrete constant estimates in the probability bound limits the immediate practical applicability of the results, though this does not detract from the theoretical novelty.
