This paper addresses the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the set of mean parameters in a Gaussian mixture model. The number of Gaussian variables, the weights of the mixture, and the covariance matrices of the Gaussian variables are known and fixed. The authors prove three negative results for the EM algorithm. Firstly, they show that even for large sample sizes, there can be local maxima for the likelihood function with likelihood values arbitrarily smaller than the maximal likelihood value. Secondly, they demonstrate that the EM algorithm, randomly initiated, converges with high probability to a critical point with a likelihood value arbitrarily smaller than the maximal likelihood value. Lastly, they show that the same negative result holds for the gradient EM algorithm.

The question addressed by the paper is interesting and worthy of attention. To obtain rigorous proofs, the authors restrict themselves to a simplified Gaussian mixture model: the number of components is known, the weights are known and uniform, and the covariance matrices are equal to identity. While these simplifications are technical, the obtained results are already informative and can be useful in understanding the EM algorithm in practice.

The authors provide a thorough analysis of the EM algorithm and its limitations. However, I would like to see a more detailed discussion on the possibility of extending the proofs to arbitrary dimension d. The authors mention that the proofs address the case d=1, but it is unclear whether similar proofs would be possible for higher dimensions and what additional difficulties might arise.

My primary concern with the paper is the proof of Theorem 1. While the authors provide a clear outline of the proof, I believe that more details are necessary to convince the reader of its validity. Specifically, the part of the proof from line 288 to 306 requires more explanation. The authors provide limits of supremums of likelihood functions over certain domains, but it is unclear how these limits are obtained, especially when the domain is unbounded or depends on the parameters that go to infinity. Additionally, the authors mention the continuity of the likelihood function, but this does not directly imply the types of convergences stated in the paper.

Furthermore, I found the part of the paper addressing the Gradient EM (from line 162 to line 172) more challenging to follow than the rest of the paper. The authors could provide more explanation on how Equation (5) is obtained and clarify the relationship between the Gradient EM updates and the population likelihood function.

Overall, the paper provides valuable insights into the limitations of the EM algorithm for Gaussian mixture models. However, with some additional clarification and detail, the paper could be even more convincing and easier to follow.

Strengths:
- The paper addresses an interesting and important question in the context of Gaussian mixture models.
- The authors provide a thorough analysis of the EM algorithm and its limitations.
- The results obtained are informative and can be useful in understanding the EM algorithm in practice.

Weaknesses:
- The proof of Theorem 1 could be more detailed and clear.
- The possibility of extending the proofs to arbitrary dimension d is not discussed.
- The part of the paper addressing the Gradient EM could be clarified.
