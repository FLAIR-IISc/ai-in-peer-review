- Paper shows local maxima exist in the GMM log-likelihood even with infinite data and can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.  
- Analysis focuses on isotropic Gaussians with known scale and configurations where centers split into well-spaced clusters.  
- Authors prove random initialization causes EM to miss the global optimum with high probability as the number of components grows.  
- Paper is well-written and presents significant, nontrivial theoretical results about local maxima and EM’s limitations.  
- Main concern: Theorem 1 is proved only for a specific center configuration; the paper lacks analysis of how likely such “bad” configurations are in practice.  
- Theorem 2 partially addresses likelihood but does not provide clear quantitative statements about the prevalence of bad configurations.  
- Main concern: Theorem 2’s reliance on purely random initialization may be unrealistic for clearly clustered data—practitioners would not typically place centers in low-density regions.  
- Example on lines 74–81 is unclear and unconvincing regarding Srebro’s conjecture for k = 2.  
- Requirement on lines 233–235 (initializing a center between two clusters) is unclear—why is this configuration especially problematic?  
- Question about lines 267–273: the practical importance of proving EM unlikely to converge to a saddle point is unclear—are saddle points less or more problematic than local optima?  
- Numerous minor wording and typo issues to fix (line-specific suggestions: line 18, 38, 60, 138, 172, 177, 218, 220, 225, 245, 249, 308).  
- Strengths listed for acceptance: thorough theoretical analysis, clear exposition, and demonstrated limitations of EM.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear practical relevance, insufficient discussion of real-world implications, and minor editorial issues to address.
