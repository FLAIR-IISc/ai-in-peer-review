The paper explores the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models, focusing on the simplest possible mixture model with isotropic Gaussian components and known scale. The authors demonstrate that local modes exist even with infinite data, and these modes can be arbitrarily bad in terms of differences in log-likelihood between the local and global optima. They investigate particular configurations where the mixture centers are divided between well-spaced clusters and show that random initialization can cause the EM algorithm to miss the global optima almost certainly as the number of components increases.

The paper is well-written, with occasional typos. However, I have two main concerns regarding the practical relevance of the paper. Firstly, Theorem 1 shows that the log-likelihood has a local mode even with infinite data, but it relies on a specific configuration of the centers. I question the likelihood of this configuration occurring in practice. To strengthen the paper's practical relevance, the authors could make assumptions about the distribution of the centers and demonstrate that "bad" configurations become increasingly likely as the number of components (k) or the dimensionality of the problem increases. Although lines 210-218 suggest that Theorem 2 addresses these concerns, I am not convinced. A clear statement on the likelihood of "bad" configurations under certain assumptions would be beneficial.

Secondly, Theorem 2 involves the initialization of the EM algorithm. The authors consider initializing the centers of the components by randomly sampling the mixture, which can cause the EM algorithm to fail to converge to the global optimum under specific configurations of the centers. However, I find it unlikely that someone would initialize EM by placing centers in areas with fewer data points, especially when the data is clearly clustered. For instance, if the data is generated using three Gaussians, two of which are close and one far apart, it is improbable that someone would initialize EM by placing two centers where there are only N points and one center where there are N observations. I question the practical relevance of random initialization in such cases.

I also have some minor concerns and suggestions for improvement. On line 74-81, I am unclear about the purpose of the example, as it does not convince me that Srebro's conjecture holds when k = 2. On lines 233-235, I understand requirement (2), but I do not see the necessity of requirement (1). The authors prove that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point, but I am unsure about the practical significance of this result. Is converging to a saddle point more or less problematic than converging to a local optimum?
