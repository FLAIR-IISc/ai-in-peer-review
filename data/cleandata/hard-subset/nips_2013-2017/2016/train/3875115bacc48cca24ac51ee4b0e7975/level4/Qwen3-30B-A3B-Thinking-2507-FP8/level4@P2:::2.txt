

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the behavior of the EM algorithm for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors resolve an open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of "bad" local maxima in the population likelihood function of GMMs, demonstrating that even for well-separated, equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians with M ≥ 3 components, such local maxima exist with arbitrarily worse likelihood values than global optima. They further prove that the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to these suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)), and that this failure is attributable to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper makes important connections to prior work. While Srebro (2007) conjectured that no bad local maxima exist for population GMMs, the authors provide a counterexample. This contrasts with Balakrishnan et al. (2015), who showed that for two-component mixtures with sufficient separation, EM converges to near-global optima from reasonable initializations. The results also complement the growing literature on non-convex optimization (e.g., Ge et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016), demonstrating that for GMMs, bad local maxima rather than saddle points present the primary challenge.

Strengths: The paper addresses a fundamental theoretical question with clear practical implications for Gaussian mixture estimation. The results are technically rigorous and well-motivated, demonstrating that even in highly favorable settings (uniform weights, spherical Gaussians, well-separated components), careful initialization is essential for EM. The authors provide a clear structural analysis of the likelihood function, which could inform the development of better initialization schemes for GMMs. The paper excels in quality, with strong theoretical analysis supported by well-constructed proofs (except for one key result, as noted below).

Originality: The work is highly original in addressing the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function for M ≥ 3 components, a question that had remained open since Srebro (2007). While previous work focused on algorithmic convergence under separation assumptions, this paper provides a fundamental characterization of the likelihood landscape itself.

Significance: The results have important practical implications for practitioners using EM for GMM estimation. They explain why random initialization schemes can fail in practice, even for well-separated mixtures, and provide theoretical justification for using more sophisticated initialization methods (e.g., method of moments). The findings advance our understanding of non-convex optimization for latent variable models.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, with clear exposition of the main results and their implications. However, the proof of Theorem 1 (which establishes the existence of bad local maxima) is somewhat brief. The authors claim the results hold for general dimension d but only provide proofs for d=1. While the d=1 case is sufficient to establish the main point, the authors should clarify whether their techniques extend to higher dimensions and, if not, briefly explain the additional challenges. The proof from lines 288-306, which involves limits of supremums of likelihood functions over unbounded domains, would benefit from more detailed justification. The continuity of the likelihood function alone does not fully establish the convergence results claimed, and additional arguments would strengthen the proof.

The explanation of the gradient EM algorithm (lines 162-172) is less clear than the rest of the paper. The derivation of Equation (5) could be made more transparent to help readers understand how the gradient updates relate to the population likelihood.

The paper's main limitation is that it focuses on the simplified case of equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians with known covariance. While the authors justify this simplification as necessary for technical tractability, they might briefly discuss how their results might extend to more general settings (e.g., unequal weights, non-spherical Gaussians). However, given the technical complexity of the proofs, this limitation is understandable and does not undermine the significance of the results.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important open question in the analysis of the EM algorithm. The results are significant for both theoretical understanding and practical application of GMMs. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria and makes a clear contribution to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves a long-standing open question in GMM theory
- Provides fundamental insight into the limitations of the EM algorithm
- Has clear practical implications for practitioners
- Demonstrates that bad local maxima exist even in highly favorable settings
- Proves that random initialization can fail with exponentially high probability

Arguments against acceptance:
- Proof of Theorem 1 could be more detailed
- Limited discussion of extension to higher dimensions (though not essential for main result)
- Some parts of the gradient EM explanation could be clearer

The paper is technically sound, original, and significant. The minor weaknesses in proof detail and exposition do not detract from the paper's overall contribution. I recommend acceptance.
