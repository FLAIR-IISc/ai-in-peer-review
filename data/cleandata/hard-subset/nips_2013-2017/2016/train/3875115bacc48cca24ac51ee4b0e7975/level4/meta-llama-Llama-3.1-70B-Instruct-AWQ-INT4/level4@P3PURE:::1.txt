The paper studies the convergence of the EM algorithm and iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation in learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, presenting several key results that demonstrate non-convergence to global maxima. Specifically, the authors show that: (1) even with three well-separated clusters and infinite samples, algorithms like EM can become stuck in local optima; (2) for mixtures of k Gaussians, random initialization for EM succeeds with at most exponential probability in -Ω(k); and (3) Gradient EM generically avoids strict saddle points, suggesting that bad local maxima are the primary issue. The paper presents a coherent set of results indicating that EM does not converge to global maxima, even with infinite samples.

While the results are technically sound, I found them to be somewhat unsurprising, and the techniques employed follow expected lines. The primary example used to demonstrate the non-convergence of EM is a mixture with three components, where two are closer together and the third is far apart. The authors show that for the log-likelihood objective, a local maximum exists with one center near the first two components and two centers near the distant cluster. This behavior is fairly expected, particularly when the centers are separated by a distance significantly greater than √d. Under such separation, learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians becomes similar to k-means clustering. Similar examples have been used to demonstrate the failure of Lloyd's algorithm for k-means clustering.

The authors extend this example to k components using a recursive construction, which, while requiring some effort, is also somewhat expected. Similarly, the failure of random initialization can be attributed to reasons similar to those for k-means clustering, with comparable failure probabilities. In fact, this serves as motivation for alternative initialization methods, such as picking k log k centers or using distance-squared sampling.

In conclusion, while the results provide a coherent narrative about the non-convergence to global optima, I believe they are largely along expected lines. A comparison with k-means clustering and Lloyd's heuristic, highlighting the similarities and differences between these methods, would be a valuable addition to the paper. Notably, Kumar and Kannan have established convergence guarantees for Lloyd's heuristic in the context of k-means clustering, which may provide a useful point of contrast.
