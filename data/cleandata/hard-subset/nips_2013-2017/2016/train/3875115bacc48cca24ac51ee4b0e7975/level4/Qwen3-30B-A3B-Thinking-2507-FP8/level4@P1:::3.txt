

This paper addresses a fundamental question regarding the likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components. The authors resolve an open question posed by Srebro (2007) by demonstrating that the population likelihood function can indeed have arbitrarily bad local maxima even in the idealized setting of equally-weighted, well-separated, spherical Gaussians with known scale parameters. The paper makes two key contributions: (1) it constructs a specific GMM configuration where the log-likelihood of a local maximum can be arbitrarily worse than that of the global optimum, and (2) it shows that the EM algorithm (and a first-order variant) with random initialization will converge to such suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)).

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with technical arguments that are logically developed. The authors provide a thorough background on GMMs and the EM algorithm, and their results build naturally upon prior work by Balakrishnan et al. (2015) and others who have studied the convergence properties of EM for Gaussian mixtures. The connection to the Srebro conjecture is particularly well-motivated, and the paper makes a clear contribution to understanding the theoretical limitations of local search methods for GMM estimation.

I have two primary concerns about the paper's practical relevance. First, while Theorem 1 establishes the existence of bad local maxima, it does so for a specific configuration of the mixture centers (where centers are grouped into well-separated clusters with certain proportions). The paper does not address how likely such configurations are to occur in practice. It would strengthen the paper significantly if the authors could demonstrate that "bad" configurations become increasingly probable as the number of components M or dimensionality d increases, under reasonable assumptions about the distribution of true centers. The current discussion in L210-218 addresses initialization rather than the prevalence of bad configurations, which doesn't fully address this concern.

Second, Theorem 2 shows that random initialization (selecting centers uniformly from the data) leads to failure with high probability under specific configurations. However, in practical settings where data clusters are well-separated (e.g., two clusters close together and one far away), it seems implausible that a practitioner would initialize EM with two centers in the smaller cluster and one in the larger cluster. The paper should address whether random initialization is actually relevant in such clearly clustered data scenarios. The example on lines 74-81 appears to be intended to support Srebro's conjecture for M=2, but the connection is unclear and doesn't sufficiently demonstrate the conjecture's validity for the two-component case.

I also have a few questions about the technical arguments. On line 233-235, the authors state that "the only favorable initialization schemes" require both (1) all initialized centers falling within a single cluster, and (2) matching the number of initialized centers to true centers within each cluster. I'm unclear why requirement (1) is necessary—wouldn't a center initialized between two clusters eventually move toward the correct cluster? Additionally, the result that the first-order EM algorithm avoids strict saddle points (Theorem 4) is theoretically interesting, but the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why this matters practically. Is convergence to a saddle point more problematic than convergence to a local maximum, and if so, why?

The paper is well-written with only minor grammatical issues. I recommend addressing the following minor points:

- L38: Change "quality of the estimate" to "quality of the estimates"
- L60: Rephrase "to characterize when it is that efficient algorithms" to "to characterize under which conditions efficient algorithms"
- L138: Clarify that O(d^1/2)-separated means all means are separated by a constant factor of d^1/2
- L172: Remove the redundant sentence
- L177: Change "equivalent of" to "equivalent to"
- L218: Define "well-separated constant" more clearly
- L220: Correct "initialized by the the random initialization" to "initialized by the random initialization"
- L225: Clarify that Ω(M) refers to a function of M
- L245: Change "unfortunate coincident" to "unfortunate coincidence"
- L249: Add "that" after "recall" ("Recall that uniform weighted")
- L308: Add "that" after "claim" ("claim that")

Overall, this is a technically strong paper that makes an important theoretical contribution to understanding the limitations of local search methods for Gaussian mixture estimation. The results resolve a long-standing open question and have important implications for practitioners using EM in practice. While the practical relevance of the specific bad configurations could be strengthened, the paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces. I recommend acceptance.
