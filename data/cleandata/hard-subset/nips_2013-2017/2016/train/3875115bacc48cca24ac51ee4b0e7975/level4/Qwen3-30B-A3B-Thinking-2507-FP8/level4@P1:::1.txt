

The paper addresses a fundamental question about Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) that has been open since Srebro (2007): whether the population likelihood function of GMMs with M ≥ 3 components has bad local maxima. The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture, demonstrating that even for well-separated, equally-weighted mixtures of spherical Gaussians with unit variance, the population log-likelihood function contains local maxima whose log-likelihood values can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. They further establish that the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to these bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)), and that the first-order EM algorithm avoids strict saddle points, indicating that the poor performance stems from bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper's main strengths are its technical precision and clear exposition of a theoretically significant question. The authors provide a rigorous construction of bad local maxima for M = 3 components, showing that when two true centers are close together while the third is far away, a local maximum exists with one center placed between the two close centers and two centers placed at the far center. This construction is then extended to M components through a recursive clustering structure, which elegantly demonstrates the exponential failure probability of random initialization. The theoretical arguments are sound, and the connection between the GMM likelihood surface and k-means clustering (where similar bad examples exist for Lloyd's algorithm) is insightful. The paper also makes important practical contributions by highlighting the necessity of careful initialization for EM even in highly favorable settings.

Regarding originality, while the general idea that bad local maxima exist for M ≥ 3 might be somewhat expected, the specific construction, the exponential failure probability for random initialization, and the careful distinction between M = 2 and M ≥ 3 cases represent significant advances. The paper's approach to proving the existence of bad local maxima through geometric constructions, rather than relying on probabilistic arguments, is novel and adds to the theoretical toolkit for analyzing non-convex optimization problems.

The significance of these results is substantial. They provide a theoretical explanation for the well-known practical challenge of poor EM convergence due to bad local maxima, even when the data comes from a well-specified model. The paper's findings directly inform algorithmic design, suggesting that effective initialization schemes (such as those based on the method of moments) are essential for finding good solutions in general GMMs. This has important implications for practitioners using EM for GMMs across various applications.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with related work on k-means clustering, which would strengthen the context of the results. While the connection to k-means is mentioned, a more explicit comparison would help situate the contribution relative to the broader literature on clustering algorithms. Specifically, Kumar and Kannan (2004) provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd's heuristic under sufficient separation conditions, and it would be valuable to contrast how these results differ from the EM behavior presented here, particularly regarding the role of initialization.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially replicate the results. The theoretical arguments are carefully presented, and the connection between the structural results and algorithmic consequences is well-motivated.

In terms of acceptance considerations:

Pros:
- Resolves a long-standing open question about GMM likelihood surfaces
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis of EM convergence behavior
- Demonstrates the necessity of careful initialization even in favorable settings
- Establishes a clear connection between the existence of bad local maxima and algorithmic failure
- Offers a clean recursive construction that extends the M=3 case to general M

Cons:
- The main construction for M=3 is somewhat expected given the analogy to k-means clustering
- The comparison with k-means clustering could be more developed to better situate the contribution
- The results are limited to the population setting (infinite samples), though the authors note the extension to finite samples is straightforward

Overall, the paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to understanding the behavior of the EM algorithm for Gaussian mixture models. While the core ideas might be somewhat expected to experts familiar with non-convex optimization and clustering, the precise formulation, rigorous proofs, and careful analysis of the failure probability represent a significant advancement. The results have clear practical implications and should be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers working with mixture models. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important open question in the field.
