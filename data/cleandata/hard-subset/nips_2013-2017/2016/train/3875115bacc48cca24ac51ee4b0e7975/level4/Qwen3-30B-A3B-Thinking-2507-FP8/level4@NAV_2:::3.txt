

The paper investigates the existence of poor local maxima in the population log-likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components under idealized settings (isotropic, unit-variance, equally-weighted, well-separated Gaussians). The authors establish two key results: first, they construct an example where the population likelihood contains local maxima with log-likelihood values arbitrarily worse than global optima, resolving an open question posed by Srebro (2007). Second, they demonstrate that with random initialization (drawing centers from the data distribution), the EM algorithm and its first-order variant converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)), indicating that poor performance stems from bad local maxima rather than saddle points. This work builds on prior NIPS contributions addressing GMM identifiability (Allman et al., 2009), convergence rates (Chen, 1995), and EM convergence properties (Balakrishnan et al., 2015).

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving the open question about local maxima in GMM likelihood functions. The technical arguments are sound, with clear proofs of the main results. The presentation is generally well-structured, though some sections could benefit from greater clarity (e.g., the purpose of the k=2 case discussion in L74-81). The work has strong theoretical significance as it demonstrates fundamental limitations of local search methods for GMMs even in favorable settings.

However, I have two main concerns regarding practical relevance. First, Theorem 1 establishes the existence of poor local maxima under specific configurations of the true centers (e.g., where some centers are grouped into well-separated clusters), but the paper does not address how likely such configurations are to occur in practice. It would strengthen the argument if the authors could characterize the probability of encountering such "bad" configurations under natural assumptions about center distributions, showing how this probability increases with the number of components or dimensionality. While Theorem 2 addresses initialization issues, it does not directly answer whether the problematic configurations are practically relevant.

Second, the analysis of random initialization (L220-225) assumes drawing centers uniformly from the data. For the case where data is generated from three Gaussians with two close clusters and one well-separated cluster, the paper's analysis suggests that random initialization would frequently place two centers near the denser cluster (with 2N points) and one center near the sparser cluster (with N points). However, this initialization strategy seems implausible in practice, as practitioners would likely recognize the clear clustering structure and use a more informed initialization approach. The paper should clarify whether random initialization remains practically relevant when the data exhibits strong clustering patterns.

Additional concerns include:
- L74-81: The example discussing k=2 does not convincingly demonstrate that Srebro's conjecture holds for two components, and its purpose is unclear.
- L233-235: Requirement (1) (all initialized centers falling in the neighborhood of exactly one cluster) appears unnecessary; if a center is initialized between clusters, it should still be able to move toward the correct cluster.
- L267-273: The proof that EM avoids strict saddle points is technically interesting, but the paper should clarify why this is practically important compared to convergence to local maxima.

Overall, this is a technically strong paper that makes an important theoretical contribution by resolving an open question about GMM likelihood surfaces. While the results are significant, the practical relevance would be strengthened by better addressing how common the problematic configurations are in real-world scenarios. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it provides rigorous theoretical analysis of a fundamental problem in statistical learning.
