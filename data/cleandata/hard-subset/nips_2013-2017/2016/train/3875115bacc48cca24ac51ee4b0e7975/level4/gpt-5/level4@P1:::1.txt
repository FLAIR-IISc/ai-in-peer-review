Summary and relation to prior work
This paper analyzes the population (infinite-sample) likelihood of equally weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and studies the behavior of EM and first-order EM under random initialization. The main contributions are:
- Structural: For M ≥ 3, the population log-likelihood has spurious local maxima even for well-separated mixtures, and the likelihood gap relative to the global optimum can be made arbitrarily large. This resolves a conjecture of Srebro (2007) in the negative.
- Algorithmic: With standard random initialization (drawing initial centers i.i.d. from the mixture), both EM and first-order EM converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). Moreover, first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, implying that bad local maxima—not saddles—drive the failures.

These results complement prior analyses of EM and its variants that require good initializations for local convergence (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015), as well as alternative consistent estimators via clustering or moments (e.g., Dasgupta–Schulman, Hsu–Kakade, Moitra–Valiant). The paper’s saddle-avoidance conclusion is aligned with recent results on gradient methods and strict saddles (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas–Piliouras, 2016). Conceptually, the negative example for M = 3 (two nearby components and a third far away) and the recursive construction for general M echo classical pathologies in k-means/Lloyd’s algorithm under large separation.

Strengths
- Resolves a notable open conjecture (Srebro, 2007) by constructing arbitrarily bad local maxima in a highly favorable GMM setting.
- Gives a sharp, quantitative failure probability for random initialization: success is exponentially unlikely in M without careful initialization.
- Clarifies that the typical failure mode for first-order EM is due to local maxima rather than strict saddles, aligning the landscape view with dynamical properties of gradient methods.
- The recursive instance construction for general M is clean and technically competent.

Weaknesses
- The negative examples and the failure of random initialization are, in spirit, similar to well-known issues with Lloyd’s algorithm for k-means under large separation; thus the results, while rigorous and useful, are not very surprising.
- The proof strategies follow expected lines once one adopts a k-means-like viewpoint in the large-separation regime (e.g., trapping within clusters).
- The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison to clustering-based guarantees (e.g., Kumar–Kannan) and a discussion of how EM and Lloyd’s algorithm coincide or differ under large separation.

Quality
The results appear technically sound and carefully stated. The population-level arguments, the construction for M = 3, and the recursive extension to general M are clear. The first-order EM analysis and the exclusion of strict saddles are consistent with the cited dynamical systems literature, and the step-size condition (s ∈ (0, 1)) is reasonable and matches prior analyses in simpler two-component cases.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with a coherent narrative from structural properties to algorithmic consequences. Suggestions to improve exposition:
- Provide a more direct and detailed comparison to k-means/Lloyd’s behavior in the same separation regime, including a brief discussion of why EM essentially reduces to k-means when components are well separated.
- Clarify the dependence on dimension and separation (e.g., the role of Ω(√d) separation) in the constructions and probability bounds.
- Clean up several reference formatting issues in the bibliography.

Originality
The resolution of Srebro’s conjecture and the explicit exponential failure probability for random initialization at the population level are the most original aspects. However, the qualitative phenomenon is aligned with widely expected behavior from the clustering literature, and the techniques—constructing imbalanced clusterings and showing trapping—are conceptually standard in that context.

Significance
The results sharpen our understanding of the GMM likelihood landscape and the limitations of naive random initialization for EM, even in the infinite-sample, well-specified, well-separated setting. They underscore the practical importance of careful initialization (e.g., moments-based pilots, spectral methods, or improved seeding) when using EM. The impact is primarily conceptual and cautionary rather than algorithmically prescriptive.

Pros
- Resolves a known conjecture on the absence of bad local maxima for population GMM likelihoods.
- Provides explicit constructions with arbitrarily bad likelihood gaps.
- Establishes exponentially small success probability for random initialization of EM/first-order EM.
- Shows that first-order EM avoids strict saddle points, isolating local maxima as the central obstacle.

Cons
- Phenomenon and proof ideas are closely related to known k-means/Lloyd’s pathologies under separation, reducing the surprise factor.
- Limited comparative discussion with clustering heuristics and guarantees (e.g., Kumar–Kannan).
- No positive algorithmic contributions beyond highlighting the need for better initialization.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a dedicated comparison to k-means and Lloyd’s heuristic in the large-separation regime: when do EM updates effectively coincide with Lloyd steps? How do the constructed bad instances map to classical k-means counterexamples?
- Discuss the relevance of guarantees like Kumar–Kannan for Lloyd’s algorithm and contrast with the negative EM results here, clarifying the separation assumptions under which positive results are known.
- Briefly comment on the efficacy of practical initialization schemes (k-means++, spectral/moment-based pilots) in avoiding the constructed traps, and how many restarts are needed in practice versus the eΩ(M) lower bound.
- Improve the bibliography formatting and consistency.
