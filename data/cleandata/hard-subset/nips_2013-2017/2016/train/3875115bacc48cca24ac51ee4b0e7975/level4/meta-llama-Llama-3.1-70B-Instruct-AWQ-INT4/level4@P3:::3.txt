The paper "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences" presents a thorough analysis of the existence of local maxima in the likelihood of Gaussian mixture models. The authors focus on the simplest possible mixture model, considering isotropic Gaussian components with known scale, and demonstrate that local maxima exist even when infinite data is available. Furthermore, they show that these local maxima can be arbitrarily bad in terms of differences in log-likelihood between the local and global optima.

The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and proofs of the main results. The authors consider particular configurations, specifically cases where the mixture centers are divided between well-spaced clusters, and show that random initialization can lead to the EM algorithm missing the global optima almost certainly as the number of components increases.

However, there are two main concerns regarding the practical relevance of the paper. Firstly, the authors show that a particular configuration of the centers leads to the existence of local maxima, but it is unclear how likely this configuration is to occur in practice. It would be beneficial to make assumptions about the distribution of the centers and demonstrate that "bad" configurations become increasingly likely as the number of components or dimensionality increases.

Secondly, the initialization of the EM algorithm is a crucial aspect of the paper. The authors consider initializing the centers of the components by randomly sampling the mixture, which may not be practically relevant when the data is clearly clustered. It is unclear whether random initialization is a common practice in such scenarios.

Minor points include a few typos and formatting issues, such as the use of "that the" instead of "that" in some instances. Additionally, some sentences could be rephrased for clarity, and a few definitions could be provided for non-expert readers.

In terms of the evaluation criteria, the paper excels in quality, as the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and proofs. The paper is also clear and well-organized, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with the topic. However, the originality of the paper is somewhat limited, as it builds upon existing work in the field. The significance of the paper is moderate, as it provides important insights into the behavior of the EM algorithm, but its impact may be limited to a specific niche within the machine learning community.

Overall, the paper is a solid contribution to the field, but could benefit from additional context and discussion on the practical relevance of the results. With some revisions to address these concerns, the paper has the potential to make a more significant impact in the field.
