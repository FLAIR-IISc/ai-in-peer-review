The paper examines the convergence of the EM algorithm and iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation when learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians. The key findings presented in the paper demonstrate non-convergence to global maxima, specifically: 

1. The EM algorithm can become stuck in local optima even with infinite samples and well-separated clusters, as evidenced by an example with three clusters. 
2. For mixtures of k Gaussians, the probability of successful convergence using random initialization for EM is at most exp(-\Omega(k)). 
3. Gradient EM generally avoids convergence to strict saddle points, suggesting that bad local maxima are the primary issue. 

The paper presents a cohesive set of results indicating that EM does not converge to global maxima, even with infinite samples. However, the findings and techniques employed seem somewhat expected and unsurprising. 

The primary example illustrating the non-convergence is a mixture with three components, where two are close together and the third is far away. The log-likelihood objective exhibits a local maxima with one center near the first two components and two centers near the distant cluster. This behavior is fairly predictable, especially when the centers are separated by a distance significantly greater than \sqrt{d}, causing the clusters to become non-overlapping and the problem to resemble k-means clustering. 

Similar examples have been observed in Lloyd's algorithm for k-means clustering, and the authors extend this example to k components using a recursive construction. While this requires some effort, the outcome is somewhat expected. The failure of random initialization is also comparable to k-means clustering, with a similar failure probability. 

In conclusion, although the results provide a coherent narrative about the non-convergence to global optima, they seem to follow expected lines.
