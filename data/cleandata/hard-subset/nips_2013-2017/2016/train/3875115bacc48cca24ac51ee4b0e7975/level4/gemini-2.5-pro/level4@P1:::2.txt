This paper provides a theoretical analysis of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters in a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). The analysis is conducted in a simplified setting where the number of components, the mixture weights (assumed uniform), and the covariance matrices (assumed to be the identity) are known and fixed. The paper is theoretical in nature and establishes several important negative results concerning the population log-likelihood landscape of GMMs and the behavior of the EM algorithm.

First, the authors demonstrate that the population log-likelihood function can possess "bad" local maxima whose values are arbitrarily worse than that of the global optimum, even for well-separated mixtures. This resolves an open question posed by Srebro (2007). Second, they show that a randomly initialized EM algorithm converges to such a suboptimal critical point with high probability (exponential in the number of components, M). Third, they extend this negative result to a first-order variant of EM, i.e., gradient ascent on the likelihood.

**Strengths:**
The questions addressed in this paper are both fundamental and highly relevant. The theoretical results are very interesting and provide significant new insights into the behavior of the EM algorithm, challenging the optimistic view that the population likelihood for well-behaved GMMs might be free of poor local optima. To achieve these rigorous results, the authors focus on a simplified GMM setting. This simplification seems entirely appropriate; the proofs are already quite technical, and the resulting insights are informative for understanding the practical challenges of EM.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Proof of Theorem 1:** My main concern relates to the proof of Theorem 1. The current proof sketch in the main paper is quite brief, and I believe it would benefit from significant expansion, perhaps at the expense of some of the more general discussion. The argument relies on showing that the likelihood value at a specific suboptimal point is greater than the supremum of the likelihood on the boundary of a chosen neighborhood. Specifically, the steps from lines 288 to 306, which involve taking limits of these suprema, are not immediately obvious. While computing the limit of the likelihood function at a fixed point is straightforward, justifying the interchange of the limit and supremum operators requires a more careful argument (e.g., via uniform convergence), especially when the domain of the supremum is unbounded or depends on the parameters going to infinity. The authors' appeal to the continuity of the likelihood function does not, by itself, seem sufficient to justify these claims. A more detailed and rigorous argument is needed here to make the proof fully convincing to the reader.

2.  **Generalization to Higher Dimensions:** A point for clarification is the dimensionality of the problem. While the paper is framed in terms of a general dimension 'd', the key constructions and proofs appear to be presented for the one-dimensional case (d=1). It would be helpful if the authors could explicitly comment on the extension of their results to higher dimensions. Do the proofs carry over directly, or are there significant additional challenges in constructing similar bad local maxima for d > 1?

3.  **Clarity on First-Order EM:** A minor point of clarification concerns the introduction of the first-order EM algorithm (lines 162-172). This section was somewhat difficult to follow compared to the rest of the paper. In particular, it would be helpful to provide a clearer derivation of the gradient EM update rule (Equation 6) from the general formulation involving the Q-function. The connection between taking a gradient step on Q(θ'|θ) and arriving at the specific update in Equation (6) could be made more explicit for the reader.

In summary, this paper presents important and surprising theoretical results on the landscape of the GMM likelihood function and its consequences for the widely used EM algorithm. The findings are valuable and will likely be of great interest to the community. My primary recommendation is for the authors to strengthen the proof of their main structural result (Theorem 1) to make it more transparent and self-contained.
