- Paper shows local modes exist in the log-likelihood of well-specified isotropic Gaussian mixtures, even with infinite data, and these modes can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.  
- Authors focus on configurations where true centers split into well-spaced clusters and prove random initialization makes EM miss the global optimum with high probability as the number of components grows.  
- Manuscript is generally well-written but contains occasional typos.  

Main concerns:
- Practical relevance concern 1: Theorem 1 relies on a specific configuration of centers — the reviewer asks how likely such "bad" configurations are in practice and requests assumptions on the distribution of centers with probability statements as k or dimension grows.  
- Theorem 2 does not convincingly resolve concern 1; the reviewer wants a clear statement about the likelihood of bad center configurations (not bad initializations).  
- Practical relevance concern 2: Theorem 2’s analysis assumes random initialization by sampling the mixture, but when data are clearly clustered (e.g., two close Gaussians and one far), such random initializations seem implausible in practice; the reviewer questions the practicality of random initialization in clearly clustered data.  

Specific methodological/questions:
- Lines 74–81: The provided example is unclear and does not convince that the Srebro conjecture holds for k = 2; its purpose is questioned.  
- Lines 233–235: Requirement (1) is questioned — initializing a center between two clusters does not guarantee movement to the correct cluster, so its necessity is unclear.  
- Lines 267–273: The claim that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point is proven, but the reviewer questions the practical importance of avoiding saddle points versus local optima.
