This paper theoretically investigates the behavior of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters of a Gaussian Mixture Model. The analysis is conducted under the assumption that the number of components, mixture weights, and covariance matrices are known and fixed. The paper's main contribution is a set of three negative theoretical results:
1) For any large sample size, the likelihood function can possess local maxima whose likelihood values are arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum.
2) When randomly initialized, the EM algorithm converges with high probability to a critical point with a likelihood value that is arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum.
3) A similar negative result is shown to hold for the Gradient EM algorithm.

The research question addressed is both interesting and important. The authors focus on a simplified GMM setting (known number of components, uniform weights, and identity covariance matrices) to ensure rigorous proofs. This simplification is well-justified, as the proofs are already quite technical and the resulting insights are significant. The findings are highly interesting and provide valuable theoretical insight into the practical behavior and potential pitfalls of the EM algorithm.

However, I have some concerns that should be addressed:

My primary concern regards the proof of Theorem 1. (Note: I did not have the opportunity to review the proofs in the supplementary material.) The proof in the main paper is overly condensed, and I would strongly recommend expanding it, even if it requires shortening other discussion sections, to ensure its arguments are fully transparent and convincing. Specifically, the reasoning from lines 288 to 306 requires more detailed justification. The proof presents limits of supremums of the likelihood function over domains that are either unbounded or dependent on parameters (e.g., gamma, R) that tend to infinity. While the limit of the likelihood function for a fixed set of parameters is straightforward, the convergence of the *supremum* over such domains is non-trivial. The paper's appeal to the continuity of the likelihood function is insufficient to rigorously establish this type of convergence. A more detailed argument is needed to make the proof fully persuasive.

Secondly, the paper is framed as addressing the general d-dimensional case, yet the proofs provided are for the one-dimensional case (d=1). The authors should explicitly discuss the generalizability of their results. Could the proofs be extended to an arbitrary dimension d? If not, what are the key technical challenges that arise for d > 1? Clarifying this point would better frame the scope of the paper's contribution.

Finally, I have a minor concern with the section on Gradient EM (lines 162-172). I found this part more difficult to follow than the rest of the paper. It would be helpful if the authors provided a more detailed explanation of how Equation (5) is obtained.
