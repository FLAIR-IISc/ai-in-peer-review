The paper explores the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models, focusing on the simplest possible mixture model with isotropic Gaussian components of known scale and a well-specified model. The authors demonstrate that local modes exist even with infinite data and that these modes can be arbitrarily bad in terms of the difference in log-likelihood between the local and global optima. They concentrate on specific configurations where the mixture centers are divided between well-spaced clusters, showing that random initialization causes the EM algorithm to miss the global optima almost certainly as the number of components increases.

The paper is well-written, with occasional typos. However, I have two primary concerns regarding the paper's practical relevance. Firstly, Theorem 1 shows that the log-likelihood has local modes even with infinite data, but it relies on a specific configuration of the centers. I question the likelihood of this configuration occurring in practice. To strengthen the paper's practical relevance, the authors could make assumptions about the distribution of the centers and demonstrate that "bad" configurations become increasingly likely as the number of components (k) or the dimensionality of the problem increases. Although lines 210-218 suggest that Theorem 2 addresses these concerns, I am not convinced. A clear statement on the likelihood of "bad" configurations under certain assumptions would be beneficial. It is essential to note that I am referring to "bad" configurations (positions of the true centers), not "bad" initializations.

Secondly, Theorem 2 involves the initialization of the EM algorithm. The authors consider initializing the centers of the components by randomly sampling the mixture, which often causes the EM algorithm to fail to converge to the global optimum under specific center configurations. However, I find it unlikely that someone would initialize EM by placing two centers where there are only N points and one center where there are N observations, especially when the two clusters are well-separated. This raises questions about the practical relevance of random initialization when the data is clearly clustered.

I also have some minor points and questions. The example on lines 74-81 is unclear, and I do not see how it supports Srebro's conjecture for k = 2. The purpose of this example is not apparent. I understand requirement (2) on lines 233-235, but I do not see why requirement (1) is necessary. The authors prove that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point, but I am unsure why this result is important from a practical perspective. Is converging to a saddle point more or less problematic than converging to a local optimum?
