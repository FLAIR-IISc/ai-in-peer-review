

The paper investigates the convergence properties of the EM algorithm (and related iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation) in learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, establishing three key results demonstrating non-convergence to global maxima: (1) Even with three well-separated clusters and infinite samples, algorithms like EM can converge to local optima; (2) For mixtures of $k$ Gaussians, random initialization for EM succeeds with probability at most $\exp(-\Omega(k))$; (3) Gradient EM avoids strict saddle points generically, indicating that poor local maxima—not saddle points—are the primary obstacle. While the paper presents a coherent narrative of EM's failure to reach global optima (even with infinite data), the results appear largely unsurprising given existing knowledge. The core counterexample involves a three-component mixture where two clusters are close together and the third is far from both. The authors demonstrate a local maximum where one center aligns near the two proximal clusters and two centers localize near the distant cluster—a behavior expected under sufficient separation (specifically, separation $>> \sqrt{d}$), where spherical Gaussian mixtures become analogous to $k$-means clustering. Similar examples are known to yield poor instances for Lloyd's algorithm in $k$-means. The extension to $k$ components via a recursive construction, while requiring effort, also follows anticipated patterns. The random initialization failure probability mirrors that of $k$-means, where techniques like distance-squared sampling or $k \log k$ initialization are motivated. Overall, the results form a consistent story but largely align with established intuition. A critical comparison with $k$-means clustering (and Lloyd's heuristic) would strengthen the paper. Specifically, Kumar-Kannan provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd's algorithm under sufficient separation conditions, and while EM differs from Lloyd's when separation is insufficient (as noted by Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu), the two methods become effectively equivalent under the large-separation regime used in the paper's counterexamples. Explicitly contrasting this result with $k$-means guarantees would clarify the novelty and context of the findings.
