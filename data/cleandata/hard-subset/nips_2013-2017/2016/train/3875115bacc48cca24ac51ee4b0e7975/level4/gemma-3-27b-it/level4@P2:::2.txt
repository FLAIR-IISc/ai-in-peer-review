This paper investigates the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters in a Gaussian mixture model. The mixture weights and covariance matrices are fixed and known. The paper presents three negative results concerning the algorithm’s performance: 1) the likelihood function can possess local maxima with arbitrarily worse values than the global maximum, even in the large-sample limit; 2) a randomly initialized EM algorithm converges with high probability to a suboptimal critical point; and 3) the same holds for a first-order variant of the EM algorithm. The research addresses an important and relevant question regarding the reliability of a widely used algorithm. The theoretical focus and rigorous proofs are strengths of the work. The restrictions to a simplified Gaussian mixture model – known number of components, uniform mixture weights, and identity covariance matrices – are reasonable given the complexity of the proofs and the informative nature of the results. These results could offer valuable insights into the practical behavior of EM.

A notable aspect of the paper is its consideration of general dimension *d*, although the proofs primarily address the *d*=1 case. It would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss the challenges of extending the proofs to higher dimensions, or to indicate whether similar results are expected to hold, and under what conditions.

My primary concern relates to the proof of Theorem 1 (though I haven’t examined the supplementary material in detail). While I appreciate the effort to be concise, I believe the proof could benefit from greater elaboration. Specifically, the argument from lines 288 to 306 requires more justification. While the calculation of limits of likelihood functions evaluated at fixed parameters seems sound, establishing the limit of a supremum over an unbounded or parameter-dependent domain is not immediately obvious. The authors assert continuity of the likelihood function, but this alone does not guarantee the stated convergence properties. A more detailed explanation, potentially at the cost of brevity elsewhere, would enhance the clarity and persuasiveness of the proof.

I also found the discussion of the first-order EM algorithm (lines 324-335) somewhat difficult to follow. Providing additional explanation regarding the derivation of Equation (5) would be beneficial. For example, a brief justification as to why the equation follows from the gradient of Q(θ′ | θ) would be helpful.

**Summary of Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents negative results regarding the EM algorithm’s convergence properties, specifically demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima and the high probability of converging to them under random initialization. This is a significant and previously unresolved issue.
*   **Significance:** The findings have practical implications for the use of EM in Gaussian mixture modeling, highlighting the potential for suboptimal solutions and the need for careful initialization strategies.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical framework is well-established, and the proofs, despite some areas requiring greater detail (as noted above), appear logically sound. The simplifications made to the model do not invalidate the core results.
*   **Clarity:** Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results.

**Summary of Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Proof Detail:** The proof of Theorem 1 lacks sufficient detail in certain sections, specifically concerning the limits of suprema over unbounded domains.
*   **First-Order EM Explanation:** The explanation of the first-order EM algorithm and the derivation of Equation (5) could be more thorough.
*   **Dimensionality:** The focus on the 1-dimensional case, while understandable, could be addressed with a discussion of higher-dimensional extensions.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor concerns regarding proof detail and clarity in specific sections, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm. The results are novel, significant, and supported by a rigorous, albeit sometimes terse, mathematical analysis. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should expand the proof of Theorem 1 and provide additional explanation for the first-order EM algorithm section.
