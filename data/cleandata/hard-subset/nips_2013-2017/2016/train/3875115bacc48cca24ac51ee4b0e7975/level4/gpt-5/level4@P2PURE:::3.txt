This paper studies the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models, focusing on the simplest setting: isotropic Gaussian components with known scale, and a well-specified model. The authors show that local modes persist even with infinite data, and that these modes can be arbitrarily suboptimal in terms of the log-likelihood gap to the global optimum. The analysis centers on particular configurations, especially those where the true mixture centers are partitioned into well-spaced clusters. Under such configurations, the authors argue that random initialization causes EM to miss the global optimum with overwhelming probability as the number of components grows. The paper is generally well written, though there are occasional typos.

I have two main concerns about practical relevance:
1. Theorem 1 establishes the existence of local modes even in the infinite-data limit, but does so by constructing a specific configuration of the true centers. How likely is this configuration in practice? I would be more convinced of practical impact if the authors posited a distribution over the true centers and then proved that “bad” configurations (of the true centers, not initializations) occur with increasing probability as k or the dimension grows. Lines 210–218 state that Theorem 2 addresses this concern, but I am not persuaded. It would help to provide a clear, quantitative statement about the probability of these “bad” configurations under some distributional assumptions.
2. In Theorem 2, initialization of EM becomes central. The authors consider initializing centers by random samples from the mixture and show that, for certain center configurations, EM often fails to reach the global optimum. Consider, however, the case of three Gaussians: two close together and one far apart, with N samples per component. Then roughly 2N points lie near the tight pair and N near the distant component. It seems implausible in practice to initialize EM with two centers in the sparser region (with only N points) and only one center in the denser region (with 2N points), especially when the two regions are well separated. Is random initialization really the right practical model when the clustering structure is so evident?

Comments on specific parts:
- L74–81: I am unsure what this example is intended to demonstrate. It does not convince me that the Srebro conjecture holds for k = 2; what is its purpose?
- L233–235: I understand requirement (2), but I do not see why requirement (1) is necessary. Do you mean that if a center is initialized between two clusters, you cannot guarantee it will move toward the correct cluster?
- L267–273: You prove EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point. Why is this practically important? Is converging to a saddle point more or less problematic than converging to a local optimum?
