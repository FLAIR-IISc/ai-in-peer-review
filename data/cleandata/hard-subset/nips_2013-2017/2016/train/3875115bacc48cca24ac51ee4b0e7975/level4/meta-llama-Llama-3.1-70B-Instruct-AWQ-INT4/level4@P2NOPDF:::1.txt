The paper investigates the convergence of the EM algorithm and iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation when learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians. The authors present several key findings that demonstrate non-convergence to global maxima. Specifically, they show that even with infinite samples and well-separated clusters, algorithms like EM can become stuck in local optima. Furthermore, for mixtures of k Gaussians, random initialization for EM succeeds with a probability of at most exp(-Ω(k)), indicating a high likelihood of convergence to suboptimal solutions. Additionally, the authors prove that Gradient EM will not converge to strict saddle points generically, suggesting that bad local maxima are often the primary issue.

The paper presents a cohesive set of results that demonstrate the EM algorithm's failure to converge to global maxima, even with infinite samples. However, the reviewer finds these results somewhat unsurprising, as they align with expected behavior. The primary example presented in the paper involves a mixture with three components, where two are close together and the third is far apart. The authors demonstrate that the log-likelihood objective has a local maximum with one center near the first two components and two centers near the far-off cluster. This behavior is consistent with expectations, particularly when the centers are separated by a distance significantly greater than √d. Under such conditions, learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians becomes analogous to k-means clustering.

Similar examples have been shown to provide bad instances of Lloyd's algorithm for k-means clustering. The authors extend this example to k components using a recursive construction, which, although requiring effort, is somewhat expected. The failure of random initialization for EM can be attributed to similar reasons as those for k-means clustering, with a comparable failure probability. This has motivated the use of alternative initialization methods, such as picking k log k centers or distance-squared sampling.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a coherent narrative about the non-convergence to global optima, the reviewer believes that these findings are largely in line with expectations. The results would benefit from a comparison with k-means clustering and Lloyd's heuristic, highlighting the similarities and differences between these methods. Notably, Kumar and Kannan have established convergence guarantees for Lloyd's heuristic in the context of k-means clustering, which may provide a useful contrast to the results presented in this paper.
