This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and the behavior of EM and first-order EM under random initialization. The setting fixes the number of components, uses equal mixture weights, and assumes spherical unit covariance. The authors prove three negative results for M ≥ 3 components: (1) the population log-likelihood admits bad local maxima, with objective values arbitrarily worse than the global maximum, even for well-separated mixtures in 1D; (2) EM with natural random initialization converges to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M); and (3) an analogous failure holds for first-order EM (gradient EM) with step size s ∈ (0, 1). They further show first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, suggesting bad local maxima—not saddle points—cause failure.

Relation to prior work: The paper resolves a conjecture of Srebro (COLT 2007), who speculated that the population GMM likelihood for equally weighted spherical mixtures might be free of spurious local maxima. It complements and sharpens analyses of EM’s local convergence (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., Annals of Statistics 2015), which require good initialization, by showing random restarts fail with overwhelming probability in constructed instances. The results also connect to recent work on gradient methods and saddle points (e.g., Lee et al., COLT 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016), using these insights to rule out convergence to strict saddles in this setting. The construction is consistent with known pathologies at finite samples (Améndola et al., 2015), but crucially shows bad local maxima persist at the population level.

Strengths:
- Resolves a long-standing open question by demonstrating arbitrarily bad local maxima in a highly favorable regime (well-separated, equal weights, spherical covariances).
- Provides sharp algorithmic consequences: EM and first-order EM with random initialization fail with probability 1 − e−Ω(M), implying exponential restarts are needed.
- Conceptually clarifies that bad local maxima—not strict saddles—drive failures of local ascent methods in these models.
- Techniques (geometric construction; hierarchical clustering of means; dynamical-systems reasoning) are likely to inform analyses of other nonconvex problems.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Dimensionality: Although the paper is framed for general dimension d, the core constructions and proofs are given for d = 1. It would help to explicitly discuss whether the arguments extend to d > 1 (e.g., via embedding the 1D construction along a coordinate axis) and to outline any additional technical obstacles.
- Theorem 1 proof details: The argument around lines 288–306 is too compressed. The proof relies on limits of suprema of likelihoods over domains that are unbounded and/or parameter-dependent (as γ or R → ∞). Continuity of the likelihood alone does not directly justify these limit-interchange steps. Please provide a more detailed argument, for example:
  - Restrict attention to compact neighborhoods and use Weierstrass to guarantee attainment of suprema, then control approximation error as the compact sets expand.
  - Replace informal limit arguments with an epi-convergence or Γ-convergence statement, or supply lemmas that bound the difference between suprema over expanding domains and the target objective.
  - Make explicit how the separation assumptions yield uniform bounds needed to pass limits through the suprema.
  These additions would substantially strengthen confidence in Theorem 1.
- First-order EM section (lines 162–172): This part is harder to follow than the rest. Please expand the derivation of the first-order EM update from the Q-function and clarify its equivalence to gradient ascent on the population log-likelihood. A brief, self-contained derivation of equation (6) (and the role of step size s ∈ (0, 1)) would help readers unfamiliar with Balakrishnan et al. (2015).
- Scope of random initialization: The results consider initializing means by sampling from the mixture (population analogue of picking data points). It would be useful to comment on whether the negative results extend to other common heuristics (e.g., k-means++-style seeding, small random perturbations around the origin), and to highlight initialization regimes that can succeed (e.g., method-of-moments pilot estimates).
- Minor clarity: Clearly separate population vs sample notation early on; define “strict saddle” before invoking theorems; and state explicitly that M ≥ 3 is necessary for your construction, with a brief discussion of the M = 2 open case.

Quality: The results are technically strong and well motivated. While I have not reviewed the supplemental proofs in full detail, the main concern is the brevity and limit/supremum steps in Theorem 1’s proof segment; fuller justification would address this.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized, with the exceptions noted above. Expanding the first-order EM derivation and the Theorem 1 proof would markedly improve readability.

Originality: High. The paper settles an open conjecture and provides a novel hierarchical construction yielding exponentially small success probability for random initialization.

Significance: High. EM and its variants are ubiquitous; showing that even in benign settings random restarts are exponentially unlikely to succeed is an important caution and motivates principled initialization.

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves a notable open problem; strong theoretical contributions.
- Clear algorithmic implications for widely used methods.
- Techniques of broader interest in nonconvex analysis.

Cons for acceptance:
- Core proofs are 1D; limited discussion of higher-dimensional extension.
- Some key proof steps are terse and need more rigorous treatment.
- Clarity issues around first-order EM derivation.

Overall, I find the results important and timely. With expanded details in Theorem 1 and clearer exposition of first-order EM, this paper would make a valuable contribution.
