This paper investigates the theoretical properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters of a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). The analysis is conducted in an idealized setting where the number of components is known, the mixture weights are uniform, and the component covariances are the identity matrix. The main contribution is a set of strong negative results concerning the population log-likelihood landscape and the behavior of EM.

First, the paper resolves a long-standing open question posed by Srebro (2007) by proving that the population log-likelihood function for a GMM with three or more components can possess "bad" local maxima. These are local optima whose likelihood values can be arbitrarily worse than that of the global maximum. This is demonstrated via a constructive proof, even for mixtures of well-separated Gaussians.

Second, the paper explores the algorithmic consequences of this structural property. It shows that the standard EM algorithm, when initiated with a common random sampling strategy, converges to a suboptimal critical point with a probability that is overwhelmingly high (at least 1 - e⁻ᶜᴹ, where M is the number of components). This implies that an exponential number of random restarts are required to find a globally optimal solution with constant probability.

Finally, these negative results are extended to a first-order variant of the EM algorithm (equivalent to gradient ascent on the likelihood). The paper further shows that this first-order method will almost surely not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that the failure of local search is fundamentally due to the existence of poor local maxima, rather than the challenge of navigating saddle points.

**Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental and important question about the most widely used algorithm for fitting GMMs. By formally refuting Srebro's conjecture, it provides a definitive and somewhat surprising theoretical result that clarifies the optimization landscape associated with GMMs. These findings have significant implications for both theorists and practitioners, highlighting the crucial role of initialization.

2.  **Originality and Quality:** The results are novel and technically sound. The construction used to demonstrate the existence of bad local maxima is insightful. The subsequent analysis, which uses a recursive structure to prove the exponential failure probability of random initialization, is a clever and powerful argument. These contributions substantially advance our understanding of the theoretical limitations of local search methods like EM for this problem.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and motivated. The introduction clearly situates the work within the existing literature and articulates the importance of the questions being addressed. The main theorems are stated precisely, and their implications are discussed thoughtfully.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Detail in Proof of Theorem 1:** My main concern lies with the proof of Theorem 1. While the overall strategy is clear, the presentation is highly condensed. The argument from line 288 to 306, which involves taking the limit of a supremum of the likelihood function over domains that are unbounded or depend on parameters that go to infinity, lacks sufficient detail. The convergence of a function at fixed points does not automatically imply the convergence of its supremum over a set. A more rigorous justification is needed here to make the proof fully convincing. I would strongly recommend that the authors expand this proof in the main text, potentially by shortening other discussion sections, as this is the foundational result of the paper.

2.  **Generalization to Higher Dimensions:** The paper is framed as addressing the general d-dimensional case. However, the constructions and proofs for the main theorems appear to be developed specifically for the one-dimensional case (d=1). The authors should clarify whether the results extend to an arbitrary dimension d. If so, a discussion of the additional technical challenges and how the proof would be adapted is warranted. If the results are currently limited to d=1, this should be explicitly stated as a limitation of the current work.

3.  **Explanation of First-Order EM:** The background section describing the first-order EM algorithm (lines 162-172) could be made clearer. In particular, the derivation of the population update in Equation (6) is stated with a reference to prior work. A brief, intuitive explanation of how this update corresponds to a gradient step on the Q-function would improve the paper's self-containedness and make it more accessible to a broader audience.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of the GMM likelihood landscape and the behavior of the EM algorithm. The results are novel, important, and rigorously established for the setting considered. While the simplifications (known uniform weights, identity covariances) are notable, they are justified by the technical depth of the proofs and the fundamental nature of the findings. My primary reservations concern the conciseness of a key proof and the clarity regarding the generalization to higher dimensions. If the authors can address these points, particularly by providing more detail in the proof of Theorem 1, I believe this will be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.
