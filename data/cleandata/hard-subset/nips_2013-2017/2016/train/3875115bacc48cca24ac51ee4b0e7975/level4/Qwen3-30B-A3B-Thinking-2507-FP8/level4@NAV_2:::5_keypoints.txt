- Addresses Srebro (2007) open question on whether GMMs with M ≥ 3 can have bad local maxima in the population likelihood.

- Constructs a general class of counterexamples showing bad local maxima exist even for equally-weighted, well-separated, spherical Gaussians.

- Proves the log-likelihood at these bad local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than the global optima, resolving the open problem.

- Shows EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to bad critical points with probability at least 1 - e^{-Ω(M)}.

- Establishes first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, so failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.

- Technical quality: rigorous, correct theoretical proofs underpinning the claims.

- Clarity: exceptionally clear, well-organized exposition with helpful geometric intuition (e.g., M=3, d=1 case).

- Originality: novel resolution of a long-standing theoretical question not fully addressed by prior work.

- Significance: changes understanding of EM convergence for GMMs and has important implications for practitioners using random initialization.

- Main weakness: missing concrete estimates for the constant in the probability bound (1 - e^{-cM}) and no discussion of practical threshold M where failure becomes relevant.

- Pro: theoretically rigorous, significant insight, clear writing, and practical implications.

- Con: lack of explicit constant estimates limits direct practical applicability despite strong theoretical contribution.
