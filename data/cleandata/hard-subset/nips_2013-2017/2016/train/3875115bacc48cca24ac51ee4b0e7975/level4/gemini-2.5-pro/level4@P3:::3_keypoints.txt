- Paper shows population log-likelihood for well-specified, equally weighted, spherical, well-separated GMMs can have arbitrarily bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 (resolves Srebro 2007).
- Paper proves EM and a first-order variant, with common random initialization (centers i.i.d. from true mixture), converge to suboptimal critical points with probability →1 exponentially in M.
- Manuscript is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a fundamental long-standing question; the negative resolution of Srebro’s conjecture is important.
- Concern: Theorem 1 is an existence result for specific (clustered) center configurations; practical impact depends on how likely such “bad” configurations are in real data.
- Suggestion: Authors should argue or show under reasonable priors on true centers (or increasing M/d) that bad configurations occur with non-negligible probability.
- Concern: Theorem 2 uses a random initialization that may be unrealistic for clearly clustered data; practitioners would likely use cluster-aware initializations.
- Suggestion: Discuss relevance of common practical initializations (e.g., k-means++) and whether they avoid the demonstrated failure modes.
- Clarification requested (L74-81): The M=2 example’s purpose is unclear since the M=2 case remains unresolved—what should the reader take away?
- Clarification requested (L233-235): Why is it necessary that every initialized center lie inside the neighborhood of exactly one true cluster—can centers between clusters be pulled correctly by EM?
- Clarification requested (L267-273 / Theorem 4): What is the practical significance of first-order EM not converging to strict saddles—how problematic is saddle vs. bad local maximum in practice?
- Minor edits/typos: fix phrasing at L18, L38, L60; explicitly define ξ-separated at L138; small wording fixes at L177, L220; explain e^Ω(M) as “exponentially many in M” at L225.
