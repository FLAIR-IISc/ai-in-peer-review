

This paper addresses an important open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components. Srebro conjectured that for well-separated, equally-weighted GMMs, any local maximum of the likelihood function would be a global maximum in the infinite-sample limit. The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture by constructing a counterexample showing that bad local maxima do exist, with log-likelihood values arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.

The paper makes two significant contributions. First, it establishes the structural result that bad local maxima exist for GMMs with M ≥ 3 components, resolving Srebro's open question. Second, it demonstrates that the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to these bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)). The authors further prove that the failure of these algorithms is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points, as the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear organization and effective use of a simple M=3, d=1 case to provide intuition before presenting the general results. The authors carefully situate their work within the broader literature, referencing key contributions on GMM identifiability (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009), theoretical properties of GMM estimation (Chen, 1995; Ho and Nguyen, 2015), and algorithmic analyses of EM (Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015). The connection to non-convex optimization is well-motivated, particularly in relation to the observation that for some non-convex problems, all local optima are near global optima (Loh and Wainwright, 2013).

The paper excels in all four evaluation criteria:

Quality: The theoretical results are rigorously established with new proof techniques that advance our understanding of the population likelihood surface. The authors carefully distinguish between the existence of bad local maxima and practical algorithmic failure, providing a compelling argument for why the failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally clear, with well-structured arguments and effective use of intuitive examples. The authors provide sufficient mathematical detail while maintaining accessibility, and the presentation balances theoretical rigor with practical implications.

Originality: This work resolves a specific open question that has been unresolved since Srebro's 2007 paper. The construction of counterexamples for M ≥ 3 components represents a significant advance over previous work, which had only established the existence of bad local maxima for finite samples (Améndola et al., 2015) or for M=2 (which remains an open problem).

Significance: The results have direct practical implications for the use of EM algorithms in GMM estimation. They demonstrate that even in favorable settings (well-separated, equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians), random initialization can fail with high probability, necessitating more careful initialization schemes. This insight is valuable for practitioners and theoretical researchers working with non-convex optimization problems.

The main limitation of the paper is the lack of concrete estimates for the constants in the probability bound (1 - e^(-cM)). While the asymptotic result is theoretically significant, the practical relevance depends on the value of c. The authors might have provided estimates of c for specific cases or included simulations to illustrate when the phenomenon becomes relevant in practice. However, this limitation does not detract from the paper's theoretical importance.

The paper's strengths are substantial: it resolves a fundamental open question with a clean construction, provides clear intuition through well-chosen examples, and demonstrates important algorithmic consequences. The authors' careful analysis of the distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points is particularly noteworthy.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the understanding of Gaussian mixture models and non-convex optimization. It advances our theoretical understanding while providing practical insights for algorithm design. The results are important for both practitioners and researchers working with mixture models and non-convex optimization.

I recommend acceptance of this paper as a strong contribution to the field. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses a fundamental question with significant implications. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides unique theoretical insights that will be valuable to the broader machine learning community.
