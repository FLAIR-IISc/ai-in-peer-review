This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 equally weighted, spherical components and known variance. It provides two main results. First, it constructs well-separated configurations in which the population log-likelihood admits suboptimal local maxima whose values can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum, resolving a conjecture by Srebro (2007) in the negative. Second, it shows that EM and a first-order EM (gradient EM) with random initialization converge to such bad critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). It further proves that first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that the algorithmic failures are driven by true bad local maxima rather than saddles. The work relates to a substantial literature on EM analysis (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and algorithmic alternatives for learning GMMs via clustering or moments (e.g., Vempala & Wang, 2002; Moitra & Valiant, 2010; Hsu & Kakade, 2013). The negative results here complement prior local convergence guarantees and strengthen the message that careful initialization is necessary even in favorable, well-specified settings.

Strengths:
- Resolves an open question about the population likelihood landscape under very benign assumptions (equal weights, isotropic covariances, infinite data).
- Presents clear structural intuition for the M ≥ 3 case and rigorous proofs that bad local maxima can be arbitrarily suboptimal.
- Establishes that random initialization leads EM/first-order EM to suboptimal critical points with exponentially high probability in M, highlighting the limits of naive restarts.
- Clarifies that first-order EM avoids strict saddles almost surely, focusing attention on local maxima as the root cause.

Main concerns and questions (practical relevance):
1) How likely are the “bad” center configurations?
- Theorem 1 constructs specific well-separated configurations with bad local optima. For practical relevance, it would help to quantify the prevalence of such configurations under a plausible generative model for the true centers (e.g., centers drawn i.i.d. from a distribution, or placed according to a Poisson process, with minimum separation). Can you show that the measure (or probability) of “bad” configurations increases with M or dimension d? Lines 210–218 suggest Theorem 2 addresses this, but Theorem 2 analyzes failure under random initialization given an adversarial center configuration. My question concerns the frequency of bad configurations themselves, not bad initializations. A clear statement under explicit assumptions would strengthen the practical message.

2) Practicality of random initialization when clusters are obvious:
- In Theorem 2 (and its discussion), centers are initialized by sampling from the mixture (equivalently from the data). Consider the 3-component case with two nearby means and one far apart, and equal mixture weights, yielding about 2N samples near the “double” cluster and N near the isolated component. If clusters are very well separated, it seems implausible in practice to initialize with two centers in the 2N region and only one in the N region and then fail to reassign, given the stark separation. Many practitioners use k-means++ or a preliminary clustering to seed EM. Could you discuss how your negative results interact with such common initializations? Is pure random draw from the data the right baseline in clearly clustered regimes? Some empirical evidence or theoretical comparison would help.

Requests for clarification:
- L74–81: The two-component example does not convince me the Srebro conjecture holds for M = 2 (and you state this remains open). What is the intended role of this example beyond intuition? Please clarify.
- L233–235: You list two conditions for “favorable” initialization. I understand (2), but why is (1) necessary? Do you mean that a center initialized between two well-separated clusters might not move to the correct cluster, making convergence unreliable?
- L267–273: You prove first-order EM does not converge to strict saddles. From a practical viewpoint, why is this important relative to the prevalence of bad local maxima? Is converging to a saddle more or less problematic than converging to a suboptimal local maximum in this setting? A brief discussion would help contextualize this result.

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves a well-known open question with clean, rigorous counterexamples.
- Provides sharp algorithmic consequences (exponentially small success probability under random initialization).
- Offers techniques likely relevant to other nonconvex likelihood landscapes.

Cons for acceptance:
- Practical implications hinge on adversarial center configurations; the prevalence of such configurations under reasonable generative assumptions is not quantified.
- The random initialization baseline may understate what practitioners use (e.g., k-means++), and the paper does not assess these alternatives.
