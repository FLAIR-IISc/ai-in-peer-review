

The paper addresses a fundamental question in Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) that has been open since Srebro's 2007 paper: "Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?" The authors provide a negative answer by constructing counterexamples demonstrating that the population likelihood function for GMMs with M ≥ 3 components can have bad local maxima whose log-likelihood values can be arbitrarily worse than global optima. This resolves a long-standing open problem in the field.

The paper begins by situating its work within the broader context of GMMs, noting that while the EM algorithm is the dominant practical method for estimating GMMs, it is only guaranteed to converge to stationary points of the likelihood function. The authors review relevant work on identifiability (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009), rates of convergence (Chen, 1995; Ho and Nguyen, 2015), and theoretical analysis of EM (Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015). The paper makes a clear connection to recent work on non-convex optimization (Loh and Wainwright, 2013; Wang et al., 2014) that has shown for some problems local optima are close to global optima, but the authors demonstrate this does not hold for GMMs.

The main contributions are twofold: (1) The authors construct a specific GMM with M = 3 well-separated, unit-variance spherical Gaussians whose population likelihood has arbitrarily bad local maxima, and (2) They show that both EM and a first-order variant of EM with random initialization converge to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability in M. The authors further prove that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, indicating the poor performance is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs establishing the existence of bad local maxima and the convergence properties of EM. The authors provide a clear construction for the M = 3 case in one dimension, which gives intuition for the general case. The theoretical results are well-supported by the proofs, though the supplementary material would benefit from explicit constants in the probability bounds to better understand the phase transition where convergence to bad solutions becomes likely.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors provide clear intuition before presenting theorems, and the exposition balances mathematical rigor with accessibility. The one-dimensional example for M = 3 makes the theoretical results tangible and easy to grasp, which is particularly valuable given the technical nature of the content.

Originality: This work directly addresses a question that has been open since 2007 (Srebro, 2007) and provides a negative answer through a novel construction. The paper distinguishes itself from prior work by focusing on the population likelihood (infinite sample size) setting rather than finite samples, and by showing that even in this favorable setting with well-separated components, bad local maxima exist. The authors also show that these bad local maxima are not merely artifacts of the optimization algorithm but are inherent to the likelihood surface.

Significance: The results have significant implications for practitioners using EM for GMMs, as they demonstrate that even in idealized settings (well-separated, spherical Gaussians), careful initialization is necessary to avoid poor local optima. The paper also contributes to the broader understanding of non-convex optimization by showing that for GMMs, the landscape is more complex than previously thought, with bad local maxima that are not avoided by simple optimization algorithms. This work is important for both theoretical understanding and practical applications of GMMs.

Strengths:
- Resolves a long-standing open question in the field
- Provides clear, well-structured arguments with intuitive examples
- Demonstrates practical implications of theoretical results
- Connects to broader themes in non-convex optimization
- Exceptionally well-written and organized

Weaknesses:
- The probability bounds in Theorems 2 and 3 lack explicit constants, making it difficult to assess practical relevance for moderate M
- The paper does not provide empirical evidence to illustrate convergence to bad local maxima, though the theoretical arguments are strong

Pro/Con for Acceptance:
Pro: The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that resolves a longstanding open question. The results have clear implications for the use of EM in practice and advance our understanding of non-convex optimization in a fundamental statistical model. The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making complex results accessible.

Con: The absence of explicit constants in the probability bounds limits the ability to assess practical relevance for moderate numbers of components. While the theoretical results are strong, the lack of empirical validation of the phenomenon is a minor weakness.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of statistical learning and optimization. It addresses a fundamental question with important practical implications, and the results are both theoretically rigorous and clearly presented. I recommend acceptance.
