- Paper shows local maxima exist in GMM likelihoods even with infinite data, and these can be arbitrarily suboptimal in log-likelihood.
- Analysis focuses on specific configurations, especially well-separated clusters of mixture centers.
- Authors prove random initialization of EM fails with high probability to reach the global optimum as the number of components increases.
- Paper is generally well-written but contains several minor typographical and wording errors.
- Main concern: practical relevance is unclear — how likely are the constructed “bad” configurations in real data?
- Theorem 1 demonstrates existence for a specific configuration, but its applicability to typical settings is not established.
- Theorem 2 is intended to address prevalence of bad configurations but does not convincingly quantify their probability under realistic assumptions.
- A clear statement quantifying the likelihood of bad configurations under assumptions about the distribution of true centers is needed.
- Questioning the realism of the random initialization model: it may be an unrealistic initialization strategy when data exhibits clear clustering.
- Concrete counterexample: with three Gaussians (two close, one distant), random initialization placing centers in sparse regions seems implausible.
- The k=2 example (lines 74–81) is unclear in purpose and does not demonstrate Srebro’s conjecture for k=2, making its inclusion feel misplaced.
- Requirement (2) is clarified (lines 233–235), but the necessity of requirement (1) is not explained; does initializing a center between clusters preclude correct convergence?
- The practical significance of Theorem 4 (EM unlikely to converge to a saddlepoint) is not explained — relative harm of saddlepoints vs. local optima is unclear.
- Minor points to fix: wording/typos at lines 18, 38, 60, 138, 172, 177, 218, 220, 225, 245, 249, 308 (specific corrections listed in review).
- Arguments for acceptance: rigorous treatment of an important theoretical question, technically sound proofs, highlights EM’s vulnerability to poor local optima, contributes to understanding non-convex estimation.
- Arguments against acceptance: practical relevance is questionable, assumptions (e.g., random initialization) may not hold, lacks discussion on probability of problematic configurations, limited connection to practical algorithmic remedies.
- Overall recommendation: solid theoretical contribution but requires stronger justification of practical impact and prevalence of the analyzed configurations to merit acceptance.
