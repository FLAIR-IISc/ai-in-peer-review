The paper explores the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models, focusing on the simplest possible mixture model: isotropic Gaussian components with known scale, and a well-specified model. The authors demonstrate that local modes exist even with infinite data, and that these modes can be arbitrarily bad in terms of the difference in log-likelihood between the local and global optima. The paper concentrates on specific configurations, particularly cases where the mixture centers are divided between well-spaced clusters. Under these configurations, the authors show that random initialization will cause the EM algorithm to miss the global optimum almost certainly, as the number of components increases. The paper is well-written, with occasional typos.

I have two main concerns regarding the practical relevance of the paper. Firstly, Theorem 1 shows that the log-likelihood has local modes even when infinite data is available. The authors demonstrate the existence of this mode for a particular configuration of the centers. However, I question how likely this configuration is to occur in practice. To strengthen the practical relevance of the paper, I would like to see the authors make assumptions about the distribution of the centers and then show that "bad" configurations become increasingly likely as the number of components (k) or the dimensionality of the problem increases. Lines 210-218 of the paper suggest that Theorem 2 addresses these concerns, but I am not convinced. A clear statement on the likelihood of "bad" configurations under certain assumptions would be beneficial. It is essential to note that I am referring to "bad" configurations (positions of the true centers), not "bad" initializations.

Secondly, Theorem 2 involves the initialization of the EM algorithm. The authors consider initializing the centers of the components by randomly sampling the mixture. With this initialization and under specific configurations of the centers, the EM algorithm often fails to converge to the global optimum. However, considering a scenario where the data is generated using three Gaussians, two of which are close and one far apart, it seems unusual to initialize EM by placing two centers where there are only N points and one center where there are N observations, especially given that the two clusters are well-separated. I question the practical relevance of random initialization when the data is clearly clustered.

I also have some minor concerns and suggestions. The example on lines 74-81 does not convince me that Srebro's conjecture holds when k = 2, so its purpose is unclear. I understand requirement (2) on lines 233-235, but I do not see why requirement (1) is necessary. It seems that initializing a center between two clusters should guarantee that it will move toward the correct cluster. The authors prove that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point, but from a practical perspective, I am unsure why this result is important. Is converging to a saddle point more or less problematic than converging to a local optimum?
