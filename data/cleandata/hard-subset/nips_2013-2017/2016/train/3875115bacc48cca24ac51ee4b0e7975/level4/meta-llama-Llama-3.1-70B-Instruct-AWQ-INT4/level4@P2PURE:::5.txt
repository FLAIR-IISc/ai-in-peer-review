The paper provides a negative answer to the open question posed by Srebro in his 2007 paper, "Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?" The authors achieve this by constructing a general class of counterexamples. They begin by outlining the methods used to maximize the likelihood in the context of Gaussian Mixture Models, including the EM and gradient EM algorithms. Additionally, they address the issue of initialization and present a common choice. The authors then state their primary theorems, which provide counterexamples to Srebro's question. Furthermore, they demonstrate that even with a common random initialization, the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to non-optimal maxima with exponentially high probability. To provide intuition for their proofs, the authors analyze a simple case (k=3, d=1). This paper is well-written and clearly presents its arguments, effectively balancing the need for conciseness with the requirement to provide insight into the theoretical results. The question addressed by the authors is crucial for understanding the challenges associated with non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic limit for simple distributions.

I did not thoroughly examine the supplementary material, but I believe it would be beneficial to include estimates of the constants in the probability term. This information is important because, in practice, the value of k is unlikely to be excessively large. Providing estimates of the constants would be helpful in determining when convergence to suboptimal solutions occurs in the asymptotic limit, effectively identifying the phase transition point. If calculating the constants is too complex or impractical, supporting simulations would be valuable in demonstrating the observability of this phenomenon. However, this issue does not detract from the paper's novelty or impact.
