This paper investigates the existence of spurious local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models. The analysis is focused on a simplified setting: a well-specified model with isotropic Gaussian components of known scale. The authors demonstrate that even with infinite data, arbitrarily poor local modes can exist for specific configurations of the true component centers. Furthermore, for such configurations, they show that a random initialization strategy will cause the EM algorithm to miss the global optimum with near certainty as the number of components increases. The paper is generally well-written, with occasional typographical errors.

I have two main concerns regarding the practical relevance of the findings.

1.  Theorem 1 demonstrates that the log-likelihood possesses spurious local modes, even in the infinite data regime, for a particular configuration of the true component centers. My question is: how likely is this configuration to occur in practice? The paper's practical implications would be significantly strengthened if the authors could, for instance, assume a distribution over the true centers and then show that these "bad" configurations become increasingly probable as the number of components (k) or the dimensionality (d) increases. The text in lines 210-218 suggests that Theorem 2 addresses these concerns, but I am not convinced it does. A clear statement about the likelihood of encountering these "bad" configurations of true centers (as distinct from "bad" initializations) under some reasonable assumptions would be valuable.

2.  Theorem 2 introduces the initialization of the EM algorithm. The authors consider initializing the component centers by randomly sampling from the mixture itself. With this initialization, and under the specific center configurations discussed, EM is shown to fail to find the global optimum with high probability. However, consider a practical scenario where the data is generated from three Gaussians: two are close together and one is far apart. If we sample an equal number of data points (N) from each component, then 2N points will cluster together, while N points will form a distinct, more distant cluster. It seems highly improbable that a practitioner would initialize two centers in the region containing only N points and just one center in the region containing 2N points, especially when the clusters are well-separated. Is this random initialization scheme practically relevant when the data is clearly clustered?

I also have some specific questions and comments on the text:

*   **L74-81:** The purpose of the example presented here is unclear. It does not seem to convince me that Srebro's conjecture holds for k=2, so its role in the paper is ambiguous.
*   **L233-235:** I understand the need for requirement (2), but the motivation for requirement (1) is not obvious. Is it needed because a center initialized between two clusters cannot be guaranteed to move toward the correct one?
*   **L267-273:** The authors prove that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point. From a practical standpoint, why is this result important? Is convergence to a saddle point considered more or less problematic than convergence to a local optimum?
