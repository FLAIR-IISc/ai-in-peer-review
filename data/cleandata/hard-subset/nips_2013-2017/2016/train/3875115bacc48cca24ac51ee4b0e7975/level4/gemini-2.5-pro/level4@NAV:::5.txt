This paper resolves the open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding whether non-optimal local maxima exist in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture models. The authors answer this question in the negative by constructing a general class of counterexamples. The work begins by reviewing standard likelihood maximization techniques for Gaussian Mixture Models, such as the EM and gradient EM algorithms, and also discusses the topic of parameter initialization, highlighting a common approach. The paper then proceeds to state its main theorems, which formally establish the counterexample that answers Srebro's question. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that with a standard random initialization, both EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to these spurious maxima with exponentially high probability. To provide intuition for their main theoretical results, the paper includes a proof for a simplified setting (k=3, d=1).

The manuscript is exceptionally clear and well-written. It strikes an excellent balance between the formal presentation of its theoretical contributions and the provision of intuition, all within the constraints of the page limit. The resolution of this question is a significant contribution to our understanding of challenges in non-convex optimization, particularly showing that such issues can persist even in the asymptotic, infinite-sample regime for fundamental models.

While I did not review the supplementary material in detail, the paper could be strengthened by providing estimates for the constants in the derived probability bounds. Such estimates would be valuable because, in many practical applications, the number of mixture components (k) is not excessively large. Characterizing these constants could help identify the threshold (i.e., a phase transition) at which convergence to suboptimal solutions becomes a significant concern. If deriving analytical estimates for these constants is intractable, empirical support through simulations would be a welcome addition to demonstrate that this is an observable phenomenon in practice. Nevertheless, this is a minor suggestion and does not diminish the novelty or the significant impact of this work.
