- Paper studies EM for Gaussian mixtures with known components, weights, and covariances, proving three negative results about its landscape and convergence.
- Main negative results: (1) likelihood can have local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global maximum even with infinite data.
- Main negative results: (2) randomly initialized EM converges with high probability to a suboptimal critical point.
- Main negative results: (3) a first-order (gradient) variant of EM exhibits the same high-probability convergence to suboptimal critical points.
- The question is important for understanding theoretical limitations of a widely used algorithm.
- The paper reasonably simplifies to uniform weights and identity covariances to make proofs tractable without greatly reducing impact.
- Builds on prior mixture-model literature and directly refutes Srebro (2007), situating results within optimization-landscape work.
- Strength: tackles a fundamental, practically relevant problem.
- Strength: proofs appear mathematically rigorous and provide strong theoretical support.
- Strength: clear, well-organized presentation and motivated introduction.
- Strength: results reveal significant and surprising limitations of EM practitioners should know.
- Weakness: results are proved only in one dimension (d=1) and extension to higher dimensions is not discussed.
- Weakness: proof of Theorem 1 is compressed (lines 288–306) and needs more detailed justification for limits/suprema arguments.
- Weakness: description/derivation of the first-order EM (lines 162–172, Eq. 5) is insufficiently intuitive and needs clearer explanation.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a significant open problem, delivers surprising negative results, and is well-written with rigorous analysis.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited generality due to d=1, key proof steps lack detail, and the first-order EM exposition is unclear.
- Overall recommendation: accept conditional on clarifying the proof of Theorem 1, improving the first-order EM explanation, and briefly discussing challenges for higher-dimensional extension.
