

This paper investigates the convergence properties of the EM algorithm for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), focusing on the population (infinite-sample) likelihood function. The authors present three key results: (1) they construct an example of a well-separated mixture of three spherical Gaussians with arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood, resolving an open question by Srebro (2007); (2) they demonstrate that EM with random initialization converges to bad critical points with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)) for M-component mixtures; and (3) they prove that first-order EM avoids strict saddle points, indicating that poor performance stems from bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper makes a significant contribution by rigorously addressing Srebro's conjecture that population likelihoods for GMMs have no bad local maxima. The authors connect their work to foundational studies on GMM identifiability (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009) and recent theoretical advances on EM (Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and alternative GMM estimation methods (Hsu and Kakade, 2013), providing appropriate context for their results.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs for all main results. The authors develop a clear theoretical framework and present a novel construction of bad local maxima, extending the basic three-component example to general M through a recursive approach. The proof techniques are innovative in their application to GMM likelihood surfaces, though the overall approach aligns with recent advances in non-convex optimization (Lee et al., 2016). The population setting avoids sampling effects, providing clean theoretical insights.

The clarity of the paper is generally excellent, with well-organized sections and accessible exposition. The authors provide helpful geometric intuition for their main construction, particularly for M=3, and the recursive extension for general M is well-explained. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with k-means clustering (see below), but the presentation is otherwise clear and accessible to a NIPS audience.

Originality is moderate to high. While bad local maxima in non-convex optimization are well-known, the specific construction for GMMs in the population setting with well-separated components directly addresses Srebro's open question. The extension to general M using a recursive construction is a notable technical contribution. The authors properly contextualize their work against recent NIPS papers on GMMs.

Regarding significance, the results have important practical implications: the finding that random initialization fails with exponentially high probability in M suggests that careful initialization strategies (like those based on method of moments) are essential even in favorable settings. This is a valuable insight for practitioners. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the specific configuration required for bad local maxima (two components close together, one far away), which may not be typical in applications.

Strengths:
- Resolves a significant open question with rigorous theoretical results
- Provides important practical insights about initialization requirements for EM
- Well-motivated and technically sound with clear presentation
- Demonstrates the necessity of careful initialization even in idealized settings

Weaknesses:
- The main construction (two components close together, one far away) is somewhat expected, particularly given the connection to k-means clustering
- The significance could be strengthened with a more detailed discussion of practical implications
- The connection to k-means clustering (and Lloyd's algorithm) is underdeveloped

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed comparison with k-means clustering. For instance, Kumar and Kannan (2004) provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd's algorithm under sufficient separation conditions, and the authors' bad example for EM resembles bad examples for Lloyd's algorithm when clusters are not sufficiently separated. A discussion of how EM differs from Lloyd's algorithm in the well-separated regime would help contextualize their results and strengthen the paper's significance.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes a clear theoretical contribution with important practical implications. While the results may not be entirely surprising to experts in the field, they provide a rigorous resolution to an open question and have meaningful consequences for practitioners. The paper is well-written and makes a valuable addition to the literature on GMMs and non-convex optimization.

Pro-acceptance arguments: Resolves a significant open question with rigorous results; provides important practical insights about initialization requirements for EM; well-motivated and technically sound.

Con-acceptance arguments: Results are somewhat expected based on connections to k-means clustering; significance is somewhat limited by the specific configuration required for bad local maxima.

I recommend acceptance, as the paper makes a clear theoretical contribution with important practical implications for practitioners using EM for GMMs.
