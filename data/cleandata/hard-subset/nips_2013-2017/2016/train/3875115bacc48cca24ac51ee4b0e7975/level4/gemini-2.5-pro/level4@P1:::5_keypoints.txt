- Problem and result: Resolves Srebro (2007) by showing the population GMM likelihood can have bad local maxima for any M ≥ 3.  
- Construction: Presents a general class of well-separated, spherical, uniform GMM counterexamples with local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.  
- Algorithmic consequence: For these GMMs, EM and first-order EM with centers drawn from the true distribution converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^(−cM).  
- Complexity implication: Achieving the global optimum therefore requires an exponential number of random restarts in M.  
- First-order EM property: First-order EM converges to strict saddle points with probability zero, indicating failures stem from bad local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Significance/originality: Resolves a long-standing fundamental question and tempers expectations about the population likelihood’s benignity.  
- Technical quality: Theoretical results are strong, technically sound, and include a clear, intuitive construction starting from M=3.  
- Analytical strength: The link between the existence of bad maxima and high-probability failure of random-init EM is well-analyzed and crucial.  
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and balances formalism with intuition.  
- Practical caveat: The practical impact depends on the constant c and the value of M where failure probability becomes significant.  
- Suggestion: Provide bounds on the constants or supporting simulations to illustrate the phase transition and practical thresholds for M.
