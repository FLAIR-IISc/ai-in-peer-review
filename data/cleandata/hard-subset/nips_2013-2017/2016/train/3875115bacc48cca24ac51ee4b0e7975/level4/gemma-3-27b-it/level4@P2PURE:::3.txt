The paper investigates the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models. It focuses on the simplest case: isotropic Gaussian components with known scale, and assumes a well-specified model. The authors demonstrate that local modes persist even with infinite data, and that the difference in log-likelihood between these local modes and the global optimum can be arbitrarily large. The analysis centers on specific configurations where mixture centers are well-spaced. Under these conditions, the authors show that random initialization of the EM algorithm will, with high probability, lead it to miss the global optimum as the number of components increases. The paper is generally well-written, though it contains a few minor typos. I have two primary concerns regarding the practical significance of this work.

First, Theorem 1 establishes the existence of local modes even with infinite data, but does so for a particular configuration of mixture centers. A critical question is the likelihood of encountering such a configuration in practical applications. I would be more convinced of the paper’s relevance if the authors presented assumptions regarding the distribution of the centers and subsequently demonstrated that “bad” configurations become increasingly probable as the number of components (k) or the dimensionality of the problem grows. The authors state (lines 210-218) that Theorem 2 addresses this issue, but I find this unconvincing. A clear statement quantifying the probability of “bad” configurations, under defined assumptions, would be valuable. It's important to clarify that here I'm referring to the probability of “bad” configurations of the *true centers*, not simply “bad” initializations.

Second, Theorem 2 concerns the impact of the EM algorithm’s initialization. The authors consider initializing component centers by randomly sampling from the data mixture. Under specific center configurations, this initialization often causes EM to fail to converge to the global optimum. However, consider a scenario where data is generated from three Gaussians, two closely clustered and one isolated. If an equal number of data points (N) are drawn from each Gaussian, then 2N points will fall near the first cluster and N near the third. It seems counterintuitive to initialize EM with 2 centers positioned where only N points exist, and 1 center where N points are observed, especially given the clear separation of the clusters. Is random initialization a realistic scenario when the data exhibits strong clustering? 

I also have a few specific points: lines 74-81, the purpose of the example is unclear. It doesn't convincingly demonstrate the validity of Sresbro's conjecture when k=2, and its relevance is therefore uncertain. Lines 233-235, I understand the requirement (2), but the necessity of requirement (1) is unclear. Does it mean that initializing a center between two clusters doesn’t guarantee its convergence toward the correct cluster? Lines 267-273, the proof that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddlepoint is presented. However, the practical importance of this result is unclear – is convergence to a saddlepoint more or less problematic than convergence to a local optimum?
