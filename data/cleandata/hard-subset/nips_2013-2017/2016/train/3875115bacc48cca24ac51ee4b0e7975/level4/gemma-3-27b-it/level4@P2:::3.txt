This paper investigates the properties of the likelihood function for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), focusing on the existence of bad local maxima and the implications for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors consider a simplified setting: isotropic Gaussian components with known scale, and a well-specified model. They demonstrate that, even with infinite data, bad local maxima exist and can be arbitrarily worse than global optima. They further show that, under specific configurations of the mixture centers (well-spaced clusters), random initialization of the EM algorithm leads to convergence to these suboptimal points with high probability as the number of components increases. The paper is generally well-written, though a few minor typos are present.

My primary concern revolves around the practical relevance of these findings. While Theorem 1 establishes the existence of bad local maxima for a specific configuration of centers, it doesn't address the likelihood of encountering such configurations in practice. I would be more convinced of the paper’s practical significance if the authors explored the probability of these “bad” configurations occurring under reasonable assumptions about the distribution of the true mixture centers—perhaps as a function of the number of components (k) or the dimensionality of the problem. Although the authors attempt to address these concerns in Theorem 2, I remain unsure if it sufficiently establishes the practical significance. Specifically, a clearer statement clarifying how likely “bad” configurations are, under specific assumptions, would be beneficial. I also question the relevance of the considered initialization scheme. Theorem 2 analyzes the EM algorithm with initialization based on randomly sampling from the dataset. In scenarios where the data clearly exhibits clustered structure (e.g., two clusters close together and one far apart), initializing EM with a number of centers mismatched to the underlying cluster sizes seems unrealistic. Is this random initialization strategy truly representative of how EM is used in practice when data clearly shows clustering? 

The example on lines 74-81 regarding the two-component case is unclear. It doesn't convincingly support the claim that Srebro’s conjecture holds for k=2, leaving me uncertain about its purpose. Regarding lines 233-235, I understand the requirement for initializing centers appropriately within each cluster, but I don’t see the necessity of the first requirement – initializing a center between clusters. Does this mean that, even if a center falls between two clusters, there’s no guarantee it will converge to the correct cluster? Lines 267-273 highlight that the EM algorithm is unlikely to converge to a saddlepoint. However, the paper doesn’t explain *why* this is important from a practical perspective. Is convergence to a saddle point more or less detrimental than convergence to a local optimum?

Here’s a summary of strengths and weaknesses to aid in the decision process:

**Strengths:**
*   **Rigorous Theoretical Results:** The paper presents strong theoretical guarantees regarding the existence of bad local maxima and the behavior of the EM algorithm.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly defines the problem of non-convexity in GMM estimation and its impact on algorithm performance.
*   **Well-Written (mostly):** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a logical flow of ideas.
*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** The non-convexity of GMM estimation is a significant challenge, and this work contributes to understanding its consequences.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Limited Practical Relevance:** The analysis focuses on specific configurations, and the likelihood of these configurations occurring in real-world datasets is unclear.
*   **Initialization Assumption:** The random initialization scheme may not be representative of common practices, diminishing the practical implications of the results.
*   **Unclear Motivation (lines 74-81):** The purpose of the example regarding the two-component case is not fully explained.
*   **Unjustified Requirement (lines 233-235):** The justification for one of the initialization requirements is missing.
*   **Lack of Context (saddle points):** The practical significance of avoiding saddle points isn't adequately explained.



**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper provides a mathematically rigorous analysis of a fundamental problem in statistical estimation.
*   The results contribute to a deeper understanding of the limitations of the EM algorithm.
*   The proofs are novel and may inspire further research in non-convex optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The practical relevance of the results is questionable due to the specific configurations considered and the initialization assumptions.
*   The paper doesn’t fully address the likelihood of encountering the problematic scenarios in real-world applications.
*   The lack of clear justification for certain claims weakens the overall impact of the work.

**Overall assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the theoretical results are interesting and mathematically sound, the limited practical relevance and unanswered questions regarding the applicability of the results to real-world scenarios outweigh the contributions. Addressing the concerns regarding the likelihood of encountering “bad” configurations and the realism of the initialization scheme would significantly strengthen the paper.
