- Main theoretical results: proves that for M ≥ 3, well-separated isotropic GMMs can have local maxima with log-likelihood arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, and that EM (and first-order EM) with random initialization converges to such bad critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{−Ω(M)}.  
- Significance: demonstrates even idealized equally-weighted, spherical GMMs admit bad local maxima, resolving a long-standing conjecture and showing local-search algorithms cannot guarantee global convergence.  
- Novelty: introduces new proof techniques for establishing bad local maxima that could inform future non-convex optimization work.  
- Algorithmic insight: provides theoretical justification that careful initialization is critical for EM by analyzing EM and first-order EM behavior relative to saddle points.  
- Presentation: paper is well-organized, clearly written, and gives a thorough background linking to recent non-convex optimization advances.  
- Practical relevance concern 1: the paper does not quantify how likely the “bad” center configurations are in practice or under reasonable distributional assumptions.  
- Practical relevance concern 2: the random initialization model in Theorem 2 may be unrealistic in clearly clustered data (e.g., uneven cluster sizes), and needs more discussion about practical relevance.  
- Minor corrections: several wording/typo fixes recommended at L18, L38, L60, L138, L172, L177, L218, L220, L225, L245, L249, and L308 for clarity and grammar.  
- Strengths summary: clear resolution of an open question, novel proofs, thorough algorithmic analysis, and strong connections to non-convex optimization literature.  
- Weaknesses summary: limited discussion on practical likelihood of bad configurations, questionable applicability of the random-init model, and a handful of minor editorial issues.  
- Recommendation: the paper makes a significant theoretical contribution and is recommended for acceptance.
