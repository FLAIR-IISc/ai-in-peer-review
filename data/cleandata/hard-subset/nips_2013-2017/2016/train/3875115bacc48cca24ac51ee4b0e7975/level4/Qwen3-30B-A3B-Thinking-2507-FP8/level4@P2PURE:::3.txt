

The paper investigates the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models. It considers the simplest case: isotropic Gaussian components with known scale, under a well-specified model. The authors demonstrate that local modes exist even with infinite data, and that these modes can be arbitrarily poor in terms of the log-likelihood difference between local and global optima. The analysis focuses on specific configurations where mixture centers are partitioned into well-separated clusters. Under such configurations, the authors show that random initialization causes the EM algorithm to miss the global optimum almost surely as the number of components increases. The paper is well-written, though it contains occasional typos. I have two primary concerns regarding practical relevance.

1.  Theorem 1 establishes the existence of a local mode under a specific center configuration with infinite data. My question is: how probable is this configuration in practice? I would find the practical relevance more compelling if the authors assumed a distribution for the true centers and demonstrated that "bad" configurations (i.e., arrangements leading to poor local modes) become increasingly likely as the number of components $k$ or dimensionality increases. Lines L210–L218 claim Theorem 2 addresses this, but it is unclear how. A clear statement on the likelihood of "bad" configurations under reasonable assumptions is needed. Note: this concerns the *positions of the true centers*, not poor initialization.

2.  Theorem 2 examines EM initialization via random sampling of the mixture. Under specific center configurations, EM often fails to converge to the global optimum with this initialization. Consider data generated from three Gaussians, where two clusters are close together and one is far apart, with equal sample sizes $N$ per cluster. Thus, $2N$ points cluster near the first two Gaussians and $N$ near the third. It seems implausible that one would initialize EM with two centers in the $2N$-point region and one in the $N$-point region, especially given the clear separation between the clusters. Is random initialization practically relevant when data exhibits such strong clustering? Additionally, the example in lines L74–L81 does not convince me that the Sresbro conjecture holds for $k=2$; its purpose is unclear.

Further technical points:
*   L233–L235: Requirement (1) is unclear. Does it mean that initializing a center between two clusters prevents guaranteeing convergence to the correct cluster?
*   L267–L273: The proof that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddlepoint is interesting, but why is this practically important? How does convergence to a saddlepoint compare to convergence to a local optimum in terms of practical impact?
