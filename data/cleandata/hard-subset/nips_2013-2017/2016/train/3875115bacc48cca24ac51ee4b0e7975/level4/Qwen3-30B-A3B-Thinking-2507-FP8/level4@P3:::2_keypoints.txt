- Problem addressed: resolves whether population likelihood can have arbitrarily bad local maxima for well-separated GMMs, answering Srebro (2007) negatively for M ≥ 3.  
- Main contributions: (1) construct GMMs with arbitrarily bad local maxima; (2) show EM with random initialization converges to these bad maxima with probability ≥ 1−e^−Ω(M); (3) show a first-order EM variant also fails under random initialization.  
- Strength — originality/significance: resolves a long-standing open question, challenging theoretical justification for EM even in favorable (uniform, spherical, well-separated) settings and motivating improved initialization methods.  
- Strength — quality: technically sound proofs, clear distinction between population and sample likelihoods, and demonstration that failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.  
- Strength — clarity: well-organized paper, effective geometric intuition for the construction, and clear distinction between EM and its first-order variant.  
- Weakness — Theorem 1 proof: too brief; justification for limits and converting pointwise convergence to convergence of suprema over unbounded domains needs more rigorous detail.  
- Weakness — dimensional generalization: proofs focus on d=1 and do not clearly establish whether the construction and Theorem 1 extend to d>1 or discuss the challenges in higher dimensions.  
- Weakness — first-order EM explanation: derivation of Eq. (5) and relation to gradient ascent is hard to follow and would benefit from a brief derivation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: important theoretical resolution, rigorous EM failure guarantees, technically solid, and simplifications are justified for obtaining rigorous results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient detail in Theorem 1 proof, limited discussion of higher-dimensional extension, and unclear exposition of first-order EM updates.  
- Overall assessment: valuable, significant, and well-supported theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify Theorem 1 and dimensional generalization.
