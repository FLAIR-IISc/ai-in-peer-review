

This paper addresses a fundamental question in Gaussian mixture model (GMM) estimation: whether the population likelihood function can have bad local maxima. The authors prove two main results: (1) for M ≥ 3 components, well-separated isotropic GMMs can have local maxima with log-likelihood arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, resolving Srebro's (2007) conjecture that such bad local maxima do not exist in the population limit; and (2) with random initialization, the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) converges to these bad critical points with probability at least 1 - e^{-Ω(M)}.

The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that even in the idealized setting of equally-weighted, well-separated, spherical Gaussians, local search algorithms cannot guarantee global convergence. The authors' proof techniques for establishing the existence of bad local maxima are novel and could inform future work on non-convex optimization. Their analysis of EM's behavior under random initialization provides important theoretical justification for the empirical observation that careful initialization is critical for GMM estimation.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough background that contextualizes the problem within prior work on mixture models and EM. The authors effectively relate their results to recent advances in understanding non-convex optimization landscapes, particularly through their analysis of the first-order EM algorithm's behavior with respect to saddle points.

However, I have two main concerns about the practical relevance of the results. First, while Theorem 1 establishes the existence of bad local maxima for specific configurations of centers, the paper does not address how likely such configurations are to occur in practice. The authors could strengthen their argument by considering reasonable assumptions about the distribution of centers and showing that "bad" configurations become increasingly probable as M increases or dimensionality grows. The claim in lines L210-218 that Theorem 2 addresses this concern is not fully convincing, as Theorem 2 focuses on the behavior of EM under random initialization rather than the probability of encountering bad configurations.

Second, the random initialization scheme analyzed in Theorem 2 appears questionable in practical settings where data is clearly clustered. For instance, in a 3-component mixture where two components are close together and one is far away, it would be unusual to initialize EM with two centers in the region containing 2N points and one center in the region with N points, especially when the clusters are well-separated. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of whether random initialization is practically relevant in such scenarios.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by resolving an open question about the likelihood landscape of GMMs. While the practical relevance could be strengthened by addressing the likelihood of encountering "bad" configurations and the practicality of the random initialization scheme, the theoretical results are sound and well-supported. The work provides important insights for practitioners about the limitations of standard EM implementations and highlights the necessity of careful initialization strategies.

Strengths: Clear theoretical contribution resolving a longstanding open question; novel proof techniques; thorough analysis of algorithmic behavior; strong connections to recent advances in non-convex optimization.

Weaknesses: Limited discussion of practical relevance of "bad" configurations; questionable practicality of random initialization in clustered data scenarios; some minor grammatical issues.

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the optimization landscape of Gaussian mixture models. While the practical implications could be strengthened, the results are important for the field and would be of interest to researchers working on mixture models and non-convex optimization. I recommend acceptance.
