The paper investigates the properties of the likelihood function for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically focusing on the existence of bad local maxima and their impact on the performance of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors consider a well-specified model – isotropic Gaussian components with known scale – and demonstrate that, even with infinite data, bad local maxima exist and can be arbitrarily worse than global optima. They further show that with random initialization, the EM algorithm will converge to these suboptimal points with high probability as the number of components increases. The paper is generally well-written and technically sound.

The core contribution lies in rigorously demonstrating the failure of the EM algorithm under specific, albeit carefully constructed, conditions. This resolves an open question posed by Srebro [2007] and provides a theoretical explanation for the difficulties often encountered in practice when estimating GMMs. The results highlight the importance of careful initialization when using EM, even in seemingly favorable settings. The inclusion of Theorem 4, demonstrating that the first-order EM algorithm is unlikely to converge to saddle points, strengthens the argument that bad local optima are the primary cause of performance issues, rather than saddle points.

However, the practical relevance of the findings requires further consideration. While Theorem 1 establishes the existence of bad local maxima, it does so for a particular configuration of mixture centers. A crucial question is how likely such configurations are to arise in real-world datasets. The paper acknowledges this concern but doesn't fully address it.  While Theorem 2 attempts to demonstrate that the algorithm fails *with high probability* for these configurations, it doesn’t quantify the probability of encountering these configurations in the first place.  Adding a discussion on the probability of “bad” configurations under reasonable assumptions about the data generation process (e.g., distributions of cluster centers) would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact. 

Furthermore, the random initialization scheme, where centers are sampled uniformly from the data, feels somewhat unrealistic in scenarios with clear cluster structure. If the data is generated from, for example, three Gaussians with two closely grouped and one distant, it seems unlikely that a truly random initialization would place two centers within the small region occupied by the two close Gaussians and only one center near the isolated Gaussian. Exploring the sensitivity of the results to different initialization strategies would be valuable.

The example on lines 74-81 regarding the two-component case is unclear in its motivation. If it isn’t a rigorous proof of Srebro’s conjecture for *k*=2, its purpose needs to be clarified. The current presentation doesn’t effectively illustrate the point it intends to make. Lines 233-235: The justification for requiring both conditions in the initialization scheme isn't fully explained. Elaborating on why initializing a center *between* clusters hinders convergence to the correct cluster would improve clarity.  The significance of Theorem 3, showing that EM avoids saddle points, is not clearly articulated. Is converging to a saddle point more or less detrimental than converging to a local optimum in practice?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong theoretical results:** The paper provides rigorous proofs for the existence of bad local maxima and the failure of the EM algorithm under specific conditions.
*   **Resolves an open problem:** The work answers a long-standing question in the field of Gaussian mixture modeling.
*   **Well-written and technically sound:** The paper is generally clear and logically structured.
*   **Addresses a practically relevant issue:** Poor initialization can significantly impact the performance of EM.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited discussion of practical relevance:** The paper doesn’t fully address the likelihood of encountering the problematic configurations in real-world data.
*   **Unrealistic initialization scheme:** The random initialization strategy may not be representative of practical scenarios.
*   **Unclear motivation for certain examples:** The purpose of the example on lines 74-81 isn’t clearly explained.
*   **Unclear justification for initialization requirements:** The reasoning behind certain initialization constraints is not fully elaborated.
