This paper investigates the structure of local maxima in the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), specifically those with equal mixing weights and identity covariance matrices. It presents three main results: (1) a counterexample demonstrating the existence of local maxima (for M=3 and d=1) with arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global maximum; (2) a showing that, with random initialization, the probability of converging to a bad local maximum can be exponentially small in M under a specific mean configuration; and (3) a proof that first-order gradient-based EM methods do not converge to strict saddle points almost surely. 

The paper builds upon previous work examining identifiability and convergence rates in GMMs [Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009; Chen, 1995; Ho & Nguyen, 2015]. It addresses a conjecture by Srebro [2007] regarding the absence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood. Related work on the EM algorithm, including analyses of its convergence properties [Dempster et al., 1977; Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015] and alternatives based on clustering or method of moments [Arora et al., 2005; Belkin & Sinha, 2010], provides context for the current investigation. The study also connects to broader research on non-convex optimization and the role of saddle points [Pascanu et al., 2014; Lee et al., 2016].

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper offers a significant and original contribution by explicitly characterizing the structure of local maxima in GMM likelihoods. The connection between the mean configuration of Gaussians and the properties of local optima is insightful.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The proofs, particularly for Theorem 1 (which I examined in detail), appear sound and well-reasoned. The proposed approach for Theorem 2 seems plausible, although a full verification requires checking the appendix.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, results, and intuition behind the analysis. The problem setup is clearly defined, and the authors provide insightful interpretations of their findings.
*   **Potential Impact:** The results have potential implications for both theoretical understanding and practical algorithm design. The findings could stimulate further research on the local optima structure of related clustering tasks (e.g., k-means) and inform the development of more robust initialization schemes for EM.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Specific Configuration:** The result concerning the high probability of converging to a bad local maximum (Theorem 2) relies on a specific, somewhat contrived, hierarchical structure of the true means. It would be valuable to explore the robustness of this result to more general configurations.
*   **Dimensionality:** The counterexample demonstrating bad local maxima (Theorem 1) is restricted to d=1. Investigating whether similar behavior exists in higher dimensions (even for specific cases) would strengthen the findings.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the theoretical results are strong, some empirical validation (even on a small scale) demonstrating the practical relevance of these findings could enhance the impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important open question in the theory of Gaussian Mixture Models.
*   The results are mathematically rigorous and contribute novel insights into the structure of the likelihood function.
*   The clear and well-organized presentation makes the paper accessible and valuable to the NIPS community.
*   The potential impact on both theoretical and practical aspects of mixture modeling is significant.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The reliance on a specific configuration in Theorem 2 limits the generalizability of the findings.
*   The restriction to d=1 in the counterexample (Theorem 1) leaves open the question of whether similar phenomena occur in higher dimensions.
*   The lack of empirical validation weakens the claim of practical relevance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of Gaussian Mixture Models. While the results have some limitations (particularly the specific configuration required for Theorem 2 and the dimensionality restriction of Theorem 1), the theoretical rigor, clarity of presentation, and potential impact outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS. I recommend a score of 8/10.
