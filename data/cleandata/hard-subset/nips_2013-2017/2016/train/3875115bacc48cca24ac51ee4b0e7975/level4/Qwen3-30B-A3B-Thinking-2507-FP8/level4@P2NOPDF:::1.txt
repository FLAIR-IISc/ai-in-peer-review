

The paper investigates the convergence properties of the EM algorithm and related iterative methods for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, establishing three key results concerning non-convergence to global maxima: (1) EM can converge to local optima even with three well-separated clusters (sufficiently separated by Ω(√d)) and infinite samples; (2) random initialization succeeds with probability at most exp(-Ω(k)) for k-component mixtures; (3) gradient EM avoids strict saddle points generically, indicating that failure stems from suboptimal local maxima rather than saddle points. While the paper presents a coherent narrative demonstrating EM’s potential failure to reach global optima, the results largely align with established insights in clustering theory, diminishing their novelty.  

The central counterexample involves three components: two clusters close together, one distant. The log-likelihood objective exhibits a local maximum where one center aligns with the two proximate clusters, and two centers capture the distant cluster. This behavior is unsurprising under sufficient separation (>>√d), as the problem reduces to a k-means-like regime where clusters are non-overlapping and learning becomes combinatorial. Similar counterexamples for k-means (e.g., Lloyd’s algorithm) are well-documented, and the paper’s recursive extension to k components, while technically sound, follows the same expected pattern. The random initialization failure probability (exp(-Ω(k))) parallels known k-means results, where distance-squared sampling or k log k initialization is required to avoid such pitfalls.  

The primary gap lies in the lack of meaningful contrast with k-means convergence literature. Kumar and Kannan established convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic under sufficient separation, while Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu showed that EM behaves similarly to Lloyd’s when clusters are well-separated—precisely the regime where the paper’s counterexamples arise. The authors should explicitly address this connection: their bad examples for EM are effectively adaptations of k-means failure cases under the same separation conditions. A comparative discussion would clarify whether the EM-specific analysis offers new insights beyond the k-means framework or merely rephrases known phenomena. Without this, the work risks appearing as a routine extension rather than a distinct contribution.  

While the technical execution is solid, the results do not significantly advance the understanding of EM convergence beyond what is already implied by k-means theory. The paper’s narrative is logically consistent but lacks the element of surprise or unexpected insight expected in top-tier venues. For instance, the observation that EM fails under large separation is not new—k-means fails identically in this regime, and EM is known to behave like Lloyd’s under such conditions. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what *new* challenges EM faces beyond the k-means analogy, or a stronger argument for why spherical Gaussian mixtures necessitate separate analysis.  

**Comments:**  
1. **Critical comparison with k-means is missing.** The paper should explicitly compare its results to Kumar-Kannan’s k-means convergence guarantees and Balakrishnan-Wainwright-Yu’s EM-k-means equivalence under separation. This would contextualize the novelty: Are the counterexamples fundamentally different from k-means failures, or merely rephrased? If the latter, the contribution is incremental.  
2. **Clarify the separation threshold.** The paper states "very far apart" but does not precisely define the separation required for the counterexamples (e.g., Ω(√d) or larger). Explicitly linking this to the k-means regime would strengthen the argument and align with existing literature.  
3. **Strengthen the "why it matters" for practitioners.** While the paper shows EM can fail, it does not discuss practical remedies (e.g., initialization strategies beyond random). Given that k-means uses distance-squared sampling to avoid such failures, a brief discussion of analogous EM improvements would add value.  

In summary, the paper delivers a technically sound analysis of EM’s limitations but fails to demonstrate how these results transcend the well-understood k-means failure modes. The results are coherent and expected, not novel, and the work would benefit significantly from a focused comparison with the established k-means literature. Without this, the contribution appears marginal for a venue like NIPS.
