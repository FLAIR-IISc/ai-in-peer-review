This paper addresses a fundamental open question posed by Srebro in his 2007 work, "Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?". The authors provide a negative answer, constructing a general class of counterexamples demonstrating the existence of such local maxima. The paper begins with a clear outline of likelihood maximization techniques for Gaussian Mixture Models, including the Expectation-Maximization (EM) and gradient-based EM algorithms, and discusses a typical initialization strategy. They then present their main theorems, establishing the counterexamples to Srebro’s conjecture [2007]. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that even with common random initialization, the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to these non-optimal maxima with exponentially high probability. To provide intuition, they present a simplified proof for a specific case (k=3, d=1). The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, effectively balancing theoretical rigor with intuitive explanation within the space constraints. Addressing the question at hand is crucial for advancing our understanding of the challenges inherent in non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic limit for relatively simple distributions. While I did not perform a detailed examination of the supplementary material, providing estimates for the constants within the probability bounds would be valuable. This is particularly relevant as the value of *k* is unlikely to be excessively large in practice. Such estimates would help pinpoint the point at which convergence to suboptimal solutions becomes significant in the asymptotic limit – effectively identifying a phase transition. If obtaining closed-form constants proves difficult, supporting simulations would bolster the claim that this phenomenon is observable. However, this point does not diminish the novelty or significant impact of the work.
