This paper studies the structure of local maxima of the log-likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (with equal mixing weights and identity covariance). It presents three related results: 1) It provides a counterexample showing that bad local maxima exist (when *M* = 3 and *d* = 1), whose likelihood can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. 2) It shows that with random initialization, the probability of *not* converging to a bad local maximum can be exponentially small in *M*, under a specific configuration of the Gaussian means. 3) It also shows that first-order EM does not converge to a strict saddle point of the log-likelihood almost surely.

**Summary:** This paper investigates the properties of the likelihood surface for Gaussian Mixture Models, demonstrating the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima and analyzing the behavior of the EM algorithm under random initialization. 

**Technical Quality:** I checked the proof of Theorem 1 and find it sound. Having reviewed the analysis of Theorem 1, the proof idea for Theorem 2 also appears reasonable, although I haven't verified the details in the Appendix. The theorems are well-stated and clearly interpreted after their presentation.

**Novelty & Originality:** The paper connects the mean configuration of a GMM to the structure of local maxima in its log-likelihood function. This observation, while intuitive, is a significant and original contribution. The core idea behind Theorem 2, which leverages the hierarchical grouping structure of true means to explain the behavior of local maxima for *M* > 3, is also a clever approach.

**Potential Impact & Usefulness:** The results have the potential to impact both theoretical and practical research. Theoretically, it could inspire further study of the local optima structure in related clustering tasks, such as k-means. Examining whether Lemma 7 extends to *d* > 1 would also be an interesting direction. Practically, the findings underscore the importance of appropriate initialization strategies – specifically, seeding enough points in each true cluster – and may provide insights for designing new algorithms.

**Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly presents its results. It effectively introduces the problem setup and offers intuitive explanations of the analysis. The arguments are logical and easy to follow.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Technical Contribution:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical results on a challenging problem in statistical learning.
*   **Novelty:** The connection between GMM mean configurations and the local maxima structure is a novel and insightful observation.
*   **Theoretical Importance:** The findings shed light on the fundamental limitations of local search methods like EM and highlight the necessity of careful initialization.
*   **Well-Written:** The paper is clear, concise, and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Specific Configuration:** The analysis in Theorem 2 relies on a somewhat restrictive configuration of the Gaussian means. While insightful, the results might not generalize to all GMM structures.
*   **Limited Practical Guidance:** While the paper highlights the importance of initialization, it doesn’t offer concrete algorithms for achieving good initialization in practice.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of the challenges in estimating Gaussian mixture models. The results are well-motivated, technically sound, and clearly presented. Despite the limitations regarding the specific configuration examined in Theorem 2 and the lack of immediate practical algorithms, the theoretical insights are valuable and warrant acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
