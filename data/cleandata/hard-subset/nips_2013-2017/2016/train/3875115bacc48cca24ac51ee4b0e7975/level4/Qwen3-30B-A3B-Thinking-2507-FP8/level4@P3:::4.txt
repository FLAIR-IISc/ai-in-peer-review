

This paper addresses an important open question raised by Srebro (2007) regarding the structure of local maxima in the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). Srebro conjectured that for GMMs with M ≥ 2 components, any local maximum of the population likelihood would be a global maximum in the infinite-sample limit. The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture by demonstrating that for M ≥ 3, there exist well-separated, equally-weighted GMMs with bad local maxima whose log-likelihood can be arbitrarily worse than that of the global optimum. They further show that with random initialization, the EM algorithm (and a first-order variant) converges to these bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^{-Ω(M)}.

The paper makes three key contributions. First, Theorem 1 constructs a counterexample showing the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 in 1D, resolving Srebro's open question. Second, Theorem 2 establishes that random initialization of EM leads to convergence to suboptimal points with exponentially high probability in M, highlighting the critical importance of careful initialization in practice. Third, Theorem 4 shows that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, suggesting that the poor performance is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs for Theorems 1 and 4, and well-motivated arguments for Theorem 2. The construction of the counterexample is clever and the hierarchical grouping argument for Theorem 2 is well-justified. The authors carefully distinguish between population and sample settings, and provide clear connections to previous work on GMMs (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. 2015, Dasgupta and Schulman 2007). The analysis of EM behavior is thorough and addresses a significant practical concern.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written. The problem setup is clearly defined, the intuition behind constructions is well-explained (including the helpful Figure 1 for the two-component case), and the theorems are well-motivated. The authors balance mathematical rigor with accessibility, making the paper accessible to a broad NIPS audience. The structure is logical, moving from theoretical results to algorithmic consequences.

Originality: The paper's core contribution—demonstrating the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima for M ≥ 3—represents a significant theoretical advance. While previous work has considered EM behavior for specific GMM configurations (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. 2015 for M=2), this paper provides the first rigorous evidence that bad local maxima can exist for M ≥ 3 in the population setting. The connection between hierarchical grouping of true means and likelihood surface structure is particularly novel.

Significance: The results have important theoretical and practical implications. Theoretically, they provide a fundamental understanding of the non-convex optimization landscape of GMM likelihood, which has been a persistent challenge in the field. Practically, they explain why random initialization of EM can fail for GMMs with M ≥ 3, even in favorable settings, and suggest that effective initialization strategies (such as those based on method of moments) are crucial. This work could inspire further research on the optimization landscape of related clustering problems.

Strengths: 
- Clear resolution of an important open question
- Well-structured and well-written paper
- Strong technical results with significant theoretical implications
- Practical insights for algorithm design

Weaknesses:
- The results are limited to the idealized setting of equally-weighted, spherical GMMs with unit variance. While this is common for theoretical work, it would be interesting to see whether similar phenomena occur in more general settings (e.g., with unequal weights or non-spherical Gaussians).
- The paper focuses on population likelihood, and while the authors note results extend to finite samples via standard arguments, a more detailed discussion of the sample size required for these phenomena to manifest would be valuable.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the optimization landscape of GMMs. The results are well-motivated, rigorously proven, and have clear implications for both theory and practice. The paper is well-written and makes a strong case for its importance. I believe it would be a valuable contribution to NIPS.

Pro: Resolves an important open question with a clever counterexample, provides practical insights about EM initialization, and makes a clear theoretical contribution to understanding non-convex optimization in GMMs.
Con: Limited to idealized GMM settings, though this is common for theoretical work.

I recommend acceptance.
