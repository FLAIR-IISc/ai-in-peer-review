This paper investigates the population log-likelihood landscape of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and its implications for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors present three main results. First, they resolve an open question from Srebro (2007) by showing that the population log-likelihood for GMMs with three or more components can have "bad" local maxima, even in the idealized setting of well-separated, spherical, and equally-weighted Gaussians. The value of these local optima can be arbitrarily worse than that of the global optimum. Second, they demonstrate that for an M-component GMM, the EM algorithm with random initialization (drawing initial centers from the underlying data distribution) will converge to these suboptimal critical points with a probability of at least 1 - exp(-Ω(M)). Finally, they show that a first-order variant of EM almost surely avoids converging to strict saddle points, suggesting that the algorithm's failure is primarily due to being trapped in poor local maxima rather than navigating saddle points.

The paper's primary strength lies in providing a definitive negative answer to a long-standing conjecture about the GMM likelihood landscape. The results are presented as a coherent and compelling narrative, rigorously connecting the structural properties of the objective function (the existence of bad local optima) to significant algorithmic consequences (the high probability of failure for randomly initialized EM). The technical arguments, particularly the recursive construction used to establish the exponential failure probability for M components, are clear and well-executed.

However, the main weakness of the paper concerns the novelty and surprisingness of the results when viewed in the broader context of clustering algorithms. The analysis is conducted under a strong assumption that the Gaussian components are well-separated. In this regime, where the clusters have minimal overlap, the problem of maximizing the GMM likelihood becomes very similar in character to the k-means clustering problem. The failure modes identified in the paper are highly analogous to well-documented issues with Lloyd's algorithm for k-means.

For instance, the construction of a bad local maximum for M=3—where one estimated center is placed between two nearby true clusters, and two estimated centers are placed on a single, distant true cluster—is a classic example used to illustrate how Lloyd's algorithm can converge to a suboptimal partitioning. Similarly, the finding that random initialization fails with exponentially high probability is a known phenomenon for k-means. This very issue motivated the development of more sophisticated initialization schemes, such as k-means++ (which uses distance-squared sampling) and methods based on oversampling centers. While the authors have rigorously proven these behaviors for the GMM population likelihood, the underlying phenomena feel somewhat expected given the established understanding of k-means in similar settings.

To strengthen the paper and better contextualize its contributions, I recommend the following:

1.  **Add a discussion on the relationship with k-means:** The paper would be significantly improved by explicitly discussing the connection to k-means clustering and Lloyd's algorithm. Under the large separation conditions assumed in the paper's constructions, the E-step of the EM algorithm (which calculates membership weights) approximates the hard assignment step of Lloyd's algorithm. A formal discussion of this relationship would help clarify to what extent the results are a direct consequence of this similarity.

2.  **Contrast with existing analyses of clustering algorithms:** It would be valuable to cite and contrast these results with the literature on the convergence of Lloyd's algorithm (e.g., work by Kumar and Kannan). Such a comparison would help the reader understand the specific contributions of this work. For example, does the analysis reveal subtleties in the GMM likelihood objective that are not present in the k-means objective, even under strong separation? Or is the primary contribution to formally extend these known failure modes from the discrete k-means objective to the continuous GMM likelihood setting? Addressing this would help frame the paper's originality more clearly.

In summary, the paper presents a coherent set of theoretical results that formally characterize the challenging optimization landscape of GMMs, even in favorable settings. While the findings are rigorously established, their connection to well-known failure modes in k-means clustering under similar conditions makes them feel less surprising. A more thorough discussion of this relationship would strengthen the paper by better situating its contributions within the existing literature.
