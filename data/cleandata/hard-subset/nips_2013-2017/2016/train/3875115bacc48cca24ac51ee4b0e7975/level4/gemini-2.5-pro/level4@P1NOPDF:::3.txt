This paper investigates the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models. The analysis focuses on a simple, well-specified mixture model of isotropic Gaussian components with a known, shared scale. The authors demonstrate the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood function, even in the infinite data limit. Furthermore, they show these local modes can be arbitrarily poor, exhibiting large differences in log-likelihood compared to the global optimum. The paper's results are derived for specific configurations where the true mixture centers are themselves grouped into well-separated clusters. For these configurations, the authors prove that a random initialization strategy will cause the EM algorithm to miss the global optimum with high probability as the number of components increases. The paper is generally well-written, though it contains some minor typos.

I have two main concerns regarding the practical relevance of the findings.

1.  My first concern is about the configurations of the true centers that are studied. Theorem 1 shows that the log-likelihood has spurious local modes for a particular arrangement of centers. A key question is how likely these specific configurations are to occur in practice. The practical relevance of this result would be more convincing if the authors could show, under some reasonable assumptions about the distribution of the true centers, that such "bad" configurations become more probable as the number of components (k) or the dimensionality (d) increases. The authors state on lines 210-218 that Theorem 2 addresses these concerns, but I am not convinced it does so fully. A clear statement on the likelihood of these "bad" configurations, supported by analysis, would greatly strengthen the paper's claims. Note that this concern is about the configuration of the *true* centers, not the initialization of the algorithm.

2.  My second concern relates to Theorem 2 and the choice of initialization for the EM algorithm. The authors consider initializing the component centers by randomly sampling data points from the mixture. They show that for their specific center configurations, this initialization strategy leads to a high probability of failure to find the global optimum. However, consider a practical scenario with data generated from three Gaussians, two of which are close together and one that is far apart. If we draw N data points from each Gaussian, then 2N points will be concentrated in one region and N points in another, well-separated region. In this case, randomly sampling from the data could easily lead to an initialization with two centers in the smaller cluster and only one in the larger one. This seems like a poor and unlikely initialization choice for a practitioner, especially when the data is so clearly clustered. This raises the question of whether random initialization by sampling from the mixture is a relevant or realistic strategy for datasets that exhibit clear clustering structure.

**Specific Questions and Comments:**

*   **L74-81:** The purpose of the example on lines 74-81 is unclear. It does not appear to provide convincing evidence for Srebro's conjecture when k=2. Could the authors clarify its role in their argument?
*   **L233-235:** Regarding the requirements on these lines, the need for requirement (2) is clear, but the motivation for requirement (1) is not. Is the concern that a center initialized between two clusters is not guaranteed to move toward the correct one?
*   **L267-273:** On these lines, the authors prove that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point. What is the practical significance of this result? Is convergence to a saddle point considered more or less problematic than convergence to a local optimum in this context?
