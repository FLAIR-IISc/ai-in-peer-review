The paper investigates the convergence properties of the EM algorithm and other iterative Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) methods when applied to learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians. It demonstrates non-convergence to global optima with the following key findings: first, even with well-separated clusters (k=3) and an infinite number of samples, algorithms like EM can become trapped in local optima; second, for mixtures of *k* Gaussians, random initialization of EM succeeds in finding a global optimum with a probability of at most exp(-\Omega(k)); and third, gradient-based EM methods generally do not converge to strict saddle points, indicating that poor local maxima are the primary obstacle to convergence. The paper presents a consistent set of results establishing that EM does not guarantee convergence to global optima, even in the limit of infinite data. However, I find these results largely unsurprising, and the techniques employed are fairly standard. 

The primary illustrative example involves a mixture of three components where two are close together and the third is significantly distant. The authors show that this configuration leads to a local maximum where one center is positioned near the first two components and two centers are near the far-off cluster. This behavior is predictable, especially when the cluster centers are separated by a distance much greater than \sqrt{d}. Under such separation, cluster overlap is minimal, and learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians becomes qualitatively similar to k-means clustering. Analogous examples exist demonstrating poor performance of Lloyd's algorithm for k-means. This example is generalized to *k* components through a recursive construction – an effortful but reasonably anticipated extension. The observed failure of random initialization similarly reflects known issues in k-means clustering and exhibits the same failure probability, motivating strategies such as initializing with *k* log *k* centers or using distance-squared sampling. 

In conclusion, while the paper delivers a coherent narrative regarding non-convergence to global optima, the results follow lines that are, to a significant extent, expected. A comparison with k-means clustering (and Lloyd's heuristic) would be valuable, particularly highlighting how EM and Lloyd’s heuristic behave similarly with large separations – a regime relevant to the presented bad examples. Existing work by Kumar-Kannan provides convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic under sufficient separation conditions, and contrasting these guarantees with the observed EM behavior would strengthen the analysis, especially given the differences between EM and Lloyd's heuristic when separations are smaller (as noted in Balakrishnan-Wainwright-Yu).
