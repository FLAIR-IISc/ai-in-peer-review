This paper studies the convergence behavior of EM (and related iterative heuristics for MLE) for mixtures of spherical Gaussians and presents a coherent set of negative results demonstrating non-convergence to global maxima, even with infinite samples. The main claims are:
1) Even with three well-separated components and infinite samples, algorithms like EM can get stuck in local optima.
2) For mixtures of k Gaussians, random initialization for EM succeeds with probability at most exp(-Ω(k)).
3) Gradient EM generically avoids strict saddle points; thus, bad local maxima are typically the true obstruction.

Overall, the results fit together well and clearly illustrate that EM need not reach global optima in these settings (even in the population limit). That said, the results and techniques feel largely unsurprising and follow expected lines.

The central counterexample uses a mixture with three components: two are closer to each other, and the third is very far from both. For the log-likelihood objective, there is a local maximum with one center near the pair of closer components and two centers near the far-away component. This behavior is fairly expected, especially when centers are separated by >> sqrt(d). Under such separation, the clusters barely overlap, and learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians becomes similar in spirit to k-means. Closely related constructions are known to produce bad instances for Lloyd’s algorithm in k-means clustering. The paper extends this example to k components via a clean recursive construction—this requires some work, but again the overall phenomenon seems expected. The random-initialization failure also parallels the k-means situation, leading to the same exp(-Ω(k)) success probability; for k-means, this motivates selecting roughly k log k initial centers or using distance-squared sampling.

In sum, the paper tells a coherent and technically sound story about the non-convergence of EM to global optima, but along lines that are largely anticipated given the analogy to k-means under large separation.

Comments:
- Kumar–Kannan provides convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic for k-means (and, by extension, for mixtures of Gaussians under sufficient separation). While EM can differ from Lloyd’s when Gaussians are not sufficiently separated (as in Balakrishnan–Wainwright–Yu), the two are essentially similar under large separation—the regime in which the bad examples here operate. A direct, careful comparison with k-means/Lloyd’s (emphasizing where the behaviors coincide or diverge) would significantly strengthen the presentation and situate the contributions more clearly relative to known results.
