This paper addresses the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the set of mean parameters in a mixture of Gaussian variables model. The number of Gaussian variables, mixture weights, and covariance matrices are assumed to be known and fixed. The paper is theoretical in nature and presents three negative results for the EM algorithm. Firstly, it shows that even for large sample sizes, local maxima can exist in the likelihood function with likelihood values arbitrarily smaller than the maximum likelihood value. Secondly, the EM algorithm, when randomly initialized, converges with high probability to a critical point with a likelihood value that can be arbitrarily smaller than the maximum likelihood value. Lastly, the same negative result holds for the gradient EM algorithm. I find the question addressed by the paper to be interesting and worthy of attention. To obtain rigorous proofs, the paper considers a simplified mixture of Gaussian variable model, where the number of components is known, the weights are known and uniform, and the covariance matrices are equal to the identity matrix. I do not believe these simplifications detract from the paper, given the technical nature of the proofs and the informative results obtained. These results are very interesting and can be useful in understanding the EM algorithm in practice.

It is worth noting that the paper is written to address the general case of dimension d, while the proofs are specifically for the case d=1. The authors may want to discuss whether similar proofs are possible for arbitrary dimension d and, if not, briefly mention the additional difficulties encountered in higher dimensions. My primary concern with the paper is the proof of Theorem 1, which I found to be too concise. I would have preferred a longer proof, potentially at the expense of shortening some of the discussions in the paper. Specifically, I believe more details are necessary from line 288 to 306, where the authors provide limits of supremums of likelihood functions over certain domains. While I do not have issues with computing limits of likelihood functions evaluated at fixed parameters, obtaining the limit of a maximum is less obvious, particularly when the domain is unbounded or dependent on the parameters going to infinity. Furthermore, the authors mention the continuity of the likelihood function, but this does not directly imply the types of convergences stated in the paper. I believe a longer proof with more details is necessary to fully convince the reader of the validity of the proof of Theorem 1.

Additionally, I have a minor concern with the section on the Gradient EM algorithm, from line 162 to 172, which I found more challenging to follow than the rest of the paper. The authors may want to provide more explanation on how Equation (5) is obtained.
