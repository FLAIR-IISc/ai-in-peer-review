Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with equal mixing weights and identity covariances, and analyzes the algorithmic behavior of EM and a first-order (gradient) variant under random initialization. It makes three main contributions:

1) Structural result (Theorem 1): For any M ≥ 3, even in d = 1 and with well-separated, uniformly weighted, spherical components, the population log-likelihood can have spurious local maxima whose values are arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. This resolves a conjecture posed by Srebro (2007), who hypothesized the absence of bad local maxima in the population limit for such favorable settings.

2) Algorithmic consequence for EM (Theorem 2): With random initialization (drawing initial means i.i.d. from the mixture distribution), EM converges to a suboptimal critical point with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M) for a specially constructed family of mixtures. The construction uses a hierarchical clustering of true centers to show exponential (in M) decay of the success probability, implying that exponentially many restarts may be needed to find a near-global maximum.

3) Behavior of first-order EM (Theorems 3–4): The first-order EM (equivalently, gradient ascent on the population likelihood with stepsize s ∈ (0, 1)) exhibits the same failure mode as EM under random initialization (exponentially small success probability), yet almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points. This indicates that poor performance is driven by bad local maxima, not by saddles.

These results complement a large literature on EM and GMMs. Prior work has provided local convergence guarantees under good initializations or strong separations (e.g., Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015), algorithmic alternatives via clustering and moments (Arora et al., 2005; Vempala and Wang, 2002; Moitra and Valiant, 2010; Hsu and Kakade, 2013), and discussions of saddle points in nonconvex optimization (Pascanu et al., 2014; Lee et al., 2016). This paper fills an important gap by showing that even in the population setting, bad local maxima exist and are algorithmically relevant under natural random initializations.

Strengths
- Resolves a well-known conjecture by Srebro (2007) with a clean counterexample for M ≥ 3, d = 1, and well-separated spherical components.
- Provides a compelling algorithmic implication: random initialization plus local search (EM or gradient ascent) can fail with exponentially high probability, using an insightful hierarchical construction.
- Clarifies the role of saddles vs. local maxima: first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, so failures stem from suboptimal local maxima.
- Clear exposition with helpful intuition and connections to existing literature; the geometric explanation for M = 3 is especially accessible.

Weaknesses and questions
- Population-only analysis: While the authors note that finite-sample analogs follow by standard arguments, it would be helpful to state explicit sample-size conditions under which the constructions and failure probabilities are preserved with high probability.
- Scope of assumptions: The negative results are shown for equal weights and identity covariances. How robust are they to mild weight imbalance or anisotropy? Small extensions or a discussion would increase practical relevance.
- M = 2 case remains open: The paper acknowledges this. Given the empirical figure and intuition for M = 2, a more systematic discussion of what fails in the M = 2 extension would be useful.
- Practical mitigations: The paper recommends careful initialization. It would be valuable to more concretely connect the negative results to known successful initializations (e.g., k-means++, moment-based pilot estimators) and to quantify when these mitigations overcome the failure modes.
- Reference formatting: The bibliography contains noticeable formatting issues and inconsistencies; please correct and complete the citations.

Quality
The results are technically solid and well-motivated. I carefully checked the main structural argument (Theorem 1) and found it sound; the constructive, geometric intuition is persuasive. The high-level proof strategy for the exponential failure probability (Theorem 2) is reasonable and consistent with the recursive clustering construction; while I did not verify all appendix details, the argument appears credible. The analysis of first-order EM’s avoidance of strict saddles is consistent with recent gradient dynamics theory and is carefully stated.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The population vs. sample setting is clearly delineated, and the algorithms and initialization scheme are precisely defined. The intuition accompanying each theorem is particularly helpful. Minor presentation issues: fix reference formatting, and consider adding a small illustrative experiment (even synthetic) demonstrating the hierarchical construction and success probability scaling to aid reader intuition.

Originality
The negative structural result for the population likelihood of well-separated spherical mixtures is, to my knowledge, new and directly resolves an open conjecture. The hierarchical construction yielding exponentially small success probability for random initialization is novel and insightful. The strict-saddle avoidance result for first-order EM, while built on recent gradient dynamics theory, is a useful and targeted application in this context.

Significance
The work is significant for both theory and practice. Theoretically, it sharpens our understanding of the nonconvex landscape of GMM likelihoods and the limitations of EM under standard random initialization. Practically, it cautions against naive restarts and underscores the importance of informed initialization schemes, even in seemingly benign settings. The ideas may inspire analogous analyses for related clustering objectives (e.g., k-means) and for mixtures beyond spherical Gaussians.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide explicit finite-sample statements (with sample-size scaling) that mirror the population results.
- Discuss robustness to slight deviations from equal weights and identity covariances; even partial results or conjectures would be valuable.
- Elaborate on the M = 2 case: outline barriers to extending the proof or provide additional empirical evidence.
- Add a brief section comparing and quantifying practical initialization strategies (e.g., k-means++, spectral/moment-based pilots) in light of the negative results.
- Clean up references and minor typos.

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves a notable open question; strong, clear theoretical contributions.
- Provides compelling algorithmic consequences with practical import.
- Well-written and insightful, likely to influence future research on nonconvex statistical estimation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Results are confined to the population setting and very specific model assumptions; finite-sample and robustness discussions are limited.
- Lack of empirical demonstrations may reduce accessibility for some readers.

Overall evaluation
A strong theoretical paper that makes a clear, original, and consequential contribution to our understanding of EM and GMM likelihood landscapes. I recommend acceptance, contingent on minor revisions to address clarity, references, and a brief discussion of finite-sample robustness and practical mitigations.
