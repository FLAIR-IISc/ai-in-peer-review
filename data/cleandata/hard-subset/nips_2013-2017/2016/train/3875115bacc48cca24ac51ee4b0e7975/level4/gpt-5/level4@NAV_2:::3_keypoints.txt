- Paper proves local maxima exist in the population log-likelihood of isotropic, correctly specified GMMs and can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.
- Analysis focuses on geometric arrangements where component means are grouped into well-separated clusters.
- Shows that under these arrangements, EM with random initialization will almost surely miss the global optimum as the number of components grows.
- Paper is clearly written overall, with a few typos.

Principal concerns:
- How plausible are the “bad” center configurations in realistic settings; need a generative model and probability bound showing these occur with non-negligible probability (and ideally grow with k or d).
- Theorem 2’s use of random initialization: is sampling initial means from the mixture a realistic baseline when data are clearly clustered, since random init seems unlikely to place centers proportionally to cluster sizes?

Specific questions/clarifications requested:
- Lines 74–81: unclear role of the example and why it supports Srebro’s conjecture for k = 2.
- Lines 233–235: why is condition (1) necessary in addition to condition (2); are you suggesting centers initialized between two clusters may fail to move to the correct cluster?
- Lines 267–273: practical importance of showing EM (first-order) unlikely to converge to strict saddles—how does ending at a saddle compare to ending at a suboptimal local maximum?
