This paper investigates the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, and more generally iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE), when applied to learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians. The authors present a compelling set of results demonstrating that EM is not guaranteed to converge to a global maximum, even in the limit of infinite samples. Specifically, they show: (1) EM can get stuck in local optima even with well-separated clusters (k=3) and an infinite dataset; (2) random initialization of EM succeeds in finding a global optimum with a probability of at most exp(-Ω(k)); and (3) gradient-based EM generally does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that poor local maxima are the primary obstacle to optimization. 

The paper delivers a coherent narrative establishing the potential for EM to fail in finding globally optimal solutions. However, I found the results, while technically sound, to be largely unsurprising. The core intuition behind the observed non-convergence is well-illustrated with a three-component mixture example where two components are close together and the third is distant. The authors demonstrate the existence of a local maximum where a single center clusters near the closer pair, and two centers cluster around the isolated component. This behavior is readily anticipated, particularly when the separation between clusters significantly exceeds the square root of the dimensionality (>> √d). In such scenarios, cluster overlap is minimal, and learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians bears a strong resemblance to k-means clustering.

Indeed, analogous examples are known to induce poor performance in Lloyd’s algorithm for k-means. The extension of this bad example to *k* components, achieved through a recursive construction, is a noteworthy effort, but again, the outcome aligns with existing expectations. Furthermore, the observed failure of random initialization is comparable to the challenges faced by k-means clustering, exhibiting a similar failure probability. Current remedies for k-means, such as initializing with *k* log *k* centers or using distance-squared sampling, offer potential parallels for addressing EM’s initialization sensitivity.

In conclusion, while the paper provides a clear and consistent argument for the non-convergence of EM to global optima, the findings feel incremental rather than groundbreaking. A significant opportunity for strengthening the contribution would be a more thorough comparison with k-means clustering and Lloyd’s heuristic. The authors acknowledge the relationship between EM and Lloyd’s algorithm, noting their equivalence under conditions of sufficient cluster separation (as highlighted by Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu). However, a detailed discussion contrasting the algorithms – particularly in the context of the presented examples where separation *is* large – would provide valuable context and emphasize the unique aspects of EM’s convergence behavior. Specifically, exploring *when* EM diverges from Lloyd’s in performance, and *why*, would be highly beneficial. 

The connection to Kumar and Kannan’s convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic could be further elaborated. Showing where EM’s behavior deviates from those guarantees, and explaining the root cause, would significantly enrich the analysis.
