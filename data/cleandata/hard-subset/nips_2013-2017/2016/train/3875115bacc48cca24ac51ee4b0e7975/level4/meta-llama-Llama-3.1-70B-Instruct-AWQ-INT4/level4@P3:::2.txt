This paper addresses the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the set of mean parameters for a model of mixture of Gaussian variables. The number of Gaussian variables, the weights of the mixture, and the covariance matrices of the Gaussian variables are known and fixed. The paper is theoretical and proves three negative results for the EM algorithm. 

Firstly, the authors show that even for large sample sizes, local maxima can exist for the likelihood function, with likelihood values arbitrarily smaller than the maximal likelihood value. This result resolves an open question posed by Srebro (2007) and highlights the potential pitfalls of using local search algorithms for Gaussian mixture models. 

Secondly, the authors demonstrate that the EM algorithm, when randomly initiated, converges with high probability to a critical point with a likelihood value arbitrarily smaller than the maximal likelihood value. This result is concerning, as it suggests that the EM algorithm may not be reliable in practice, even when the model is well-specified and the sample size is large. 

Thirdly, the authors show that the same negative result holds for the gradient EM algorithm. This is particularly noteworthy, as the gradient EM algorithm is often used in practice due to its computational efficiency. 

Overall, I think that the question addressed by the paper is interesting and worthy of attention. The authors' use of a simplified mixture of Gaussian variable model, where the number of components is known, the weights are known and uniform, and the covariance matrices are equal to identity, is reasonable given the technical nature of the proofs. The obtained results are informative and can be useful in understanding the EM algorithm in practice. 

However, I do have some concerns regarding the proof of Theorem 1. While the authors provide a clear and concise proof, I believe that more details are needed to fully convince the reader of its validity. In particular, the part of the proof from line 288 to 306 could benefit from additional explanation, as the computation of limits of supremums of likelihood functions over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains is not entirely obvious. Furthermore, the authors' mention of the continuity of the likelihood function does not directly imply the types of convergences stated in the paper. I would have preferred a longer proof that addresses these concerns, even if it means shortening some of the discussions in the paper. 

Additionally, I found the part of the paper addressing the Gradient EM (from line 162 to line 172) to be more difficult to follow than the rest of the paper. Perhaps the authors could provide more explanation on how Equation (5) is obtained, as it is not immediately clear from the text. 

In terms of the broader implications of the paper, I believe that the authors' results highlight the need for careful initialization when using local search algorithms for Gaussian mixture models. The fact that the EM algorithm can converge to suboptimal solutions with high probability, even in well-specified models, is concerning and suggests that practitioners should be cautious when using this algorithm in practice. 

Overall, I think that the paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of the EM algorithm and its limitations. While there are some minor concerns regarding the proof of Theorem 1 and the clarity of the Gradient EM section, these do not detract from the overall impact of the paper.
