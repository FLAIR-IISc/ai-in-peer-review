Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 equally weighted, spherical components (known variance). It resolves an open conjecture of Srebro (2007) by constructing well-separated mixtures whose population log-likelihood possesses suboptimal local maxima with arbitrarily large likelihood gaps from the global optimum. The paper also shows that EM and a first-order (gradient) EM variant, when initialized by sampling initial means from the mixture, converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). Finally, it proves that first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, suggesting that failures arise from bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

These results complement prior theoretical analyses of EM’s local convergence under good initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and contrast with algorithmic alternatives that attain global guarantees under separation or moment conditions (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Hsu & Kakade, 2013; Moitra & Valiant, 2010). The negative results here emphasize the importance of initialization even in favorable population settings.

Strengths
- Resolves a well-known conjecture by exhibiting explicit, well-separated counterexamples at the population level.
- Sharp algorithmic implications: with common random-initialization heuristics, EM/gradient EM fail with exponentially high probability in M.
- Clarifies the role of landscape geometry: gradient EM avoids strict saddles almost surely, so poor performance is attributable to bad local maxima.
- The exposition is generally clear, with intuitive constructions and discussion of implications.

Weaknesses and questions (quality, clarity, originality, significance)
- Practical prevalence of “bad” true-center configurations: Theorem 1 proves existence via carefully constructed configurations (e.g., clusters of components at multiple scales). For practical relevance, it would help to quantify how likely such configurations are under natural generative models for the true centers. For example, if true centers are drawn i.i.d. from a distribution with bounded density or from a hierarchical clustering prior, can you bound below the probability (as M or d grow) of producing a configuration that induces bad local maxima with a constant gap? Absent such a measure-theoretic statement or empirical prevalence study, the adversarial nature of the construction leaves open how often such pathologies arise in practice.
- Initialization model: The random-initialization scheme (sample initial means from the mixture/data) is common but often outperformed in practice by k-means++ or simple clustering pre-steps. Your negative result is strongest when the data form well-separated top-level clusters: practitioners would typically detect those clusters and initialize one (or more) centers in each, reducing the chance of the particular “unbalanced” assignments that trap EM. Could you (i) justify the practical relevance of the chosen initialization in clearly clustered regimes, or (ii) provide theory or experiments for more informed initializations (e.g., k-means++, spectral/moment-based pilots)?
- Two-component example (L74–81): The visualization for M = 2 seems intended as intuition that, unlike M ≥ 3, the population objective does not exhibit bad local maxima in the settings considered. Since the M = 2 case remains open, please clarify the purpose: is it purely illustrative, or do you conjecture no spurious local maxima with supporting evidence (and if so, cite or expand)?
- Clarify requirements in Section 3.2 (L233–235): You list two conditions for “favorable” initialization. Requirement (2) (matching the number of initialized centers per cluster) is clear. Requirement (1) (“all initialized centers fall in the neighborhood of exactly one cluster”) is confusing and appears counter to the need to cover multiple top-level clusters. Did you mean “each initialized center falls in the neighborhood of some true cluster (i.e., no initial centers in between clusters)”? If a center is initialized between clusters, will responsibilities split in a way that prevents eventual migration to the correct cluster? A formal statement or lemma would help.
- Significance of avoiding strict saddles (L267–273): From a practical perspective, it would help to articulate why Theorem 4 matters: e.g., it isolates bad local maxima as the true source of difficulty for local search, aligning with recent theory that gradient methods escape strict saddles. A brief empirical check (histograms of Hessian eigenvalues at EM fixed points) would strengthen this message.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Practical prevalence of bad configurations (major): Provide either (a) a probabilistic statement that under a reasonable distribution over true centers (e.g., i.i.d. draws with separation), the chance of generating a configuration that produces an O(1) likelihood gap local maximum does not vanish as M increases; or (b) empirical evidence over random configurations quantifying the fraction that yield bad local maxima and the typical gap, as functions of M and d.
- Initialization (major): Discuss how your negative results relate to common initializations beyond random sampling from the mixture. Even a partial analysis or experiments for k-means++ would improve practical relevance.
- Scope (minor): Emphasize earlier that σ2 is known and equal weights are assumed; comment briefly on whether analogous constructions exist when variances or weights are unknown.
- Notation/definitions: Precisely define “well-separated”: e.g., a mixture is c√d-separated if min i≠j ||μi* − μj*||2 ≥ c√d for some universal c. Clarify “well-separated constant” (L218) and define the constants hidden in Ω(·) and e−Ω(M) when possible.

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves a notable open question with clean constructions and clear algorithmic implications.
- Provides a compelling conceptual message about EM’s failure modes at the population level.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical prevalence of the constructed pathologies is not quantified; adversarial nature may limit significance for typical use.
- Negative results hinge on a specific, relatively weak initialization scheme; no theory for more informed (and common) initializations.
- Some expository points need clarification (two-component example; requirements in Section 3.2; separation definition).

Overall evaluation
A strong theoretical contribution with clear implications for optimization landscapes and EM. Strengthen the discussion of practical prevalence and initialization, clarify the noted points, and the paper will make a valuable addition.
