This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters of a Gaussian Mixture Model. The analysis is conducted under the assumption that the number of components, mixture weights, and covariance matrices are known and fixed. The paper establishes three important negative results:
1.  Even for large sample sizes, the likelihood function can possess local maxima with values arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum.
2.  The standard EM algorithm, when randomly initialized, converges with high probability to a critical point with a likelihood value arbitrarily smaller than the optimal one.
3.  A similar negative result holds for the gradient EM algorithm.

**Strengths:**

The research question is both interesting and significant. Understanding the failure modes of the EM algorithm, a cornerstone of statistical learning, is of great importance to the community. The theoretical results presented are striking and provide valuable insights into the practical behavior of EM. They formally characterize a phenomenon that practitioners have likely observed: the algorithm's sensitivity to initialization and its tendency to converge to suboptimal solutions.

The authors focus on a simplified GMM setting where the number of components is known, the weights are uniform, and the covariance matrices are the identity. While these assumptions simplify the problem, they are justified by the technical depth of the proofs and do not detract from the value of the findings. The results, even in this simplified setting, are highly informative and serve as a crucial warning about the landscape of the GMM likelihood function.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

While the paper's contributions are potentially very valuable, there are several areas that require significant improvement, primarily concerning the rigor and clarity of the proofs and explanations.

1.  **Proof of Theorem 1:** My primary concern lies with the proof of Theorem 1 presented in the main text. The argument, particularly between lines 288 and 306, feels overly compressed and leaves several key steps inadequately justified. Specifically, the paper claims convergence for the supremum of the likelihood function over domains that are either unbounded or dependent on parameters that tend to infinity (e.g., gamma or R). While the convergence of the likelihood for fixed parameters is straightforward, establishing the convergence of the supremum (i.e., exchanging the limit and supremum operators) requires more rigorous justification than is provided. The simple appeal to the continuity of the likelihood function is insufficient to guarantee this type of convergence, especially over non-compact domains. To make the proof convincing, the authors should provide a more detailed and self-contained argument in the main paper, even if it requires shortening other discussion sections. This would significantly strengthen the paper's core contribution. (Note: This assessment is based on the main text, as I have not reviewed the supplementary material.)

2.  **Generalization to Higher Dimensions:** The paper is framed as addressing the general d-dimensional case, yet the provided proofs are for the one-dimensional case (d=1). This discrepancy should be addressed more directly. It would be beneficial for the authors to explicitly discuss the challenges of extending their analysis to d > 1. Are the techniques directly applicable, or do new fundamental difficulties arise? Clarifying the scope of the results and the potential for generalization would be a valuable addition.

3.  **Clarity of the Gradient EM Section:** The section on Gradient EM (lines 162-172) was less clear than the rest of the paper. In particular, a more detailed derivation or explanation of how Equation (5) is obtained would improve readability and help the reader follow the argument.

**Conclusion:**

This paper tackles an important theoretical problem and presents interesting and cautionary results about the EM algorithm. The findings are significant and deserve attention. However, the paper's main claims hinge on proofs that, as presented in the main text, lack sufficient detail to be fully convincing. My recommendation is contingent on a major revision that addresses the concerns raised above, especially by providing a more rigorous and detailed proof for Theorem 1 in the main body of the paper.
