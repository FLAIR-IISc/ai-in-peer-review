This paper investigates the structure of local maxima in the log-likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) with equal mixing weights and identity covariance matrices. The authors present three interconnected results. First, they demonstrate, via a counterexample (k=3, d=1), that the likelihood associated with certain local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than that of the global maximum. Second, they show that, under random initialization and a specific configuration of Gaussian means, the probability of escaping a “bad” local maximum can be exponentially small in *k*. Finally, they prove that gradient Expectation-Maximization (EM) almost surely does not converge to a saddle point of the log-likelihood.

**Summary:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis of the local optima landscape of GMMs under specific constraints. The three results collectively illuminate the factors influencing the quality of local maxima and the behavior of gradient EM.

**Technical Quality:** I focused my review on the proof of Theorem 1 in the main paper and found it to be sound. The analysis underlying Theorem 1 suggests a reasonable approach for proving Theorem 2, although I did not verify the details presented in the Appendix. The theorems are clearly stated and well-interpreted following their presentations.

**Novelty & Originality:** The paper establishes a connection between the configuration of Gaussian means and the structure of local maxima in GMMs. While perhaps intuitive, this link is presented with substantial originality. The core idea behind Theorem 2 – that the hierarchical grouping structure of the true means dictates the characteristics of local maxima for *k* > 3 – is particularly clever and insightful. This builds directly and effectively upon the foundation laid by Theorem 1.

**Potential Impact & Usefulness:** The results have the potential to impact both theoretical understanding and practical applications. Theoretically, this work could motivate further research into the local optima structure of related clustering problems, such as k-means. An interesting extension would be to investigate whether the result presented in Lemma 7 generalizes to dimensions *d* > 1. Practically, the findings underscore the importance of initializing with sufficient seed points within each underlying cluster. This insight could prove valuable in the design of more robust and effective clustering algorithms. 

**Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presents its results. The authors effectively explain the intuition behind their analysis, thoroughly introducing the problem setup and providing insightful interpretations of their findings. The presentation consistently supports understanding and appreciation of the technical contributions.
