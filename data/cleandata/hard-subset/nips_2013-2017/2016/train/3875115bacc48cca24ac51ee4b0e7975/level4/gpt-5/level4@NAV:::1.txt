The paper investigates the convergence behavior of the EM algorithm (and related iterative MLE heuristics) for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, and establishes the following non-convergence-to-global-optima results: 1. Even with three well-separated clusters and access to infinite data, methods like EM can become trapped in suboptimal local maxima. 2. For mixtures of k Gaussians, EM with random initialization succeeds with probability at most exp(-Ω(k)). 3. Gradient EM generically avoids strict saddle points; thus, poor local maxima are usually the culprit. The work offers a cohesive set of results demonstrating that EM may fail to reach global maxima (even in the infinite-sample regime). However, the findings are not particularly unexpected, and the proof strategies largely follow anticipated approaches. The primary hard instance involves a 3-component mixture where two components are relatively close, while the third is far from both. For the log-likelihood objective, they identify a local optimum with one center near the pair of close components and two centers assigned near the distant cluster. This behavior is quite expected, especially when the separations are much larger than sqrt(d). Under such separation, the clusters barely overlap, and learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians closely resembles k-means; analogous constructions yield bad cases for Lloyd’s algorithm. The example is extended to k components via a neat recursive construction—this takes some work but still aligns with intuition. Likewise, random initialization fails for reasons paralleling those in k-means, with a comparable failure probability (which, for k-means, motivates choosing k log k initial centers or using distance-squared sampling). To sum up, while the results give a consistent narrative about non-convergence to global optima, they largely align with expected phenomena. Comments: 1. Kumar–Kannan provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic for k-means (and thus for mixtures of Gaussians under sufficient separation). While EM differs from Lloyd’s when the Gaussians are not well separated (as in Balakrishnan–Wainwright–Yu), they are essentially similar under large separation (which applies to the hard instances here). A direct comparison with k-means (and Lloyd’s heuristic) highlighting these contrasts would be very valuable.
