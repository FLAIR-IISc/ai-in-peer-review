This paper investigates the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically focusing on isotropic Gaussian components with known scale and a well-specified model. The authors demonstrate that even with infinite data, these local modes persist and can be arbitrarily distant from the global optimum in terms of log-likelihood. Their analysis concentrates on scenarios where mixture centers are well-spaced. They show that, under these configurations, the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm will almost certainly fail to find the global optimum as the number of components increases, given random initialization. The paper is generally well-written, though a few minor typos are present.

My primary concerns relate to the practical implications of these findings. Theorem 1 establishes the existence of local modes for a specific configuration of centers. However, the practical relevance hinges on the likelihood of encountering such a configuration in real-world data. I would be more persuaded if the authors incorporated assumptions about the distribution of the true centers and subsequently demonstrated that "bad" configurations—those prone to trapping EM in local optima—become more probable as the number of components (k) or the dimensionality of the problem increases. The authors state that Theorem 2 addresses this concern (lines 210-218), but I find this unconvincing. A clearer statement quantifying the probability of encountering these detrimental configurations under reasonable assumptions would significantly strengthen the paper. It’s important to emphasize that this concerns the probability of the *true* center configurations, not simply "bad" initializations.

My second concern arises with Theorem 2 and its reliance on random initialization. The authors consider initializing component centers by randomly sampling from the data mixture. However, this initialization strategy appears questionable in the presence of clear clustering. For example, consider a dataset generated from three Gaussians, where two are closely clustered and one is isolated. If each Gaussian contributes an equal number of data points (N), then 2N points will be concentrated near the first cluster and N near the third. It seems counterintuitive to initialize EM with 2 centers positioned in the sparse region and only 1 center in the dense region, especially when the clusters are well-separated. The practical relevance of random initialization diminishes when the underlying data exhibits strong clustering. 

I also have some questions regarding specific sections of the paper. The example on lines 74-81 is unclear in its purpose. It doesn’t effectively support the assertion regarding Sresbro’s conjecture for k=2, and its inclusion feels unmotivated. On lines 233-235, I understand the necessity of requirement (2), but the justification for requirement (1) is unclear. Does it imply that initializing a center between two clusters doesn't guarantee convergence to the nearest correct cluster? Finally, lines 267-273 prove EM is unlikely to converge to a saddlepoint. I'm unclear why this is practically important; is convergence to a saddlepoint more or less problematic than convergence to a local optimum?
