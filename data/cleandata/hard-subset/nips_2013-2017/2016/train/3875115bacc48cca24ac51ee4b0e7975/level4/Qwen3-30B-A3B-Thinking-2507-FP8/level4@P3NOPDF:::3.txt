

The paper investigates the existence of spurious local modes in the log-likelihood of isotropic Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) under well-specified conditions with known scale. It demonstrates that even with infinite data, such modes exist and can be arbitrarily poor relative to the global optimum, particularly when component centers form well-separated clusters. The authors further show that random initialization causes the EM algorithm to miss the global optimum almost surely as the number of components increases. The writing is clear and precise, though minor grammatical errors require correction (noted below).

Two primary concerns regarding practical relevance warrant clarification. First, Theorem 1 establishes the existence of poor local modes under specific, constructed center configurations. However, the argument lacks a quantitative assessment of how likely such configurations are to arise in practice. The authors claim Theorem 2 addresses this (L210–218), but it does not. To strengthen the practical impact, the paper should explicitly model the distribution of true centers (e.g., as i.i.d. draws from a distribution) and demonstrate that "bad" configurations become increasingly probable as the number of components \(k\) or dimension \(d\) grows. Without this, the theoretical result remains detached from real-world scenarios where center configurations are not adversarially designed.

Second, Theorem 2 analyzes EM’s failure under random initialization when centers are configured as described. However, the practicality of this initialization is questionable for datasets with clear cluster structure. Consider a GMM with three components: two closely spaced Gaussians (generating \(2N\) points) and one well-separated Gaussian (generating \(N\) points). Randomly initializing two centers near the dense cluster and one near the sparse cluster seems implausible when cluster separation is evident. The paper should address whether such initialization strategies are realistic for typical clustered data, or if alternative initialization methods (e.g., k-means++) would mitigate this issue. The example in L74–81 fails to clarify the relevance of the Sresbro conjecture for \(k=2\), which appears tangential to the core argument.

The paper’s technical contribution is sound, but its practical significance hinges on addressing the likelihood of the assumed center configurations. The current analysis focuses on adversarial cases without justifying their prevalence. For instance, the claim that random initialization "misses the global optimum almost surely" (L220) requires justification under realistic data-generating processes. If "bad" configurations are rare in practice (e.g., due to natural clustering), the result may not impact typical EM applications. The authors should either: (i) quantify the probability of such configurations under plausible assumptions about center distributions, or (ii) explicitly acknowledge this limitation and discuss scenarios where it might apply (e.g., high-dimensional data with adversarial center placement).

Regarding Theorem 2 (L233–235), requirement (1) is unclear. The text states that if a center is initialized between two clusters, it cannot be guaranteed to move toward the correct cluster. This seems to describe a known EM limitation (e.g., centroid bias), but its necessity for the theorem’s proof is not justified. Clarifying why both requirements (1) and (2) are needed would strengthen the argument.

The result on saddle points (L267–273) is technically interesting but lacks practical motivation. Converging to a saddle point versus a local optimum—while both suboptimal—may have different implications for model quality or training time. The paper should briefly discuss why avoiding saddle points is practically relevant (e.g., saddle points often require more iterations to escape).

In summary, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding EM’s limitations in GMMs. However, its practical relevance is undermined by insufficient justification of the assumed data configurations and the realism of the random initialization scenario. Addressing these points would significantly enhance the paper’s impact. The minor grammatical and notational issues should be resolved in revision. The work is technically sound and well-written, but the practical implications require stronger grounding.
