This paper studies the structure of local maxima of the log-likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (with equal mixing weights and identity as covariance). It presents three related results regarding this vein: (1) a counter-example showing that there exist local maxima (when k = 3 and d=1), whose likelihood is arbitrarily worse than the global maximum, thus resolving an open question of Srebro [2007]; (2) a demonstration that with random initialization, the probability of not converging to a bad local maximum could be exponentially small in k when the means of Gaussians are configured in a special way; and (3) a proof that gradient EM does not converge to a saddle point of the log-likelihood almost surely.

The paper's main contribution lies in its rigorous analysis of the local maxima of the log-likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models. The authors' observation that the mean configuration of GMM is tied to the local maxima of its log-likelihood is both intuitive and original. The key idea of Theorem 2, which builds upon Theorem 1, that the hierarchical grouping structure of the true mean determines the local maxima for k > 3, is also very clever.

The technical quality of the paper is sound, with the proofs being well-organized and easy to follow. The authors have done a good job in presenting their results and explaining the intuition behind their analysis. The paper clearly introduces the problem setup and provides intuition and interpretation of all their analysis.

In terms of novelty and originality, the paper's results are significant, as they provide new insights into the structure of local maxima of the log-likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models. The authors' use of a hierarchical grouping structure to analyze the local maxima is a novel approach that could have implications for other clustering tasks, such as k-means problems.

The potential impact and usefulness of the paper's results are substantial. The authors' findings could inspire the study of the local optima structure of related clustering tasks and provide insights into designing new algorithms. In practice, the results highlight the importance of seeding enough points in each true cluster and could lead to the development of more effective initialization schemes for local search methods.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors have done an excellent job in presenting their results and explaining the intuition behind their analysis. The paper's contributions are significant, and the results have the potential to make a substantial impact on both theory and practice.
