The paper presents a resolution to the open question posed by Srebro in his 2007 work, “Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?”. The authors answer this question in the negative by constructing a general class of counterexamples. They begin by outlining standard methods for maximizing the likelihood in Gaussian Mixture Models, including the Expectation-Maximization (EM) and gradient EM algorithms, and discussing a common initialization strategy. The authors then state their main theorems, which provide the counterexample to Srebro’s conjecture and further demonstrate that, even with a standard random initialization scheme, the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to suboptimal maxima with exponentially high probability. The authors offer intuition for their proofs through a simplified case (M=3, d=1). The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, effectively balancing the space constraints with the need to convey intuition for the theoretical results. The question addressed by the authors is of significant importance for advancing our understanding of the challenges inherent in non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic limit for simple distributions. 

While I did not meticulously examine the supplementary material, it would be valuable to see estimates of the constants involved in the probability term presented. This is particularly relevant because the practical significance of the result depends on the size of these constants; for example, understanding at what value of *M* the convergence to suboptimal solutions becomes likely. If precise estimates are difficult to obtain, supporting simulations demonstrating this phenomenon would be beneficial. However, this point does not diminish the novelty or impact of the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong technical contribution:** The paper provides a definitive answer to an important open question in the field, resolving a long-standing conjecture.
*   **Clear and well-written:** The paper is remarkably clear and accessible, even for readers not intimately familiar with the specific details of Gaussian mixture models.
*   **Significant implications:** The results have broad implications for the theoretical understanding of non-convex optimization and the practical design of algorithms for mixture estimation.
*   **Rigorous analysis:** The theoretical results are supported by rigorous proofs, and the authors provide insightful intuition through a simplified example.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of constant estimates:** The absence of estimates for the constants in the probability bounds limits the immediate practical implications of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of Gaussian mixture models and non-convex optimization. The clarity of exposition and the importance of the results strongly support acceptance. The minor concern regarding the estimation of constants does not outweigh the overall strengths of the work. I recommend acceptance.
