

This paper investigates the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating mean parameters in a Gaussian mixture model with known, fixed components: the number of mixtures, component weights (uniformly distributed), and covariance matrices (identity). The authors establish three theoretical negative results: (1) the likelihood function can exhibit local maxima with values arbitrarily below the global maximum, even for large samples; (2) random initialization of EM converges with high probability to critical points with likelihoods arbitrarily below the global maximum; and (3) analogous results hold for the gradient EM algorithm. The question addressed is highly relevant for understanding EM's practical behavior, and the results are valuable despite the model's simplifications. The restriction to known weights, uniform mixing, identity covariances, and dimension d=1 is reasonable given the technical depth of the proofs and the informative nature of the conclusions.  

A notable gap is the paper's claim of generality for arbitrary dimension d, while the proofs explicitly address only d=1. The authors should clarify whether the results extend to d>1 and, if not, briefly discuss the additional challenges involved. This would strengthen the paper's scope and applicability.  

My primary concern pertains to the proof of Theorem 1 (not verified in the supplementary material). The argument is currently too concise, particularly in the critical segment from lines 288–306. The authors derive limits of likelihood function suprema over parameter domains as γ or R diverges. While the limit computations for fixed parameters appear valid, extending these to suprema over unbounded domains requires rigorous justification. Specifically, the continuity of the likelihood function alone does not suffice to justify the stated convergence properties—e.g., the behavior of suprema over parameter sets that depend on diverging parameters (γ, R) needs explicit clarification. A more detailed treatment of these steps would significantly enhance the proof's persuasiveness.  

Additionally, the section on Gradient EM (lines 162–172) is less accessible than the rest of the paper. The derivation of Equation (5) lacks sufficient explanation, making it difficult to follow the connection between the gradient step and the subsequent analysis. Providing a brief intermediate derivation or intuitive justification for Equation (5) would improve clarity and coherence in this section.  

Overall, the paper presents compelling theoretical insights into EM's limitations, and the simplifications are justified by the proof complexity. Addressing the dimensionality gap and strengthening Theorem 1's proof would substantially elevate the work. The results are important for practitioners seeking to understand EM's potential pitfalls, and the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical foundations of the algorithm.
