

The paper provides a definitive negative answer to Srebro's 2007 open question regarding the existence of local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors construct a general class of counterexamples that resolve this long-standing theoretical question. They begin by systematically outlining the likelihood maximization frameworks for GMMs, including the EM algorithm and its gradient variant, and discuss standard initialization schemes. The core contribution lies in the authors' main theorems, which rigorously establish the counterexamples. Crucially, they further demonstrate that even with standard random initialization, both EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to suboptimal maxima with exponentially high probability as the sample size grows. To build intuition, the authors provide a detailed proof for the simple case of $k=3$ components in one dimension ($d=1$), effectively bridging theoretical rigor with accessibility.

The exposition is exceptionally clear and well-structured, striking an admirable balance between conciseness and sufficient detail to convey the proof's essence. The significance of the question addressed is substantial: it advances fundamental understanding of non-convex optimization challenges in the asymptotic regime, particularly for simple distributions where theoretical guarantees are often assumed to hold. This work has clear implications for the broader field of statistical learning theory and optimization.

The paper's primary strength is its theoretical contribution, which is both novel and impactful. The counterexamples effectively dismantle the assumption that local maxima might not exist in the infinite-sample limit for GMMs, a point that has implications for algorithm design and theoretical analysis. The authors’ analysis of algorithmic behavior under standard initialization provides critical practical insight, showing that poor convergence is not merely a pathological artifact but a likely outcome.

A minor suggestion for improvement concerns the probability bounds in the main theorems. While the exponential decay rate is established, the paper does not provide concrete estimates for the constants involved. Explicit bounds would greatly enhance the paper’s utility by clarifying the "phase transition" point—i.e., for which sample sizes the probability of converging to a suboptimal maximum becomes non-negligible. Without examining the supplementary material, it is unclear whether such estimates are present there. If the constants are complex or lack intuitive interpretation, the authors might consider adding brief empirical validation (e.g., small-scale simulations demonstrating the convergence behavior) to ground the asymptotic claims in observable phenomena. However, this is a refinement rather than a substantive critique: the core theoretical contribution remains robust and compelling regardless.

The paper does not require significant revisions to address this point, as the main theorems and their implications are already clearly presented and rigorously proven. The work stands as a significant advance in understanding GMM likelihood landscapes and the limitations of standard optimization procedures. Its clarity, precision, and theoretical depth make it a strong candidate for NIPS. The minor suggestion regarding constant estimates is offered constructively, but it does not detract from the paper’s substantial contribution or novelty. This is a well-executed piece of theoretical work that answers a fundamental question with important ramifications.
