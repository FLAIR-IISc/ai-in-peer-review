

This paper provides a significant contribution by resolving an open question posed by Srebro in his 2007 work—"Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?"—by demonstrating that such maxima do not exist, using a carefully constructed class of counterexamples. The authors begin by clarifying key optimization schemes for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), including the EM algorithm and gradient-based EM, and discuss common initialization practices. They then present their core theorems, which establish the counterexample to Srebro's question. Crucially, they further demonstrate that even with standard random initialization, both EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to suboptimal maxima with exponentially high probability. To aid intuition, the authors prove a simplified case (k=3 components, d=1 dimension) before generalizing their results.  

The paper is exceptionally well-written, striking an excellent balance between theoretical rigor and accessible exposition. It effectively manages space constraints while ensuring readers grasp the significance of the theoretical findings. The question addressed is of profound importance for understanding non-convex optimization challenges in the asymptotic regime, particularly for simple distributions. This work substantially advances the theoretical foundation for GMM analysis and optimization.  

While the main contribution is compelling, a minor suggestion for enhancement is offered. The authors provide probabilistic bounds with unspecified constants. Quantifying these constants would be valuable, as it would clarify the practical relevance of the exponential convergence to suboptimal solutions. Specifically, determining where the phase transition occurs—i.e., for which parameter regimes this phenomenon becomes observable—would strengthen the interpretation. Given that k is rarely excessively large in practice, such estimates would help practitioners gauge when this behavior might manifest. If the constants prove intractable, supporting simulations demonstrating the observed convergence to bad solutions in the asymptotic limit would be a useful addition. However, this note does not diminish the paper's novelty or impact. The core theoretical results are robust, well-motivated, and represent a clear advancement in the field. The work is a strong candidate for acceptance.
