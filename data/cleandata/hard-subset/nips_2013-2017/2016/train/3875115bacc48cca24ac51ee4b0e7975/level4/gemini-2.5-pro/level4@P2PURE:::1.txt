This paper studies the convergence of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians and presents several results demonstrating its failure to converge to the global maximum likelihood solution. The key findings are: (1) even for a 3-component mixture with well-separated centers and infinite samples, EM can get trapped in a local optimum; (2) for a k-component mixture, random initialization succeeds with a probability of at most exp(-Ω(k)); and (3) convergence issues typically stem from poor local maxima, as Gradient EM will generically avoid strict saddle points.

While the paper presents a coherent set of results, the findings and techniques are not particularly surprising. The primary counterexample involves a 3-component mixture where two components are close to each other and a third is far away. The authors show that the log-likelihood objective has a local maximum where one learned center is placed between the two close true components, while two learned centers are placed near the single distant true component. This behavior is expected, particularly under the paper's assumption of large separation (centers separated by >> sqrt(d)). In this regime, the clusters have minimal overlap, and the problem of learning spherical Gaussian mixtures closely resembles k-means clustering. Indeed, similar counterexamples are well-known for Lloyd's algorithm in the k-means setting.

The paper's extension of this example to k components, while relying on an elegant recursive construction, yields a similarly anticipated result. Likewise, the analysis showing that random initialization fails with probability exp(-Ω(k)) mirrors well-known results for k-means, where this very issue has motivated the development of improved initialization schemes (e.g., k-means++ or distance-squared sampling). In conclusion, although the paper tells a consistent story about EM's non-convergence to global optima, the arguments follow somewhat expected lines.

Comments:
1. The connection to k-means warrants a more explicit discussion. In the large separation regime used for this paper's counterexamples, EM for spherical Gaussians behaves almost identically to Lloyd's algorithm for k-means. While the two algorithms differ when components are not well-separated (as noted in Balakrishnan et al.), their similarity in this setting is crucial. Given that works like Kumar & Kannan provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd's algorithm (and by extension, for Gaussian mixtures under sufficient separation), a detailed comparison that contrasts the results of this paper with the existing k-means literature would be very valuable.
