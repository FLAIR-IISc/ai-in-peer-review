This paper addresses the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the set of mean parameters in a model of mixture of Gaussian variables, where the number of Gaussian variables, mixture weights, and covariance matrices are known and fixed. The paper is theoretical and presents three negative results for the EM algorithm. Firstly, it shows that even for large sample sizes, local maxima can exist in the likelihood function with likelihood values arbitrarily smaller than the maximal likelihood value. Secondly, the EM algorithm, when randomly initiated, converges with high probability to a critical point with a likelihood value arbitrarily smaller than the maximal likelihood value. Lastly, the same negative result holds for the gradient EM algorithm.

I find the question addressed by the paper to be interesting and worthy of attention. To obtain rigorous proofs, the paper focuses on a simplified mixture of Gaussian variable model, where the number of components is known, the weights are known and uniform, and the covariance matrices are equal to the identity matrix. I do not consider these simplifications to be problematic, given the technical nature of the proofs and the informative results obtained. These results are very interesting and can be useful for understanding the EM algorithm in practice.

It is worth noting that the paper is written in a general form for dimension d, while the proofs address the case d=1. The authors may want to consider discussing whether similar proofs are possible for arbitrary dimension d and, if not, briefly mentioning the additional difficulties encountered in higher dimensions.

My primary concern with the paper is the proof of Theorem 1. I found the proof to be too concise and would have preferred a more detailed explanation, even if it meant shortening some of the discussions in the paper. Specifically, the part of the proof from line 288 to 306 requires more details. The authors provide limits of supremums of likelihood functions over certain domains as gamma or R approaches infinity. While I do not have issues with computing the limits of likelihood functions evaluated at fixed parameters, obtaining the limit of a maximum is not entirely obvious when the domain is unbounded or depends on the parameters approaching infinity. Furthermore, the authors mention the continuity of the likelihood function, but this does not directly imply the types of convergences stated in the paper. I believe that a longer proof with more details is necessary to convince the reader of the validity of the proof of Theorem 1.

Additionally, I have a minor concern with the section on the Gradient EM algorithm, from line 162 to line 172. I found this part more challenging to follow than the rest of the paper. The authors may want to provide more explanation on how Equation (5) is derived to improve clarity.

Overall, the paper presents interesting and useful results that can contribute to a better understanding of the EM algorithm in practice. Addressing the concerns mentioned above can further strengthen the paper and improve its overall quality.
