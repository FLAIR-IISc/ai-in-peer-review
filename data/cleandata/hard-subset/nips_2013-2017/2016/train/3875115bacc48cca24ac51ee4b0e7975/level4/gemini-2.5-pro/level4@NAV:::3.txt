This paper investigates the existence of spurious local optima in the log-likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models. The analysis is focused on a simplified yet fundamental setting: a well-specified mixture of isotropic Gaussian components with known variance. The authors demonstrate that spurious local modes can exist even in the population log-likelihood (i.e., with infinite data). Furthermore, they show these modes can be arbitrarily suboptimal when measured by the gap in log-likelihood to the global optimum. The paper's theoretical results hinge on specific configurations of the true mixture centers, particularly when they form well-separated clusters. For these configurations, the authors prove that a random initialization strategy for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm will, with high probability, fail to find the global optimum as the number of components grows. Overall, the paper is clearly written, though it contains some minor typographical errors.

I have two primary concerns regarding the practical relevance of the findings:

1.  Theorem 1 demonstrates the existence of a local mode in the population log-likelihood for a particular configuration of the true component centers. My main question is: how probable are such configurations in practice? The paper's practical implications would be significantly strengthened if the authors could provide an argument for why these configurations are not merely pathological cases. For instance, by assuming a prior distribution over the true centers, could the authors show that the probability of encountering such "bad" configurations increases with the number of components (k) or the data dimensionality (d)? While the text (L210-218) suggests Theorem 2 addresses this point, its focus seems to be on initialization rather than the properties of the true centers. A clear statement on the prevalence of these "bad" ground-truth configurations, under reasonable assumptions, would be beneficial.

2.  My second concern pertains to the initialization strategy analyzed in Theorem 2. The authors analyze an initialization scheme where centers are chosen by sampling from the mixture distribution itself. With this approach, and for specific center configurations, EM frequently fails to converge globally. However, consider a scenario with three ground-truth components, where two are clustered closely together and the third is distant. If each component contributes N data points, the dense region will contain 2N points while the sparse region contains N. The analyzed initialization scheme could plausibly place one initial center in the dense region and two in the sparse region. This seems counter-intuitive, as a practitioner would likely avoid placing more initial centers in a sparse region than a dense one, especially when the data exhibits clear visual clustering. This raises the question of whether this specific type of random initialization is a relevant model for practical use cases.
