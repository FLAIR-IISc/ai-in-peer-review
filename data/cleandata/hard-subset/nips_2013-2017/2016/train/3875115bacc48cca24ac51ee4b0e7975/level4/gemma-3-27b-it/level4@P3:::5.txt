This paper addresses a fundamental open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors provide a definitive negative answer, constructing a well-separated mixture of three Gaussians exhibiting such suboptimal local maxima. Furthermore, they demonstrate that the commonly used Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, and its first-order variant, converge to these bad critical points with exponentially high probability when initialized randomly. This work has significant implications for understanding the challenges of non-convex optimization in statistical estimation, even in seemingly well-behaved asymptotic settings.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly structured. It provides a thorough background on GMMs, the EM algorithm, and relevant prior work, making it accessible to a broad audience. The authors successfully balance the need for mathematical rigor with intuitive explanations, aiding comprehension of the core ideas and proofs. The connection to Srebro’s (2007) conjecture is clearly established, and the significance of resolving this open question is well-motivated. The paper builds on a substantial body of work in mixture models, citing key contributions from Teicher (1963), Chen (1995), Genovese & Wasserman (2000), Ghosal & Van Der Vaart (2001), Dasgupta & Schulman (2007), and Balakrishnan et al. (2015), among others.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper provides a novel and significant result resolving a longstanding open problem in the field. The construction of a counterexample to Srebro’s conjecture is a substantial contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The proofs appear sound and are presented with a reasonable level of detail. The theoretical analysis is a strong point of the paper.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and explained, making it easy to follow the complex arguments.
*   **Significance:** The results have broad implications for the design and analysis of algorithms for GMM estimation, highlighting the importance of careful initialization strategies.
*   **Algorithmic Consequences:** Demonstrating the high probability of converging to bad local optima with random initialization is a strong algorithmic finding.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Constant Estimates:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper lacks concrete estimates for the constants involved in the probability bounds (e.g., the constant *c* in Theorem 2). Providing some insight into the magnitude of these constants, or at least discussing their dependence on the dimensionality *d* and number of components *M*, would strengthen the practical implications of the results. Supporting simulations demonstrating the observed behavior for relevant parameter regimes could compensate for the lack of analytical estimates.
*   **Practical Implications:** While the paper thoroughly examines the theoretical limitations of random initialization, a more in-depth discussion of practical initialization strategies that might mitigate these issues would be beneficial. While the paper mentions the importance of alternative methods, such as those based on the method of moments (Moitra & Valiant, 2010; Hsu & Kakade, 2013), further elaboration would be helpful.
*   **First-Order EM & Saddle Points:** Although the authors establish the non-convergence to strict saddle points (Theorem 4), the connection between this result and the observed convergence to bad local maxima could be more explicitly highlighted. While the authors state this relationship, reinforcing it with a more detailed discussion would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper resolves a significant open problem with a rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   The results have important implications for both the theoretical understanding and practical application of GMM estimation.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.
*   The work is highly relevant to the NIPS audience, given the conference’s focus on machine learning and optimization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of concrete estimates for the constants in the probability bounds limits the immediate practical impact of the results.
*   A more in-depth discussion of potential mitigation strategies for the observed issues would strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of Gaussian mixture model estimation. The theoretical results are compelling, and the presentation is excellent. While the lack of constant estimates and a more detailed discussion of practical strategies are minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2016.
