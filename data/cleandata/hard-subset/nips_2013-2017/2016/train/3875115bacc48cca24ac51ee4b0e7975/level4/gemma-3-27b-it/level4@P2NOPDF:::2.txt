This paper investigates the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm in the context of estimating the means of a Gaussian mixture model. Importantly, the number of Gaussian components, mixture weights, and covariance matrices are assumed known and fixed, allowing the authors to focus specifically on the behavior of the EM algorithm during mean parameter estimation. The work is largely theoretical, presenting three significant negative results: first, the likelihood function can possess local maxima even with large sample sizes, and the likelihood values at these local maxima can be arbitrarily far from the global maximum; second, the EM algorithm, when initialized randomly, converges with high probability to a critical point yielding a suboptimal likelihood; and third, the gradient EM algorithm exhibits the same problematic behavior. 

I find the research question addressed by this paper to be both interesting and valuable. The pursuit of rigorous theoretical understanding of EM’s limitations is crucial. To achieve these results, the authors necessarily restrict their analysis to a simplified Gaussian mixture model – specifically, a known number of components, uniform mixture weights, and identity covariance matrices. While these simplifications are present, I believe they are justified. The proofs appear inherently technical, and the insights gained from this constrained model are still highly informative regarding EM’s broader behavior. These findings could prove useful in gaining a more nuanced understanding of EM’s practical performance.

One point requires clarification. The paper develops its results for the general dimensionality *d*, yet the presented proofs are solely for the case *d* = 1. It would be beneficial if the authors could comment on the feasibility of extending the proofs to arbitrary dimensions. If such an extension is challenging, a brief discussion of the additional difficulties encountered in higher dimensions would be appreciated.

My primary concern lies with the proof of Theorem 1. While I did not examine the supplementary material in detail, I found the presented proof to be somewhat concise. A more expansive explanation, perhaps at the expense of some of the preceding discussion, would enhance clarity. Specifically, the section spanning lines 288 to 306 warrants further elaboration. The authors compute limits of suprema of likelihood functions, and while I find the computation of limits of likelihood functions evaluated at fixed parameters plausible (and believe the three equations on line 288 are likely correct), establishing the limit of a *maximum* over an unbounded domain, or a domain dependent on parameters approaching infinity, is not immediately obvious. Furthermore, while the authors note the continuity of the likelihood function in several places, this alone does not guarantee the stated types of convergence. A more detailed explanation would significantly strengthen the reader’s confidence in the proof’s validity.

Finally, I encountered some difficulty following the discussion of the Gradient EM algorithm (lines 162-172). I suggest that the authors provide additional context or explanation regarding the derivation of Equation (5) to improve its accessibility.
