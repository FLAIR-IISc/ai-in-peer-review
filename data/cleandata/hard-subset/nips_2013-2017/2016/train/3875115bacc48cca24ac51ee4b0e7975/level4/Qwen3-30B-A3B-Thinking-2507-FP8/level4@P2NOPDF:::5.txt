

This paper provides a definitive answer to Srebro's (2007) open question regarding the existence of local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), conclusively demonstrating that none exist. The authors construct a general class of counterexamples to resolve this long-standing theoretical question, making a significant contribution to the understanding of non-convex optimization in statistical learning.  

The paper begins with a clear exposition of key optimization frameworks for GMMs, including the EM algorithm and its gradient-based variant. The authors systematically address initialization strategies, noting that common practices (e.g., random initialization) are insufficient to avoid suboptimal solutions. Their primary theorems establish the counterexample by explicitly constructing GMM configurations where the likelihood function lacks local maxima in the infinite-sample limit. Crucially, they prove that even with standard random initialization, both EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to non-optimal maxima with exponentially high probability as the sample size grows. This result challenges conventional assumptions about the behavior of these algorithms in asymptotic settings.  

The authors further strengthen their argument by providing an accessible proof for a simplified case (k=3 components, d=1 dimension), offering intuitive insight into the underlying mechanism. The paper is exceptionally well-written, balancing technical precision with clear exposition. The structure effectively prioritizes conceptual clarity without sacrificing mathematical rigor, making the theoretical contributions accessible to a broad audience. The choice to focus on the core counterexample while deferring technical details to supplementary material is judicious, preserving the paper’s readability.  

The significance of this work extends beyond GMMs to fundamental questions in non-convex optimization. By resolving the asymptotic behavior of likelihood maximization for a canonical model, the paper illuminates broader challenges in statistical learning, such as the risk of algorithmic convergence to suboptimal solutions despite seemingly benign initialization. This has direct implications for the design and analysis of optimization methods in high-dimensional settings.  

While the main results are compelling, the paper would benefit from more precise bounds on the constants in the probability estimates for convergence to suboptimal maxima. Explicit numerical values for these constants would clarify the phase transition point—i.e., for which parameter regimes the probability of suboptimal convergence becomes non-negligible. Without such bounds, the practical relevance of the asymptotic result remains somewhat abstract, particularly since the authors note that "k is never too large" in typical applications. Supporting simulations demonstrating this phase transition would be valuable, as they would empirically validate the theoretical prediction and provide guidance for practitioners. However, this limitation is minor: the core contribution stands independently as a rigorous resolution of Srebro’s question.  

The paper’s novelty is substantial, as it closes a theoretical gap that has persisted since 2007. Prior work often assumed or implicitly relied on the existence of local maxima, but this analysis demonstrates their absence under general conditions. The authors’ approach—constructing explicit counterexamples rather than relying on probabilistic arguments—adds robustness to their conclusion. Their proof techniques, while technical, are presented with sufficient intuition to be convincing.  

In summary, this work is a major advance in theoretical statistics and machine learning. It not only answers a specific open question but also provides a framework for analyzing optimization landscapes in other non-convex settings. The clarity of presentation, combined with the depth of the theoretical contribution, makes it a strong candidate for publication. The minor suggestion regarding constant estimation does not detract from the paper’s impact but rather offers a pathway for future refinement. This is a well-executed, important contribution that will likely influence both theoretical and applied research in the field.
