This paper studies the expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the component means in a Gaussian mixture model, where the number of components, the mixture weights, and the covariance matrices are known and fixed. The paper is theoretical and establishes three negative results for EM:
- Even with large sample sizes, the likelihood can have local maxima whose values are arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum.
- With random initialization, EM converges with high probability to a critical point whose likelihood is arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum.
- The same negative behavior holds for the gradient EM algorithm.

I find the question addressed by the paper interesting and worthy of attention. To obtain rigorous proofs, the paper focuses on a simplified Gaussian mixture model in which the number of components is known, the weights are known and uniform, and the covariances are identity. I do not view these simplifications as problematic, given the technical nature of the proofs and the informativeness of the results. In my opinion, the results are very interesting and can help practitioners better understand EM in practice.

One point that warrants clarification is the role of the ambient dimension d. The paper frames the results as addressing general dimension d, but the proofs are carried out for d = 1. It would be helpful if the authors could indicate whether similar proofs are possible for arbitrary d and, if not, briefly explain the additional difficulties that arise for d > 1.

My main concern is the proof of Theorem 1 (I did not consult the supplementary proofs). The proof in the main text feels too compressed; I would prefer a more detailed argument, even at the expense of shortening some of the surrounding discussion. In particular, the segment from lines 288 to 306 needs more explanation. The authors provide limits (as γ or R go to infinity) of suprema of likelihood functions over certain domains. While I have no issue with taking limits of the likelihood evaluated at fixed parameter values (for example, the three equations on line 288 seem correct), passing to the limit of a maximum/supremum is not obvious when the domain is unbounded or depends on parameters that diverge. Similarly, continuity of the likelihood, by itself, does not directly imply the types of convergence asserted. More detail is needed to justify these steps so that the reader can be fully convinced of the validity of Theorem 1.

Finally, I have a minor concern regarding the Gradient EM section (lines 162–172). I found this part more difficult to follow than the rest of the paper. In particular, it would help to include additional explanation of how Equation (5) is derived.
