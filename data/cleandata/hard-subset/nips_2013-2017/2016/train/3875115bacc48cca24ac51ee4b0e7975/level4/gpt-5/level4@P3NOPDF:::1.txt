This paper analyzes the convergence behavior of EM and related iterative heuristics for maximum likelihood estimation in mixtures of spherical Gaussians and presents a coherent set of negative results showing non-convergence to global maxima, even in the population setting (infinite samples). The main findings are:
- For a mixture with three well-separated components, EM can converge to a suboptimal local maximum.
- For mixtures of k Gaussians, random initialization leads to success with probability at most exp(-Ω(k)).
- Gradient EM avoids strict saddle points generically, so failures arise from attraction to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

Overall assessment:
The results form a clear and unified narrative about the failure modes of EM. However, many of the examples, techniques, and probability bounds closely parallel well-known phenomena for k-means/Lloyd’s algorithm under large separation and are therefore not particularly surprising. The paper would benefit from a sharper contrast with established results for k-means and EM, and from a more precise articulation of the regimes and assumptions under which the negative results hold.

Strengths:
- Clean and coherent negative results that highlight the role of bad local maxima rather than saddle points.
- A simple and intuitive three-component construction, extended to general k via a recursive design.
- Conceptual clarity in connecting strict-saddle avoidance with the practical failure modes of EM-like methods.

Weaknesses/limitations:
- The main counterexample (two nearby clusters and one far-away cluster) and its extension are closely aligned with classic bad instances for Lloyd’s algorithm, especially in the regime of large separation where EM behaves similarly to k-means.
- The exp(-Ω(k)) random-initialization failure probability mirrors known initialization failures for k-means and thus feels expected.
- The novelty beyond translating k-means-style phenomena to population EM is limited, and the paper underplays connections to positive results for Lloyd’s/EM under separation or good initialization.

Requests for clarification and improvements:
1) Precise assumptions and regime:
- Please formalize the “very far apart” condition. How does the separation scale with dimension (e.g., >> sqrt(d)) and variance, and is the failure robust across a range of separations?
- Specify whether components have equal variances and mixing weights. Are the constructions robust to small perturbations in weights/variances?
- Clarify whether the results hold in both the population and finite-sample regimes, and whether the counterexamples persist with finite samples (and at what sample sizes).

2) Relation to k-means and prior work:
- Provide a careful comparison to Lloyd’s algorithm and its known failure modes under large separation. The constructions here appear to fall squarely in a regime where EM’s soft assignments effectively mimic k-means.
- Contrast with positive guarantees for Lloyd’s under separation (e.g., Kumar–Kannan), and with analyses showing divergence between EM and Lloyd’s in less-separated regimes (e.g., Balakrishnan–Wainwright–Yu). What, if anything, is uniquely EM-specific in your negative examples?
- Discuss connections to prior literature on local maxima in Gaussian mixture likelihoods and EM’s sensitivity to initialization.

3) Random initialization and success probability:
- For the exp(-Ω(k)) success probability, please quantify constants and dependencies on k, dimension, separation, and weight imbalance. Is this bound tight?
- Discuss whether standard remedies (e.g., multiple restarts scaling with k, k log k seeding, or distance-squared/k-means++ initialization) alleviate the failure, and whether your constructions remain hard under such strategies.

4) Gradient EM and saddle-point avoidance:
- The claim that gradient EM does not converge to strict saddles is consistent with general strict-saddle theory for gradient methods. Please state the precise assumptions (step sizes, smoothness, Lipschitz constants) under which your result holds and make the connection to known strict-saddle results explicit.
- Clarify implications for standard EM (alternating E/M steps) versus gradient EM: which conclusions transfer, and where do they diverge?

5) Geometry and intuition:
- Include a clear geometric explanation (ideally with a figure) for the three-component example showing why one center is attracted to the two-close components while two centers split the far cluster.
- For the k-component recursive construction, provide more intuition about how attraction basins evolve with k and how the construction scales.

6) Practical implications:
- Given that failures stem from bad local maxima rather than saddles, discuss practical initialization strategies and diagnostics (e.g., k-means++ seeding, spectral or method-of-moments initializers, restarts) and whether your theory suggests concrete guidelines on the number of restarts or seeding quality required.
- If possible, provide small-scale empirical illustrations on synthetic data to visualize the attraction basins and corroborate the population-level theory.

Significance and originality:
The paper offers a useful synthesis of negative results for EM on spherical Gaussian mixtures and clarifies that strict saddles are not the primary obstacle. That said, in the high-separation regime the behavior mirrors that of k-means, and the main constructions and failure-probability bounds are largely anticipated by analogous k-means results. Sharpening the distinctions from k-means, expanding on robustness to model misspecification, and clarifying the precise assumptions would improve both the perceived novelty and practical value.

Recommendation:
Promising in clarity and cohesion, but currently limited in novelty. I encourage the authors to strengthen the comparative analysis with k-means/Lloyd’s and to tighten technical statements about assumptions, robustness, and initialization so that the theoretical contributions and practical takeaways are more sharply delineated.
