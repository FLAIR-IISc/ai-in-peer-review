The paper studies the convergence of the EM algorithm (and iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation) for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, and presents several results that show non-convergence to global maxima. 

The authors' main contributions can be summarized as follows. First, they provide a negative answer to an open question of Srebro [2007], showing that even when the mixture has three clusters that are very far apart and with infinite samples, algorithms like EM can get stuck in local optima. This result is established through the construction of a well-separated uniform mixture of three spherical unit-variance Gaussians, whose population log-likelihood function contains a bad local maximum. Second, the authors demonstrate that for mixtures of k Gaussians, random initialization for EM only succeeds with at most exp(-Ω(k)) probability. This result is obtained through a recursive construction of a Gaussian mixture distribution, where the true centers can be grouped into two clusters that are far apart, and then further grouped into mini-clusters within each cluster. Finally, the authors show that gradient EM will not converge to strict saddle points generically; hence, bad local maxima are typically the issue.

The paper presents a coherent set of results that show that EM does not converge to global maxima (even with infinite samples). However, I feel that the results are not particularly surprising, and the techniques also follow expected lines. The main bad example is a mixture with three components: two of them are closer to each other, and the third is very far from both. This is fairly expected behavior, particularly when the centers are separated by >> √d. Under this amount of separation, the clusters do not overlap and learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians becomes very similar in flavor to k-means. Similar examples provide bad instances of Lloyd's algorithm for k-means clustering.

The authors' results are technically sound, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow. However, I believe that the paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing results on k-means clustering. For instance, Kumar and Kannan provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd's heuristic for k-means clustering (and hence mixtures of Gaussians under sufficient separation conditions). While EM is different from Lloyd's heuristic when the Gaussians are not sufficiently separated (as in Balakrishnan-Wainwright-Yu), they seem essentially equivalent when there is large separation (this is true of the bad examples presented in the paper). A comparison with k-means (and Lloyd's heuristic) that also contrasts this result would be very useful.

Quality: 8/10. The paper presents a coherent set of results that are technically sound. However, the results are not particularly surprising, and the techniques also follow expected lines.

Originality: 6/10. While the paper presents some new results, the techniques and ideas are not particularly novel.

Significance: 7/10. The paper presents some interesting results on the non-convergence of EM to global maxima. However, the results are not particularly surprising, and the implications are not entirely clear.

Overall, I would give the paper a score of 30/40. While the paper presents some interesting results, it is not particularly surprising or novel. However, the paper is well-written and easy to follow, and the results are technically sound.
