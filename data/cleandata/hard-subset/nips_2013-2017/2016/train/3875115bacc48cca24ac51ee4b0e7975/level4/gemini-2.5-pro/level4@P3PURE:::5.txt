This paper resolves a significant open question posed by Srebro (2007): "Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?". The authors answer this question in the negative by constructing a general class of counterexamples, demonstrating that spurious local maxima can indeed exist even in the asymptotic limit.

The paper begins by outlining the standard likelihood maximization schemes for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), such as the EM and gradient EM algorithms, and discusses common initialization strategies. The authors then present their primary theorems, which formalize the counterexample to Srebro's question. Crucially, they further show that even with a common random initialization strategy, both the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to these non-optimal maxima with exponentially high probability. To build intuition for their proofs, the authors provide a clear and self-contained proof for the simpler case of k=3 components in d=1 dimension.

The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and strikes an excellent balance between the formal presentation of its theoretical results and the need to provide intuition, all within the constraints of the conference format. The question resolved here is of fundamental importance for understanding the challenges of non-convex optimization, showing that issues with local optima can persist even for simple models in the infinite-sample setting.

While I did not carefully check the supplementary material, one area for potential improvement would be to provide estimates for the constants in the probability bounds. This is important because, in practice, the number of mixture components (k) is often not excessively large. Estimates of these constants would help characterize the regime where convergence to bad solutions becomes a practical concern and could perhaps identify a phase transition. If an analytical derivation of the constants is too complex, supporting simulations that demonstrate this phenomenon for moderate k would be a valuable addition. However, this is a suggestion for improvement and does not detract from the significant novelty and impact of this paper's theoretical contributions.
