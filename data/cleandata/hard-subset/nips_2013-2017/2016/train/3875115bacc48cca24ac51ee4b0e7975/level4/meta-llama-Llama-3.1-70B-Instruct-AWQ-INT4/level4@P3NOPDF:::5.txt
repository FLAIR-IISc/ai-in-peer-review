The paper presents a significant contribution to the field by answering an open question posed by Srebro in his 2007 paper, "Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?" The authors provide a definitive answer to this question, demonstrating the existence of local maxima through the construction of a general class of counterexamples.

The paper begins by outlining the schemes used to maximize the likelihood in the context of Gaussian Mixture Models, including the EM and gradient EM algorithms. The authors also address the issue of initialization, discussing a common choice used in practice. They then present their primary theorems, which provide a counterexample to Srebro's question. Furthermore, the authors show that even with a common choice of random initialization, the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to non-optimal maxima with exponentially high probability.

To provide intuition for their proofs, the authors present a simple case (k=3, d=1) and walk the reader through the key steps. This approach helps to balance the need for theoretical rigor with the need for clarity and understanding. The paper is well-written and clear, making it accessible to a wide range of readers.

The question addressed by the authors is of great importance, as it sheds light on the issues that arise in non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic limit for simple distributions. The results have significant implications for our understanding of the behavior of these algorithms and highlight the need for careful consideration of initialization and optimization schemes.

One potential area for improvement is the provision of estimates for the constants in the probability term. While the authors demonstrate that the convergence to non-optimal maxima occurs with exponentially high probability, it would be useful to have a better understanding of when this phenomenon is likely to occur in practice. Specifically, it would be helpful to know when the number of components (k) becomes too large, and the algorithm is likely to converge to a bad solution. Providing estimates of the constants or supporting simulations would help to clarify this point and provide further insight into the behavior of the algorithm.

However, this issue does not detract from the novelty or impact of the paper. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field, and their results are likely to have a lasting impact on our understanding of non-convex optimization and Gaussian Mixture Models.
