This paper resolves a long-standing open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of non-optimal local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors answer this question affirmatively by constructing a general class of counterexamples, demonstrating that even in the asymptotic limit, the likelihood landscape for GMMs can contain spurious local optima.

The paper begins by contextualizing the problem, outlining standard algorithms like Expectation-Maximization (EM) and gradient EM for maximizing the GMM likelihood. The authors also discuss the critical role of initialization, noting a common random initialization strategy. They then present their main theorems, which formally establish the existence of a GMM for which the infinite-sample likelihood possesses a non-optimal local maximum. A key result is that even with a standard random initialization, the EM and gradient EM algorithms will converge to this spurious maximum with exponentially high probability in the number of mixture components. To build intuition for their technical arguments, the authors provide a clear and self-contained proof for a simpler case (k=3 components in d=1 dimension).

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The authors strike an excellent balance between the formal presentation of their theoretical results and the intuitive explanations needed to make them accessible, all within the constraints of the conference format. The question addressed is of fundamental importance to our understanding of non-convex optimization, showing that challenges can persist even for simple models in the ideal infinite-data regime.

While I did not perform a detailed check of the supplementary material, the paper could be strengthened by including estimates for the constants in the high-probability statements. This would be valuable because, in many practical applications, the number of mixture components, k, is not excessively large. Characterizing these constants would help clarify the practical regime in which convergence to spurious local maxima becomes a significant concern—in essence, identifying where a phase transition might occur. If the constants are intractable or not insightful, supporting simulations would be a valuable alternative to demonstrate that this is an observable phenomenon for moderate k.

Nevertheless, this is a minor suggestion and does not diminish the novelty or significant impact of this paper's core theoretical contributions. The work provides a definitive and important answer to a foundational question in machine learning.
