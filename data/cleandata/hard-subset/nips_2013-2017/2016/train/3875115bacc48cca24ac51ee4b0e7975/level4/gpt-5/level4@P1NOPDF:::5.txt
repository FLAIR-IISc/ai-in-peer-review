This paper resolves an open question posed by Srebro (2007): “Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?” It answers in the affirmative by constructing a broad class of counterexamples. The authors begin by outlining standard approaches to maximizing the likelihood in Gaussian mixture models, focusing on EM and gradient EM. They also discuss initialization and adopt a commonly used random-initialization scheme. The main theorems provide the counterexamples resolving Srebro’s question, and further show that, under this common random initialization, both EM and gradient EM converge to suboptimal local maxima with exponentially high probability. To build intuition, the paper works through a simple illustrative case (k=3, d=1) before presenting the general results.

The paper is clear, well organized, and strikes a good balance between space constraints and providing intuition for the theoretical results. The question it answers is important for understanding the pitfalls of non-convex optimization, even in the population (infinite-sample) limit for seemingly simple models.

I did not carefully check the supplementary material, but it would be valuable to include estimates of the constants appearing in the probability bounds. This matters because, in practice, k is seldom taken to be extremely large; knowing the constants would clarify when one should expect convergence to bad solutions in the asymptotic limit (i.e., where the phase transition occurs). If the constants are unwieldy or otherwise uninformative, supporting simulations would help demonstrate that the phenomenon is observable in realistic regimes. That said, this concern does not detract from the novelty or impact of the work.
