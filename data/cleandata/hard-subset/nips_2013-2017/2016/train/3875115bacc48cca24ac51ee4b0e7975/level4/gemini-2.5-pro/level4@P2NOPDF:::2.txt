This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters of a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). The analysis is conducted under the assumption that the number of Gaussian components, their mixture weights, and their covariance matrices are known and fixed. The authors prove three important negative results:

1.  Even for large sample sizes, the likelihood function can exhibit local maxima with values arbitrarily lower than the global maximum.
2.  When randomly initialized, the EM algorithm converges with high probability to a critical point whose likelihood value is arbitrarily lower than the maximal likelihood.
3.  The same negative result holds for the gradient variant of the EM algorithm.

**Significance and Strengths**

The paper addresses a fundamental and important question regarding the practical performance of the EM algorithm, a cornerstone of statistical machine learning. The theoretical results are insightful and provide a formal basis for understanding the failure modes of EM that are often observed empirically. These findings are valuable for the community and can help guide the development of more robust estimation techniques.

The authors appropriately restrict their analysis to a simplified GMM setting—specifically, where the number of components and their uniform weights are known, and the covariance matrices are the identity matrix. Given the technical complexity of the proofs, this simplification is well-justified. The results obtained, even in this constrained setting, are informative and shed significant light on the algorithm's behavior.

**Areas for Improvement**

While the paper's contribution is significant, there are two main areas that require attention to strengthen the manuscript.

1.  **Discrepancy in Dimensionality:** The paper is framed as addressing the general case of dimension `d`, but the proofs are provided only for the one-dimensional case (`d=1`). This is a notable limitation that should be addressed explicitly. It would be beneficial for the authors to comment on the feasibility of extending these proofs to an arbitrary dimension `d`. If the extension is non-trivial, a brief discussion of the additional technical challenges posed by higher dimensions would be necessary to accurately scope the paper's contribution.

2.  **Clarity and Rigor in the Proof of Theorem 1:** My primary concern lies with the proof of Theorem 1, which I found to be too condensed to be fully convincing. The critical section from lines 288 to 306, in particular, would benefit from a more detailed explanation. The proof relies on establishing limits of suprema of the likelihood function over domains that are either unbounded or dependent on parameters that tend to infinity (e.g., `gamma`, `R`). While the computation of a limit for a function evaluated at a fixed parameter point is straightforward (as in the three equations on line 288), establishing the limit of a supremum over a changing domain is a much stronger claim that requires careful justification. The paper's appeal to the continuity of the likelihood function is, in my opinion, insufficient to directly imply the stated convergence results. To make the proof verifiable and compelling, I strongly recommend that the authors expand this section with a more rigorous and step-by-step derivation, even if it requires shortening other discussion-oriented parts of the paper.

**Minor Points**

The section introducing the Gradient EM algorithm (lines 162-172) was more difficult to follow than the rest of the paper. Specifically, the derivation of Equation (5) is not immediately obvious. The authors could improve readability by providing more context and explanation for how this equation is obtained.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper with interesting and important results. Addressing the concerns regarding the proof of Theorem 1 and the scope of dimensionality would significantly enhance its quality and impact.
