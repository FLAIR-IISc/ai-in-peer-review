This paper provides a definitive response to a long-standing question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation, demonstrating – through the construction of carefully designed counterexamples – that such local maxima do *not* exist. The authors begin with a clear exposition of standard Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) likelihood maximization techniques, including the Expectation-Maximization (EM) and gradient-based EM algorithms, and a discussion of common initialization strategies. They then present their central theorems, directly addressing and resolving Srebro’s question [2007]. Importantly, the authors extend their analysis to demonstrate that, even with typical random initialization, the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to suboptimal maxima with exponentially high probability. To aid understanding, they provide an accessible proof sketch for a simplified scenario (k=3, d=1). The paper is exceptionally well-written and achieves an excellent balance between theoretical rigor and intuitive explanation, effectively managing space constraints while providing necessary context.  The addressed question is highly significant, contributing valuable insights into the challenges inherent in non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic regime for relatively simple distributions. While I have not exhaustively verified the supplementary material, providing estimates for the constants within the stated probability bounds would strengthen the practical implications of the work. Specifically, understanding these constants is crucial for identifying the parameter regimes (e.g., the value of *k*) where convergence to suboptimal solutions becomes prominent – essentially pinpointing a phase transition. If analytical estimation proves intractable, supporting simulations demonstrating this phenomenon would be valuable. However, this suggestion does not diminish the paper’s novelty or overall impact.
