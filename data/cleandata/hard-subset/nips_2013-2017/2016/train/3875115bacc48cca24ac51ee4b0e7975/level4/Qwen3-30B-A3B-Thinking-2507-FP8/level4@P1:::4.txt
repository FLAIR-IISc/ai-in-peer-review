

This paper addresses a fundamental question about the likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with equal weights and spherical Gaussians. It resolves an open problem posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of "bad" local maxima in the population likelihood function. The authors demonstrate that for any M ≥ 3 components, there exist well-separated, equally-weighted GMMs in one dimension whose population log-likelihood function contains local maxima with arbitrarily worse likelihood values than the global optimum. This result directly contradicts Srebro's conjecture that all local maxima would be global in the infinite-sample limit for well-separated mixtures. The authors further establish that the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to such bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)), and that first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a concrete counterexample to Srebro's conjecture, showing that even in the favorable setting of well-separated, equal-weighted spherical Gaussians, bad local maxima can exist. Second, it demonstrates that random initialization for EM leads to exponentially poor convergence probabilities in the number of components M. Third, it clarifies that the failure of local search methods stems from bad local maxima rather than saddle points, by showing that first-order EM avoids strict saddle points.

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The proof of Theorem 1 (the main structural result) is carefully constructed and well-motivated. The authors provide clear intuition for their construction, particularly for M=3 in one dimension. The argument for Theorem 2 (algorithmic consequences) builds logically on Theorem 1, with a clever recursive construction that demonstrates the exponentially small success probability for random initialization. The proof of Theorem 4 (avoidance of strict saddle points) is particularly elegant, leveraging recent results on gradient methods without requiring smoothness assumptions. The paper's theoretical results are well-supported and carefully presented.

The novelty is significant. While the existence of local maxima in finite-sample settings is known (Améndola et al., 2015), the paper provides the first rigorous demonstration of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population setting for M ≥ 3. The hierarchical construction that leads to exponentially small success probabilities for random initialization is a particularly clever insight. The connection between the geometric configuration of the true means and the existence of bad local maxima represents a novel perspective on GMM likelihood landscapes.

The significance of these results is substantial. Theoretically, the paper fundamentally changes our understanding of the likelihood landscape for GMMs, showing that even in the most favorable settings (well-separated, equal-weighted, spherical Gaussians), local search methods can fail catastrophically. Practically, the results strongly suggest that careful initialization is essential for EM and related algorithms, even in large-sample settings. This has direct implications for practitioners who rely on random initialization schemes, suggesting that such approaches may require exponentially many restarts to achieve reasonable performance. The paper also opens up new directions for research on the optimization landscape of non-convex problems in statistical learning.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The authors provide clear motivation, careful problem setup, and intuitive explanations of their constructions. The technical arguments are well-organized, and the paper effectively balances mathematical rigor with accessible intuition. The figures (not shown in the draft) would presumably aid in visualizing the key constructions.

Strengths:
- Resolves an important open problem in GMM theory
- Provides a concrete counterexample to Srebro's conjecture
- Demonstrates the practical implications of bad local maxima for EM
- Shows that the failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points
- Offers a clear theoretical explanation for the poor performance of random initialization
- Well-written with excellent organization and intuition

Weaknesses:
- The paper focuses on the one-dimensional case for Theorem 1; extending the result to higher dimensions would strengthen the theoretical contribution
- The paper doesn't address how practitioners might overcome these initialization challenges beyond noting that method-of-moments approaches could help
- While the paper shows the failure of random initialization, it doesn't provide a thorough comparison with alternative initialization strategies

The paper is highly relevant to NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental theoretical question about a widely used statistical model with significant practical implications. The results contribute substantially to our understanding of non-convex optimization in statistical learning, which is a central topic at NIPS.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that provides deep insights into the behavior of EM for GMMs. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field. It should be accepted.
