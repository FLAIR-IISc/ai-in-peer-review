The paper "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences" presents a comprehensive study on the convergence of the EM algorithm for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians. The authors provide a set of results that demonstrate the non-convergence of the EM algorithm to global maxima, even with infinite samples.

The paper's main contribution is the construction of a Gaussian mixture model with three components, where two components are closer to each other, and the third is far away from both. The authors show that for the log-likelihood objective, there exists a local maximum with one center near the first two components and two centers near the far-off cluster. This result is then extended to k components using a recursive construction.

The authors also analyze the performance of the EM algorithm with random initialization and show that it converges to a bad critical point with high probability. Specifically, they prove that the probability of success for the EM algorithm is exponentially small as a function of the number of components, k. This result is consistent with the behavior of k-means clustering algorithms under similar separation conditions.

Furthermore, the authors study the behavior of the first-order EM algorithm and show that it does not converge to strict saddle points generically. This suggests that bad local maxima are typically the issue, rather than saddle points.

While the results presented in the paper are coherent and well-motivated, they may not be entirely surprising. The construction of the bad example for the three-component mixture is intuitive, and the extension to k components, although requiring some effort, is somewhat expected. The failure of random initialization for the EM algorithm is also consistent with the behavior of k-means clustering algorithms under similar conditions.

A comparison with k-means clustering and Lloyd's heuristic would be useful to contrast the results and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the convergence properties of the EM algorithm. The authors may also want to consider discussing the implications of their results for practical applications, such as the choice of initialization schemes and the number of components in the mixture model.

In terms of the quality of the paper, the writing is clear, and the results are well-presented. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the EM algorithm and its variants, and the proofs are well-organized and easy to follow. However, some of the results, such as the construction of the bad example, may benefit from additional intuition and explanation.

Overall, the paper provides a solid contribution to the understanding of the convergence properties of the EM algorithm for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians. While the results may not be entirely surprising, they are well-motivated and provide a coherent story about the non-convergence to global optima.
