This paper investigates the behavior of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating mean parameters in a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). The analysis is performed under the assumption that the number of Gaussian components, their corresponding mixture weights, and their covariance matrices are all known and fixed. The work is theoretical and establishes three key negative findings for the EM algorithm: 1) even for large sample sizes, the likelihood function can possess local maxima with values arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum likelihood value; 2) when randomly initialized, the EM algorithm converges with high probability to a critical point whose likelihood is arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum; and 3) the same negative result is shown to hold for the gradient EM algorithm.

The problem explored in this work is both relevant and significant. To maintain analytical tractability, the paper restricts its focus to a simplified GMM where the number of components is known, the weights are uniform, and the covariance matrices are equal to the identity. I find these simplifications to be well-justified, given the already considerable technical complexity of the proofs and the informative nature of the results obtained. In my opinion, these findings are very interesting and can contribute to a deeper understanding of the practical performance of the EM algorithm.

A point of clarification is needed regarding the paper's scope. While the work is presented as addressing a general dimension *d*, the formal proofs are conducted for the one-dimensional case (*d*=1). It would strengthen the paper if the authors could comment on whether similar proofs could be constructed for an arbitrary dimension *d*. If not, a brief discussion of the additional difficulties encountered in dimensions greater than one would be valuable.

My primary concern with the manuscript pertains to the proof of Theorem 1 (it should be noted that I did not review the proofs in the supplementary material). The proof, in its current form, appears overly condensed. I would recommend expanding this proof, even if it requires abbreviating other descriptive sections of the paper. The specific portion that I believe warrants more detail spans from line 288 to 306. Here, the authors present limits of supremums of likelihood functions over certain domains. I am comfortable with the computation of limits of the likelihood function evaluated at fixed parameters, and thus I can accept that the three equations in line 288 are likely correct. However, establishing the limit of a maximum is not straightforward when the optimization domain is unbounded or dependent on the parameters that tend to infinity. Similarly, the authors mention at several points that the likelihood function is continuous, but this property alone does not directly imply the types of convergence that are asserted. A more detailed derivation is necessary to fully substantiate the proof of Theorem 1 for the reader.

Lastly, I have a minor concern with the clarity of the section discussing the Gradient EM algorithm, from line 162 to line 172. I found this part more difficult to follow than the rest of the paper. Specifically, the authors could improve reader comprehension by providing more explanation on the derivation of Equation (5).
