This paper addresses the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters of a Gaussian mixture model. The number of Gaussian components, the mixture weights, and the covariance matrices are known and fixed. The paper presents three negative results concerning the EM algorithm. First, even with infinite sample size, the likelihood function can have local maxima with arbitrarily worse likelihood values than the global optimum. Second, the EM algorithm, with random initialization, converges with high probability to a suboptimal critical point. Third, the same negative result holds for a first-order variant of the EM algorithm. I believe the question addressed by the paper is interesting and warrants investigation. 

To obtain rigorous proofs, the paper focuses on a simplified Gaussian mixture model: the number of components is known, the weights are uniform, and the covariance matrices are identity matrices. While these simplifications are present, I do not consider them a significant drawback, given the technical complexity of the proofs and the informative nature of the results. These findings are valuable for understanding the behavior of the EM algorithm in practical settings. It’s worth noting that the paper generally considers the case of general dimension *d*, while the proofs presented are specifically for *d* = 1. The authors might benefit from briefly discussing whether similar proofs would be possible for arbitrary dimension *d*, and, if not, outlining the additional difficulties encountered in higher dimensions.

My primary concern lies with the proof of Theorem 1 (and I have not reviewed the supplementary material). I found this proof to be somewhat concise and would have preferred a more detailed exposition, potentially at the cost of shortening some of the preceding discussion. Specifically, the section from line 288 to 306 requires further clarification. While I do not question the calculations of the likelihood functions evaluated at fixed parameters, establishing the limits of maximum values over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains is not immediately obvious. Additionally, the authors mention the continuity of the likelihood function, but this alone does not necessarily imply the specific types of convergence stated. More detail and a more extended proof would strengthen the reader’s confidence in the validity of Theorem 1.

Finally, I found the section addressing the first-order EM algorithm (lines 362-372) somewhat challenging to follow. More explanation regarding the derivation of Equation (5) would be beneficial. 

**Summary of arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significant Negative Results:** The paper rigorously demonstrates the existence of bad local maxima and the likely convergence to these maxima with standard initialization schemes, challenging the common assumption of reliable EM convergence.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The results provide valuable theoretical insights into the behavior of the EM algorithm, particularly regarding its sensitivity to initialization.
*   **Well-Motivated Problem:** The study addresses a fundamental issue in mixture model estimation and contributes to a better understanding of the limitations of the EM algorithm.
*   **Clear Presentation (Mostly):** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the problem, the results, and the motivation behind the research.

**Summary of arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Proof Conciseness (Theorem 1):** The proof of Theorem 1 could benefit from greater detail and explanation, particularly in the section regarding the limits of suprema.
*   **Limited Dimensionality:** The proofs are currently only for the 1D case, and the extension to higher dimensions is not discussed.
*   **Complexity of First-Order EM Section:** The explanation of the first-order EM algorithm section could be clarified for a broader audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor concerns regarding the proof of Theorem 1 and the clarity of the first-order EM section, I believe this paper presents a valuable and significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm. The negative results are compelling and could influence the development of more robust initialization strategies or alternative optimization methods. I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
