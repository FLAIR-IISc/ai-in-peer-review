- Resolves Srebro (2007): constructs a counterexample showing M ≥ 3 well-separated, equal-weight, unit-variance spherical Gaussians in d=1 have population log-likelihood local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.  
- Shows random initialization causes EM and first-order EM to converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{-Ω(M)}, i.e., success probability is exponentially small in M.  
- Proves first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, so poor convergence is due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Technical quality: Theorem 1 proof is rigorous; Theorem 2’s hierarchical grouping argument is logical; saddle-avoidance leverages recent nonconvex optimization results.  
- Clarity: Exposition is excellent, with a clear geometric intuition for M=3, precise formal statements, and well-articulated links to EM limitations.  
- Originality: Significant and novel resolution of a long-standing open question, with new constructions and hierarchical initialization analysis not previously explored.  
- Significance: Practically implies random initialization is insufficient even for well-separated mixtures and motivates new initialization schemes and further study of nonconvex clustering landscapes and finite-sample behavior.
