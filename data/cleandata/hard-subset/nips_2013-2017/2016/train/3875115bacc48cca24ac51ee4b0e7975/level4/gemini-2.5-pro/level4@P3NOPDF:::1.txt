This paper investigates the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, demonstrating its fallibility in converging to the global maximum of the likelihood function. The authors present a coherent set of theoretical results highlighting scenarios where EM and related iterative heuristics fail. The main contributions are:

1.  A proof that even for a mixture of three well-separated components and with access to the true population distribution (i.e., infinite samples), the EM algorithm can converge to a spurious local maximum.
2.  An analysis showing that for a mixture of k Gaussians, random initialization leads to convergence to the global optimum with a probability that is, at best, `exp(-Ω(k))`.
3.  A result indicating that for generic cases, Gradient EM will not converge to strict saddle points, which implies that suboptimal local maxima, rather than saddle points, are the primary obstacles to global convergence.

While the paper presents a rigorous and unified analysis of EM's non-convergence, the core findings and the techniques used to derive them are largely in line with existing intuition from the related field of k-means clustering.

The central counterexample involves a mixture of three components where two are relatively close to each other, and a third is situated far from this pair. The authors demonstrate that the log-likelihood objective for this configuration contains a local maximum where one cluster center is positioned between the two close components, and two cluster centers are placed near the single, distant component. This behavior is quite predictable, especially in the high-separation regime considered (`>> sqrt(d)`), where the component distributions have negligible overlap. In this setting, maximizing the likelihood for a mixture of spherical Gaussians becomes functionally very similar to minimizing the objective for k-means clustering. Indeed, analogous counterexamples are well-known to cause Lloyd's algorithm for k-means to converge to suboptimal solutions. The paper extends this 3-component case to a k-component mixture through a clever recursive construction. While this generalization requires non-trivial technical effort, the underlying principle remains familiar.

Similarly, the result on the failure of random initialization mirrors well-understood phenomena in k-means. The low success probability of `exp(-Ω(k))` is the same known failure rate for random initialization in k-means, which has motivated the development of more sophisticated initialization schemes like k-means++ (which uses distance-squared sampling) or seeding with `k log(k)` initial centers.

In summary, the paper provides valuable formal proofs for the non-convergence of EM, confirming long-held suspicions with theoretical rigor. However, the impact is somewhat diminished by the fact that the failure modes described are deeply analogous to those already well-documented for k-means clustering.

**Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper would be significantly strengthened by explicitly addressing its relationship to the extensive literature on k-means clustering. A direct comparison with the analysis of Lloyd's algorithm would be particularly insightful. For instance, the work by Kumar and Kannan provides convergence guarantees for Lloyd's algorithm under certain conditions. While EM is distinct from Lloyd's heuristic in the general case of overlapping Gaussians (as studied by Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu), the algorithms behave almost identically in the large-separation regime that is the focus of the counterexamples constructed here.

A discussion that contrasts the results of this paper with the known properties of k-means would help clarify the novel contributions. Does the analysis here offer new insights beyond translating known k-means pathologies to the Gaussian mixture setting? Such a comparison would better contextualize the work and highlight what is fundamentally new about this analysis of the likelihood landscape for Gaussian mixtures versus the well-explored geometry of the k-means objective.
