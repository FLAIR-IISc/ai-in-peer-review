This paper addresses the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the set of mean parameters for a model of a mixture of Gaussian variables, where the number of Gaussian variables, the weights of the mixture, and the covariance matrices of the Gaussian variables are known and fixed. The paper is theoretical in nature and presents three negative results for the EM algorithm. Firstly, it demonstrates that even for large sample sizes, local maxima can exist for the likelihood function, with likelihood values arbitrarily smaller than the maximal likelihood value. Secondly, it shows that the EM algorithm, when randomly initiated, converges with high probability to a critical point with a likelihood value arbitrarily smaller than the maximal likelihood value. Lastly, the same negative result holds for the gradient EM algorithm.

I find the question addressed by the paper to be interesting and worthy of attention. To obtain rigorous proofs, the paper focuses on a simplified mixture of Gaussian variable models, where the number of components is known, the weights are known and uniform, and the covariance matrices are equal to the identity matrix. I do not consider these simplifications to be a problem, given that the proofs are already technical and the obtained results are informative. These results are very interesting and can be useful in understanding the EM algorithm in practice.

It is worth noting that the paper is written to address the case of general dimension d, while the proofs address the case d=1. Perhaps the authors could discuss whether similar proofs would be possible for arbitrary dimension d and, if not, briefly mention the additional difficulties of the case d larger than 1.

My primary concern about the paper is the proof of Theorem 1. I think that this proof is too concise and would have preferred it to be longer, potentially at the expense of shortening some of the discussions in the paper. Specifically, the part of the proof from line 288 to 306 requires more details. The authors provide limits (as gamma or R goes to infinity) of supremums of likelihood functions over some domains. While I do not have a problem with the computation of limits of likelihood functions evaluated at fixed parameters, obtaining the limit of a maximum is not entirely obvious when the domain is unbounded or depends on the parameters that go to infinity. Furthermore, the authors mention that the likelihood function is continuous, but this does not directly imply the types of convergences stated in the paper. I believe that more details and a longer proof are necessary to convince the reader of the validity of the proof of Theorem 1.

Finally, I have a minor concern with the part of the paper addressing the Gradient EM from line 162 to line 172. I found this section more challenging to follow than the rest of the paper. Perhaps the authors could provide more explanation on how Equation (5) is obtained, which would improve the clarity of the paper.

Overall, the paper presents interesting and informative results that can be useful in understanding the EM algorithm in practice. Addressing the concerns mentioned above would further strengthen the paper and improve its overall quality.
