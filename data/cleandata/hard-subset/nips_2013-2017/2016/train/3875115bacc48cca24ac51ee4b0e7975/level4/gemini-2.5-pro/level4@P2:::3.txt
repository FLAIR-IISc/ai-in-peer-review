This paper investigates the structure of the population log-likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The analysis focuses on a favorable and simplified setting: a well-specified model with an equal mixture of M ≥ 3 isotropic Gaussian components with known, unit variance. The primary contributions are twofold. First, the authors demonstrate that even in this idealized setting and with infinite data, the population log-likelihood function possesses local maxima that can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. This result resolves a long-standing open question posed by Srebro (2007). Second, the paper analyzes the algorithmic consequences of this structural property. It shows that for specific GMM configurations, the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm (and a first-order variant), when initialized by drawing samples from the mixture distribution, will converge to a suboptimal critical point with a probability approaching 1 exponentially in M.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper provides a definitive and negative answer to an important conjecture regarding the optimization landscape of GMMs. By demonstrating the existence of arbitrarily poor local maxima in the population likelihood, the work clarifies a fundamental aspect of this widely used model class. This is a significant theoretical contribution.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The proofs appear sound and the constructions used to establish the existence of bad local maxima and analyze the failure probability of EM are insightful. The connection drawn to dynamical systems theory to analyze the behavior of first-order EM around saddle points is also a strong point.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out, and the main results are stated precisely.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

The main concerns relate to the practical implications and the generality of the findings, which could be clarified to strengthen the paper's impact.

1.  **Prevalence of "Bad" GMM Configurations:** Theorem 1 establishes the existence of a poor local maximum for a specific configuration of true mixture centers (e.g., when centers are themselves clustered). A key question is how likely these "bad" configurations are to occur in practice. The paper's practical relevance would be significantly strengthened if the authors could provide an argument for the prevalence of such configurations. For example, could the authors assume a generative process or distribution for the true centers µ* and then show that configurations susceptible to poor local maxima become increasingly likely as the number of components (M) or the data dimensionality (d) grows? While lines 210-218 suggest Theorem 2 addresses this, the theorem still relies on a fixed, adversarially chosen configuration of true centers. A more formal statement on the likelihood of encountering such problematic GMM instances would be very valuable.

2.  **Practicality of the Random Initialization Scheme:** Theorem 2 analyzes an initialization scheme where centers are drawn i.i.d. from the underlying mixture distribution. While theoretically standard, the practical relevance of this scheme is questionable in the very settings the paper constructs. For instance, in the example of three components where two are close together and one is far apart, two-thirds of the data will be concentrated in the first cluster. It seems unlikely that a practitioner, observing this clear clustering, would initialize two centers in the sparse region and only one in the dense region. When data is visibly clustered, initialization is often guided by that structure (e.g., using k-means++ or manually placing centers). Could the authors comment on how their results might change under more sophisticated or practically-minded initialization schemes?

**Detailed Questions:**

*   **L74-81:** The purpose of the discussion and visualization for the M=2 case is somewhat unclear. It is mentioned that the conjecture's status remains an open problem for M=2, but the example does not seem to conclusively support or refute it. Could the authors clarify what conclusion the reader should draw from this section?
*   **L233-235:** In the sketch of the proof for Theorem 2, the authors describe two conditions for a favorable initialization. The need for condition (2) (matching the number of initialized centers to true centers within a cluster) is clear. However, the necessity of condition (1) ("all initialized centers fall in the neighborhood of exactly one cluster of true centers") is less obvious. If an initial center is placed between two clusters, is it guaranteed to move toward the wrong one? A brief elaboration would be helpful.
*   **L267-273 (Theorem 4):** The paper proves that first-order EM almost surely avoids converging to strict saddle points. From a practical perspective, what is the significance of this result? Is convergence to a saddle point considered more or less problematic than convergence to a poor local maximum? The paper's primary message seems to be that bad local maxima are the main culprits, and this result supports that claim. It would be helpful to explicitly state why ruling out saddle points is an important part of the overall argument.
