The paper studies the convergence of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm – and more generally, iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation – when applied to learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians. The core findings demonstrate non-convergence to global optima, specifically: (1) Even with well-separated, three-component mixtures and an infinite sample size, algorithms like EM can become trapped in local optima. (2) For mixtures of *k* Gaussians, random initialization of EM succeeds with a probability of at most exp(-Ω(*k*)). (3) First-order EM methods do not generically converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that poor local maxima are the primary cause of performance limitations. The paper presents a cohesive set of results highlighting the challenges of achieving global convergence in this setting.

However, the conclusions, while technically sound, are not entirely surprising, and the techniques employed largely follow established approaches. The central counterexample involves a three-component mixture where two components are closely clustered, while the third is significantly distant. The authors demonstrate the existence of a local maximum where one center approximates the closer pair, and two centers approximate the distant component. This behavior is foreseeable, particularly when the inter-cluster separation is significantly larger than √*d*. In such scenarios, the problem begins to resemble *k*-means clustering, where similar phenomena are known to occur. Bad instances for Lloyd's algorithm in *k*-means clustering share conceptual similarities. This example is generalized to *k* components through a recursive construction, which requires effort but is, again, reasonably predictable. Similarly, the failure of random initialization echoes observed behavior in *k*-means clustering, where it motivates strategies such as initializing with *k* log *k* centers or using distance-squared sampling.

In summary, while the paper tells a coherent and technically rigorous story regarding the non-convergence to global optima, the results largely align with existing intuition and prior work in related areas.

**Specific Comments:**

1.  The work of Kumar and Kannan provides convergence guarantees for Lloyd's heuristic in *k*-means clustering (and, consequently, for mixtures of Gaussians under conditions of sufficient separation). While EM differs from Lloyd's heuristic when Gaussian components exhibit limited separation (as discussed by Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu), the two algorithms become remarkably similar under conditions of large separation—the very conditions present in the paper's counterexamples. Therefore, a direct comparison with *k*-means and Lloyd’s heuristic, contrasting these results, would be valuable.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. Claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis, and the proof strategy appears correct. It represents a complete piece of work, although the primary contribution lies in solidifying known intuitions rather than presenting a radically new result. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations and assumptions of their analysis.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are reasonably clear. Providing more geometric intuition for the construction of the bad local optima could further improve clarity.
*   **Originality:** The originality lies primarily in the rigorous demonstration of expected behavior in a specific setting (Gaussian mixtures). While the results are not groundbreaking, they provide a formal analysis that strengthens existing understanding. The recursive construction for extending the three-component example is a reasonable contribution. Related work is adequately referenced.
*   **Significance:** The results are important for reinforcing the need for careful initialization strategies when employing EM or related algorithms for Gaussian mixture models. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the fact that the observed behavior is not entirely unexpected. The paper does not offer a novel algorithmic solution or a fundamentally new perspective on the problem.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Rigorous mathematical analysis of a fundamental problem in statistical estimation.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of results.
*   Addresses an open question regarding the convergence of EM.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Results are largely consistent with existing intuition and related work in clustering.
*   The significance of the findings is moderate, as they primarily confirm known limitations rather than offering a breakthrough.
*   The connection to *k*-means and Lloyd’s algorithm could be more thoroughly explored to provide a more nuanced perspective.
