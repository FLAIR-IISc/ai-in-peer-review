Summary
The paper analyzes the population log-likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with equal mixing weights and identity covariances, and derives three main results with clear algorithmic implications:

1) It provides a counterexample to Srebro’s 2007 conjecture by constructing a one-dimensional, three-component, well-separated, spherical GMM whose population log-likelihood admits suboptimal local maxima. Moreover, the likelihood gap between such a local maximum and the global optimum can be made arbitrarily large.

2) It shows that, under a natural random initialization scheme (drawing initial means from the underlying mixture), both EM and a first-order variant (gradient EM) converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{−Ω(M)} for suitably constructed mixtures. The construction uses a hierarchical clustering of the true component means, yielding exponentially small success probability unless one repeats restarts exponentially many times in M.

3) It proves that first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, so the observed failures are attributable to poor local maxima rather than saddle points.

Relation to prior work
The paper resolves in the negative the long-standing conjecture of Srebro (2007) that the population likelihood for equally weighted spherical mixtures has no spurious local maxima. It complements analyses of EM that guarantee local convergence under strong initialization or separation conditions (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and stands alongside moment/spectral and clustering-based approaches that bypass likelihood optimization. It also leverages recent insights on avoiding strict saddles in gradient methods (e.g., Lee et al., 2016; Panageas & Piliouras, 2016).

Quality
I carefully checked the proof of Theorem 1 and found it sound; the construction and the argument establishing a suboptimal local maximum are convincing and well explained. The high-level strategy for Theorem 2 appears reasonable and technically plausible (hierarchical clustering of true means and trapping under EM), though I did not verify all appendix details. The argument for Theorem 4 is consistent with recent results on gradient dynamics and is clearly stated. Assumptions and limitations (population setting, equal weights, identity covariance, well-separated means, specific initialization) are stated explicitly, and the results are correctly interpreted.

Clarity
The paper is well written, logically structured, and provides strong intuition for each result. The setup and algorithms (EM and first-order EM) are clearly described, and the geometric intuition behind the counterexample and the recursive construction is helpful. Some references in the bibliography appear inconsistently formatted and should be cleaned.

Originality
The work makes an original and substantial contribution by disproving a widely held conjecture in a favorable setting and by tightly linking the geometry of the means to the emergence of bad local optima. The hierarchical construction that drives the exponentially small success probability is elegant and nontrivial.

Significance
These results have important implications for both theory and practice. They show that even in highly benign GMM settings, the population likelihood can be treacherous and random restarts of EM/gradient EM can be ineffective unless repeated exponentially many times. This underscores the necessity of careful initialization and motivates further study of algorithms with provable global guarantees. The insights also inform related nonconvex problems such as k-means and other mixture models.

Strengths
- Resolves an open question about the existence of bad local maxima in population GMM likelihoods.
- Provides strong algorithmic consequences, including exponentially small success under random initialization.
- Clarifies that failures are due to local maxima rather than strict saddle points.
- Clear exposition with useful intuition and well-motivated assumptions.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope: All results are in the population setting with equal weights and identity covariances; discussion of extensions to unequal weights or general covariances would strengthen the impact.
- Dimensionality: The counterexample is in d = 1; while the constructions extend in spirit, an explicit higher-dimensional example or a clear statement about generalization would help. The paper mentions a lemma whose extension to d > 1 would be interesting; elaborating on this would be useful.
- Practical guidance: Since the negative results hinge on random initialization, it would be valuable to discuss effective initialization schemes beyond citing moment-based methods, and to quantify when they overcome the failure modes identified here.
- Finite-sample perspective: Although standard arguments can carry population results to large-n regimes, a brief finite-sample corollary or empirical illustration would make the practical message more concrete.
- Technical details: Clarify the precise step-size conditions for first-order EM and whether analogous non-convergence-to-saddles can be shown for standard EM.
- Presentation: Fix reference formatting and minor typographical issues.

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves a notable open conjecture and advances understanding of GMM likelihood geometry.
- Delivers sharp, rigorously proved algorithmic consequences with clear practical implications.
- Well written and likely to influence subsequent research on nonconvex estimation and EM.

Arguments against acceptance
- Results rely on idealized assumptions (population, equal weights, identity covariances) and specific constructions; broader generality or empirical validation is limited.
- Some proofs central to the algorithmic consequences are deferred, and higher-dimensional extensions are not fully developed.

Overall assessment
Strong theoretical paper with clear contributions to our understanding of EM and GMM likelihood landscapes. Despite the idealized setting, the insights are important and timely. I lean toward acceptance, with minor revisions to improve clarity and discussion of scope and practical implications.
