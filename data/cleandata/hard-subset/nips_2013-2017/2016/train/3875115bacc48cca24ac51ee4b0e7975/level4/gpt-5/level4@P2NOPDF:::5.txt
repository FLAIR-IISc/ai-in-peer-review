This paper resolves the open question posed by Srebro (2007), “Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?”, by constructing a broad family of counterexamples showing that spurious, suboptimal local maxima do exist at the population level. The authors begin by clearly outlining how likelihood is optimized for Gaussian mixture models, discussing both EM and gradient EM, and addressing the practical issue of initialization, including a commonly used random scheme.

They then present their main theorems, which furnish the counterexamples to Srebro’s question. Importantly, they further prove that, even under standard random initialization, both EM and gradient EM converge to these suboptimal local maxima with exponentially high probability. To build intuition, the paper works through a simple illustrative case (k = 3, d = 1) before generalizing to the full result.

The exposition is clear and well structured, striking a good balance between brevity and the provision of intuition for the technical arguments. The result is significant: it sharpens our understanding of the challenges posed by non-convex optimization, showing that difficulties persist even in the infinite-sample (population) limit for seemingly simple distributions.

One suggestion for strengthening the paper concerns the quantitative aspects of the probability bounds. I did not carefully verify the supplementary material, but explicit estimates of the constants in these bounds would be valuable. This is particularly relevant because, in practice, k is rarely taken to be very large; concrete constants would help indicate when we should expect convergence to bad solutions in the population limit (i.e., where the phase transition occurs). If the constants are complicated or not easily interpretable, supporting simulations would be helpful to demonstrate that the phenomenon is observable in realistic regimes.

Overall, these comments are minor and do not detract from the novelty, clarity, or impact of the work.
