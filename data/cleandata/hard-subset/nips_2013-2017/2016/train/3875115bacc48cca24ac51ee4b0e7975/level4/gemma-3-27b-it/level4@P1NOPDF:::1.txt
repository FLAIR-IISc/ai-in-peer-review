The paper studies the convergence of the EM algorithm – and, more broadly, iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation – when learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians. It presents compelling results demonstrating non-convergence to global maxima: first, even with three well-separated clusters and an infinite number of samples, algorithms like EM can become trapped in local optima; second, for mixtures of *k* Gaussians, random initialization for EM succeeds in finding the global optimum with at most exp(-Ω(*k*)) probability; and third, gradient EM does not generically converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that poor local maxima are the typical cause of failure. The paper presents a coherent set of results that collectively show EM’s susceptibility to getting stuck in suboptimal solutions, even with infinite data. 

However, I found the results somewhat unsurprising, and the techniques employed largely follow established lines of reasoning. The primary illustrative example involves a mixture with three components: two clustered closely together, and a third significantly distant. The authors demonstrate the existence of a local maximum where one center is positioned near the first two components, while two centers gravitate towards the isolated cluster. This behavior is fairly predictable, especially when the centers are separated by a distance much greater than √*d*. Under such strong separation, cluster overlap diminishes, and learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians begins to resemble *k*-means clustering. Similar problematic instances are well-known for Lloyd’s algorithm in the context of *k*-means. This example is extended to *k* components through a clever recursive construction – a non-trivial undertaking, but again, one that feels relatively intuitive. The failure of random initialization is also explained by similar phenomena observed in *k*-means clustering, with a matching failure probability. (This observation motivates techniques like initializing *k* log *k* centers or employing distance-squared sampling for *k*-means.)

In conclusion, while the results present a cohesive narrative regarding non-convergence to global optima, I believe the findings largely align with expected behavior.

Comments:

1. Kumar and Kannan provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic for *k*-means clustering (and, consequently, for mixtures of Gaussians under conditions of sufficient separation). While EM differs from Lloyd’s heuristic when the Gaussians are not well-separated (as highlighted by Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu), the two algorithms appear essentially equivalent in scenarios with large separation – a characteristic of the examples presented here. Therefore, I believe a comparison with *k*-means and Lloyd’s heuristic, explicitly contrasting these results, would be highly valuable.
