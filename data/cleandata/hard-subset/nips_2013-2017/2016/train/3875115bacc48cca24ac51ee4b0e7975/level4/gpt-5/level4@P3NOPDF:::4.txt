Summary
This paper analyzes the geometry of the (population) log-likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models with equal mixing weights and identity covariance. It establishes three main results:
1) A counterexample (k = 3, d = 1) showing the existence of suboptimal local maxima whose likelihood can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum.
2) For a carefully constructed configuration of the true means, random initialization leads to convergence to a bad local maximum with high probability; equivalently, the probability of avoiding such bad local maxima can decay exponentially in k.
3) Gradient EM almost surely does not converge to saddle points of the log-likelihood.

Technical quality
I carefully checked the proof of Theorem 1 in the main text and found it sound. The proof strategy for Theorem 2, as outlined and motivated by the analysis of Theorem 1, appears reasonable, although I did not verify all details in the appendix. The three main theorems are clearly stated and well interpreted, with helpful intuition connecting the mean configuration to the structure of local optima. Assumptions are stated cleanly (equal weights, identity covariance), and the arguments are coherent within this setting.

Novelty and originality
The paper offers an original and insightful perspective by tying the configuration of component means to the local maxima of the GMM log-likelihood. While the intuition that geometry drives optimization landscapes is broadly expected, the formalization here—especially the hierarchical grouping idea underpinning Theorem 2 for k > 3—is clever and, to my knowledge, novel in this specific context. The explicit counterexample demonstrating arbitrarily bad local maxima in a simple (k = 3, d = 1) setting is instructive and sharpens our understanding of potential EM failures beyond folklore.

Potential impact and usefulness
- Theory: The results contribute to a clearer picture of nonconvex likelihood landscapes in mixture models and suggest directions for analyzing related clustering objectives, such as k-means. The hierarchical structure highlighted by the authors could inspire general frameworks for characterizing local optima in other mixture or clustering models.
- Practice: The findings underscore the importance of careful initialization—e.g., ensuring adequate seeding within each true cluster—and may inform the design of improved initialization schemes or continuation methods that avoid pathological basins. It would also be interesting to test whether the phenomenon proven in Lemma 7 for d = 1 extends to higher dimensions (d > 1), which would broaden practical relevance.

Clarity and presentation
The paper is well written and well organized. The problem setup is clearly introduced, the assumptions are transparent, and the main ideas are conveyed with useful intuition alongside formal statements. The interpretations following each theorem help the reader understand both the scope and the implications of the results. Figures/diagrams (if any) and examples that illustrate the constructed configurations are particularly valuable; expanding these slightly could further improve accessibility for a broader audience.

Weaknesses and limitations
- The results are developed under equal mixing weights and identity covariance. While this is a natural first step and standard in theoretical analyses, it limits immediate generality. A brief discussion of which arguments might extend to unequal weights or non-identity covariances would strengthen the paper.
- The key negative results hinge on specific (albeit illuminating) mean configurations. Some commentary on how “robust” these phenomena are to perturbations of the construction would help gauge practical prevalence.
- The statement that gradient EM avoids saddle points almost surely is useful but perhaps expected under standard smoothness and random initialization assumptions; clarifying the exact conditions (e.g., strict saddles, initialization distribution) would make the contribution sharper.

Suggestions
- Provide a short discussion on whether the hierarchical grouping perspective can be leveraged to design initialization heuristics or multi-start strategies that provably avoid the bad basins identified in Theorem 2.
- Comment on the plausibility of extending Lemma 7 and related arguments to d > 1 and to mild covariance heterogeneity (e.g., spherical with different scales).
- Clearly specify the randomness with respect to which “almost sure” statements are made (initialization only, or data as well, if applicable).

Overall assessment
This is a clear, technically solid, and insightful paper that advances our understanding of the nonconvex landscape of GMM log-likelihoods. The counterexample and the exponentially small success probability under random initialization are both compelling and consequential, and the connection to hierarchical mean structure is elegant. Subject to minor clarifications and broader discussion of generality, I view this as a strong and impactful contribution.
