The paper examines the convergence of the EM algorithm and iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation when learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, presenting several key results that demonstrate non-convergence to global maxima. Specifically, the authors show that: (1) even with infinite samples and well-separated clusters (consisting of three components), algorithms such as EM can become stuck in local optima; (2) for mixtures of k Gaussians, random initialization for EM only succeeds with a probability of at most exp(-Ω(k)); and (3) Gradient EM typically does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that bad local maxima are the primary issue. The paper presents a cohesive set of results indicating that EM does not converge to global maxima, even with infinite samples. However, I found the results to be somewhat intuitive, and the techniques employed follow expected lines.

The main counterexample presented is a mixture with three components, where two are closer together and the third is far apart from both. The authors demonstrate that for the log-likelihood objective, a local maximum exists with one center near the first two components and two centers near the far-off cluster. This behavior is fairly predictable, particularly when the centers are separated by a distance significantly greater than √d. Under such conditions, learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians becomes similar to k-means, and similar examples have been shown to provide bad instances of Lloyd's algorithm for k-means clustering. The authors extend this example to k components using a recursive construction, which, although requiring some effort, is somewhat expected. Similarly, the failure of random initialization can be attributed to reasons analogous to those in k-means clustering, with a comparable failure probability (motivating the selection of k log k centers or distance-squared sampling in the context of k-means).

In conclusion, while the results collectively tell a coherent story about the non-convergence to global optima, I believe that this narrative follows a somewhat anticipated trajectory. 

Comments: 
1. Kumar-Kannan provides convergence guarantees for Lloyd's heuristic for k-means clustering (and, by extension, mixtures of Gaussians under sufficient separation conditions). Although EM differs from Lloyd's heuristic when the Gaussians are not sufficiently separated (as in Balakrishnan-Wainwright-Yu), they appear to be equivalent when there is large separation (consistent with the counterexamples presented). A comparison with k-means (and Lloyd's heuristic) that contrasts this result would be valuable, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the convergence properties of EM in this context.
