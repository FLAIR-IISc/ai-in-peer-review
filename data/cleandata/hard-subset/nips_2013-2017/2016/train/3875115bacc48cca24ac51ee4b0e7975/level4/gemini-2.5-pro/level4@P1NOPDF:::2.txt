This paper analyzes the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters of a Gaussian mixture model. In the setting considered, the number of components, mixture weights, and covariance matrices are assumed to be known and fixed. The paper is theoretical and proves three negative results concerning the EM algorithm:
1) Even with a large sample size, the likelihood function can have local maxima with values arbitrarily lower than the global maximum.
2) When randomly initialized, the EM algorithm converges with high probability to a critical point whose likelihood is arbitrarily lower than the global maximum.
3) A similar negative result holds for the gradient EM algorithm.

The research question addressed by this paper is both interesting and important. To facilitate rigorous proofs, the analysis is restricted to a simplified Gaussian mixture model where the number of components is known, the weights are uniform, and the covariance matrices are the identity matrix. These simplifications are reasonable; the proofs are already quite technical, and the resulting insights are nonetheless valuable. In my opinion, these are very interesting results that contribute to a better understanding of the practical behavior of the EM algorithm.

A point of clarification is needed regarding the dimensionality. While the paper is framed as addressing the general d-dimensional case, the proofs are presented only for d=1. The authors should comment on whether these proofs can be extended to an arbitrary dimension d. If not, a brief discussion of the additional difficulties encountered in higher dimensions (d > 1) would be beneficial.

My main concern pertains to the proof of Theorem 1 (I note that I did not review the supplementary material). I found the proof to be overly concise and would recommend expanding it, even if it requires shortening other discussion sections. Specifically, the section from line 288 to 306 requires more detail. Here, the authors provide the limits of likelihood function supremums over certain domains as parameters (gamma or R) go to infinity. While I can accept the computation of limits for the likelihood function evaluated at fixed parameters—and thus believe the three equations in line 288 are likely correct—the subsequent steps are less clear. Deriving the limit of a supremum is not straightforward, especially when the domain is unbounded or depends on parameters that are themselves approaching infinity. Similarly, the authors' reference to the continuity of the likelihood function is insufficient, in my opinion, to justify the stated convergences without further argument. To be fully convinced of the validity of Theorem 1, the reader needs a more detailed and expanded proof.

Finally, I have a minor concern regarding the section on Gradient EM (lines 162 to 172), which I found more difficult to follow than the rest of the paper. In particular, the authors could provide a clearer explanation for the derivation of Equation (5).
