- Paper analyzes population log-likelihood for spherical, equally weighted GMMs (M ≥ 3) and shows existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima and algorithmic failure of EM/first-order EM with probability ≥ 1 − e−Ω(M).
- Shows first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, so failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.
- Complements prior positive results for EM with good initialization and contrasts with moment/spectral methods offering global guarantees; connects to gradient-dynamics saddle-avoidance literature.
- Strength: Resolves Srebro (2007) by constructing arbitrarily bad local maxima for well-separated, uniform spherical GMMs.
- Strength: Provides a clear recursive construction yielding sharp exponential dependence on M for random-initialization failure probability.
- Strength: Carefully distinguishes convergence to saddles vs. suboptimal maxima using modern dynamical-systems results.
- Strength: Practically relevant message that naive random initialization can fail even in benign, infinite-data settings.
- Weakness: Construction feels expected/analogous to known k-means/Lloyd failure modes; needs deeper comparison to k-means literature (e.g., Kumar & Kannan, 2010).
- Weakness: Restrictive assumptions (uniform weights, equal spherical covariances); lacks discussion of robustness to weight or covariance heterogeneity.
- Weakness: Finite-sample implications are vague; explicit finite-n statements (rates, sample sizes) are missing.
- Weakness: Quantitative details like constants and illustrative parameter settings are not provided, reducing transparency.
- Weakness: Presentation issues—separation requirement should be formalized, constructions need clearer figures/summaries, and bibliography has formatting/placeholders.
- Quality assessment: Results appear technically sound and proofs plausible; clarify regularity assumptions for stable-manifold use.
- Clarity assessment: Generally well-written but needs the minor improvements noted (separation definition, figures, constants, references).
- Originality assessment: Novel in resolving the Srebro conjecture and proving exponential failure probability; argument about maxima vs. saddles is useful.
- Significance assessment: Important cautionary population-level negative result motivating principled initializers and informing practice.
- Pros for acceptance: Resolves open problem, sharp exponential bounds, timely connection to saddle-avoidance, and practical implications for initialization.
- Cons against acceptance: May seem unsurprising to k-means experts, restrictive assumptions, missing empirical/finite-sample validation, and minor presentation issues.
- Questions for authors: How sensitive are constructions to small weight/variance heterogeneity; can attraction basins be quantified under other initializers (e.g., k-means++); do analogous negatives hold for M=2; can explicit finite-sample bounds be given; compare failure probabilities to Lloyd’s algorithm results.
- Overall: Solid, informative theoretical paper that leans toward acceptance pending stronger discussion of k-means connections, robustness, finite-sample effects, and polished references.
