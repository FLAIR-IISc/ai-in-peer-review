This paper investigates the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), even with infinite data. The authors focus on isotropic GMMs with known scale and a well-specified model, demonstrating that arbitrarily bad local optima exist and that, under specific configurations of mixture centers – particularly well-spaced clusters – the EM algorithm will almost certainly fail to find the global optimum as the number of components increases. The paper is generally well-written, though a few minor typos are present.

My primary concern revolves around the practical relevance of these findings. While Theorem 1 establishes the existence of local modes under particular center configurations, the likelihood of encountering these configurations in real-world datasets is not adequately addressed. I would be more convinced of the paper’s practical significance if the authors presented assumptions about the distribution of true mixture centers and then demonstrated that “bad” configurations become more probable as the number of components (k) or the dimensionality of the problem increases. The authors state (lines 210-218) that Theorem 2 addresses this concern, but I find the argument unpersuasive. A clearer statement quantifying the likelihood of encountering such “bad” configurations – distinct from the likelihood of “bad” initializations – would be valuable.

My second major question concerns the chosen initialization strategy for the EM algorithm. Theorem 2 analyzes the performance of EM when initialized by randomly sampling from the data mixture. However, the scenario considered feels somewhat artificial. For example, consider generating data from three Gaussians, with two closely clustered and one separated. If each Gaussian contributes an equal number (N) of data points, 2N points will be concentrated around the first cluster, and N around the third. It seems counterintuitive, and potentially unrealistic, to initialize EM with two centers placed within the denser cluster and only one within the sparse cluster, especially given the clear separation between the groups.  The practical relevance of random initialization, particularly in clearly clustered data, warrants further discussion.

The example on lines 74-81, referencing Sresbro's conjecture for k=2, is unclear in its purpose. If it doesn't demonstrably support the conjecture, its inclusion feels unnecessary and potentially misleading. 

Regarding the requirements in the proof of Theorem 2 (lines 233-235), I understand the rationale for requirement (2), but the necessity of requirement (1) is less apparent. Is the concern that initializing a center between two clusters doesn’t *guarantee* movement toward the correct cluster? This point could benefit from clarification.

Finally, the result stating that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddlepoint (lines 267-273) lacks clear practical implications. The paper doesn’t explain whether convergence to a saddlepoint is more or less problematic than convergence to a local optimum.
