Summary
This paper answers Srebro’s 2007 question—whether the infinite-sample (population) likelihood for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) has only global maxima—by providing a negative result. The authors construct a general class of counterexamples showing that, for equally weighted mixtures of spherical, well-separated Gaussians with M ≥ 3 components, the population log-likelihood admits bad local maxima whose values can be arbitrarily worse than those of any global optimum. They then analyze algorithmic implications: with a standard random-initialization scheme (drawing initial centers from the mixture), both EM and its first-order (gradient-based) variant converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). They further establish that the first-order EM algorithm almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, implicating bad local maxima—not saddle points—as the primary obstacle. The paper introduces the EM and first-order EM procedures, discusses the practical random-initialization scheme, states the main theorems, and provides intuition via a simple case (M = 3, d = 1). The presentation balances rigor with clear intuition under tight space constraints.

Relation to prior work
The result directly addresses and refutes Srebro’s conjecture in the population setting, which has been influential in shaping intuitions about the benignity of GMM likelihood landscapes. While past work has analyzed EM’s local convergence and regions of attraction under separation assumptions, the present paper shows that, even under strong regularity (equal weights, spherical covariances, well-separated means), the global landscape contains arbitrarily bad local maxima, and random restarts with standard initialization fail with exponentially high probability.

Strengths
- Resolves a long-standing open question with a clean, compelling counterexample construction.
- Sharp algorithmic consequences: EM and first-order EM with random initialization fail with probability 1 − e−Ω(M), implying an exponential number of restarts is needed to succeed.
- Clarifies that failures are due to bad local maxima rather than strict saddle points, aligning with recent insights on gradient dynamics.
- Clear writing and organization; the M = 3, d = 1 example effectively conveys the geometric intuition behind the proofs.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- The paper states success/failure probabilities in asymptotic form (e−Ω(M)). Estimates of the implicit constants would help assess when the effect becomes pronounced in practice, especially since practitioners seldom consider very large M. If the constants are unwieldy, supporting simulations would still be valuable to illustrate where the “phase transition” to frequent convergence to bad solutions occurs.
- I did not carefully check the supplementary material; if available there, highlighting constant estimates or empirical evidence in the main text would strengthen the practical message.

Evaluation by NIPS criteria
- Quality: The claims appear technically sound and are supported by precise population-level constructions and analyses. The theorems are clearly stated, and the intuition is well explained. While I did not verify the supplementary proofs in detail, the results are plausible and carefully framed.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The exposition of EM/first-order EM and the random-initialization scheme sets up the results cleanly. The simple 1D, M = 3 example effectively illustrates the mechanisms underlying bad local maxima.
- Originality: The work is original and significant. It overturns a widely held conjecture in a favorable regime and provides new structural and algorithmic insights into non-convex likelihood landscapes for GMMs.
- Significance: High. The results recalibrate expectations for EM and related local search methods, even in idealized settings, and underscore the importance of careful initialization (e.g., via method-of-moments pilot estimators). The methodological message is likely to influence both theory and practice.

Pros
- Definitive negative resolution of an important open conjecture.
- Strong, interpretable algorithmic consequences (exponentially small success probability under random initialization).
- Clear identification of bad local maxima as the culprit, not saddle points.
- Well-written with intuitive examples.

Cons
- Lack of explicit constants in probability bounds limits practical guidance on when failures are likely for moderate M.
- No empirical illustrations; simulations could substantiate the theoretical phase transition in practice.

Overall assessment
This is a clear, well-executed paper that makes a substantial theoretical contribution. While constant estimates or simulations would enhance practical insight, the core results are novel and impactful, and the exposition is excellent.
