This paper investigates the structure of the population log-likelihood surface for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The authors focus on an idealized but fundamental setting: a well-specified mixture of M isotropic Gaussians with known, equal variance. Their primary contribution is to resolve an open conjecture by Srebro (2007), demonstrating that even in this favorable setting, the population log-likelihood function can have spurious local maxima for any M ≥ 3. They construct specific configurations of the true component means where such local maxima exist, and show that the log-likelihood values at these points can be arbitrarily worse than that of the global optimum.

A second key result demonstrates a significant algorithmic consequence of this structural property. For the GMM configurations they construct, the authors prove that the EM algorithm—when initialized by drawing centers from the mixture distribution—will converge to a suboptimal critical point with a probability that approaches one exponentially fast as M increases. Finally, the paper shows that a first-order variant of EM almost surely avoids converging to strict saddle points, which suggests that these bad local maxima, rather than saddle points, are the primary obstacle for local search algorithms in this setting.

The paper is well-written and its theoretical contributions are significant. Resolving a long-standing conjecture is a clear strength, and the analysis provides a strong cautionary tale about the pitfalls of local search algorithms like EM, even in highly idealized scenarios.

However, my main concerns relate to the practical implications of the results, which hinge on specific constructions of the true GMM parameters.

1.  **The prevalence of "bad" parameter configurations:** Theorem 1 proves the existence of bad local maxima for a specific type of GMM configuration where the true centers are grouped into well-separated clusters. While this is sufficient for a proof of existence, it raises the question of how common such configurations are in practice. The paper's impact would be strengthened if the authors could provide some argument about the likelihood of encountering such problematic structures. For example, under a reasonable prior distribution for the true centers, could the authors show that the probability of a "bad" configuration increases with the number of components (M) or the data dimensionality? The paper states that Theorem 2 addresses these concerns (lines 210-218), but it seems to address the consequences of a bad configuration rather than its likelihood of occurring. A clearer statement on this point would be valuable.

2.  **The practicality of the random initialization scheme:** Theorem 2 analyzes an initialization scheme where centers are drawn i.i.d. from the mixture distribution. In the scenarios constructed by the authors, the data would exhibit a clear multi-modal structure. For example, in a three-component mixture where two centers are close and one is far away, two-thirds of the data would form one dense cluster and one-third would form a sparse, distant one. A practitioner observing this structure would likely initialize a number of centers in each visible cluster proportional to its apparent mass. The analyzed random initialization scheme, however, could easily place centers in a way that mismatches the cluster structure (e.g., placing one center in the dense two-component cluster and two centers in the sparse one-component cluster). It would be helpful if the authors could discuss the relevance of their chosen initialization scheme in settings where the data has a clear clustered structure, which their constructions imply.

**Questions and suggestions for clarification:**

*   **Lines 74-81:** The purpose of the discussion and figure for the M=2 case is somewhat unclear. The text states that resolving this case remains an open problem, so the example seems intended to provide intuition that the conjecture *might* hold for M=2. As presented, it is not a conclusive argument. Clarifying the goal of this section would be beneficial.
*   **Lines 233-235:** In the sketch of the proof for Theorem 2, the authors describe two conditions for a "favorable" initialization. The need for condition (2) (matching the number of initialized centers to true centers within a cluster) is clear. However, the necessity of condition (1) (all initialized centers must fall in the neighborhood of a cluster) is less obvious. Is the issue that a center initialized in the space *between* two well-separated clusters will not converge correctly, or is there another reason this is an unfavorable initialization?
*   **Lines 267-273:** The result that the first-order EM algorithm avoids strict saddle points (Theorem 4) is interesting. To help the reader appreciate its significance, it would be useful to briefly explain the practical implications. For instance, is convergence to a saddle point considered more or less problematic than convergence to a bad local optimum in the context of GMM estimation? A sentence of context would clarify why ruling out saddle points strengthens the paper's main claim that bad local maxima are the primary issue.
