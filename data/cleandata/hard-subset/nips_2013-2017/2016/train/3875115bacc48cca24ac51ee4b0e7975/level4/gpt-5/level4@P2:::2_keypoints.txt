- Paper analyzes the population likelihood landscape of equally-weighted, spherical GMMs and behavior of EM / first-order EM under random initialization.
- Main theoretical results for M ≥ 3: existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population log-likelihood (even for well-separated 1D mixtures).
- Main theoretical results for M ≥ 3: EM with natural random initialization converges to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e−Ω(M).
- Main theoretical results for M ≥ 3: first-order EM (gradient EM, step size s ∈ (0,1)) exhibits analogous failure with high probability.
- Additional result: first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, implying bad local maxima (not saddles) drive algorithmic failure.
- Resolves Srebro’s COLT 2007 conjecture that population GMM likelihood might be free of spurious local maxima.
- Complements local-convergence analyses (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. 2015) by showing random restarts are exponentially unlikely to find the global optimum in constructed instances.
- Connects to recent work on gradient methods and saddle-point avoidance (Lee et al., Panageas & Piliouras), leveraging that to rule out saddle convergence here.
- Construction aligns with finite-sample pathologies (Améndola et al. 2015) but crucially shows persistence of bad maxima at the population level.
- Strength: settles a long-standing open problem by demonstrating bad local maxima in a highly favorable regime.
- Strength: provides sharp algorithmic implication that exponential restarts are needed for random initialization.
- Strength: conceptually clarifies that spurious local maxima, not strict saddles, cause failures of local ascent methods.
- Strength: techniques (geometric construction, hierarchical clustering, dynamical-systems reasoning) are likely useful for other nonconvex analyses.
- Weakness: main constructions and proofs are given in d = 1; extension to d > 1 is not fully discussed or justified.
- Weakness: Theorem 1 proof (lines 288–306) is too compressed—limit/sup interchange and unbounded parameter domains need more rigorous justification.
- Suggestion: strengthen Theorem 1 by restricting to compact neighborhoods, using Weierstrass, or employing epi-/Γ-convergence or explicit uniform bounds.
- Weakness: first-order EM derivation (lines 162–172) is hard to follow; request a self-contained derivation of equation (6) and clarity on equivalence to gradient ascent and step-size role.
- Suggestion: discuss whether negative results extend beyond the specific random initialization (sampling from the mixture) to heuristics like k-means++ or small perturbations, and note initialization regimes that can succeed (e.g., method-of-moments).
- Minor clarity requests: separate population vs sample notation early, define “strict saddle” before use, and explicitly state M ≥ 3 necessity and the open M = 2 case.
- Quality assessment: technically strong and well motivated, but main concern is brevity and limit/supremum steps in Theorem 1 that need fuller justification.
- Clarity assessment: generally clear and organized, but expand first-order EM derivation and Theorem 1 proof to improve readability.
- Originality: high — settles an open conjecture with a novel hierarchical construction.
- Significance: high — shows common EM heuristics can fail exponentially often even in benign settings, motivating principled initialization.
- Pros for acceptance: resolves notable open problem, strong theoretical and algorithmic contributions, and broadly relevant techniques.
- Cons for acceptance: core proofs are 1D with limited higher-dimension discussion, some key proof steps are terse, and clarity issues around first-order EM.
