- Paper studies convergence of EM for GMMs on the population (infinite-sample) likelihood.  
- Main result 1: Constructs a well-separated 3-component spherical GMM example with arbitrarily bad local maxima, resolving Srebro (2007).  
- Main result 2: Shows EM with random initialization converges to bad critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^(−Ω(M)) for M-component mixtures.  
- Main result 3: Proves first-order EM avoids strict saddle points, so failures are due to bad local maxima, not saddles.  
- Context: Connects to GMM identifiability (Teicher; Allman et al.) and recent EM and alternative estimation work (Balakrishnan et al., Hsu & Kakade).  
- Technical quality: Rigorous proofs, novel construction of bad maxima, and recursive extension from M=3 to general M.  
- Methodology: Innovative proof techniques applied to GMM likelihood surfaces, aligned with recent non-convex optimization advances.  
- Population setting advantage: Avoids sampling noise, yielding clean theoretical insights.  
- Clarity: Well-organized, accessible exposition with strong geometric intuition for the M=3 construction and clear recursive extension.  
- Originality: Moderate–high; addresses an open conjecture with a specific population-setting construction, though bad maxima in non-convex problems are known.  
- Significance: Important practical implication that careful initialization (e.g., method-of-moments) is necessary; however, significance limited by the specific, perhaps atypical, configuration required.  
- Weakness: Main construction (two close components, one far) is somewhat expected and resembles k-means counterexamples.  
- Weakness: Paper underdevelops comparison to k-means/Lloyd’s algorithm and related convergence guarantees (e.g., Kumar & Kannan).  
- Suggested improvement: Add a detailed comparison with k-means to clarify differences in the well-separated regime and strengthen practical relevance.  
- Recommendation: Pro-accept — technically sound, resolves an open question, and has practical implications; accept despite some expectedness and limited significance.
