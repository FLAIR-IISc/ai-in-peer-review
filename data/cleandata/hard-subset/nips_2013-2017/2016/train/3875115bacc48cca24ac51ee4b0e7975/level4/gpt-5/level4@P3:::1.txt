Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper analyzes the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 equally weighted, spherical components, and studies the algorithmic consequences for EM and a first-order (gradient) variant. The authors show:
- Structural: Even in this favorable setting and with well-separated components, the population likelihood can have suboptimal local maxima whose objective value is arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, resolving a conjecture of Srebro (2007).
- Algorithmic: With random initialization (sampling initial means i.i.d. from the mixture), both EM and first-order EM converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). Moreover, first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, so failure is attributable to bad local maxima rather than saddles.

These findings complement a line of work that provided positive results for EM and variants under good initialization and/or moderate separation (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and contrast with moment/spectral approaches that achieve global guarantees (e.g., Vempala & Wang, 2002; Hsu & Kakade, 2013; Moitra & Valiant, 2010). The paper also connects to recent results on gradient dynamics and avoidance of strict saddles (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas & Piliouras, 2016).

Strengths:
- Resolves an open conjecture (Srebro, 2007) by explicitly constructing arbitrarily bad local maxima for the population likelihood of well-separated, uniform, spherical GMMs with M ≥ 3. This is a clean and informative negative result for a canonical model.
- The recursive construction establishing exponentially small success probability for random initialization is conceptually clear and yields sharp exponential dependence on M.
- The distinction between convergence to saddles vs. suboptimal local maxima is handled carefully, leveraging modern results on gradient dynamics; this pinpoints the true obstacle for first-order EM.
- The paper’s message is practically relevant: even in benign settings and with infinite data, naive random initialization can lead EM-type methods to poor solutions with high probability, underscoring the need for sophisticated initialization.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Expectedness of constructions: The three-component configuration (two nearby, one far) and the recursive clustering reflect known failure modes analogous to those for k-means/Lloyd’s algorithm in the strong separation regime. The paper would benefit from a deeper comparison to the k-means literature—e.g., guarantees for Lloyd under separation (Kumar & Kannan, 2010) and known counterexamples—clarifying when EM and Lloyd behave similarly/differently as separation increases.
- Scope of assumptions: The results focus on uniform weights and known, equal spherical covariances. It would strengthen the contribution to discuss robustness to slight weight imbalance, mild covariance mismatch, or unknown variances (even at a qualitative level).
- Finite-sample implications: Although extensions via empirical process arguments are mentioned, providing explicit finite-n statements (rates, sample sizes at which the population pathology manifests with high probability) would clarify practical impact.
- Quantitative details: Theorems assert “arbitrarily bad” local maxima and e−Ω(M) success probabilities. Providing more explicit constants or illustrative parameter settings would increase transparency.
- Clarity and presentation: Formalize the separation requirement once (e.g., ξ = Ω(√d)), and state exactly where it is used. It would help to add a figure/intuition for the M = 3 construction (beyond the M = 2 illustration) and to summarize the recursive construction schematically. The bibliography appears to have formatting issues and placeholders; please correct and align with citations in the text.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The results appear technically sound, with clear statements and appropriate use of tools from dynamical systems for the saddle-point analysis. The high-level proof ideas are plausible and well-motivated; proofs deferred to the appendix seem substantial. Clarify any regularity assumptions required for applying the stable manifold ideas (the paper claims to avoid global smoothness).
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized. Minor improvements suggested above (separation definition, figures, constants, and cleaned-up references).
- Originality: While the failure constructions echo k-means pathologies, resolving the Srebro conjecture in the population GMM setting and establishing exponential failure probability for random initialization of EM/first-order EM are novel and valuable. The argument that first-order EM fails due to local maxima rather than saddles in this setting is also a useful clarification.
- Significance: The results inform both theory and practice, cautioning against blind use of random initialization for EM and motivating principled initializers (e.g., moment/spectral methods). The population-level negative result in a favorable regime is impactful for our understanding of nonconvex likelihood optimization.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Resolves a recognized open problem for a foundational model.
- Clear structural and algorithmic consequences, with sharp exponential probability bounds.
- Timely connection to modern saddle-point avoidance results.
- Practical message regarding initialization and multiple restarts.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Constructions and takeaways may feel unsurprising to those familiar with k-means/Lloyd failure modes.
- Assumptions are restrictive (uniform weights, spherical unit variance); limited discussion of robustness.
- Lack of empirical or finite-sample demonstrations to complement population results.
- Bibliography and minor presentation issues.

Questions for the authors:
- How sensitive are the constructions to small deviations from equal weights or identical variances? Can the results be extended qualitatively to mildly heterogeneous mixtures?
- Can you quantify the measure of the attraction basins for bad local maxima under other natural initialization schemes (e.g., k-means++, split-merge heuristics)?
- Do analogous negative results hold for M = 2 under any conditions, or can you formalize why M ≥ 3 seems necessary?
- Can you provide explicit finite-sample bounds showing persistence of the bad local maxima with high probability for large but finite n?
- Please compare and contrast your constructions and failure probabilities with known results for Lloyd’s algorithm (e.g., Kumar & Kannan, 2010) under separation.

Overall: This is a solid and informative theoretical paper that settles an open question and sharpens our understanding of EM’s limitations. Despite the somewhat expected flavor of the counterexamples, the results are well-executed and relevant. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on improved discussion of connections to k-means/Lloyd, robustness beyond the idealized setting, and polished references.
