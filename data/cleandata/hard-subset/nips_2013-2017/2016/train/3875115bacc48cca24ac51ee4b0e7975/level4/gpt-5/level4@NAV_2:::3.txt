The paper examines the presence of local maxima in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). It studies the simplest setting: isotropic Gaussian components with known variance, and a correctly specified model. The authors prove that local maxima exist even at the population level (infinite samples) and that their values can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. The analysis focuses on specific geometric arrangements, particularly when component means are grouped into well-separated clusters. In these scenarios, the authors show that with random initialization, EM will almost surely miss the global optimum as the number of components grows. The paper is clearly written, with a few typos. I have two principal concerns about the practical implications:

1) Theorem 1 demonstrates the existence of a local maximum in the population likelihood under a specific arrangement of component means. How plausible is this configuration in realistic settings? I would find the practical significance stronger if the authors posited a generative model for the locations of the true centers and showed that “bad” configurations occur with non-negligible probability—and ideally, that this probability increases with the number of components (k) or with dimension. Lines 210–218 state that Theorem 2 addresses this concern, but I do not see that it does. A precise statement quantifying how likely “bad” center configurations are, under explicit assumptions, would help. To be clear, I am referring to “bad” placements of the true means, not “bad” initializations.

2) Theorem 2 brings initialization into play. The authors initialize the component means by sampling from the mixture distribution. Under certain true configurations, EM then frequently fails to reach the global optimum. Consider three Gaussians, two of which are close and one far away. If each component generates N samples, then roughly 2N observations lie near the pair and N near the isolated component. It seems implausible in practice to initialize two centers in the region with only N points and one center in the region with 2N points—especially when the clusters are very well separated. Is random initialization a realistic baseline when the data are clearly clustered?
