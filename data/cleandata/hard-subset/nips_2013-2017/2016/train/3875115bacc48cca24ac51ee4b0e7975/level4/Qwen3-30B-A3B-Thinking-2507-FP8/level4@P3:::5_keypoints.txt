- Addresses Srebro (2007) open question on existence of bad local maxima in population likelihood for GMMs with M ≥ 3.  
- Provides a negative answer by constructing counterexamples where bad local maxima exist and can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.  
- Two main contributions: (1) structural proof of bad local maxima for M ≥ 3, and (2) demonstration that EM (and first-order EM) with random initialization converges to these bad maxima with probability ≥ 1 − e^(−Ω(M)).  
- Shows algorithmic failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points; first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles.  
- Uses a clear M=3, d=1 example to build intuition before generalizing the results.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and balances intuition with mathematical detail.  
- Situates work within relevant literature on GMM identifiability, estimation theory, and EM analyses.  
- Advances connections to non-convex optimization, contrasting with problems where all local optima are near-global.  
- Theoretical results are rigorous with novel proof techniques and careful distinction between local maxima and algorithmic failure modes.  
- Originality: resolves a long-standing open problem and extends beyond prior finite-sample or M=2 results.  
- Significance: practical implications for EM initialization—even for well-separated, equally weighted spherical Gaussians—requiring more careful initialization schemes.  
- Main limitation: lacks concrete estimates for the constant c in the probability bound 1 − e^(−cM); practical relevance depends on c.  
- Suggestion: authors could provide estimates of c for specific cases or simulations to illustrate practical regimes.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — technically sound, clearly presented, and a substantial contribution to GMM and non-convex optimization understanding.
