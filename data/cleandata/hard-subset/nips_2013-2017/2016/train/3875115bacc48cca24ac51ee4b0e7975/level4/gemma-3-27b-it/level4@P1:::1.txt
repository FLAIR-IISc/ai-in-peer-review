The paper studies the convergence of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm—and more generally, iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation—when learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians. It presents a coherent set of results demonstrating non-convergence to global optima, specifically: (1) Even with well-separated Gaussian components and an infinite sample size, algorithms like EM can become trapped in local optima; (2) For mixtures of *k* Gaussians, random initialization for EM succeeds with a probability of at most exp(-Ω(*k*)); and (3) First-order EM will not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that poor local maxima are the primary cause of failure. The paper presents a cohesive narrative highlighting the challenges in achieving global optima for Gaussian mixture models. 

However, while technically sound, the results feel somewhat expected, and the techniques employed are largely standard. The primary example illustrating this behavior involves a mixture of three components: two closely clustered, and a third significantly distant. The authors show the existence of a local maximum where one center is positioned near the first two components, and two centers near the distant cluster. This behavior is predictable, especially when the component centers are separated by a distance much larger than √*d*. Under such strong separation, learning these mixtures resembles *k*-means clustering, as the Gaussians exhibit minimal overlap.  Similar pathological instances exist for Lloyd’s algorithm in *k*-means clustering, providing a relevant point of comparison.  This example is cleverly extended to *k* components via a recursive construction, requiring considerable effort, but the underlying concept remains intuitive. Similarly, the failure of random initialization mirrors observations in *k*-means clustering, with a comparable failure probability. 

In conclusion, while the results paint a clear picture of the difficulties in achieving global optima, the overall findings are not entirely surprising. 

**Comments:**

1. Kumar and Kannan provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic for *k*-means clustering (and consequently for mixtures of Gaussians under conditions of sufficient separation). While EM differs from Lloyd’s heuristic when Gaussian components are not adequately separated (as noted in Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu [2015]), the two algorithms become comparable when components are well-separated—a condition met in the authors’ illustrative examples. A discussion comparing the current results to those for *k*-means and Lloyd’s heuristic would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs supporting the claims. However, the novelty of the findings is somewhat limited.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, but some sections, particularly those detailing the proofs, could benefit from simplification.
*   **Originality:** While the results are correct, the core ideas and techniques are not groundbreaking. The combination of these ideas and their application to EM are valuable, but not dramatically novel.
*   **Significance:** The results are important in that they provide a theoretical justification for the difficulties encountered in practice when using EM. However, the findings largely confirm existing intuitions and do not open up entirely new avenues of research.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the EM algorithm for Gaussian mixtures.
*   The results offer valuable insights into the challenges of non-convex optimization.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents its findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The results are not particularly surprising, and build on well-established ideas.
*   The connection to existing work on *k*-means clustering is not fully explored.
*   The practical impact of the findings may be limited.
