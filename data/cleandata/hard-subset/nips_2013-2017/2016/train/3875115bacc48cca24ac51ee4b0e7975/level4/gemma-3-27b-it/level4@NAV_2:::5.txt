The paper presents a resolution to the open question posed by Srebro in his 2007 work, "Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?". The authors provide a negative answer through the construction of counterexamples within a general framework. They begin by outlining standard techniques for maximizing the likelihood in Gaussian Mixture Models, including the Expectation-Maximization (EM) and gradient-based EM algorithms, and address the crucial aspect of initialization with a commonly used scheme. The core results are then presented as theorems, demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima and showing that, even with random initialization, the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to these suboptimal points with exponentially high probability. The authors complement their theoretical findings with an intuitive illustration through a simpler case (M=3, d=1). The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, effectively balancing the constraints of space with the need to convey the intuition behind the theoretical results. The addressed question is significant, offering valuable insights into challenges arising in non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic limit for relatively simple distributions. While the supplementary material wasn't thoroughly examined, providing estimates for the constants within the probability bounds would be beneficial. This is particularly relevant as the size of M impacts the probability of convergence to suboptimal solutions; estimating these constants would help identify potential phase transitions where convergence to bad solutions becomes increasingly likely. If precise analytical estimates are challenging, supporting simulations could at least demonstrate the phenomenon’s observability. However, this suggestion does not diminish the novelty or impact of the work.
