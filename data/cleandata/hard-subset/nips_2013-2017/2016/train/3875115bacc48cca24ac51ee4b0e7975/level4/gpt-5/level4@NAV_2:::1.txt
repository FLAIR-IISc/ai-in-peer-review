The paper examines the convergence behavior of EM (and related iterative MLE heuristics) for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, and establishes the following negative results at the population level: 1) Even for a 3-component, well-separated mixture with infinite samples, EM-like methods can converge to suboptimal local maxima. 2) For mixtures with k components, random initialization succeeds with probability at most exp(-Ω(k)). 3) Gradient EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points; thus, poor local maxima are the primary obstacle. The results collectively demonstrate that EM need not reach global optima (even in the infinite-sample limit).

While the contributions form a coherent narrative, the findings seem largely unsurprising, and the proof techniques follow expected lines. The central counterexample uses a 3-component mixture where two components are close to each other and the third is far away. The log-likelihood exhibits a local maximizer placing one center near the close pair and duplicating centers on the far cluster. This behavior is intuitive under strong separation (≫ sqrt(d)), where mixture learning closely mirrors k-means dynamics. Analogous pathologies are well known for Lloyd’s algorithm. The extension to k components via a recursive construction is clean and technically nontrivial, but again follows an anticipated pattern. Likewise, the failure of random initialization parallels standard k-means analyses, yielding a similar exp(-Ω(k)) success probability (motivating strategies like k log k seeding or distance-squared sampling in the k-means setting).

In sum, the paper provides a clear and consistent account of non-convergence to global optima, but the main ideas align with well-understood intuitions.

Comments: 1) Kumar–Kannan gives convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic for k-means (and hence for mixtures of Gaussians) under sufficient separation. Although EM can differ from Lloyd’s when separation is modest (as in Balakrishnan–Wainwright–Yu), in the strongly separated regime considered here they behave similarly (including on the failure cases). A more explicit comparison with k-means/Lloyd’s—highlighting similarities and differences, and contrasting these results with known positive guarantees—would substantially strengthen the paper.
