

This paper addresses a fundamental question about the likelihood surface of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically whether bad local maxima can exist in the population (infinite-sample) likelihood for well-separated mixtures. The authors resolve an open question posed by Srebro (2007), who conjectured that for GMMs with M ≥ 3 components, the population likelihood function would have no bad local maxima in the well-separated case. The authors provide two main contributions: (1) they construct an example of a uniform mixture of M ≥ 3 well-separated, isotropic Gaussians where the population likelihood has arbitrarily bad local maxima (Theorem 1), and (2) they show that with random initialization, the EM algorithm and its first-order variant converge to these bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)) (Theorem 2). They further prove that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely (Theorem 4), suggesting that the poor performance is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper builds upon previous work on GMMs, including Srebro's conjecture (2007), Balakrishnan et al.'s (2015) local convergence analysis for two Gaussians, and Dasgupta and Schulman's (2007) two-round EM algorithm for well-separated components. It makes a significant contribution by resolving a long-standing open question in the theoretical understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs. The construction of bad local maxima is elegant and carefully reasoned. The theoretical results are well-supported by mathematical analysis, and the authors are careful to address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

*Originality:* This work directly addresses a specific open question in the field that has been unresolved for over a decade. The construction of bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 components is novel and significant. The connection between the existence of bad local maxima and the failure of random initialization for EM represents a new insight.

*Significance:* The results have important practical implications for the use of EM in GMM estimation. By showing that even in the idealized setting of well-separated, isotropic Gaussians with random initialization, EM is likely to converge to suboptimal solutions, the paper provides a theoretical justification for more sophisticated initialization strategies. This is particularly relevant for practitioners who rely on EM without careful initialization.

*Clarity:* The paper is well-written with clear definitions and logical flow. The main results are presented concisely, and the proofs are detailed in the appendix. The paper effectively communicates complex mathematical concepts with appropriate notation and structure.

**Weaknesses:**

*Practical relevance:* The authors construct pathological configurations where centers are divided into well-separated clusters (e.g., two centers close together and the rest far apart). The main concern is whether such configurations are likely to occur in real-world applications. While Theorem 2 shows that random initialization fails for these specific configurations, the paper doesn't establish how common these configurations are in practice. The authors should clarify whether they believe these configurations are typical or rare in real data, and provide some intuition about their likelihood under natural data-generating processes.

*Example for M=2:* The example in lines 74-81 appears to be intended to show that Srebro's conjecture holds for M=2, but it doesn't convincingly demonstrate the absence of bad local maxima. The purpose of this example is unclear and could be better justified.

*Minor issues:* Several grammatical and notational issues need correction (e.g., "gradient EM algorithm" should be "that the gradient EM algorithm," "well-separated constant" needs clarification, and Ω(M) should be explained for readers unfamiliar with the notation).

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper resolves a long-standing open question in the field with rigorous mathematical proof. The theoretical results are significant and important for understanding the limitations of EM. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The practical relevance of the constructed pathological configurations is not sufficiently established. The paper doesn't address how likely these configurations are to occur in real-world applications, which weakens the practical impact of the findings.

**Minor revisions needed:** The authors should clarify the practical relevance of their pathological configurations, address the confusion around the M=2 example, and correct the minor grammatical and notational issues noted in the draft review.

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the likelihood surface of Gaussian mixture models. While the practical relevance of the specific pathological configurations could be strengthened with a discussion of their likelihood in real data, the theoretical results are sound and important for the field. The paper is well-written and addresses a fundamental question that has been open for over a decade.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the practical relevance concerns and clarify the minor issues noted above. The theoretical contribution is substantial and directly resolves a long-standing open question in the field.
