

The paper investigates the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models, focusing on the simplest well-specified case: isotropic Gaussian components with known scale. The authors demonstrate that local modes exist even with infinite data, and these modes can be arbitrarily poor in terms of log-likelihood difference from the global optimum. Under specific configurations where component centers form well-spaced clusters, the authors prove that random initialization causes EM to miss the global optimum almost surely as the number of components increases. The paper is well-written but contains occasional typos. I have two primary concerns regarding practical relevance:  
1. Theorem 1 establishes local modes for a specific center configuration. How likely is this configuration in practice? To strengthen practical relevance, the authors should assume a distribution for the centers and show that "bad" configurations become increasingly probable as $k$ or dimensionality grows. While L210–218 claim Theorem 2 addresses this, it does not provide a clear statement on the likelihood of such configurations (distinct from poor initialization).  
2. Theorem 2 analyzes EM with random initialization (sampling centers from the data). However, for data generated from three Gaussians (two close clusters, one distant), random initialization placing two centers in the denser cluster (with $2N$ points) and one in the sparser cluster (with $N$ points) seems implausible given clear separation. Is random initialization practically relevant when clusters are well-defined? L74–81 fails to convincingly support the Sresbro conjecture for $k=2$; its purpose is unclear.  
L233–235: Requirement (1) is unclear—why is it necessary if a center initialized between clusters might not move toward the correct cluster? L267–273: Why is proving EM avoids saddlepoints practically important? Is saddlepoint convergence more or less problematic than local optima convergence?
