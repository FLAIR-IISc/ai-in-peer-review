The paper analyzes the convergence of EM and related iterative heuristics for maximum likelihood estimation in learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, and establishes several negative results showing failure to reach global maxima:
1. Even with three widely separated clusters and infinite data, algorithms such as EM can get trapped in local optima.
2. For mixtures of k Gaussians, random initialization for EM succeeds with probability at most exp(-Ω(k)).
3. Gradient EM generically avoids strict saddle points, implying that suboptimal local maxima are typically the cause of failure.

The results form a coherent narrative demonstrating that EM does not converge to global maxima (even with infinite samples). However, they are not particularly surprising, and the techniques proceed along expected lines. The principal counterexample is a three-component mixture where two components are close to each other and the third is far from both. The authors show that, for the log-likelihood objective, there exists a local maximum with one center near the two close components and two centers near the distant component. This behavior is fairly expected, especially when the inter-center separation is much larger than sqrt(d). At such separations, clusters barely overlap and learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians closely resembles k-means. Similar constructions yield bad instances for Lloyd’s algorithm for k-means. The paper extends this example to k components via a clean recursive construction—this requires some work, but still feels expected. Likewise, random initialization fails for reasons analogous to k-means, with the same failure probability (in k-means, this motivates choosing k log k centers or using distance-squared sampling).

In summary, while the results give a clear picture of non-convergence to global optima, they largely follow anticipated patterns.

Comments:
1. Kumar–Kannan provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic for k-means (and thus for mixtures of Gaussians under sufficient separation). While EM differs from Lloyd’s when Gaussians are not well separated (as in Balakrishnan–Wainwright–Yu), they are essentially equivalent under large separation (as in the bad examples here). A direct comparison with k-means/Lloyd’s that highlights these contrasts would be very useful.
