

This paper investigates local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models under specific conditions: isotropic components with known scale, well-specified models, and infinite data. The authors demonstrate that local modes exist even asymptotically, and these modes can be arbitrarily poor compared to the global optimum in log-likelihood. Focusing on configurations where centers form well-separated clusters, they prove that random initialization causes EM to miss the global optimum with high probability as the number of components increases. The paper is well-written, though it contains minor typos.  

My primary concern is the practical relevance of the findings. **First**, Theorem 1 establishes local modes under a specific center configuration but does not address how probable such configurations are in real data. To strengthen the argument, the authors should specify assumptions about center distributions (e.g., uniform or Gaussian) and demonstrate that "bad" configurations become increasingly likely as \(k\) (number of components) or dimensionality \(d\) grows. While Theorem 2 references this concern (L210–L218), it does not provide a probabilistic analysis of configuration likelihood—only analyzing behavior *given* such configurations. A clear statement quantifying the probability of encountering "bad" center arrangements under plausible assumptions would significantly bolster the paper’s practical significance. (Note: This concerns the *true center positions*, not initialization.)  

**Second**, Theorem 2 analyzes EM with random initialization (centers sampled from the data). However, the practicality of this initialization is questionable for clearly clustered data. Consider a scenario with three Gaussians: two closely spaced clusters (each with \(N\) points) and one isolated cluster (\(N\) points). Here, random initialization placing two centers near the dense cluster (with \(2N\) points) and one near the sparse cluster seems implausible, especially given the strong separation. The paper should address whether random initialization remains reasonable when clusters are well-defined. The example in L74–81 fails to convincingly support the Sresbro conjecture for \(k=2\); its purpose is unclear and does not advance the main argument.  

**Additional clarifications needed**:  
- L233–235: Requirement (1) is redundant. If a center is initialized between two clusters, it may still converge to the correct cluster; why is avoiding such initialization necessary?  
- L267–273: The result on saddle-point avoidance is theoretically interesting but lacks practical justification. Is convergence to a saddle-point *more* or *less* problematic than convergence to a local optimum in practice? The paper should clarify this.  
- L218: "Well-separated constant" is ambiguous; define this term.  
- L220: "initialized by the the random initialization" → correct to "by random initialization."  
- L225: \(\Omega(k)\) requires standard asymptotic notation clarification.  
- L245: "unfortunate coincident" → "unfortunate coincidence."  
- L249: "Recall for uniform weighted" → "Recall that uniform weights..."  

**Minor corrections**:  
- L18: "We further show gradient EM algorithm" → "We further show that the gradient EM algorithm..."  
- L38: "quality of the estimate" → "quality of the estimates" (plural).  
- L60: "to characterize when it is that efficient algorithms..." → "to characterize under which conditions efficient algorithms..."  
- L138: Clarify "O(d^{1/2})-separated" means all means are \(c\)-separated with \(c = O(d^{1/2})\).  
- L172: The sentence is redundant; remove or rephrase.

- L308: "And claim when" → "And claim that..."  

**Overall assessment**: The theoretical contributions are sound and well-presented, but the paper’s practical impact hinges on demonstrating that the problematic configurations are not pathological. Without probabilistic analysis of center arrangements under realistic data-generating processes, the implications for real-world applications remain speculative. Addressing the concerns above would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance pending these clarifications.
