Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the means in a Gaussian mixture model (GMM) under a simplified but standard setting where the number of components is known, the component weights are known and equal, and all covariances are the identity. The analysis is carried out at the population (infinite-sample) level. The authors establish three negative results: (i) even for uniformly weighted, well-separated, spherical mixtures, the population log-likelihood can have local maxima whose values are arbitrarily worse than the global optimum; (ii) EM with random initialization converges with high probability to a suboptimal critical point with arbitrarily poor likelihood; and (iii) an analogous failure holds for the gradient/first-order EM method. These findings address a long-standing question, providing a negative resolution to Srebro (2007)’s conjecture about the absence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood for such mixtures, and they complement prior work analyzing EM’s local convergence under good initialization.

Strengths:
- The paper tackles a fundamental and timely question about the geometry of the GMM population likelihood and the algorithmic behavior of EM and its first-order variant.
- The results are sharp and informative: they show that bad local maxima not only exist but can be arbitrarily suboptimal, and that random initialization can lead to failure with high probability.
- The modeling simplifications (known M, uniform weights, identity covariances) are reasonable for obtaining clean theoretical insights, and the proofs are already technically involved.
- The work provides practical takeaways about the necessity of careful initialization for EM, even under favorable mixture conditions.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Scope across dimensions: although the paper presents the setup in general dimension d, the proofs focus on d = 1. It would help to clarify whether similar constructions or proof techniques extend to higher dimensions, and if not, to briefly outline what additional obstacles arise for d > 1.
- Proof details for Theorem 1: I found the proof too terse (I did not read the supplementary material). In particular, the argument in lines 288–306 relies on limits of suprema of likelihoods over domains that are unbounded and/or depend on parameters tending to infinity (e.g., as γ or R → ∞). While pointwise likelihood limits at fixed parameters are straightforward (so the three equations around line 288 are plausible), passing limits through maxima over such domains is not immediate. Continuity alone does not justify the stated modes of convergence in my view. Expanding these steps to make the limit/supremum interchanges rigorous would greatly strengthen the presentation of Theorem 1.
- Gradient EM section: the discussion in lines 162–172 was harder to follow than the rest. In particular, a clearer derivation of Equation (5) would be useful.

Quality:
- The technical development appears solid overall, but key steps in Theorem 1’s proof need more detail to convincingly justify the limiting arguments over varying/unbounded domains. The gradient EM section would also benefit from additional exposition (e.g., explicit derivation leading to Equation (5)).

Clarity:
- Generally well written and well organized. The two areas noted above (Theorem 1’s limiting arguments; gradient EM derivation) are less clear and would benefit from expansion.

Originality:
- The results are novel: they give a definitive negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture in a favorable regime and show high-probability failure of random initialization for both EM and first-order EM.

Significance:
- The findings are important for both theory and practice. They reshape our understanding of the GMM population likelihood landscape and caution against relying on naive random initialization for EM, motivating principled initialization strategies.

Suggestions for the authors:
- Clarify the dimensionality issue (general d versus d = 1): indicate whether the constructions/proofs can be generalized, or describe the barriers in higher dimensions.
- Expand the proof of Theorem 1, especially the steps involving limits of suprema over unbounded or parameter-dependent sets (lines 288–306), to make the limit operations precise.
- Add more exposition to the gradient EM discussion (lines 162–172), including a more explicit explanation of how Equation (5) is obtained.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves an open question (Srebro, 2007) with clear structural insights.
- Demonstrates substantial algorithmic consequences, showing high-probability failure of random initialization for EM and its first-order variant.
- Provides practically relevant guidance on the importance of initialization.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Theorem 1’s proof, as presented, is too concise at critical steps involving limiting arguments; more rigorous justification is needed in the main text.
- Claims and presentation are framed for general d, but proofs are for d = 1; the gap and potential extensions are not discussed.
- Parts of the gradient EM exposition (including the path to Equation (5)) are hard to follow and need clarification.
