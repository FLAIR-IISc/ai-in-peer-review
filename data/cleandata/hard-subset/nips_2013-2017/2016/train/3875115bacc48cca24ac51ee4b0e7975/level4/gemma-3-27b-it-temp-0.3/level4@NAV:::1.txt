The paper investigates the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, and more broadly iterative methods for Maximum Likelihood Estimation, when applied to learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians. The core findings demonstrate a failure to consistently converge to global optima, specifically showing: (1) even with well-separated clusters (k=3) and an infinite sample size, EM can become trapped in local optima; (2) for k Gaussian mixtures, random initialization of EM yields a success probability of at most exp(-\Omega(k)); and (3) gradient-based EM generally does not converge to strict saddle points, indicating that poor local maxima are the primary obstacle to optimal solutions. The work presents a logically sound and interconnected set of results establishing the non-convergence of EM to global maxima, even in the limit of infinite data. 

However, the results presented feel largely unsurprising, and the employed techniques are generally in line with established approaches. The primary illustrative example involves a 3-component Gaussian mixture where two components are closely positioned, and the third is significantly distant. The authors demonstrate the existence of a local maximum where one center is located near the first two components, while the remaining two centers cluster around the distant component. This behavior is predictable, particularly when the inter-cluster separation exceeds approximately the square root of the dimensionality (>> \sqrt{d}), as the problem then closely resembles k-means clustering due to minimal cluster overlap.  Analogous examples are known to produce unfavorable instances for Lloyd’s algorithm in k-means. This example is then generalized to k components via a recursive construction – a non-trivial effort, but one that follows a reasonably anticipated direction. The observed failure of random initialization mirrors similar behavior in k-means clustering and exhibits a comparable failure probability.  

In summary, while the paper articulates a cohesive narrative regarding the non-convergence to global optima, the conclusions largely align with existing expectations. 

Comment: The work of Kumar and Kannan provides convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic in k-means clustering (and consequently for mixtures of Gaussians under conditions of sufficient separation). While EM differs from Lloyd’s heuristic when Gaussian separation is limited (as highlighted by Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu), the two algorithms behave similarly when separation is large – a characteristic of the presented examples. A comparative discussion with k-means and Lloyd’s heuristic, explicitly contrasting these results, would significantly strengthen the paper.
