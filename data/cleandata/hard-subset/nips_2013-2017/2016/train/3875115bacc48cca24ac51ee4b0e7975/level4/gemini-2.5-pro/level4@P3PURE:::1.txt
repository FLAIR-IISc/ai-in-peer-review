This paper studies the convergence of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians and presents a set of negative results demonstrating its failure to converge to global maxima. The main contributions are:
1.  Even for a simple mixture of three well-separated components and with infinite samples, algorithms like EM can converge to a suboptimal local maximum.
2.  For mixtures of k Gaussians, random initialization succeeds with a probability of at most exp(-Ω(k)).
3.  For Gradient EM, convergence is generically not to strict saddle points, which implies that spurious local maxima are the primary obstacle.

The paper presents a coherent set of results showing that EM does not converge to the global maximum, even in the infinite sample setting. However, the results and the techniques used to derive them are not very surprising and follow somewhat expected lines.

The primary counterexample involves a mixture of three components where two are relatively close to each other and a third is far from both. The authors show that the log-likelihood objective has a local maximum where one learned center is positioned near the two close true components, while two learned centers are placed near the single distant component. This behavior is fairly expected, particularly in the regime considered where the centers are separated by a distance much greater than sqrt(d). Under such large separation, the clusters have minimal overlap, and the problem of learning a spherical Gaussian mixture becomes qualitatively very similar to k-means clustering. Indeed, analogous constructions are known to create problematic instances for Lloyd's algorithm for k-means.

This example is extended to k components via a nice recursive construction, which, while requiring effort, is also somewhat expected. Similarly, the result that random initialization fails is for reasons analogous to its failure in k-means clustering and with a similar failure probability. For k-means, this high failure probability is the motivation for more sophisticated initialization schemes like k-means++ (which uses distance-squared sampling).

To conclude, while the results tell a coherent story about the non-convergence of EM to global optima, the findings are largely parallel to known difficulties in the closely related k-means setting.

**Comments:**

1.  The paper would be strengthened by a direct comparison with the literature on k-means clustering and Lloyd's algorithm. For instance, works like Kumar & Kannan provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd's heuristic under certain separation conditions. While EM and Lloyd's algorithm differ when Gaussian components have significant overlap (as in the setting of Balakrishnan, Wainwright, & Yu), they behave almost identically under the large separation conditions used for the counterexamples in this paper. A thorough comparison and contrast with these results would help to better contextualize the paper's contribution.
