This paper resolves Srebro’s 2007 question “Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?” by constructing a general class of counterexamples that demonstrate the existence of spurious (non-optimal) local maxima in the population likelihood for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors clearly outline the likelihood maximization framework for GMMs, discuss both EM and gradient EM algorithms, and address initialization strategies commonly used in practice. They then present their main theorems exhibiting the counterexamples and further show that, under standard random initialization, both EM and gradient EM converge to these non-optimal maxima with exponentially high probability. To build intuition, they work through a simple illustrative case (k=3 components in one dimension), which effectively complements the general theory.

The paper is very well written and easy to follow. It strikes a strong balance between technical rigor and accessible explanation, carefully allocating space to provide intuition alongside the formal results. The question it addresses is important: it clarifies the landscape of non-convex optimization for mixture models even at the infinite-sample (population) level, challenging overly optimistic views that the population likelihood might be benign. The convergence-to-bad-solutions result under random initialization is especially consequential for understanding the practical limitations of EM-type methods.

A suggestion that would strengthen the contribution is to provide explicit estimates of the constants in the probability bounds that quantify convergence to suboptimal maxima. This is practically relevant because, in many applications, the number of components k is not very large; knowing the constants would help identify the regimes where the “bad” basins dominate and when the issue becomes observable in practice. In particular:
- Give interpretable constants (or at least scaling laws) showing how the probability depends on k, dimension d, separation/overlap of components, and weight imbalance.
- Indicate where a qualitative “phase transition” might occur as these parameters vary.
- If the constants are complicated or do not yield clear thresholds, supporting simulations would be valuable to illustrate when and how often EM/gradient EM get trapped in suboptimal maxima in realistic settings.

These additions need not be extensive; even a brief discussion, heuristics, or small-scale experiments would make the theoretical insights more actionable and help readers relate the asymptotic population results to finite-sample behavior.

Overall, this is a strong and impactful paper. It settles an open question with a general and persuasive construction, offers meaningful implications for widely used algorithms, and presents the material with clarity. The suggestions above are primarily to enhance interpretability and practical relevance; they do not detract from the novelty or significance of the main results. I recommend acceptance.
