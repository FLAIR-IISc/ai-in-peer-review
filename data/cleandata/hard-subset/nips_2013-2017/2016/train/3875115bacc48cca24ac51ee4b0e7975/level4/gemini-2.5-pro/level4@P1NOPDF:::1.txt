This paper analyzes the convergence of the EM algorithm for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, presenting a series of negative results on its ability to find the global maximum of the likelihood function. The main contributions are:

1.  In the population setting (i.e., with infinite samples), EM can converge to a spurious local optimum even for a simple mixture of three widely separated spherical Gaussian components.
2.  For a mixture of k components, random initialization leads to a successful convergence for EM with a probability that is exponentially small in k, specifically at most exp(-Ω(k)).
3.  The paper argues that for Gradient EM, convergence to strict saddle points is not a generic behavior, implying that spurious local maxima, rather than saddle points, are the primary obstacle to finding the global optimum.

The paper presents a coherent set of results that build a compelling case for the failure of EM to converge to the global maximum, even in idealized settings. However, the central findings are arguably not surprising, and the analytical techniques follow a predictable path, closely mirroring known results for the k-means algorithm.

The primary counterexample involves a mixture of three components: two are located near each other, while the third is distant from both. The authors show that the log-likelihood objective has a spurious local maximum where one learned center is positioned between the two nearby true components, while the other two learned centers are placed near the distant true component. This behavior is not entirely unexpected, especially in the high-separation regime (centers separated by >> sqrt(d)) considered here. Under such conditions, the clusters have minimal overlap, and the maximum likelihood problem for Gaussian mixtures becomes nearly equivalent to the k-means objective. Indeed, similar constructions are known to create problematic instances for Lloyd's algorithm for k-means clustering.

The paper extends this 3-component example to a k-component mixture using an elegant recursive construction. While the construction is non-trivial, the underlying principle remains the same, making the result somewhat predictable. Similarly, the analysis showing the failure of random initialization closely parallels the reasoning for k-means clustering, yielding a similar failure probability. In the k-means literature, this high failure probability of random initialization is precisely the motivation for more sophisticated seeding strategies like k-means++ (which uses distance-squared sampling) or running the algorithm from multiple initializations.

In summary, while the paper tells a cohesive story about the non-convergence of EM, the results and the mechanisms behind them feel largely analogous to well-understood limitations of k-means clustering, particularly in the well-separated setting that the paper focuses on.

**Comments:**

A more detailed discussion of the k-means literature would significantly strengthen the paper. For instance, Kumar and Kannan (2010) provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd's algorithm under certain conditions which are related to cluster separation. While EM and Lloyd's algorithm differ in general (as noted by Balakrishnan et al., 2017), they behave almost identically in the well-separated regime that this paper's counterexamples inhabit. A discussion that contrasts the negative results presented here with the positive guarantees for k-means would provide valuable context and clarify the paper's specific contribution to understanding EM, beyond what could be inferred from the k-means analogy.
