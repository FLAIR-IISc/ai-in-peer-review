This paper provides a definitive response to a long-standing question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation, demonstrating that such maxima do not exist through the construction of a comprehensive set of counterexamples. The authors begin with a clear exposition of standard Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) likelihood maximization techniques, including the Expectation-Maximization (EM) and gradient-based EM algorithms, and discuss typical initialization procedures. They then present their central theorems, which directly address and resolve Srebro’s question [2007]. Importantly, the authors demonstrate that even with a commonly used random initialization strategy, the EM and gradient EM algorithms are highly likely to converge to suboptimal local maxima – specifically, with exponentially high probability. To aid understanding, the authors provide an intuitive illustration of their proof strategy by analyzing a simplified case (k=3, d=1). The paper is exceptionally well-written and achieves a commendable balance between theoretical rigor and intuitive explanation, effectively managing space constraints. The addressed problem is of fundamental significance, offering valuable insights into the challenges of non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic regime for relatively simple distributions. While a detailed examination of the supplementary material was not undertaken, providing estimates for the constants within the reported probability bounds would be beneficial. Understanding these constants is crucial for determining the practical relevance of the findings – specifically, identifying the conditions (e.g., the value of *k*) under which convergence to suboptimal solutions becomes significant in the asymptotic limit (i.e., characterizing a potential phase transition). If analytical estimation proves difficult, supporting simulations demonstrating this phenomenon would be valuable. Nevertheless, this minor point does not diminish the novelty or substantial impact of this work.
