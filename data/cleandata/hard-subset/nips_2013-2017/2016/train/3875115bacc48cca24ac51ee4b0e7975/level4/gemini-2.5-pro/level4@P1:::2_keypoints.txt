- Paper studies EM for GMM mean estimation in a simplified setting (known component count, uniform weights, identity covariances).  
- Main negative result: population log-likelihood can have arbitrarily bad local maxima, even for well-separated mixtures.  
- Randomly initialized EM converges to a suboptimal critical point with high probability (probability scales exponentially in the number of components M).  
- The negative result extends to a first-order EM variant (gradient ascent on the likelihood).  
- Strength: Addresses fundamental, relevant questions and provides surprising, rigorous theoretical insights challenging optimistic views of the GMM likelihood landscape.  
- Strength: Simplified model is appropriate given the technical complexity and yields informative insights for practice.  
- Weakness: Proof of Theorem 1 is too brief; the step interchanging limit and supremum (lines 288–306) needs a more careful, rigorous justification (e.g., uniform convergence).  
- Weakness: Dimensionality is unclear — constructions and proofs appear 1D; authors should clarify whether and how results extend to d > 1.  
- Weakness: The first-order EM section is hard to follow; derive Equation (6) more clearly from the Q-function and clarify the gradient-step connection.  
- Recommendation: Strengthen and expand the proof of Theorem 1 to be more transparent and self-contained.
