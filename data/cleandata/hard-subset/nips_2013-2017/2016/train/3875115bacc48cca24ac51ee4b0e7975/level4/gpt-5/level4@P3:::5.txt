Summary and relation to prior work
This paper settles an open question posed by Srebro (COLT 2007), who conjectured that in the infinite-sample (population) setting, the likelihood surface of equally weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) might be free of spurious local maxima. The authors construct counterexamples for any M ≥ 3, even with well-separated components, showing that the population log-likelihood can have local maxima whose values are arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. They further analyze the algorithmic implications: with random initialization (drawing initial centers i.i.d. from the mixture), both EM and first-order EM (gradient EM) converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). Finally, leveraging results on gradient dynamics, they show that first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, indicating that failure is driven by bad local maxima rather than saddles.

The work builds on and contrasts with classic EM analyses and alternatives for learning mixtures (e.g., clustering- and moment-based approaches: Arora et al., Vempala & Wang, Moitra & Valiant, Hsu & Kakade), as well as recent local analyses of EM (Dasgupta & Schulman; Balakrishnan et al.). It also connects to results on optimization landscapes showing gradient methods avoid strict saddles (Lee et al.; Panageas & Piliouras) and to robustness of MLE under misspecification (Donoho & Liu). In contrast to positive landscape results in other nonconvex settings (e.g., Loh & Wainwright; Wang et al.), this paper provides a clear negative landscape result for a prototypical statistical model.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The statements are precise and well-motivated. The geometric construction (for M = 3, d = 1) is intuitive, and the recursive multi-level construction underpinning the e−Ω(M) failure probability is plausible and carefully argued at the population level. I did not fully verify all proofs in the appendix.
- Claims and support: The structural counterexample directly refutes Srebro’s conjecture. The algorithmic consequences are derived under a practical random-initialization scheme consistent with how EM is often used. The strict-saddle non-convergence result for first-order EM is appropriately justified via recent dynamical systems insights.
- Scope of results: The analysis is at the population level; the authors state that finite-sample extensions follow by standard arguments, but the paper would benefit from a brief, explicit statement of sample complexity requirements and stability to sampling noise.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The authors balance proofs with intuition (e.g., the 1-D, M = 3 illustration). Some minor issues remain in the bibliography formatting and a few typos. The notion of “well-separated” is defined, but providing explicit constants in key statements would further help readers assess when the phenomenon appears.

Originality
The work is original and significant: it resolves a long-standing conjecture by giving explicit, well-separated counterexamples and quantifies the failure probability of widely used algorithms under natural initialization. The strict-saddle discussion for first-order EM sharpens the narrative about the source of difficulty.

Significance
These results have important implications for practice and theory. They demonstrate that even in benign GMM settings, random restarts of EM/gradient EM may require exponential effort to find good optima, underscoring the necessity of informed initialization (e.g., via moments or clustering) and guiding future analyses of likelihood-based methods for mixtures.

Strengths
- Resolves a well-known open problem with a clean, constructive counterexample.
- Clear algorithmic consequences: exponential-in-M failure probability for random-initialized EM/first-order EM.
- Conceptual clarity: distinguishes bad local maxima from saddle-point issues via a no-convergence-to-strict-saddles result for first-order EM.
- Intuitive geometric explanations that complement the formal proofs.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Constants and quantitative thresholds: Provide explicit dependence of the separation parameter, the gap Cgap, and the universal constant c in the e−cM bound. Concrete constants would help readers gauge when the phenomenon is observable for moderate M.
- Finite-sample guidance: Include a short corollary outlining sample sizes under which the population results transfer (with high probability), and how sampling fluctuations affect the constructed landscape.
- Empirical illustration: A small simulation demonstrating the bad local maxima and the decay in success probability with M would make the results more tangible and help assess constants.
- EM vs. first-order EM: The strict-saddle avoidance is shown only for first-order EM. While you conjecture an analogous result for EM, a brief discussion of technical obstacles or a partial result would strengthen the paper.
- Scope of constructions: Comment on extensions beyond equal weights and unit variances. Do similar bad maxima exist under mild heterogeneity in weights/covariances?
- Positive frontier: Since M = 2 appears special, adding a formal statement of what is known (and open) for M = 2 would contextualize the boundary between positive and negative results.

Minor comments
- Fix typos and reference formatting (e.g., placeholders, question marks, inconsistent citation styles, “th Conference,” small grammatical errors).
- Clarify the E-step/M-step indices and notational consistency.
- In Figure 1, explain the negative gradient-norm plot and its interpretive purpose.
- State the precise stepsize conditions for first-order EM and whether backtracking/line-search alters conclusions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves a notable open question with crisp structural and algorithmic results.
- High potential impact on how practitioners and theorists approach EM for GMMs, especially regarding initialization.
- Well written, with clear intuition and connections to contemporary optimization theory.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of empirical illustration and explicit constants may limit immediate practical uptake.
- Results are population-level and worst-case; the breadth of their manifestation in typical data scenarios is not explored.
- The strict-saddle result is limited to first-order EM; no analogous theorem for EM proper.

Overall assessment
A strong theoretical contribution that answers a long-standing question and clarifies the optimization landscape of GMM likelihoods. Despite some desiderata (constants, finite-sample and empirical illustrations), the paper’s novelty and clarity justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions addressing constants/finite-sample discussion and polishing the references.
