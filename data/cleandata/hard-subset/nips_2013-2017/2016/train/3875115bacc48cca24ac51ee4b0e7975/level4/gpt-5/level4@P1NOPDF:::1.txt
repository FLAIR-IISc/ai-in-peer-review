This paper analyzes the convergence behavior of the EM algorithm (and related iterative MLE heuristics) for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, and establishes several negative results on convergence to global optima:
1) Even with three well-separated clusters and infinite samples (the population setting), algorithms like EM can get trapped in local optima.
2) For mixtures of k Gaussians, random initialization of EM succeeds with probability at most exp(−Ω(k)).
3) Gradient EM generically avoids strict saddle points; thus, the principal obstacle is bad local maxima.

Overall, the paper presents a coherent set of results showing that EM need not converge to a global maximum, even with infinite data. However, I find the results largely unsurprising, and the techniques proceed along expected lines.

The central counterexample is a mixture with three components: two that are relatively close to each other and a third that is very far from both. The paper shows that for the log-likelihood objective there exists a local maximum with one center near the pair of close components and two centers near the far-away component. This behavior is fairly intuitive, especially when the separation between true centers is much larger than sqrt(d). Under such separation, clusters have negligible overlap and learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians closely resembles k-means. Analogous constructions yield bad instances for Lloyd’s algorithm in k-means clustering.

The paper extends this construction to k components via a neat recursive gadget. While this requires some care, the overall phenomenon remains expected: local maxima arise in which centers overfit certain regions and underfit others, mimicking the well-known pathologies of k-means under poor initialization.

Similarly, the failure of random initialization occurs for reasons parallel to k-means, with the same exponentially small success probability in k. This mirrors the classical motivation in k-means to select roughly k log k initial centers or to use distance-squared (k-means++) seeding to avoid placing too many initial centers in one region while neglecting others.

In sum, while the paper offers a clear and consistent narrative about the non-convergence of EM to global optima, the negative examples and failure modes align with established intuitions from k-means under large separation and random seeding.

Comment:
1) Kumar and Kannan provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic in k-means (and, by extension, for learning mixtures of Gaussians under sufficient separation). While EM and Lloyd’s can behave differently when Gaussians are not well separated (as in work by Balakrishnan–Wainwright–Yu), they are essentially similar under large separation—precisely the regime of the bad examples here. A more explicit comparison to k-means/Lloyd’s, contrasting these results with the positive guarantees of Kumar–Kannan and discussing where EM’s behavior meaningfully diverges, would strengthen the paper.
