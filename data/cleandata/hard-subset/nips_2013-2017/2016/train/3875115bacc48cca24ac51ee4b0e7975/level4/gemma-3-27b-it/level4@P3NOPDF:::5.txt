This paper addresses a fundamental open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood function for Gaussian mixture estimation. The authors definitively answer this question in the negative, constructing a general class of counterexamples demonstrating that such local maxima *do* exist. 

The paper begins with a clear and concise overview of standard techniques for maximizing the likelihood in Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), including the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and gradient-based EM methods.  A common initialization strategy is also discussed, providing crucial context for the subsequent results. The core of the paper lies in the presentation of the authors’ main theorems, which formally establish the existence of these undesirable local maxima, directly refuting the conjecture made in Srebro’s work.

Importantly, the authors go beyond simply demonstrating the *possibility* of suboptimal solutions. They show that, even when employing a standard random initialization procedure, the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to these non-optimal maxima with exponentially high probability. This is a significant result, demonstrating a practical concern for the performance of these widely-used algorithms.

To build intuition, the authors present a simplified proof for a specific case (k=3, d=1), which aids in understanding the more general theoretical arguments.  The paper is remarkably well-written and maintains a good balance between rigorous mathematical detail and intuitive explanation. The authors effectively manage the space constraints while still providing sufficient background and motivation for their findings.

The question addressed by this work is of fundamental importance. A deeper understanding of the challenges inherent in non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic limit and for relatively simple distributions like GMMs, is crucial for advancing the field of machine learning. This paper makes a substantial contribution in that direction.

My primary suggestion for improvement concerns the practical implications of the derived probabilities. While the theoretical result is strong, it would be beneficial to have some estimates of the constants involved in the exponential probability bounds. Knowing the magnitude of these constants would help determine the regimes (e.g., the number of components *k*, dimensionality *d*) where convergence to suboptimal solutions becomes likely.  Specifically, understanding the "phase transition" – where the probability of converging to a bad solution becomes significant – would be invaluable. If explicit estimates are difficult to obtain, supporting simulations demonstrating this phenomenon would be highly desirable. Observing the effect of *k* and *d* on the convergence behavior empirically would strengthen the paper's impact.

However, this is a relatively minor point. The lack of such estimates does not detract from the novelty or significance of the paper’s core contribution.  The existence of these local maxima and the demonstrated tendency of standard algorithms to converge to them are compelling results, and this paper represents a substantial advancement in our theoretical understanding of GMM estimation.
