This paper addresses the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for the estimation of the set of mean parameters for a model of mixture of Gaussian variables. The number of Gaussian variables, the weights of the mixture, and the covariance matrices of the Gaussian variables are known and fixed. The paper is theoretical and proves three negative results for the EM algorithm. 

Firstly, the authors show that there can be, even for large sample size, local maxima for the likelihood function, with likelihood values arbitrarily smaller than the maximal likelihood value. This result resolves an open question of Srebro (2007) and highlights the necessity of careful initialization when using the EM algorithm in practice, even when applied in highly favorable settings. 

Secondly, the authors demonstrate that the EM algorithm, randomly initiated, converges with high probability to a critical point with likelihood value arbitrarily smaller than the maximal likelihood value. This result has significant implications for the practical application of the EM algorithm, as it suggests that repeated random initialization followed by local search (via either EM or its first-order variant) can fail to produce useful estimates under reasonable constraints on computational complexity. 

Lastly, the authors establish that the same negative result holds for the gradient EM algorithm. They further prove that under the same random initialization scheme, the first-order EM algorithm with a suitable step-size does not converge to a strict saddle point with probability one, indicating that the poor performance of the first-order method can be attributed to the existence of bad local maxima rather than bad saddle points. 

The question addressed by the paper is interesting and worthy of attention. The authors' decision to restrict their analysis to a simplified mixture of Gaussian variable model, where the number of components is known, the weights are known and uniform, and the covariance matrices are equal to identity, is reasonable given the technical nature of the proofs and the informativeness of the obtained results. 

However, the authors could provide more clarity on the applicability of their results to cases of general dimension d, as the proofs primarily address the case d=1. It would be beneficial to discuss the additional difficulties of the case d larger than 1 and whether similar proofs would be possible for arbitrary dimension d. 

One concern with the paper is the proof of Theorem 1, which appears to be too concise. The authors could provide more details, particularly from line 288 to 306, where they compute the limits of supremums of likelihood functions over some domains. A more detailed explanation of the computation of these limits and the types of convergences stated in the paper would enhance the validity of the proof. 

Additionally, the part of the paper addressing the Gradient EM, from line 162 to line 172, could be clarified. The authors could provide more explanation on how Equation (5) is obtained and elaborate on the underlying assumptions and reasoning. 

Overall, the paper provides valuable insights into the EM algorithm and its limitations, highlighting the importance of careful initialization and the potential pitfalls of local maxima and saddle points. With some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
