The paper investigates the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models. The authors consider a simplified scenario: isotropic Gaussian components with known scale, and a well-specified model. They demonstrate that local modes persist even with infinite data, and that the difference in log-likelihood between these local modes and the global optimum can be arbitrarily large. The analysis focuses on configurations where mixture centres are well-spaced. Under these conditions, the authors prove that, as the number of components increases, random initialization will almost certainly lead the EM algorithm to miss the global optimum. The paper is generally well-written, though contains a few minor typos. 

I have two primary concerns regarding the practical relevance of this work. First, Theorem 1 establishes the existence of local modes even with infinite data, but does so for a specific configuration of centres. A crucial question remains: how likely is such a configuration to occur in practice? I would be more convinced of the paper’s practical significance if the authors presented assumptions about the distribution of the centres, and then demonstrated that “bad” configurations become increasingly probable as the number of components (k) or the dimensionality of the problem grows. While lines 210-218 of the paper claim that Theorem 2 addresses this, I find this unconvincing. A clear statement quantifying the likelihood of “bad” configurations under reasonable assumptions would be highly valuable. Here, I refer to “bad” configurations as pertaining to the *positions of the true centres*, not merely “bad” initializations.

Second, Theorem 2 examines the impact of EM initialization. The authors consider initializing component centres by randomly sampling from the mixture. Under specific configurations of centres, this initialization frequently causes EM to fail to converge to the global optimum. However, consider a scenario where data is generated by three Gaussians, two closely clustered and one isolated. If each Gaussian contributes an equal number (N) of data points, then 2N points will be near the first cluster and N near the third. It seems counterintuitive to initialize EM with 2 centres in the region of N points and only 1 centre in the region of the other N points, especially given the clear separation between the clusters. Is random initialization, as considered here, truly representative of practical data with clear clustering structure?

I also have a few specific points. Lines 74-81 provide an example that fails to convince me of the validity of Sresbro’s conjecture for k=2; the purpose of this example is unclear. Lines 233-235: I understand the motivation behind requirement (2), but the necessity of requirement (1) is not apparent. Does this imply that initializing a centre between two clusters does not guarantee convergence to the correct cluster? Lines 267-273 demonstrate that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddlepoint. However, from a practical perspective, the significance of this result is unclear. Is convergence to a saddlepoint more or less problematic than convergence to a local optimum?
