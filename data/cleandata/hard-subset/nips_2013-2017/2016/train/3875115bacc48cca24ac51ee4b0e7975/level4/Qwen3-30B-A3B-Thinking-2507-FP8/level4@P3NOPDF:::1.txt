

The paper investigates the convergence properties of the EM algorithm and related iterative methods for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, establishing three key results: (1) EM can converge to local optima even with infinitely many samples when the mixture comprises three well-separated clusters (two close together, one far away); (2) Random initialization for EM succeeds with probability at most exp(-Ω(k)) for k-component mixtures; (3) Gradient EM avoids strict saddle points generically, indicating that poor local maxima—not saddles—are the primary convergence barrier. While the results collectively demonstrate EM’s failure to converge to global optima (even asymptotically), they largely align with established intuition in clustering theory.

The central counterexample (for k=3) is well-constructed: with cluster centers separated by Ω(√d), the log-likelihood function admits a local maximum where one center aligns with the two close clusters and two centers approximate the distant cluster. This behavior is unsurprising, as such separation renders mixture learning analogous to k-means clustering, where similar failure modes are well-documented (e.g., Lloyd’s algorithm converging to suboptimal partitions when clusters are near the boundary of Voronoi regions). The extension to k components via recursive construction is technically sound but follows standard techniques in clustering literature. Similarly, the random initialization failure probability mirrors known results for k-means (e.g., requiring k log k centers via distance-squared sampling), reinforcing the expectation rather than introducing novelty.

The most substantive gap lies in contextualizing these results against the broader literature on clustering convergence. The authors cite Balakrishnan et al. (2017) on EM’s behavior under insufficient separation, yet omit a critical comparison with Kumar and Kannan’s (2004) convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s algorithm under sufficient separation (Ω(√d)). When separation exceeds √d, EM and Lloyd’s algorithm become effectively equivalent for spherical Gaussians, as clusters become non-overlapping and the Gaussian likelihood reduces to a hard assignment problem. Thus, the paper’s "bad examples" for EM are directly analogous to known counterexamples for Lloyd’s algorithm, and the failure probability for random initialization parallels k-means analysis. A direct comparison—highlighting how the paper’s results for EM complement or contrast with Kumar-Kannan’s guarantees under separation conditions—would significantly strengthen the contribution and clarify the novelty boundary. The authors should explicitly address: (i) why EM’s failure here is distinct from k-means despite the similarity in failure modes under large separation, and (ii) how their results refine or extend existing k-means insights.

While the paper’s technical execution is rigorous and the narrative coherent, the results do not advance the field beyond what is already anticipated from clustering theory. The insights are incremental rather than transformative, as they confirm expected behavior under separation conditions already studied in k-means literature. The contribution would be more compelling if it identified a *new* failure mechanism specific to EM (e.g., under moderate separation where Gaussians overlap but k-means succeeds), but the presented examples rely on the same separation regime where EM and k-means are equivalent.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a valid technical contribution to understanding EM’s limitations, but its significance is limited by the lack of novelty relative to k-means results. The authors should revise the discussion to explicitly contrast their findings with Kumar-Kannan’s work, clarify the relationship between EM and Lloyd’s algorithm under separation conditions, and refine the narrative to emphasize what (if anything) is uniquely EM-specific. This would better position the work within the existing literature and justify its submission to NIPS.
