- Paper analyzes the population log-likelihood of GMMs and EM behavior in the infinite-sample setting, highlighting fundamental challenges for local search methods.
- Result 1: Resolves Srebro (2007) by proving bad local maxima exist for M ≥ 3 well-separated, spherical, uniformly-weighted Gaussians, with arbitrarily worse likelihood than global optimum.
- Result 2: Shows EM (and first-order variants) with standard random initialization converges to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − exp(−Ω(M)), requiring exponentially many restarts to find the global optimum.
- Result 3: Proves first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddles, indicating failures are due to poor local maxima rather than saddle trapping.
- Strength: Provides a significant theoretical contribution by resolving a long-standing open problem about GMM optimization landscapes.
- Strength: Presents a coherent, comprehensive analysis linking structural landscape results to concrete algorithmic consequences for EM.
- Strength: Uses an elegant, technically sound construction and recursive extension to prove exponential failure probability.
- Weakness: Lacks sufficient contextualization with k-means literature, where analogous failure modes are well-known.
- Weakness: In high-separation regimes posteriors become hard assignments, making EM behave qualitatively like Lloyd’s k-means, which should be explicitly discussed.
- Weakness: The M=3 counterexample and exponential failure of random initialization closely mirror classic k-means failures and the motivation for k-means++; this connection is under-discussed.
- Suggestion: Explicitly compare and contrast results with k-means analyses (e.g., Kumar & Kannan) and prior GMM separation regimes (e.g., Balakrishnan et al.) to better position the contribution.
- Conclusion: Technically sound and significant results resolving an open question, but impact would improve with a clearer discussion of parallels to k-means to delineate novelty.
