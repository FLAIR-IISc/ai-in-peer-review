- Main finding: EM can converge to suboptimal local maxima for three well-separated spherical Gaussian components, even in the population setting.  
- Main finding: With k components, random initialization succeeds with probability at most exp(-Ω(k)).  
- Main finding: Gradient EM generically avoids strict saddles, so failures are due to attraction to bad local maxima, not saddle points.

- Overall: The paper gives a clear, unified narrative about EM failure modes but often parallels known k-means/Lloyd’s phenomena and needs sharper contrast with prior work.

Strengths:
- Presents clean, coherent negative results highlighting bad local maxima as the core failure mechanism.  
- Provides a simple intuitive three-component counterexample and a recursive extension to general k.  
- Clarifies the role of strict-saddle avoidance in explaining practical EM failures.

Weaknesses:
- Main counterexamples closely mirror classic bad instances for Lloyd’s/k-means in large-separation regimes.  
- The exp(-Ω(k)) bound for random initialization resembles known k-means initialization failures and feels expected.  
- Limited novelty beyond translating k-means-style phenomena to population EM and underemphasizes positive EM/Lloyd’s results under separation or good initialization.

Requests for clarification and improvements:
- Precisely formalize the “very far apart” separation condition, its scaling with dimension/variance, and robustness across separations.  
- State assumptions on equal variances and mixing weights, and robustness to small perturbations.  
- Clarify whether results hold in both population and finite-sample regimes, and required sample sizes for persistence.  
- Provide a careful comparison to Lloyd’s algorithm and existing failure modes, and explain what is uniquely EM-specific.  
- Contrast with positive guarantees for Lloyd’s under separation (e.g., Kumar–Kannan) and analyses showing EM vs. Lloyd’s divergence.  
- Quantify constants/dependencies in the exp(-Ω(k)) bound (on k, d, separation, weights) and discuss tightness.  
- Analyze whether standard remedies (multiple restarts, k-means++/k log k seeding) mitigate the constructions and whether they remain hard.  
- State precise assumptions (step sizes, smoothness/Lipschitz) for gradient EM’s strict-saddle avoidance and link to known theory.  
- Clarify which conclusions transfer between gradient EM and standard alternating E/M EM, and where they differ.  
- Add geometric intuition (with a figure) for the three-component example and explain why attraction basins produce the failure.  
- Provide more intuition for the k-component recursive construction and how attraction basins scale with k.  
- Discuss practical implications: initialization strategies, diagnostics, recommended number of restarts, and whether theory yields concrete guidelines.  
- Include small-scale empirical illustrations on synthetic data to visualize attraction basins and corroborate population-level theory.

- Significance: Useful synthesis clarifying that strict saddles aren’t the main obstacle, but much of the behavior in high-separation regimes mirrors k-means and anticipated results.  
- Recommendation: Promising and clear but limited in novelty; strengthen comparisons to k-means/Lloyd’s, tighten assumptions and robustness statements, and improve practical guidance to increase impact.
