This paper analyzes the population (infinite-sample) likelihood for mixtures of spherical Gaussians and the behavior of EM and gradient-based variants under random initialization. It establishes three core results: (1) for M ≥ 3, even in a highly favorable setting (equally weighted, spherical, well-separated components), the population log-likelihood has spurious local maxima whose values can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, resolving a conjecture of Srebro (2007); (2) with random initialization, EM (and a first-order EM/gradient-ascent variant) converges to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − exp(−Ω(M)); and (3) the first-order EM algorithm almost surely avoids strict saddle points, indicating that poor outcomes are driven by bad local maxima rather than saddles. Together, these results provide a clear structural and algorithmic picture: the population landscape can be treacherous, and naive random restarts require exponential repetitions in M to succeed.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to a substantial literature on EM’s local convergence (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and to studies of clustering/moment-based algorithms for GMMs (Arora et al., 2005; Hsu and Kakade, 2013; Moitra and Valiant, 2010). The negative results complement empirical and two-component analyses suggesting benign landscapes for M = 2. The constructions and failure modes are closely aligned with known behaviors of Lloyd’s algorithm for k-means under large separation. In particular, the basic 3-component example (two nearby means and a third far away) mirrors classical bad instances for Lloyd’s, and the recursive construction that yields exponentially small success probability echoes failure probabilities for random seeding in k-means, motivating schemes like k-means++ (D2 sampling) and oversampling (e.g., k log k). A more explicit comparison to this literature—including convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s under separation (e.g., Kumar and Kannan) and practical initialization strategies—would strengthen the paper’s positioning.

Strengths:
- Resolves a well-known open question (Srebro, 2007) with a clean, one-dimensional construction.
- Provides sharp algorithmic consequences: exponential failure probability in M for EM/first-order EM with random initialization, and almost-sure avoidance of strict saddles for first-order EM.
- Clear articulation of why initialization matters even in population settings with well-separated, spherical components.
- Techniques that may generalize to other nonconvex likelihoods.

Weaknesses:
- While important, the negative results are, to some extent, expected by analogy with k-means behavior under large separation; the core 3-component construction is intuitive.
- The exponentially small success probability hinges on a carefully crafted hierarchical instance; its frequency in natural data is unclear.
- Results are purely at the population level; practical guidance for finite samples, noise, or model misspecification is limited.
- Limited discussion contrasting EM vs Lloyd’s in the regimes where their behaviors coincide (large separation) and differ (overlap).

Quality: The results are technically sound and well motivated. The constructions are reasonable, and the arguments tying EM/first-order EM dynamics to basin-of-attraction properties are persuasive. The reliance on population analysis is appropriate for the questions at hand, though more explicit finite-sample implications (e.g., stability under sampling) would be useful.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. The motivating two-component discussion and the illustrative 3-component example aid intuition. A more direct comparison to k-means/Lloyd’s—precisely delineating where EM behaves similarly or differently under separation—would improve accessibility.

Originality: The resolution of Srebro’s conjecture is a notable contribution. The exponential failure probability with random initialization, and the strict-saddle avoidance for first-order EM, are valuable clarifications. That said, the high-level intuition aligns with known clustering pathologies, which somewhat tempers the surprise.

Significance: The results have clear implications for practice: random initialization for EM/gradient EM is unreliable in the worst case, even with infinite data, and careful initialization (e.g., method-of-moments or k-means++-style schemes) is essential. The structural insights into the likelihood landscape may inform future algorithm design and theory.

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves an open problem; tightens our understanding of GMM likelihood geometry.
- Clear, coherent narrative linking structure to algorithmic behavior.
- Relevance to a widely used algorithm (EM) and to current discussions on nonconvex optimization.

Cons against acceptance:
- Conceptual novelty is moderated by parallels to established k-means phenomena.
- Limited discussion of practical mitigations and finite-sample robustness.
- The most striking algorithmic lower bound relies on contrived hierarchical instances.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a dedicated comparison to k-means/Lloyd’s under separation, including discussion of guarantees such as those by Kumar and Kannan and practical initializations like k-means++ and oversampling.
- Clarify finite-sample implications: how large must n be for the population phenomena to manifest, and how do constants depend on separation and dimension?
- Elaborate on the step-size conditions and smoothness assumptions for first-order EM; indicate whether analogous saddle-avoidance might hold for standard EM.
- If feasible, include small synthetic experiments illustrating the failure probability trends with M and the benefit of better initialization.

Overall evaluation: A solid, well-executed negative result with clear algorithmic implications. While not entirely surprising to experts familiar with k-means pathologies, the resolution of Srebro’s conjecture and the precise population-level analysis make this a valuable contribution.
