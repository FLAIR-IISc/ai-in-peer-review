- Paper studies population EM for spherical, uniformly weighted GMMs and proves three negative results: existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima, high-probability convergence of randomly initialized EM to suboptimal critical points, and analogous failure for gradient/first-order EM.  
- Results resolve Srebro (2007)’s conjecture negatively and complement prior local-convergence analyses under good initialization.  
- Strength: tackles a fundamental question about GMM likelihood geometry and EM algorithmic behavior.  
- Strength: sharp, informative results showing bad local maxima can be arbitrarily suboptimal and random initialization can fail with high probability.  
- Strength: modeling simplifications (known M, uniform weights, identity covariances) are reasonable for clean theory; proofs are technically involved.  
- Strength: provides practical takeaway that careful initialization is necessary even under favorable conditions.  
- Weakness: proofs are presented for general d but focus on d = 1; unclear whether constructions/generalizations extend to higher dimensions.  
- Weakness: Theorem 1’s proof (lines ~288–306) is too terse—passing limits through suprema over unbounded/parameter-dependent domains needs rigorous justification.  
- Weakness: Gradient EM section (lines 162–172) is hard to follow; Equation (5) needs a clearer derivation.  
- Quality: technical development appears solid overall but requires more detail on limiting arguments and gradient EM derivation.  
- Clarity: generally well written and organized, except for Theorem 1 limiting steps and gradient EM exposition which need expansion.  
- Originality: novel—gives definitive negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture and shows high-probability failure of random initialization.  
- Significance: important for theory and practice; reshapes understanding of population likelihood landscape and motivates principled initialization.  
- Suggestions: clarify dimensionality/generalization to d>1, expand Theorem 1 limiting steps to be rigorous, and add explicit derivation/exposition for Equation (5) in gradient EM section.  
- Arguments for acceptance: resolves an open question, demonstrates substantive algorithmic consequences, and offers practical guidance on initialization.  
- Arguments against acceptance: key limiting arguments in Theorem 1 are too concise, proofs claim general d but are for d = 1 without discussion, and gradient EM exposition is unclear.
