- Paper analyzes population likelihood for spherical Gaussian mixtures and EM/gradient variants under random initialization.
- Proves for M ≥ 3 there exist spurious local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, resolving Srebro (2007).
- Shows EM and first-order EM/gradient-ascent converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − exp(−Ω(M)) under random initialization.
- Demonstrates first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, so failures are due to bad local maxima.
- Concludes the population likelihood landscape can be treacherous and naive random restarts need exponentially many attempts in M.
- Positions results relative to literature on EM local convergence and clustering/moment-based algorithms.
- Notes negative results complement benign two-component analyses and mirror k-means/Lloyd’s known failure modes.
- Suggests connections to k-means++ and oversampling strategies due to analogous exponential failure probabilities.
- Strength: resolves a long-standing open question with a clean 1D construction.
- Strength: provides sharp algorithmic consequences and rigorous saddle-avoidance results.
- Strength: clearly articulates the importance of initialization even with well-separated, spherical components.
- Strength: techniques may generalize to other nonconvex likelihoods.
- Weakness: negative results are somewhat expected by analogy to k-means behavior under separation.
- Weakness: exponentially small success probability relies on carefully crafted hierarchical instances of unclear natural frequency.
- Weakness: purely population-level analysis offers limited practical guidance for finite samples, noise, or misspecification.
- Weakness: limited discussion contrasting EM vs Lloyd’s where they coincide or differ.
- Quality: technically sound, well-motivated constructions, persuasive dynamics-to-basin arguments.
- Clarity: generally well written with helpful two-component and 3-component examples; needs more direct k-means comparison.
- Originality: notable in resolving Srebro’s conjecture and clarifying failure probabilities and saddle behavior, though intuition aligns with known clustering pathologies.
- Significance: practical implication that random initialization for EM is unreliable even with infinite data; motivates careful initialization strategies.
- Pros for acceptance: resolves open problem, links structure to algorithmic behavior, relevant to widely used EM algorithm.
- Cons against acceptance: moderated novelty due to k-means parallels, limited practical mitigation discussion, reliance on contrived instances for strongest lower bound.
- Suggested improvements: add explicit comparison to k-means/Lloyd’s and related guarantees, discuss practical initializations like k-means++.
- Suggested improvements: clarify finite-sample implications, dependence on separation and dimension, and required sample sizes.
- Suggested improvements: elaborate step-size and smoothness conditions for first-order EM and discuss saddle-avoidance for standard EM.
- Suggested improvements: include synthetic experiments showing failure probability vs M and benefits of improved initialization.
- Overall: solid, well-executed negative result with clear algorithmic implications; valuable despite limited surprise for k-means-aware experts.
