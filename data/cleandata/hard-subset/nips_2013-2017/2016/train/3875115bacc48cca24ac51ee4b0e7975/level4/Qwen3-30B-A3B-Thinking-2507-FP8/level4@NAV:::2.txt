

This paper investigates the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating mean parameters in a Gaussian mixture model where the number of components, mixture weights, and covariance matrices are known and fixed. The theoretical work establishes three negative results regarding the EM algorithm's behavior: 1) The likelihood function may possess local maxima with values arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum, even for large sample sizes; 2) Randomly initialized EM converges with high probability to a critical point yielding a likelihood value arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum; 3) The same negative convergence behavior applies to the gradient EM algorithm. The topic is inherently interesting and merits attention. The authors' restriction to a simplified model—specifically, a known number of components, uniform weights, and identity covariance matrices—is justified given the technical complexity of the proofs and the informative nature of the results. These findings offer valuable insights for understanding EM's practical behavior. A note on dimensionality: while the paper presents results for general dimension $d$, the proofs are confined to $d=1$. The authors should clarify whether similar proofs extend to arbitrary $d$ and, if not, briefly discuss the additional challenges for $d > 1$. My primary concern pertains to Theorem 1's proof (unverified in the supplementary material). The argument appears overly concise, particularly from line 288 to 306, where limits of supremums of likelihood functions over parameter domains (some unbounded or parameter-dependent) are asserted. While I have no issue with the computation of likelihood limits at fixed parameters, the transition from pointwise limits to limits of suprema over unbounded domains requires rigorous justification. The continuity of the likelihood function alone does not suffice for the stated convergence types; more detailed reasoning is needed to fully validate this step. I recommend expanding this section, even at the expense of condensing other discussions. Additionally, the Gradient EM analysis (lines 162–172) is less accessible than the rest of the paper. Clarification on the derivation of Equation (5) would significantly improve readability.
