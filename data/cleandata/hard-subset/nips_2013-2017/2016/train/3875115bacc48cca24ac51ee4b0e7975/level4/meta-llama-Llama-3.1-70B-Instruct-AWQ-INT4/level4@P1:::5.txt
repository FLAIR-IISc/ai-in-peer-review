The paper presents a significant contribution to the field by resolving an open question posed by Srebro in 2007, namely, whether there exist local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation. The authors provide a negative answer to this question through the construction of a general class of counterexamples. They begin by outlining the schemes used to maximize the likelihood in the setting of Gaussian Mixture Models, including the EM and gradient EM algorithms, and address the issue of initialization, highlighting a common choice.

The authors then state their primary theorems, which provide a counterexample to Srebro's question. They further demonstrate that, even with the common choice of random initialization, the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to non-optimal maxima with exponentially high probability. The paper is well-written and effectively balances the need to present theoretical results with the requirement to provide intuition for these results. The authors' use of a simple case (k=3, d=1) to illustrate their proofs is particularly helpful in this regard.

The question addressed by the authors is of great importance for further understanding the issues that arise with non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic limit for simple distributions. The paper's contribution is significant, and the results have important implications for the development of algorithms for Gaussian mixture models.

One potential area for improvement is the provision of estimates for the constants in the probability term. While the authors demonstrate that the probability of convergence to bad solutions is exponentially high, it would be useful to have a better understanding of when this phenomenon becomes significant. In particular, it would be helpful to know where the phase transition occurs, i.e., at what value of k do we start to see convergence to bad solutions in the asymptotic limit. If the constants are too complicated to estimate, supporting simulations would be a useful addition to the paper.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the results are significant and well-presented. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field, and the paper is likely to have a substantial impact on our understanding of non-convex optimization in the context of Gaussian mixture models.
