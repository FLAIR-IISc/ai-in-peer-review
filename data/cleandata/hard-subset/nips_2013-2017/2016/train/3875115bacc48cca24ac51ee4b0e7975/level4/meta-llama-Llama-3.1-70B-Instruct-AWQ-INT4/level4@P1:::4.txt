This paper studies the structure of local maxima of the log-likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) with equal mixing weights and identity covariance. The authors present three related results regarding this topic: (1) a counter-example showing that there exist local maxima (when k = 3 and d = 1), whose likelihood is arbitrarily worse than the global maximum; (2) a proof that with random initialization, the probability of not converging to a bad local maximum could be exponentially small in k when the means of Gaussians are configured in a special way; and (3) a demonstration that gradient EM does not converge to a saddle point of the log-likelihood almost surely.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the problem setup, and the authors clearly explain the intuition behind their analysis. The results are well-interpreted after their statements, and the paper does a good job in presenting the main ideas.

From a technical standpoint, I only checked the proofs in the main paper (Theorem 1), and I believe the proofs are sound. Having read the analysis of Theorem 1, I think the proof idea for Theorem 2 also seems reasonable, although I didn't check the details in the Appendix.

The paper's novelty and originality lie in its observation that the mean configuration of GMMs is tied to the local maxima of its log-likelihood. This idea, although intuitive, is very original. The key idea of Theorem 2, based on Theorem 1, that the hierarchical grouping structure of the true mean determines the local maxima for k > 3, is also very clever.

The results could have a significant impact on both theory and practice. In theory, it could inspire the study of the local optima structure of related clustering tasks, such as k-means problems. It could also be interesting to examine whether the result in Lemma 7 also holds for d > 1. In practice, it shows the importance of seeding enough points in each true cluster, and may provide some insights in designing new algorithms.

The paper's clarity and presentation are excellent. The authors clearly introduce the problem setup and provide intuition and interpretation of all their analysis.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a thorough analysis of the structure of local maxima of the log-likelihood function of GMMs.
* The results are novel and original, and could have a significant impact on both theory and practice.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the main ideas.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's results are limited to GMMs with equal mixing weights and identity covariance.
* The analysis is primarily focused on the population log-likelihood function, and it is unclear how the results would generalize to finite sample sizes.

Overall, I believe that the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance.
