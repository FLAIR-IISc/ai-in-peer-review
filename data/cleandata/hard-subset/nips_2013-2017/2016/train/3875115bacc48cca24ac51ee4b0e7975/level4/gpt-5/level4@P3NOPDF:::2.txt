Summary
This paper studies the expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters in a Gaussian mixture model where the number of components, the mixture weights, and the covariance matrices are known and fixed. Focusing on a simplified setting (uniform weights and identity covariances), the authors establish three negative results:
1) Even with large sample sizes, the likelihood can have suboptimal local maxima whose values are arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum.
2) With random initialization, EM converges with high probability to a stationary point with likelihood arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum.
3) An analogous negative result holds for gradient EM.

Overall assessment
The question is important and timely, and the negative results are informative for understanding EM’s limitations in practice. The simplified model is an appropriate starting point: even in this setting, the analysis is technical and the conclusions are meaningful. The main issues are expository. In particular, the proof of Theorem 1 in the main text is too terse at crucial points, and the gradient EM discussion (lines 162–172), especially the derivation of Equation (5), is difficult to follow. With clearer and more detailed arguments in these places, the paper would be significantly strengthened.

Strengths
- Clear focus on a well-motivated problem (EM behavior in Gaussian mixtures).
- Technically nontrivial negative results that challenge common expectations about EM’s performance.
- The simplified setting is justified and does not detract from the significance of the findings.
- The results have practical implications for initialization strategies and the interpretation of EM’s outputs.

Main concerns and suggestions
1) Proof of Theorem 1 (lines 288–306):
- The argument relies on limits of suprema of likelihoods over domains that either grow unboundedly or depend on parameters tending to infinity (e.g., as γ or R → ∞). While pointwise limits of likelihood values at fixed parameters are straightforward, passing limits through a supremum over varying, potentially unbounded sets requires care.
- Continuity of the likelihood is not by itself sufficient to justify the interchanges of suprema and limits asserted in the text.
- Please expand this part of the proof to supply a rigorous justification. Potential avenues include:
  - Working with compact parameter truncations and showing uniform convergence on compacts, then passing to the limit via an exhaustion argument.
  - Applying a version of Berge’s maximum theorem or related argmax/epiconvergence tools to justify continuity of the value function when domains vary.
  - Providing explicit monotonicity/continuity arguments where the domains form an increasing sequence whose union is the full space, together with tightness/coercivity of the likelihood to control behavior at infinity.
- If the detailed arguments already appear in the supplementary material, please cite them explicitly at the relevant steps and consider moving the key lemmas into the main text so that the central negative result is self-contained.

2) Gradient EM section (lines 162–172) and Equation (5):
- The presentation is substantially harder to follow than the rest of the paper. Please:
  - Derive Equation (5) explicitly, stating clearly which objective is being differentiated (e.g., the Q-function vs. the observed-data likelihood), the variable of differentiation, and any identities used to obtain the gradient.
  - Clarify step sizes, projection (if any), and the exact update rule assumed.
  - Indicate precisely how the negative result for gradient EM parallels the standard EM case and what additional assumptions (if any) are needed.

3) Dimension d:
- The exposition is written as if the results hold in general dimension d, but the proofs focus on d = 1. Please clearly delineate which statements are proven only in one dimension and which extend to higher dimensions.
- If similar constructions work for arbitrary d, a brief sketch of how the key ideas generalize would be valuable. If not, please articulate the added difficulties in d > 1 (e.g., geometric configurations of means, saddle structure of the likelihood, interaction among coordinates) and whether you expect analogous counterexamples to exist.

Additional clarity and presentation suggestions
- Quantifiers and probability statements: When asserting that EM “converges with high probability” to a bad critical point, please specify the sources of randomness (data sampling, initialization, or both) and the asymptotic regime (fixed n, large n, or as n → ∞). If the high-probability statement is over both the sample and initialization, indicate the order of quantifiers.
- Assumptions and scope: Early in the paper, state prominently that the number of components, weights (uniform), and covariances (identity) are known and fixed. Remind the reader when these assumptions are used critically in the proofs.
- Practical implications: A short discussion contrasting these negative results with known positive results under separation/regularity conditions would help contextualize the contribution and guide practitioners (e.g., when strong initialization or separation mitigates the pathologies).

Minor comments
- Terminology: Prefer “Gaussian mixture model (GMM)” and “number of components” to “mixture of Gaussian variables” and “number of Gaussian variables.”
- “Randomly initiated” → “randomly initialized.” “Arbitrary smaller” → “arbitrarily smaller.” “Critical point” → “stationary point.”
- If space allows, a small illustrative figure or schematic of the likelihood landscape in the one-dimensional construction could aid intuition.

Conclusion
This is an interesting and useful theoretical contribution on EM’s limitations for Gaussian mixtures. Strengthening the proof of Theorem 1 where limits of suprema are invoked, clarifying the gradient EM derivation, and being explicit about the dimension and probability quantifiers would substantially improve the paper’s rigor and readability. I encourage the authors to incorporate these revisions.
