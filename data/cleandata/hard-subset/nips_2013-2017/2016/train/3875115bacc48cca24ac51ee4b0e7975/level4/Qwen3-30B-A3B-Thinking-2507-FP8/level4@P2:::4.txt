

This paper addresses a fundamental question in Gaussian mixture model (GMM) theory: whether the population likelihood function of GMMs with M ≥ 3 components can have bad local maxima, as conjectured by Srebro [2007] to not exist in the infinite-sample limit. The authors provide two major theoretical contributions that resolve this open question. First, they construct a counterexample demonstrating that for M = 3 (in one dimension), equally-weighted, well-separated, spherical GMMs can possess local maxima whose likelihood values are arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. Second, they prove that with random initialization, the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) converges to such bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)), establishing that poor initialization can lead to failure even in favorable settings. The paper further shows that first-order EM avoids strict saddle points almost surely, indicating that the poor performance stems from bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper's technical quality is strong. The proof of Theorem 1 (the counterexample construction) is rigorous and well-motivated, with clear geometric intuition about how the configuration of true centers creates bad local maxima. The authors effectively leverage this result to prove Theorem 2 (the algorithmic consequence) by constructing a hierarchical mean configuration that makes favorable initialization exponentially unlikely. The proof techniques, particularly the recursive structure of the mean configuration, are elegant and novel. The paper also properly contextualizes its results within existing literature, referencing relevant work by Balakrishnan et al. [2015] on local convergence of EM for two Gaussians and Srebro [2007] on the conjecture being resolved.

The originality of this work is significant. While previous studies have examined local convergence of EM for two Gaussians [Balakrishnan et al., 2015] or identified pathological cases with finite samples [Améndola et al., 2015], this paper provides the first resolution of the infinite-sample case for M ≥ 3. The key insight—using hierarchical mean configurations to demonstrate exponentially small success probability for random initialization—represents a clever and novel approach to analyzing optimization landscapes in non-convex problems. This contribution is particularly valuable because it directly challenges the common heuristic that EM works well for well-separated mixtures, even in large samples.

The significance of these results is substantial for both theory and practice. Theoretically, this work provides a fundamental understanding of the optimization landscape of GMMs, resolving a long-standing open question. It also suggests new directions for studying local optima in related clustering problems, such as k-means. Practically, the findings strongly indicate that careful initialization is essential for EM in GMMs, even in seemingly ideal settings. This has immediate implications for practitioners, suggesting that standard random initialization schemes may fail catastrophically, and that methods like those based on the method of moments [Moitra and Valiant, 2010; Hsu and Kakade, 2013] are necessary for reliable performance. The authors' observation that success probability decays exponentially with M is particularly striking and should be incorporated into the design of practical algorithms.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by contrasting previous work on M = 2 with the open question for M ≥ 3, and the geometric intuition for the counterexample is presented with clarity. The paper is well-organized, with each section building logically on the previous one. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results, including clear definitions of the problem setup and rigorous statements of all theorems. The figures (including Figure 1 illustrating the M = 2 case) effectively complement the text. The discussion of the relationship between the results and previous work is thorough and appropriately contextualized.

Strengths: The paper resolves a significant open question with a clean, elegant construction; the proofs are sound and well-motivated; the practical implications are clear and important; the writing is exceptionally clear and well-organized.

Weaknesses: The paper focuses exclusively on the well-separated, equal-weighted, spherical case, and while the authors note that their results extend to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments, a more detailed discussion of these extensions would be valuable. The paper does not explore whether the exponential failure probability is tight or whether better initialization schemes could achieve polynomial failure probability.

Pro-acceptance arguments: This is a major theoretical contribution that resolves a long-standing open question in GMM theory; the results have clear implications for both theoretical understanding and practical algorithm design; the paper is technically sound and exceptionally well-written; the results are significant for the broader field of non-convex optimization.

Con-acceptance arguments: The focus on a very specific GMM setting (equal weights, spherical, well-separated) limits the immediate applicability to more general mixture models; the results do not directly address finite-sample behavior beyond the standard empirical process extension.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to our understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes and EM algorithm behavior. The theoretical results are rigorous, the writing is clear, and the practical implications are important for both researchers and practitioners. The paper addresses all four key evaluation criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) with exceptional thoroughness. I strongly recommend acceptance.
