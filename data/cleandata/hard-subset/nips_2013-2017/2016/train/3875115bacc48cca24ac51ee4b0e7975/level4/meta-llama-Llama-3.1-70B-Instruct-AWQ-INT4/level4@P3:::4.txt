This paper studies the structure of local maxima of the log-likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (with equal mixing weights and identity as covariance). The authors present three related results regarding this topic: (1) a counter-example showing that there exist local maxima (when the number of components is at least three and the dimension is one), whose likelihood is arbitrarily worse than the global maximum; (2) a proof that with random initialization, the probability of not converging to a bad local maximum could be exponentially small in the number of components when the means of Gaussians are configured in a special way; and (3) a demonstration that gradient Expectation-Maximization (EM) does not converge to a saddle point of the log-likelihood almost surely.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the population log-likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models, resolving an open problem of Srebro (2007) and offering new insights into the behavior of the EM algorithm. The authors' use of a hierarchical grouping structure to analyze the local maxima of the log-likelihood function is particularly clever. The paper is well-written, and the authors do an excellent job of presenting their results and explaining the intuition behind their analysis.

The technical quality of the paper is high, with sound proofs and a clear explanation of the key ideas. The authors' interpretation of their results is also well-done, and they provide a clear discussion of the implications of their work. The paper's novelty and originality are significant, as it ties the mean configuration of Gaussian Mixture Models to the local maxima of its log-likelihood function. This observation, although intuitive, is very original and has the potential to inspire further research in this area.

The potential impact and usefulness of the paper are substantial.
