This paper provides a definitive and negative resolution to the long-standing open question posed by Srebro [2007] regarding the existence of "bad" local maxima in the population (infinite-sample) likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The authors make two primary contributions. First, they construct a class of GMMs, even in the highly idealized setting of equally-weighted, spherical, and well-separated components, where the population log-likelihood possesses local maxima whose values can be arbitrarily worse than that of the global maximum. This holds for any number of components M ≥ 3. Second, the paper explores the algorithmic consequences of this structural result. It demonstrates that the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and a first-order variant, when initialized with a common and practical random sampling scheme, will converge to these suboptimal critical points with a probability that approaches 1 exponentially fast in M. The authors further show that for the first-order EM variant, this failure can be attributed to convergence to bad local maxima rather than getting stuck at strict saddle points.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is of fundamental importance. It resolves a well-known open question in the field, challenging the long-held intuition that the population likelihood landscape for GMMs might be benign. The result has significant implications for our understanding of non-convex optimization in statistical models, showing that even in the asymptotic limit and for simple, well-behaved models, problematic local optima can persist. This work clarifies that the challenges of GMM estimation are not merely finite-sample artifacts but are inherent to the structure of the likelihood function itself.

*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results are strong and rigorously proven. The construction of the counterexample is elegant and intuitive, particularly the geometric argument provided for the M=3 case, which helps build understanding for the more general result. The analysis of the EM algorithm's convergence probability is also novel, employing a recursive construction of the GMM to establish the exponential failure rate.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, clearly stating their contributions, and situating their work within the existing literature. The balance between formal statements and intuitive explanations is commendable, making a complex theoretical result accessible.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement (Arguments against acceptance):**

The paper presents a very complete and compelling theoretical story. The main suggestions are minor and relate to contextualizing the practical impact of the asymptotic results.

*   The core algorithmic results (Theorems 2 and 3) are asymptotic in the number of mixture components, M. While this is a powerful theoretical statement, in many practical applications M is often moderate (e.g., less than 20). To strengthen the paper's practical relevance, it would be highly beneficial to provide some characterization of when these phenomena begin to dominate. Specifically:
    *   Could the authors provide estimates, even if rough, for the universal constant `c` in the probability bound `1 - e^(-cM)`? This would help readers understand the scale of M for which failure becomes nearly certain.
    *   It would be valuable to supplement the theory with simulations. For the GMM construction used in the proofs, an empirical study showing the probability of converging to a bad optimum as a function of M would be illuminating. Such a simulation could also help visualize the "phase transition" where random initializations become more likely to fail than to succeed. This does not detract from the theoretical novelty but would significantly enhance the paper's impact.

Despite these minor suggestions, the weaknesses are far outweighed by the strengths. The resolution of a significant open problem is a major contribution in itself.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the theoretical understanding of Gaussian mixture models and the behavior of the EM algorithm. It is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a question of significant interest to the NIPS community. The work is a clear advancement of the state of the art and will likely be highly cited. I strongly recommend acceptance.
