This paper studies local maxima of the population log-likelihood for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 equally weighted, spherical components and known variance. The authors prove two main results. First, they construct well-separated mixtures for which the population log-likelihood has suboptimal local maxima whose value can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, resolving an open question of Srebro (2007) in the negative. Second, they show that EM and a first-order variant, when randomly initialized from the data-generating distribution, converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). They further prove that first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, suggesting the primary obstruction is bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on classical EM (Dempster et al., 1977) and recent analyses of EM’s local convergence for mixtures (Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015). It contrasts with consistent, polynomial-time alternatives based on clustering and moments (e.g., Vempala and Wang, 2002; Hsu and Kakade, 2013). The core contribution is structural: it refutes Srebro’s conjecture that population GMM likelihoods have no spurious local maxima in favorable settings, and ties this structure to algorithmic behavior of EM-like methods.

Strengths:
- Clear negative resolution of a long-standing conjecture under arguably favorable conditions (equal weights, spherical covariances, well-separated means).
- Careful structural construction and a recursive argument yielding exponentially small success probabilities for random initialization.
- Distinguishes failure due to local maxima from saddle points using recent results on gradient dynamics.

Concerns and questions:
1) Practical prevalence of “bad” configurations (Theorem 1). The counterexample relies on particular geometric arrangements (e.g., clusters of centers at multiple separated scales). How likely are such configurations under natural generative assumptions on the true centers? The discussion (L210–L218) points to Theorem 2, but that addresses failure given such configurations and random initialization, not the probability that the configurations themselves arise. It would strengthen the practical relevance to assume a prior on center locations (e.g., centers drawn i.i.d. from a distribution with separated modes or from a high-dimensional sphere) and quantify how the probability of “bad” arrangements scales with M and/or d.

2) Practicality of random initialization (Theorem 2). The analysis shows that drawing initial centers i.i.d. from the mixture leads EM to suboptimal points with high probability for the constructed instances. In settings with clearly separated clusters (e.g., M = 3 with two close and one far apart), practitioners often use heuristics that reflect the empirical density (k-means/k-means++, multiple restarts, or seeding from cluster centroids). If two true components are close, 2N points concentrate near that cluster and N near the third; it seems unlikely one would initialize with two centers in the sparse cluster and one in the dense cluster when the separation is large. Could the authors comment on the robustness of their negative result to more practical initializations (e.g., k-means++ or seeding from distinct samples)? Is random seeding from the data the right baseline when clusters are visually/separably apparent?

3) Example for M = 2 (L74–81). The figure and discussion illustrate the landscape for two components but do not provide evidence in favor of or against the conjecture for M = 2. What is the intended takeaway beyond intuition building? Clarifying this would help, since the necessity of M ≥ 3 is an interesting boundary case highlighted in the conclusion.

4) Clarification on initialization requirements (L233–235). The text lists two conditions for “favorable” initializations: (1) all initialized centers fall in the neighborhood of exactly one top-level cluster of true centers, and (2) the number of initialized centers per cluster matches the number of true centers. Condition (2) is clear. Why is (1) necessary? Is the issue that centers initialized between clusters may be captured by the wrong cluster or oscillate, so one cannot guarantee migration to the correct cluster under EM updates?

5) Significance of avoiding strict saddle points (L267–273). From a practical standpoint, why should we care that first-order EM avoids strict saddles if it still converges to suboptimal local maxima with high probability? A brief discussion comparing the relative prevalence and impact of saddles versus poor local optima in GMMs would clarify the algorithmic implications.

Quality and clarity: The paper is well written overall and technically careful, with a few typos/minor issues noted below. The proofs introduce useful techniques for reasoning about population likelihood landscapes.

Originality: The negative structural result and the probabilistic failure of random initialization at population level are novel and important.

Significance: The results underscore the importance of initialization for EM, even in highly favorable, well-specified, well-separated settings. They also temper optimism motivated by recent “benign landscape” results in other nonconvex problems.

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves an open problem with clean, rigorous constructions.
- Bridges structural properties and algorithmic behavior.
- Likely to influence both theory and practice in mixture modeling.

Cons for acceptance:
- Practical prevalence of the constructed “bad” configurations is unclear.
- Baseline initialization model may be weaker than common practice; robustness to stronger initializations is not explored.
- Some expository points (M = 2 case; role of condition (1); practical value of the saddle-point result) need clarification.
