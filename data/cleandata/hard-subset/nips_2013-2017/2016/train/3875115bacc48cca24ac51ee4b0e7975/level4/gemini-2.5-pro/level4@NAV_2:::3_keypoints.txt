- Paper studies population log-likelihood landscape for well-specified, equally-weighted spherical GMMs with known variance.  
- Contribution 1: Constructs a counterexample to Srebro (2007) showing for M ≥ 3 the population log-likelihood can have arbitrarily bad local maxima.  
- Contribution 2: Shows EM and a first-order EM variant, under random initialization, converge to suboptimal critical points with probability →1 exponentially fast in M.  
- Additional algorithmic result: First-order EM avoids strict saddle points almost surely, attributing failures to bad local maxima.  

Strengths:
- Significance: Resolves a long-standing open question by giving a negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture.  
- Technical quality: Proofs appear sound and introduce novel analytic techniques for the likelihood structure.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written with clear exposition and good literature context.  
- Originality: Novel construction of GMMs with arbitrarily bad local maxima and analysis of exponential failure probability for random-init EM.  

Weaknesses / major concerns:
- Practical relevance: Bad local maxima are shown for a specific, hierarchically clustered construction; unclear how prevalent such instances are in practice.  
- Problem vs. initialization adversariality: Paper addresses adversarial initialization but not whether adversarially chosen problem instances are realistic under natural priors.  
- Initialization realism: Theorem 2 assumes i.i.d. initialization from the mixture; this may be unrealistic since data-aware initializations (e.g., k-means) would likely place centers proportionally in dense clusters and avoid the failure modes.  
- Robustness to practical inits: Unclear how conclusions change under more sophisticated, practical initialization strategies.  

Detailed technical questions:
- M=2 case: Example (Lines 74–81) is ambiguous; does it resolve the conjecture for M=2 or just illustrate a point?  
- Initialization necessity: Why must all initialized centers fall in neighborhoods of exactly one true cluster—wouldn’t centers between clusters move toward the nearest/largest cluster?  
- Theorem 4 interpretation: What is the practical implication of avoiding strict saddles—are strict saddles less problematic than bad local maxima in practice?
