

The paper analyzes the convergence properties of the EM algorithm (and related iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation) in learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, establishing non-convergence to global maxima through three key results: (1) Even with three well-separated clusters and infinite samples, EM may converge to local optima; (2) For mixtures of $k$ Gaussians, random initialization succeeds with probability at most $\exp(-\Omega(k))$; (3) Gradient EM generically avoids strict saddle points, indicating that poor local maxima—not saddles—are the primary barrier. While the results form a coherent narrative demonstrating EM’s failure to reach global optima (even asymptotically), they largely align with established intuition and follow predictable technical pathways. The central counterexample involves a three-component mixture where two clusters are closely spaced and the third is far from both. The log-likelihood objective exhibits a local maximum with one center near the two proximal clusters and two centers near the isolated cluster—behavior expected when separation exceeds $\sqrt{d}$, rendering the problem analogous to $k$-means under sufficient separation. This example generalizes to $k$ components via a non-trivial recursive construction, but the underlying mechanism mirrors known $k$-means failure modes (e.g., Lloyd’s algorithm). Similarly, the random initialization failure probability echoes $k$-means literature, where strategies like distance-squared sampling are motivated by such bounds. Overall, while the results consistently illustrate non-convergence, they do not substantially advance beyond conventional expectations.  

Comments:  
1. Kumar-Kannan established convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic in $k$-means (and thus Gaussian mixtures under sufficient separation). Although EM diverges from Lloyd’s behavior when Gaussians are poorly separated (as noted in Balakrishnan-Wainwright-Yu), the two methods empirically and theoretically converge to similar outcomes under large separation (as in the paper’s counterexamples). A comparative discussion contrasting this work with $k$-means convergence theory would significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution.
