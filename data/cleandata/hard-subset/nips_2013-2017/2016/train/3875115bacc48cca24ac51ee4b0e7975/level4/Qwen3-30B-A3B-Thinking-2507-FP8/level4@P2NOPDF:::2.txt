

This paper considers the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters in a Gaussian mixture model with known, fixed components: the number of Gaussians, their uniform weights, and identity covariance matrices. The work presents three theoretical negative results concerning EM's behavior. First, the likelihood function may possess local maxima with values arbitrarily close to zero, even for large sample sizes. Second, EM with random initialization converges with high probability to a critical point whose likelihood is arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum. Third, the gradient EM variant exhibits the same deficiency. The question addressed is compelling and merits attention, particularly given the paper's focus on a simplified model. The restrictions—known component count, uniform weights, and identity covariances—are justified, as the proofs are already technically demanding and yield informative insights about EM's limitations. The results hold significant potential for improving practical understanding of the algorithm.

A notable point concerns the paper's claim of generality in dimension \(d\), while the core proofs are restricted to \(d=1\). The authors should explicitly address whether their results extend to arbitrary dimensions and, if not, briefly outline the additional challenges for \(d > 1\).

My primary concern pertains to Theorem 1's proof (Section 3.1). The argument is currently too concise, particularly in the critical interval from lines 288–306, where the authors establish limits of supremums of the likelihood function over unbounded domains as parameters diverge. While the computations for fixed parameters appear sound (e.g., the three equations at line 288), the justification for passing limits through suprema over parameter-dependent, unbounded sets requires more rigor. The continuity of the likelihood function alone does not suffice to guarantee the stated convergence properties; a more detailed argument is needed to establish that \(\lim_{R \to \infty} \sup_{\theta \in \mathcal{D}_R} \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \sup_{\theta \in \mathcal{D}} \mathcal{L}(\theta)\) under the given conditions. A longer, more precise proof would substantially strengthen the paper's technical foundation.

Additionally, the Gradient EM analysis (lines 162–172) is less clear than the rest of the paper. The derivation of Equation (5) lacks sufficient explanation. The authors should elaborate on how this equation follows from the preceding setup, particularly regarding the role of the gradient and the specific form of the update rule. A brief clarification would greatly enhance readability and verifiability.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution by rigorously demonstrating fundamental limitations of EM in a well-defined setting. The results are theoretically sound and practically relevant. The suggested clarifications—on dimensionality, Theorem 1's proof, and the Gradient EM derivation—are minor but necessary for full technical completeness. With these revisions, the paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
