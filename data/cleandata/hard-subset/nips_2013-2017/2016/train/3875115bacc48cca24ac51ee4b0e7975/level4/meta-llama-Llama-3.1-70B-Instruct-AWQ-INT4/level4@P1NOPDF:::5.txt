The paper provides a definitive answer to the open question posed by Srebro in his 2007 paper, "Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?" The authors' response to this question is a resounding "no", which they establish through the construction of a general class of counterexamples.

The authors begin by outlining the methods used to maximize the likelihood in the context of Gaussian Mixture Models, including the EM and gradient EM algorithms. They also address the issue of initialization, presenting a common choice used in practice. The paper's primary theorems are then stated, which provide the counterexample to Srebro's question. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that even with the commonly used random initialization, the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to non-optimal maxima with exponentially high probability.

To provide insight into their proofs, the authors consider a simple case (k=3, d=1) and walk the reader through the key steps. This paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, striking a balance between the need for concise presentation and the importance of providing intuition for the theoretical results. The question addressed by the authors is of significant importance for advancing our understanding of the challenges associated with non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic limit for simple distributions.

While I did not thoroughly review the supplementary material, I believe it would be beneficial to include estimates of the constants in the probability term. This information is crucial, as it seems unlikely that one would consider k to be excessively large. Providing estimates of the constants would be valuable in understanding where the phase transition occurs, i.e., at what point we can expect to see convergence to suboptimal solutions in the asymptotic limit. If the constants are too complex or do not lend themselves to meaningful interpretation, supporting simulations would be helpful in demonstrating that this phenomenon can be observed in practice. However, this issue does not detract from the novelty or impact of this paper.
