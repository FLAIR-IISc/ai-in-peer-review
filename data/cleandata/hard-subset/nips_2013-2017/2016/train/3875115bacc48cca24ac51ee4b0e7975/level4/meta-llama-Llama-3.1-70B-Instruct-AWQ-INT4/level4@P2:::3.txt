The paper "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences" provides a thorough analysis of the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models with M ≥ 3 components. The authors present two main results: first, they show that the population likelihood function has bad local maxima even in the special case of equally-weighted mixtures of well-separated and spherical Gaussians; second, they prove that the EM algorithm (or a first-order variant of it) with random initialization will converge to bad critical points with probability at least 1− e−Ω(M).

The paper is well-written, with clear explanations and proofs of the main results. The authors also provide a detailed discussion of the implications of their results, including the necessity of careful initialization when using the EM algorithm in practice.

The main contribution of the paper is the resolution of an open question of Srebro [2007], which conjectured that any local maximum of the likelihood function is a global maximum in the limit of infinite samples. The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture, showing that bad local maxima can exist even in the idealized situation where each component is uniformly weighted, spherical with unit variance, and well-separated.

The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the EM algorithm and its variants, including the first-order EM algorithm. The authors show that the EM algorithm can converge to bad critical points with high probability, even when the initialization is random. They also prove that the first-order EM algorithm cannot converge to strict saddle points with probability one.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its clarity and readability. The authors provide a detailed introduction to the problem, including the necessary background and notation. The proofs of the main results are also clearly explained, making it easy for readers to follow.
