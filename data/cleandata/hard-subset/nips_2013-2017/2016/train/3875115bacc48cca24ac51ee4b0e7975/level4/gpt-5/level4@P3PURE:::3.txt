This paper studies the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models under an especially simple and well-specified setting: isotropic Gaussian components with known variance. The authors show that local modes persist even in the infinite-sample limit, and that these modes can be arbitrarily suboptimal in terms of the log-likelihood gap between the local and global optima. The analysis focuses on particular configurations, especially those where the true component means are partitioned into well-separated groups. In such settings, the authors argue that random initialization causes EM to miss the global optimum with probability approaching 1 as the number of components grows. The paper is well written overall, with occasional typos.

My main concerns are about practical relevance:

1) Likelihood of “bad” true configurations. Theorem 1 establishes the existence of a local mode even with infinite data, but it does so by constructing specific configurations of the true centers. How likely are such configurations to arise in practice? I would be more convinced of the practical implications if the paper posited a distribution over the true centers and then quantified how the probability of “bad” configurations scales with the number of components k and/or the dimension d. Lines 210–218 suggest that Theorem 2 addresses this, but I do not think it does. A clear statement quantifying the likelihood of bad configurations of the true centers (as opposed to bad initializations) under explicit assumptions would greatly strengthen the case.

2) Practicality of random initialization in clearly clustered data. Theorem 2 brings initialization into play by considering EM initialized via random draws from the mixture. Under certain configurations, EM then often fails to reach the global optimum. Consider data from three Gaussians, two close together and one far away, with N samples from each component. Then roughly 2N points lie near the “paired” region and N points near the isolated component. It seems odd that a practitioner would initialize EM by placing two centers in the sparse (N-point) region and only one in the dense (2N-point) region, especially when the two regions are well separated. Is random initialization a practically relevant model of behavior when the data are clearly clustered?

Specific comments:
- L74–81: I am not sure what the example here is intended to demonstrate. It does not convince me that the Srebro conjecture holds when k = 2. What is the precise purpose of this example?
- L233–235: I understand requirement (2), but why is requirement (1) necessary? Is the point that if a center is initialized between two clusters, you cannot guarantee it will move toward the “correct” cluster?
- L267–273: The paper shows EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point. From a practical standpoint, why is this important? Is convergence to a saddle point more or less problematic than convergence to a suboptimal local maximum?
