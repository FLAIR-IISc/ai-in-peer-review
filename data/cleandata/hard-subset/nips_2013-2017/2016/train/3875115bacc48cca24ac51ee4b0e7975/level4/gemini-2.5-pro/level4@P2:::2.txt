This paper investigates the behavior of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and the performance of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in the population (infinite-sample) setting. It directly addresses and refutes a conjecture by Srebro (2007), which posited that the population likelihood for well-behaved GMMs has no spurious local maxima.

The paper presents three main theoretical results, all of which are negative in nature:
1.  First, the authors demonstrate that for any number of components M ≥ 3, the population log-likelihood can possess "bad" local maxima, whose values can be arbitrarily worse than that of the global maximum. This is shown to hold even in an idealized setting with well-separated, spherical, and uniformly weighted mixtures.
2.  Second, they prove that the standard EM algorithm, when initialized by drawing points from the true data distribution, converges to these suboptimal critical points with a probability that approaches 1 exponentially in M.
3.  Third, they extend this negative result to a first-order variant of EM (equivalent to gradient ascent) and further argue that this algorithmic failure is due to the existence of bad local maxima, rather than the proliferation of strict saddle points.

**Strengths and Contributions:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary contribution is a strong, definitive, and negative answer to a significant open question in the field. The results are highly original and counterintuitive, challenging the common belief that the population likelihood landscape for well-behaved GMMs might be benign. These findings have important implications for both theory and practice, providing a formal justification for the necessity of sophisticated initialization schemes (e.g., method of moments) over simple random initialization for EM, even in idealized, large-sample settings.

*   **Quality:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis. While the analysis is restricted to a simplified GMM setting (known uniform weights, known identity covariances), this is a reasonable simplification that makes the complex proofs tractable. The insights gained from this focused setting are still highly relevant and informative for understanding the broader challenges of non-convex optimization in mixture models.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity and persuasiveness could be significantly improved in a few critical areas, particularly concerning the central proof of Theorem 1.

*   **Clarity of the Proof of Theorem 1:** The proof sketch for Theorem 1 is too brief to be fully convincing. The core of the argument relies on constructing a neighborhood that contains a suboptimal point but no global maximum, and then showing that the likelihood on the boundary of this neighborhood is lower than at the interior point. However, the proof asserts claims about the supremum of the likelihood over certain domains in the limit as parameters (e.g., γ or R) go to infinity. Establishing the limit of a supremum is non-trivial, as `lim sup f(x)` is not generally equal to `sup lim f(x)`, especially when the domain over which the supremum is taken is also changing or unbounded. The paper's brief appeal to the continuity of the likelihood function is insufficient to rigorously justify these steps. A more detailed argument, perhaps using uniform convergence results or a more explicit epsilon-delta proof, is needed in the main text to substantiate this central claim. I would strongly recommend expanding this proof, even at the expense of shortening other discussion sections.

*   **Generalization to Higher Dimensions:** The constructions in the proofs appear to be for the one-dimensional case (d=1), whereas the theorems are stated for a general dimension d. It would strengthen the paper to clarify whether the proofs generalize directly to d > 1 or if new challenges arise. A brief discussion of any additional difficulties in higher dimensions would be valuable for the reader.

*   **Explanation of First-Order EM:** The introduction of the first-order EM algorithm could be clearer. The paper states that the update in Equation (6) "turns out to be equivalent to gradient ascent on the population likelihood L" and refers to Balakrishnan et al. (2015) for details. While space is limited, providing a brief derivation or intuition for this connection—for instance, by showing that `E[w_i(X)(X-μ_i)]` is proportional to the gradient of L with respect to `μ_i`—would significantly improve the paper's self-containedness and readability.

In summary, this paper tackles an important problem and delivers surprising and significant theoretical results about the optimization landscape of GMMs. The primary concern is the lack of detail in the proof of the main structural result (Theorem 1), which undermines the reader's confidence in this cornerstone claim. If the authors can expand and clarify this proof and address the other minor points, this would be a very strong contribution to the conference.
