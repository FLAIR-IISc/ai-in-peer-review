The paper presents an answer to the open question asked by Srebro in his 2007 paper "Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?". This paper answers the question in the negative through the construction of a general class of counterexamples. The authors begin by outlining the schemes by which the likelihood is maximized in the setting of Gaussian Mixture Models, including the EM and gradient EM algorithms. They also address the issue of initialization and give a common choice. The authors then state their primary theorems, which give the counterexample to the question by Srebro [2007]. The authors further show that even with the common choice of random initialization, the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to non-optimal maxima with exponentially high probability.

The paper is well-written and clear, striking a good balance between the space requirements of the paper and the need to give intuition for their theoretical results. The question answered by the authors is of great importance for further understanding the issues that arise with non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic limit for simple distributions.

The main contributions of the paper can be summarized as follows: (i) the authors provide a negative answer to the open question of Srebro [2007] by constructing a counterexample, and (ii) they show that the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to non-optimal maxima with exponentially high probability, even with random initialization. These results have significant implications for the use of these algorithms in practice, highlighting the need for careful initialization.

The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with a clear structure that makes it easy to understand the main contributions and results. The authors provide a good balance of theoretical and intuitive explanations, making the paper accessible to a wide range of readers.

One potential area for improvement is the provision of estimates for the constants in the probability term. While the authors provide a theoretical analysis, it would be useful to have a better understanding of the practical implications of their results, particularly in terms of the phase transition where the convergence to bad solutions becomes significant. Supporting simulations or numerical experiments would be a useful addition to the paper, providing further insight into the behavior of the algorithms in practice.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field, providing new insights into the behavior of the EM and gradient EM algorithms in the context of Gaussian Mixture Models. The results have important implications for practice, highlighting the need for careful initialization and the potential risks of convergence to non-optimal maxima.
