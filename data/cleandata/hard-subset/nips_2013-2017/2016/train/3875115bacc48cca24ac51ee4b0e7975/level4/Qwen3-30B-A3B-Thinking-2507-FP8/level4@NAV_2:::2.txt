

This paper investigates the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating mean parameters in Gaussian mixture models with known component counts, uniform weights, and identity covariance matrices. The theoretical work establishes three key negative findings: (1) the population likelihood function can possess local maxima with arbitrarily worse likelihood values than global optima, even in the infinite-sample limit; (2) the EM algorithm with random initialization converges to suboptimal critical points with high probability; and (3) the gradient variant of EM exhibits the same problematic behavior. The research question is highly relevant and merits attention, as the results provide important theoretical insights into the limitations of EM in practice. The simplifications to the Gaussian mixture model (known component count, uniform weights, identity covariance matrices) are reasonable given the technical complexity of the proofs and the informative nature of the results. The paper's theoretical contributions are significant and would be valuable for practitioners seeking to understand the behavior of EM in favorable settings. While the paper claims to address general dimension d, the proofs are restricted to d=1; the authors should clarify whether similar results can be extended to arbitrary dimensions and, if not, discuss the additional challenges involved. My primary concern pertains to Theorem 1's proof, which appears too brief, particularly from lines 288-306. The authors compute limits of supremums of likelihood functions over parameter-dependent domains as certain parameters approach infinity, but the justification for these limits is insufficient. While the computations of likelihood limits at fixed parameters seem plausible, the convergence of suprema over unbounded domains requires more rigorous justification. Additional details would strengthen the proof's credibility. I also found the Gradient EM section (lines 162-172) less clear than the rest of the paper; the authors could provide more explanation regarding the derivation of Equation (5) to improve readability.
