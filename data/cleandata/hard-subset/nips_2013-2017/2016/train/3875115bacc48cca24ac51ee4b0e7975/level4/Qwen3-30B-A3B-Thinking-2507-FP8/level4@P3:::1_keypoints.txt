- Paper resolves Srebro’s conjecture by showing population likelihood for well-separated GMMs can have bad local maxima.  
- Main theoretical results: (1) For M ≥ 3 equal-weight spherical Gaussians, population log-likelihood has local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.  
- (2) EM (and first-order EM) with random initialization converges to such suboptimal points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{−Ω(M)}.  
- (3) First-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, implying bad local maxima—not saddles—are the dominant failure mode.  
- Conclusion: careful initialization remains necessary even in idealized settings (infinite samples, spherical Gaussians, strong separation).  
- Strength: Significant resolution of a long-standing open problem with a rigorous, elegant structural counterexample (Theorem 1).  
- Strength: Algorithmic results (Theorems 2–4) are tightly argued, with a clear recursive construction extending M=3 to general M.  
- Strength: Proof that first-order EM avoids strict saddles leverages recent non-convex optimization results and adds theoretical depth.  
- Strength: Analysis is technically sound, well-connected to prior GMM and k-means literature, and written with excellent clarity.  
- Weakness: Novelty is somewhat limited—counterexample is intuitively expected and parallels known k-means failures.  
- Weakness: Paper could better contrast results with k-means theory (e.g., Kumar & Kannan) to clarify whether exponential failure is unique to likelihood methods.  
- Weakness: Practical implications underplayed; finite-sample extensions are noted but not deeply discussed.  
- Overall: High-quality, technically rigorous paper suitable for NIPS; advances understanding of non-convexity in mixtures though novelty is incremental.  
- Recommendation: Accept; suggest adding a more explicit comparison to k-means and expanding discussion of practical/finite-sample relevance.
