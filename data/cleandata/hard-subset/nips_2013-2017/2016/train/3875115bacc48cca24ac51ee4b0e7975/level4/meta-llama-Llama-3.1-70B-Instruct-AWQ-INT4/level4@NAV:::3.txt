The paper explores the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models, focusing on the simplest possible mixture model with isotropic Gaussian components and known scale. The authors demonstrate the existence of local modes, even with infinite data, and show that these modes can be arbitrarily suboptimal in terms of log-likelihood differences between local and global optima. They concentrate on specific configurations where mixture centers are divided between well-spaced clusters, illustrating that random initialization can cause the EM algorithm to miss the global optimum with high probability as the number of components increases.

The paper is well-written, with occasional typos. However, I have two primary concerns regarding the paper's practical relevance. Firstly, Theorem 1 shows that local modes exist in the log-likelihood, even with infinite data, under a specific configuration of centers. I question the likelihood of this configuration occurring in practice. To address this, the authors could make assumptions about the distribution of centers and demonstrate that "bad" configurations become increasingly probable as the number of components or problem dimensionality increases. Theorem 2 supposedly addresses these concerns, but I am uncertain about its relevance.

Secondly, Theorem 2 focuses on the initialization of the EM algorithm, specifically considering random initialization of component centers by sampling the mixture. Under certain configurations, the EM algorithm often fails to converge to the global optimum with this initialization. However, I find it unlikely that someone would initialize EM by placing centers in areas with fewer data points, especially when the data is clearly clustered.

Some specific points require clarification or revision. The example on lines 74-81 does not convincingly demonstrate the Sresbro conjecture for k = 2, making its purpose unclear. The necessity of requirement (1) on lines 233-235 is also unclear, as initializing a center between two clusters should intuitively cause it to move toward the correct cluster. Furthermore, the practical importance of the result on lines 267-273, which proves that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddlepoint, is not immediately apparent.

Minor points include typos and suggested revisions: adding "that the" before "gradient" on line 18, changing "quality of the estimate" to "estimates" on line 38, revising the sentence on line 60 to "to characterize under which conditions efficient algorithms...," and clarifying the definition of O(d^1/2)-separated on line 138. Additionally, the sentence on line 172 appears redundant, and the phrase "well-separated constant" on line 218 requires explanation.
