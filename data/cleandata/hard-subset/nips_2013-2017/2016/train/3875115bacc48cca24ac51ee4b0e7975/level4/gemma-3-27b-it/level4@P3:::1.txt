This paper investigates the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), focusing on scenarios where it may fail to find global optima. The authors present a comprehensive set of results demonstrating that, even with well-separated components and an infinite number of samples, EM can converge to suboptimal solutions. Specifically, they show (1) the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function for mixtures of three or more Gaussians, resolving a conjecture by Srebro [2007]; (2) a vanishingly small probability (exponentially decreasing with the number of components, *M*) of successful convergence with random initialization; and (3) that, unlike saddle points, bad local maxima are the primary obstacle to convergence for first-order EM methods. 

The paper is well-structured, clearly presenting the problem, related work, and key contributions. It builds upon existing literature regarding the challenges of estimating GMMs, citing foundational work by Teicher [1963], Genovese and Wasserman [2000], and more recent analyses of EM by Dasgupta and Schulman [2007] and Balakrishnan et al. [2015]. The connection to the broader context of non-convex optimization, including work on high-dimensional regression by Loh and Wainwright [2013] and Wang et al. [2014], is also appropriately highlighted.  The mathematical exposition is rigorous, and the proofs, while detailed (as expected for NIPS), appear sound.

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees regarding the behavior of EM, going beyond empirical observations.
*   **Resolution of Open Problem:**  The negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMM estimation.
*   **Comprehensive Results:** The combination of results concerning local maxima, initialization failure, and the role of saddle points paints a complete picture of the challenges.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making the complex technical details accessible.
*   **Relevance:** The findings are relevant to a wide range of applications utilizing GMMs, as they highlight the need for careful initialization strategies.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Surprise:** While mathematically rigorous and insightful, the core findings – that EM can get stuck in local optima, especially with poor initialization, and that this is more likely as the number of components increases – are not entirely unexpected within the field. The connection to the well-studied difficulties of k-means clustering (and Lloyd’s heuristic) isn’t explicitly drawn, which weakens the claim of novelty.  Kumar and Kannan's work on k-means convergence guarantees, for example, provides a useful frame of reference that could be explored to further contextualize these results.
*   **Restricted Setting:** The analysis focuses on a specific, albeit important, setting: equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians. While the authors acknowledge this simplification, it limits the direct applicability of the results to more general GMMs. The extent to which these findings generalize to anisotropic or non-uniform weighting schemes is unclear.
*   **Practical Impact:** While the paper identifies a problem, it doesn't offer concrete solutions.  The paper points to the need for careful initialization, but doesn’t propose novel initialization schemes beyond the standard random sampling from data.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a mathematically sound and rigorous analysis of a fundamental problem in statistical estimation.
*   The resolution of Srebro’s conjecture is a significant theoretical result.
*   The comprehensive nature of the findings provides valuable insights into the behavior of EM.
*   The clarity of presentation makes the paper accessible to a broad audience within NIPS.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The core findings, while rigorous, are not dramatically surprising to researchers familiar with clustering and non-convex optimization.
*   The limited scope (spherical Gaussians, equal weights) reduces the immediate practical impact of the results.
*   The paper does not offer new algorithmic solutions to mitigate the identified problems.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of the limitations of the EM algorithm for GMM estimation. While the results may not be entirely surprising, the rigorous analysis and resolution of the open conjecture are significant achievements. The paper is well-written and clearly presented.  Given the strengths, I believe the paper is **acceptably strong** for NIPS, but it would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed comparison to existing results in k-means clustering and a discussion of potential directions for developing more robust initialization strategies. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Explicitly discuss the connections to k-means clustering and Lloyd's heuristic, highlighting similarities and differences in convergence guarantees and failure modes.
2.  Briefly address the potential for extending these results to more general GMMs (e.g., anisotropic covariances, unequal weights).
3.  Include a short discussion of potential avenues for developing improved initialization methods, even if a full solution is beyond the scope of the paper.
