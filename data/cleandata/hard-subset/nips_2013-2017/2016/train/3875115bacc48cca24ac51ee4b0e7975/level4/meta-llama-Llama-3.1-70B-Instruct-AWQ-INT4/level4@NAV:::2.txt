This paper investigates the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the set of mean parameters in a Gaussian mixture model, where the number of Gaussian variables, mixture weights, and covariance matrices are known and fixed. The paper takes a theoretical approach and presents three key findings that highlight limitations of the EM algorithm. Firstly, it demonstrates that even with large sample sizes, local maxima can exist in the likelihood function with arbitrarily lower likelihood values compared to the global maximum. Secondly, the EM algorithm is shown to converge with high probability to a critical point with a likelihood value significantly lower than the maximum when initialized randomly. Lastly, similar negative results are obtained for the gradient EM algorithm. I find the question addressed in this paper to be intriguing and deserving of attention. To facilitate rigorous proofs, the authors have restricted their analysis to a simplified Gaussian mixture model with a known number of components, uniform weights, and identity covariance matrices. While these simplifications may seem limiting, I believe they are justified given the technical nature of the proofs and the insight provided by the results. The findings are not only interesting but also offer valuable insights into the practical behavior of the EM algorithm. Notably, the paper presents its analysis in the context of general dimension d, although the proofs focus on the d=1 case. It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss the feasibility of extending their proofs to higher dimensions and highlight any additional challenges that may arise. My primary concern with the paper lies in the proof of Theorem 1. I find the proof to be overly concise, particularly between lines 288 and 306. The authors provide limits of supremums of likelihood functions over specific domains, which, while plausible, may benefit from further elaboration. Specifically, I am uncertain about the validity of taking limits of maxima over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains. Additionally, the authors' assertion of continuity of the likelihood function does not directly imply the stated convergence properties. To fully convince the reader of the proof's validity, I believe a more detailed explanation is necessary. Furthermore, I found the section on the Gradient EM (lines 162-172) to be somewhat challenging to follow. Providing additional context for the derivation of Equation (5) would enhance the clarity of this section.
