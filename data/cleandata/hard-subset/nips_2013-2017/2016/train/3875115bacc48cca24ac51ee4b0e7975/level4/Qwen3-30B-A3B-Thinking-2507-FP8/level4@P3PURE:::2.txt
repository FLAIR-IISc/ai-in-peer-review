

This paper investigates the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters in a Gaussian mixture model with known, fixed components: the number of Gaussians, their uniform weights, and identity covariance matrices. The work presents three theoretical negative results regarding the EM algorithm's behavior. First, the likelihood function may possess local maxima with values arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum, even for large sample sizes. Second, when randomly initialized, the EM algorithm converges with high probability to a critical point whose likelihood is arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum. Third, analogous negative results hold for the gradient EM algorithm. The question addressed is highly relevant and merits attention. The authors' restriction to a simplified model—specifically, known component count, uniform weights, and identity covariances—is justified given the technical complexity of the proofs and the informative nature of the results. These findings offer valuable theoretical insights into the practical behavior of EM.  

A notable point is that while the paper claims generality for dimension $d$, the proofs are restricted to $d=1$. The authors should clarify whether similar results extend to arbitrary $d$ and, if not, briefly discuss the additional challenges in higher dimensions.  

My primary concern pertains to Theorem 1. The proof, particularly the passage from lines 288 to 306, is insufficiently detailed. The authors compute limits (as $\gamma$ or $R \to \infty$) of suprema of the likelihood over unbounded domains, but the justification for transitioning from pointwise limits to limits of suprema requires rigorous treatment. While the computations for fixed parameters appear sound, the convergence of suprema over parameter-dependent unbounded sets is non-trivial. Continuity of the likelihood alone does not suffice to establish the claimed limits; additional arguments (e.g., uniform convergence, compactness arguments) are needed. A more detailed proof, particularly in this section, would strengthen the paper's validity.  

Additionally, the Gradient EM analysis (lines 162–172) is less transparent than the rest of the paper. The derivation of Equation (5) should be clarified to improve readability and ensure the argument's correctness.
