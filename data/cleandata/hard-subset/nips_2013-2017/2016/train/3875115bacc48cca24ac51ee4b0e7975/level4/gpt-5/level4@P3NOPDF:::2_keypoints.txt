- Paper analyzes EM for estimating means in GMMs with known/fixed number of components, weights, and covariances, focusing on uniform weights and identity covariances.
- Main theoretical findings: (1) likelihood can have suboptimal local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global maximum; (2) random-initialized EM converges with high probability to such bad stationary points; (3) analogous negative result for gradient EM.
- Contribution is timely and informative: technically nontrivial negative results in a justified simplified setting with practical implications for initialization and interpretation of EM.
- Major exposition issue: proof of Theorem 1 is too terse when interchanging limits and suprema over growing/unbounded domains and needs rigorous justification.
- Suggested remedies for Theorem 1: use compact truncation and uniform convergence, apply Berge’s maximum theorem/argmax convergence tools, or provide monotonicity/coercivity/tightness arguments; if present in supplement, cite/move key lemmas to the main text.
- Gradient EM section (lines 162–172) and Equation (5) are hard to follow; the derivation must be shown explicitly, specifying the objective differentiated, variable, identities used, step sizes, projections, and the exact update rule.
- Clarify how the gradient EM negative result parallels standard EM and list any additional assumptions required.
- Dimension issues: exposition implies general d but proofs focus on d=1; explicitly state which results are one-dimensional, whether/how constructions generalize to d>1, and discuss added difficulties in higher dimensions.
- Precision in probability/quantifier statements: state sources of randomness (data vs. initialization), the asymptotic regime (fixed n, large n, n→∞), and the order of quantifiers when claiming “high probability.”
- Early and explicit statement of assumptions/scope: emphasize known/fixed components, uniform weights, and identity covariances and remind readers where these are used.
- Add a short practical discussion contrasting these negative results with positive results under separation/regularity, to guide practitioners on when pathologies are mitigated.
- Minor presentation fixes: terminology (use “GMM”, “number of components”), wording corrections (“randomly initialized”, “arbitrarily smaller”, “stationary point”), and consider a small illustrative figure of the 1D likelihood landscape.
- Overall recommendation: interesting, useful theoretical contribution that would be substantially improved by strengthening Theorem 1’s proof, clarifying gradient EM derivations, and being explicit about dimension and probability quantifiers.
