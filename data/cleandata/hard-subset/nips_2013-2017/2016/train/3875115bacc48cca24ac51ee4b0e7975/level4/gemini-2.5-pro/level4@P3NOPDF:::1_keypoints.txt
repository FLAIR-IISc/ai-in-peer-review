- EM can converge to a spurious local maximum even for a mixture of three well-separated spherical Gaussians with access to the population distribution (infinite samples).  
- For a k-component Gaussian mixture, random initialization succeeds at best with probability exp(-Ω(k)).  
- Gradient EM avoids strict saddle points, implying suboptimal local maxima — not saddles — are the primary barrier to global convergence.  
- Central counterexample: two nearby components plus one distant component create a local maximum with one center between the close pair and two centers near the distant component.  
- In the high-separation regime (≫√d) the Gaussian-likelihood behavior becomes functionally similar to the k-means objective due to negligible overlap.  
- The 3-component counterexample generalizes to k components via a recursive construction; technically nontrivial but conceptually familiar.  
- The random-init failure rate mirrors known k-means behavior and motivates advanced initializations such as k-means++ or k log k seeding.  
- The paper gives rigorous, unified proofs formalizing long-held intuitions about EM’s non-convergence.  
- The novelty/impact is limited because the failure modes closely parallel well-documented k-means pathologies.  
- Improvement suggestion: explicitly compare and relate the results to extensive k-means literature (e.g., Lloyd’s algorithm analyses like Kumar & Kannan).  
- Clarification request: explain what new insights the analysis provides beyond translating known k-means pathologies to the Gaussian-mixture likelihood landscape, and contrast with prior EM analyses (e.g., Balakrishnan, Wainwright, Yu).
