- Paper studies the population likelihood landscape of GMMs and the behavior of EM in an idealized setting (known M, uniform weights, identity covariances).  
- Main theoretical result: constructs a simple 1-D, well-separated GMM (M ≥ 3) whose population log-likelihood has spurious local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global maximum, refuting Srebro [2007].  
- Algorithmic consequence: standard EM and a first-order EM variant, under common random initialization, converge to these suboptimal critical points with probability exponentially approaching one in M.  
- For first-order EM, the failure is attributed to convergence to bad local maxima rather than strict saddles.  
- Strength: Resolves a notable open question and provides a fundamental negative result about the GMM optimization landscape.  
- Strength: Practical implication that simple random initialization is provably inadequate even in infinite-sample, well-behaved settings, motivating careful initialization methods.  
- Strength: Restricting to a simplified GMM setting enables clear, rigorous exposition of the core phenomenon.  
- Weakness: Proof of Theorem 1 is overly compressed; the limit and supremum interchange and boundary supremum arguments lack rigorous justification and need expansion.  
- Weakness: Results and constructions are presented only for d=1; the paper should explicitly address generalization to higher dimensions and potential technical challenges.  
- Weakness: The first-order EM section is not fully self-contained; it needs a brief derivation of the population update and clearer linkage to gradient ascent on the likelihood.
