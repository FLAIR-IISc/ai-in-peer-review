This paper investigates the structure of local maxima in the population log-likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) with equal mixing weights and identity covariance matrices. The authors present three main results: (1) They provide a counterexample demonstrating the existence of “bad” local maxima – those with arbitrarily worse log-likelihood values than the global maximum – for a mixture of three Gaussians in one dimension. This resolves an open question posed by Srebro [2007]. (2) They show that, with random initialization, the EM and first-order EM algorithms converge to a bad critical point with a probability that decreases exponentially with the number of mixture components (M). (3) They prove that the first-order EM algorithm almost surely does *not* converge to a strict saddle point, suggesting that the poor performance of first-order methods stems from bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon existing work in mixture model identifiability [Teicher, 1963, Allman et al., 2009], convergence rates [Genovese & Wasserman, 2000, Ghosal & Van Der Vaart, 2001, Chen, 1995, Ho & Nguyen, 2015], and the analysis of EM [Dempster et al., 1977, Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007, Balakrishnan et al., 2015]. They appropriately connect their work to recent analyses of optimization landscapes in high dimensions [Loh & Wainwright, 2013, Wang et al., 2014, Pascanu et al., 2014, Lee et al., 2016, Panageas & Piliouras, 2016].  The introduction clearly articulates the problem and its relevance, highlighting the challenges of estimating GMMs despite their widespread use.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees about the existence of bad local maxima and the behavior of the EM algorithm. These are non-trivial results that address a long-standing open question.
*   **Novelty and Insight:** The connection between the GMM mean configuration and the structure of the log-likelihood landscape is insightful and original. The hierarchical construction used to prove Theorem 2 is particularly clever.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presents the mathematical details and intuition behind the results. The notation is consistent, and the proofs are well-structured.
*   **Practical Implications:** The findings have important practical implications for the use of EM and related algorithms, emphasizing the need for careful initialization strategies.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature, properly citing and contextualizing their work within the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Specific Setting:** The results are derived under strong assumptions (equal mixing weights, identity covariance). While the authors acknowledge this, it would be valuable to briefly discuss the potential for extending these findings to more general GMMs.
*   **Complexity of Initialization:** While the paper demonstrates the failure of random initialization, it doesn't offer concrete guidance on *how* to initialize effectively in practice beyond suggesting clustering-based approaches [Arora et al., 2005, Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007, Vempala & Wang, 2002, Chaudhuri & Rao, 2008].  A discussion of more practical initialization techniques could strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper is highly theoretical, and lacks any empirical validation to support the theoretical findings. Although the authors focus on population-level results, some simulations demonstrating the behavior of EM on finite samples could add weight to the conclusions.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a fundamental open problem in statistical estimation with rigorous mathematical analysis.
*   The results are novel, insightful, and have the potential to impact both theoretical understanding and practical algorithm design.
*   The presentation is exceptionally clear and well-organized.
*   The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS: quality, clarity, originality, and significance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumptions limit the immediate applicability of the results to real-world scenarios.
*   The lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.
*   The paper doesn’t provide definitive guidance on optimal initialization strategies.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and important paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of Gaussian Mixture Models and the EM algorithm. While the assumptions are restrictive and empirical validation is lacking, the theoretical results are compelling and well-presented. The originality and potential impact of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with a strong encouragement for future work exploring the generalization of these results to more general GMMs and the development of practical initialization schemes.
