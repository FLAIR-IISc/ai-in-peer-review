- Paper studies EM for estimating mixture Gaussian means with known component count, weights, and covariances.
- Proves three negative results showing problematic behavior of EM in this setting.
- First: there exist local maxima with likelihood arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum, even for large samples (resolves Srebro 2007 open question).
- This first result implies careful initialization is necessary in practice, even under favorable conditions.
- Second: random initialization leads EM, with high probability, to critical points with likelihood arbitrarily below the maximum, so repeated random restarts plus local search can fail under realistic computational limits.
- Third: the same negative result holds for gradient EM; the first-order EM with suitable step-size almost surely avoids strict saddles, implying failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.
- Restricting analysis to the simplified model (known, uniform weights and identity covariances) is reasonable given proof complexity.
- Reviewer requests more clarity/applicability discussion for general dimension d, since proofs focus mainly on d=1.
- Concern that Theorem 1’s proof (lines 288–306) is too concise; ask for expanded limits/supremum calculations and convergence types.
- Request clarification of the Gradient EM section (lines 162–172), specifically derivation/assumptions behind Equation (5).
- Overall assessment: results are interesting and valuable but paper needs revisions addressing the above clarifications to strengthen contribution.
