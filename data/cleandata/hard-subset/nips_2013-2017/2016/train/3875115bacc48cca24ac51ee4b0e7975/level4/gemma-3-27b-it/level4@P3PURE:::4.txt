This paper studies the structure of local maxima of the log-likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) with equal mixing weights and identity covariance matrices. It presents three related results in this area: 1. A counter-example demonstrating the existence of local maxima (for k=3 and d=1) whose likelihood can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. 2. A demonstration, using random initialization, that the probability of escaping a bad local maximum can be exponentially small in *k* when the means of the Gaussians are configured in a specific manner. 3. A proof that gradient EM does not converge to a saddle point of the log-likelihood almost surely. 

**Summary:** This paper investigates the structure of local maxima in the log-likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (with equal mixing weights and identity covariance). It presents three related results: a counter-example illustrating arbitrarily poor local maxima, an analysis of convergence probability with random initialization, and a result concerning convergence to saddle points.

**Technical Quality:** I focused on the proof of Theorem 1 in the main paper and found it to be sound. Based on this analysis, the proof idea for Theorem 2 also appears reasonable, although I did not verify the details in the Appendix. All three theorems are clearly explained and well-interpreted following their statements.

**Novelty & Originality:** The paper establishes a connection between the mean configuration of a GMM and the local maxima of its log-likelihood function. This observation, while intuitive, is highly original. Furthermore, the key idea underlying Theorem 2 – that the hierarchical grouping structure of the true means determines the local maxima for *k* > 3, building upon Theorem 1 – is particularly clever.

**Potential Impact & Usefulness:** The results presented here could have significant impact in both theoretical and practical domains. Theoretically, this work could inspire further research into the local optima structure of related clustering tasks, such as *k*-means. It would also be interesting to investigate whether the result in Lemma 7 extends to cases where *d* > 1. Practically, the findings emphasize the importance of seeding sufficient points within each true cluster and may offer valuable insights for the design of new algorithms.

**Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presents its results. The authors effectively explain the intuition behind their analysis, clearly introducing the problem setup and providing insightful interpretations of all their findings.
