The paper studies the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models, focusing on the simplest setting: isotropic Gaussian components with known variance, and a well-specified model. The authors show that local modes exist even with infinite data, and that these modes can be arbitrarily suboptimal in terms of log-likelihood gap from the global optimum. The analysis concentrates on particular configurations—especially when component means are partitioned into well-separated clusters—and shows that, under such configurations, random initialization causes EM to miss the global optimum with (almost) certainty as the number of components grows. The paper is clearly written, with occasional typos.

I have two main concerns about practical relevance.

1) Theorem 1 establishes the existence of local modes even with infinite data, under a specific arrangement of the true component means. How likely is such a configuration in practice? I would be more persuaded of the practical significance if the paper assumed a distribution over the true centers and then quantified the probability that “bad” configurations arise, showing that this probability increases with the number of components k or with dimension d. Lines 210–218 suggest Theorem 2 addresses this, but I am not convinced it does. A clear statement quantifying the likelihood of “bad” configurations under explicit assumptions would strengthen the paper. To be clear, here I mean “bad” configurations of the true centers, not “bad” initializations.

2) In Theorem 2, initialization of EM plays a key role. The authors consider initializing component centers by random samples from the mixture. Under certain center configurations, EM often fails to reach the global optimum with such random starts. Consider data from three Gaussians: two are close together and one is far apart, with N points per component. Then roughly 2N points lie near the close pair and N near the far component. It seems odd, in practice, to initialize EM with two centers in the sparser region (N points) and only one in the denser region (2N points), especially when the clusters are well separated. Is pure random initialization a practically relevant model of how practitioners initialize EM when the data are clearly clustered? Some discussion comparing random initialization to widely used heuristics (e.g., k-means++ or data-dependent seeding) would help clarify the scope.

Line-specific questions and requests:

- L74–81: I am unsure what this example is intended to demonstrate. It does not convince me that Srebro’s conjecture holds when k = 2. What is the precise purpose of this example?

- L233–235: I understand requirement (2), but why is requirement (1) necessary? Are you saying that if a center is initialized between two clusters, you cannot guarantee it will move toward the “correct” cluster?

- L267–273: You show EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point. From a practical standpoint, why is this important? Is convergence to a saddle point more or less problematic than convergence to a local optimum?

Overall, the results are interesting and the negative examples are carefully constructed. To bolster practical relevance, I encourage the authors to (i) quantify the probability of “bad” true-center configurations under plausible generative assumptions, and (ii) discuss how the failure modes interact with more realistic initialization schemes beyond pure random seeding.
