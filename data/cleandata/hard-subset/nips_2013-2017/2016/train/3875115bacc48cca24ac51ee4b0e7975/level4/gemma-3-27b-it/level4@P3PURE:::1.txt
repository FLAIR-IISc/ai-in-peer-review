The paper studies the convergence of the EM algorithm (and iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation) for learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, presenting results that demonstrate non-convergence to global maxima. Specifically, the authors show: (1) even with three well-separated clusters and an infinite sample size, algorithms like EM can get stuck in local optima; (2) for mixtures of *k* Gaussians, random initialization of EM succeeds in finding a global maximum with at most exp(-\Omega(*k*)) probability; and (3) gradient EM will not generically converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that poor local maxima are the primary obstacle to convergence. The paper presents a coherent set of results establishing that EM does not converge to global maxima, even in the limit of infinite samples. However, I find these results unsurprising and the techniques employed largely follow expected lines. 

The main illustrative example involves a mixture with three components: two close together, and a third significantly distant. The authors demonstrate the existence of a local maximum where one center is positioned near the first two components, while the other two centers are near the far-off cluster. This behavior is reasonably predictable, particularly when the centers are separated by a distance >> \sqrt(*d*). Under such separation, cluster overlap is minimal, and learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians becomes analogous to *k*-means clustering. Similar problematic instances are known for Lloyd's algorithm in the context of *k*-means. This example is extended to *k* components via a recursive construction, which, while requiring effort, is again intuitively expected.  The observed failure of random initialization echoes similar behavior in *k*-means clustering, with a comparable failure probability; this motivates strategies for *k*-means such as initializing with *k* log *k* centers or using distance-squared sampling. 

In conclusion, while the results tell a coherent story regarding non-convergence to global optima, I believe the findings are largely consistent with prior expectations. 

Comments: 1. Kumar and Kannan provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd's heuristic for *k*-means clustering (and consequently for mixtures of Gaussians under sufficient separation conditions). While EM differs from Lloyd’s heuristic when Gaussians are not sufficiently separated (as noted by Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu), the algorithms appear essentially equivalent when there *is* large separation, as exemplified in the paper's examples. Therefore, a comparison with *k*-means and Lloyd's heuristic, contrasting the present results with existing theory, would be highly beneficial.
