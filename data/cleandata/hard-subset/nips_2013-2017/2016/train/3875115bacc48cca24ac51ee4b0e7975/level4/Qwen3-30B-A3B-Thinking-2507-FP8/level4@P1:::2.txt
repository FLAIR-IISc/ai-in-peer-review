

This paper addresses a fundamental question about the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically investigating the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function. The authors resolve an open question posed by Srebro (2007) by demonstrating that even for well-separated, equally-weighted mixtures of spherical Gaussians with M ≥ 3 components, the population likelihood can have local maxima whose log-likelihood values are arbitrarily worse than global optima. They further show that the EM algorithm (and a first-order variant) with random initialization converges to such suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)), while proving that the first-order EM algorithm avoids strict saddle points almost surely. These results highlight the critical importance of careful initialization in practical applications of EM, even in highly favorable settings.

The paper makes significant theoretical contributions to understanding the behavior of the EM algorithm in non-convex optimization contexts. Previous work on GMMs has largely focused on identifiability (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009), convergence rates (Chen, 1995; Ho and Nguyen, 2015), or algorithmic guarantees under strong separation assumptions (Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015). While these works established theoretical foundations for GMMs, they did not address the fundamental question of whether the likelihood surface contains bad local maxima. Srebro's conjecture that all local maxima would be global in the population setting for M ≥ 3 was a natural hypothesis given the success of EM in practice and the favorable properties of maximum likelihood estimation (Donoho and Liu, 1988). This paper directly refutes that conjecture, providing a rigorous negative answer with important implications for algorithm design.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The results are technically sound and address a long-standing open question in the field. The authors demonstrate that even in the most favorable settings for GMMs (equal weights, spherical Gaussians, well-separated components), local search algorithms cannot guarantee convergence to global optima. This has important practical implications, as it explains why repeated random initialization is often necessary in practice. The proof techniques for establishing the existence of bad local maxima are novel and could prove valuable for understanding other non-convex optimization problems. The authors also provide a clear theoretical explanation for the poor performance of EM in practice, distinguishing between bad local maxima and saddle points.

I have two main concerns regarding the paper. First, while the paper claims generality across dimensions, the proofs for Theorem 1 are restricted to d=1. The authors state that the results extend to arbitrary dimensions via standard empirical process arguments, but this claim is not substantiated in the main text. It would be valuable for the authors to briefly discuss whether the proof techniques would generalize to higher dimensions, or to explain the additional challenges that would arise for d > 1. This clarification would strengthen the paper's contribution.

Second, the proof of Theorem 1 appears to be somewhat brief, particularly in the section from line 288 to 306 where the authors analyze limits of supremums of likelihood functions over unbounded domains. The authors claim that the log-likelihood is continuous and use this to establish convergence properties, but the relationship between continuity and the types of convergence required for their argument is not sufficiently developed. More detailed justification would be helpful for readers to fully understand and verify the proof. While the paper is technically sophisticated, additional clarity in this section would make the argument more accessible and convincing.

I also found the exposition of the first-order EM algorithm (from line 162 to 172) somewhat difficult to follow. The derivation of Equation (5) could be clarified with a brief explanation of how the population M-step relates to the gradient update. A small elaboration here would improve the paper's overall clarity.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important question in the field. The results are significant for both theoretical understanding and practical implementation of the EM algorithm. The paper is well-structured and makes a clear contribution to the literature on Gaussian mixture models. The concerns I've raised are relatively minor and could be addressed with minor revisions to the paper. I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to NIPS and should be accepted.
