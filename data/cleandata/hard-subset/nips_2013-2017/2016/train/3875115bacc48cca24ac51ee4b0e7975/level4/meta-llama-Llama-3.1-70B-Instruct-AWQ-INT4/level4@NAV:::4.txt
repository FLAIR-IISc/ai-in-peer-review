This paper delves into the structure of local maxima of the log-likelihood function in Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) with equal mixing weights and identity covariance. Three key findings are presented: 1. A counter-example is provided, demonstrating that local maxima (for k=3 and d=1) can have arbitrarily poor likelihood compared to the global maximum. 2. The authors show that with random initialization, the probability of avoiding convergence to a suboptimal local maximum can be exponentially small in k, given a specific configuration of Gaussian means. 3. Furthermore, they prove that gradient Expectation-Maximization (EM) almost surely does not converge to a saddle point of the log-likelihood.

Technical Quality: I have reviewed the proofs in the main paper (Theorem 1) and found them to be sound. Although I did not thoroughly check the details in the Appendix, the analysis of Theorem 1 suggests that the proof idea for Theorem 2 is reasonable. The authors provide clear interpretations of all three theorems following their statements.

Novelty and Originality: The paper establishes a connection between the mean configuration of GMMs and the local maxima of their log-likelihood. This observation, while intuitive, is highly original. The key idea of Theorem 2, which leverages Theorem 1 to show that the hierarchical grouping structure of the true mean determines local maxima for k > 3, is particularly clever.

Potential Impact and Usefulness: I believe the results have significant implications for both theory and practice. Theoretically, they could inspire the study of local optima structures in related clustering tasks, such as k-means problems. It would be interesting to investigate whether the result in Lemma 7 holds for d > 1. Practically, the findings highlight the importance of seeding sufficient points in each true cluster and may provide valuable insights for designing new algorithms.

Clarity and Presentation: The paper excels in presenting its results and explaining the intuition behind its analysis. The authors clearly introduce the problem setup and provide intuitive interpretations of their findings.
