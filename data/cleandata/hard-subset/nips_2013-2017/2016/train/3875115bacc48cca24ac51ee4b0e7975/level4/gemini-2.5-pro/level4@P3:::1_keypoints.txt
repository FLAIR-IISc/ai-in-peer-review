- Paper studies population log-likelihood of equally-weighted, spherical, well-separated GMMs and EM algorithm behavior.  
- Main result 1: For M ≥ 3, the population log-likelihood has bad local maxima whose values can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.  
- Main result 2: EM and a first-order EM variant, with common random initialization, converge to these suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − exp(−Ω(M)), implying exponentially many restarts needed.  
- Main result 3: A first-order EM variant almost surely avoids strict saddle points, indicating failures stem from bad local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Strength: Resolves the open conjecture of Srebro (2007) with a definitive negative answer.  
- Strength: Provides rigorous, complete analysis with a clear construction of bad local maxima and a recursive argument for exponential failure probability.  
- Strength: Clarifies the distinction that poor local maxima, not saddles, are the primary barrier for EM in this setting.  
- Weakness: Paper lacks discussion of strong parallels to k-means clustering, especially in the well-separated regime.  
- Weakness: Conceptual novelty is muted because the phenomenon closely mirrors known k-means failure modes and random-initialization issues.  
- Detailed comment: Authors should add a section comparing their bad-optima construction to known Lloyd’s algorithm/k-means failure cases.  
- Detailed comment: The random-initialization failure analysis should be related to k-means literature (e.g., probability of failure and k-means++ motivation).  
- Detailed comment: Discuss how the high-separation regime compares to low-separation regimes (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and whether the k-means parallel breaks down.  
- For acceptance: Paper gives a definitive, rigorous resolution of a notable open question and links landscape theory to EM practice.  
- Against acceptance: Lack of framing against extensive k-means literature reduces perceived conceptual novelty and clarity of contribution.  
- Recommendation: Recommend acceptance, with a strong suggestion to revise and explicitly discuss connections to k-means to improve clarity and impact.
