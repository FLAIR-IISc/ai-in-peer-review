- Paper studies population likelihood landscape of equally weighted spherical GMMs (M ≥ 3) and algorithmic behavior of EM and first-order EM under random initialization.
- Resolves Srebro (2007) by constructing well-separated examples with spurious local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.
- Shows EM and first-order EM with centers drawn from the mixture converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e−Ω(M).
- Proves first-order EM (with step size s ∈ (0,1)) almost surely avoids strict saddles, implicating bad local maxima as the failure mode.
- Complements prior positive EM guarantees for special cases and connects to recent saddle-avoidance results in nonconvex optimization.
- Structural contribution: existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in 1D population likelihood for any M ≥ 3.
- Algorithmic contribution: exponentially small success probability under random initialization, with a fixed likelihood gap Cgap > 0.
- Landscape contribution: gradient-based first-order EM dynamics avoid strict saddles, focusing blame on local maxima.
- Technical claims are interesting and, if correct, settle a longstanding conjecture.
- Key proofs (esp. Theorem 1) are terse in steps exchanging limits and suprema over expanding domains and need more justification.
- Reviewer requests explicit lemmas for limit/sup exchanges, compactness/tightness, and uniform continuity/uniform convergence arguments.
- Clarity is generally good with helpful intuition, but derivation of first-order EM updates is hard to follow and should be made self-contained.
- Theorem 1 proof exposition should be expanded even at expense of condensing other parts.
- Novelty: negative resolution of Srebro’s conjecture in a favorable regime and an elegant recursive construction are original.
- Significance: results imply EM requires careful initialization even in well-specified, well-separated regimes, affecting theory and practice.
- Strengths: resolves an open question, links structural and algorithmic results, and highlights initialization’s role; connects to modern nonconvex theory.
- Weaknesses: insufficient rigor/detail in Theorem 1, mostly 1D constructions with limited discussion of higher dimensions, and unclear first-order EM derivation.
- Suggested improvements: add lemmas on lim/sup exchanges, uniform boundary likelihood bounds, derivation of gradient EM and stepsize role, discussion of higher-d extensions, and an illustrative M=3 figure.
- Minor fixes: standardize citations and reference formatting.
- Pros for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution with clear algorithmic implications and interesting constructions.
- Cons against acceptance: central proof lacks detail, limited higher-dimensional discussion, and some exposition issues.
- Overall recommendation: lean toward acceptance conditional on strengthening Theorem 1’s technical details and clarifying first-order EM derivation.
