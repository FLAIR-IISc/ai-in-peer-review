- Paper studies population log-likelihood local maxima for equally weighted spherical GMMs (M ≥ 3) and proves existence of arbitrarily bad suboptimal local maxima and high-probability EM convergence to such points.  
- First main result: constructs well-separated mixtures with suboptimal local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, refuting Srebro (2007).  
- Second main result: EM and first-order EM, with random initialization from the data-generating distribution, converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e−Ω(M); first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles.  
- Relation to prior work: builds on classical EM and recent local-convergence analyses, contrasting with polynomial-time clustering/moment methods; core contribution is a negative structural result linking landscape to algorithm behavior.  
- Strength: Provides a clear, negative resolution of a long-standing conjecture under favorable GMM conditions.  
- Strength: Uses careful structural construction and recursive arguments to show exponentially small success probability for random initialization.  
- Strength: Differentiates failures due to bad local maxima from saddle points using gradient dynamics results.  
- Concern 1: Practical prevalence of the constructed “bad” configurations is unclear—need probabilistic analysis under natural priors for center locations (scaling with M and d).  
- Concern 2: Random initialization baseline may be weaker than common practical heuristics (k-means, k-means++, multiple restarts); need robustness analysis to more realistic initializations.  
- Concern 3: The M = 2 example is presented but its intended takeaway regarding the conjecture boundary is unclear and needs clarification.  
- Concern 4: Initialization condition (1) requiring each init center to fall near a single top-level cluster needs justification—explain why centers between clusters are problematic.  
- Concern 5: Practical significance of first-order EM avoiding strict saddles is unclear if suboptimal maxima still dominate; compare prevalence/impact of saddles vs poor local optima.  
- Quality/clarity: Well written and technically careful, with useful proof techniques, though several typos and phrasing issues need fixing.  
- Originality: Novel and important negative structural and algorithmic results at the population level.  
- Significance: Highlights the critical role of initialization for EM even in well-specified, well-separated settings, tempering optimism from benign landscape results.  
- Pros for acceptance: Resolves an open problem rigorously, connects structure to algorithmic failure, and will influence theory and practice in mixture modeling.  
- Cons for acceptance: Unclear practical prevalence of bad instances, limited analysis of stronger/realistic initializations, and some expository points need clarification.  
- Specific edits: Multiple phrasing and typo fixes requested (e.g., L18, L38, L60, clarify L74–81 and L138 separation notation, remove redundancy at L172, L177, define separation at L218, fix duplicated “the” at L220, clarify Ω(k)/M at L225, explain L233–235, and other wording fixes).  
- Overall: Strong, well-executed theoretical paper; addressing prevalence of constructed configurations and robustness to practical initializations would improve impact.
