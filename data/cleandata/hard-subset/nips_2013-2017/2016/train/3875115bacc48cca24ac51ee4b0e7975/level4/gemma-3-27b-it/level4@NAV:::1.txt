The paper investigates the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, and related iterative methods for Maximum Likelihood Estimation, when applied to mixtures of spherical Gaussians. The key findings demonstrate scenarios where these algorithms fail to converge to global optima: even with well-separated clusters and an infinite number of samples, EM can become trapped in local optima; the probability of successful convergence via random initialization is at most exp(-\Omega(k)) for a k-component mixture; and gradient-based EM methods generally do not converge to strict saddle points, indicating that local maxima are the primary obstacle. The paper presents a consistent set of results establishing that EM does not guarantee convergence to a global maximum, even in the limit of infinite data.

However, the results presented feel largely unsurprising, and the techniques employed are rather standard. The primary illustrative example involves a 3-component mixture where two components are closely positioned while the third is distant. The analysis reveals a local maximum where one center is located near the first two components, and two centers are near the isolated cluster – a behavior largely anticipated, especially when the component centers are separated by a distance significantly greater than \sqrt{d}. Such separation diminishes cluster overlap, effectively reducing the problem to one akin to k-means clustering.  Analogous instances are known to cause issues for Lloyd's algorithm in k-means. This example is then generalized to k components via a recursive construction, a non-trivial but still predictable extension.  The failure of random initialization similarly echoes established results for k-means clustering, exhibiting the same order of failure probability, and mirroring the motivations for alternative initialization strategies like k log k center selection or distance-squared sampling.

In summary, the paper delivers a cohesive account of the challenges in achieving global optimality with EM. However, the conclusions align with existing expectations in the field. 

Comment: Recent work by Kumar and Kannan offers convergence guarantees for Lloyd’s heuristic in k-means clustering (and consequently for mixtures of Gaussians under conditions of sufficient component separation). While EM diverges from Lloyd’s heuristic when separations are insufficient (as highlighted by Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu), the two methods behave similarly in the regime of large separation—the very conditions present in the paper's illustrative examples. Therefore, a more direct comparison to k-means and Lloyd’s heuristic, explicitly contrasting these results, would be highly beneficial.
