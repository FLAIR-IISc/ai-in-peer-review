The paper investigates the existence of local modes in the log-likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models. Focusing on the simplest case – isotropic Gaussian components with known scale and a well-specified model – the authors demonstrate that local modes persist even with infinite data, and can be arbitrarily distant from the global optimum in terms of log-likelihood. The analysis centers on specific configurations of mixture centers, particularly those exhibiting well-spaced clusters, revealing that random initialization of the EM algorithm will almost certainly lead to convergence to a suboptimal local mode as the number of components increases. The paper is generally well-written, though contains some minor typographical errors. 

I have two primary concerns regarding the practical implications of this work. First, while Theorem 1 establishes the existence of local modes under specific center configurations, the likelihood of encountering such configurations in real-world datasets remains unclear. Strengthening the practical relevance would involve, for example, making assumptions about the distribution of mixture centers and demonstrating that “bad” configurations become more probable as the number of components (k) or the dimensionality of the problem grows. The authors state that Theorem 2 addresses this point (lines 210-218), but I find the connection unconvincing. A clearer statement quantifying the probability of encountering these detrimental configurations, under reasonable assumptions, would be beneficial. This refers to the probability of “bad” center *positions*, distinct from the probability of “bad” initializations.

Second, Theorem 2 examines the impact of EM initialization. The authors consider a random sampling initialization scheme, showing that, under specific center configurations, EM frequently fails to converge to the global optimum. However, consider a scenario with three Gaussians, where two are closely clustered and one is distant, and each Gaussian contributes an equal number of data points (N). This results in 2N points near the first cluster and N points near the third. It seems counterintuitive to initialize EM with 2 centers placed in the sparsely populated regions and only 1 center in the densely populated region, especially given the clear separation between the clusters. The practical relevance of random initialization is therefore questionable when data exhibits clear clustering structure. 

The example on lines 74-81 is unclear in its purpose. It doesn’t convincingly support the Sresbro conjecture for k=2, and its overall contribution is unclear. The necessity of requirement (1) in lines 233-235 is also unclear, while requirement (2) is understandable. Specifically, the argument that initializing a center between two clusters doesn't guarantee movement towards the correct cluster needs further justification. Finally, the result in lines 267-273 demonstrating that EM is unlikely to converge to a saddlepoint lacks clear practical significance; it's unclear whether converging to a saddlepoint is more or less problematic than converging to a local optimum.
