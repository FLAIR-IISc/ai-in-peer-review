

The paper investigates the convergence properties of the EM algorithm (and related iterative heuristics for Maximum Likelihood Estimation) in learning mixtures of spherical Gaussians, establishing three key results demonstrating non-convergence to global maxima: (1) Even with three sufficiently separated clusters and infinite samples, algorithms like EM can converge to local optima; (2) For mixtures of $k$ Gaussians, random initialization for EM succeeds with probability at most $\exp(-\Omega(k))$; (3) Gradient EM generically avoids strict saddle points, indicating that poor local maxima—not saddle points—are the primary obstacle. While the results form a coherent narrative regarding EM's failure to reach global optima (even with infinite samples), they largely follow expected theoretical lines and lack significant novelty. The core counterexample involves a three-component mixture where two clusters are close together and the third is far from both. The authors demonstrate that the log-likelihood objective admits a local maximum with one center near the two proximal clusters and two centers near the distant cluster—a behavior that is predictable under separation conditions exceeding $\sqrt{d}$, where spherical Gaussian mixtures resemble $k$-means (and similar bad examples exist for Lloyd's algorithm). The extension to $k$ components via a recursive construction is technically sound but follows anticipated patterns. Similarly, the failure of random initialization mirrors $k$-means clustering, where the same $\exp(-\Omega(k))$ failure probability motivates alternative initialization strategies (e.g., $k\log k$ centers or distance-squared sampling). In summary, while the results cohesively document EM's non-convergence, they do not substantially advance beyond established intuition for well-separated mixtures.  

Comments: Kumar and Kannan provide convergence guarantees for Lloyd's heuristic under sufficient separation conditions (and thus for Gaussian mixtures under similar assumptions). Although EM differs from Lloyd's heuristic when Gaussians are not sufficiently separated (as noted in Balakrishnan, Wainwright, and Yu), they become effectively equivalent under large separation (as in the paper's counterexamples). A comparative discussion contrasting this work with Kumar-Kannan's results and clarifying the relationship between EM and Lloyd's heuristic under separation thresholds would significantly strengthen the paper.
