This paper studies the expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the component means in a Gaussian mixture model. The number of components, the mixture weights, and the covariance matrices are assumed known and fixed. The paper is theoretical and proves three negative results: 1) Even for large sample sizes, the likelihood can have local maxima whose values are arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum. 2) With random initialization, EM converges with high probability to a critical point whose likelihood is arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum. 3) The same negative result holds for the gradient EM algorithm.

The question the paper addresses is interesting and important. To obtain rigorous proofs, the authors focus on a simplified Gaussian mixture setting: the number of components is known, the weights are known and uniform, and the covariances are identity. I do not view these simplifications as problematic, given the technical nature of the arguments and the informativeness of the results. In my view, the findings are very interesting and helpful for understanding EM in practice.

It should be noted that, while the paper is written as if treating general dimension d, the proofs cover only the case d = 1. The authors might clarify whether similar proofs are possible for arbitrary d and, if not, briefly describe the additional difficulties for d > 1.

My main concern is the proof of Theorem 1 (unfortunately, I did not read the proofs in the supplementary material). I find the proof too brief and would have preferred more detail, even at the expense of shortening some of the discussion. In particular, lines 288–306 provide limits (as gamma or R tend to infinity) of suprema of likelihood functions over certain domains. I have no issue with limits of the likelihood evaluated at fixed parameter values—for example, the three equations on line 288 seem correct. However, taking the limit of a maximum is not entirely straightforward when the domain is unbounded or depends on parameters that diverge. Similarly, while the likelihood is continuous, this alone does not directly imply the types of convergence claimed, in my view. More detail—and thus a longer proof—would help fully convince the reader of the validity of Theorem 1.

Finally, I have a minor concern about the section on Gradient EM (lines 162–172). I found this part harder to follow than the rest of the paper. In particular, additional explanation of how Equation (5) is derived would be helpful.
