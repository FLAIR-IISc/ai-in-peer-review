Summary
This paper analyzes the landscape of the population log-likelihood for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with equal mixing weights and identity covariance, focusing on the consequences for EM and gradient-based variants. It presents three main results. First, it gives a counterexample for k = 3 components in d = 1 showing that the population log-likelihood can have bad local maxima whose values are arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. Second, it shows that with random initialization, the probability of avoiding a bad critical point can be exponentially small in k when the true means have a particular (but well-motivated) hierarchical configuration; consequently, one needs exponentially many random restarts to succeed with constant probability. Third, it shows that gradient EM (first-order EM) almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that the observed failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points. Together, these results clarify the structural pitfalls of likelihood-based estimation and the algorithmic consequences for EM, even in favorable settings.

Technical quality
I carefully checked the proof of Theorem 1 in the main paper and found it sound. The geometric construction and neighborhood argument establishing the existence of a suboptimal local maximum are clearly explained. Based on the intuition provided, the proof strategy for Theorem 2—using a recursive, hierarchical clustering of true centers to drive the success probability down to e−Ω(k)—also seems reasonable, though I did not verify all details in the appendix. The statements are well contextualized, and the interpretation of each theorem is clear. The avoidance of strict saddle points by gradient EM is consistent with recent results on gradient methods.

Novelty and originality
The paper convincingly ties the geometry of the true mean configuration to the structure of local maxima in the population likelihood. While the intuition is natural, the formal negative result (a simple, well-separated 1D, k = 3 example with arbitrarily bad local maxima) is strong and, to my knowledge, new in this precise form. The hierarchical construction used to obtain exponentially small success probabilities (building on the k = 3 construction) is particularly clever.

Significance and potential impact
These results are important for both theory and practice. Theoretically, they sharpen our understanding of the nonconvex landscape for GMMs and offer techniques that could inform analyses of related clustering objectives (e.g., k-means) and other mixture models. Practically, they underscore the importance of careful initialization: seeding enough points within each true cluster is crucial, and naive random restarts may be ineffective unless used exponentially many times. It would also be interesting to examine whether the structural result used in Lemma 7 extends to d > 1.

Clarity and presentation
The paper is very well written. The problem setup is clearly introduced, and the intuition behind the constructions and their consequences is well developed. The explanations accompanying the theorems help the reader understand both the claims and their implications.

Suggestions and questions
- Scope: The main counterexample is in d = 1 with k = 3. While this is sufficient to refute the conjecture, a brief discussion of how the construction might extend—or fail to extend—to higher dimensions would strengthen the paper. In particular, clarifying whether the argument behind Lemma 7 carries over to d > 1 would be valuable.
- Practical guidance: Since the results argue for the necessity of careful initialization, a short discussion of practical seeding strategies (e.g., moment-based pilot estimators or robust heuristics) and when they might succeed would increase practical impact.
- Empirical illustration: Although the focus is theoretical, a simple empirical demonstration of the hierarchical construction’s effect on EM’s success probability would be informative.

Pros
- Clear, rigorous structural counterexample showing arbitrarily bad local maxima in a clean, well-separated setting.
- Strong algorithmic consequence: random initialization fails with probability 1 − e−Ω(k).
- Clarifies that failures are due to bad local maxima rather than strict saddle points.
- Elegant and insightful constructions; proofs are well motivated and explained.

Cons
- Main negative example is in 1D and for k ≥ 3; extension to higher dimensions is not established.
- The exponential failure result relies on a specific hierarchical mean configuration; the prevalence of such configurations in practice is not discussed.
- I did not verify all technical details in the appendix (Theorem 2 and related lemmas).

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed paper with clear contributions to our understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes and EM’s behavior. The results are novel, technically solid (as far as I checked), and significant, with insightful implications for initialization in practice. I recommend acceptance.
