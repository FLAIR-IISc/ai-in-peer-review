Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the population (infinite-sample) likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 equally weighted, spherical components, and the algorithmic behavior of EM and first-order EM under random initialization. It resolves an open question posed by Srebro (2007) by constructing well-separated examples in which the population likelihood admits spurious local maxima whose values are arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. It then shows that EM and a first-order variant, when initialized by drawing centers from the mixture (the population analogue of picking random data points), converge to such suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). Finally, it proves that, with a suitable step size, first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, suggesting that failures in practice arise from bad local maxima rather than saddles.

These results complement a line of work analyzing EM and alternatives for learning mixtures. Positive guarantees for EM under good initialization and separation are known for two-component mixtures (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and for specialized variants (Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007). Robust and provable alternatives via clustering or moments exist (e.g., Vempala and Wang, 2002; Arora et al., 2005; Moitra and Valiant, 2010; Hsu and Kakade, 2013). On the optimization side, the finding that gradient-based dynamics avoid strict saddles connects to Lee et al. (2016) and Panageas and Piliouras (2016). The authors’ negative results clarify that, even in highly favorable settings and at the population level, local search methods require careful initialization.

Main contributions:
- Structural: For any M ≥ 3, existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood of well-separated, equally weighted, spherical GMMs in 1D.
- Algorithmic: With random initialization, EM and first-order EM converge with probability ≥ 1 − e−cM to suboptimal critical points with a fixed likelihood gap Cgap > 0.
- Saddle-point landscape: First-order EM (with stepsize s ∈ (0, 1)) almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, so poor performance is attributable to bad local maxima.

Quality:
The technical claims are interesting and, if correct, settle a long-standing conjecture. The constructions (e.g., the recursive clustering of true centers) are plausible and well motivated, and the algorithmic consequences are carefully argued. However, parts of the proof of Theorem 1 (in particular, the steps exchanging limits and suprema over domains that expand with the limit parameters, lines 288–306) are terse. The argument would benefit from explicit lemmas justifying:
- When and how limits can pass through suprema over parameter sets that depend on the diverging quantities (γ, R).
- Compactness or tightness arguments ensuring maxima are attained on the boundary and that boundary likelihood is strictly smaller than the interior point (µ).
- Uniform continuity or uniform convergence needed for the limiting inequalities.

Providing these details would substantially strengthen confidence in the central structural result.

Clarity:
The paper is, overall, clearly written and well organized, with helpful intuition (e.g., geometric explanation for M = 3 and the two-vs.-many component discussion). Two parts were harder to follow:
- Derivation of first-order EM updates (lines 162–172). Please provide a brief, self-contained derivation from the Q-function to the gradient EM update and its equivalence to gradient ascent on L, or give a precise pointer and short recap from Balakrishnan et al. (2015).
- Theorem 1 proof exposition (see Quality). A longer, more explicit proof here would be preferable, even at the expense of condensing some nonessential discussion.

Originality:
Resolving Srebro’s conjecture negatively in a favorable regime is novel. The recursive construction driving the exponentially small success probability under random initialization is elegant. The connection to strict saddle avoidance for first-order EM is timely and aligns with recent nonconvex optimization theory.

Significance:
The results have clear implications: in practice, EM for GMMs demands careful initialization even in well-specified, well-separated regimes, and repeated random restarts may be computationally prohibitive as M grows. This will influence both theoretical analyses (e.g., what assumptions are necessary for EM guarantees) and practical guidance (e.g., use of moment-based pilot estimators or informed initializers).

Strengths:
- Resolves an open question with crisp, negative results.
- Provides both structural and algorithmic consequences, tightly linked.
- Highlights the role of initialization in a widely used algorithm.
- Bridges to modern insights about saddle points in nonconvex optimization.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Proof of Theorem 1 is too concise at key technical junctures; needs rigorous justification of limit/supremum exchanges and boundary arguments.
- Most constructions and proofs are for d = 1. The paper presents the setting in general d but does not discuss extensions. Clarifying which parts extend to higher dimensions, or what obstacles arise, would help.
- The exposition of first-order EM derivations could be clearer.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Expand Theorem 1’s proof with:
  - A lemma on exchanging lim and sup over expanding domains (e.g., via equicontinuity, monotone families, or epi-convergence), and an explicit construction of the neighborhood and its boundary.
  - A uniform bound showing the likelihood on the boundary is strictly below L(µ) by a fixed margin.
- Add a short derivation of the gradient EM update and a brief proof of its equivalence to gradient ascent on L, including the role of the stepsize bound s ∈ (0, 1).
- Discuss the extension beyond d = 1: do the constructions trivially embed into higher dimensions (e.g., by placing all means on a line), or are there phenomena unique to higher d?
- Consider a small illustrative figure showing basins of attraction in the M = 3 construction to aid intuition.
- Minor: standardize citations and fix formatting inconsistencies in the references.

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves an active, longstanding question; strong theoretical contribution.
- Clear algorithmic implications for a central method (EM).
- Technically interesting constructions with broader relevance to nonconvex optimization.

Cons against acceptance:
- Central proof (Theorem 1) lacks some technical detail; readers may struggle to verify key steps without additional justification.
- Limited discussion of higher-dimensional extensions despite general-d framing.
- Some exposition (first-order EM section) could be clearer.

Overall recommendation:
A strong, timely theoretical paper with significant implications for practice. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening the exposition and technical details in Theorem 1’s proof and clarifying the first-order EM derivation.
