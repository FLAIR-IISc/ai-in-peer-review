This paper resolves an open question posed by Srebro [2007]: "Are there local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of Gaussian mixture estimation?". The authors answer this question in the negative by constructing a general class of counterexamples.

The paper begins by outlining standard likelihood maximization schemes for Gaussian Mixture Models, including the EM and gradient EM algorithms, and discusses common initialization strategies. The authors then present their primary theorems, which formalize the counterexample to Srebro's question. They further show that even with a common random initialization scheme, the EM and gradient EM algorithms converge to these spurious, non-optimal local maxima with exponentially high probability. To build intuition for their proofs, the authors first analyze a simple case with k=3 components in d=1 dimension.

The paper is very clear, well-written, and effectively balances the theoretical rigor of its results with intuitive explanations, all within the space constraints of the format. The question answered by the authors is of significant importance for advancing the understanding of issues in non-convex optimization, even in the asymptotic limit for simple distributions.

While I did not carefully check the supplementary material, the analysis could be strengthened by providing estimates for the constants in the probability bounds. This is important because, in practice, the number of components (`k`) is often not excessively large. Such estimates would help clarify the regime where convergence to bad local maxima becomes a practical concern, potentially identifying a "phase transition." If an analytical treatment of the constants is too complex, supporting simulations that demonstrate this phenomenon would be a valuable addition. However, this issue does not detract from the novelty or significant impact of this paper.
