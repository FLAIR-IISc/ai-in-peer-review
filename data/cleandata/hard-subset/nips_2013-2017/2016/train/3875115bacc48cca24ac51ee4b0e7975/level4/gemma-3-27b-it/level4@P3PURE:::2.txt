This paper addresses the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the mean parameters of a mixture of Gaussian variables. The number of Gaussian components, the mixture weights, and the covariance matrices are assumed to be known and fixed. The paper presents a theoretical analysis, proving three negative results concerning the EM algorithm: 1) The likelihood function can possess local maxima, even with large sample sizes, with likelihood values arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum. 2) When randomly initialized, the EM algorithm converges with high probability to a critical point with a likelihood value arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum. 3) The same negative result holds for the gradient EM algorithm. 

I find the question addressed by this paper interesting and worthy of investigation. To facilitate rigorous proofs, the authors restrict their analysis to a simplified mixture of Gaussian model where the number of components is known, the weights are known and uniform, and the covariance matrices are equal to the identity matrix. While these simplifications are present, I do not believe they significantly detract from the value of the work, given the inherent technical complexity of the proofs and the informative nature of the results. These findings are valuable and can contribute to a better understanding of the behavior of the EM algorithm in practical applications.

It is important to note that the paper presents results for the general dimension *d*, but the proofs are explicitly for the case *d*=1. The authors could strengthen the paper by briefly discussing the feasibility of extending the proofs to arbitrary dimension *d*, and if such an extension is not possible, outlining the additional difficulties encountered when *d* > 1.

My primary concern lies with the proof of Theorem 1 (I have not examined the detailed proofs in the supplementary material). I find the proof somewhat concise, and a more extended explanation would be beneficial, even at the cost of shortening other sections. Specifically, the portion of the proof from line 288 to 306 requires further clarification. While I do not dispute the computation of limits of likelihood functions evaluated at fixed parameters (e.g., the three equations on line 288 appear correct), establishing the limit of a *maximum* is not straightforward when the domain is unbounded or depends on parameters approaching infinity. Similarly, while the authors mention the continuity of the likelihood function, this alone does not directly imply the stated convergence properties. Increased detail and a more comprehensive argument are needed to fully convince the reader of the proof’s validity.

Finally, I have a minor concern regarding the section on the Gradient EM (lines 162-172). I found this section more challenging to follow than the rest of the paper. Specifically, the authors could provide additional explanation regarding the derivation of Equation (5).
