

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental question about the likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically whether bad local maxima can exist in the population (infinite-sample) likelihood function. The authors provide two main results: (1) they construct a GMM with M ≥ 3 well-separated, isotropic Gaussians whose population likelihood contains arbitrarily bad local maxima (resolving an open question of Srebro [2007]), and (2) they prove that with random initialization, EM and its first-order variant converge to bad critical points with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)).

This work builds on previous theoretical analyses of GMMs. Srebro [2007] conjectured that for well-separated mixtures, the population likelihood would have no bad local maxima (which would justify EM's practical success), but this paper shows the conjecture is false. The authors connect to the broader literature on non-convex optimization, particularly work by Balakrishnan et al. [2015] on local convergence of EM for two-component GMMs, and recent results on saddle points by Lee et al. [2016] and Panageas and Piliouras [2016].

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs establishing the existence of bad local maxima and the high probability of EM converging to them. The authors carefully distinguish between local maxima and saddle points, and they show that first-order EM avoids strict saddle points almost surely. However, the practical relevance of the constructed bad local maxima is questionable. The paper focuses on highly specific configurations (e.g., one cluster of centers well-separated from another, with recursive clustering) that may not occur in typical applications. The authors should better justify why these configurations are not pathological and how likely they are to arise in real-world data. The claim that "the mere existence of bad local maxima is not a practical concern" is not sufficiently supported, as the paper doesn't demonstrate how common these bad configurations are in practice.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations. However, there are several instances where the writing could be improved for clarity. For example, the example in L74-81 about M=2 is confusing and doesn't convincingly support the claim about Srebro's conjecture. The phrase "well-separated constant" (L218) is unclear, and the use of Ω(M) (L225) should be defined. Several minor grammatical issues and redundant phrases (e.g., L220 "the the") detract from readability. The authors should address these issues in a revised version.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving Srebro's conjecture in the negative. The construction of bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 is novel, and the algorithmic results about EM with random initialization provide important insights. The work clearly distinguishes itself from previous NIPS papers on GMMs (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. [2015], Dasgupta and Schulman [2007]) by focusing on the population likelihood and proving the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima. This is a substantial contribution to the understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes.

**Significance:** The paper's significance is somewhat limited by the question of practical relevance. The authors show that EM with random initialization can fail for certain pathological configurations, but it's unclear how common these configurations are in real-world applications. The authors should address this by (1) showing that the probability of encountering such bad configurations increases with M or dimension d, or (2) arguing why these configurations are representative of typical GMMs. The claim that "careful initialization is required for local search methods" is important, but the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate why this is a practical concern given the specific construction. The result about avoiding strict saddle points (Theorem 4) is theoretically interesting but may have limited practical impact, as saddle points are less concerning than bad local maxima in non-convex optimization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Resolves an open question of Srebro [2007] in the negative
- Provides rigorous theoretical results about the likelihood landscape of GMMs
- Makes a clear distinction between local maxima and saddle points
- Has important implications for understanding EM's behavior in practice
- Well-written with good mathematical exposition (despite minor issues)

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The practical relevance of the constructed bad local maxima is questionable
- The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the bad configurations are likely to occur in real data
- The random initialization scheme may not be representative of typical practice
- The example for M=2 (L74-81) is confusing and unconvincing
- The significance of avoiding saddle points (Theorem 4) is unclear in practical terms

Overall, this is a technically strong paper with important theoretical contributions to the understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes. However, the practical significance is somewhat undermined by the lack of justification for why the constructed bad configurations are representative of typical GMMs. The authors should address these concerns by either demonstrating that the bad configurations are not pathological or by better contextualizing their results within practical applications. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the practical relevance of the results.

Given the theoretical novelty and rigor of the work, but with some concerns about practical relevance, I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the clarity issues and provide a stronger justification for the practical importance of their results in the final version. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs and should be published, but with revisions to strengthen the practical motivation.
