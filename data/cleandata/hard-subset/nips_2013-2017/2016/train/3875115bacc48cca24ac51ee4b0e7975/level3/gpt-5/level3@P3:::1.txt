Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) likelihood of mixtures of spherical, equally weighted Gaussians and the behavior of EM and gradient EM under random initialization. It resolves a conjecture of Srebro (2007) by constructing bad local maxima for the population likelihood even for well-separated mixtures with M ≥ 3 components. The authors further show that EM (and a first-order/gradient EM variant) with standard random initialization converges to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-Ω(M)}; equivalently, the success probability is at most e^{-Ω(M)}. Complementing recent saddle-avoidance results (Lee et al., Panageas–Piliouras), the paper proves gradient EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddles, pinpointing bad local maxima—not saddles—as the main obstruction. The results underscore that even with unlimited data and favorable assumptions (spherical, equal weights, large separation), EM need not find global maxima.

The work connects to a rich line of research: Dasgupta–Schulman’s analysis of a two-round EM variant under separation; local convergence of EM around good initializations (Balakrishnan et al., 2015); method-of-moments/spectral approaches for GMMs (Moitra–Valiant; Hsu–Kakade); and nonconvex landscapes where local optima are benign (Loh–Wainwright). The constructions here echo known pathologies for Lloyd’s k-means: with three components (two close, one far), local search can misallocate centers. The failure rate for random initialization mirrors classical k-means behavior and motivates improved seeding strategies.

Technical contributions
- Structural result (Theorem 1): For any M ≥ 3, there exist well-separated, spherical, equal-weight GMMs whose population likelihood has suboptimal local maxima with arbitrarily large suboptimality gap. The key example for M = 3 places two true means relatively close to each other (still ≳ √d apart), and one far away; a local maximum puts one center at the midpoint of the close pair and the remaining two at the far mean.
- Algorithmic consequence (Theorems 2–3): With random initialization (drawing k initial centers i.i.d. from the mixture), EM and gradient EM converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{-Ω(k)}. A recursive, hierarchical clustering of the true centers drives the exponential decrease in the success probability.
- Landscape property (Theorem 4): Gradient EM (with reasonable step size) almost surely avoids strict saddle points; hence failures arise from attraction to bad local maxima rather than saddles.

Strengths
- Resolves a long-standing open question by showing population-level bad local maxima exist for well-separated spherical mixtures with M ≥ 3, and that the suboptimality can be arbitrarily large.
- Provides a coherent picture linking landscape structure, EM dynamics, and initialization: even infinite data do not remove nonconvex pitfalls; random seeding is exponentially unlikely to succeed as k grows.
- Uses clean population analysis, avoiding sample-level degeneracies, and connects to modern results on saddle avoidance.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Some results and constructions are unsurprising to practitioners familiar with k-means failures and EM’s sensitivity to initialization. The hierarchical construction and the 3-component “two close, one far” example follow expected lines.
- The paper would benefit from an explicit comparison to the behavior and guarantees of Lloyd’s algorithm under large separation. In particular:
  - Discuss parallels to known failure modes for random-seeded k-means and how success probabilities match the exp(-Ω(k)) regime.
  - Contrast with conditions under which Lloyd’s is provably successful (e.g., results in the vein of Kumar–Kannan on recovery under large separations or stability conditions), and whether analogous separation/stability salvages EM or gradient EM.
  - Suggest and analyze practical mitigations inspired by k-means: oversampling (e.g., pick k log k initial centers and prune) and distance-squared (D^2) sampling/k-means++-style seeding. Can similar seeding provably improve EM’s success probability in the population setting?
- The “well-separated” notion (Ω(√d)) is central; it would help to quantify precisely the regime in the 3-component construction (ratio of within-close-pair separation to distance to the far mean) and how the suboptimality gap scales.
- Finite-sample implications are asserted to follow by standard arguments; providing explicit sample complexity to preserve the population geometry (as a function of separation and desired gap) would strengthen practical relevance.
- Gradient EM’s saddle-avoidance relies on step-size conditions; a brief sensitivity analysis and comments on nonconstant stepsizes would be useful.

Quality
- The results are clearly stated, and the proof ideas (geometric intuition for M=3; recursive construction for exp(-Ω(k)) failure; application of saddle-avoidance theorems) are convincing. Assuming the appendix contains full details, the technical development appears sound. Clarifying constants/separation thresholds and giving a brief finite-sample corollary would improve completeness.

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally well-organized and readable. It would benefit from:
  - A figure illustrating the 3-component landscape and the misallocated local maximum.
  - A concise summary of the hierarchical construction with a simple two-level example before the general case.
  - A table contrasting EM vs. Lloyd’s vs. spectral/moments methods under separation and initialization assumptions.

Originality
- The resolution of Srebro’s conjecture at the population level is novel and valuable. The exponential failure probability result, while thematically aligned with k-means folklore, is a useful and rigorous statement for EM. The saddle-avoidance application to gradient EM is expected given recent theory but helps complete the narrative.

Significance
- The paper clarifies fundamental limitations of EM for GMMs even in highly favorable regimes, reinforcing the necessity of careful initialization. It will influence both theoretical analyses of nonconvex estimation and practical guidance for mixture learning.

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves an open question; provides a coherent theory tying together landscape pathologies and algorithmic consequences.
- Clear message with practical implications: random seeding is inadequate; better initialization is essential.
- Bridges EM theory with modern nonconvex optimization insights (strict saddle avoidance).

Arguments against acceptance
- Constructions and failure modes are unsurprising; techniques largely follow expected templates from clustering/k-means analyses.
- Limited exploration of remedies (e.g., principled seeding) and of finite-sample quantitative guarantees.

Requested additions
- Compare explicitly with Kumar–Kannan–style guarantees for k-means under large separation; contrast EM vs. Lloyd’s success/failure regimes.
- Discuss and, if possible, analyze EM with improved seeding: oversampling (k log k) and D^2 sampling.
- Provide finite-sample corollaries quantifying n as a function of separation and desired likelihood gap.
