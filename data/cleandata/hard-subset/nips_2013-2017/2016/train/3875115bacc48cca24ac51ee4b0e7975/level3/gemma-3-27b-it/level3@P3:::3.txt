This paper investigates local maxima in the log-likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), focusing on isotropic, known-scale, well-specified models. It demonstrates that arbitrarily bad local maxima can exist even with infinite data, resolving an open question posed by Srebro [2007]. The authors also show that, with random initialization, the EM algorithm converges to these suboptimal critical points with high probability as the number of components *k* increases. The work builds upon prior research in GMM identifiability (Allman et al. [2009]), convergence rates (Chen [1995], Ghosal & Van Der Vaart [2001]), and EM algorithm analysis (Dasgupta & Schulman [2007], Balakrishnan et al. [2015]). It’s a valuable contribution to understanding the challenges of GMM estimation, particularly highlighting the sensitivity to initialization.

**Quality:** The paper is generally technically sound. The proofs appear rigorous, and the theoretical results are well-motivated. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations and frame their results within the context of existing literature. The analysis of the first-order EM algorithm and its convergence properties is a particularly strong aspect. However, the presentation occasionally lacks clarity, and the connection between some theoretical examples and practical relevance isn't fully established.

**Clarity:** The writing is mostly good, but suffers from occasional typos and phrasing issues (detailed below). The organization is logical, progressing from background to main results and then to discussion. However, the purpose of certain examples isn't always immediately clear, hindering understanding. Sufficient information is provided for an expert reader to follow the proofs, although some steps could benefit from further elaboration.

**Originality:** The primary contribution – demonstrating arbitrarily bad local maxima even in the infinite-sample limit – is novel and addresses a longstanding open question. The analysis of random initialization's impact on EM convergence is also original and insightful. The connection made between saddle points and local maxima is a worthwhile observation, furthering understanding of the EM algorithm's behavior.

**Significance:** The results are significant because they cast doubt on the unconditional reliability of EM, even in well-specified and seemingly benign settings. The findings emphasize the critical role of initialization strategies for GMM estimation. While the specific configurations leading to bad local maxima might not be ubiquitous, the theoretical possibility raises important concerns for practical applications and motivates further research into robust initialization methods. The demonstration that first-order methods fail primarily due to local optima rather than saddle points is also important.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant open problem in GMM theory.
*   Provides rigorous theoretical results with clear implications for algorithm performance.
*   Highlights the importance of initialization in GMM estimation.
*   Contributes to a deeper understanding of the EM algorithm's limitations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical relevance of the demonstrated "bad" configurations is questionable.
*   The random initialization assumption might not reflect real-world scenarios.
*   Some examples lack clear motivation and convincing explanations.



**Specific Concerns & Requests:**

1.  **Practical Relevance of Bad Configurations:** The core concern is whether the specific configurations that trigger bad local maxima are likely to occur in real-world datasets. The authors should address this more directly. I request that they *assume a distribution on the true centers* and show that the probability of these "bad" configurations increases with either *k* (number of components) or *d* (dimensionality). While Theorem 2 offers some insight, it doesn't fully resolve this issue.

2.  **Initialization Relevance:** The random initialization scheme feels unrealistic when data are clearly clustered. Wouldn’t a more informed initialization (e.g., k-means) be more representative of practical scenarios? The authors should discuss the implications of different initialization strategies and whether the failure cases are practically likely with more reasonable initialization methods. The example with 3 Gaussians (two close, one far) – it seems improbable that random initialization would consistently place centers in the problematic way described.

3.  **L74-81:** The purpose of the example with the one-dimensional GMM is unclear. It doesn’t convincingly demonstrate that Srebro’s conjecture holds for k=2; it merely shows a specific case.

4.  **L233-235:** Requirement (1) is unclear and potentially unnecessary. Why is this restriction needed, and why wouldn’t a center initialized between clusters simply move to the correct cluster during EM iterations?

5.  **L267-273:** The practical importance of distinguishing between convergence to a saddle point *versus* a local optimum feels limited. While theoretically interesting, it's unclear how this distinction impacts practical algorithm design or performance.

6.  **Phrasing/Notation:**
    *   L18: Add "that" ("…demonstrates that…")
    *   L38: Use plural "estimates" ("…maximum likelihood estimates…")
    *   L60: Rephrase to “under which conditions” (“…the EM algorithm can be used…”)
    *   L138: Clarify O(d^1/2)-separation meaning. What does this imply practically about the distance between means?
    *   L172: Reword or remove redundancy ("…the EM algorithm with random initialization, as well as a variant…")
    *   L177: Replace “equivalent to” with a more precise term like “approximates” or “can be viewed as.”
    *   L220: Remove duplicate “the” (“…distribution GMM(µ∗)…”)
    *   L245: “coincidence” is a weak descriptor. Consider "configuration" or "alignment."
    *   L249: Add “that” (“…the EM algorithm converges…”)
    *   L308: Add “that” (“…sufficiently large sample sizes…”)



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution with clear implications for GMM estimation. Addressing the concerns regarding practical relevance and providing clarifications on the points raised above would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
