- Studies the population log-likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixtures with equal weights and identity covariances.
- Resolves Srebro (2007) conjecture negatively: for M ≥ 3, infinite-sample likelihood can have bad local maxima.
- Constructs 1D, well-separated k=3 mixtures with suboptimal local maxima whose likelihood gap from the global optimum is arbitrarily large (Theorem 1).
- Construction intuition: two true centers close, third far; a candidate with two means collapsed on the far mode and one between the close pair yields a local optimum.
- Shows EM and first-order EM with standard random initialization can converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{-Ω(M)} on designed instances (Theorems 2 and 3).
- Builds a recursive hierarchical arrangement of true means (Ω(log M) levels) to force exponential trapping probability under random initialization.
- Proves first-order EM with constant step size s ∈ (0,1) almost surely avoids strict saddle points, so failures stem from poor local maxima (Theorem 4).
- Complements prior local-convergence analyses and goes beyond finite-sample pathologies by focusing on the population limit.
- Connects to alternative algorithmic approaches (clustering, moments, spectral methods) and clarifies when likelihood-based methods need careful seeding.
- Theorem 1’s construction and proof strategy (neighborhood with lower-boundary likelihood) are crisp and persuasive.
- Hierarchical instance convincingly yields the claimed e^{-Ω(M)} probability bound; appendix details plausibly consistent with high-level argument.
- Theorem 4 is carefully stated and correctly adapts the strict-saddle notion to maximization, leveraging dynamical systems results.
- Assumptions (population setting, equal weights, identity covariance, well-separated means) are explicit and finite-sample analogs argued via concentration.
- Presentation is clear with helpful figures for M = 2 and schematics for M = 3; intuition and setup are well explained.
- Suggested clarity improvements: quantify the separation dependence on C_gap, visually illustrate hierarchical trapping, and state smoothness assumptions for Theorem 4.
- Originality: novel negative resolution of Srebro’s conjecture and a clever recursive instance causing exponential failure under random initialization.
- Significance: sharpens understanding of likelihood landscapes, sets limits on random-init local search, and motivates principled seeding methods.
- Arguments for acceptance: resolves an open question, provides strong theoretical results, and offers insightful constructions about optimization landscape.
- Arguments against acceptance: results are population-level only, main counterexample is 1D, and exponential failure relies on carefully crafted worst-case instances.
- Suggested improvements: add finite-sample simulations for M = 3 and scaling with M, discuss extensions to d > 1 and separation scaling, and compare with alternative initialization schemes.
- Also suggest clarifying step-size/adaptive-step robustness for first-order EM and discussing the M = 2 case or partial progress toward it.
- Overall evaluation: strong theoretical paper with clear negative results and meaningful algorithmic implications; recommendation to accept.
