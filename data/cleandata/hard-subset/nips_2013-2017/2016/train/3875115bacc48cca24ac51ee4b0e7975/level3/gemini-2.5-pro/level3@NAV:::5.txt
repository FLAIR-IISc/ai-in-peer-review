This paper addresses a significant open question in the study of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), posed by Srebro (2007), concerning the existence of spurious local maxima in the population likelihood function. The authors provide a definitive negative answer by constructing a general class of counterexamples for mixtures with three or more components. The paper shows not only that "bad" local maxima exist but that their likelihood values can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. Critically, the work extends this structural result to an algorithmic one, proving that standard methods like the EM algorithm and gradient EM, under a common random initialization scheme, will converge to these non-optimal solutions with a probability that is exponentially high in the number of components.

The paper's primary strengths are its originality and significance. Resolving a decade-old conjecture is a major theoretical contribution that deepens our understanding of non-convex optimization landscapes, a central topic at NeurIPS. The results are highly significant, cautioning against the blind faith in local search heuristics like EM, even in the idealized, infinite-sample limit where one might expect optimization to be easier. The technical quality of the work is high; the authors provide clear intuition behind their counterexample construction, supported by a detailed proof for a simple case (k=3, d=1), which greatly enhances the paper's clarity. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized, effectively balancing formal statements with accessible explanations.

The main area for improvement lies in the characterization of the probability bounds. While the paper proves that convergence to bad maxima occurs with probability at least 1 - e^(-cM), the constant `c` is not estimated. This makes it difficult to ascertain the practical regime where this failure mode becomes dominant. While I did not fully check the supplementary material, I would strongly encourage the authors to either provide estimates for these constants or include supporting simulations. A simulation showing the frequency of convergence to suboptimal solutions as a function of `M` would strengthen the practical implications.

However, this is a minor point that does not detract from the paper's novelty or impact. It is a complete and important piece of work that should be accepted. The resolution of a long-standing question, combined with strong theoretical results on algorithmic consequences, makes this a clear and valuable contribution to the community.
