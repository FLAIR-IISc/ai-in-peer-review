- Main result 1: Construct a 1D mixture of three well-separated Gaussians whose population log-likelihood has local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global maximum.  
- Main result 2: With random initialization, the probability of avoiding a bad local maximum can be exponentially small in the number of components.  
- Main result 3: The first-order EM algorithm almost surely does not converge to a saddle point of the log-likelihood.  
- Technical quality: Proofs in the main paper are sound and the overall technical work is high-quality (appendix details less thoroughly checked).  
- Novelty/originality: Relates mean configuration to local maxima; hierarchical grouping insight for >3 components is clever.  
- Potential impact: Results could inspire theoretical work on clustering optima and practical seeding/initialization strategies.  
- Suggested extension: Worth checking whether Lemma 7 and constructions extend to higher dimensions.  
- Clarity/presentation: Excellent exposition, clear setup, and helpful intuition and interpretations.  
- Relation to prior work: Provides a negative answer to Srebro (2007) by showing non-global local maxima in the population limit.  
- Algorithmic consequences: EM and first-order EM with random initialization can converge to bad critical points with high probability, stressing the need for careful initialization.  
- Fixed-point implications: Since first-order EM avoids strict saddles, the common sub-optimal convergence points are likely bad local maxima.  
- Acceptance arguments: Significant negative resolution of an open problem, insightful construction, and important algorithmic implications.  
- Rejection arguments: Results restricted to equal weights and identity covariance, rely on a specific recursive construction that may not generalize, and do not offer clear practical remedies for avoiding bad maxima.
