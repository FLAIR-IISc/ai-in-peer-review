Summary
The paper studies the population (infinite-sample) likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) under a simplified but common setting: known number of components M, equal weights, spherical covariances fixed to the identity. It resolves an open conjecture by Srebro (2007) by proving that, for M ≥ 3, the population likelihood can have bad local maxima even for well-separated mixtures; moreover, the likelihood gap between such local maxima and the global optimum can be made arbitrarily large. Algorithmically, the paper shows that both EM and a first-order (gradient) variant, when randomly initialized from the population distribution, converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). It further proves that gradient EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles. The results underscore the importance of careful initialization for EM even in very favorable settings.

Relation to prior work
The paper complements and contrasts with works that analyze EM’s local convergence under good initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. 2015), as well as algorithmic alternatives based on clustering or moments with provable guarantees under separation (e.g., Dasgupta and Schulman 2007; Hsu and Kakade 2013; Moitra and Valiant 2010). It also connects to the literature on nonconvex landscapes where all local optima can be good (e.g., Loh and Wainwright 2013), and to recent results on gradient methods avoiding strict saddles (Lee et al. 2016; Panageas and Piliouras 2016). The work answers Srebro’s conjecture in the negative and clarifies why EM can fail despite the absence of saddle-avoidance issues.

Strengths
- Resolves a well-known open question by constructing arbitrarily bad local maxima for population likelihood of well-separated, equal-weight, spherical GMMs with M ≥ 3.
- Provides sharp algorithmic consequences: EM and gradient EM with natural random initialization fail with probability ≥ 1 − e−Ω(M).
- Clarifies that failures are due to local maxima, not strict saddles, for gradient EM.
- Results are informative for both theory and practice: they quantify the limitations of naïve random restarts and motivate principled initialization.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Major concern about Theorem 1 proof rigor (lines 288–306). Several steps involve limits of suprema/maxima over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains. Continuity alone does not justify these limit exchanges. Please:
  - Explicitly restrict to a compact neighborhood around the constructed configuration and apply the extreme value theorem to ensure existence of a boundary maximizer.
  - Prove a strict likelihood gap on the boundary of that neighborhood; then deduce the existence of a local maximum inside by continuity.
  - If parameter-dependent feasible sets are used, justify continuity of the value function via Berge’s maximum theorem (under compactness) or by establishing coercivity and uniform convergence; or provide a dominated convergence/epi-convergence argument with clear measurability and compactness conditions.
  - Spell out the exact neighborhoods, boundaries, and continuity/compactness lemmas used.
- Scope versus dimension. Although the setup is for general d, the constructions and proofs appear in d = 1. Please state explicitly whether the constructions extend to d > 1 (e.g., by embedding the 1D instance along a coordinate axis or via product constructions) and what, if any, new difficulties arise (e.g., separation scaling with √d, interaction across coordinates).
- Clarity of gradient EM discussion (lines 162–172). The derivation of Equation (5) and the relationship between the Q-function gradient and gradient ascent on L are hard to follow. Please provide a self-contained derivation showing:
  - How the E-step weights lead to the population M-step in (5).
  - That the first-order EM update µ ← µ + s E[wi(X)(X − µi)] is exactly gradient ascent on the population likelihood for s > 0 (include the short calculation or a precise pointer).
- The main text currently contains only a brief sketch of the core constructions; many readers will not consult the supplement. Given the centrality of Theorem 1, consider expanding its proof in the main paper, even at the expense of shrinking other discussion.

Quality
The technical direction is strong and the results, if the proofs are tightened as above, are sound and impactful. The recursive clustering argument that yields e−Ω(M) success probability for random initialization is compelling; a slightly longer proof sketch in the main text would aid verification. The claim that gradient EM almost surely avoids strict saddles is well-motivated by recent dynamical systems arguments; it would help to state the precise smoothness/regularity conditions verified for the population objective in your proof.

Clarity
Overall well written and well organized. Suggestions:
- Expand the proof of Theorem 1 in the main text; define all neighborhoods and boundary sets precisely.
- Improve the exposition of first-order EM, explicitly connecting the Q-function to the gradient of L and stating the step-size requirements.
- Consider adding a figure for the M = 3 construction analogous to the M = 2 illustration.
- Minor: fix reference formatting inconsistencies and ensure consistent notation.

Originality
High. The negative structural result and the quantitative failure probability for random initialization are new and resolve a known conjecture. The combination of landscape analysis and algorithmic consequences is original and insightful.

Significance
High. The work directly informs the community’s understanding of EM and nonconvex likelihood landscapes, with concrete implications for practice (need for careful initialization) and for theory (limits of random restarts). Although derived in a simplified setting and at the population level, the authors argue for standard extensions to large finite samples.

Pros for acceptance
- Resolves an open question; delivers clear, rigorous negative results with practical implications.
- Strong connection to and advancement over prior EM analyses.
- Clear message: random initialization is insufficient; initializers based on moments or clustering are needed.

Cons for acceptance
- Key proof steps in Theorem 1 need more rigorous justification.
- Dimension-generalization is not clearly addressed.
- Some expository gaps in the gradient EM section.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a concise, rigorous argument in the main text that handles the limit-of-maxima steps in Theorem 1 (e.g., via compactness and Berge’s theorem)?
- Do your constructions and probability bounds extend verbatim to d > 1? If so, please state this explicitly and quantify the separation requirements in d.
- Can you include a finite-sample corollary with explicit n scaling to make the population-to-sample transfer concrete?

Overall assessment
I find the question important and the results significant. I would support acceptance provided the proof of Theorem 1 is strengthened in the main text and the gradient EM exposition is clarified.

Confidence
Moderate.
