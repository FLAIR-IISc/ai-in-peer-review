

This paper addresses a fundamental question in the statistical theory of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) that has been open since Srebro (2007). The authors provide a negative answer to Srebro's conjecture that the population likelihood function for GMMs has no bad local maxima in the limit of infinite samples. Their first main result constructs a counterexample showing that for M ≥ 3 components, the population log-likelihood function can have local maxima whose log-likelihood values are arbitrarily worse than those of global optima. This resolves an important open question in the field and has significant implications for understanding the theoretical limitations of maximum likelihood estimation in GMMs.

The authors further establish algorithmic consequences of their structural results. They prove that the EM algorithm (and a first-order variant) with random initialization converges to bad critical points with probability at least 1−e−Ω(M). This exponentially high probability of failure under common initialization schemes demonstrates that even in highly favorable settings (equally-weighted, well-separated, spherical Gaussians), random initialization can lead to poor solutions. The authors also prove that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, indicating that the failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding why careful initialization is required in practice for GMM estimation, even when the model is well-specified. Second, it advances our understanding of non-convex optimization in the asymptotic regime, showing that even in favorable settings, bad local maxima can exist and trap optimization algorithms. Third, it establishes a clear connection between the structural properties of the likelihood function and the behavior of practical algorithms.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide intuitive explanations for their results, including a detailed analysis of the M=3, d=1 case that illustrates the key intuition behind their construction. The theoretical arguments are carefully developed, with a good balance between mathematical rigor and accessibility. The manuscript effectively relates their results to previous work, particularly the contributions of Balakrishnan et al. (2015) on local convergence of EM for two-component mixtures.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides important theoretical justification for the need for careful initialization in GMM estimation, which has been observed empirically but lacked strong theoretical backing. The results also have broader implications for understanding non-convex optimization in statistical settings, where the existence of bad local maxima can severely impact algorithmic performance.

The main limitation of the paper is the lack of concrete constants in the probability bounds. The authors prove that the probability of failure is at least 1−e−cM for some universal constant c, but do not provide estimates of c or how it depends on other parameters. This makes it difficult to assess when the exponential failure probability becomes practically relevant for realistic values of M. The reviewer recommends including numerical estimates of c or providing supporting simulations that demonstrate the phenomenon for specific parameter values, as this would help practitioners understand the practical implications of the theoretical results.

Despite this limitation, the paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces and the behavior of optimization algorithms. The results are novel, well-supported by rigorous proofs, and have important implications for both theory and practice. The authors successfully address an open question that has been of interest to the community since Srebro's 2007 paper.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical results are complete and carefully argued. The clarity of the presentation is excellent, with appropriate use of figures and intuitive explanations. The originality is high, as the paper directly addresses an open question in the field with a negative result that has not been previously established. The significance is substantial, as the findings have implications for understanding the theoretical limitations of maximum likelihood estimation in GMMs and for guiding practical algorithm design.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it provides a fundamental theoretical contribution to a core area of machine learning research. It advances our understanding of non-convex optimization in statistical settings and has clear connections to important practical algorithms. The work is distinct from previous NIPS papers on GMMs, particularly in its focus on the population likelihood and its resolution of Srebro's conjecture.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that should be accepted for publication. The results are important, the arguments are rigorous, and the writing is clear. The minor limitation regarding the lack of concrete constants in the probability bounds does not detract from the paper's novelty or impact, but would be worth addressing in future work or through supplemental material.
