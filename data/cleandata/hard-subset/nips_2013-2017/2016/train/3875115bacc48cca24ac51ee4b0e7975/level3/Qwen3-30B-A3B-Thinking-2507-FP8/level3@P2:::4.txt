

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses an important open question in the theoretical analysis of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically whether the population likelihood function of GMMs with M ≥ 3 components can contain bad local maxima (i.e., local maxima with significantly worse likelihood than the global maximum). This question was posed by Srebro [2007] as a conjecture that, if true, would provide strong theoretical justification for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in the infinite-sample limit. The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture, demonstrating that bad local maxima do exist in the population likelihood for certain well-separated GMMs with M ≥ 3 components.

The paper makes three main contributions. First, Theorem 1 constructs a counterexample for M = 3, d = 1 showing that local maxima can have arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global maximum. Second, Theorem 2 establishes that with random initialization, the EM algorithm and its first-order variant converge to bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)). Third, Theorem 4 proves that the first-order EM algorithm almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, indicating that the poor performance of local search methods is primarily due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper is technically strong and well-structured. The authors provide clear geometric intuition for their constructions, particularly in the M = 2 case as a contrast to the M ≥ 3 case. The hierarchical grouping construction for Theorem 2 is particularly clever and novel, providing a clean explanation for why random initialization fails with exponentially high probability. The connection between the population likelihood and practical algorithmic performance is well-motivated, and the paper's focus on the idealized case of equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians with identity covariance is appropriate for addressing the open question.

The paper's strengths are evident across all four NIPS criteria. In terms of quality, the theoretical results are rigorously proven, with clear construction of counterexamples and sound reasoning about the behavior of optimization algorithms. The clarity of the presentation is excellent - the problem is well-motivated, the results are clearly stated, and the key insights are well-explained with helpful visualizations. The novelty is significant, as the hierarchical grouping technique for constructing bad local maxima represents a new contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces. The significance of the results is substantial, as they directly inform practical considerations about the use of EM and related algorithms in GMM estimation, suggesting that careful initialization (e.g., using method of moments) is essential even in highly favorable settings.

A few minor weaknesses should be noted. While the paper establishes results for d = 1, the extension to higher dimensions (d > 1) is not explicitly addressed, though the authors note it could be handled with standard empirical process arguments. The connection to broader non-convex optimization literature could be strengthened by more explicitly relating their results to recent work on saddle points (e.g., Ge et al. [2015]) and the "benign" properties of optimization landscapes observed in other settings. Additionally, while the paper notes the importance of careful initialization, it could more concretely suggest how practitioners might implement better initialization strategies based on these theoretical results.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs and their optimization landscape. It resolves an important open question posed by Srebro [2007] and provides valuable theoretical insights that directly inform practical algorithm design. The results have clear implications for the use of EM and related algorithms in practice, particularly regarding the critical importance of initialization strategies.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper resolves a significant open question in GMM theory with rigorous proofs and novel techniques. The results have clear theoretical and practical significance, and the paper is well-written and well-organized. The hierarchical construction technique is particularly elegant and could inspire further theoretical work on non-convex optimization.

Arguments against acceptance: The results are limited to the specific case of equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians, which may restrict the immediate applicability to more general settings. The extension to higher dimensions (d > 1) is not explicitly addressed in the main text, though the authors note it could be handled with standard techniques.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important question in the field. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides significant insights into the behavior of optimization algorithms for GMMs. The results have clear implications for both theory and practice, making this an important contribution to the NIPS community.

I recommend acceptance.
