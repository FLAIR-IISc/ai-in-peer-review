This paper studies the population log-likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and the performance of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for parameter estimation. The analysis is situated in an idealized setting where the number of components (M), the mixture weights (uniform), and the component covariances (identity) are known and fixed, leaving only the component means to be estimated. The paper's main contributions are a set of fundamental negative results. First, it refutes a 2007 conjecture by Srebro by constructing GMMs (for M ≥ 3) whose population log-likelihood possesses bad local maxima, i.e., local maxima with likelihood values that can be arbitrarily worse than that of the global maximum. This result is shown to hold even for well-separated mixtures. Second, the paper demonstrates a stark algorithmic consequence: both the standard EM algorithm and a first-order variant (equivalent to gradient ascent) will converge to such suboptimal critical points with overwhelmingly high probability (at least 1 - e⁻ᶜᴹ) when initialized randomly by sampling from the data-generating distribution. Finally, the authors show that the first-order EM variant almost surely avoids strict saddle points, strengthening the conclusion that the algorithm's failure is due to the presence of bad local maxima rather than the proliferation of saddle points. This work contrasts with recent literature on non-convex optimization which often identifies saddle points as the primary obstacle.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The paper addresses an interesting and long-standing question about the optimization landscape of one of the most fundamental models in machine learning. The results are significant, informative, and provide valuable insight into the practical behavior and theoretical limitations of the EM algorithm.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
1.  **Significance:** The paper resolves a well-known open question about the existence of bad local maxima in the GMM population likelihood. This is a strong theoretical contribution that deepens our understanding of the problem.
2.  **Originality & Impact:** The connection between the existence of these local maxima and the exponential failure probability of randomly initialized EM is novel and impactful. It provides a rigorous explanation for why naive initialization can fail and strongly motivates the need for more sophisticated initialization techniques (e.g., method of moments), which have been developed in parallel in the literature.
3.  **Clarity of message:** The paper's narrative is clear and compelling. The progression from the structural result (existence of bad maxima) to the algorithmic consequence (EM gets stuck) to the finer point about local maxima vs. saddle points is logical and persuasive.

**Arguments for Rejection (Cons):**
1.  **Quality/Technical Soundness (Major Concern):** The proof of Theorem 1, which is the cornerstone of the entire paper, is presented in a highly condensed and unconvincing manner in the main text (lines 288-306). The argument relies on establishing the existence of a local maximum within a specific neighborhood by showing that the function value at an interior point is greater than the supremum of the function values on the boundary. However, the proof sketch makes claims about the limits of these values without proper justification. In particular, it is not clear why the limit of a supremum over a parameter-dependent domain is equal to the value claimed. Continuity alone is insufficient to justify such an interchange of operations. This is a critical technical gap that must be filled for the paper's claims to be credible.
2.  **Clarity & Scope:** The paper claims results for general dimension `d`, but the construction for Theorem 1 and the subsequent proofs appear to be specific to the one-dimensional case (`d=1`). The authors should explicitly state whether the results extend to `d > 1` and briefly discuss what, if any, additional difficulties arise. Without this clarification, the scope of the main claims is ambiguous.
3.  **Clarity (Minor Concern):** The section introducing the first-order EM algorithm (lines 162-172) is difficult to parse. The paper states that the update in Equation (6) is equivalent to gradient ascent on the population likelihood and cites another paper. For self-containment and clarity, a brief explanation or sketch of the derivation should be included.

### Detailed Comments

My primary concern is the technical justification for Theorem 1. While I did not read the supplementary material, a result of this importance requires a convincing proof sketch in the main paper. The current argument is too brief and makes a logical leap that is not obviously correct. I strongly urge the authors to expand this proof, even at the expense of trimming the discussion, to provide a rigorous argument for why the log-likelihood on the boundary of the constructed neighborhood is strictly smaller than at the interior point µ.

The restriction to a simplified model (uniform weights, identity covariances) is acceptable given the technical nature of the proofs and the goal of demonstrating a fundamental negative result. However, the ambiguity regarding the dimensionality of the results should be resolved. If the construction easily generalizes to higher dimensions, this should be noted. If not, the paper's claims should be restricted to `d=1`.

In summary, this paper presents potentially important and impactful results on a classic problem. The main obstacle to my recommending acceptance is the significant concern about the technical soundness of the proof of Theorem 1. If the authors can address this point convincingly in their revision, the paper would represent a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference.
