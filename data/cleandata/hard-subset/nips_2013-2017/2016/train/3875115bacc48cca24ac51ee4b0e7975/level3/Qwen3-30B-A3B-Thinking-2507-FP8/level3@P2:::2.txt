

# Review for "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses an important open question in the theoretical understanding of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) by resolving Srebro's (2007) conjecture about the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function. The authors prove that for M ≥ 3 components, even in the idealized setting of equally-weighted, well-separated, spherical Gaussians with unit variance, the population log-likelihood can have local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. They further demonstrate that random initialization of the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) converges to these suboptimal critical points with high probability (at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M))), and show that the failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

**Strengths:**
- The paper resolves a fundamental open question in the GMM literature with significant theoretical importance.
- The results provide crucial theoretical justification for the empirical observation that careful initialization is necessary for EM in practice.
- The authors make a clear distinction between the behavior of EM in the M=2 case (where no bad local maxima exist) and M≥3 (where they do exist), which is an important conceptual contribution.
- The paper's focus on the population likelihood (infinite-sample) setting provides clean theoretical insights that are not obscured by finite-sample effects.
- The connection between the structural results and practical algorithmic consequences is well-motivated and clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**
- The proof of Theorem 1 (existence of bad local maxima) is too short (lines 288-306). The arguments about limits of supremums/maximums over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains require more detailed justification. Continuity alone does not imply the claimed convergences, and the proof would benefit from a more rigorous treatment of these technical points.
- The gradient EM section (lines 162-172) is difficult to follow. The derivation of Equation (5) could be clarified with more explanation of the steps between the general form and the specific GMM case.
- While the paper proves results for d=1, it states results for general dimension d without discussing whether the proofs extend to d > 1 or what additional difficulties might arise. The authors should clarify this point.

**Quality:** The paper provides technically sound results that address an important open question. The theoretical claims are well-supported by the proofs, though the proof of Theorem 1 requires more rigor. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their simplified model (fixed weights, identity covariances, etc.) and justify these simplifications as necessary for technical tractability. The paper carefully distinguishes between the behavior for M=2 (where the conjecture holds) and M≥3 (where it fails).

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with a good balance between theoretical results and their practical implications. The main results are clearly stated and well-motivated. However, as noted above, the proof of Theorem 1 is too brief, and the gradient EM section would benefit from more detailed exposition.

**Originality:** This work directly addresses and resolves an open question posed in Srebro (2007), making it a significant theoretical contribution. Previous NIPS papers have studied the behavior of EM for GMMs (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015; Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007), but this paper provides the first characterization of the population likelihood surface for M≥3 components. The paper is substantially different from prior work in its focus on the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima.

**Significance:** The results have important practical implications for the use of EM in GMMs. The paper demonstrates that even in the most favorable settings (well-separated, equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians), random initialization can fail with high probability, which explains why practitioners often need careful initialization schemes. This work provides a theoretical foundation for the common practice of using multiple random restarts or method-of-moments-based initialization, and will likely influence future work on GMM estimation algorithms.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Resolves an important open question in the GMM literature
- Provides theoretically rigorous justification for practical observations about EM behavior
- The results have clear implications for algorithm design in GMM estimation
- The paper is well-motivated and well-written (with minor exceptions)

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The proof of Theorem 1 is insufficiently detailed and requires more rigorous treatment
- The gradient EM section lacks sufficient explanation
- The extension to higher dimensions (d > 1) is not properly addressed

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that addresses an important open question, and the results have clear practical implications. The concerns about the proof of Theorem 1 and the gradient EM section are fixable with relatively minor revisions. The authors should expand the proof of Theorem 1 to provide more detailed justification for the limits they take, and clarify the gradient EM derivation. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of whether the results extend to d > 1 or what additional challenges would arise in higher dimensions.
