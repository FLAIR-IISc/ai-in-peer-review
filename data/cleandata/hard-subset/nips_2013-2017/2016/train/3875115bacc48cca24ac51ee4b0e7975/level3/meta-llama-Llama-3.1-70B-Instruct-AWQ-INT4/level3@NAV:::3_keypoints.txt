- Paper studies local maxima of the population log-likelihood for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and implications for the EM algorithm.  
- Theorem 1: For any M ≥ 3, there exist well-separated uniform mixtures of M unit-variance spherical Gaussians with arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population log-likelihood.  
- Theorem 2: EM with random initialization converges to bad critical points with high probability (≥ 1 − e^{-cM}) for certain well-separated mixtures.  
- Theorem 3: First-order EM also converges to bad critical points with high probability, even with small step sizes.  
- Theorem 4: First-order EM cannot converge to strict saddle points with probability one.  
- The results show pathological likelihood landscapes (even with infinite data), contradicting intuition that EM typically finds the global maximum.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, technically sound, and provides thorough proofs and analysis.  
- Raises open questions about whether at least three components are necessary for the bad behavior and why EM-style methods succeed empirically despite these failures.  
- Pro-acceptance: surprising and important theoretical contribution, thorough analysis of EM variants, and novel combination of techniques.  
- Con-acceptance: analysis assumes uniform spherical Gaussians, which may be unrealistic in practice.  
- Con-acceptance: paper lacks experimental results to empirically support the theoretical claims.  
- Con-acceptance: limited discussion of implications for other optimization algorithms or techniques.
