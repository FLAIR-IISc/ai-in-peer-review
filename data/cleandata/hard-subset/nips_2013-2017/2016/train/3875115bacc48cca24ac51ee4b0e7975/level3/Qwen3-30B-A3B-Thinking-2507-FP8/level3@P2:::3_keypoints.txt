- Main result: Constructs GMMs (M ≥ 3) with arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood, refuting Srebro [2007]'s conjecture.
- Algorithmic result: Shows EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to bad critical points with probability ≥ 1 - e^{-Ω(M)}.
- Technical quality: Rigorous proofs, careful distinction between local maxima and saddle points, and solid theoretical analysis.
- First-order EM property: Proves first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points.
- Practical relevance concern: Constructed bad local maxima rely on specific, possibly pathological configurations that may be uncommon in practice.
- Missing empirical grounding: Paper does not demonstrate how likely the bad configurations are to occur in real-world data.
- Request for additional justification: Authors should argue why constructions are not pathological or show occurrence probability grows with M or dimension d.
- Clarity issues: M=2 example (L74-81) is confusing and unconvincing regarding Srebro's conjecture.
- Terminology/notation problems: Phrases like "well-separated constant" (L218) and use of Ω(M) (L225) need clarification/definition.
- Minor edits needed: Several grammatical errors and redundant phrases (e.g., "the the" at L220) detract from readability.
- Originality: Novel theoretical contribution resolving an open question and distinguishing itself from prior work on finite-sample/local analyses.
- Significance caveat: Theoretical importance is clear, but practical impact is limited unless occurrence of bad configurations is better motivated.
- Pros for acceptance: Resolves open problem, rigorous results, clear distinction of critical point types, and solid exposition overall.
- Cons against acceptance: Questionable practical relevance, unclear representativeness of random initialization, and confusing M=2 example.
- Recommendation: Accept with revisions to improve clarity and provide stronger justification of practical importance.
