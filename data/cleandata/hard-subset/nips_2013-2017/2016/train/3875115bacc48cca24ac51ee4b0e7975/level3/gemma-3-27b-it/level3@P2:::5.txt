This paper addresses a fundamental question regarding the population likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs): are there “bad” local maxima, i.e., local optima that are arbitrarily worse than the global optimum? Srebro [2007] conjectured the negative, but this paper provides a definitive counterexample, resolving the open question. Beyond this structural result, the authors demonstrate that the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, and its first-order variant, converge to these bad critical points with exponentially high probability when initialized randomly – a common practical approach. This work is a significant contribution to understanding the challenges of optimization in the context of GMMs and provides valuable insight into the behavior of widely used algorithms.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It starts with a comprehensive introduction outlining the problem, its historical context, and related work. The authors effectively connect their contribution to previous investigations on identifiability, convergence rates, and the theoretical analysis of EM. The notation is consistent and the mathematical arguments are presented with sufficient detail for an expert reader to follow. The inclusion of a geometrical illustration for the M=3 case is particularly helpful in understanding the construction of the counterexample.

The originality of the work is high. While the challenges of GMM estimation are well-known, the explicit demonstration of bad local maxima in the population likelihood, and the analysis of EM's convergence behavior under a natural initialization scheme, are novel contributions. The authors appropriately reference related work, including early studies on identifiability (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009) and more recent investigations into the convergence of EM (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015). They also acknowledge connections to broader themes in non-convex optimization (Loh & Wainwright, 2013; Wang et al., 2014). The proofs, while technically involved, are clearly structured and build upon existing techniques in the field.

The significance of this work is substantial. The findings highlight the potential pitfalls of relying on local search methods like EM, even in seemingly well-behaved settings (well-separated Gaussians, large sample sizes). This has practical implications for practitioners using GMMs and underscores the importance of careful initialization strategies.  The theoretical results also contribute to a broader understanding of non-convex optimization landscapes and the factors that influence the convergence of gradient-based algorithms. The observation that the failure of first-order methods is more attributable to bad local maxima rather than saddle points (Theorems 3 & 4) is a particularly valuable insight, especially given recent attention on the role of saddle points in high-dimensional optimization.

**Strengths:**

*   **Resolution of an Open Problem:** The paper definitively answers a long-standing question in the GMM literature.
*   **Strong Theoretical Results:** The theorems are mathematically rigorous and provide quantifiable bounds on the convergence behavior of EM.
*   **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the subject matter.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The findings have implications for the practical use of GMMs and highlight the importance of initialization strategies.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points is an important contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Explicit Constants:** The probability bounds in Theorems 2 and 3 contain unspecified constants. While the authors acknowledge this, knowing the values (or at least estimates) of these constants would be highly valuable. It would help to understand the phase transition where the probability of converging to a bad local maximum becomes significant, and assess the practical impact for realistic values of *M* (number of components).
*   **Absence of Supporting Simulations:**  Without knowing the specific constants, simulations demonstrating the phenomenon described in Theorems 2 and 3 would be extremely helpful. Showing the empirical convergence rate to suboptimal solutions under random initialization would strengthen the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental and important problem in the field.
*   The theoretical results are rigorous and novel.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains its contributions.
*   The findings have significant implications for both theory and practice.
*   Even without the explicit constants, the theoretical framework is valuable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of explicit constants and supporting simulations limits the practical interpretability of the results. However, this is a relatively minor concern given the novelty and theoretical strength of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMM estimation. The resolution of Srebro’s conjecture and the analysis of EM’s convergence behavior are important results. While the lack of explicit constants and simulations is a minor drawback, it does not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with the suggestion that the authors consider adding simulations or providing estimates for the constants in a future version.
