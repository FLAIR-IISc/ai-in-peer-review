This paper investigates the population likelihood landscape for mixtures of spherical Gaussians, providing a set of coherent theoretical results on the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors formally demonstrate that, contrary to a conjecture by Srebro (2007), the likelihood function can possess bad local maxima, even for well-separated components with infinite data. Key contributions include a construction with M=3 components that yields arbitrarily poor local optima, a proof that random initialization fails to find the global optimum with probability approaching one exponentially in the number of components M, and an argument that for gradient EM, these bad local maxima—not strict saddle points—are the primary barrier to convergence. This work connects to a long line of research at NIPS and elsewhere on the theoretical guarantees for clustering and mixture model estimation, as well as the more recent focus on the geometry of non-convex optimization landscapes.

The primary strength of the paper is its rigorous and clear presentation of a set of negative results for a fundamental algorithm. By formally resolving an open question and quantifying the failure probability of a common heuristic like random initialization, the paper makes a solid theoretical contribution. The finding that bad local maxima, rather than saddle points, impede gradient EM is a timely and significant insight that distinguishes this problem from others in non-convex optimization where saddles are the main challenge. The technical analysis appears sound and well-supported.

However, the main weakness is a lack of surprisingness in the results and techniques. The construction used to create a bad local optimum—placing two true centers close together and a third far away—is a standard method for demonstrating the failure modes of iterative clustering algorithms. The resulting analysis mirrors well-known failure cases for Lloyd’s algorithm for k-means. Similarly, the exponential failure rate for random initialization is analogous to known results for k-means, which motivates common initialization strategies like k-means++ or selecting O(k log k) initial centers.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should explicitly discuss these parallels with k-means. A detailed comparison contrasting the guarantees and failure conditions for EM versus Lloyd’s algorithm under large separation would be highly valuable. For instance, discussing how their results relate to the analyses of algorithms with proven guarantees under similar separation conditions (e.g., Kumar and Kannan) would help clarify the paper’s unique contribution and better position it within the existing literature.
