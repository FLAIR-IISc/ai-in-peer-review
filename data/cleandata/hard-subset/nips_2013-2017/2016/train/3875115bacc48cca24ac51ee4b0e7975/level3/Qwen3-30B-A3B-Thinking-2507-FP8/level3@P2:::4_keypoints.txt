- Addresses an open question (Srebro 2007): shows population likelihood for GMMs with M ≥ 3 can have bad local maxima.  
- Theorem 1: constructs an M=3, d=1 counterexample where local maxima have arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global maximum.  
- Theorem 2: with random initialization, EM and first-order EM converge to bad local maxima with probability ≥ 1 − e^{−Ω(M)}.  
- Theorem 4: first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, so failures stem from bad local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Technical novelty: hierarchical grouping construction is clever and provides clean geometric intuition for failure modes.  
- Rigorous proofs and well-structured presentation with helpful visualizations and clear explanations.  
- Focuses on equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians (identity covariance), which is appropriate for the question but limits generality.  
- Extension to higher dimensions (d > 1) is not explicitly shown in the main text, though authors claim standard arguments can handle it.  
- Could strengthen connections to broader non-convex optimization literature (e.g., recent saddle-point results).  
- Lacks concrete, practical guidance for improved initialization strategies despite highlighting their importance.  
- Significant contribution: resolves an important theoretical question and has clear implications for EM algorithm usage.  
- Recommendation: accept — strong theoretical results, novelty, and practical relevance.
