## Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper investigates the challenging problem of local maxima in the likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The authors provide compelling theoretical results demonstrating the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima even in a simplified setting – equally-weighted mixtures of well-separated, spherical Gaussians – resolving a conjecture by Srebro [2007]. They further show that the EM algorithm, and its first-order variant, converge to these suboptimal points with high probability when initialized randomly. This work is a valuable contribution to understanding the practical limitations of EM, even in seemingly favorable scenarios.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon a substantial body of work on GMM estimation and the theoretical analysis of EM (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman, Balakrishnan et al.). The problem addressed is significant, as the non-convexity of the GMM likelihood is a known obstacle to reliable estimation. The results are informative, providing a clear explanation for why EM can struggle despite its widespread use. The focus on a simplified model (known K, uniform weights, identity covariances) is a reasonable trade-off, allowing for rigorous analysis that would be intractable in the general case. 

However, the paper’s generality is limited by the proofs being presented only for d=1. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed discussion of the difficulties in extending the results to d>1 would be beneficial.  The major concern lies within the proof of Theorem 1 (lines 288-306). The justification for the convergence arguments involving limits of supremums/maximums feels incomplete. Continuity alone doesn’t guarantee the claimed convergence, and a more detailed explanation is needed to convince the reader of its validity. I did not examine the supplementary proofs, but I would strongly recommend the authors consider expanding the main proof, even at the expense of some discussion, to bolster its clarity and rigor. 

Finally, the section on gradient EM (lines 162-172) is somewhat difficult to follow. A more explicit derivation of Equation (5) would greatly improve readability. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and open problem in GMM estimation.
*   Provides strong theoretical results with clear implications for the performance of EM.
*   Well-written and motivated, building upon existing literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Proofs limited to d=1; extension to higher dimensions unclear.
*   Proof of Theorem 1 lacks sufficient detail and justification.
*   Gradient EM section is difficult to follow.



**Overall:** Despite the concerns regarding the proof’s completeness and the limited dimensionality, the paper presents a significant and worthwhile contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
