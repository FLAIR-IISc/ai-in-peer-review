This paper provides a rigorous analysis of the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components, shedding light on the existence of bad local maxima and their implications for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors demonstrate that even in the idealized setting of equally weighted, spherical, and well-separated mixtures, the population log-likelihood can have arbitrarily bad local maxima, resolving an open question of Srebro (2007). Furthermore, they show that the EM algorithm, when initialized randomly, converges to a bad critical point with high probability, and that this phenomenon persists even for the first-order EM algorithm.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, including a detailed background on GMMs and the EM algorithm. The main results are presented in a logical and easy-to-follow manner, with a focus on the structural properties of the population log-likelihood and the algorithmic consequences of these properties.

The authors' use of a specific configuration, where the centers are split into well-spaced clusters, is a clever way to prove the existence of bad local maxima. The proof of Theorem 1 is particularly noteworthy, as it provides a clear and intuitive explanation of why the population log-likelihood can have arbitrarily bad local maxima.

The paper's main contribution is the demonstration that the EM algorithm, when initialized randomly, can fail to converge to the global optimum with high probability, even in the idealized setting of equally weighted, spherical, and well-separated mixtures. This result has significant implications for the practical use of the EM algorithm in GMM estimation.

However, the paper raises some questions regarding the practical relevance of the "bad" center configurations and the initialization scheme used. The authors' assumption of a random initialization scheme may be unrealistic in practice, as data are often clearly clustered. Additionally, the example challenge presented in the paper, where three Gaussians are considered (two close, one far), suggests that the problematic initialization described in the paper may be unlikely in practice.

Furthermore, the paper's focus on the EM algorithm's convergence to a saddle point versus a local optimum may not be practically important. The authors' result that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to a strict saddle point with probability one may be seen as a minor contribution compared to the main result on the existence of bad local maxima.

Some minor edits are requested to clarify phrasing and notation issues throughout the paper. For example, the term "well-separated constant" is unclear, and the notation Ω(k) could be better explained.

In conclusion, this paper provides a significant contribution to the understanding of the population likelihood function of GMMs and the EM algorithm. While some questions remain regarding the practical relevance of the results, the paper's main contribution is a valuable addition to the field.
