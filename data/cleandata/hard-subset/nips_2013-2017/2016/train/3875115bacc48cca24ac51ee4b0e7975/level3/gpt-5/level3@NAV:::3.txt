This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) likelihood of equally weighted, isotropic, known-variance GMMs and establishes three results: (i) for M ≥ 3, the landscape admits arbitrarily bad local maxima even under strong separation, resolving Srebro (2007) in the negative; (ii) EM (and a first-order EM variant) with random initialization converges to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{−Ω(M)} via a recursive cluster construction; (iii) first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, so failures stem from bad local optima. The work complements prior analyses of EM’s local convergence (e.g., Dasgupta–Schulman; Balakrishnan et al.) and adds to the broader literature on nonconvex landscapes and saddle-point avoidance (Ge et al., Lee et al.).

Quality: The core theorems appear technically careful and the constructions are insightful; the population-level focus sharpens the message by removing finite-sample artifacts. Clarity: Writing is generally good, though there are places where motivation and notation can be clarified (see below). Originality: Resolving the population-local-maxima question for well-separated spherical mixtures is novel. Significance: The results importantly qualify when EM-style methods can be expected to work, emphasizing initialization.

Main concerns about practical relevance:
- Distribution over true centers: The “bad” configurations are adversarial. To assess prevalence, assume a prior over µ∗ (e.g., centers i.i.d. from a base distribution, random partition into meta-clusters, or a Poisson cluster process) and show that the probability of the problematic split-cluster structures increases with M or d. As stated, Theorem 2 does not address this.
- Initialization realism: Random seeding from the data may be pessimistic when clusters are visually separable. Quantify failure under more practical schemes (k-means++, farthest-first, or a few Lloyd iterations) and for simple cases (e.g., three Gaussians with two close and one far, where one expects at least one seed per cluster). Even a small empirical study or a theorem bounding failure probability under k-means++ would strengthen impact.
- L233–235: Requirement (1) feels stronger than needed—if a center starts between clusters, why wouldn’t it migrate? Please justify with a dynamical argument or relax it.
- L74–81: The 2-component example is illustrative but does not substantiate the k = 2 conjecture; clarify its purpose and temper claims.
- L267–273: Emphasize why ruling out strict saddles materially changes practice versus confirming bad local maxima as the culprit.

Notation/phrasing: L218 “well-separated constant” → “a fixed separation constant”; L225 clarify Ω(k); L138 clarify O(d^{1/2}) separation; minor edits—L18 add “that the”; L38 “estimates”; L60 “under which conditions”; L172 reduce redundancy; L177 “equivalent to”; L220 remove duplicate “the”; L245 “coincidence”; L249 add “that”; L308 add “that”.

Pros: Resolves a notable conjecture; crisp, rigorous constructions; clear algorithmic implications about initialization. Cons: Practical likelihood under realistic priors/initializations is unclear; some exposition can be tightened.

Overall: Technically strong and original. With added evidence on prevalence and practical initializations, I lean toward acceptance.
