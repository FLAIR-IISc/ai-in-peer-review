- Paper constructs well-separated GMMs (M≥3) with arbitrarily bad local maxima, disproving Srebro (2007) in the negative.
- Main algorithmic results: with random-from-data initialization, EM and gradient EM converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^−Ω(M).
- Shows gradient EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, so failures are due to bad local maxima, not saddles.
- Provides a complete, intuitive proof for the k=3, d=1 case and extends via a recursive, well-separated clustering construction to general M.
- Complements prior local-convergence results requiring good initialization and contrasts with methods that avoid local search.
- Strength: resolves a long-standing conjecture and exhibits arbitrarily large suboptimality gaps under uniform weights and unit variance.
- Strength: clear exposition, geometric intuition, and balanced presentation including detailed k=3 example.
- Strength: important implications for non-convex optimization practice — naive random initialization can be provably inadequate.
- Strength: Theorem on avoidance of strict saddles is valuable for the optimization community.
- Weakness: asymptotic bounds (e^−Ω(M)) and separation requirements lack explicit constants and thresholds for practical guidance.
- Weakness/request: supplementary technical details should surface rough estimates of constants and their dependence on M and d in the main text.
- Request: provide simulations (1D and moderate-d) showing EM/gradient EM success probability vs. M, sensitivity to separation, and comparison with practical initializations.
- Request: clarify allowable gradient-EM step-size range beyond s∈(0,1) and whether line search affects the phenomenon.
- Question: can techniques offer partial evidence about the two-component case (M=2) and whether M≥3 is necessary?
- Quality: technically solid and self-contained at the population level; recursive argument and saddle avoidance result convincing.
- Clarity: very well written and organized; recommend adding figures illustrating the recursive construction and attraction basins.
- Originality: high — settles an open question and links landscape structure to algorithmic consequences under standard initialization.
- Significance: high — sharpens understanding of non-convex likelihoods and highlights need for principled initialization.
- Reason to accept: resolves open question, shows exponential-in-M failure, and provides clear intuition with complete simple-case proof.
- Reason to reject: lacks explicit constants/practical thresholds and minimal empirical demonstrations to quantify when asymptotic phenomena appear.
- Overall: strong, impactful, and recommend acceptance pending better exposition of constants and light empirical support.
