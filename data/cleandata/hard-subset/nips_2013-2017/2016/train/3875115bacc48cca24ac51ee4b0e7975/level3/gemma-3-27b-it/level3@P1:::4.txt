This paper investigates the challenging problem of local optima in the likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically focusing on the well-studied case of equal mixing weights and identity covariance matrices. The authors provide fundamental theoretical results demonstrating the existence of “bad” local maxima and analyzing the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm under random initialization. The work builds on prior investigations into the identifiability and convergence rates of GMMs (e.g., Allman et al. 2009, Chen 1995, Ghosal & Van Der Vaart 2001) and the limitations of EM (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman 2007, Balakrishnan et al. 2015), directly addressing a long-standing conjecture by Srebro (2007). 

The first main result presents a concrete counterexample to Srebro’s conjecture. The authors construct a three-component, one-dimensional GMM with well-separated components that exhibits local maxima with arbitrarily worse likelihood values than the global optimum. This is a significant contribution as it rigorously demonstrates that, even in a relatively simple and favorable setting, the GMM likelihood landscape is riddled with suboptimal critical points. The second result establishes a critical link between this structural property and the practical performance of the EM algorithm. They demonstrate that, with random initialization, EM is highly likely to converge to one of these bad local maxima, with a probability that decays exponentially with the number of components (M). This result underscores the sensitivity of EM to initialization and suggests that naive approaches can lead to substantially suboptimal solutions.  Finally, the authors prove that a first-order variant of EM does *not* converge to strict saddle points almost surely, thereby attributing the algorithm's poor performance primarily to the existence of bad local maxima rather than saddle points—a point relevant in the context of recent work on saddle points in non-convex optimization (e.g., Ge et al. 2015, Lee et al. 2016).

The paper's strengths lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis, the clear presentation of complex ideas, and the direct connection between structural properties and algorithmic behavior. The counterexample constructed in Theorem 1 is particularly elegant, and the exponential decay in Theorem 2 provides a strong warning about the limitations of random initialization. The authors meticulously build their argument, grounding their work in the existing literature and clearly articulating the implications of their findings. The discussion surrounding the practical ramifications of these results, especially concerning initialization strategies, is insightful and valuable.  The organization is logical, progressing from background and problem setup to the core theoretical results and their consequences.

However, the paper also has some areas that could be strengthened. While the proof sketch for Theorem 1 appears sound within the main text, the details are deferred to the appendix. A more thorough examination of the appendix, including a verification of the technical lemmas and supporting arguments, would increase confidence in the result. Similarly, the authors acknowledge that the details of the proof for Theorem 2 are also presented in the appendix, and while the overall strategy seems reasonable, a more detailed analysis would be beneficial. A potential avenue for future work, alluded to by the authors, would be to explore whether Lemma 7, central to their arguments, extends to higher dimensions (d>1). Furthermore, while the paper convincingly argues against random initialization, it does not offer concrete guidance on *how* to find good initializations. Addressing this would further enhance the practical impact of the work.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel and important contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMM estimation. The connection between mean configuration and the existence of bad local maxima is a fresh perspective, and the hierarchical grouping insight used in the proof of Theorem 2 is particularly clever. The authors are thorough in referencing related work, demonstrating an awareness of the existing literature. This work significantly advances our understanding of a fundamental problem in statistical learning. 

The significance of this work is substantial. The results highlight a critical limitation of widely used algorithms like EM and underscore the need for careful initialization strategies in GMM estimation. The findings are likely to be of interest to researchers working on clustering, density estimation, and non-convex optimization. The paper could also inspire the development of new algorithms that are less susceptible to getting trapped in suboptimal local maxima. The demonstration that standard approaches can fail even in idealized settings is a crucial warning for practitioners. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical results with rigorous proofs (as far as can be assessed from the main text).
*   Novel and insightful contribution to the understanding of GMM optimization.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Addresses a long-standing open problem (Srebro’s conjecture).
*   Significant potential impact on both theoretical and practical work in the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Detailed proof verification requires inspecting the appendix (limited access during review).
*   Does not offer concrete solutions for improved initialization strategies.
*   Limited to specific settings (equal mixing weights, identity covariance).



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs. Despite the minor points for improvement, the novelty, rigor, and potential impact of the work strongly justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS 2016.
