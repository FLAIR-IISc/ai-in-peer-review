- Main result: For GMMs with M ≥ 3, the population log-likelihood can have spurious local maxima; authors construct well-separated, spherical, equally-weighted examples where local maxima are arbitrarily worse than the global maximum.  
- Algorithmic consequence: For these bad GMMs, EM and a first-order EM variant with standard random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability approaching 1 exponentially fast in M.  
- Saddle vs. maxima: The first-order EM variant converges to strict saddle points with probability zero, implicating poor local maxima (not saddles) as the failure mode.  
- Technical quality: The paper is technically sound; constructions and probabilistic analyses are rigorous and resolve a long-standing open question (Srebro 2007).  
- Practical relevance concern: The pathological GMM constructions rely on hierarchical/clustered center arrangements, and the paper does not argue how likely such configurations are in real data.  
- Need for prior-based argument: The significance would increase if authors showed these bad configurations arise with non-trivial probability under reasonable priors for true centers.  
- Clarity issue (M=2 discussion): The M=2 example (Lines 74–81) is confusing—its purpose (motivation vs. contrast) should be clarified.  
- Clarity issue (Theorem 2 proof sketch): The favorable initialization description is unclear and over-restrictive; need more detail on why centers become trapped rather than move to correct clusters.  
- Clarity issue (Theorem 4 motivation): The practical importance of distinguishing saddle convergence from local maxima should be better explained and tied to algorithmic implications.  
- Originality: The work is highly original, giving a definitive negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture and rigorously linking EM failure to likelihood geometry at the population level.  
- Significance: Results are important and likely influential, showing even idealized settings can defeat local-search methods and motivating provable initialization schemes.  
- Limitation: Primary limitation is uncertain practical prevalence of the constructed pathological cases, which weakens immediate applicability.  
- Specific skepticism: For M=3, it seems unlikely a random initialization would frequently place centers in the failure-inducing arrangement; authors should better justify their random initialization analysis.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Resolves an important open question; provides rigorous explanation for EM sensitivity; shows high-probability convergence to bad maxima; motivates provable initialization research.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Practical relevance of pathological constructions is unclear; random initialization analysis needs stronger justification for real-world likelihood.  
- Recommendation: Accept—the theoretical contributions are significant and definitive, and practical concerns are avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws.  
- Minor edits: Multiple typos and phrasing issues noted (L18, L38, L60, L138, L172, L177, L218, L220, L225, L245, L249, L308) and requests for clarification (e.g., what O(d^{1/2})-separation means).
