This paper studies the population log-likelihood surface of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and the behavior of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm in a simplified setting where the number of components, mixture weights, and covariances are known and fixed. The main contribution is a set of negative results that run counter to common intuition. The authors prove that for M ≥ 3 components, the population log-likelihood can have local maxima whose values are arbitrarily worse than the global maximum, providing a definitive negative answer to a conjecture by Srebro (2007). Building on this structural result, the paper shows that both the standard EM algorithm and a first-order (gradient) variant, when initialized randomly by drawing samples from the true mixture, will converge to these suboptimal critical points with a probability that is overwhelmingly high (approaching 1 exponentially in M). Finally, it is shown that the first-order EM algorithm almost surely avoids strict saddle points, suggesting that the observed algorithmic failure is genuinely due to the presence of bad local maxima.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a fundamental and important question regarding the optimization landscape of GMMs, one of the most widely used models in machine learning. The resolution of Srebro's (2007) conjecture is a significant theoretical contribution. The results provide a formal basis for the folk wisdom that EM for GMMs is sensitive to initialization, but the strength of the results—demonstrating not just the existence of bad local maxima but the high probability of converging to them with a standard random initialization—is both surprising and impactful.

2.  **Interesting and Informative Results:** The findings are valuable for both theorists and practitioners. They clearly establish that even in a highly idealized setting (population likelihood, well-separated spherical Gaussians), the likelihood surface can be treacherous. This provides strong motivation for the development and use of more sophisticated initialization techniques (e.g., based on the method of moments) over simple random sampling, especially as the number of mixture components grows.

3.  **Acceptable Simplifications:** The analysis is restricted to a simplified model with a known number of components, uniform weights, and identity covariances. Given the technical difficulty of analyzing the GMM likelihood surface, these simplifications are reasonable and allow the authors to isolate and clearly demonstrate the core issue. The main message of the paper remains powerful despite these assumptions.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Major Concern: Soundness of the Proof of Theorem 1:** My main concern lies with the proof of Theorem 1 (lines 288–306), which is the cornerstone of the paper. The argument presented is too brief to be verifiable and appears to make unjustified logical leaps. Specifically, the proof relies on showing that the supremum of the log-likelihood on the boundary of a specially constructed neighborhood is strictly smaller than the value at a suboptimal interior point. This involves reasoning about suprema over domains that are either unbounded or change with a parameter that goes to infinity. Continuity of the likelihood function alone is not sufficient to guarantee the claimed convergences and interchanges of limits and suprema. A much more detailed and rigorous justification is required here to establish the paper's central claim. While I did not consult the supplementary material, a result of this importance should be proven convincingly in the main paper.

2.  **Generalization to Higher Dimensions (d > 1):** The paper is presented as being applicable to general dimension *d*, but the proofs are provided only for the one-dimensional case (*d*=1). It is not obvious how the geometric construction or the subsequent analysis extends to higher dimensions. The authors should explicitly clarify whether the results and proofs hold for *d* > 1. If they do, a sketch of the extension should be provided. If not, the paper's claims should be carefully scoped to *d*=1, and a discussion of the challenges in higher dimensions would be welcome.

3.  **Clarity of Gradient EM Section:** The section introducing the first-order EM algorithm (lines 162–172) could be clearer. The derivation of the population gradient update in Equation (6) is stated without much explanation. While the authors cite prior work, a brief sketch of how this update is derived from the gradient of the Q-function would improve the paper's readability and make it more self-contained.

In summary, this paper tackles an important problem and presents significant, albeit negative, results about the behavior of EM for GMMs. The potential impact is high. However, the recommendation for acceptance is conditional on addressing the major concern regarding the proof of Theorem 1. Without a rigorous and expanded proof for this foundational result, the technical soundness of the paper is in question. I would strongly encourage the authors to use the extra page available for the final version to move the detailed proof from the appendix into the main text, even at the expense of some of the more high-level discussion.
