- Addresses Srebro (2007) conjecture and shows it is false: population GMM likelihood can have bad local maxima.  
- Main constructive result: for M ≥ 3 components, population log-likelihood admits local maxima whose values are arbitrarily worse than global optima.  
- Algorithmic consequence: EM (and a first-order variant) with random initialization converges to bad critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{-Ω(M)}.  
- First-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, so failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Theoretical implication: provides rigorous justification for the practical need for careful initialization in GMM estimation even in well-specified, favorable settings.  
- Advances understanding of non-convex optimization in the asymptotic (population) regime and links likelihood geometry to algorithm behavior.  
- Presentation quality: well-written, clear organization, intuitive M=3, d=1 example that illustrates the construction.  
- Technical quality: rigorous, carefully developed proofs and appropriate connection to prior work (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. 2015).  
- Significance and originality: substantial and novel resolution of a long-standing open question, relevant to both theory and practice.  
- Main limitation: probability bound lacks concrete constant c and dependence on other parameters; reviewer recommends providing numerical estimates or simulations.  
- Recommendation: strong accept for NIPS — technically sound, significant, and well-presented.
