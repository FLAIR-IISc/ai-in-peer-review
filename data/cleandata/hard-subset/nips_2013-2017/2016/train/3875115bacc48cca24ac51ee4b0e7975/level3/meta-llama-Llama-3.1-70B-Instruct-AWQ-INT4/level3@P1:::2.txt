This paper studies the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the means in a Gaussian mixture model with a known and fixed number of components, weights, and covariances. The authors prove three negative results regarding the EM algorithm and its first-order variant, which are significant for understanding the behavior of these algorithms in practice.

The first main result (Theorem 1) shows that, even for large samples, the population log-likelihood function of a Gaussian mixture model can have local maxima with likelihood values arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum. This result resolves an open question of Srebro (2007) and highlights the potential difficulties in using local search algorithms to estimate the parameters of a Gaussian mixture model.

The second main result (Theorem 2) demonstrates that the EM algorithm, when initialized randomly, converges to a bad critical point with high probability. This result is significant because it shows that the EM algorithm can fail to recover the true parameters of the model, even when the model is well-specified and the sample size is large.

The third main result (Theorem 3) extends the second result to the first-order EM algorithm, which is a variant of the EM algorithm that uses gradient ascent updates. The authors show that the first-order EM algorithm also converges to a bad critical point with high probability, when initialized randomly.

The paper is well-written, and the results are informative and worthwhile for understanding the behavior of the EM algorithm in practice. The analysis is restricted to a simplified model with known K, uniform weights, and identity covariances, which is acceptable given the technicality of the results.

However, there are some concerns regarding the proof of Theorem 1. The proof is too short, and the lines 288-306, which involve limits of supremums/maximums over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains, need more detailed justification. Continuity alone does not imply the claimed convergences, and the reviewer suggests lengthening the main proof (even at the expense of discussion) to convince readers.

Additionally, the gradient EM section (lines 162-172) is hard to follow, and the reviewer requests more explanation of how Equation (5) is derived.

Overall, the paper provides significant insights into the behavior of the EM algorithm and its variants, and the results are important for understanding the limitations of these algorithms in practice. With some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field.
