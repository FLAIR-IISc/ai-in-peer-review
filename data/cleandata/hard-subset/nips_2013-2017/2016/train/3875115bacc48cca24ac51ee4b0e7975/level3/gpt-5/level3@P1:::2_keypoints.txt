- Paper resolves Srebro’s conjecture: in population GMMs with M ≥ 3, equal weights, spherical covariances, bad local maxima exist even under strong separation, and can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.  
- EM with random initialization converges to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{−Ω(M)}; same exponential-failure holds for first-order (gradient) EM.  
- First-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, implying bad local maxima—not saddles—explain EM failures.  
- Complements prior positive local-convergence and method-of-moments results by showing the global landscape can be treacherous even in favorable settings.  

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity and importance: addresses a long-standing conjecture with a clean population-level counterexample.  
- Technical contributions: constructs arbitrarily bad local maxima and a hierarchical instance giving e^{−Ω(M)} failure probability for random restarts.  
- Algorithmic insight: “no strict saddle convergence” distinguishes failure modes and reconciles saddle proliferation observations with practical failures.  
- Scope/impact: results are meaningful in large-sample regimes and emphasize the need for careful initialization or alternative estimators.  

Major weaknesses / required clarifications:
- Theorem 1 proof in main text (lines 288–306) is too terse; needs explicit neighborhood definition, compactness/tightness arguments, and justification that the objective attains a maximum.  
- Must justify interchange of limits and suprema when feasible sets depend on parameters (recommend epi-convergence, uniform convergence, or direct bounding).  
- If proof uses separation → ∞, isolate quantitative inequalities (e.g., bounds on cross-responsibilities) and state uniformity over the neighborhood.  
- Scope vs. dimension: constructions/proofs are in d = 1 but problem is phrased for general d; authors should state whether and how results extend to d > 1 (e.g., embedding argument or required geometric considerations).  
- Gradient EM exposition (lines 162–172) is hard to follow: derive Eq. (5) from maximizing Q in the population, compute ∇θ′Q|θ′=θ, and explicate equivalence to gradient ascent on L with a citation to Balakrishnan et al. (2015).  

Minor requests:
- Clarify dependence of step size restriction s ∈ (0,1) in Theorem 3 on M, separation, and d, and whether larger steps affect saddle avoidance or attraction to bad maxima.  

Quality assessment:
- Technically sound overall and tackles an important open question, but main proof needs more rigor in the main text rather than only in the supplement.  

Clarity suggestions:
- Add a more complete proof sketch of Theorem 1 in the main text.  
- Provide a clearer, self-contained derivation for gradient EM and its relation to ascent on L.  
- Include a brief schematic figure for the recursive two-cluster construction behind Theorem 2 to illustrate why success probability is e^{−Ω(M)}.  

Originality:
- High — novel negative structural results at the population level and exponential failure rate of random restarts.  

Significance:
- High — changes expectations about EM even in idealized regimes and motivates research on initialization and alternative objectives.  

Concrete suggestions for improvement:
- Move a strengthened proof of Theorem 1 into the main paper.  
- Add a concise lemma bounding responsibilities under large separation that is reused across results.  
- Discuss robustness: do constructions and negative results persist under mild misspecification (weight/variance asymmetry)?  
- Clarify how population results transfer to finite samples (assumptions on n relative to M, d, and separation).  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves an open question with clean constructions, gives sharp algorithmic consequences, and is conceptually impactful.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Main proof is currently too terse and key steps need to be made explicit; presentation focused on d = 1 without clear higher-dimensional implications.  

Recommendation:
- Accept, contingent on strengthening Theorem 1’s proof and improving the gradient EM exposition.
