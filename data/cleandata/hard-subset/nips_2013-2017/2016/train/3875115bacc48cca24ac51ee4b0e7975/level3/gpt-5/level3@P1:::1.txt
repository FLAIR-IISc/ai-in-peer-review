This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood of equally weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and the behavior of EM and a first-order (gradient) EM variant under random initialization. The main structural result resolves an open question of Srebro (2007): for M ≥ 3 components, even in 1D and under generous separation, the population likelihood has spurious local maxima whose values can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. Algorithmically, the authors show that EM and first-order EM, when initialized by drawing M i.i.d. centers from the underlying population (the infinite-sample analogue of sampling from the data), converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^−Ω(M). They further show that first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, so the failure mode is genuinely bad local maxima rather than saddles. Together, these results give a coherent and rigorous account of how and why EM can fail to find global optima even with unlimited data and in well-separated, spherical settings.

Relative to prior work, the paper complements local convergence analyses of EM that require good initializations (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and algorithmic alternatives based on clustering (Arora & Kannan, 2005; Vempala & Wang, 2002; Chaudhuri & Rao, 2008) or the method of moments (Moitra & Valiant, 2010; Hsu & Kakade, 2013). The negative results here clarify that such initialization steps are not merely technical artifacts: without them, EM’s success probability can be exponentially small in M. The strict-saddle avoidance for first-order EM connects to recent gradient-method theory (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas & Piliouras, 2016).

Technical contributions and intuition:
- Structural counterexample (Theorem 1): For M = 3, the construction places two true means close together and the third far away; a configuration that merges the close pair and over-allocates centers to the far component forms a basin containing a suboptimal local maximum. The log-likelihood gap can be made arbitrarily large by tuning separations. This extends to any M ≥ 3.
- Algorithmic failure (Theorems 2–3): A recursive “clusters-within-clusters” arrangement implies that for random initialization, to reach a global maximizer one must get the correct number of initial centers in every cluster at every level; the probability of this event is e^−Ω(M). The same phenomenon holds for EM and first-order EM (for non-aggressive step sizes).
- Saddle-point avoidance (Theorem 4): First-order EM converges to critical points and almost surely avoids strict saddles, so convergence to poor local maxima is the dominant failure mode.

Quality:
- The results appear technically sound, with clear statements and proof roadmaps. The construction for e^−Ω(M) success probability is particularly compelling. The use of recent dynamical-systems insights to rule out strict-saddle convergence for first-order EM is appropriate; it would help to more explicitly state the regularity conditions under which the measure-zero arguments apply to L, and to clarify the stepsize range for which the mapping is a diffeomorphism or at least locally stable.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable. Suggestions:
  - Add an illustrative figure for the M = 3 bad-local-maximum construction analogous to Figure 1 (used for M = 2), to aid intuition.
  - Precisely define “bad critical point” and “well-separated” early (e.g., scale of separation relative to √d) and keep notation for neighborhoods and basins consistent.
  - When claiming extension from population to finite-sample settings, provide a brief, concrete statement (with rates and sample complexity) via uniform convergence bounds to avoid leaving this as an informal remark.

Originality:
- Resolving Srebro’s conjecture is novel and important. The exponential failure probability under random initialization is a strong, clean statement. The strict-saddle avoidance draws on known theory but its specialization to first-order EM in this setting is useful. Some of the intuitions (misallocation of centers, hierarchical traps) echo well-known failure modes of Lloyd’s algorithm for k-means, but the contribution here is a rigorous population-likelihood analysis for EM.

Significance:
- The results matter: EM is ubiquitous, and the message that random restarts can have exponentially small success probability under benign-looking models should influence practice. The work also motivates principled initializations (e.g., spectral or moment-based). Limitations to note: the constructions are spherical, equal-weight, and 1D (though extendable), and the study is population-level with finite-sample consequences sketched rather than proved in full generality. No empirical demonstrations are provided; a small experimental section illustrating the constructions would strengthen the case for practitioners.

Relation to clustering/k-means:
- The bad examples mirror known failures of Lloyd’s algorithm; the exponentially small success probability for naive random seeding is consistent with k-means folklore and theory. It would be valuable to compare with seeding strategies such as overspecifying initial centers (e.g., pick Θ(M log M) and prune) or distance-squared sampling (k-means++; Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007), and with separation-based guarantees for k-means/Lloyd or spectral initializations (e.g., Kumar & Kannan, 2010). This comparison could sharpen the practical message for EM initialization and connect to literature familiar to the NIPS audience.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide empirical illustrations of the 3-component and recursive constructions, showing EM/first-order EM trajectories and the frequency of trapping as M grows.
- Discuss whether and how k-means++-style seeding or spectral/moment initializations change the failure probability, and whether the analysis can bound the success probability under such seeds.
- Clarify the finite-sample extension with a theorem capturing the sample size needed for the population geometry to dominate.
- Expand discussion on the M = 2 case and whether any partial progress is possible (e.g., ruling out bad local maxima under certain separations).
- Contrast EM vs. Lloyd’s algorithm under large separation; situate results with Kumar–Kannan (2010) and related separation-based analyses.

Pros:
- Resolves a long-standing open question (Srebro, 2007) with a sharp counterexample.
- Strong, clean algorithmic consequence: random-init EM/gradient-EM fail with probability 1 − e^−Ω(M).
- Clarifies that bad local maxima—not saddles—drive failures for first-order EM.
- Clear implications for practice (need for careful initialization).

Cons:
- Results are negative and may feel expected given known k-means failures; limited constructive guidance.
- Restrictive model class (spherical, equal weights) and population focus; finite-sample guarantees not fully developed.
- No experiments; limited discussion of practical seeding strategies and direct comparison to Lloyd/k-means guarantees.

Overall: A solid and timely theoretical contribution with clear importance for the community. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing clarity points and adding a comparative discussion with k-means-style initialization strategies (and, if space permits, brief empirical illustrations).
