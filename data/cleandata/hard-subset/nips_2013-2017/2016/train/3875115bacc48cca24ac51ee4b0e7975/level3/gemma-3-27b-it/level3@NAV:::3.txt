## Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper investigates the existence of bad local maxima in the population log-likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) with isotropic, known-scale components. The authors demonstrate that such bad local maxima exist even with infinite data, resolving a conjecture by Srebro [2007]. Furthermore, they prove that with random initialization, the EM algorithm is likely to converge to these suboptimal points as the number of components increases. The work is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question about the reliability of EM in GMMs, a widely used statistical tool. It builds upon prior work studying identifiability (Allman et al. [2009]) and convergence rates (Chen [1995], Ho & Nguyen [2015]) in GMMs, and acknowledges the practical relevance of EM (Dempster et al. [1977]) while exploring its theoretical limitations.

The paper's strength lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis and clear presentation of results. Proving the existence of bad local maxima and demonstrating the high probability of EM converging to them represents a significant contribution. The analysis of the first-order EM algorithm and the demonstration that it’s unlikely to converge to saddle points adds nuance to the understanding of its performance. 

However, the practical relevance of the findings is questionable. The specific configurations required to exhibit these “bad” centers seem contrived. It's unclear how likely such configurations are to arise in real-world datasets, even if the model is well-specified. While Theorem 2 demonstrates failure with random initialization, a stronger argument would involve assuming a distribution on the *true* centers and showing the probability of “bad” configurations increases with k or d. The authors acknowledge this in their response to a previous reviewer but haven't fully addressed it. 

Furthermore, the random initialization scheme feels somewhat unrealistic. In many practical scenarios, when data clearly exhibits clustering, initialization wouldn't be purely random. The example in L74-81, intended to support Srebro’s conjecture for k=2, isn't convincing. Also, the necessity of requirement (1) in L233-235 isn’t clear, and the practical importance of distinguishing convergence to a saddle point versus a local optimum (L267-273) is debatable. There are also minor phrasing issues (e.g., "well-separated constant" in L218, Ω(k) in L225) and typos as noted by the author.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical results addressing a fundamental question in GMM estimation.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Addresses the limitations of a widely-used algorithm (EM).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited practical relevance of the demonstrated failure modes.
*   Unrealistic initialization assumption.
*   Some minor clarity and technical issues.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some concerns about practical relevance, this paper presents a solid theoretical contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity issues and a more compelling discussion regarding the likelihood of the demonstrated failure modes in real-world applications. Specifically, addressing the reviewer's request for a distribution over true centers would significantly strengthen the paper.
