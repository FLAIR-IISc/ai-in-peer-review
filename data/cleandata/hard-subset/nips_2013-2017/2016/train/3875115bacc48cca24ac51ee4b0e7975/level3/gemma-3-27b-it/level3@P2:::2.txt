This paper presents compelling theoretical results demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), and subsequently showing that the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, even its first-order variant, is likely to converge to these suboptimal points when initialized randomly. The work addresses a long-standing open question posed by Srebro [2007] and provides crucial insights into the challenges of GMM estimation, even in seemingly well-behaved settings.

The authors clearly articulate the problem and its relevance, positioning it within the broader context of mixture model theory and the limitations of existing algorithms. They build upon previous work by Teicher [1963], Genovese & Wasserman [2000], and Dasgupta & Schulman [2007], among others, to rigorously demonstrate the problematic behavior of the likelihood surface. The results are significant because they highlight the necessity of careful initialization strategies for EM, even in high-dimensional scenarios and with well-separated components. The analysis of the first-order EM algorithm (Theorem 4) further strengthens the argument, indicating that the issues stem primarily from local optima rather than saddle points, a point relevant to recent discussions regarding optimization landscapes in high dimensions [Pascanu et al., 2014].

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents mathematically sound proofs for its main claims, offering a strong theoretical foundation for the observed phenomena.
*   **Relevance & Significance:** Addressing the Srebro conjecture and providing insights into the practical limitations of EM is a significant contribution to the field.  The findings have implications for a wide range of applications utilizing GMMs.
*   **Clear Exposition:** The introduction provides a good overview of the problem, related work, and the authors' contributions. The motivation is clear, and the importance of the work is well-articulated.
*   **Novelty:** Demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima even for well-separated Gaussian mixtures is a novel result that challenges prior intuition.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Dimensionality Restriction:** While the paper is formulated for general dimension *d*, the proofs are presented solely for *d* = 1.  The authors should explicitly state whether extending the proofs to *d* > 1 is possible, or detail the key additional difficulties that arise in higher dimensions. This is a crucial point for assessing the broader applicability of the results.
*   **Proof Justification (Theorem 1):** The proof of Theorem 1, specifically lines 288-306, feels somewhat compressed. The claimed convergences involving suprema and maxima over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains require more detailed justification. Establishing continuity is insufficient; a more rigorous argument demonstrating the convergence of the relevant functions is needed to fully convince the reader.  Even at the expense of some discussion, lengthening the main proof to provide this clarity is strongly recommended.
*   **First-Order EM Explanation:** The discussion of the first-order EM algorithm around lines 162-172 is a bit difficult to follow. Providing a more detailed derivation or explanation of how Equation (5) is obtained would improve the clarity and accessibility of this section.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work within its scope. The authors are careful to present their results and acknowledge the limitations.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but as noted above, the explanation of the first-order EM updates and the justification of the proof of Theorem 1 could be improved.
*   **Originality:** The results are novel and represent a significant advancement in our understanding of the EM algorithm for GMMs. The choice to tackle the Srebro conjecture directly adds to the originality.
*   **Significance:** The findings are important, as they provide a theoretical justification for the observed sensitivity of EM to initialization. This has practical implications for researchers and practitioners who rely on EM for GMM estimation.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important open problem and provides a rigorous solution.
*   The results are theoretically sound and have significant implications for the design and analysis of GMM estimation algorithms.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented (with the noted exceptions).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The dimensionality restriction limits the broader applicability of the results.
*   The lack of sufficient justification in the proof of Theorem 1 raises concerns about the completeness of the argument.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns regarding the dimensionality restriction and provide a more detailed justification for the proof of Theorem 1. Clarifying the explanation of the first-order EM algorithm would also improve the paper. Despite these minor issues, the paper presents a significant and valuable contribution to the field. The presented insights are insightful and address a crucial issue in Gaussian mixture modeling.
