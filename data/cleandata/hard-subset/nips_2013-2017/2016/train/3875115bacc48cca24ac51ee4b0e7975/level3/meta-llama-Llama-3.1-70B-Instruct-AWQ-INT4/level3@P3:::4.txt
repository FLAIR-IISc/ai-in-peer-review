This paper studies the structure of local maxima of the Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) log-likelihood with equal mixing weights and identity covariance. The authors provide three key results. Firstly, they present a counterexample showing that local maxima can have arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global maximum for a mixture of three well-separated Gaussians in one dimension. Secondly, they prove that with random initialization, the probability of avoiding a bad local maximum can be exponentially small in the number of mixture components. Lastly, they show that the first-order EM algorithm almost surely does not converge to a saddle point of the log-likelihood.

The technical quality of the paper is high, with sound proofs in the main paper and a reasonable proof idea for Theorem 2, although the appendix details were not thoroughly checked. The novelty and originality of the paper lie in relating the mean configuration to local maxima, with the hierarchical grouping insight for more than three components being particularly clever.

The potential impact and usefulness of the paper are significant, as it could inspire theoretical work on clustering optima and practical seeding strategies. The authors' suggestion to check whether Lemma 7 extends to higher dimensions is also noteworthy. The clarity and presentation of the paper are excellent, with well-explained results and intuition, a clear problem setup, and insightful interpretations.

The paper provides a negative answer to the open question of Srebro [2007], which conjectured that any local maximum of the likelihood function is a global maximum in the limit of infinite samples. The authors' construction of a mixture of three well-separated Gaussians in one dimension, whose population log-likelihood function contains local maxima with arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global maximum, is particularly insightful.

The algorithmic consequences of the paper's results are also significant. The authors show that the EM algorithm, as well as the first-order EM algorithm, with random initialization, converges to a bad critical point with high probability. This result highlights the necessity of careful initialization when using the EM algorithm in practice, even in highly favorable settings.

The paper's results also have implications for the type of fixed points reached by the first-order EM algorithm. The authors show that the algorithm cannot converge to strict saddle points, which suggests that the sub-optimal points to which the algorithm frequently converges are bad local maxima.

In conclusion, this paper provides a significant contribution to the understanding of the structure of local maxima of the GMM log-likelihood and the algorithmic consequences of these results. The technical quality, novelty, and potential impact of the paper make it a valuable addition to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a negative answer to an open question of Srebro [2007], which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The authors' construction of a mixture of three well-separated Gaussians in one dimension is insightful and provides a clear understanding of the problem.
* The algorithmic consequences of the paper's results are significant, highlighting the necessity of careful initialization when using the EM algorithm in practice.
* The paper's results have implications for the type of fixed points reached by the first-order EM algorithm, suggesting that the sub-optimal points to which the algorithm frequently converges are bad local maxima.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's results are limited to the case of equal mixing weights and identity covariance, which may not be representative of all GMMs.
* The authors' proof of Theorem 2 relies on a specific construction of a Gaussian mixture distribution with a recursive structure, which may not be generalizable to other cases.
* The paper's results do not provide a clear solution to the problem of avoiding bad local maxima in practice, although they do highlight the necessity of careful initialization.
