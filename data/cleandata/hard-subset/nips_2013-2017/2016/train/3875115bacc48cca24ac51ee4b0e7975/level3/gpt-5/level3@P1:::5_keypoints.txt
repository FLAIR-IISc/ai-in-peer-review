- Paper proves existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population GMM log-likelihood for equally weighted, spherical, well-separated mixtures with M ≥ 3 (even in 1D).  
- Shows EM and first-order EM with common random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{-Ω(M)}.  
- Establishes first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, so failures are due to bad local maxima, not saddles.  
- Answers Srebro’s 2007 conjecture negatively and reframes the role of initialization for EM in benign settings.  
- Complements prior identifiability and estimation work on GMMs and contrasts with positive local-convergence EM results under good initialization.  
- Leverages and extends strict-saddle and nonconvex optimization literature to the GMM likelihood geometry.  
- Theorem 1: constructs, for any M ≥ 3 and target gap, a uniformly weighted, unit-variance, well-separated GMM with a local maximum arbitrarily below the global optimum.  
- Theorem 2: proves random (uniform-from-data) initialization leads EM to suboptimal critical points with exponentially high probability via a recursive cluster-within-cluster construction.  
- Theorem 3: extends the negative convergence result to first-order (gradient) EM for step sizes in (0,1).  
- Theorem 4: shows first-order EM avoids strict saddles almost surely, pinpointing bad local maxima as the failure mode.  
- Technical arguments are sound at the population level and the 1D M=3 example provides clear intuition for the construction.  
- Reviewer requests committee verification of recursive construction and boundary arguments in the appendix.  
- Finite-sample extension is plausible via empirical process tools but lacks explicit sample-complexity quantification.  
- Manuscript is well organized and clearly written; recommends moving key appendix lemmas into the main text for readability.  
- Originality: high — resolves an open problem and provides structural understanding of GMM likelihoods.  
- Significance: high — impacts expectations for likelihood-based mixture methods and motivates improved initialization/hybrid methods.  
- Main weakness: missing explicit constants and scaling (c, required separation as functions of M, d, Cgap).  
- Main weakness: no empirical validation — suggests small synthetic experiments and EM trajectory visualizations.  
- Main weakness: lacks explicit finite-sample rates relating n to separation and M.  
- Main weakness: unclear robustness to weight imbalance, covariance perturbations, or mild anisotropy.  
- Main weakness: limited discussion of other initializations (k-means++, pruning) and how they interact with the negative results.  
- Main weakness/question: M=2 case remains open; requests more discussion or partial insights.  
- Suggestions: provide explicit (or conservative) constants, add experiments comparing initialization schemes, state a finite-sample theorem, and discuss robustness to model perturbations.  
- Pros: resolves open question, strong algorithmic implications, bridges likelihood geometry and strict-saddle theory, and is pedagogically written.  
- Cons: lacks concrete constants, empirical demonstration, and robustness/initialization discussion.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Reviewer confidence: High on contributions/significance; medium on full technical verification of appendices.
