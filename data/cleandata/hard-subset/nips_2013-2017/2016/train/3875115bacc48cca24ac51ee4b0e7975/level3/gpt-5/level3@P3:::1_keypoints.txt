- Studies population likelihood of spherical, equal-weight GMMs and EM/gradient EM under random initialization; resolves Srebro (2007) by constructing bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 even when well-separated.  
- Shows EM and gradient EM with i.i.d. random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{-Ω(M)} (success ≤ e^{-Ω(M)}).  
- Proves gradient EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, so failure stems from attraction to bad local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Connects to prior work (Dasgupta–Schulman, Balakrishnan et al., Moitra–Valiant/Hsu–Kakade, Loh–Wainwright) and echoes k-means pathologies.  
- Theorem 1: constructs well-separated GMMs (key M=3 example: two close means and one far) with arbitrarily large suboptimal local-maxima gap.  
- Theorems 2–3: random initialization leads to exponential failure probability via a recursive hierarchical clustering construction.  
- Theorem 4: gradient EM with reasonable step size avoids strict saddles, isolating bad local maxima as the main obstacle.  
- Strength: Resolves a long-standing open question by proving population-level bad local maxima exist for M ≥ 3 with large suboptimality.  
- Strength: Provides a coherent link between landscape structure, EM dynamics, and initialization, showing infinite data do not eliminate nonconvex pitfalls.  
- Strength: Clean population-level analysis that leverages modern saddle-avoidance results.  
- Weakness: Constructions and failure modes are unsurprising to practitioners familiar with k-means/EM sensitivity.  
- Weakness/Suggestion: Needs explicit comparison to Lloyd’s k-means behavior and matching exp(-Ω(k)) success probabilities for random-seeded k-means.  
- Suggestion: Contrast with Kumar–Kannan-style guarantees for k-means to assess whether analogous separation/stability can rescue EM.  
- Suggestion: Analyze practical mitigations inspired by k-means (oversampling k log k and D^2/k-means++ seeding) and whether they provably improve EM in the population setting.  
- Weakness: “Well-separated” (Ω(√d)) should be quantified precisely; clarify the ratio of within-pair vs. far-mean separation and how the suboptimality gap scales.  
- Weakness: Finite-sample implications are only sketched; an explicit sample-complexity bound to preserve population geometry is needed.  
- Weakness: Saddle-avoidance claims rely on step-size conditions; include sensitivity analysis and comments on nonconstant step sizes.  
- Quality: Results are clearly stated and proof ideas convincing; adding constants/separation thresholds and a finite-sample corollary would improve completeness.  
- Clarity: Manuscript is readable but would benefit from a figure of the 3-component landscape and a concise two-level example of the hierarchical construction.  
- Clarity: Recommend a table contrasting EM vs. Lloyd’s vs. spectral/moments methods under separation and initialization assumptions.  
- Originality: Novel population-level resolution of Srebro’s conjecture; exponential failure formalizes known folklore; saddle-avoidance application is expected but valuable.  
- Significance: Clarifies fundamental limitations of EM even in favorable regimes and underscores need for careful initialization; relevant to theory and practice.  
- For acceptance: Resolves an open question, ties landscape pathologies to algorithmic consequences, and delivers a clear practical message about initialization.  
- Against acceptance: Constructions are predictable to some audiences and the paper lacks in-depth exploration of remedies and finite-sample quantitative guarantees.  
- Requested additions: explicit comparison with Kumar–Kannan guarantees, analysis of improved seeding (k log k, D^2), and finite-sample corollaries relating n to separation and desired likelihood gap.
