This paper studies the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the means in a Gaussian mixture model with a known/fixed number of components, weights, and covariances. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the EM algorithm and its first-order variant, shedding light on their limitations in practice. Specifically, they prove three negative results: (i) local maxima with likelihood arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum can exist even for large samples; (ii) randomly initialized EM converges with high probability to such suboptimal critical points; and (iii) the same holds for gradient EM.

The reviewer finds the question interesting, the results informative, and worthwhile for understanding EM in practice. The analysis is restricted to a simplified model with known K, uniform weights, and identity covariances, which the reviewer deems acceptable given the technicality of the results. The paper is written for general dimension d, but the proofs are given for d = 1. The reviewer asks the authors to state whether extensions to d > 1 are possible or to discuss additional difficulties.

The main contribution of the paper is Theorem 1, which shows that the population log-likelihood of a Gaussian mixture model can have local maxima with arbitrarily bad likelihood. The proof of this theorem is concise but may benefit from additional justification, particularly in lines 288-306, where the limits of supremums/maximums over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains are taken. The reviewer suggests lengthening the main proof, even at the expense of discussion, to convince readers. The supplementary proofs are not included in the main text, but the reviewer recommends including them to provide a complete understanding of the results.

The paper also discusses the implications of Theorem 1 for the EM algorithm and its first-order variant. The authors show that randomly initialized EM converges to a bad critical point with high probability, and the same holds for gradient EM. These results have significant implications for the practice of using EM in Gaussian mixture models, highlighting the need for careful initialization.

The reviewer has a minor concern with the gradient EM section (lines 162-172), which is hard to follow. The authors could provide more explanation of how Equation (5) is derived to improve the clarity of the text.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the results are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The writing is clear, and the organization is logical. The reviewer appreciates the authors' effort to provide a thorough analysis of the EM algorithm and its limitations.
