

This paper addresses a fundamental question about the behavior of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically whether the population likelihood function can have "bad" local maxima. The authors resolve an open question posed by Srebro (2007) by constructing examples of well-separated, equally-weighted, spherical GMMs (with M ≥ 3 components) where the population log-likelihood contains local maxima with arbitrarily worse values than the global maximum. They further prove that both standard EM and gradient EM with random initialization converge to these suboptimal critical points with high probability (at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M))), and show that first-order EM avoids strict saddle points.

The paper makes an important theoretical contribution by revealing a fundamental limitation of local search methods for GMMs, even in favorable settings. This work complements prior studies on EM convergence (Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and provides crucial insights for practitioners about the necessity of careful initialization. The analysis is restricted to the simplified model (known K, uniform weights, identity covariances), which is well-justified given the technical complexity of the problem, though the authors should clarify whether their results extend to higher dimensions.

The paper is generally well-written and technically sound, but contains two significant concerns. First, the proof of Theorem 1 (lines 288-306) lacks sufficient justification for the claimed convergence of supremums over unbounded domains—continuity alone does not suffice, and the authors should provide more detailed arguments. Second, the gradient EM derivation (lines 162-172) is hard to follow, particularly the derivation of Equation (5), which needs clearer explanation.

The work demonstrates high quality and significance, resolving a long-standing open question with important implications for understanding EM's behavior. While the technical depth is appropriate for the simplified model, the insufficient justification in Theorem 1's proof and the unclear gradient EM derivation are substantial concerns that require addressing before acceptance. With these revisions, this paper would be a valuable contribution to the literature on non-convex optimization and mixture models.
