This paper studies the structure of local maxima of the Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) log-likelihood, focusing on the idealized situation where every mixture component is equally weighted and spherical with unit variance. The authors provide two fundamental results on the population likelihood function of GMMs with M ≥ 3 components.

The first main result shows that the population likelihood function has bad local maxima even in the special case of equally-weighted mixtures of well-separated and spherical Gaussians. The authors prove that the log-likelihood value of these bad local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than that of any global optimum, thereby resolving an open question of Srebro [2007]. This result is presented in Theorem 1, which states that for any M ≥ 3 and any constant Cgap > 0, there is a well-separated uniform mixture of M unit-variance spherical Gaussians GMM(µ∗) and a local maximum µ′ such that L(µ′) ≤ L(µ∗)− Cgap.

The second main result shows that the EM algorithm (or a first-order variant of it) with random initialization will converge to bad critical points with probability at least 1− e−Ω(M). This result is presented in Theorem 2, which states that there exists a universal constant c, for any M ≥ 3 and any constant Cgap > 0, such that there is a well-separated uniform mixture of M unit-variance spherical Gaussians GMM(µ∗) with P [ ∀t ≥ 0, L(µt) ≤ L(µ∗)− Cgap ] ≥ 1− e−cM.

The authors also prove that under the same random initialization scheme, the first-order EM algorithm with a suitable stepsize does not converge to a strict saddle point with probability one. This result is presented in Theorem 4, which states that for any M-component mixture of spherical Gaussians, the iterates µt converge to a critical point of the log-likelihood, and for any strict saddle point µss, we have P (limt→∞ µt = µss) = 0.

The technical quality of the proofs appears sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-explained problem setup and interpretations. The novelty and originality of the results are high, particularly the hierarchical grouping insight for k>3, which is clever and original. The potential impact and usefulness of the results are significant, as they could inspire theoretical work on clustering optima and practical seeding strategies.

* The paper provides a negative answer to the open question of Srebro [2007], showing that the population likelihood function of GMMs with M ≥ 3 components can have bad local maxima.
* The authors prove that the EM algorithm (or a first-order variant of it) with random initialization will converge to bad critical points with probability at least 1− e−Ω(M).
* The paper provides a clear and well-explained problem setup and interpretations.

Weaknesses:

* The authors do not provide empirical evidence to support their theoretical results.
