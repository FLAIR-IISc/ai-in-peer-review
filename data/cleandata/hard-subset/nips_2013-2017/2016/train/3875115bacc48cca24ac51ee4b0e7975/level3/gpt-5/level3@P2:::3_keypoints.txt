- Paper shows population log-likelihood for equal-weight spherical GMMs (M ≥ 3) can have arbitrarily bad local maxima even in d=1, resolving Srebro’s conjecture negatively (Thm 1).  
- Proves EM and first-order EM/gradient-ascent with i.i.d. mixture initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e−Ω(M) (Thms 2–3).  
- Shows first-order EM with fixed stepsize almost surely avoids strict saddles, so failures are due to bad local maxima, not saddles (Thm 4).  
- Builds on and connects to classical identifiability/statistics of mixtures, algorithmic EM analyses, algebraic geometry of optima, and gradient-dynamics/saddle literature.  
- Technical construction (“two-close-one-far” and recursive clustering) is plausible, convincing, and yields arbitrarily bad maxima via separation scaling.  
- Assumptions (equal weights, known spherical covariance) are favorable, so negative results under them strengthen the claim.  
- Full proofs deferred but exposition suggests sound ideas; finite-sample simulations would be valuable to confirm phenomena and exponential failure trend.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized, with useful geometric intuition; some places need clarification and reframing of practical implications.  
- M = 2 example is ambiguous in purpose and should be clarified as illustrative or related to the conjecture.  
- Novel contributions: sharp structural negative result for population likelihood, e−Ω(M) failure probability for random initialization, and adaptation of strict-saddle avoidance to first-order EM.  
- Significance: important theoretical insight warning against unstructured local search for mixtures; practical impact depends on how typical the adversarial center layouts are.  
- Main concern 1: Practical relevance — need average-case analysis or justification how likely “bad” cluster-of-clusters layouts are under natural priors.  
- Main concern 2: Initialization realism — compare random initialization to practical schemes (k-means++, PCA seeding, short k-means) and discuss interactions with failure instances.  
- Main concern 3: EM basin claims — provide formal lemma/justification that centers initialized between clusters cannot migrate to correct clusters (attraction basin invariant).  
- Main concern 4: Clarify role of M = 2 example and whether M = 2 remains an open problem.  
- Main concern 5: Tighten the contribution of the strict-saddle result — explain what new insight it provides beyond generic gradient-flow theory.  
- Specific suggestions: clarify separation parameters and notation (e.g., define Ω(M), explicit separation constants), state invariants about trapped centers, and reword ambiguous lines.  
- Minor edits: several line-specific wording/typo fixes listed (L18, L38, L60, L172, L177, L220, L245, L249, L308).  
- Arguments for acceptance: resolves Srebro’07 open question, provides sharp algorithmic consequences and influential techniques for nonconvex likelihood analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical relevance uncertain, initialization model may be too weak versus practice, and strict-saddle result offers limited incremental insight.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept, with encouragement to add average-case discussion, finite-sample experiments, and clearer treatment of initialization and the M = 2 case.  
- Reviewer confidence: High.
