- Paper analyzes population likelihood of equally weighted, isotropic, known-variance GMMs and proves three main results about the landscape and EM behavior.  
- Result (i): For M ≥ 3, the likelihood landscape admits arbitrarily bad local maxima even under strong separation, resolving Srebro (2007) negatively.  
- Result (ii): EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{−Ω(M)} via a recursive cluster construction.  
- Result (iii): First-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, so failures arise from bad local optima rather than saddle trapping.  
- Quality: Core theorems are technically careful and insightful; population-level focus removes finite-sample artifacts.  
- Clarity: Writing is generally good but some motivation and notation need clarification.  
- Originality: Novel resolution of the population local-maxima question for well-separated spherical mixtures.  
- Significance: Results importantly qualify when EM-style methods can be expected to work, highlighting initialization issues.  
- Concern — prevalence: The “bad” configurations are adversarial; the paper should assess how likely they are under realistic priors (e.g., i.i.d. centers, random meta-clusters, Poisson cluster process).  
- Concern — initialization realism: Random seeding may be pessimistic; quantify failure under practical schemes (k-means++, farthest-first, a few Lloyd iterations) or add empirical/theoretical bounds.  
- Concern — L233–235: Requirement (1) seems stronger than necessary; provide a dynamical justification or relax the assumption.  
- Concern — L74–81: The 2-component example is illustrative but does not substantiate the k=2 conjecture; clarify its purpose and temper claims.  
- Concern — L267–273: Emphasize why ruling out strict saddles materially changes practice versus simply confirming bad local maxima.  
- Notation/phrasing: Multiple minor edits and clarifications recommended (e.g., “well-separated constant” → “a fixed separation constant”, clarify Ω(k), O(d^{1/2}) separation, and several line-specific wording fixes).  
- Pros: Resolves a notable conjecture, offers crisp constructions, and gives clear algorithmic implications about initialization.  
- Cons: Practical likelihood under realistic priors/initializations is unclear and some exposition needs tightening.  
- Overall: Technically strong and original; recommend acceptance conditional on added evidence about prevalence and practical initialization behavior.
