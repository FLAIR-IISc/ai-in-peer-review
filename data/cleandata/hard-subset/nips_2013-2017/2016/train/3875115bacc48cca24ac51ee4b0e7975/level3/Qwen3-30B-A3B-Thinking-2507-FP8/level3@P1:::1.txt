

This paper addresses an important open question in Gaussian mixture model (GMM) theory: whether the population likelihood function of well-separated GMMs can have bad local maxima, as conjectured by Srebro (2007). The authors provide two main results that resolve this question in the negative. First, they construct a 3-component mixture of well-separated spherical Gaussians (with unit variance) where the population log-likelihood contains local maxima with arbitrarily worse log-likelihood values than global optima. Second, they show that the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to such bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)) for M ≥ 3 components.

The paper's structural results are theoretically sound and resolve a long-standing open question. The counterexample construction (two close centers and one far center) is intuitive and mirrors known failure modes of k-means clustering, which the authors acknowledge. The proof techniques, while not entirely novel, are carefully executed and demonstrate the existence of bad local maxima even in highly favorable settings (equally-weighted, well-separated, spherical Gaussians). The paper also establishes that the first-order EM algorithm avoids strict saddle points almost surely, suggesting that the failure of these methods is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The algorithmic results are particularly compelling. The authors show that for M components, random initialization leads to failure with exponentially high probability (1 - e^(-Ω(M))). This implies that to find a good solution with constant probability, one would need to repeat the random initialization procedure exponentially many times (in M), which is computationally infeasible. This result strongly supports the need for careful initialization strategies in practice, aligning with recent work on moment-based estimators (Moitra & Valiant, 2010; Hsu & Kakade, 2013).

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the technical results accessible. The connections to previous work (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015) are well-established, and the authors appropriately acknowledge the expected nature of their counterexamples in light of k-means failure modes. The proofs are detailed and rigorous, with technical details appropriately deferred to the appendix.

The main limitation of the paper is that the results, while correct, follow expected lines of reasoning given the literature on non-convex optimization. The authors acknowledge this by noting that the construction "is somewhat expected" in light of k-means failures. While the resolution of Srebro's conjecture is significant, the techniques used to establish it are largely standard for this type of analysis. The paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison with the Kumar-Kannan (2007) and Vempala-Wang (2002) approaches to GMM estimation, which provide alternative algorithms with guarantees under strong separation assumptions. The authors note the relation to k-means, but a more detailed contrast between EM and Lloyd's algorithm guarantees under large separation conditions would strengthen the paper's contribution.

Regarding significance, the paper has important implications for understanding the limitations of EM in practice, particularly regarding initialization. The results demonstrate that even with infinite data and highly favorable conditions (well-separated, spherical Gaussians), EM can fail to converge to global optima with high probability under standard random initialization. This provides a theoretical foundation for the empirical observation that careful initialization is crucial in practice, even when using the EM algorithm on well-behaved data.

The paper makes a solid contribution to understanding the landscape of GMM likelihood functions and the behavior of EM. While the results themselves are not entirely surprising given the non-convex nature of the problem, their rigorous demonstration resolves an important open question and provides valuable theoretical guidance for practitioners. The exponential failure probability under random initialization is particularly impactful for understanding the practical limitations of the EM algorithm.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a clear and important point about the limitations of the EM algorithm. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important open question. While the techniques are not entirely novel, the results are significant and have clear implications for both theory and practice. The authors should consider strengthening the connection to prior algorithmic work on GMMs (e.g., Kumar-Kannan, Vempala-Wang) to better contextualize their results within the broader literature on efficient GMM estimation.

The paper should be accepted as it makes a clear and important contribution to understanding the limitations of the EM algorithm for Gaussian mixture models. The results are rigorous, well-motivated, and have practical implications for practitioners. The connection to k-means is noted but could be expanded to better position the work within the broader literature on mixture model estimation.
