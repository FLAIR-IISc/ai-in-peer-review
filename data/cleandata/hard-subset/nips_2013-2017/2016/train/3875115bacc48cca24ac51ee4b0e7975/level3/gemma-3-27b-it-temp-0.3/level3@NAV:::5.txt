## Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental question regarding the landscape of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs): do bad local maxima exist even in the asymptotic limit? The authors answer this question in the negative, constructing a class of counterexamples to Srebro’s (2007) conjecture. They further demonstrate that the widely used Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, and its gradient-based variant, converge to these suboptimal solutions with high probability under common random initialization. 

The work builds upon a rich history of research into GMM estimation, referencing key contributions from Teicher (1963) on identifiability, Genovese & Wasserman (2000) on rate of convergence, and Dasgupta & Schulman (2007) on EM analysis. It correctly positions itself within the ongoing debate about the challenges of non-convex optimization in statistical estimation, particularly in relation to recent work highlighting the prevalence of saddle points (Pascanu et al., 2014). The authors effectively demonstrate that, at least in the specific setting they analyze, bad local maxima are the primary obstacle to efficient estimation, rather than saddle points.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear exposition, rigorous theoretical analysis, and significant contribution to our understanding of GMM estimation. The authors strike a good balance between mathematical detail and intuitive explanation, making the core ideas accessible. The construction of the counterexample to Srebro’s conjecture is particularly elegant, and the proof for the simple k=3, d=1 case provides valuable insight. The results are highly significant, as they underscore the importance of careful initialization strategies for EM and related algorithms, even in seemingly well-behaved settings.

However, a key weakness is the lack of concrete values for the constants appearing in the probability bounds (e.g., the ‘c’ in Theorem 2). Knowing these constants is crucial for assessing the practical relevance of the findings. Specifically, understanding the phase transition – when does the probability of converging to a bad local maximum become significant for realistic values of *k* (number of components)? While the authors acknowledge this and state that simulations could address this, their absence weakens the paper’s immediate impact. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel and important theoretical results, clear and well-written, addresses a long-standing open question, significant implications for practical GMM estimation.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Lack of explicit constants in probability bounds hinders practical assessment.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. While the missing constants are a concern, they do not detract from the novelty or theoretical significance of the work. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors providing estimates for the key constants or including supporting simulations to illustrate the phenomenon.
