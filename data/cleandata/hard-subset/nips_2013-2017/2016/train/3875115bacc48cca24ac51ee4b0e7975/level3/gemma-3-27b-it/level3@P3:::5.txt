This paper addresses a fundamental question regarding the likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs): whether, in the infinite-sample limit, all local maxima are also global maxima (a conjecture posed by Srebro in 2007). The authors answer this question negatively by constructing a specific class of counterexamples – a three-component, well-separated GMM in one dimension – demonstrating the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima. Furthermore, they show that the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, and its first-order variant, converge to these suboptimal solutions with high probability when initialized randomly by sampling from the data. The paper complements these structural results with an analysis of initialization schemes and a detailed proof for the simplest non-trivial case (k=3, d=1). 

The work builds upon a rich history of research into GMMs, referencing key papers on identifiability (Allman et al., 2009), convergence rates (Chen, 1995; Ghosal & Van Der Vaart, 2001; Ho & Nguyen, 2015), and algorithmic analysis of EM (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015). It places itself within a broader context of non-convex optimization, drawing connections to recent findings on the behavior of gradient descent in high-dimensional settings (Loh & Wainwright, 2013; Wang et al., 2014; Ge et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016; Panageas & Piliouras, 2016). The motivation, drawing from the robustness of maximum likelihood estimation (Donoho & Liu, 1988), is clearly articulated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper provides a definitive negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture, a longstanding open problem. This is a significant contribution to our understanding of the landscape of GMM likelihoods and the limitations of local search algorithms.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proofs appear rigorous and well-structured, particularly the detailed case study for k=3, d=1. The analysis of the first-order EM algorithm and its convergence properties is also compelling.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, balancing theoretical depth with intuitive explanations. The organization is logical, and the notation is consistent.  The authors effectively connect their results to the broader literature.
*   **Algorithmic Relevance:** The demonstration of high-probability convergence to bad local maxima under random initialization has practical implications for the use of EM and gradient-based methods.
*   **Theoretical Insight:** The paper provides new techniques for reasoning about the population log-likelihood function, potentially applicable to other non-convex optimization problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Constant Estimates:** The reviewer's primary concern, and a valid one, is the lack of explicit estimates for the constants in the probability bounds (e.g., the 'c' in Theorem 2). Knowing these constants is crucial for assessing the practical relevance of the results.  For what values of *M* (number of components) does the bad-solution convergence truly become problematic?  Without this information, it's difficult to determine the phase transition.
*   **Limited Simulations:** Related to the above, the paper would be strengthened by supporting simulations illustrating the phenomenon of convergence to bad local maxima for realistic values of *k*. These simulations could provide empirical evidence for the tightness of the bounds and offer practical guidance on the behavior of the algorithms.
*   **Step-size Sensitivity:** While the theoretical results on the first-order EM algorithm are interesting, the requirement of a step size *s* ∈ (0, 1) seems somewhat restrictive. Further analysis of the impact of different step-size strategies would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper answers a fundamental open question in the field.
*   The theoretical results are rigorously proven and well-supported.
*   The clarity of writing and organization are exceptional.
*   The findings have significant implications for the practical application of EM and related algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of explicit constant estimates hinders the practical interpretation of the results.
*   The absence of supporting simulations makes it difficult to assess the empirical validity of the theoretical findings.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses regarding constant estimates and simulations, this is a very strong paper. The novelty and significance of the results, coupled with the technical rigor and clarity of presentation, outweigh the limitations. The paper makes a substantial contribution to our understanding of the challenges in optimizing GMM likelihoods.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors providing either:

1.  Estimates for the constants in the probability bounds (even if approximate).
2.  Supporting simulations demonstrating the phenomenon of convergence to bad local maxima for a range of *k* values.

Even without these additions, the paper remains a valuable contribution, but addressing these concerns would significantly enhance its impact.
