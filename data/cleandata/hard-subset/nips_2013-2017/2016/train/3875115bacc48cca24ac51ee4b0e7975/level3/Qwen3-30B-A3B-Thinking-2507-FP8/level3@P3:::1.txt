

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental question in Gaussian mixture model (GMM) theory: whether the population likelihood function for well-separated, spherical GMMs has only global maxima (Srebro's conjecture, 2007). The authors provide two main contributions: (1) a counterexample showing that for M ≥ 3 components, the population likelihood can have arbitrarily bad local maxima even with well-separated clusters and infinite samples; (2) a proof that random initialization for EM and first-order EM fails with exponentially high probability (at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M))) to converge to global maxima, while avoiding strict saddle points.

The paper builds on previous work in GMM theory, particularly referencing the identifiability results of Teicher (1963), the density estimation work of Genovese and Wasserman (2000), and the EM convergence analysis by Dasgupta and Schulman (2007) and Balakrishnan et al. (2015). The authors connect their findings to k-means failures, noting that their bad examples mirror known failures of Lloyd's algorithm. This connection is valuable as it suggests that the same initialization strategies that work for k-means (e.g., picking k log k centers or distance-squared sampling) might be useful for EM.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical results are technically sound, with rigorous proofs for all main claims. The counterexample construction for M=3 (two close components, one far away) is elegant and clearly explained, and the proof techniques for showing existence of bad local maxima represent a novel contribution to non-convex optimization analysis. The connection between avoiding strict saddle points (via recent results from Lee et al., 2016) and the failure of EM being due to bad local maxima rather than saddles is particularly insightful.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. As the key points indicate, the results and techniques are "unsurprising and follow expected lines," which limits the paper's novelty. The counterexample for M=3 is somewhat artificial, and the paper does not explore whether three components are strictly necessary (the authors note this as an open question but don't investigate it). Most significantly, the paper omits a comparison with Kumar-Kannan (2005) or a contrast between EM and Lloyd's guarantees under large separation conditions, which would strengthen the connection to existing work on GMM estimation.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear exposition of the key results and their implications. The proofs are technically sound, and the authors are careful to note the limitations of their results (e.g., only for M ≥ 3, and only for the population setting). The practical implications are clear: even with unlimited data and well-separated clusters, random initialization for EM can fail with high probability, making careful initialization schemes critical.

The significance of the paper is moderate. It resolves an important theoretical question (Srebro's conjecture), which is valuable for understanding the fundamental limitations of EM for GMMs. However, it does not provide new algorithms or practical solutions, only theoretical insights. The connection to k-means is helpful for practitioners, but the paper doesn't translate its theoretical results into practical guidance for implementation.

In terms of the four evaluation criteria:
- Quality: High. The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs supporting all claims.
- Clarity: High. The paper is well-organized and clearly written.
- Originality: Moderate. The negative result is novel in resolving Srebro's conjecture, but the techniques are expected.
- Significance: Moderate. The result is theoretically important but doesn't directly lead to better algorithms.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution that resolves an open question in GMM theory. While the results are not surprising to experts in the field, they provide important theoretical justification for the empirical observation that careful initialization is critical for EM in practice. The paper would be strengthened by a comparison with Kumar-Kannan and a more thorough discussion of the relationship between EM and Lloyd's algorithm guarantees.

Pro: Resolves a long-standing theoretical question; provides clear counterexample to Srebro's conjecture; shows failure probability of random initialization is exponentially small in M.

Con: Results follow expected lines; counterexample somewhat artificial; omits comparison with Kumar-Kannan; doesn't provide practical algorithmic improvements.

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces and EM convergence. While the results may not be surprising to experts, they are important for establishing the fundamental limitations of EM in well-behaved settings. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
