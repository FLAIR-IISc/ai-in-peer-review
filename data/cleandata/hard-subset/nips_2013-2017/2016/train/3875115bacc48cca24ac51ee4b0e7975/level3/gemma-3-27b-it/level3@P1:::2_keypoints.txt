- Paper shows existence of “bad” local maxima in the population likelihood for mixtures of Gaussians even in a simplified setting (known K, uniform weights, identity covariances).
- Demonstrates that standard random initialization causes EM and first-order EM to converge to suboptimal solutions with high probability.
- Definitively disproves Srebro’s 2007 conjecture that the population likelihood lacks bad local maxima.
- Theoretical results challenge the assumption that EM reliably finds good solutions even with large samples, highlighting the need for careful initialization.
- Analysis is rigorous and builds on prior work in identifiability, convergence rates, and EM variants.
- Negative results are surprising and practically relevant for statistical estimation and non-convex optimization research.
- Strength: clear motivation, solid writing, and relevance to researchers in theory and algorithms.
- Weakness: proofs are provided only for the 1-dimensional case despite claims for general d, limiting immediate generality.
- Major concern: proof of Theorem 1 (lines 288–306) lacks sufficient justification for limit/suprema exchanges over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains.
- Suggestion: provide explicit bounds or convergence arguments in the main text rather than relying solely on continuity claims.
- Clarity issue: derivation of Equation (5) in the first-order EM section (lines 162–172) is hard to follow and needs a more detailed walkthrough.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—address Theorem 1’s proof gaps, clarify Eq. (5) derivation, and discuss or outline extensions to higher dimensions.
