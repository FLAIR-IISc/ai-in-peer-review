- Paper analyzes the population likelihood landscape for equally-weighted, spherical GMMs in an infinite-sample, well-specified setting.  
- Main contribution 1: Disproves Srebro (2007) by showing for M ≥ 3 there can be bad local maxima even with well-separated true components.  
- Main contribution 2: Proves EM and a first-order EM variant with random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability →1 exponentially fast in M.  
- Main contribution 3: Shows first-order EM converges to local maxima (not strict saddles), identifying poor local optima as the primary obstacle for that method.  
- Paper is well-written, technically sound, with clear and coherent progression from structure (Theorem 1) to algorithmic implications (Theorems 2–4).  
- The distinction that failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles (Theorem 4) is a timely and useful insight.  
- Primary weakness: limited originality and contextualization—results closely mirror well-known k-means behavior.  
- EM for spherical GMMs with fixed identity covariances is essentially a soft version of Lloyd’s k-means, so the failure modes are canonical.  
- Theorem 1’s construction (two nearby centers, one far) is a classic Lloyd’s failure case where two clusters are merged.  
- The exponential random-initialization failure (Theorems 2 & 3) echoes known k-means issues that motivated k-means++ and oversampling strategies.  
- Paper misses an opportunity to connect formally to extensive k-means literature, making contributions seem more novel than they are.  
- Suggested improvement: add a section discussing conceptual similarities to Lloyd’s algorithm and cite k-means initialization work (e.g., Arthur & Vassilvitskii 2007).  
- Suggested improvement: compare EM failure conditions under random initialization with success guarantees of alternatives (spectral methods, k-means++/oversampling).  
- Arguments for acceptance: formally resolves an open problem, is technically solid, and clarifies the role of local maxima for first-order EM.  
- Arguments against acceptance: findings are conceptually unsurprising given k-means parallels, and lack of contextual discussion weakens perceived originality and impact.
