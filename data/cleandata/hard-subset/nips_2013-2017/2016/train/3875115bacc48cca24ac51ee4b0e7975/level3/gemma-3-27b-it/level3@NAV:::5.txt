## Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The authors answer this question in the negative, constructing a class of counterexamples demonstrating that such bad local maxima *do* exist, even in well-separated, spherical Gaussian mixtures. They further demonstrate that the EM algorithm, with a common random initialization scheme, converges to these suboptimal points with exponentially high probability. This work builds on a rich history of research into GMM estimation, including identifiability studies (Teicher 1963, Allman et al. 2009), convergence rate analysis (Chen 1995, Ghosal & Van Der Vaart 2001), and algorithmic improvements to EM (Dasgupta & Schulman 2007, Balakrishnan et al. 2015). Recent work has also considered the impact of saddle points on optimization landscapes (Pascanu et al. 2014), which the authors specifically address, showing their method is not hampered by them.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. It tackles a long-standing open problem with a clear and well-defined negative answer. The technical results are significant, providing rigorous guarantees regarding the convergence of EM under random initialization, even in idealized settings. The paper is exceptionally well-written, balancing technical depth with intuitive explanations – a particular achievement given the complexity of the subject matter. The clear presentation of the main ideas and proofs contributes to the overall quality.  The identification of the underlying reason for EM’s failure – the existence of bad local maxima rather than saddle points – is a valuable insight. This is a complete piece of work that advances the state of the art in understanding non-convex optimization in the context of GMMs.

However, a key weakness lies in the lack of concrete values for the constants within the probability bounds presented in Theorem 2. Knowing these constants is crucial for determining the phase transition—when, for realistic values of *k* (number of components), the convergence to bad solutions becomes a significant issue. While the authors acknowledge this and state that such estimates are complex, providing simulations illustrating the phenomenon for different values of *k* would substantially strengthen the practical impact of the results. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a fundamental open question in the field.
*   Provides rigorous theoretical results with significant implications for GMM estimation.
*   Exceptionally clear and well-written manuscript.
*   Advances our understanding of non-convex optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of explicit constants in the probability bounds limits the ability to assess the practical significance of the results.

Despite the lack of quantified constants, the novelty and impact of this work are undeniable. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the issue of constant estimation or provide supporting simulations in a revised version. The theoretical contribution is compelling, and the paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS.
