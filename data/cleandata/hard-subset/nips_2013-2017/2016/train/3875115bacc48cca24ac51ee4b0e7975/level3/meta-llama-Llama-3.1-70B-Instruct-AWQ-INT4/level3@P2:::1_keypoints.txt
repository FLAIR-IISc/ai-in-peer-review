- Shows EM for GMMs (M ≥ 3) can fail to find the global maximum even with infinite data and well-separated components.  
- Main result 1: the population likelihood for M ≥ 3 can have arbitrarily bad local maxima, even for equally-weighted, well-separated spherical Gaussians (resolves Srebro 2007 negatively).  
- Main result 2: EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1−e^{−cM}, where the log-likelihood loss can be arbitrarily large.  
- Detailed analysis of first-order EM indicates it typically avoids strict saddles, implying bad local maxima (not saddles) are the primary convergence obstacle.  
- Proves first-order EM cannot converge to strict saddle points with probability one.  
- Paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow.  
- Technical presentation is sound and comprehensive.  
- Results have important practical implications, suggesting EM may be unsuitable in some settings.  
- Strength: comprehensive convergence analysis and clear presentation.  
- Weakness: results are unsurprising and follow expected lines.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical/comparative evaluation with other algorithms (e.g., Kumar–Kannan) and no contrast with Lloyd’s guarantees under large separation.  
- Weakness: needs more detailed discussion of practical implications and guidance.  
- Originality: new negative result on population likelihood local maxima for M ≥ 3 and detailed first-order EM analysis.  
- Recommendation: accept, but request comparisons to other algorithms and more detailed practical implications.
