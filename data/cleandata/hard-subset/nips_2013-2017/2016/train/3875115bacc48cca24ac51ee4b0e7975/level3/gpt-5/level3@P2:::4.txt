Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with equal mixing weights and identity covariance. It resolves a long-standing conjecture of Srebro (2007) by constructing, for M ≥ 3 components, well-separated spherical mixtures whose population log-likelihood has suboptimal local maxima that can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. Beyond existence, the paper shows algorithmic consequences: with a practical random initialization scheme (sampling initial centers i.i.d. from the mixture distribution), both EM and a first-order variant (gradient EM) converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{−Ω(M)}. Finally, it proves gradient EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, supporting the conclusion that failures are due to bad local maxima, not saddles.

These results connect to and contrast with several strands of prior work. They answer Srebro’s conjecture negatively for M ≥ 3, complement local convergence guarantees for EM under good initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and contrast with benign landscapes in other nonconvex problems where all local optima are near-global (Loh and Wainwright, 2013; Wang et al., 2014). On the algorithmic side, they highlight limitations of EM relative to recent clustering- or moment-based algorithms (Arora et al., 2005; Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Vempala and Wang, 2002; Hsu and Kakade, 2013; Moitra and Valiant, 2010). The strict-saddle non-convergence leverages modern dynamical-systems analyses of gradient methods (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016; Ge et al., 2015).

Main contributions and intuition
- Theorem 1 (structural): For any M ≥ 3, there exist 1D, well-separated, equal-weight spherical mixtures whose population log-likelihood has suboptimal local maxima with arbitrarily large suboptimality gap. The construction places two true means close and the third far away; a configuration that duplicates one “far” mean and collapses the two “near” means yields a local maximum. The proof isolates a neighborhood around this misassignment and shows the boundary has strictly lower likelihood, forcing a local maximizer inside that is not global.
- Theorem 2 (algorithmic, EM): With random initialization (drawing M initial centers i.i.d. from the mixture), EM converges to a suboptimal critical point with probability ≥ 1 − e^{−cM}, for some universal c > 0. The construction uses a recursive, hierarchical clustering of means into increasingly separated groups; unless the initial centers match counts correctly at each level, iterates get trapped within groups, making successful initialization exponentially unlikely.
- Theorem 3 (algorithmic, gradient EM): The same exponentially small success probability holds for gradient EM for step sizes s ∈ (0, 1). Since gradient EM equals gradient ascent on the population likelihood, this extends the negative message to first-order local search.
- Theorem 4 (landscape vs saddles): Gradient EM converges to a critical point, and the probability of converging to a strict saddle is zero. Hence, failures stem from bad local maxima, not saddles.

Evaluation
Quality: The results are technically solid and carefully motivated. Theorem 1’s construction and barrier argument are convincing and appear sound. The hierarchical construction underlying Theorem 2 is plausible and well explained; I did not verify every appendix detail, but the high-level probabilistic counting argument is compelling. The strict-saddle result leans on recent theory while avoiding strong smoothness assumptions via properties of gradient EM; conditions and scope are clearly stated (constant step size s ∈ (0, 1)).

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The population vs sample distinction, initialization model, and step-by-step intuition are easy to follow. The figures for M = 2 help; a complementary illustration for the M = 3 counterexample would strengthen intuition further. The notion of “well-separated” is stated; providing explicit constants where feasible would help.

Originality: High. It settles an open conjecture for M ≥ 3, introduces a clean geometric construction for bad local maxima, and the hierarchical grouping idea that yields exponentially small success probability is clever. The strict-saddle convergence statement in this setting is a useful addition.

Significance: High. The results shape our understanding of the likelihood landscape for GMMs and the limitations of EM with naive initialization, even in favorable regimes (equal weights, isotropic, well separated). They have implications for how practitioners seed EM and for theoretical work on nonconvex landscapes and clustering.

Pros
- Resolves a prominent conjecture; crisp negative result for population likelihoods.
- Clear algorithmic consequences with strong quantitative bounds (e^{−Ω(M)} success).
- Bridges to modern nonconvex optimization theory by separating saddles from bad local maxima.
- Clean setting (equal weights, spherical, population) isolates the phenomenon.

Cons and suggestions
- Dimensionality: Most constructions and statements emphasize d = 1. It would help to clarify which lemmas and barriers extend to d > 1, and whether similar exponentially small success probabilities hold in higher dimensions.
- Model assumptions: Equal weights and identity covariances are restrictive. A discussion (or brief lemma) indicating which arguments extend to mild weight/covariance perturbations would broaden impact.
- Finite-sample implications: The conclusion mentions straightforward extensions by empirical process arguments. Providing an explicit sample complexity statement (uniform convergence bounds and how they propagate through the barrier argument) would strengthen the practical message.
- Initialization model: Since practitioners often use k-means++ or moment-based initializers, it would be valuable to discuss whether such schemes circumvent the exponential failure probability on your hard instances.
- Theorem 4 conditions: Please state the minimal regularity and step-size conditions ensuring that the gradient map is a local diffeomorphism, and delineate whether non-isolated critical sets arise in your constructions.
- Minor: Reference formatting has several inconsistencies; please clean up authors/years and venues.

Recommendation
Overall, this is a strong and timely theoretical contribution with clear impact on our understanding of EM for GMMs. I recommend acceptance.

Reviewer confidence: High on the correctness and significance of Theorem 1 and the qualitative conclusions; medium on full verification of the hierarchical counting details in Theorem 2’s appendix.
