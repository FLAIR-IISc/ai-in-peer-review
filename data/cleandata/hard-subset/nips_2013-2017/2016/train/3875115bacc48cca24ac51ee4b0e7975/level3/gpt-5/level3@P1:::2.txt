Summary and relation to prior work
The paper analyzes the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) in the favorable setting of known number of components, equal weights, and spherical covariances. It resolves Srebro’s (2007) conjecture by proving the existence of bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 even under strong separation; moreover, the value at these local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. It then shows that EM with random initialization converges to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-Ω(M)}; the same exponential-failure phenomenon holds for first-order EM (gradient EM). Finally, building on gradient-dynamical systems results (e.g., Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016), the paper proves that first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, suggesting that bad local maxima—not saddle points—explain failure.

This work complements and contrasts with prior analyses: Dasgupta and Schulman (2007) and Balakrishnan et al. (2015) provide positive, local convergence guarantees for EM-type methods given good initializations; Moitra and Valiant (2010) and Hsu and Kakade (2013) give method-of-moments alternatives with provable guarantees under separation. The results here show that, despite favorable modeling assumptions, the global landscape can be treacherous, and random restarts do not reliably overcome it, sharpening the negative landscape results hinted at by finite-sample pathologies (Améndola et al., 2015) and contrasting with benign nonconvexity phenomena in other problems (e.g., Loh and Wainwright, 2013).

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and importance: The paper squarely addresses a long-standing conjecture and provides a clean, population-level counterexample, which is highly relevant for the practical use of EM.
- Technical contributions: The construction for arbitrarily bad local maxima and the hierarchical instance yielding e^{-Ω(M)} success probability for random initialization are insightful and well-motivated.
- Algorithmic insight: The “no strict saddle convergence” result for first-order EM distinguishes failure modes and helps reconcile observations about saddle-point proliferation (Pascanu et al., 2014) with practical failures in GMMs.
- Scope and impact: Results remain meaningful in large-sample regimes and highlight the necessity of careful initialization, potentially steering practice toward pilot estimators or moment-based initializations.

Weaknesses and required clarifications
- Major: The proof of Theorem 1 in the main text (lines 288–306) is too terse. The argument appears to rely on limits of suprema/optima over parameter-dependent or unbounded domains. Continuity alone does not guarantee the claimed convergences or the existence of maximizers; compactness/tightness or uniform convergence is needed. Please:
  - Explicitly define the neighborhood over which you argue, prove it is closed and bounded (or otherwise ensure the objective attains a maximum via Weierstrass), and show that the boundary objective value is strictly smaller than the interior reference configuration.
  - If taking limits where feasible sets depend on parameters, justify interchange of limits and suprema (e.g., via epi-convergence, uniform convergence under a dominated envelope, or a direct bounding argument). As written, key steps could be misinterpreted as pointwise continuity implying convergence of maxima, which is not generally valid.
  - If the proof depends on separation growing to infinity, isolate the quantitative inequalities (e.g., bounds on cross-responsibilities) and state the uniformity with respect to parameters in the neighborhood.
- Scope vs. dimension: Although the problem is phrased for general d, the constructions and proofs are in d = 1. Please state clearly whether the results extend to d > 1 and what additional difficulties arise (e.g., geometric placement, coupling between coordinates). If a lift to higher dimensions is straightforward (by embedding the 1D construction along one axis), say so and state the formal argument.
- Gradient EM exposition: The section around lines 162–172 is hard to follow. Please expand the derivation connecting the Q-function to the population updates:
  - Show how Equation (5) follows from maximizing Q(θ′|θ) for standard EM in the population setting.
  - For first-order EM, derive the gradient ∇θ′Q(θ′|θ)|θ′=θ and explain the equivalence to gradient ascent on L, citing and briefly summarizing Balakrishnan et al. (2015).
- Minor: Discuss the dependence of the step size restriction s ∈ (0,1) in Theorem 3 on M, separation, and d (if any), and whether larger steps could change the avoidance of saddles or attraction to bad local maxima.

Quality
- Technically sound overall and addresses an important open question. However, the main proof needs more rigorous justification in the main text to be fully convincing without relying solely on the supplement.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, with helpful intuition for the constructions. The main text would benefit from:
  - A more complete proof sketch of Theorem 1 (see above).
  - A clearer, self-contained derivation for gradient EM and its relation to ascent on L.
  - A brief schematic figure for the recursive two-cluster construction underlying Theorem 2 to convey why success probability is e^{-Ω(M)}.

Originality
- High. The negative structural results at the population level for well-separated, equal-weight, spherical GMMs, and the exponential failure rate of random restarts, are new and settle a notable conjecture.

Significance
- High. The results recalibrate expectations about EM’s behavior even in idealized regimes and motivate research on initialization and alternative objectives for mixtures.

Suggestions for improvement
- Move a strengthened proof of Theorem 1 into the main paper, even at the expense of shortening discussion.
- Add a concise lemma bounding responsibilities under large separation that is reused across results.
- Discuss robustness: do the constructions and negative results persist under slight misspecification (e.g., mild weight asymmetry or variance differences)?
- Clarify how the population results transfer to finite samples (assumptions on n vs. M, d, separation).

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves an open question with clean, general constructions.
- Provides sharp, practically relevant algorithmic consequences.
- Conceptually impactful and likely to influence subsequent work on EM and nonconvex learning.

Arguments against acceptance
- The main proof is currently too terse; key technical steps need to be made explicit.
- Results are presented in d = 1; higher-dimensional implications should be clearly stated.

Recommendation
Accept, contingent on strengthening the main proof of Theorem 1 and improving the gradient EM exposition. The contributions are timely, insightful, and significant for the theory and practice of mixture model estimation.
