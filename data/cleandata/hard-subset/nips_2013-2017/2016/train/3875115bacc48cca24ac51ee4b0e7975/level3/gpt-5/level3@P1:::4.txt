This paper studies the population log-likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) in a favorable regime: equal mixing weights and identity covariances. It resolves a conjecture of Srebro (2007) by showing that, contrary to optimistic folklore, even the infinite-sample likelihood can have bad local maxima when M ≥ 3. The authors construct one-dimensional, well-separated mixtures with k = 3 components in which suboptimal local maxima have likelihood arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. They then translate this structural insight into algorithmic consequences: with standard random initialization (sampling initial means from the mixture), both EM and its first-order variant converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-Ω(M)} on carefully designed instances. Finally, using recent results on gradient dynamics, they show that first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, attributing failure to poor local maxima rather than saddles. The work complements prior analyses of EM’s local convergence around good optima (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and goes beyond earlier finite-sample pathologies (Améndola et al., 2015) by focusing on the population limit. It also connects to algorithmic alternatives based on clustering or moments (Arora et al., 2005; Vempala and Wang, 2002; Hsu and Kakade, 2013; Moitra and Valiant, 2010), clarifying when likelihood-based methods need careful seeding.

Technical contributions and intuition:
- Structural negative result (Theorem 1): For any M ≥ 3, there exist 1D, well-separated, equal-weight spherical mixtures with a suboptimal local maximum whose likelihood gap from the global optimum can be made arbitrarily large. The construction places two true centers relatively close and the third far away; a configuration with two candidate means collapsed on the far mode and one at the midpoint of the close pair yields a local optimum that is not globally optimal.
- Algorithmic failure with random initialization (Theorems 2 and 3): The paper designs a recursive, hierarchical arrangement of true means (two far-apart clusters, each further decomposed into two mini-clusters, recursively for Ω(log M) levels). With initial means drawn from the mixture, EM and first-order EM get trapped in wrong cluster allocations with exponentially high probability in M, implying that repeated random restarts need e^{Ω(M)} trials to succeed.
- Characterizing limit points (Theorem 4): First-order EM with constant step size s ∈ (0, 1) almost surely avoids strict saddle points; thus, the observed failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles, aligning with the “strict saddle” viewpoint (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016).

Quality:
- Theorem 1’s construction is crisp and persuasive. The proof strategy—exhibiting a neighborhood around a suboptimal assignment whose boundary has lower likelihood—seems sound.
- The hierarchical instance for Theorem 2 convincingly drives the probability bound to e^{-Ω(M)}; while I did not verify all appendix details, the high-level probability calculation and trapping argument are coherent.
- Theorem 4 is carefully stated for gradient EM with step size constraints and leverages modern dynamical systems insights appropriately. The definition of strict saddles is correctly adapted to maximization.
- Assumptions are explicit (population setting, equal weights, identity covariance, well-separated means), and the paper argues that finite-sample analogs follow by standard concentration.

Clarity:
- The presentation is clear, with good intuition and a clean setup of EM, first-order EM, and random initialization. Figures for the M = 2 case and schematic diagrams for the M = 3 construction help.
- Suggestions: (i) quantify the dependence of the “well-separated” parameter on the likelihood gap C_gap in Theorem 1; (ii) visually illustrate the multi-level hierarchical construction and basin trapping; (iii) state explicitly the smoothness needed (if any) for Theorem 4 and clarify where the proof departs from generic stable manifold arguments.

Originality:
- Resolving Srebro’s conjecture negatively at the population level for M ≥ 3 is novel and important. The recursive, hierarchical instance for exponential failure probability under random initialization is clever and, to my knowledge, new in this context. The connection to strict-saddle avoidance for first-order EM gives a sharper landscape interpretation than prior EM analyses.

Significance:
- The results sharpen our understanding of likelihood landscapes for mixtures and set clear limits on what random initialization plus local search can achieve, even in benign regimes. They motivate principled seeding (e.g., moment-based pilot estimators, clustering-based seeds, or k-means++-style heuristics) and could guide future analyses of basin geometry and initialization design.
- While the constructions are worst-case and population-level, the message is practically relevant: robust seeding matters. Empirical demonstrations on finite samples would further broaden impact.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves a well-known open question; strong, clean theoretical results.
- Introduces insightful constructions illuminating the optimization landscape.
- Clarifies the role of saddles vs. local maxima for EM-type methods.

Arguments against acceptance:
- All results are at the population level; finite-sample experiments validating the predicted failure probabilities would strengthen the paper.
- Main hard counterexample is 1D; discussion of extensions to higher dimensions (beyond definitional remarks about separation) could be expanded.
- The exponential failure result depends on carefully crafted instances; practical prevalence could be discussed more.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide finite-sample simulations illustrating (a) the bad local maximum for M = 3 and (b) the scaling of success probability with M under random initialization.
- Discuss whether the construction and Lemma-level arguments extend to d > 1 and how separation scales with d.
- Compare against alternative initialization schemes (moments-based, k-means++ seeding, spectral) to illustrate that careful seeding mitigates failures.
- Clarify step-size requirements and robustness for first-order EM; note whether adaptive step sizes change the conclusions.
- Include a brief discussion of the M = 2 case and any partial progress toward that open problem.

Overall evaluation: Strong theoretical paper with clear negative results and meaningful algorithmic implications. I recommend acceptance.
