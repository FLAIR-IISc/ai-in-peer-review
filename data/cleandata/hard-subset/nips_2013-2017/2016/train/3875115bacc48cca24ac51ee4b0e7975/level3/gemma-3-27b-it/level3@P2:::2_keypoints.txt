- Main contribution: Proves existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood of GMMs and shows EM (including first-order) often converges to these suboptimal points from random initialization.
- Significance: Resolves a longstanding open question (Srebro 2007) and highlights fundamental challenges in GMM estimation even for well-separated components.
- Theoretical rigor: Provides mathematically sound proofs and a strong theoretical foundation for the claims.
- First-order EM analysis: Theorem 4 shows issues arise from local optima rather than saddles, reinforcing the generality of the problem.
- Context and novelty: Builds on prior work (Teicher, Genovese & Wasserman, Dasgupta & Schulman) and delivers novel insight that counters prior intuition about well-separated mixtures.
- Clarity of exposition: Introduction and motivation are clear and the paper is generally well-written.
- Dimensionality limitation: All proofs are given for d = 1 despite claims for general d; authors should state if extension to higher dimensions is possible or explain added difficulties.
- Theorem 1 justification: Proof (lines 288–306) is compressed; the convergence arguments over unbounded/parameter-dependent domains need more rigorous justification beyond continuity.
- First-order EM derivation clarity: Explanation around lines 162–172 and derivation of Equation (5) is hard to follow and needs a more detailed derivation.
- Quality assessment: Technically sound and complete within its scope but with some presentation and justification gaps.
- Originality and significance: Novel results that materially advance understanding of EM’s sensitivity to initialization with practical implications.
- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses an important open problem with rigorous results and clear presentation overall.
- Arguments against acceptance: Dimensionality restriction and insufficient proof detail in Theorem 1 raise concerns about broader applicability and completeness.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address the dimensionality issue, strengthen Theorem 1’s convergence arguments, and clarify the first-order EM derivation.
