Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the behavior of EM and gradient EM for estimating the means of Gaussian mixture models when the number of components M, the mixture weights, and the covariances are known/fixed. Focusing on the population (infinite-sample) likelihood of equally weighted, spherical (identity covariance) mixtures, the authors resolve a conjecture of Srebro (2007) by exhibiting bad local maxima even for well-separated mixtures, and they show that both EM and a first-order (gradient) EM algorithm, when randomly initialized, converge with high probability to suboptimal critical points whose likelihood can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. They further establish that gradient EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, so the failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.

The work connects to a large literature on EM and mixtures, including early theory on identifiability and estimation (Teicher; Chen; Ghosal & van der Vaart), algorithmic alternatives via clustering and method-of-moments (Arora et al.; Vempala & Wang; Hsu & Kakade; Moitra & Valiant), and recent analyses of local convergence of EM under good initialization (Balakrishnan et al., 2015). The avoidance of strict saddles builds on modern gradient dynamics results (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas & Piliouras, 2016) and contrasts with viewpoints emphasizing saddle points (Pascanu et al., 2014).

Contributions
- Structural: For M ≥ 3, the population log-likelihood of a well-separated, equally weighted spherical GMM can have local maxima whose value is arbitrarily worse than the global maximum.
- Algorithmic: With random initialization (sampling initial means from the mixture), both EM and gradient EM converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). Moreover, gradient EM does not converge to strict saddles almost surely.
- Implication: To succeed from random initialization in this setting, one needs exponentially many restarts, emphasizing the necessity of careful initialization even under very favorable modeling assumptions.

Strengths
- Resolves a natural and long-standing question (Srebro, 2007) with clear, negative results of broad interest to practitioners who rely on EM.
- The construction and recursive argument that drive the e−Ω(M) failure probability are conceptually clean and informative.
- The clarification that gradient EM’s failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles is valuable and timely given current nonconvex optimization theory.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope: The analysis is limited to known M, uniform weights, and identity covariances, and the main structural proof is carried out in one dimension. While I find these simplifications acceptable for a first step given the technical difficulty, the paper should be clearer about which parts extend to d > 1 and which do not.
- Major concern with Theorem 1 proof (lines 288–306): Several steps involve limits of suprema or maxima over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains. Continuity alone is insufficient to justify the asserted convergences. The argument needs uniformity/compactness or explicit control (e.g., uniform continuity on compact sets plus tight control of tails; epi-convergence; or an explicit coercivity/compactness lemma) to justify taking limits while keeping the gap. As written, the sketch is too terse to let readers verify these delicate steps.
- Clarity: The gradient EM section (lines 162–172) is hard to follow; more detail is needed to explain how the stated update (Equation (5) in that section) is obtained from the Q-function/gradient connection.

Quality
The results are important and, overall, appear technically sound, but the main body’s proof of Theorem 1 omits crucial details precisely where uniformity and limit interchanges matter. I did not read the supplementary material; however, given the centrality of Theorem 1, the paper should include a self-contained, rigorous argument (or at least the key lemmas) in the main text. The algorithmic results are convincing and nicely tied to the structural picture.

Clarity
The exposition is generally well-organized and readable. Two areas need improvement: (i) The proof of Theorem 1 should be expanded with explicit lemmas justifying the use of limits over boundary/supremal sets (see detailed request below). (ii) The gradient EM derivation should be spelled out: start from Q(θ′|θ), take ∇θ′Q, evaluate at θ′=θ, and show explicitly how the mixture responsibilities produce the update in Equation (5). If space is tight, shorten the broader discussion in Section 3 to make room for these essentials.

Originality
High. The negative structural result and the sharp e−Ω(M) failure probability for random initialization significantly advance our understanding of EM/gradient EM for mixtures. The strict-saddle avoidance in this context clarifies the failure mode.

Significance
High. The results directly inform how practitioners should use EM (initialization matters even in benign cases) and guide theory by showing limits of local search in mixture estimation.

Requests for the authors
- Dimensionality: The paper is written for general d, but the core construction and proofs are for d = 1. Please state clearly whether the construction extends to d > 1 (e.g., by embedding the 1D instance along a coordinate axis with identity covariances) and whether any new issues arise (such as manifolds of equivalent critical points or degeneracies). If extension is nontrivial, explain the obstacles.
- Theorem 1 details (lines 288–306): Provide rigorous justifications for the limit/supremum arguments. Concretely:
  • Specify compact neighborhoods and prove uniform continuity bounds for L on those sets.
  • Control tails to ensure boundary likelihood is uniformly below the suboptimal configuration by a fixed margin, independent of the limiting parameter.
  • When domains depend on parameters (e.g., radii → ∞ or shrinking neighborhoods), justify exchanging lim and sup via standard theorems or self-contained lemmas.
  • If appropriate, cite and apply epi-convergence or continuity-of-argmax results.
  Even at the expense of some discussion, please lengthen this proof in the main paper so readers can be fully convinced without consulting the supplement.
- Gradient EM derivation: Expand lines 162–172 to explicitly derive Equation (5), connecting Q(θ′|θ) to the gradient ascent step, and clarifying the relation to ascent on the population likelihood.
- Clarify the necessity of M ≥ 3: Is your evidence purely constructive, or can you argue that M = 2 is genuinely different? A brief discussion would be helpful.

Pros for acceptance
- Resolves an open problem with clear, elegant constructions.
- Strong algorithmic consequences with practical relevance.
- Good alignment with NIPS interest in nonconvex optimization and learning theory.

Cons for acceptance
- Key proof details are missing from the main text; Theorem 1’s argument needs strengthening.
- Gradient EM derivation is terse and may confuse readers.

Overall recommendation
Accept if the authors substantially strengthen the main proof of Theorem 1 and clarify the gradient EM derivation. The simplified model assumptions are reasonable here, but the paper should explicitly discuss extension to d > 1 or the obstacles thereto. The results are interesting, informative, and likely to influence both theory and practice around EM.
