## Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper investigates the challenging problem of local maxima in the likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The authors demonstrate the existence of “bad” local maxima – those with arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global optimum – even for well-separated mixtures with known covariance and equal weights. They further prove that the EM algorithm, and its first-order variant, converge to these suboptimal points with high probability when initialized randomly. This work builds upon the earlier conjecture by Srebro [2007] and addresses a fundamental question regarding the reliability of EM in practice. The problem is both theoretically interesting and practically relevant, as EM is a widely used algorithm for GMM estimation.

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear identification of a key limitation of EM – its susceptibility to poor local optima even in a simplified, favorable setting. The negative results are strong and rigorously proven, contributing valuable insight into the behavior of the likelihood surface. The analysis of both EM and gradient EM, and the demonstration that the latter's failures aren’t merely due to saddle points, are particularly noteworthy. The paper is well-motivated, placing the work within the broader context of GMM estimation and the challenges of non-convex optimization.

However, the paper does have weaknesses. The restriction to d=1 in the proofs is a significant limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, a more thorough discussion of the difficulties extending the results to higher dimensions would be beneficial. More critically, the proof of Theorem 1 (lines 288-306) feels compressed and lacks sufficient justification. The claims regarding limits of supremums and maximums over unbounded/parameter-dependent domains require significantly more detail. The continuity argument alone does not convincingly establish the claimed convergences. I did not examine the supplementary proofs, but strongly suggest expanding the core proof even at the cost of brevity in other sections, to fully convince the reader. Finally, the description of the gradient EM section (lines 162-172) is somewhat difficult to follow. Equation (5) is introduced without sufficient explanation of its derivation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a fundamental and important question about EM's performance.
*   Presents rigorous and informative negative results.
*   Provides valuable insight into the likelihood surface of GMMs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited to d=1 dimension in proofs.
*   Proof of Theorem 1 lacks sufficient detail and justification.
*   Gradient EM section is unclear.



**Overall:** Despite the noted weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a worthwhile contribution. The results are significant and add to our understanding of the behavior of EM. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding the clarity and rigor of the proof of Theorem 1, and providing more explanation for the gradient EM section. A discussion on the potential difficulties in extending the results to d > 1 would also strengthen the paper considerably.
