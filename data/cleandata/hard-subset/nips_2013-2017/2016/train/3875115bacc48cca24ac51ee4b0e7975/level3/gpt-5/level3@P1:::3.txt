This paper resolves an open question of Srebro (COLT’07) by proving that the infinite-sample (population) log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 equally weighted, spherical components admits bad local maxima, even under strong separation. The authors quantify that such local maxima can have arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global optimum. They further show that EM and first-order EM (gradient EM) with random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-Ω(M)}, and that first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, implicating local maxima rather than saddles as the algorithmic culprit. These results are established in an idealized, well-specified, known covariance setting and rely on explicit one-dimensional constructions and a recursive cluster-within-cluster arrangement to drive the exponentially small success probability. The work connects to a long line of EM analyses (Dasgupta & Schulman’07; Balakrishnan et al.’15) and algorithmic alternatives (e.g., spectral/moment methods by Moitra & Valiant’10; Hsu & Kakade’13), and complements robustness motivations for maximum likelihood (Donoho & Liu’88).

Strengths
- Clear and important theoretical contribution: settling a conjecture by exhibiting arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood for well-separated, equal-weight, isotropic GMMs with M ≥ 3.
- Algorithmic consequences are sharp: probability of success of EM/gradient EM with standard random initialization decays exponentially in M, highlighting the computational burden of multi-restarts.
- Conceptual clarity: the gradient EM non-convergence to strict saddles rules out a popular hypothesis (saddles as main obstacle) in this canonical model, focusing attention on local maxima.
- Techniques (geometric construction, hierarchical clustering of centers, and careful boundary arguments) are likely to generalize to other nonconvex likelihoods and aid future analyses.
- Writing is generally good, the setting is crisp, and the assumptions are explicit.

Weaknesses / concerns
- Practical relevance of the constructions: The worst-case center configurations appear contrived. It is unclear how likely such pathologies are under natural generative models for the true centers. Theorem 2 shows failure probability over initialization conditional on a fixed hard instance, but does not quantify how often such instances arise if centers themselves are random.
- Initialization model: Uniformly sampling initial centers i.i.d. from the mixture distribution is common in theory, but many practical pipelines use more informative heuristics (e.g., k-means++, seeding by cluster summaries), especially when clusters are well-separated. The results do not address such schemes.
- Two-component case: The discussion around M = 2 (L74–81) is inconclusive and could be clearer about what is known vs. conjectured; the example does not substantiate the conjecture and may distract.
- Some assumptions and statements require clarification (e.g., “well-separated constant,” big-Omega usage with M), and there are a few typos.

Quality
- The technical development appears sound. The 1D constructions and the neighborhood/ boundary likelihood comparison for Theorem 1 are convincing. The recursive clustering argument driving e^{-Ω(M)} success probability in Theorem 2 is elegant. The first-order EM strict-saddle avoidance aligns with modern dynamical systems analyses; the paper usefully avoids global smoothness assumptions via algorithm-specific properties.
- One request: make the finite-sample carryover explicit by giving a simple uniform convergence bound and an n scaling sufficient to reflect the population phenomena with high probability.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable. The high-level intuition for each theorem is appreciated. However, several phrasings and notational choices could be tightened (see detailed comments).
- It would help to visually illustrate the 3-component bad local maximum (beyond the 2-component figure) and to schematize the recursive instance used in Theorem 2.

Originality
- High. The negative structural result at the population level for well-separated GMMs with M ≥ 3 is new and settles a long-standing conjecture. The exponential failure probability for random initialization in this setting is also novel.

Significance
- High theoretical significance for our understanding of likelihood landscapes and EM. It cautions against over-reliance on random restarts and motivates principled initializers (e.g., moments/spectral). Practical significance would be strengthened by analysis under distributions over centers or by examining more realistic initializations.

Suggestions to strengthen the paper
- Practical relevance: Assume a distribution on true centers (e.g., i.i.d. from a mixture of two well-separated meta-Gaussians with random within-cluster spreads) and show that the probability of generating a “bad” configuration increases with M (or with dimension d) or at least does not vanish. This would complement Theorem 2 and address the “how often do these instances arise?” question.
- Initialization: Analyze k-means++ or a simple two-stage initializer (coarse clustering or moment-based pilot followed by EM), to delineate which initializations avoid the exponential failure and under what separations.
- Clarify requirement (1) in L233–235: Why must all initialized centers fall within exactly one top-level cluster to be “favorable”? Would a center initialized between clusters migrate to the nearest true cluster under EM or gradient EM? A short lemma or counterexample would help.
- Two-component discussion (L74–81): State precisely that the M = 2 case remains open; the figure illustrates intuition but is not evidence. Consider moving it to an appendix or reframing as context.
- Finite-sample implications: Provide a corollary with an explicit n so that the sample likelihood inherits the bad local maxima and EM failures with high probability.

Line-level and minor comments

- L38: use plural “estimates”.
- L60: “under which conditions” instead of “when”.
- L138: clarify separation notation; earlier you define well-separated as Ω(√d). Be consistent about rates.
- L172: reword to avoid redundancy.
- L177: “equivalent to” (not “is equivalent to” twice).
- L218: “well-separated constant” is unclear; specify a numerical constant c > 0 or use “a fixed separation parameter”.
- L220: remove duplicate “the”.
- L225: clarify Ω(M) in e^{-Ω(M)} (i.e., constants c, C such that ≤ e^{-cM}).
- L233–235: clarify necessity of requirement (1) and discuss behavior for a center initialized between clusters.
- L245: “coincidence” seems off; consider “coincides”.
- L249: add “that”.
- L267–273: discuss practical value of distinguishing saddles from local maxima; e.g., does strict-saddle avoidance suggest any algorithmic remedy?
- L308: add “that”.

Pros
- Resolves an open problem about population likelihood of GMMs for M ≥ 3.
- Sharp negative algorithmic consequences for random initialization of EM/gradient EM.
- Clean theoretical insights and techniques likely useful beyond this setting.

Cons
- Practical frequency of hard instances not quantified; results conditional on adversarial center configurations.
- Initialization model less aligned with common practice; missing analysis of more informative initializers.
- Some exposition and notation could be clearer; minor typos.

Recommendation
- Accept (or strong accept for theory). The paper delivers a definitive and impactful theoretical message about nonconvex likelihood landscapes and EM, and it will influence how the community interprets empirical successes and designs initialization schemes. The suggested clarifications and extensions would further strengthen the contribution but are not prerequisites for acceptance.
