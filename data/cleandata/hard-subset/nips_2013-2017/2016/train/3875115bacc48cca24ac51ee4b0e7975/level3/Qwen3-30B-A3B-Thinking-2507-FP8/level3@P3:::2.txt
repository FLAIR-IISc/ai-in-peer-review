

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental question about the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically whether bad local maxima can exist in the population likelihood function. The authors resolve an open question posed by Srebro (2007), who conjectured that for well-separated, equally-weighted spherical Gaussians, the population likelihood function would have no spurious local maxima. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) it constructs a counterexample showing that for M ≥ 3 components, bad local maxima with arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global optimum can exist; (2) it proves that random initialization of EM (and gradient EM) converges to such suboptimal critical points with high probability; and (3) it demonstrates that the failure of first-order EM is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper builds on previous work in the area. Dasgupta and Schulman (2007) and Balakrishnan et al. (2015) showed that for two-component mixtures, EM can converge to global optima with reasonable initialization. The paper also relates to recent results on non-convex optimization (e.g., Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016) that show gradient methods can avoid strict saddle points, but this paper demonstrates that for GMMs, the main challenge is not saddle points but bad local maxima.

**Strengths:**

The paper's main strength is its clear demonstration that even in the most favorable settings for GMMs (known number of components, uniform weights, spherical covariances, well-separated components), the EM algorithm can fail to find global maxima due to the existence of bad local maxima. This is a significant theoretical contribution with practical implications, as it explains why careful initialization is necessary in practice even when data perfectly follows the generative model. The results are technically sound and provide important insights into the limitations of local search methods for non-convex optimization in statistics.

The simplifications made (uniform weights, identity covariances, fixed number of components) are well-justified, as the authors argue these represent a "best-case" scenario where one might expect good behavior from EM. The paper's focus on the population setting (infinite samples) is appropriate for understanding fundamental limitations of the algorithm, with the authors noting that results extend to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments.

**Weaknesses:**

The major weakness is the insufficient justification of Theorem 1, which is central to the paper's main claim. The proof (lines 288-306) claims convergence of certain supremums over unbounded domains, but merely invoking continuity does not suffice. The authors need to provide a more detailed argument for why the limits of these supremums/maximums converge as claimed. The reviewer suggests the authors should either provide a more rigorous proof or explicitly state that this step requires additional justification.

Additionally, the gradient EM section (lines 162-172) is difficult to follow. The derivation of Equation (5) is not sufficiently explained, making it challenging to verify the correctness of the first-order EM updates. The authors should clarify how the gradient updates are derived from the population likelihood function.

The paper also does not address whether the results extend to dimensions d > 1. While the authors provide proofs for d = 1, they should discuss whether the constructions can be generalized to higher dimensions or whether additional difficulties arise. This is particularly important as the paper is written for general dimension d.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental question about the behavior of EM for GMMs. The results are important for understanding the limitations of local search methods in non-convex optimization, with clear implications for practitioners who use EM in real-world applications. The technical results are sound, and the paper is well-motivated by the open question posed by Srebro (2007).

The paper is of high quality, with strong technical content and significant theoretical contribution. The main weaknesses (incomplete proof of Theorem 1 and unclear gradient EM derivation) are fixable with minor revisions. The authors should address these points to strengthen the paper's technical rigor.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses a fundamental question in the theory of EM algorithms with important practical implications. The results are novel and significant, resolving an open problem that has been discussed in the literature since Srebro (2007). The minor weaknesses identified are technical in nature and can be addressed with additional explanation in the main paper.

The paper's significance is high, as it provides a theoretical foundation for understanding why careful initialization is necessary in practice for EM, even in seemingly ideal settings. This work will likely influence future algorithm design for GMMs and non-convex optimization more broadly.
