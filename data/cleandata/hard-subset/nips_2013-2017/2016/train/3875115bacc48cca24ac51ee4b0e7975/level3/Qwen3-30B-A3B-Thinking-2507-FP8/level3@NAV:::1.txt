

This paper addresses a fundamental question about the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs): whether EM can converge to global maxima even with infinite data. The authors provide a negative answer to Srebro's (2007) conjecture by demonstrating that the population likelihood function for mixtures of M ≥ 3 well-separated spherical Gaussians can have arbitrarily bad local maxima. They further show that EM with random initialization converges to these suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 - exp(-Ω(M)), while gradient variants avoid strict saddle points, indicating that bad local maxima—not saddles—are the primary obstacle.

The paper's strengths include a rigorous theoretical foundation for the existence of bad local maxima, clear construction of the key 3-component counterexample (two close centers, one far away), and insightful connection to known failures of k-means algorithms. The results are well-motivated and address an important open question in the field, providing practical guidance for EM implementation.

However, the results and techniques are largely unsurprising in the context of non-convex optimization literature, as the existence of bad local maxima for non-convex problems is well-established. The paper would benefit from a more substantial comparison with Kumar-Kannan's work on GMMs and a detailed contrast with k-means guarantees under large separation conditions, as suggested in the key points. While the findings are technically sound (Quality), the novelty is limited relative to existing knowledge about non-convex optimization challenges.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized (Clarity), but the originality (Originality) is modest, as the core insights follow expected lines of reasoning for non-convex problems. The significance (Significance) lies in providing concrete theoretical justification for the empirical observation that careful initialization is necessary for EM in practice, even under ideal conditions.

Arguments for acceptance: Provides a rigorous negative answer to a long-standing conjecture, offers practical insights for algorithm implementation, and establishes a connection to k-means failures.

Arguments against acceptance: Results follow expected theoretical lines without substantial novelty, and the key example mirrors known k-means failures.

Overall, the paper makes a solid contribution to understanding EM's limitations, but the novelty is limited for an NIPS audience.
