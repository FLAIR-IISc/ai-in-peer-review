This paper provides a comprehensive analysis of the convergence of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) with multiple components. The authors present a set of results that demonstrate the EM algorithm may not converge to the global maximum of the likelihood function, even in the idealized setting of infinite samples and well-separated components.

The paper begins by introducing the GMM model and the EM algorithm, and then presents two main results. The first result shows that the population likelihood function of a GMM with M ≥ 3 components can have arbitrarily bad local maxima, even in the special case of equally-weighted mixtures of well-separated and spherical Gaussians. This result resolves an open question of Srebro [2007] and provides a negative answer to the conjecture that any local maximum of the likelihood function is a global maximum in the limit of infinite samples.

The second main result shows that the EM algorithm, as well as a variant thereof known as the first-order EM algorithm, with random initialization, converges to a bad critical point with an exponentially high probability. Specifically, the authors show that for a universal constant c > 0, with probability at least 1− e−cM, the EM and first-order EM algorithms converge to a suboptimal critical point, whose log-likelihood could be arbitrarily worse than that of the global maximum.

The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the first-order EM algorithm and show that it typically avoids strict saddles, suggesting that bad local maxima (not saddles) are the main obstacle to convergence. They also present a result that shows that the first-order EM algorithm cannot converge to strict saddle points with probability one.

The paper is well-written and provides a clear and concise presentation of the results. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the EM algorithm and its variants, and their results have important implications for the use of these algorithms in practice. The paper also provides a good discussion of the related work and the limitations of the results.
