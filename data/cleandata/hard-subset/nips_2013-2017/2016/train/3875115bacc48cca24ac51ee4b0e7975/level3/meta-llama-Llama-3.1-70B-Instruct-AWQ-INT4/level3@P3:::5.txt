This paper addresses a fundamental question about the likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), resolving an open problem posed by Srebro [2007]. The authors provide a negative answer to the question of whether any local maximum of the likelihood function is a global maximum in the limit of infinite samples. They construct a counterexample, a well-separated mixture of three spherical unit variance Gaussians, whose population log-likelihood function has a bad local maximum. This result has significant implications for the use of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and its variants in estimating GMMs.

The paper is well-written and clear, with a good balance between technical details and intuitive explanations. The authors provide a thorough background on GMMs, the EM algorithm, and its first-order variant, making the paper accessible to a broad audience. The main results are presented in a logical and easy-to-follow manner, with a clear explanation of the implications of the theorems.

The paper's main contributions are two-fold. First, the authors provide a negative answer to Srebro's question, showing that the population log-likelihood function of a GMM can have bad local maxima. This result is significant because it implies that any local search algorithm, including the EM algorithm, cannot guarantee global convergence to the optimal solution. Second, the authors show that the EM algorithm and its first-order variant, with random initialization, converge to a bad critical point with an exponentially high probability. This result has important implications for the use of these algorithms in practice, suggesting that careful initialization is required to ensure convergence to a good solution.
