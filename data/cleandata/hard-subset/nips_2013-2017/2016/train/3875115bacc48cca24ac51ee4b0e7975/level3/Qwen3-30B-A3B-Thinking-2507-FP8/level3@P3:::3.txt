

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental question in Gaussian mixture model (GMM) estimation: whether the population log-likelihood function can contain "bad" local maxima that are arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. The authors provide two key contributions: (1) they construct a counterexample to Srebro's (2007) conjecture by showing that for M ≥ 3 well-separated, isotropic, equally-weighted GMMs, arbitrarily bad local maxima exist even in the infinite-sample limit; and (2) they prove that with random initialization, the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) converges to these bad local maxima with high probability (at least 1-e^(-Ω(M))).

The paper builds on prior work in GMM theory, including identifiability results (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009), convergence rates (Chen, 1995; Ho and Nguyen, 2015), and algorithmic analysis (Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015). It also relates to recent work on non-convex optimization (Loh and Wainwright, 2013; Lee et al., 2016), particularly the question of whether all local optima are near global optima. The authors' results fundamentally challenge the common intuition that well-separated GMMs have benign likelihood surfaces.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a theoretically important contribution by resolving Srebro's open question. The construction of bad local maxima is elegant and the proof techniques are novel. The algorithmic results (Theorem 2) are significant, showing that random initialization fails with exponentially high probability in M. The authors also provide valuable insight by distinguishing between failure due to bad local maxima versus saddle points (Theorem 4), which addresses a key concern in non-convex optimization.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1. **Practical relevance (Main Doubt 1):** The paper does not adequately address how likely the "bad" center configurations are in real-world applications. The authors should assume a distribution on true centers (e.g., uniform or Gaussian) and show how the probability of bad configurations increases with k or d. Theorem 2 does not resolve this, as it only considers specific adversarial configurations, not typical ones.

2. **Initialization relevance (Main Doubt 2):** The random initialization scheme (sampling from data) is unrealistic when data are clearly clustered. The authors claim that random initialization "is often more reasonable than drawing µ from a fixed distribution," but for well-separated clusters, this scheme would naturally place centers near true clusters. The example of 3 Gaussians (two close, one far) demonstrates this: it seems highly unlikely that random initialization would place centers in the problematic configuration described.

3. **Technical clarity:** Several points require clarification:
   - L74-81: The example for k=2 does not convincingly support Srebro's conjecture. The authors should clarify why this example is relevant.
   - L233-235: Requirement (1) is unclear. Why is it necessary to have centers initialized between clusters? Why wouldn't a center initialized between clusters move to the correct cluster?
   - L267-273: The distinction between convergence to saddle points versus local maxima seems less practically important than the main result about local maxima.
   - Notation issues: L218 ("well-separated constant" is unclear), L225 (Ω(k) not defined), and other minor phrasing issues (e.g., L18, L38, L60 as noted in the prompt).

4. **Minor writing issues:** The paper contains several typos and unclear phrases that would benefit from revision (e.g., L172 redundancy, L177 "equivalent to" should be clarified, L220 duplicate "the").

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes an important theoretical contribution to understanding the likelihood surface of Gaussian mixtures. The main results are technically sound and address a long-standing open question. However, the practical relevance of the results is questionable without addressing how likely the bad configurations are in typical applications. The authors' focus on the worst-case scenario, while theoretically interesting, does not fully address the practical concerns of using EM in real-world applications.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following conditions: (1) The authors should address the practical relevance question by analyzing the probability of bad configurations under natural distributions of true centers; (2) They should clarify the initialization scheme and its practicality; (3) They should revise the paper to address the notation and clarity issues noted above. With these revisions, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the NIPS community.
