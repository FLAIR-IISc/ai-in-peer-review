This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and establishes two main results: (i) the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima for equally weighted, spherical, well-separated mixtures with M ≥ 3 components, even in 1D; and (ii) that EM and first-order EM (gradient EM) with a widely used random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-Ω(M)}. The authors also show first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, so the failures stem from bad local maxima rather than saddles. These findings answer Srebro’s 2007 conjecture in the negative and reframe the role of initialization for EM, even in benign settings.

Relation to prior work: The paper complements a long line of work on GMMs, identifiability, and estimation rates (Teicher; Chen; Genovese & Wasserman; Ghosal & van der Vaart; Ho & Nguyen), as well as algorithmic approaches via clustering and moments (Vempala & Wang; Arora et al.; Chaudhuri & Rao; Belkin & Sinha; Moitra & Valiant; Hsu & Kakade). It contrasts with positive local convergence guarantees for EM under good initialization (Dasgupta & Schulman; Balakrishnan et al.). On the nonconvex optimization side, the strict-saddle perspective (Pascanu et al.; Ge et al.) and convergence to non-saddle critical points for gradient methods (Lee et al.; Panageas & Piliouras) are leveraged and extended to the likelihood geometry of GMMs.

Technical contributions: 
- Theorem 1 constructs, for any M ≥ 3 and any target gap Cgap > 0, a uniformly weighted, unit-variance, well-separated GMM whose population log-likelihood admits a local maximum with value at least Cgap below the global optimum. The 1D, M=3 construction is especially illuminating: two true means are close, the third is far; a configuration with two centers placed near the far cluster and the remaining center between the close pair forms a basin containing a suboptimal local maximum.
- Theorem 2 shows EM with random initialization (sampling M initial means i.i.d. from the mixture, equivalently uniform-from-data in the population limit) converges to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{-cM}. The recursive “cluster-within-cluster” construction ensures that a correct allocation across all levels is exponentially unlikely.
- Theorem 3 extends the negative convergence result to first-order EM for step sizes s ∈ (0, 1), noting gradient EM equals gradient ascent on the population likelihood.
- Theorem 4 shows first-order EM does not converge to strict saddles almost surely; thus poor performance arises from bad local maxima rather than saddles.

Quality: The results are technically sound and carefully argued at the population level. The paper clearly presents the geometry behind the constructions and links them to algorithmic consequences. I did not fully check the full appendix; given the centrality of the recursive construction and boundary arguments, I recommend an additional careful proof check by the committee. The finite-sample extension is plausible via standard empirical process tools, but it would be valuable to quantify sample complexity explicitly.

Clarity: The manuscript is well organized and clearly written. The 1D, M=3 example and accompanying intuition are particularly helpful. The connection between first-order EM and gradient ascent is clearly stated. Minor suggestion: gather key lemmas from the appendix into the main text to aid readability.

Originality: High. The paper resolves an open conjecture by Srebro (2007) and provides a sharp, structural understanding of the GMM likelihood landscape, paired with algorithmic consequences for EM and gradient EM.

Significance: High. The results recalibrate expectations for likelihood-based methods in mixtures, even asymptotically and under favorable conditions. They inform the design of initialization schemes and motivate hybrid methods (e.g., moment-based pilots followed by EM). The work also contributes to the broader nonconvex optimization literature by isolating bad local maxima as a key obstacle.

Main weaknesses and questions:
- Constants and scaling: The theorems guarantee probabilities of failure of the form 1 − e^{-cM}, but the constants (c and the separation needed as a function of M, d, and Cgap) are not explicit. Concrete constants would help identify the “phase transition” for realistic M (e.g., M ∈ [3, 20]). If deriving explicit constants is messy, supporting simulations would suffice.
- Empirical validation: Since claims are population-level, small synthetic experiments (M=3–10, d=1–10) showing failure probabilities versus M and separation would substantiate practical relevance. Visualizing EM trajectories on the 1D construction would be helpful.
- Finite-sample rates: Please state an explicit sample size bound ensuring the empirical likelihood inherits the bad local maxima structure with high probability. How large must n be relative to separation and M?
- Robustness: How sensitive are the constructions to slight weight imbalance, small covariance perturbations, or mild anisotropy? Do the negative results persist if weights deviate from uniform by O(1/M) or covariances are I + εΔ?
- Initialization variants: The negative results target uniform-from-data initialization. How do they interact with k-means++ or pruning-based schemes (e.g., Dasgupta–Schulman)? A brief discussion would be valuable, even if exhaustive lower bounds are out of scope.
- Two-component case: The paper notes M=2 remains open; can the authors add more discussion or partial results clarifying obstacles to extending the construction?

Pros:
- Resolves an open question with a clean, interpretable construction.
- Strong algorithmic consequences with clear practical message on initialization.
- Bridges likelihood geometry and modern strict-saddle theory.
- Well written and pedagogical.

Cons:
- Lack of explicit constants and finite-sample quantification.
- No empirical demonstrations to calibrate the asymptotic phenomena.
- Limited discussion of robustness and common practical initializations beyond random seeding.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide explicit (even conservative) constants for c in the exponential bound, separation levels, and the dependence on Cgap.
- Add a short experimental section illustrating failure probabilities versus M and separation, and contrasting random seeding with k-means++.
- State a finite-sample theorem with n scaling and probability statements.
- Discuss robustness to small deviations from the idealized model.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper offers a definitive and impactful answer to a longstanding question and will influence both theory and practice of EM for mixtures.

Reviewer confidence: High on the assessment of contributions and significance; medium on the full verification of technical appendices.
