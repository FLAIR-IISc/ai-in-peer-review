This paper investigates the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm when applied to Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), focusing on the critical issue of local optima. The authors provide rigorous theoretical results demonstrating that, even in seemingly well-behaved settings – specifically, uniformly weighted mixtures of well-separated spherical Gaussians – EM can converge to suboptimal solutions with high probability. This resolves an open question posed by Srebro [2007] and provides a nuanced understanding of the algorithm’s limitations. The work builds upon a substantial body of research concerning mixture models, dating back to Teicher [1963] on identifiability and extending to more recent investigations into convergence rates by Chen [1995], Ghosal and Van Der Vaart [2001], and Ho and Nguyen [2015].  The authors position their work within the broader context of algorithms for GMM estimation, acknowledging prior efforts based on clustering (Arora et al. [2005], Dasgupta and Schulman [2007]) and method of moments (Belkin and Sinha [2010], Moitra and Valiant [2010]). 

The core contribution of the paper is two-fold. First, they construct a specific GMM with three components—where two components are closely positioned and the third is well-separated—that demonstrably possesses bad local maxima in its population likelihood function. This directly refutes Srebro’s conjecture. Second, they show that with random initialization, EM will converge to such suboptimal critical points with a probability that decreases exponentially with the number of mixture components (M).  Furthermore, they demonstrate that this poor performance is not attributable to the presence of saddle points, but rather to the existence of these bad local maxima.  The analysis of the first-order EM variant reinforces these findings. 

The strengths of this work lie in its rigorous mathematical treatment of a long-standing problem. The proofs appear to be sound and the results are presented clearly. The exponential dependence on M in the failure probability is a particularly strong and concerning finding, highlighting the difficulties inherent in using EM for high-dimensional mixture modeling. The effort to disentangle the roles of local maxima and saddle points is also valuable.  The paper is well-motivated, situating the investigation within the broader landscape of GMM estimation and acknowledging the practical relevance of the problem.  The writing is clear and well-organized, and the related work is appropriately referenced. 

However, the work also has some weaknesses. While the results are mathematically strong, they build on fairly intuitive ideas. The construction of the three-component GMM with a bad local maximum feels relatively straightforward, particularly given the earlier work on k-means clustering, where similar phenomena are well-documented. The exponential failure rate, while significant, is consistent with observations in other clustering algorithms.  The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison to existing theoretical results on k-means (e.g., Kumar and Kannan [2010]) and a discussion of how the techniques used here relate to those developed for k-means analysis.  Specifically, contrasting the guarantees obtained under conditions of large separation (as considered here) in both EM and Lloyd’s algorithm (k-means) would strengthen the paper’s impact. A discussion of the practical implications of these results, beyond simply stating the need for careful initialization, would also be beneficial.  Are there existing initialization strategies (beyond those mentioned – moment matching) that can mitigate these issues? 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Rigorous mathematical analysis of a fundamental problem in statistical estimation.
*   Clear and compelling results demonstrating the limitations of EM.
*   Addresses a long-standing open question.
*   Well-written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The results, while strong, are somewhat predictable given prior work in clustering.
*   Lacks a detailed comparison to related work on k-means.
*   Limited discussion of practical implications and potential mitigation strategies.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the theoretical understanding of EM and GMMs. While the results are not entirely surprising, the rigorous analysis and clear presentation are valuable. The exponential failure rate with increasing dimensionality is a significant finding.  I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding the comparison to k-means and the discussion of practical implications in a revised version. The paper is of sufficient quality for NIPS, provided these revisions are incorporated.
