

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses an important open question in statistical learning theory: whether the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components can have bad local maxima. Srebro (2007) conjectured that for the population likelihood, any local maximum would be a global maximum in the limit of infinite samples, which would provide strong theoretical justification for the EM algorithm. The authors resolve this conjecture in the negative, demonstrating that even for equally-weighted mixtures of well-separated and spherical Gaussians, bad local maxima exist whose log-likelihood values can be arbitrarily worse than global optima.

The paper makes two main contributions. First, it constructs counterexamples to Srebro's conjecture for M ≥ 3, showing that bad local maxima exist even in favorable settings. Second, it demonstrates that the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to these bad critical points with probability at least 1 - e^{-Ω(M)}. The authors further prove that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, indicating that the poor performance is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper is well-motivated and builds upon previous work on GMMs, including the identifiability results of Teicher (1963), the density estimation work of Genovese and Wasserman (2000), and the algorithmic analysis of EM by Dasgupta and Schulman (2007) and Balakrishnan et al. (2015). The authors clearly position their work as resolving a specific open question that has been unresolved for nearly a decade.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a clear and rigorous resolution to an important open question in the field.
2. The theoretical results are technically sound and well-supported by detailed proofs.
3. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations and intuitive geometric constructions (particularly for M=3, d=1).
4. The paper makes the important distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points, which has significant implications for understanding optimization in non-convex settings.
5. The results have direct practical implications for the use of EM in GMM estimation, highlighting the necessity of careful initialization.

Weaknesses:
1. The constants in the probability bounds (1 - e^{-cM}) are not specified. Knowing these constants would help practitioners understand when the exponential failure probability becomes relevant for realistic values of M.
2. While the paper establishes results for the population likelihood, the connection to finite-sample settings, though mentioned in the conclusion, would benefit from more concrete discussion.
3. The paper does not include empirical validation of the theoretical claims through simulations, which would help illustrate the phenomenon for specific parameter values.

Quality: The paper is technically excellent, with rigorous proofs that address the main question thoroughly. The authors carefully consider both strengths and limitations of their work. The results are complete and not merely a position paper.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and accessible to experts in the field. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the results.

Originality: The paper provides a novel construction of counterexamples to Srebro's conjecture, which has been a longstanding open question. The distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points is also a significant contribution that differentiates this work from previous studies.

Significance: The results have substantial significance for both theoretical understanding and practical application of GMMs. They fundamentally change how we view the behavior of local search methods in this important statistical model, with implications for the design of initialization strategies in practice. The work advances the state of the art in understanding non-convex optimization for statistical models.

Pro and con for acceptance:
Pro: Resolves an important open question with rigorous theoretical results; clear and well-written; significant implications for both theory and practice.
Con: Lack of specific constants in probability bounds; no empirical validation; limited discussion of finite-sample implications.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of statistical learning. While the lack of constants in the probability bounds and absence of empirical validation are notable limitations, they do not detract from the paper's theoretical novelty or significance. The results are important for understanding the limitations of local search methods in GMM estimation, and the paper is well-positioned to influence both theoretical and applied research in the area.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical results are strong, the paper is well-written, and the implications for both theory and practice are significant. The minor limitations noted do not undermine the paper's core contributions.
