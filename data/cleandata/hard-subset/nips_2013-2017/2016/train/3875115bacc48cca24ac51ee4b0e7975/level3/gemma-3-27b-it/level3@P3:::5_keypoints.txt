- Paper answers Srebro’s 2007 conjecture negatively by constructing a 3-component, well-separated 1D GMM with arbitrarily bad local maxima.
- Shows EM and first-order EM converge with high probability to these suboptimal solutions when initialized randomly from data.
- Provides detailed proofs and a complete case study for the simplest non-trivial setting (k=3, d=1).
- Analyzes initialization schemes and offers techniques for reasoning about the population log-likelihood.
- Situates the work within literature on identifiability, convergence rates, EM algorithm analysis, and non-convex optimization.
- Strength — novelty/significance: settles a longstanding open problem and clarifies limitations of local search for GMMs.
- Strength — technical soundness: proofs are rigorous and the EM/first-order EM analyses are compelling.
- Strength — clarity: manuscript is well-written, well-organized, and connects results to prior work.
- Strength — algorithmic relevance: high-probability convergence to bad maxima has practical implications for EM use.
- Strength — theoretical insight: introduces methods potentially applicable to other non-convex problems.
- Weakness — missing explicit constant estimates (e.g., constant c in Theorem 2), limiting practical interpretation and phase-transition understanding.
- Weakness — limited simulations: lack of empirical results for realistic k values weakens assessment of practical impact and bound tightness.
- Weakness — step-size sensitivity: requirement s ∈ (0,1) for first-order EM is restrictive and needs further analysis.
- Arguments for acceptance: fundamental question answered, rigorous proofs, clear writing, and significant practical implications.
- Arguments against acceptance: absence of constant estimates and supporting simulations hampers empirical/practical validation.
- Overall assessment: very strong paper; merits acceptance despite noted weaknesses.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—ask authors to provide approximate constant estimates or supporting simulations across k values.
