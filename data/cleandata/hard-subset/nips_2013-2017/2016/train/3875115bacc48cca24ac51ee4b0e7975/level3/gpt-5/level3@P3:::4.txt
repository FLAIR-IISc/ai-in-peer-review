Summary
This paper analyzes the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) under an idealized but widely studied setting: equal mixing weights and identity covariances. It resolves a conjecture of Srebro (2007) by proving that even for well-separated, spherical, equally weighted mixtures with M ≥ 3, the population objective can have bad local maxima whose likelihood is arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. Beyond existence, the paper shows that EM and gradient EM with a standard random-initialization scheme converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M) for carefully constructed mean configurations, via a hierarchical grouping/recursive clustering construction. Finally, building on recent results about gradient methods and strict saddles, the paper proves that gradient EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, attributing failures to bad local maxima rather than saddles.

Relative to prior work, this paper complements lines of research that either (a) show favorable local convergence of EM given good initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015 for M=2), or (b) avoid likelihood-based methods in favor of spectral/moment or clustering algorithms under separation (e.g., Vempala & Wang, Arora et al., Hsu & Kakade, Moitra & Valiant). It also addresses the open positive hypothesis suggested by Srebro (2007) for population likelihoods. The two-component case remains unresolved, consistent with empirical impressions that M=2 may be benign.

Main contributions
- Result 1 (structure/existence): For M=3, d=1 (and generalizable M≥3), construct well-separated means where a suboptimal local maximum exists with log-likelihood arbitrarily below the global optimum. The construction assigns two centers near one true mean and leaves two nearby true means effectively covered by one center, and then shows a local basin around this suboptimal configuration.
- Result 2 (random init failure): With the practical random initialization wherein initial centers are i.i.d. from the mixture, the probability of hitting a “good” configuration can be exponentially small in M for a recursive, well-separated cluster-within-cluster arrangement of true means; consequently EM/gradient EM must be restarted exponentially many times (in M) to succeed with constant probability.
- Result 3 (no convergence to strict saddles): Gradient EM (first-order EM) with a reasonable step size almost surely avoids strict saddles; hence failures stem from bad local maxima, not saddles.

Quality
- Theorem 1 appears technically sound and well-motivated. The neighborhood/boundary likelihood comparison argument is clear and persuasive for M=3, d=1, and the extension to general M via analogous constructions is plausible.
- Theorem 2’s proof idea is compelling—the hierarchical grouping (binary tree of clusters with well-separated levels) sharply reduces the mass of successful initializations. While I did not verify every appendix detail, the high-level counting/probability argument and trapping intuition are solid.
- Theorem 3 follows naturally from Theorem 2 given gradient EM’s equivalence to gradient ascent on the population likelihood for this model.
- Theorem 4 is consistent with the stable manifold perspective (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas & Piliouras, 2016). The paper’s proof sketch indicating how to avoid strong global smoothness assumptions for L by exploiting structure of gradient EM is appreciated.

Clarity
- The setup, intuitions, and consequences are clearly presented. The geometric intuition for M=3 in 1D, and the recursive construction for the exponentially small success probability, are well explained. The paper relates results to known positive results (e.g., M=2 local convergence under separation) and motivates why negative results in the population setting matter.

Originality
- The explicit linkage between mean configuration (hierarchical grouping) and the prevalence of bad local maxima is original and insightful. The recursive construction that drives success probability to e−Ω(M) is particularly clever. The clean separation of issues—local maxima vs. saddle points—via the “no strict saddle convergence” result adds a fresh perspective to the EM landscape discussion.

Significance
- The results definitively close a long-standing conjecture in a central model class, and they have concrete algorithmic implications: naive random seeding with EM/gradient methods can be provably unreliable, even in population and with well-separated components. This should inform both theoretical work on the landscape of clustering objectives and practical seeding strategies that use coarse pilot estimates (e.g., spectral/moment-based, k-means++ variants, or hierarchical seeding).
- The hierarchical grouping idea may inspire broader analyses of nonconvex landscapes with latent “tree” structure, and motivate new initialization schemes that respect multi-scale structure.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope: Results focus on equal weights and identity covariance. While this is standard for isolating landscape phenomena, it would be valuable to discuss how the constructions extend to unequal weights, shared non-identity covariances, or mild covariance misspecification.
- Dimensionality: The bad-local-maxima construction is 1D (for M=3). It would be helpful to clarify which lemmas (e.g., Lemma 7) extend to d>1 and whether dimensionality alleviates or exacerbates the basins of bad maxima under similar hierarchical layouts embedded in higher dimensions.
- Quantitative separation: The statement of “well-separated” is qualitative in the main text; more explicit constants for the separation needed at different levels of the hierarchy would improve reproducibility and understanding of trade-offs.
- Practical guidance: Given the negative result for random initialization, it would help to provide actionable alternatives (e.g., spectral or moment-based pre-initialization, or provably better-than-random seeding that targets the hierarchical structure) and, if possible, simple diagnostics to detect trapped configurations.
- Verification: Some technical details in the appendix (especially for Theorem 2) were not checked in depth; a concise, self-contained lemma summarizing the key probability calculation would be useful.

Suggestions
- Clarify whether the “no strict saddle convergence” result for gradient EM might extend to standard EM, and what would be required (e.g., a suitable generalized stable manifold theorem).
- Explore whether the recursive grouping construction can be adapted to mixtures with moderate weight imbalance or tied/diagonal covariances.
- Provide a short finite-sample discussion translating the population results via empirical process arguments (which you mention), highlighting sample sizes at which the negative phenomena become observable.
- Consider a brief numerical illustration of the 1D, M=3 example and a small M hierarchical case to visualize trapping and likelihood gaps.

Pros
- Resolves a notable open question; clear negative structural and algorithmic results.
- Clever and original hierarchical construction yielding exponentially small success probability.
- Rigorous and well-motivated proofs; good intuition and presentation.
- Useful perspective distinguishing bad local maxima from saddle-point issues.

Cons
- Limited to equal weights/identity covariance; 1D construction for core counterexample.
- Some technical appendices are dense; quantitative constants and d>1 extensions could be clearer.
- Limited practical guidance beyond “avoid random initialization.”

Recommendation
- Accept. The paper makes a clean, rigorous, and impactful contribution to our understanding of EM and GMM likelihood landscapes, with clear theoretical and practical implications.

Confidence
- High on the assessment of Theorems 1 and 4 and the overall interpretation; moderate on the full technical details of Theorem 2 in the appendix.
