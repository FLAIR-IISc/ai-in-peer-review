This paper resolves an open problem of Srebro [2007] by demonstrating the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima for the population log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models, even in the idealized situation where each component is uniformly weighted, spherical with unit variance, and well-separated. The authors also provide evidence that even in this favorable setting, random initialization schemes for the population EM algorithm are likely to fail with high probability.

The main contributions of the paper are two-fold. Firstly, the authors construct a general class of counterexamples to Srebro's question, showing that for any M ≥ 3, there exists a well-separated uniform mixture of M unit-variance spherical Gaussians GMM(µ∗) and a local maximum µ′ such that L(µ′) ≤ L(µ∗)− Cgap. Secondly, the authors show that the EM algorithm, as well as a variant thereof known as the first-order EM algorithm, with random initialization, converges to a bad critical point with an exponentially high probability.

The paper is well-written and clear, and the authors do an excellent job of balancing space with intuitive explanation. The results are important for understanding non-convex optimization even in the asymptotic limit. The manuscript provides a detailed proof for a simple case (k=3, d=1), which helps to build intuition about the problem.

One potential drawback of the paper is that the supplementary material was not fully checked by the reviewer, and the reviewer requests estimates of constants in the probability bounds. Knowing the constants is important to identify the phase transition and when bad-solution convergence appears for realistic k. If the constants are complex or unclear, supporting simulations should be provided to demonstrate the phenomenon. However, the lack of constants/simulations does not detract from the paper's novelty or impact.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is also well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors do an excellent job of explaining the main ideas and results. The notation is clear, and the definitions are well-defined.
