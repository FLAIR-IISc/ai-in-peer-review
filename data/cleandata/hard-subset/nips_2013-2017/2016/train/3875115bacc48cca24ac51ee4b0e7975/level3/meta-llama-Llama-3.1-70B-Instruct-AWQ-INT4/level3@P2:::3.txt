This paper studies the local maxima in the likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components. The authors provide two fundamental results on the population likelihood function of GMMs. The first main result shows that the population likelihood function has bad local maxima even in the special case of equally-weighted mixtures of well-separated and spherical Gaussians. The second main result shows that the EM algorithm (or a first-order variant of it) with random initialization will converge to bad critical points with probability at least 1− e−Ω(M). The authors also establish that a first-order variant of EM will not converge to strict saddle points almost surely.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and the main results. The technical proofs are relegated to the appendices, making the main body of the paper easy to follow. The authors also provide a detailed discussion of the implications of their results and the related work in the field.

The main contribution of the paper is the resolution of an open question of Srebro [2007] regarding the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function of GMMs. The authors provide a negative answer to this question, showing that bad local maxima can exist even in the idealized situation where each component is uniformly weighted, spherical with unit variance, and well-separated.

The paper also provides some evidence that even in this favorable setting, random initialization schemes for the population EM algorithm are likely to fail with high probability. The authors show that the probability of success for the EM algorithm is exponentially small as a function of M, and that in order to guarantee recovering a global maximum with at least constant probability, the EM algorithm with random initialization must be executed at least eΩ(M) times.

However, I have some doubts about the practical relevance of the results. The authors consider specific configurations (centres split into well-spaced clusters) and prove that random initialization makes EM miss the global optimum with high probability as k increases. However, it is unclear how likely these "bad" centre configurations are in real problems. I would like to see some discussion on this point, and perhaps some additional analysis assuming a distribution on true centres and showing that the probability of bad configurations increases with k or d.

Additionally, I am not convinced that the random sampling initialization is a realistic assumption when data are clearly clustered. The authors should discuss this point and perhaps provide some additional analysis on the effect of different initialization schemes on the performance of the EM algorithm.

Some minor comments on the writing style and notation:

* L74–81: The purpose of the example is unclear, and it does not convince that Srebro’s conjecture holds for k=2.
* L233–235: The requirement (1) is unclear or unnecessary. Why is it needed, and does a centre initialized between clusters not move to the correct cluster?
* L267–273: The practical importance of showing EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point versus a local optimum is unclear.
* Clarify phrasing/notation issues: L218 “well-separated constant” is unclear; L225 what does Ω(k) mean?
* Minor edits: L18 add “that the”; L38 use plural “estimates”; L60 rephrase to “under which conditions”; L138 clarify O(d^1/2)-separation meaning; L172 reword or remove redundancy; L177 “equivalent to”; L220 remove duplicate “the”; L245 “coincidence”; L249 add “that”; L308 add “that”.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and the main results. However, I have some doubts about the practical relevance of the results, and I would like to see some additional analysis and discussion on this point.
