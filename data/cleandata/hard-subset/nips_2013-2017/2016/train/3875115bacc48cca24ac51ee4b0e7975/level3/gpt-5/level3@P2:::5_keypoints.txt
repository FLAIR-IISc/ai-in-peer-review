- Resolves Srebro (2007) conjecture: shows population GMMs with M ≥ 3 (equal-weight, spherical, well-separated) admit spurious local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.  
- Constructs explicit 1D examples (generalizable to any M) and gives geometric intuition via the M=3 case where two means are close and a third is far.  
- Identifies a neighborhood excluding global optima but containing a local maximum that merges the nearby pair and duplicates the far center.  
- Proves EM and gradient EM with standard i.i.d. random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e−Ω(M).  
- Shows naive restart strategies require exponentially many restarts in M via a recursive clustering construction.  
- Proves first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, so algorithmic failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Complements prior positive local-convergence EM results and contrasts with finite-sample pathologies, refining the nonconvexity narrative for GMMs.  
- Technical soundness: precise statements and convincing geometric/recursive arguments, though appendix proofs were not fully verified by the reviewer.  
- Population-level claims are well-supported; sample-level implications are discussed qualitatively but lack quantitative bounds.  
- Missing explicit constants: asymptotic bounds (e−Ω(M)) and separation conditions lack numerical constants for practical assessment.  
- Paper clarity: well written and organized; M=3, d=1 construction and intuition are helpful.  
- Clarity suggestions: state explicit dependence of “well-separated” on d, M, and target gap; add a schematic for the recursive cluster construction; summarize key lemmas showing EM trajectories remain trapped.  
- Originality: novel and important negative resolution plus exponential failure probability and strict-saddle avoidance constitute an original algorithmic message.  
- Significance: high conceptual impact and strong practical motivation to use informed initializations instead of naive random restarts.  
- Pros: resolves an open question with a clear counterexample, gives sharp algorithmic consequences, and situates results well in literature.  
- Cons: lacks explicit constants, no simulations to illustrate phase transition or trapping, limited to equal weights/spherical covariances, and no quantified finite-sample rates.  
- Requests: provide explicit constants/bounds for separation ξ, exponent c in success probability, and permissible step-sizes; if complex, add simulations for success vs. M, likelihood gaps, and sensitivity to model deviations.  
- Additional questions: quantify finite-sample n vs. d, M, separation conditions for population-to-sample transfer; applicability to M=2 under different covariances; performance of common initializations (k-means++, spectral, moment-based) on hard instances.  
- Recommendation: strong accept conditional on adding explicit constants or supportive simulations to calibrate the phenomenon for moderate M; novelty and significance outweigh omissions.  
- Reviewer confidence: high.
