

This paper addresses an important open question regarding local maxima in Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) likelihood functions, specifically resolving Srebro's conjecture about the absence of bad local maxima in the population setting. The authors demonstrate that for M ≥ 3 well-separated, isotropic, unit-variance Gaussians, arbitrarily bad local maxima exist even with infinite data, and that random initialization for EM (and first-order EM) converges to these bad critical points with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)). This is a significant theoretical contribution that challenges conventional wisdom about EM's behavior in favorable settings.

The paper makes strong theoretical contributions with rigorous proofs of the existence of bad local maxima and the poor convergence properties of random initialization. The construction of bad local maxima by grouping centers into well-separated clusters is elegant and well-motivated. The distinction between convergence to bad local maxima versus saddle points (Theorem 4) is particularly valuable.

However, several concerns warrant attention. First, the practical relevance of the constructed bad configurations remains questionable. The authors should address whether such configurations are likely to occur under natural distributions of true centers, as requested in the key points. Theorem 2 does not fully resolve this, and the paper would benefit from a probabilistic analysis showing how the probability of bad configurations increases with model complexity (k or d).

Second, the assumption of random initialization from the data distribution may be unrealistic in practice when data exhibit clear clustering structure. The example with three Gaussians (two close, one far) suggests that random initialization would rarely place centers in the problematic configuration described, raising questions about the practical significance of the results.

Several minor issues require attention: L74-81's example fails to convincingly address Srebro's conjecture for k=2; L233-235's requirement (1) is unclear; L218's "well-separated constant" needs clarification; L225's Ω(k) notation should be defined; and numerous small phrasing issues require correction.

The paper's significance is somewhat diminished by these practical relevance concerns, though the theoretical contribution remains strong. While the paper is technically sound with clear proofs, the practical implications for real-world applications need stronger justification. The writing is generally clear but requires careful attention to the noted minor issues.

Overall, this is a technically strong theoretical contribution with clear novelty and significance in the context of understanding GMM likelihood landscapes. However, the practical relevance concerns and unclear connections to real-world applications prevent it from being a highly impactful paper for the NIPS community.
