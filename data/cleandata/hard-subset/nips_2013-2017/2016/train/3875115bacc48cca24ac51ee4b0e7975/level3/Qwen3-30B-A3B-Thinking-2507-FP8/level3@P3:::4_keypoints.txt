- Resolves Srebro (2007) open question by showing bad local maxima can exist even for equally-weighted, spherical GMMs with identity covariance.  
- Theorem 1: provides a k=3, d=1 counterexample where local maxima have arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global maximum.  
- Theorem 2: with certain mean configurations, random initialization has exponentially small probability (in k) of avoiding bad local maxima, implying repeated random restarts are necessary.  
- Theorem 4: gradient EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, so failures are primarily due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Extends prior work (which focused on k=2) by addressing k>2 and introducing a hierarchical grouping construction.  
- Technical quality is strong; proofs (notably Theorem 1) are rigorous, though some appendix details (Theorem 2) were not verified by the reviewer.  
- Exceptionally clear and well-organized exposition, with strong geometric intuition behind the counterexample.  
- Original contribution: the hierarchical grouping insight for k>3 is novel and provides a new framework for understanding GMM likelihood landscapes.  
- Significant implications: advances theory of non-convex optimization and has practical impact by highlighting the need for careful initialization/seeding strategies for EM.  
- Suggests future work, particularly extending Lemma 7 to higher dimensions (d>1).  
- Limitation: results are confined to equal mixing weights and spherical Gaussians, though they likely extend to large finite samples via standard arguments.  
- Overall recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance.
