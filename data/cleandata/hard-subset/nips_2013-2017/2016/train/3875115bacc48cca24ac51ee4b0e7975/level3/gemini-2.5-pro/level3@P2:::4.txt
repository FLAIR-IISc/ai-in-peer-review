This paper investigates the structure of the population log-likelihood surface for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) in a simplified but important setting: equally-weighted components with known identity covariance. The authors provide three main results. First, they resolve an open question posed by Srebro (2007) by demonstrating that for M ≥ 3 components, the population likelihood function can have "bad" local maxima whose objective value is arbitrarily worse than that of the global maximum. They provide a constructive counterexample for M=3 in d=1 to prove this. Second, they show that for certain GMM configurations, the EM algorithm (and a first-order variant) with random initialization converges to such a bad critical point with probability approaching 1 exponentially in M. This implies that a naive random initialization strategy is computationally infeasible for finding a good solution. Third, they leverage recent results from optimization theory to show that a first-order EM variant (equivalent to gradient ascent) will almost surely not converge to a strict saddle point, suggesting that bad local maxima are the primary obstacle for this algorithm, rather than saddle points.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by rigorous theoretical analysis. The proof for the existence of bad local maxima (Theorem 1), outlined in the main text, is intuitive and appears sound. The construction relies on a clever geometric arrangement of the true means, which is then used to bound the likelihood on the boundary of a specific region to prove a local maximum must exist inside. The overall work is a complete and self-contained contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation and a concise summary of previous work, positioning its contributions effectively. The main results are stated clearly, and the authors do an excellent job of explaining the intuition behind their constructions and their algorithmic consequences. The geometric description of the M=3 counterexample and the recursive clustering idea for the M>3 case are particularly helpful in making the core ideas accessible.

*   **Originality & Significance:** This work makes several original and significant contributions.
    1.  **Resolving an Open Problem:** The negative answer to Srebro's (2007) conjecture is a fundamental result that clarifies our understanding of the GMM likelihood landscape. It formally establishes that even in the idealized population setting with well-separated components, the optimization problem is genuinely difficult due to the presence of spurious optima.
    2.  **Characterizing Algorithmic Failure:** The second result provides a powerful quantitative statement about the failure of the widely-used "random-initialization + EM" heuristic. By constructing a hierarchical GMM and showing an exponential failure probability, the paper moves beyond mere existence proofs of bad optima to show they are a genuine trap for practical algorithms. This provides strong theoretical justification for the necessity of more sophisticated initialization procedures, such as those based on the method of moments or spectral techniques, especially in high-dimensional or large-M settings.
    3.  **Connecting to Modern Optimization Theory:** The result on avoiding saddle points (Theorem 4) connects the specific analysis of EM for GMMs to the broader, active research area of non-convex optimization. It provides a concrete, important example where bad local minima, not saddle points, are the dominant challenge for first-order methods.

**Weaknesses / Questions for Authors:**

The analysis is performed in an idealized setting (population likelihood, equal weights, known identity covariances). This is a standard and necessary simplification for making theoretical progress, and the authors rightly note that results likely extend to the large-sample regime. However, it would be interesting to discuss the potential challenges in extending this analysis to unknown/unequal weights or covariances. Does the geometry of bad local maxima become even more complex?

The main construction in Theorem 1 is for d=1. The paper claims this is a well-separated mixture, which seems to depend on the scaling of the distances. Could the authors clarify if the results extend to higher dimensions (d>1) and what the separation requirements would be in that case?

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper makes significant theoretical contributions by resolving an open question and providing a rigorous analysis of why a common heuristic (EM with random initialization) can fail. The results are novel, technically sound, and clearly presented. The work has important implications for both the theory and practice of fitting mixture models.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The analysis is restricted to a simplified GMM setting, which is a necessary but important limitation.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that provides fundamental new insights into the optimization landscape of GMMs. The strengths far outweigh the minor limitations. It is a significant contribution to the field and should be of great interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
