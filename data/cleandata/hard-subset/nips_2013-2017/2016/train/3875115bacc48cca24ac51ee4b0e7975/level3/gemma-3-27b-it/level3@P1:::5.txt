The paper addresses a fundamental question in Gaussian mixture modeling: the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function. It directly refutes a conjecture by Srebro (2007) that such bad local maxima do not exist, even for well-separated Gaussian components. The authors rigorously demonstrate the existence of these suboptimal solutions and, crucially, show that standard algorithms like the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and its first-order variant are highly likely to converge to them when initialized randomly.

The work builds on a rich history of research into mixture models, referencing early work on identifiability (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009) and more recent studies focusing on convergence rates (Genovese & Wasserman, 2000; Chen, 1995; Ho & Nguyen, 2015).  It positions itself within the algorithmic landscape dominated by EM (Dempster et al., 1977) and alternative approaches aiming for stronger theoretical guarantees (Arora et al., 2005; Belkin & Sinha, 2010). The authors appropriately situate their work relative to advancements in understanding EM’s local convergence properties (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and the broader challenge of robustness in maximum likelihood estimation (Donoho & Liu, 1988).

The paper’s strengths lie in its clarity, technical soundness, and significance. The authors present a clear and well-structured argument, progressing logically from the problem statement to the theoretical results and their implications. The construction of the counterexample to Srebro’s conjecture is insightful, and the subsequent analysis of the EM algorithm’s convergence behavior is rigorous. The proofs are detailed and well-explained, and the paper effectively conveys the intuition behind the results.  The significance stems from the broad applicability of the findings. The prevalence of Gaussian mixture models in various fields, coupled with the widespread use of EM, makes this work highly relevant.  Demonstrating the limitations of standard algorithms, even in idealized settings, underscores the necessity for careful initialization strategies in practice.  Furthermore, the investigation of saddle points versus local maxima adds nuance to the understanding of optimization challenges in this context.

However, a weakness concerns the concrete values of the constants appearing in the probabilistic bounds (e.g., the constant 'c' in Theorem 2). While the authors establish exponential decay in the probability of convergence to a good solution, knowing the magnitude of this decay is crucial for assessing the practical impact of the results.  Specifically, understanding how the probability of convergence to a bad local maximum scales with the number of components *M* is vital for determining when the proposed issue becomes significant. Providing estimates for these constants or, if analytically intractable, supporting simulations illustrating the phase transition as *M* increases would substantially strengthen the paper.  For instance, a plot demonstrating the empirical probability of convergence to a bad local maximum as a function of *M* would be incredibly valuable.  

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper resolves an open question in the field and provides a negative result that challenges a previously held belief.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proofs are rigorous and well-supported, demonstrating a deep understanding of the theoretical underpinnings of Gaussian mixture models and optimization algorithms.
*   **Significance:** The findings have practical implications for the use of EM and other local search methods in a wide range of applications.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with the details of mixture model theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Concrete Constants:** The absence of estimated constants in the probabilistic bounds limits the practical interpretability of the results. This is not a fatal flaw, but addressing it would enhance the paper's impact.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weakness regarding the quantification of the probabilistic bounds, I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field, is meticulously crafted, and addresses a fundamental problem with clear and compelling results. The novelty and significance of the findings outweigh the limitations, making this a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. I encourage the authors to consider including either estimates of the constants or supporting simulations in a revised version to further strengthen the paper’s impact.
