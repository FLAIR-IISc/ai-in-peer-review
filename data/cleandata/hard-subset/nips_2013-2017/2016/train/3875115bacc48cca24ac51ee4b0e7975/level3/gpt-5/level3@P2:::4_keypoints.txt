- Studies population log-likelihood of equal-weight, identity-covariance GMMs and disproves Srebro (2007) for M ≥ 3 by constructing well-separated mixtures with arbitrarily bad suboptimal local maxima.
- Shows EM and gradient EM with i.i.d. random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{−Ω(M)}.
- Proves gradient EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, so failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.
- Theorem 1: 1D construction (two close means, one far) yields a misassignment-induced local maximum via a barrier argument.
- Theorem 2: A hierarchical clustering construction gives an exponentially small success probability for EM under random initialization.
- Theorem 3: The same exponential failure bound holds for gradient EM for step sizes s ∈ (0,1), since it is gradient ascent on the population likelihood.
- Theorem 4: Gradient EM converges to critical points and has zero probability of converging to strict saddles.
- Quality: Technical arguments are solid and convincing; appendix probabilistic counting details not fully verified by reviewer.
- Clarity: Well written and organized; distinctions (population vs sample, init model) are clear, though an M=3 illustration and explicit constants would help.
- Originality: High—settles an open conjecture for M ≥ 3 and introduces novel geometric and hierarchical constructions.
- Significance: High—changes understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes and highlights EM limitations even in favorable settings.
- Pros: Resolves a prominent conjecture, gives strong quantitative bounds, links to modern nonconvex theory, and uses a clean model to isolate the phenomenon.
- Cons/Suggestions: Clarify extensions to d>1, robustness to weight/covariance perturbations, give explicit finite-sample/sample-complexity bounds, discuss other initializers (k-means++, moment methods), state regularity/step-size conditions for Theorem 4, and fix reference formatting.
- Recommendation: Accept.
- Reviewer confidence: High for correctness/significance of Theorem 1 and qualitative conclusions; medium for full verification of hierarchical counting in Theorem 2.
