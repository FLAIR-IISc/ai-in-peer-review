- Paper proves existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood for Gaussian mixture models, refuting Srebro (2007).
- Shows bad local maxima can occur even for well-separated Gaussian components.
- Demonstrates EM and its first-order variant are highly likely to converge to these suboptimal solutions with random initialization.
- Constructs an insightful counterexample and provides rigorous, detailed proofs.
- Analysis of EM’s convergence behavior is technically sound and well-explained.
- Investigation distinguishes saddle points from true local maxima, adding nuance to optimization understanding.
- Positions work within historical and recent literature on identifiability, convergence rates, and algorithmic approaches to mixtures.
- Significance: results are broadly relevant due to widespread use of Gaussian mixtures and EM in applications.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and accessible even to non-experts in mixture theory.
- Main weakness: lack of concrete values for constants in probabilistic bounds (e.g., constant c in Theorem 2).
- Practical impact limited by missing scaling estimates of convergence probability with number of components M.
- Recommendation: include analytic estimates or simulations (e.g., plot of empirical probability vs M) to illustrate phase transition.
- Overall assessment: strong accept recommendation based on novelty, technical rigor, significance, and clarity.
