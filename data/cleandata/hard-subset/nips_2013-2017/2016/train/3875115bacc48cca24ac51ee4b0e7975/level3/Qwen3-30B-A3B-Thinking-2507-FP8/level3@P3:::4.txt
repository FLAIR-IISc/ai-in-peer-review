

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental open question in the statistical analysis of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically whether the population log-likelihood function can have "bad" local maxima (local maxima with significantly worse likelihood than the global maximum) when the mixture components are well-separated. The authors resolve an open question posed by Srebro (2007) by providing a negative answer, demonstrating that such bad local maxima can indeed exist even in the favorable setting of equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians with identity covariance. 

The paper makes three key contributions. First, Theorem 1 presents a k=3, d=1 counterexample showing that local maxima can have arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global maximum. This resolves Srebro's conjecture and extends the understanding beyond the two-component case, which had been conjectured to have no bad local maxima. Second, Theorem 2 shows that with random initialization, the probability of avoiding a bad local maximum can be exponentially small in k for certain mean configurations, implying that repeated random initialization is necessary to find good solutions. Third, Theorem 4 proves that gradient EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, indicating that the poor performance of local search methods is primarily due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper builds on previous NIPS work such as Balakrishnan et al. (2015) on local convergence of EM for two Gaussians and Dasgupta and Schulman (2007) on EM variants for well-separated mixtures. It makes a clear departure from this prior work by addressing the case of k>2 and the existence of bad local maxima.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The proof of Theorem 1 appears sound, with a clear construction of the counterexample and rigorous demonstration of the bad local maximum. The proof idea for Theorem 2 is reasonable, though the appendix details were not verified. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the results apply to the population setting and specific parameter configurations), and they present a balanced view of their findings.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem setup is clearly explained, and the key intuition behind the counterexample (hierarchical grouping of means) is presented with geometric clarity. The relationship between the structural results and algorithmic consequences is well-motivated and easy to follow. The presentation of the main theorems and their implications is particularly effective.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant original contribution. The hierarchical grouping insight for k>3 is novel and clever, providing a new framework for understanding the structure of local maxima in GMM likelihood functions. This approach clearly differentiates the paper from previous work on GMMs and resolves an important open question in the field.

**Significance:** The results have substantial theoretical and practical significance. Theoretically, they advance our understanding of non-convex optimization landscapes in statistical models. Practically, they strongly suggest that careful initialization is essential for EM-based methods even in highly favorable settings, which could inspire new seeding strategies. The paper also identifies a promising direction for future work: extending Lemma 7 to d>1.

The paper is well-positioned to impact both theoretical and applied machine learning communities. It provides a concrete explanation for the empirical difficulties of EM in practice, even when the data comes from a well-specified GMM. The results also suggest that alternative initialization schemes (e.g., based on method of moments) may be necessary for effective GMM estimation.

The only minor limitation is that the results are confined to the special case of equal mixing weights and spherical Gaussians. However, the authors note that their results extend to large finite samples via standard empirical process arguments, and the core insights about the likelihood landscape structure are likely to be broadly applicable.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution to understanding the optimization landscape of Gaussian mixture models. It resolves an important open question, provides valuable theoretical insights with practical implications, and is presented with exceptional clarity. The results are technically sound, well-motivated, and will likely inspire further theoretical work on clustering and non-convex optimization. I strongly recommend acceptance.
