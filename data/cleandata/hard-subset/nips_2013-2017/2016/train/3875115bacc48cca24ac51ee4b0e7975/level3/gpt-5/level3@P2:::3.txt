Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 equally weighted, spherical components with known variance. It resolves a longstanding conjecture of Srebro (COLT’07) by showing that, even in this highly favorable setting and in dimension d = 1, the population log-likelihood can have bad local maxima whose values are arbitrarily worse than the global optimum (Theorem 1). The authors then analyze the EM algorithm and a first-order EM/gradient-ascent variant under a standard random-initialization scheme (initial centers sampled i.i.d. from the mixture) and prove that these procedures converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M) (Theorems 2–3). Finally, they show that first-order EM with a fixed stepsize almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points (Theorem 4), so failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.

The work connects, contrasts, and builds on: (i) classical identifiability and statistical analyses of mixtures (Teicher’63; Genovese & Wasserman’00; Ghosal & van der Vaart’01; Chen’95; Ho & Nguyen’15); (ii) algorithmic analyses of EM (Dasgupta & Schulman’07; Balakrishnan et al.’15) and alternatives (clustering/moments: Vempala & Wang’02; Arora et al.’05; Chaudhuri & Rao’08; Belkin & Sinha’10; Moitra & Valiant’10; Hsu & Kakade’13); (iii) algebraic geometry perspectives on multiplicity of local optima at finite-sample (Améndola et al.’15); and (iv) recent results on gradient dynamics and saddle points (Ge et al.’15; Lee et al.’16; Panageas & Piliouras’16; Pascanu et al.’14). The structural negative result for population likelihood is new and sharp, and the algorithmic consequences are carefully reasoned.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The structural construction for bad local maxima is plausible and seems carefully argued (the “two-close-one-far” geometry for M = 3, generalized to M ≥ 3). The argument that bad local maxima can be arbitrarily worse via separation scaling is convincing. The recursive clustering construction underlying the e−Ω(M) failure probability for random initialization is elegant and technically credible.
- Assumptions: Equal weights and known spherical covariance are favorable; proving negative results even under these assumptions strengthens the message.
- Proof coverage: Full proofs are deferred; based on the exposition, the ideas are sound. It would be very valuable to include finite-sample simulations to confirm the population phenomena and the exponential failure trend.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with useful intuition around the geometry of the likelihood. A few places need clarification (see minor edits and phrasing notes below), and some claims about practical implications could be better qualified.
- The example for M = 2 is informative but its purpose and takeaways are currently ambiguous (see comments on L74–81).

Originality
- Resolves Srebro’s conjecture negatively in the population setting for M ≥ 3 with equal weights and spherical Gaussians; to my knowledge this is new.
- The e−Ω(M) probability-of-failure result for random initialization followed by EM/gradient EM at the population level is novel and sharp.
- The “no convergence to strict saddles” for first-order EM follows the spirit of known results for gradient methods, but adapting it to this setting (and avoiding smoothness subtleties) is a useful contribution, albeit of lesser standalone impact.

Significance
- The structural result is important for our theoretical understanding of likelihood surfaces for mixtures, and it cautions against over-reliance on unstructured local search even in benign GMMs.
- The algorithmic consequences are impactful for theory. Practical impact depends on how typical the adversarial center configurations and the initialization scheme are in applications; more discussion/empirics would strengthen significance for practitioners.

Main concerns and questions for the authors
1) Practical relevance of constructions. The hard instances rely on specific “cluster-of-clusters” center layouts. How likely are such configurations under reasonable generative models for centers (e.g., centers drawn i.i.d. from a broad prior in Rd)? Can you formalize an average-case analysis showing that the probability of a “bad” configuration rises with M or d? Theorem 2 shows there exist instances with e−Ω(M) failure for random initialization, but does not quantify typical-case behavior.

2) Initialization realism. The analyzed initialization is i.i.d. draws from the mixture (population analogue of picking random data points). In datasets with clearly separated clusters, practitioners often use k-means++, PCA-based seeds, or a short k-means warm start before EM; also, sampling multiple points tends to place at least one center per cluster. Could you (a) discuss how your failure instances interact with k-means++-style seeding, and/or (b) provide experiments comparing random-seed EM to more structured initializations on your hard instances?

3) Behavior of EM when a center starts “between” clusters (L233–235). The text states as a requirement that initial centers fall within neighborhoods of true clusters and that counts match per cluster. Please justify formally why a center initialized between two far-apart clusters cannot migrate to the correct cluster (a lemma about attraction basins for the population EM map would resolve this).

4) Two-component case (L74–81). The example is helpful but the purpose is unclear. Please clarify whether it is only illustrative or intended as evidence toward the conjecture for M = 2. If the latter, better articulate why there are no local maxima beyond permutations, or temper claims and mark it as an open problem.

5) Value of the strict-saddle result (L267–273). Given existing gradient-flow results, the main message here seems confirmatory. Please sharpen the takeaway: is there a specific insight about GMM likelihood geometry or EM dynamics (e.g., non-smoothness handling) that goes beyond generic gradient methods?

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves a natural open question (Srebro’07) with a strong, clean counterexample.
- Provides sharp algorithmic consequences: EM/gradient EM with random initialization fails with probability 1 − e−Ω(M) on well-separated, equal-weight spherical mixtures, highlighting the necessity of careful initialization.
- Techniques and constructions are likely to influence future analyses of non-convex likelihoods.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical relevance is not fully established; results are worst-case and rely on structured center configurations.
- Initialization model may be too weak relative to common practice; no empirical validation comparing different initializations on the hard instances.
- The strict-saddle non-convergence result is expected given general gradient theory and adds limited incremental insight.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Strong theoretical contribution resolving a known conjecture and clarifying the landscape/EM behavior for GMMs. I encourage the authors to bolster the paper with (i) a discussion or average-case analysis for random center priors, (ii) finite-sample experiments on their hard instances, and (iii) a clearer treatment of initialization issues and the M = 2 case.
