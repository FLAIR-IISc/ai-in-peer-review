This paper investigates the structure of local maxima in the log-likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) with equal mixing weights and identity covariance, a fundamental problem in statistical estimation and clustering. The authors address a long-standing conjecture by Srebro [2007] and provide rigorous theoretical analysis on the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The work is well-motivated, tackling a core challenge in GMM estimation – the potential for getting trapped in suboptimal solutions.

The paper builds on previous work examining identifiability (Allman et al., 2009), convergence rates (Chen, 1995; Ho & Nguyen, 2015), and algorithmic guarantees for GMMs (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015). It directly addresses Srebro’s conjecture concerning the absence of bad local maxima, and extends the investigation to the algorithmic implications for EM. Related work on non-convex optimization (Loh & Wainwright, 2013; Wang et al., 2014) provides context, though the authors convincingly argue that the GMM case exhibits different behavior.

The key contributions are threefold. First, the authors construct a k=3, d=1 counterexample demonstrating that local maxima can indeed have arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global maximum, disproving Srebro’s conjecture. Second, they show that with random initialization, the probability of converging to a bad local maximum can be exponentially small in k for specific mean configurations. Third, they prove that gradient EM almost surely does not converge to a saddle point, suggesting the failure of first-order methods is attributable to bad local maxima.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proofs appear rigorous and well-structured, particularly that of Theorem 1. The proof idea for Theorem 2, involving a hierarchical grouping of Gaussian components, is insightful and seems reasonable, although a full verification of the appendix details was not possible.
*   **Originality:** The connection between mean configuration and the existence of bad local maxima is novel. The hierarchical grouping insight for k>3 is clever and crucial for demonstrating the exponential dependence on the number of components.
*   **Significance:** The findings have potential to inspire further theoretical work on the geometry of clustering optima and the design of more robust initialization strategies for EM. The suggestion to extend Lemma 7 to d>1 is a valuable direction for future research.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem setup and interpretations of the results are easy to follow. The authors effectively contextualize their work within existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   While the theoretical results are strong, the limited dimensionality (d=1 for the counterexample) might restrict the direct applicability of the findings to higher-dimensional data. The extension to d>1 remains an open question.
*   The random initialization scheme analyzed, while practical, might not be the most common or effective in real-world applications. Exploring other initialization strategies could yield valuable insights.
*   The focus remains heavily on the idealized population setting. While empirical process arguments are mentioned, a more detailed discussion of finite-sample implications would strengthen the practical relevance of the results.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper resolves an important open problem in the theoretical analysis of GMMs.
*   The results are technically sound and provide novel insights into the landscape of the likelihood function.
*   The work has the potential to influence future research in both theoretical statistics and machine learning.
*   The clarity of presentation and strong motivation make the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The dimensionality limitation of the counterexample might raise concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
*   The analysis focuses on a specific random initialization scheme, potentially limiting the practical impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of Gaussian Mixture Models. The authors have addressed a challenging problem with rigorous analysis and insightful results. Despite the limitations regarding dimensionality and initialization schemes, the theoretical importance and clarity of presentation outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** to NIPS.



**Minor Suggestions:**

*   In the conclusion, briefly discuss the implications of the results for other optimization algorithms used in GMM estimation, beyond EM and gradient ascent.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of the computational complexity of finding global optima for GMMs, given the existence of bad local maxima.
*   Clarify the relationship between the step size 's' in the first-order EM algorithm and the convergence properties discussed in Theorem 4.
