

This paper addresses an important open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the structure of local maxima in the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors demonstrate that for M ≥ 3 components with equal weights and identity covariance, the log-likelihood can contain local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global optimum (Theorem 1), resolving Srebro's conjecture in the negative. They further show that with random initialization, the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) converges to these bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)) (Theorem 2), while proving that gradient EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points (Theorem 4).

The paper excels in technical quality, with Theorem 1's proofs appearing sound and Theorem 2's proof strategy being well-motivated. The novelty is significant: the hierarchical grouping insight for k>3 components provides a clever construction that reveals the exponential failure probability, and the connection between mean configuration and local maxima behavior is original. The clarity is excellent, with well-explained results, clear problem setup, and intuitive interpretations of the counterexamples.

The significance is substantial, as these findings directly impact practical GMM estimation. The results suggest that careful initialization strategies (beyond random sampling) are essential even in idealized settings, potentially guiding future work on seeding methods. The paper's implications for understanding the theoretical limits of EM and gradient-based methods in non-convex optimization are noteworthy.

A minor limitation is that the results are confined to d=1 for the counterexample, though the authors appropriately suggest Lemma 7's extension to d>1 as an open question. The paper also appropriately acknowledges that their counterexample requires M≥3, leaving open the two-component case.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes. The paper's clear presentation, sound technical results, and practical implications make it a valuable addition to the literature. The results directly address a long-standing open question and provide important theoretical insights with practical consequences for GMM estimation. I recommend acceptance.
