

This paper addresses an important open question in the theoretical analysis of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, specifically resolving a conjecture by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function. The authors prove three significant results: (i) for M ≥ 3 components, arbitrarily bad local maxima exist even in the idealized setting of equally-weighted, well-separated, spherical Gaussians; (ii) EM with random initialization converges to such suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 - e^{-Ω(M)}; and (iii) a first-order variant of EM exhibits the same behavior. This work provides crucial theoretical justification for the practical observation that careful initialization is necessary when applying EM to GMMs, even under favorable conditions.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the non-convex optimization landscape of GMMs, which has been a persistent challenge in both theory and practice. Previous work by Balakrishnan et al. (2015) established local convergence guarantees for two-component GMMs, while Dasgupta and Schulman (2007) analyzed a two-round EM variant. The authors' results extend this line of work by demonstrating that even in the most favorable settings, local search methods can fail catastrophically, with failure probability exponentially small in M. This directly addresses a long-standing open question in the literature and provides a theoretical foundation for the practical observation that random initialization often fails for GMMs with more than two components.

The paper's strengths include its clear problem formulation, the significance of its results, and its careful distinction between local maxima and saddle points (via Theorem 4, which shows first-order EM avoids strict saddle points almost surely). The authors successfully demonstrate that the poor performance of local search methods stems from bad local maxima rather than saddle points, a subtle but important distinction. The simplified model (known number of components, uniform weights, identity covariances) is well-justified given the technical complexity of the analysis and the focus on structural properties of the likelihood surface.

However, the paper has two main weaknesses. First, the proof of Theorem 1 (existence of bad local maxima) is insufficiently detailed. The authors claim convergence of certain limits in lines 288-306, but these claims require more rigorous justification. Specifically, the arguments about supremums and maximums over unbounded domains or parameter-dependent sets need stronger theoretical backing than the current continuity-based reasoning provides. This is a critical gap that undermines the main structural result.

Second, the gradient EM section (lines 162-172) is difficult to follow, particularly the derivation of Equation (5) from the general first-order EM update. The authors should provide more detailed intermediate steps to clarify how the gradient ascent update relates to the population likelihood.

The paper is well-written overall, with clear exposition of the key results and their implications. The authors appropriately situate their work within the NIPS literature, citing relevant prior work on GMMs, EM convergence, and non-convex optimization. The significance of the results is clear: they provide a theoretical foundation for understanding the practical limitations of EM in GMM estimation, which has important implications for both practitioners and theoreticians.

Regarding the dimensionality question, the authors state their results hold for general d but provide proofs only for d=1. They should explicitly discuss whether their results extend to d>1 or explain the additional difficulties in higher dimensions.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by resolving a long-standing open question with significant implications for understanding the behavior of EM in GMMs. The results are technically sound (with the exception of the incomplete proof of Theorem 1), and the analysis is thorough and well-motivated. The simplifications to the model are justified given the focus on structural properties rather than practical estimation algorithms.

Pro: Resolves a significant open question in GMM theory; provides strong theoretical justification for careful initialization in EM; demonstrates the failure probability is exponentially small in M; distinguishes between bad local maxima and saddle points.

Con: Proof of Theorem 1 is insufficiently detailed; gradient EM derivation is unclear; lacks discussion of extension to d>1.

The paper's contributions are substantial and well-motivated, and the results are important for understanding the limitations of EM in GMM estimation. While the proof of Theorem 1 requires strengthening, the core results are compelling and address a fundamental question in the field. The paper is suitable for NIPS as it makes a clear theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of a widely-used algorithm in machine learning. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the proof concerns.
