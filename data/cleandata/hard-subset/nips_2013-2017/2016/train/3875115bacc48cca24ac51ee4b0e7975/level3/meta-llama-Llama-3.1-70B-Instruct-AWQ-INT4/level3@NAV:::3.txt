This paper studies the local maxima in the log-likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and their implications on the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors provide two main results: (1) the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population log-likelihood of GMMs, even with infinite data, and (2) the EM algorithm with random initialization converges to bad critical points with high probability.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem, including the background and previous work on GMMs and the EM algorithm. The main results are clearly stated and proved, and the implications of these results are discussed in detail.

The first result, Theorem 1, shows that for any M ≥ 3, there exists a well-separated uniform mixture of M unit-variance spherical Gaussians for which the population log-likelihood has a bad local maximum whose log-likelihood is arbitrarily worse than that of the global maximum. This result is surprising, as it contradicts the intuition that the EM algorithm should converge to the global maximum with high probability.

The second result, Theorem 2, shows that the EM algorithm with random initialization converges to a bad critical point with high probability. Specifically, the authors show that for any M ≥ 3 and any constant Cgap > 0, there exists a well-separated uniform mixture of M unit-variance spherical Gaussians for which the probability of convergence to a bad critical point is at least 1 - e^(-cM), where c is a universal constant.

The authors also provide a result on the first-order EM algorithm, Theorem 3, which shows that the algorithm converges to a bad critical point with high probability, even with a small step size. Additionally, they show that the first-order EM algorithm cannot converge to strict saddle points with probability one, Theorem 4.

The paper raises several interesting questions and open problems, including the necessity of at least three mixture components for the log-likelihood to be poorly behaved and the empirical success of EM-style algorithms using random initialization schemes despite their failure on seemingly benign problem instances.

The related work is adequately referenced, and the paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.
