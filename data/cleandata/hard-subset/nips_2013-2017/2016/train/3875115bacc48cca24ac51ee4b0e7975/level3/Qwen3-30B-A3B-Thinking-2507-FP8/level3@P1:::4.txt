

This paper addresses a fundamental question in Gaussian mixture model (GMM) theory: whether the population likelihood function for GMMs with M ≥ 3 components can have "bad" local maxima (i.e., local maxima with arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global maximum), resolving an open question posed by Srebro (2007). The authors provide two main theoretical contributions that significantly advance our understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces and the behavior of optimization algorithms like EM.

The first contribution (Theorem 1) constructs a counterexample for M=3, d=1 showing that well-separated, equally-weighted GMMs can indeed have local maxima with arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global maximum. This directly refutes Srebro's conjecture that all local maxima would be globally optimal in the population setting. The authors provide a clear geometric intuition: when true centers are arranged such that two are close together while the third is far away, a local maximum can occur when the two closest centers are merged while the third is duplicated. The proof establishes this by defining a neighborhood around the suboptimal configuration that excludes global maxima and showing the likelihood is strictly smaller on the boundary.

The second contribution (Theorem 2) demonstrates that the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to bad critical points with exponentially high probability in M. The key insight is a hierarchical grouping construction where true centers form nested clusters, making favorable initialization exponentially unlikely. This shows that even with well-separated Gaussians, random initialization fails with probability 1-e^(-Ω(M)), meaning practitioners would need exponentially many restarts to find good solutions. This result is particularly significant as it resolves the practical concern about whether bad local maxima actually trap algorithms in practice.

The authors further prove (Theorem 4) that the first-order EM algorithm almost surely avoids strict saddle points (using recent results on gradient methods), indicating that the failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points. This distinction is important for understanding the nature of the optimization landscape.

The paper's quality is strong: the proofs for Theorem 1 appear sound, and Theorem 2's proof sketch is convincing though details are deferred to the appendix. The authors carefully distinguish between population and sample settings, and their results extend straightforwardly to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments.

The originality is high: while Srebro's conjecture had been widely assumed, the authors provide the first counterexample, and their hierarchical grouping construction for Theorem 2 is novel. The work builds on Balakrishnan et al. (2015), which analyzed two-component GMMs, but extends it to the more challenging M≥3 case where the problem becomes non-trivial.

The significance is substantial: this work explains why random initialization often fails for EM in practice, even in seemingly favorable settings, and provides theoretical justification for the use of method-of-moments initialization schemes (e.g., Moitra & Valiant, 2010; Hsu & Kakade, 2013). It also contributes to the broader understanding of non-convex optimization landscapes, showing that bad local maxima can persist even in well-separated, structured problems.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with excellent exposition of the key ideas. The geometric intuition for the counterexample is particularly effective, and the connection between mean configuration and local maxima is well-motivated.

Some minor limitations: the results are established for d=1 (with the authors noting the extension to d>1 would require Lemma 7 to hold for higher dimensions, which remains an open question). The paper focuses on the population setting, though the authors note the results extend to finite samples.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs and non-convex optimization. It resolves an important open question, provides practical insights about algorithm initialization, and demonstrates a clever technique for constructing counterexamples to conjectures about optimization landscapes. The results have clear implications for practitioners and theoretical researchers alike, explaining why seemingly simple problems can be challenging for standard optimization methods.

The paper is technically sound, original, and addresses a significant question in the field. It advances the state of the art in understanding the optimization landscape of GMMs and provides concrete theoretical justification for the use of more sophisticated initialization strategies. The work is well-motivated, well-executed, and well-written, making it an excellent contribution to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
