This paper investigates the structure of the population log-likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), a topic of long-standing interest in the field. The authors focus on the well-specified setting of isotropic GMMs with known variance. The primary contribution is a negative resolution to a conjecture by Srebro (2007), demonstrating that the GMM population log-likelihood can possess arbitrarily bad local maxima, even for well-separated mixtures with three or more components. A key algorithmic consequence is then proven: for specific GMM configurations, the EM algorithm with random initialization is shown to converge to these suboptimal critical points with a probability approaching one as the number of components M increases.

The paper's main strength lies in its significant theoretical contribution. Resolving an open conjecture is a noteworthy achievement, and the results provide a crisp, albeit negative, characterization of the GMM likelihood landscape. The analysis is technically sound, and the construction of the "bad" GMM instances is clear. The finding that random initialization leads to failure with high probability for these instances is a strong statement that highlights the fundamental necessity of careful initialization for EM, challenging the notion that large sample sizes alone can mitigate the non-convexity of the problem. The writing is generally clear and well-organized.

However, I have two main reservations concerning the practical implications of the results. First, the GMM configurations constructed to prove the existence of bad local maxima—where true centers are grouped into hierarchically separated clusters—seem highly specific. It is unclear how prevalent such structures are in real-world problems. The paper would be strengthened if the authors could analyze the probability of these "bad" configurations under a reasonable prior on the true centers, perhaps showing this probability grows with M or the dimension d. Second, the analysis of random initialization, while common, may not reflect practical use. In cases where data are clearly clustered, practitioners often use more sophisticated initializations (e.g., k-means++), which might avoid the failure modes described. For instance, with two close components and one far component, it seems unlikely a random draw from the data would initialize centers in the problematic way the paper requires.

There are also several points that would benefit from clarification:
-   The purpose of the k=2 example (L74–81) is unclear, as it does not appear to resolve the conjecture for this case.
-   In the proof sketch (L233–235), the necessity of requirement (1) is not obvious. Why would a center initialized between clusters not be pulled towards the correct one?
-   The practical importance of distinguishing convergence to a saddle point versus a local optimum (L267–273) could be better motivated.
-   Minor phrasing and notation issues should be addressed for clarity (e.g., L218 "well-separated constant", L225 definition of Ω(k), and typos listed in the detailed comments).

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper, but its claims on practical, algorithmic consequences should be contextualized more carefully.
