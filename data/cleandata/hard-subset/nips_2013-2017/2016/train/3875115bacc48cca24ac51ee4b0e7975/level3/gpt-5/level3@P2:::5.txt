Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper resolves a long-standing conjecture due to Srebro (2007) by proving that, in the population (infinite-sample) setting, the log-likelihood surface of equally weighted, spherical, well-separated Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components admits spurious local maxima whose values can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. The authors construct explicit 1D examples (with generalization to arbitrary M) and give geometric intuition via the M = 3 case, where two true means are close and the third is far; they identify a neighborhood that excludes global optima but contains a local maximum near a configuration that merges the nearby pair and duplicates the far center.

Algorithmically, they analyze EM and a first-order variant (gradient EM), showing that with standard random initialization (i.i.d. from the mixture), both methods converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). A recursive clustering construction yields the exponentially small success probability, implying that naive restart strategies require exponentially many restarts in M. They further prove that first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, leveraging recent results on gradient methods (e.g., Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016), hence the failures stem from bad local maxima rather than saddles.

This work complements prior positive results on EM’s local convergence under good initialization and separation (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015 for two-component mixtures), and contrasts with finite-sample pathologies (e.g., Améndola et al., 2015), by showing that even in the population limit and in a favorable isotropic/equal-weights regime, bad local maxima persist. It also refines the narrative around nonconvexity in high dimensions (e.g., Pascanu et al., 2014), emphasizing the algorithmic consequences for GMMs.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The statements are precise; the geometric construction and the recursive argument for exponentially small success probability are convincing and align with theorems. The strict-saddle avoidance result is carefully framed for first-order EM. I did not fully verify all proofs in the appendix.
- Claims vs. evidence: The population-level results are clearly supported by proofs. The sample-level implications are discussed qualitatively (via standard empirical process arguments) but not quantified.
- Missing constants: The bounds e−Ω(M) and separation conditions are stated asymptotically; explicit constants (e.g., c in the exponent, required gaps/separations as functions of M and d) are not provided in the main text.

Clarity:
- The paper is well written and well organized. The M = 3, d = 1 construction and accompanying intuition are helpful.
- Suggestions to improve clarity:
  - State explicit dependencies for “well-separated” (the ξ needed in terms of d, M, and target Cgap).
  - Provide a schematic/figure of the recursive cluster construction used in Theorem 2 to make the exponentially small success probability intuitive.
  - Summarize in the main text the key lemmas used to show that EM trajectories remain trapped within clusters once initialized there.

Originality:
- The negative resolution of Srebro’s conjecture in the population setting for well-separated, equal-weight, spherical GMMs is novel and important.
- The exponentially small success probability for EM/gradient EM with natural initialization and the strict-saddle avoidance result together give a clear and original algorithmic message.

Significance:
- Conceptual impact: High. The results reshape expectations about the benignity of the GMM likelihood at the population level and stress the necessity of careful initialization or alternative estimators.
- Practical implications: Strong motivation for informed initializations (e.g., moments, spectral, or clustering-based) and against naive random restarts in high-M regimes.
- Broader relevance: Useful for the nonconvex optimization community as a clear case where local maxima—not saddles—are the main obstruction.

Pros:
- Resolves an open question with a clean, transparent counterexample.
- Sharp algorithmic consequences: EM and gradient EM fail with high probability under standard random initialization.
- Clear, well-argued link between structure of the likelihood surface and algorithmic behavior.
- Careful discussion situating the work relative to EM analyses and saddle-point literature.

Cons:
- Lack of explicit constants obscures when the negative phenomena appear for realistic M (e.g., M ≲ 10–50).
- No simulations to illustrate the phase transition in success probability or to visualize the trapping effect under random initialization.
- Scope limited to equal weights and spherical covariances; implications for mild deviations (unequal weights, anisotropy, variance misspecification) are not explored.
- Sample-level finite-n rates and the size of neighborhoods where population-to-sample transfer holds are not quantified.

Requests and questions for the authors:
- Please provide explicit constants or at least numerically evaluable bounds for:
  - The separation ξ required in Theorem 1 as a function of d, M, and desired Cgap.
  - The exponent c in 1 − e−cM for Theorems 2–3 for concrete parameter choices.
  - Step-size ranges for first-order EM beyond s ∈ (0, 1) that preserve the negative results.
- If constants are intricate, add simulations demonstrating:
  - Success probability vs. M under the proposed random initialization.
  - Typical log-likelihood gaps at the suboptimal fixed points.
  - Sensitivity to small deviations from equal weights or spherical covariances.
- Can you quantify the finite-sample regime (n vs. d, M, separation) where the empirical likelihood inherits the population bad local maxima with high probability?
- Do analogous constructions hold for two components under different covariance structures, or can you strengthen the conjecture that M = 2 is benign?
- How do widely used initializations (e.g., k-means++, splitting heuristics, spectral/moment-based pilots) fare on your hard instances?

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves a notable open problem; strong theoretical contribution with clear algorithmic implications.
- High clarity and accessible intuition.
- Advances understanding of nonconvexity in a central model class.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing quantitative constants and empirical support make it hard to assess practical impact thresholds.
- Limited exploration of robustness beyond equal-weight/spherical settings.

Overall recommendation:
Strong accept, contingent only on adding either explicit constants or supportive simulations to calibrate the phenomenon for moderate M. The paper’s novelty and conceptual significance outweigh the noted omissions, and the requested additions are feasible within a conference revision. Confidence: high.
