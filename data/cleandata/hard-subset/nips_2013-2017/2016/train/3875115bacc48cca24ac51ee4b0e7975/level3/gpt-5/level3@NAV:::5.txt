This paper settles Srebro’s 2007 conjecture in the negative by constructing a general class of population-level counterexamples for GMMs with M ≥ 3 spherical, equally weighted components. The authors outline likelihood-maximization schemes (EM and gradient/first-order EM), analyze a practical “sample-from-data” random initialization, and prove that both EM and gradient EM converge to suboptimal critical points with probability 1 − e−Ω(M). They further show first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, implicating genuinely bad local maxima as the culprit. The manuscript provides clear intuition and a detailed proof in the simple case k = 3, d = 1, and relates to prior analyses of EM’s local convergence (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and recent results on saddle-point avoidance (Pascanu et al., 2014; Lee et al., 2016).

Quality: The theoretical development appears careful and technically sound, with a crisp recursive construction underpinning the exponentially small success probability under common random initialization. I did not fully verify the supplementary proofs; however, the population-level arguments are coherent and consistent with the claims.

Clarity: The paper is well written and well organized. The geometric intuition for M = 3, d = 1 is especially helpful. A schematic of the recursive clustering used in Theorem 2 would further aid comprehension.

Originality: Resolving Srebro’s open question and separating saddle points from bad local maxima for GMM likelihood are novel and important contributions. The work is well positioned relative to EM analyses and moment/spectral alternatives.

Significance: The results have clear implications for non-convex optimization and for the practice of EM in mixture models, even asymptotically. They motivate principled initializations (e.g., MoM/spectral pilots) over naive random starts.

Requests/suggestions:
- Please provide estimates or at least explicit dependencies for constants (e.g., c in 1 − e−cM, separation parameters, and the Cgap gap). These constants are crucial to pinpoint phase transitions and the regime where bad-solution convergence appears for realistic M.
- If constants are intricate or loose, include supporting simulations illustrating the phenomenon and finite-sample manifestations (how large n and what separations are needed).
- Discuss performance under alternative common initializations (e.g., k-means++), and comment on the open M = 2 case.

Pros:
- Resolves a long-standing conjecture; strong theoretical novelty.
- Clear writing; good intuition for simple cases.
- Important implications for EM and non-convex optimization.

Cons:
- No estimates of constants and limited empirical illustration.
- Results rely on population setting and adversarial constructions; guidance for finite-sample, practical M would help.
