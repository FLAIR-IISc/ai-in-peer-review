- Main contribution: analyzes population likelihood of equally weighted spherical GMMs and proves EM can fail to reach global maxima, resolving Srebro’s 2007 conjecture by constructing, for M ≥ 3, well-separated mixtures with arbitrarily bad local maxima.  
- Algorithmic result: EM and gradient/first-order EM with random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − exp(−Ω(M)); gradient EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, so bad local maxima (not saddles) are the fundamental obstruction.  
- Constructions/examples: key 3-component example (two nearby means, one far) and a recursive clustering construction mirror known k-means/Lloyd failure modes and yield the claimed exponential failure probabilities.  
- Technical quality: results are technically sound and carefully argued; the recursion giving exp(−Ω(M)) success is compelling.  
- Finite-sample gap: paper focuses on population-level; it should explicitly state finite-sample transfer (rates, sample sizes, dependence on separation and dimension) instead of relying on “standard empirical process” remarks.  
- Separation clarity: need to spell out separation thresholds (e.g., ≫ √d) required for the “arbitrarily bad gap” and make likelihood-gap dependence on separation explicit.  
- Clarity improvements: well organized overall but would benefit from an intuitive figure for the recursive instance and a tighter summary of assumptions (equal weights, known unit variance, step-size ranges).  
- Originality: while constructions align with k-means folklore, the paper offers a coherent, definitive resolution of the conjecture and a sharp exponential failure bound.  
- Significance: high for EM theory and practitioners relying on random restarts; directs attention to initialization and seeding as key issues.  
- Suggested comparisons: compare to Lloyd’s guarantees under large separation (Kumar–Kannan), discuss k-means++ or overseeding/pruning as possible mitigations, and contrast with spectral/moment initializations and two-round EM approaches.  
- Pros: resolves a long-standing conjecture with clean constructions, gives strong quantitative failure probabilities, and clarifies that bad local maxima are the bottleneck.  
- Cons: techniques and counterexamples are largely expected (consolidation over surprise), limited finite-sample analysis, and missing comparison to Lloyd’s/k-means separation guarantees.
