This paper investigates the structure of the population likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) in the simplified setting where the number of components, mixture weights, and covariances are known and fixed. The authors provide a negative answer to a conjecture by Srebro (2007), which posited that no "bad" local maxima exist in the population likelihood of well-behaved GMMs. The main contributions are three theoretical results: (i) a proof that local maxima with likelihoods arbitrarily worse than the global maximum can exist, even for well-separated components; (ii) a proof that standard EM with random initialization converges to these suboptimal critical points with high probability; and (iii) an extension of this finding to gradient-based EM. These results add important context to the literature, which has often focused on local convergence guarantees or required sophisticated initialization schemes.

The paper’s primary strength is that it addresses a fundamental and interesting question about the behavior of EM, one of the most widely used algorithms in machine learning. The theoretical results are strong and counter-intuitive, providing a clear and informative answer that deepens our understanding of the challenges of likelihood maximization in GMMs. While the analysis is restricted to a simplified model (e.g., identity covariances), this is an acceptable simplification given the technical depth of the problem. The findings have significant practical implications, underscoring the necessity of robust initialization strategies over simple random sampling.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses that should be addressed. My major concern is the rigor of the proof for Theorem 1 in the main text. The argument in lines 288–306, which involves taking limits of suprema over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains, is too brief. The justification that convergence follows from continuity is insufficient and requires a much more detailed argument. As the central existence result, this proof needs to be completely convincing on its own. I suggest the authors expand this section, even at the expense of other discussions. (Note: I did not review the supplementary material, as the main paper should be largely self-contained).

Two other points require attention. First, the paper is written for a general dimension *d*, but the proofs appear to be given for *d* = 1. The authors should clarify whether the proofs extend directly to *d* > 1 or discuss the additional technical difficulties involved. Second, the section on gradient EM (lines 162–172) is hard to follow. A clearer explanation of how Equation (5) is derived would improve the paper’s clarity.
