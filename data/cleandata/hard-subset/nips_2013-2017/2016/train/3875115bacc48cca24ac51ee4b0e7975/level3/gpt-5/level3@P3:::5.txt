Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood landscape of equally weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components. It answers a conjecture of Srebro (2007) in the negative by constructing a general class of well-separated mixtures whose population likelihood possesses bad local maxima with arbitrarily large suboptimality gaps. The authors then analyze the algorithmic implications for likelihood-maximizing schemes—EM and gradient EM—under a widely used “random-from-data” initialization that, at the population level, is equivalent to drawing initial centers i.i.d. from the true mixture.

The primary results are: (1) structural counterexamples showing arbitrarily bad local maxima already in d=1 for M≥3 (Theorem 1); (2) with common random initialization, both EM and gradient EM converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^−Ω(M) (Theorems 2–3); and (3) gradient EM does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely (Theorem 4), implying that failures are due to bad local maxima, not saddles. The paper provides clear intuition and a detailed proof for the simplest nontrivial case (k=3, d=1), and extends via a recursive, well-separated clustering construction to general M.

This work complements local convergence results that require good initializations (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and contrasts with algorithmic approaches that avoid local search (clustering- and moment-based methods, e.g., Vempala & Wang 2002; Moitra & Valiant 2010; Hsu & Kakade 2013). The “strict saddles vs. bad local maxima” theme connects to Pascanu et al. (2014) and leverages gradient-dynamics insights (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas & Piliouras, 2016).

Strengths
- Resolves a long-standing conjecture (Srebro, 2007) by constructing a broad class of counterexamples; the suboptimality gap can be made arbitrarily large even with uniform weights, unit variance, and strong separation.
- Clear algorithmic consequences: EM and gradient EM with common random initialization converge to non-optimal points with exponentially high probability in M.
- Clean, intuitive exposition, including geometric intuition and a complete proof for k=3, d=1; balanced use of space with accessible explanations.
- Careful distinction between failure due to local maxima versus saddle points; Theorem 4 is particularly informative for the optimization community.
- Important implications for non-convex optimization and practice: naïve random initialization can be provably inadequate even in asymptotically favorable settings.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Constants and thresholds are opaque: the e^−Ω(M) bounds and separation requirements are asymptotic. For practical guidance, explicit constants (c in the exponent; separation as a function of d and M; neighborhood radii in the 3-component construction) would help identify phase transitions—i.e., for what k and separation regimes “bad” convergence becomes likely in practice.
- The reviewer did not fully verify the supplementary material; please surface in the main text at least rough estimates of the constants and their dependence on M and d.
- If constants are intricate or unwieldy, provide supporting simulations. Minimal experiments could include:
  - 1D and moderate-d settings showing the success probability of EM/gradient EM vs. M under data-based random initialization.
  - Sensitivity to separation and to the recursive cluster depth.
  - Comparison with a few practical initializations (e.g., k-means++, moment-based warm starts) to illustrate how many random restarts are needed to overcome the exponential failure probability.
- Clarify the allowable step-size range for gradient EM beyond s ∈ (0,1): how tight is this for the constructions, and does the phenomenon persist with line search?
- Discuss the two-component case: while still open, can the techniques offer partial evidence that M≥3 is necessary?

Quality
Technically solid and self-contained at the population level. The structural construction and the recursive argument driving the e^−Ω(M) probability are convincing. The no-convergence-to-strict-saddles result for gradient EM is carefully argued. The main gap is the lack of explicit constants and practical illustrations quantifying where the theoretical bad behavior emerges for realistic M and d.

Clarity
Very well written and organized. The introduction situates the contribution within statistical and algorithmic literatures; Section 2 nicely outlines EM and gradient EM, and the random-from-data initialization. The k=3, d=1 intuition is especially helpful. A figure or two illustrating the recursive construction and the attraction basins would further enhance accessibility.

Originality
High. The paper not only settles Srebro’s conjecture in the negative but also ties structural landscape results to concrete algorithmic consequences for EM and gradient EM under a standard initialization. The avoidance-of-saddles insight in this GMM setting is a valuable addition.

Significance
High. The results sharpen our understanding of non-convex likelihood landscapes and the limitations of widely used local-search estimators, even in the infinite-sample limit for well-separated GMMs. They underscore the necessity of principled initialization (e.g., spectral/moment-based) and will likely influence both theory and practice.

Reasons to accept
- Resolves an open question with a general class of counterexamples.
- Strong, interpretable algorithmic implications with exponential-in-M failure probabilities.
- Clear writing with intuition and complete proof for the simplest case.
- Advances understanding of the role of local maxima vs. saddles in EM-like methods.

Reasons to reject
- Lack of explicit constants and practical thresholds; no empirical illustrations quantifying when the asymptotic phenomena manifest for moderate M and d.
- Some technical details deferred to the supplement could be surfaced to improve reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide explicit constants c in 1 − e^−cM and minimal separation requirements (in closed form or numerically) for the k=3, d=1 case, and for your recursive construction?
- How does the required separation scale with d? Are there dimension-dependent constants that materially affect the failure probability?
- For gradient EM, does a broader range of step sizes or line search change the conclusions?
- Could you add small-scale simulations demonstrating the exponential decay in success probability and the effect of separation?
- Do the results extend (qualitatively) to slight model misspecification?

Overall assessment
A strong and impactful paper that is clear, rigorous, and timely. While explicit constants and light empirical support would improve practical interpretability, the core contributions stand on their own. I recommend acceptance.
