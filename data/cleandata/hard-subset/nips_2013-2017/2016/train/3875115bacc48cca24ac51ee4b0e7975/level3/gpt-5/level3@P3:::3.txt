Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood of equally weighted, isotropic Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with known variance and provides two main results. First, it disproves Srebro’s 2007 conjecture by exhibiting, for any M ≥ 3, well-separated 1-D instances with arbitrarily bad local maxima (Theorem 1). Second, it shows that EM (and a first-order EM/gradient-ascent variant) with random initialization converges to a suboptimal critical point with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M) on a family of hierarchical, well-separated instances (Theorems 2–3). Theorem 4 further shows first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, so failure stems from bad local maxima rather than saddles.

The work connects to a long line of research on identifiability and estimation for mixtures, the practical and theoretical behavior of EM (e.g., Dasgupta–Schulman, Balakrishnan et al.), and algorithmic alternatives (clustering- and moment-based methods). It sharpens finite-sample constructions with many local optima (Améndola et al.) by giving population-level negative results in a favorable setting.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The constructions and arguments are carefully laid out, and the hierarchical instance in Theorem 2 convincingly drives the exponentially small success probability under the authors’ random initialization model. The strict-saddle avoidance result for first-order EM is consistent with recent dynamical systems analyses.
- Completeness: The population-level theory is complete for the stated initialization and model class. However, the practical import depends on how typical the adversarial center configurations and the initialization scheme are in realistic workflows.

Clarity
- The writing is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful intuition around the constructions. There are several places where phrasing/notation could be clarified (see detailed comments and minor edits below).

Originality
- Resolving Srebro’s conjecture (negatively) at the population level for well-specified, well-separated, isotropic, equal-weight GMMs is novel and important. The exponentially small success probability for random initialization is also a strong and original statement.

Significance
- The results shift our understanding of the landscape of GMM likelihoods and the limits of naive EM initialization. They motivate robust initialization strategies. The main question is practical prevalence: how often do such “bad” instances arise under natural generative models, and under standard practical initializations?

Major comments and requests
1) Practical relevance of “bad” center configurations. The paper constructs adversarial configurations. To assess prevalence:
- Please assume a distribution over the true centers and quantify the probability of configurations that provoke bad local maxima or trap EM, as M or d grows. Examples:
  - Centers i.i.d. from N(0, τ2 I). Analyze the probability of multiscale cluster trees with separations comparable to the authors’ thresholds. Extreme value/order statistics plus concentration could bound the chance of a two-way split with gap g at top level, and recursively for Ω(log M) levels; argue monotone increase with M (and characterize dependence on d and τ).
  - Centers uniform in [0, 1]d (or a Poisson point process). Use nearest-neighbor spacing distributions to bound the event that there exist two mini-clusters separated by ≥ c√d while within-cluster spacings are ≤ c′. Show that the probability of at least one such multiscale configuration grows with M (and discuss how the well-separation requirement scales with d).
- Clarify whether your well-separation thresholds are compatible with these priors as d increases; if they require separation growing with √d, is the “bad config” event still non-negligible?

2) Initialization realism. Theorems 2–3 consider sampling M initial centers i.i.d. from the mixture distribution. In practice, when clusters are visible, practitioners often use k-means++, farthest-first, PCA-based seeding, or oversample and prune. To strengthen the message:
- Empirically test whether your failure persists for k-means++ initialization (population and finite-sample), and for oversampling (e.g., O(M log M) seeds then prune).
- Provide finite-M simulations for small M (e.g., M = 3) illustrating whether the “two-close-one-far” pattern actually causes the problematic initialization outcomes; a thought experiment suggests random sampling may already place one seed near each cluster with nontrivial probability.
- Discuss whether your exponential failure bounds extend to these more realistic seeding schemes, or delineate the boundary where they do not.

3) L74–81 example (M = 2). The visualization is helpful but does not substantiate (nor refute) the conjecture for M = 2. Either (a) provide a formal partial result (e.g., ruling out bad local maxima under specified separations/symmetries), or (b) tighten the text to be clearly illustrative only.

4) L233–235, requirement (1). The statement “all initialized centers fall in the neighborhood of exactly one cluster of true centers” seems unnecessary or contradictory to success; one expects at least one initialized center per cluster. Please clarify intent. Also, discuss dynamics for a center initialized between clusters: under the E-step, responsibilities bias it toward the nearer cluster—why would it not move to the correct cluster over iterations?

5) L267–273 (saddle points). Theorem 4 is technically nice but its practical import is modest relative to the main message. Consider reframing as supportive evidence that poor local maxima, not saddles, drive failures, and briefly discuss whether a similar statement might hold for EM proper (and what technical obstacles prevent proving it).

Phrasing/notation clarifications
- L218 “well-separated constant” is unclear. Do you mean “well-separated if min i≠j ∥µi − µj∥ ≥ ξ with ξ = Ω(√d)”? State ξ explicitly and whether it is absolute or dimension-dependent.
- L225 What does Ω(k) denote here? If you mean the number of hierarchical levels, it later appears as Ω(log M). Please standardize.

Pros
- Resolves a notable open conjecture with a clean, population-level counterexample.
- Strong, clear constructions; tight probability bound (exponential in M) for failure of random initialization.
- Careful analysis distinguishing local maxima from strict saddles for first-order EM.
- Broad relevance to nonconvex learning and EM practice.

Cons
- Practical prevalence of the bad instances under natural priors on centers is not quantified.
- Initialization model may be less realistic than common practice; no experiments to assess robustness under k-means++/oversampling or finite-sample EM.
- Some confusing phrasing/notation and minor typos.

Recommendation
This is an important and well-executed theoretical paper. If the authors can address the practical relevance concerns—ideally by quantifying the probability of bad configurations under reasonable priors and by evaluating more realistic initializations—this would be a strong accept. As is, I lean toward accept on theoretical significance, with the above additions strongly encouraged.
