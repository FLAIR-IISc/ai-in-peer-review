- Constructs GMM counterexamples (for any M ≥ 3) showing population log-likelihood can have arbitrarily bad local maxima, answering Srebro (2007) negatively.
- Provides clear geometric intuition for M=3 and a general rigorous proof for all M ≥ 3.
- Shows EM and a first-order (gradient-ascent) variant, with random initialization from the data distribution, converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 - e^{-cM}.
- Concludes exponential-in-M random restarts are required to find the global optimum with constant probability under the given initialization.
- Uses recent gradient-method results to argue first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, implicating bad local maxima—not saddles—as the cause of failure.
- Theoretical analysis is sound and proofs appear rigorous.
- Manuscript is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and balances technical detail with intuition.
- Originality is high: resolves a long-standing open question and offers new insight into non-convex landscapes for GMMs.
- Significance is substantial: challenges optimism about benign non-convexity and highlights the necessity of sophisticated initialization methods.
- Suggests providing characterization of the universal constant c (e.g., dependence on separation or M) to assess practical regimes where failure matters.
- Recommends a small simulation (e.g., M=3 basins of attraction) if analytic characterization of c is difficult, to illustrate constants and practical impact.
- Acceptance arguments: definitive theoretical resolution, strong algorithmic implications, technical strength, and high-quality exposition.
- Rejection argument: practical relevance of the high-probability failure is somewhat abstract without values/empirical evidence for the constants.
