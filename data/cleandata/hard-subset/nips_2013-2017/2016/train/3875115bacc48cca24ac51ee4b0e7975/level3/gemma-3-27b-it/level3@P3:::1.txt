This paper investigates the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), specifically focusing on the existence of bad local optima even with infinite data. The authors demonstrate, counter to a conjecture by Srebro [2007], that such bad local maxima exist and provide bounds on the probability of the EM algorithm converging to them. They also show that, for first-order variants of EM, saddle points are not the primary obstacle to convergence, reinforcing the importance of local maxima. The work builds upon a line of research examining the theoretical properties of EM and alternative algorithms for GMM estimation, including work by Dasgupta & Schulman [2007], Balakrishnan et al. [2015], and Arora & Kannan [2005].  Recent investigations into rates of convergence for GMMs (Chen [1995], Ho & Nguyen [2015]) provide context for the challenges in estimation, while works addressing robustness of maximum likelihood estimation (Donoho & Liu [1988]) motivate the study of computational limitations.

The paper is technically sound and presents a coherent set of results. The proofs, while not detailed within the paper itself, are referenced, and the arguments appear logically structured. The authors carefully acknowledge the idealized settings (spherical Gaussians, equal weighting) and their implications. They demonstrate awareness of related work, citing relevant literature appropriately.  The paper provides a complete and focused exploration of the stated problem. The analysis of both the population likelihood and the behavior of the EM algorithm are well-defined. The inclusion of Theorem 4, demonstrating the avoidance of saddle points, adds nuance and strengthens the argument that bad local maxima are the dominant challenge. 

The paper's clarity is generally good. The notation is standard, and the definitions are clear. However, the paper occasionally suffers from density; expanding on the intuition behind some of the more complex constructions, particularly in the proofs sketched in the appendices, would improve readability. Providing a more intuitive explanation of the recursive construction used in the proof of Theorem 2 would be beneficial.  The organization is logical, building from background and motivation to the main results and their implications.

The originality of the work lies in definitively answering a long-standing question regarding the existence of bad local optima in the GMM likelihood. While the existence of non-convexity and potential for local optima is well-known, this paper rigorously demonstrates their presence even in a relatively simple and well-behaved setting. The result on the convergence probability of EM to these bad optima is also a novel contribution.  However, as the authors themselves acknowledge, the techniques employed are not entirely surprising and follow expected lines of reasoning within the field.  The connection to the known failures of k-means is well-observed but not deeply explored.

The significance of the results is moderate. While the setting is idealized, the finding that EM can fail even with infinite data highlights a fundamental limitation of the algorithm. The high probability of converging to suboptimal points emphasizes the importance of initialization strategies. This work informs practitioners about the potential pitfalls of relying solely on random initialization and motivates the exploration of more robust initialization methods, such as those inspired by spectral techniques or moment-based estimators.  The demonstration that saddle points aren’t the primary issue directs future research towards addressing local maxima specifically.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a definitive answer to a previously open question.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear results.
*   Highlights a fundamental limitation of the EM algorithm.
*   Motivates further research into robust initialization strategies.
*   Well-written and logically structured (despite some density).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The results, while rigorous, are perhaps not entirely surprising to experts in the field.
*   The idealized setting limits the direct applicability to real-world scenarios.
*   The connection to k-means, while mentioned, could be explored more fully.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance. While the results may not be revolutionary, the paper addresses a fundamental question with a rigorous theoretical treatment. The clarity of the presentation, the thoroughness of the analysis, and the significance of the findings justify publication at NIPS.

**Specific Suggestions:**

*   Expand the intuition behind the recursive construction in the proof of Theorem 2.
*   Provide a more detailed comparison with Kumar–Kannan and contrast EM vs. Lloyd’s guarantees under large separation conditions as suggested.
*   Elaborate on the connection to k-means – for example, discuss how strategies like k-log-k initialization or distance-squared sampling might be adapted for EM.
