- Paper proves two main results: (1) for M ≥ 3, constructs well-separated isotropic GMMs with population log-likelihood containing arbitrarily bad local maxima; (2) shows EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to these bad local maxima with probability ≥ 1 - e^(-Ω(M)).  
- Builds on and extends prior work (Srebro 2007; Balakrishnan et al. 2015; Dasgupta & Schulman 2007) by resolving an open question about GMM likelihood landscapes beyond M=2.  
- Technical contributions include novel analysis techniques for population likelihoods and precise probabilistic guarantees for algorithmic behavior.  
- Strengths: strong theoretical rigor, clear exposition of constructions, and valuable algorithmic insights.  
- Authors distinguish bad local maxima from strict saddle points, attributing EM failures primarily to poor local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Major concern: counterexample constructions are highly artificial (e.g., two centers clustered, others far apart), questioning practical relevance.  
- The paper does not demonstrate that such pathological configurations become more probable as M or d increase.  
- Specific practical-unlikelihood example: M=3 construction needing two centers very close and one far seems unlikely in real clustering tasks with balanced separation.  
- Random initialization scheme analyzed (sampling centers from data) may be unrealistic; practitioners often use k-means++, spectral methods, or other informed initializations.  
- Claim that "careful initialization is necessary even in favorable settings" lacks convincing empirical support for realistic scenarios.  
- Minor issues: unclear phrase "well-separated constant" (L218), undefined Ω(M) (L225), typos throughout.  
- M=2 case discussion (L74-81) is not convincing in explaining why Srebro's conjecture might hold for M=2, weakening the contrast with main results.  
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions to address practical significance, clarify notation, and strengthen the M=2 discussion.  
- Suggested revisions: provide evidence that counterexamples are not merely pathological (e.g., probability analysis under natural center distributions) and analyze behavior under more realistic initialization schemes.
