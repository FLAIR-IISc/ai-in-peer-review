## Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper investigates the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) with isotropic, known-scale components. The authors demonstrate that such bad local maxima exist even with infinite data, resolving a conjecture by Srebro [2007]. They further prove that with random initialization, the EM algorithm will converge to these suboptimal points with high probability as the number of components increases. The work is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question about the behavior of a widely used statistical model and algorithm. It builds upon previous work analyzing the convergence of EM (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman, Balakrishnan et al.) and the challenges of non-convex optimization in statistical estimation (e.g., Chen, Genovese & Wasserman).

The strengths of the paper lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and clear presentation of results. The proof of the existence of bad local maxima is a significant contribution, and the analysis of the EM algorithm’s behavior under random initialization is compelling. The connection drawn between the existence of bad local maxima and the performance of first-order methods is also insightful. The writing is generally good and the paper is well-organized.

However, the practical relevance of the findings is a key concern. The specific configurations of centers required to exhibit these bad local maxima seem somewhat contrived. It’s unclear how likely these configurations are to arise in real-world datasets. While the authors acknowledge this, a stronger argument for the generalizability of these results would be beneficial. Specifically, requesting the authors to assume a distribution on the true centers and demonstrate that the probability of “bad” configurations increases with either the number of components (k) or the dimensionality (d) would strengthen the paper considerably, as suggested by the reviewer guidelines. 

Furthermore, the random initialization scheme analyzed may be unrealistic in many practical scenarios where data clearly exhibits clustering structure. A more informed initialization strategy might avoid these problematic configurations. The example on L74-81 regarding k=2 is not entirely convincing and could be clarified. Several minor points regarding notation and phrasing (as noted in the guidelines) should be addressed for clarity. The significance of showing EM is unlikely to converge to a saddle point versus a local optimum is also questionable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical results resolving an open problem.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Insightful connection between local maxima and algorithm performance.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Questionable practical relevance of the specific configurations studied.
*   Potentially unrealistic assumption of random initialization.
*   Minor clarity issues in notation and explanation.



**Overall:** I believe this paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution, but its practical impact remains uncertain. Addressing the concerns regarding the likelihood of the problematic configurations and the realism of the initialization scheme would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
