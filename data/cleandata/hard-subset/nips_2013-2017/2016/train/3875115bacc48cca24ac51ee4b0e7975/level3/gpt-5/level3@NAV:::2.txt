Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies the population likelihood of Gaussian mixture models in a simplified but canonical setting: known number of components, equal weights, identity covariances. It settles an open conjecture of Srebro (2007) by constructing, for M ≥ 3, well-separated spherical mixtures whose population log-likelihood has bad local maxima with arbitrarily large suboptimality. Algorithmically, it shows that EM with random initialization converges to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M); the same holds for gradient EM (first-order EM), while gradient EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, aligning with recent results on gradient dynamics (e.g., Lee et al., 2016). The work complements analyses of EM’s local convergence under good initialization (Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and contrasts algorithmic methods with global guarantees based on clustering or moments (e.g., Arora et al., 2005; Hsu and Kakade, 2013; Moitra and Valiant, 2010).

Quality: The constructions and high-level arguments are insightful and, if correct, important. However, a major concern is the brevity of the proof of Theorem 1. In lines 288–306, limits of suprema/maxima are taken over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains; continuity alone does not justify these steps. Please provide a rigorous argument (e.g., by establishing coercivity and restricting to compact sublevel sets, proving uniform convergence on these sets, or invoking an argmax/epi-convergence theorem) and clearly stating any envelope or tightness conditions. I did not read the supplementary proofs; key steps should be moved into the main text, even at the expense of trimming discussion, to convince readers.

Clarity: Generally clear, but the gradient EM discussion (lines 162–172) is hard to follow. Please expand the derivation of Equation (5), explicitly connecting the Q-function gradient to population likelihood ascent and stating assumptions on stepsize/smoothness.

Originality: High. The structural negative results at the population level for well-separated mixtures are novel and resolve a long-standing conjecture.

Significance: High. The results sharpen our understanding of EM’s failures under random initialization and motivate principled initializers.

Scope and limitations: The analysis assumes known M, uniform weights, identity covariances; these simplifications are reasonable given technical difficulty. The paper is written for general d, but proofs are for d = 1. Please state whether extensions to d > 1 are possible and outline the additional obstacles.

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves an open question; clear conceptual impact.
- Strong, relevant algorithmic consequences.
- Connections to modern nonconvex optimization theory.

Cons for acceptance:
- Theorem 1 proof requires substantially more detail.
- Gradient EM section needs clearer derivations.
- Dimensionality scope (d > 1) left unclear.
