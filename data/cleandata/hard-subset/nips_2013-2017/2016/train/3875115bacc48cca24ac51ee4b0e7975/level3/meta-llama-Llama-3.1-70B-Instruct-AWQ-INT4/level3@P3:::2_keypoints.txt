- Topic: Analysis of EM for estimating Gaussian mixture means with known K, weights, and covariances.  
- Main theoretical contributions: (i) existence of local maxima with likelihood arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum, (ii) random initialization of EM often converges to such suboptimal critical points, (iii) gradient EM exhibits the same behavior.  
- Reviewer view: Question is interesting, results informative, and worthwhile for understanding EM in practice.  
- Model simplification: Analysis restricted to known K, uniform weights, identity covariances; reviewer finds these simplifications acceptable given technical difficulty.  
- Dimension issue: Paper states results for general d but proofs are provided for d = 1; reviewer asks authors to state whether extensions to d > 1 are possible or discuss obstacles.  
- Major concern: Proof of Theorem 1 is too short—lines 288–306 (limits/suprema over unbounded/parameter-dependent domains) need more detailed justification because continuity alone is insufficient.  
- Supplementary proofs: Reviewer did not read supplement and suggests expanding the main proof (even at expense of discussion) to convince readers.  
- Minor concern: Gradient EM section (lines 162–172) is hard to follow; request clearer explanation of how Equation (5) is derived.  
- Quality assessment: Technically sound and claims are well-supported overall, but Theorem 1 issues must be addressed.  
- Clarity assessment: Well-written overall, but some sections require more explanation and detail for clarity.  
- Originality: Problems and approaches are novel, combining familiar techniques in a new way; related work is adequately referenced.  
- Significance: Results are important and advance the state of the art on a difficult problem.  
- Pros for acceptance: Important problem, new results, technically solid analysis.  
- Cons for acceptance: Theorem 1 needs stronger justification, gradient EM section unclear, and limited generality to simplified model.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address proof details and clarity issues.
