This paper investigates the theoretical properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm when applied to Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) with a fixed number of components, known weights, and fixed covariances. The authors demonstrate the surprising existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function, even for well-separated Gaussians, resolving a conjecture by Srebro [2007]. They further prove that random initialization of EM leads to convergence to these suboptimal critical points with high probability, and that a first-order variant of EM suffers from the same issue, not due to saddle points but to bad local maxima. This work builds on a line of research analyzing the theoretical limitations of EM, including Dasgupta & Schulman [2007], Balakrishnan et al. [2015], and connects to broader efforts understanding non-convex optimization landscapes like Loh & Wainwright [2013].

The paper addresses a significant and interesting question regarding the fundamental behavior of a widely used algorithm. The negative results are informative, demonstrating a practical limitation of EM even in a seemingly benign setting. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into why EM can fail to find optimal solutions. The work is worthwhile for the NIPS audience as it highlights the importance of initialization strategies for EM and other local search methods, and contributes to our understanding of the challenges of optimization in non-convex landscapes.

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical depth and clear presentation of the negative results. The authors successfully establish the existence of bad local maxima and demonstrate the high probability of convergence to them under random initialization. The connections made to prior work are appropriate and contextualize the contributions effectively.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The analysis is heavily restricted to a simplified model – known *K*, uniform weights, and identity covariances. While the authors acknowledge this and argue its necessity for technical tractability, it limits the direct applicability of the results to more general GMMs. The extension to higher dimensions (d > 1) is not addressed beyond stating that it *might* be possible. A discussion of the challenges in extending the proofs to d > 1, even if inconclusive, would be valuable.

My major concern revolves around the proof of Theorem 1 (lines 288–306). The argument relying on limits of supremums and maximums over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains feels insufficiently detailed. Simply stating continuity doesn’t guarantee the claimed convergence to a local maximum; a more careful analysis of the likelihood function’s behavior in the neighborhood of the constructed suboptimal point is needed. The proof feels compressed, and a more elaborate justification of these critical steps is essential to convince the reader. I did not examine the supplementary proofs, but I strongly suggest that the authors consider lengthening the main proof, even at the expense of some discussion, to bolster its clarity and rigor.

A minor concern is the readability of the gradient EM section (lines 162–172). The derivation of Equation (5) is not immediately obvious and could benefit from further explanation. Specifically, it would be helpful to explicitly state how gradient ascent on the Q-function translates to the update rule given in Equation (6) in the context of the GMM.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental question about the behavior of EM.
*   The negative results are surprising and informative.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights.
*   The work is relevant to a broad audience interested in optimization and statistical inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The analysis is restricted to a simplified model.
*   The proof of Theorem 1 lacks sufficient detail and justification.
*   The gradient EM section is difficult to follow.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should significantly expand the justification of the proof of Theorem 1 and clarify the derivation of Equation (5). Addressing the limitations of the simplified model and discussing the challenges of extending the results to higher dimensions would further strengthen the paper. While the simplifications are understandable, acknowledging the gap and attempting to address it, even partially, will improve the paper's impact.
