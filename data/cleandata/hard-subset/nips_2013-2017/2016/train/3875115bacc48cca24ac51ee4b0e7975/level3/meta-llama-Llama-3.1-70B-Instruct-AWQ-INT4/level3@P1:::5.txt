This paper provides a significant contribution to the understanding of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and their estimation via the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors address an open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of bad local maxima in the infinite-sample likelihood of GMMs. They construct a counterexample, demonstrating that even in the idealized setting of equally weighted, spherical, and well-separated Gaussians, the population log-likelihood function can have arbitrarily bad local maxima.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and proofs. The introduction provides a thorough background on GMMs, the EM algorithm, and the problem of interest. The authors also discuss related work and the significance of their results.

The main contributions of the paper are two-fold. Firstly, the authors prove that the EM algorithm, as well as its first-order variant, converges to a bad critical point with exponentially high probability under a common random initialization scheme. This result has significant implications for the use of EM in practice, highlighting the need for careful initialization.

Secondly, the authors show that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points with probability one. This result is important for understanding the behavior of gradient-based methods in non-convex optimization problems.

The reviewer's comments suggest that the paper is well-written and clear, but that the supplementary material was not fully checked. The reviewer also requests estimates of the constants in the probability bounds, which are important for understanding the phase transition and when bad-solution convergence appears for realistic values of k. While the lack of constants or simulations does not detract from the paper's novelty or impact, it would be beneficial to include this information to provide a more complete understanding of the results.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the proofs are well-constructed and easy to follow. The authors provide a detailed proof for a simple case (k=3, d=1), which helps to build intuition and understanding of the more general results.

The significance of the paper lies in its contribution to the understanding of non-convex optimization problems, particularly in the context of GMMs. The results have important implications for the use of EM in practice and highlight the need for careful initialization.

Overall, the paper is well-written, clear, and makes significant contributions to the field. The reviewer's comments are constructive, and the authors should consider including estimates of the constants in the probability bounds to provide a more complete understanding of the results.
