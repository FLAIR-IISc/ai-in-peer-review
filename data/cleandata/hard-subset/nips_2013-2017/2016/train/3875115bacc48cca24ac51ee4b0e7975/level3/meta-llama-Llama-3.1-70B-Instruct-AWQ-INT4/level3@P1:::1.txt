This paper presents a thorough analysis of the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The authors provide a comprehensive study of the population likelihood function of GMMs, highlighting the existence of bad local maxima and the implications of this phenomenon on the convergence of the EM algorithm.

The paper's main contributions can be summarized as follows:

1. The authors demonstrate that the population likelihood function of GMMs can have bad local maxima, even in the idealized setting of well-separated, spherical Gaussians. This result resolves an open question of Srebro [2007] and provides a negative answer to the conjecture that any local maximum of the likelihood function is a global maximum in the limit of infinite samples.

2. The authors show that the EM algorithm, when initialized randomly, converges to a bad critical point with high probability. Specifically, they prove that the probability of success for the EM algorithm is exponentially small as a function of the number of mixture components.

3. The authors also analyze the first-order EM algorithm and demonstrate that it converges to a bad local maximum with high probability. They further show that the first-order EM algorithm cannot converge to strict saddle points.

The paper's results have significant implications for the use of EM algorithms in practice. The authors suggest that careful initialization is required for local search methods, even in large-sample settings, and even for extremely well-behaved mixture models.

The paper's technical contributions are sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the population likelihood function and the convergence properties of the EM algorithm. However, the results and techniques presented in the paper are not particularly surprising, and they follow expected lines. The key example presented in the paper, a 3-component construction with two close clusters and one far cluster, yields a local maximum with centers misallocated, which is expected when separations are much larger than the square root of the dimension.

The paper's results can be related to the known failures of Lloyd's algorithm for k-means clustering. The authors suggest that strategies such as picking k log k centers or distance-squared sampling may be useful in improving the performance of EM algorithms.

A potential suggestion for future work is to provide a comparison with the Kumar-Kannan algorithm and contrast EM versus Lloyd's guarantees under large separation conditions.
