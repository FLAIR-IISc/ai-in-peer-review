This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) likelihood of equally weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and proves that Expectation–Maximization (EM) need not reach a global maximum even in highly favorable regimes. The main structural result gives a negative answer to Srebro’s 2007 conjecture by constructing, for M ≥ 3, well-separated mixtures with arbitrarily bad local maxima. Algorithmically, the authors show that EM (and gradient/first-order EM) with random initialization converges to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − exp(−Ω(M)); moreover, gradient EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, pinpointing bad local maxima—not saddles—as the fundamental obstruction. The key 3-component example (two nearby means and one far) and a recursive clustering construction align with known failure modes of Lloyd’s algorithm in k-means, and the failure probabilities mirror classical analyses of random seeding for k-means, connecting to work such as Dasgupta–Schulman (two-round EM), Balakrishnan et al. (local convergence under good init), and dynamical-systems arguments for saddle avoidance (Lee et al., Panageas & Piliouras).

Quality: The results appear technically sound and carefully argued. The constructions are clear, and the recursion yielding exp(−Ω(M)) success is compelling. Since the focus is population-level, it would help to explicitly state finite-sample transfer (rates, sample sizes, and dependence on separation and dimension) beyond “standard empirical process” remarks, and to spell out the separation thresholds (≫ √d) needed for the “arbitrarily bad gap.”

Clarity: The paper is well organized. Adding an intuitive figure for the recursive instance and a tighter summary of assumptions (equal weights, unit variance known, step-size ranges) would aid readers. Please make the dependence of the likelihood gap on separation explicit.

Originality: While the constructions and proof ideas are not surprising given k-means folklore, the paper provides a coherent, definitive resolution of Srebro’s conjecture and a sharp exponential failure probability for random initialization, elevating a widely suspected phenomenon into a rigorous set of results.

Significance: High for the theory-of-EM community and for practitioners who rely on random restarts. The strict-saddle avoidance result helps focus future work on initialization and seeding.

Suggestions:
- Compare against Lloyd’s guarantees under large separation (e.g., Kumar–Kannan) and clarify thresholds where Lloyd’s with good seeding provably succeeds versus EM’s behavior.
- Discuss whether k-means++ (distance-squared) seeding or overseeding with O(k log k) centers followed by pruning can mitigate the failures for EM, paralleling k-means practice.
- Contrast with moment/spectral initializations (Hsu–Kakade; Moitra–Valiant) and two-round EM (Dasgupta–Schulman).

Pros:
- Resolves a long-standing conjecture with clean, general constructions.
- Strong, quantitative failure probabilities; clear algorithmic implications.
- Useful clarification that bad local maxima—not saddles—are the bottleneck.

Cons:
- Techniques and counterexamples largely expected; novelty is more in consolidation than surprise.
- Limited discussion of finite-sample rates and practical seeding remedies.
- Missing comparison to Lloyd’s/k-means guarantees under separation (e.g., Kumar–Kannan).
