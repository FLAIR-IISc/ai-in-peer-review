### Summary

This paper investigates the structure of the population log-likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), focusing on the idealized but important setting of equally-weighted, spherical, and well-separated components. The work makes two primary contributions. First, it provides a negative answer to a conjecture by Srebro [2007], demonstrating that for any number of components M ≥ 3, the population log-likelihood function can possess local maxima whose values are arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. Second, it analyzes the algorithmic consequences of this structural property, showing that the EM algorithm (and a first-order variant) with a standard random initialization scheme will converge to these suboptimal critical points with a probability that approaches 1 exponentially fast in M. The paper further argues that for the first-order EM variant, this failure is due to convergence to bad local maxima rather than getting stuck at strict saddle points.

### Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper resolves a significant and long-standing open question in the theoretical analysis of GMMs. By refuting Srebro's conjecture, it provides a definitive statement on the non-benign nature of the GMM likelihood landscape, even in highly favorable, infinite-sample settings. This is a strong theoretical contribution that clarifies the limits of local search algorithms like EM.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical results are strong and appear to be technically sound. The construction of GMMs with bad local maxima is insightful, and the proof sketch for showing that EM with random initialization fails with high probability is convincing. The result establishing that this failure probability is exponentially high (1 - e⁻ᶜᴹ) is particularly powerful and provides a stark warning about the scalability of this common heuristic.

3.  **Algorithmic Insight:** The paper provides a compelling theoretical argument for the necessity of careful initialization for EM. While practitioners have long held this belief, this work provides a rigorous justification, showing that even for well-separated mixtures, a naive but common initialization strategy is doomed to fail for a moderate number of components. The connection to the broader literature on non-convex optimization, particularly by showing that failure is due to local maxima and not strict saddle points (for first-order EM), is also valuable.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

The main weaknesses of the paper relate to the practical significance of the specific failure modes identified. While the theoretical results are strong, their connection to real-world GMM estimation problems could be strengthened.

1.  **Practical Relevance of "Bad" GMM Configurations:** The proofs rely on constructing specific GMMs where the true centers µ* are arranged in a hierarchical, clustered fashion. It is not clear how likely such configurations are to arise in practice. The paper demonstrates a worst-case scenario, but it would be more impactful to understand the *average-case* behavior. Could the authors comment on this? For instance, if one were to assume a simple prior over the true centers µ* (e.g., drawn i.i.d. from a broad Gaussian), what is the probability that the resulting GMM instance would exhibit these bad local maxima? Does this probability increase with M or the dimensionality d? The current analysis in Theorem 2 is for a fixed "bad" GMM and does not address the probability of the GMM itself being "bad."

2.  **Practical Relevance of the Initialization Failure:** The paper shows that random initialization (sampling initial centers from the data) fails when the initial centers are "misallocated" across the true, well-separated clusters of µ*. However, in such a scenario where the data itself would be clearly multi-modal, this initialization scheme might be considered naive. For example, in the M=3 case described (two centers close, one far away), the data would form two distinct clouds. It seems unlikely that a random sampling of three points from the data would frequently result in the problematic configuration where two initial centers are placed near the isolated true center and one is placed between the two close true centers. Is it possible that in the very settings where these bad local maxima exist, the random initialization scheme is less likely to place the algorithm in their basin of attraction than the analysis suggests? A small simulation study could perhaps shed light on how frequently these failure modes are encountered in practice.

**Clarity and Minor Issues:**

The paper is generally very well-written and easy to follow. However, there are a few points that could be clarified.

*   **L74–81:** The purpose of the M=2 example is unclear. It states that rigorously resolving the question for M=2 is an open problem, but the text and figure seem to suggest that Srebro's conjecture holds in this case. This section could be rephrased to state more directly what the visualization is intended to convey about the M=2 case and why it differs from M≥3.
*   **L233–235:** In the proof sketch for Theorem 2, requirement (1) "all initialized centers fall in the neighborhood of exactly one cluster of true centers" seems either unnecessary or stated confusingly. If the clusters of true centers are extremely far apart, wouldn't an initial center placed between them be quickly drawn into the basin of one of the clusters? Clarifying why this is a necessary condition for success would be helpful.
*   **L267–273 (Theorem 4):** The practical importance of showing that first-order EM does not converge to *strict saddle points* could be better motivated. Since Theorems 2 and 3 already establish that EM/first-order EM converges to suboptimal critical points with high probability, the key takeaway for the practitioner is that the algorithm gets stuck. Whether the fixed point is a local maximum or a saddle point is a secondary concern from an applied perspective.
*   **Phrasing/Notation:**
    *   L218: What is meant by a "well-separated constant"? Perhaps "for a sufficiently large separation constant"?
    *   L225: The text mentions executing EM "at least e^Ω(M) times". Given M is an integer variable, this is likely intended to mean a function that grows exponentially in M, but using Ω notation inside an exponent with a variable is non-standard. Please clarify.

### Conclusion

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution by resolving an open problem and rigorously analyzing the failure modes of a cornerstone algorithm (EM) with a common initialization strategy. The results are clear, important, and will be of high interest to the NIPS community. My main reservations concern the practical implications of the specific constructions used in the proofs. Addressing the questions about the likelihood of encountering these "bad" GMMs and initialization failures in practice would substantially strengthen the paper. Despite these concerns, the theoretical novelty and importance of the results merit acceptance.
