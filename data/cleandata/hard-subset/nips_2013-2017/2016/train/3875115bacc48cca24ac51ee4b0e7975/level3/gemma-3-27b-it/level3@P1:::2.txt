This paper investigates the theoretical properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm when applied to estimating the means of a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM). The authors focus on a simplified, yet theoretically challenging, setting where the number of components (K) is known, the mixture weights are uniform, and the covariance matrices are identity matrices. They demonstrate the existence of “bad” local maxima in the population likelihood function, and show that standard initialization strategies—specifically, random initialization—cause the EM algorithm, and its first-order variant, to converge to these suboptimal solutions with high probability. This work fundamentally challenges the assumption that EM, even with large samples, reliably finds good solutions, and highlights the importance of careful initialization.

The paper builds upon previous work examining identifiability and convergence rates for GMMs (e.g., Teicher, 1963; Chen, 1995; Allman et al., 2009). Recent studies have focused on analyzing variants of EM (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and exploring alternative algorithms with statistical guarantees (Arora et al., 2005; Belkin & Sinha, 2010). Srebro’s 2007 conjecture—that the population likelihood function lacks bad local maxima—serves as a central motivation for this paper, and the authors definitively disprove it.  Their work contributes to the broader understanding of non-convex optimization, especially in the context of statistical estimation where maximum likelihood estimation is a cornerstone.

The strengths of this paper lie in its theoretical rigor and its negative results, which are both surprising and practically relevant.  Demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood, even in a relatively benign setting, is a significant contribution. The analysis of the EM algorithm’s behavior under random initialization reinforces the need for caution when applying EM in practice. The fact that the first-order EM algorithm suffers from the same issue, and does *not* simply converge to saddle points, further clarifies the algorithmic challenges.  This work is likely to be of interest to researchers working on statistical estimation, optimization algorithms, and the theoretical foundations of machine learning.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the analysis is performed for the general d-dimensional case, the proofs are presented only for d = 1. It is important for the authors to either provide an outline for extending the results to higher dimensions, or discuss the additional challenges that arise.  This omission limits the immediate applicability of the results. 

A major concern is with the proof of Theorem 1, specifically lines 288-306. The argument concerning the limits of suprema and maxima over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains feels incomplete. Simply stating that the function is continuous is insufficient to justify the claimed convergences. A more detailed justification, perhaps with explicit bounds or convergence arguments, is needed to convince the reader of the proof’s correctness. While I haven't reviewed the supplementary material, the main body of the paper would be strengthened by a more complete presentation of the proof, even if it requires reducing the length of the discussion sections.

Finally, the section on the first-order EM algorithm (lines 162-172) is somewhat difficult to follow. The derivation of Equation (5) could benefit from more explanation. A more detailed walkthrough of the steps leading to this equation would significantly improve clarity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a fundamental question about the behavior of the EM algorithm.
*   The negative results are surprising and have implications for practical applications.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and builds upon existing work in the field.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly motivated.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The proofs are presented only for the 1-dimensional case, limiting the generality of the results.
*   The proof of Theorem 1 requires further justification.
*   The explanation of the first-order EM algorithm could be clearer.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of the EM algorithm. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the concerns raised regarding the proofs and the clarity of the first-order EM algorithm section. Addressing the limitation of the proof being only for d=1, even with a discussion of difficulties in generalization, would significantly strengthen the manuscript.
