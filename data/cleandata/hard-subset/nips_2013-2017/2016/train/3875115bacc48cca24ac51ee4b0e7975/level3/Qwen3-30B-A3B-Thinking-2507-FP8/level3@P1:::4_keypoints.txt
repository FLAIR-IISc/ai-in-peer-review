- Paper answers whether population GMM likelihoods with M ≥ 3 can have arbitrarily bad local maxima, resolving Srebro (2007).
- Theorem 1: constructs a counterexample for M=3, d=1 showing well-separated, equal-weight GMMs can have local maxima with arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global maximum.
- Geometric intuition for Theorem 1: when two true centers are close and the third is far, a local maximum arises by merging the close pair and duplicating the distant center.
- Proof strategy for Theorem 1: define a neighborhood around the suboptimal configuration that excludes global maxima and show likelihood is strictly smaller on its boundary.
- Theorem 2: EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to bad critical points with exponentially high probability in M.
- Hierarchical grouping construction: nested clusters of true centers make favorable initializations exponentially unlikely, so exponentially many restarts are needed in practice.
- Theorem 4: first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, so failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.
- Proofs are sound; Theorem 1 is rigorous and Theorem 2’s sketch is convincing (full details in appendix).
- Authors properly distinguish population vs. finite-sample settings and note standard empirical-process extensions to finite samples.
- Originality is high: first counterexample to Srebro’s conjecture and novel hierarchical construction extending beyond two-component analyses.
- Significance is substantial: explains why random initialization often fails and motivates method-of-moments or smarter initialization strategies.
- Exposition is clear and well-organized, with effective geometric intuition and motivation.
- Limitations: results proved for d=1 (extension to d>1 depends on Lemma 7 generalization) and focus is mainly on the population setting.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, original, significant, and well-written; recommendation for acceptance.
