This paper studies the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for estimating the means in a Gaussian mixture model with a known and fixed number of components, weights, and covariances. The authors prove three negative results: (i) local maxima with likelihood arbitrarily smaller than the global maximum can exist even for large samples; (ii) randomly initialized EM converges with high probability to such suboptimal critical points; and (iii) the same holds for gradient EM.

The reviewer finds the question interesting, the results informative, and worthwhile for understanding EM in practice. The analysis is restricted to a simplified model (known K, uniform weights, identity covariances), but the reviewer deems these simplifications acceptable given the technicality.

The paper is written for general dimension d, but proofs are given for d = 1. The reviewer asks the authors to state whether extensions to d > 1 are possible or to discuss additional difficulties.

The reviewer's major concern is that the proof of Theorem 1 is too short—lines 288–306 (limits of supremums/maximums over unbounded or parameter-dependent domains) need more detailed justification, as continuity alone does not imply the claimed convergences. The reviewer did not read supplementary proofs and suggests lengthening the main proof (even at the expense of discussion) to convince readers.

A minor concern is that the gradient EM section (lines 162–172) is hard to follow; the reviewer requests more explanation of how Equation (5) is derived.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. However, the reviewer has some concerns about the proof of Theorem 1, which need to be addressed.

Clarity: The paper is well-written, but some sections, such as the gradient EM section, are hard to follow. The reviewer suggests providing more explanations and details to improve clarity.

Originality: The problems or approaches are new, and the paper provides a novel combination of familiar techniques. The related work is adequately referenced.

Significance: The results are important, and the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.
