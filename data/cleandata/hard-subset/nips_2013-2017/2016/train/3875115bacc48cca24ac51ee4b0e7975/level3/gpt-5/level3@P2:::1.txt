Summary
This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood landscape of equally weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and its algorithmic consequences for EM and a first-order EM variant. It resolves an open question posed by Srebro (2007) by proving that, for M ≥ 3, even well-separated mixtures admit bad local maxima whose log-likelihood can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. The core construction already appears in 1D and uses a simple “two-close-one-far” arrangement for M = 3; for general M, the authors build a recursively clustered configuration to amplify the difficulty.

Algorithmically, the paper analyzes EM and gradient EM with random initialization drawn from the data distribution (equivalently, from the mixture in the population limit). It shows that with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M), these procedures converge to suboptimal critical points whose suboptimality can be made arbitrarily large. Complementing recent results on saddle-point avoidance (e.g., Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016), the authors prove that gradient EM with a constant stepsize avoids strict saddle points almost surely, so the dominant obstruction is the presence of bad local maxima rather than saddles. The results connect to prior local analyses of EM (Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and to algorithmic alternatives based on clustering or moments (e.g., Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Hsu and Kakade, 2013; Moitra and Valiant, 2010).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The structural example for M = 3 is clear and intuitive, and the generalization via a hierarchical clustering of centers is plausible and well-motivated. The population-level analysis avoids sample noise and isolates optimization phenomena. The probability bounds e−Ω(M) are informative and align with analogous behaviors known for k-means/Lloyd’s with random seeding.
- Scope and assumptions: The focus is on equally weighted, isotropic Gaussians with fixed variance; the main counterexamples are in 1D (extendable to higher dimensions by embedding). The random initialization model is standard in practice, but the negative results hinge critically on it.
- The saddle-point result (first-order EM avoids strict saddles a.s.) is in line with modern gradient dynamics theory; the proof sketch claims to bypass global smoothness via properties of the first-order EM map, which is interesting.
- Suggestions to strengthen rigor and applicability:
  - Provide explicit finite-sample statements and rates (the text notes they “carry over” via standard arguments). Even a brief corollary quantifying sample complexity vs. approximation error would be valuable.
  - Clarify the precise separation conditions and constants in the constructions (e.g., dependence on M and d), and whether the bad local maxima persist under small perturbations of weights/variances.
  - Detail the stepsize conditions required for the saddle-avoidance result (any Lipschitz/smoothness constants needed) and how they relate to practical settings.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow; the high-level intuition for the M = 3 case is especially helpful. It would be useful to include a schematic figure illustrating the local trap region and the recursive construction underlying the e−Ω(M) bound.
- The presentation of EM vs. gradient EM is clear. Some references are inconsistently formatted; please standardize (journal/venue, year, author lists).
- Consider adding a short discussion explicitly contrasting population vs. finite-sample landscapes and how concentration ensures transfer.

Originality
- Resolving Srebro’s conjecture in the negative at the population level (even under very favorable assumptions) is a meaningful contribution. The recursive construction giving exponentially small success probability for random initialization is a crisp and strong statement.
- While the negative phenomenon has strong precedents for k-means/Lloyd’s (e.g., local minima, poor random seeding), establishing it for the likelihood landscape of GMMs and for EM/gradient EM at the population level is novel and of independent interest.
- The strict-saddle avoidance result for first-order EM aligns with recent nonconvex optimization theory but is a useful instantiation.

Significance
- The results sharpen our understanding of when EM fails because of bad local maxima rather than saddles, underscoring the importance of initialization even with infinite data and well-separated components. This has direct implications for practice and motivates principled initialization (moments, spectral methods, or more careful seeding).
- The exponential failure probability suggests that naive multi-start strategies are computationally prohibitive as M grows.

Relation to prior work and suggestions
- Please provide a more explicit comparison with Lloyd’s algorithm and known remedies: e.g., k-means++ seeding (Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007), oversampling k log k centers, and spectral/pilot initializations (e.g., Kumar–Kannan 2010; Vempala & Wang, 2002). Drawing parallels between your bad instances and known failure cases for Lloyd’s would better situate the contribution.
- Discuss whether your constructions are also failure cases for Lloyd’s and whether the e−Ω(M) rate mirrors those seeding analyses.
- Expand on practical initialization strategies (beyond moments) that might avoid your traps; can your lower bounds be circumvented by distance-squared sampling or pruning-based EM variants (cf. Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007)?

Pros
- Resolves a notable open question; clear structural counterexamples under favorable assumptions.
- Strong negative algorithmic guarantees: EM/gradient EM with random init fail with probability 1 − e−Ω(M).
- Clarifies that bad local maxima, not strict saddles, drive failures.
- Clean population-level analysis; techniques may transfer to other latent variable models.

Cons
- Somewhat unsurprising to practitioners familiar with k-means/EM pathologies; constructions are tailored and in 1D.
- No finite-sample theorems or experiments to gauge practical impact.
- Limited discussion of alternative initializations or positive results delineating when EM succeeds.
- Step-size and robustness details for the saddle-avoidance theorem could be clearer.

Overall recommendation
A solid theoretical paper that settles an open problem and provides sharp, interpretable negative results with algorithmic implications. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on improved contextualization with clustering/initialization literature and clearer finite-sample and robustness discussions.
