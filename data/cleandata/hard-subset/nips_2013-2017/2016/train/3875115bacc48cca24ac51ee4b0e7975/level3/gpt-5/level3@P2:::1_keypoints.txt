- Proves that for M ≥ 3, equally weighted spherical GMMs can have bad local maxima whose log-likelihood is arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, resolving Srebro (2007) negatively at the population level.  
- Core M = 3 construction uses a simple 1D "two-close-one-far" arrangement; general M uses a recursive clustered configuration to amplify difficulty.  
- Analyzes EM and gradient EM with random initialization from the data distribution and shows they converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e−Ω(M).  
- Shows gradient EM with constant stepsize avoids strict saddles almost surely, implying bad local maxima (not saddles) are the dominant failure mode.  
- Population-level focus isolates optimization phenomena by avoiding sample noise; techniques likely transfer to other latent-variable models.  
- Probability bounds e−Ω(M) are informative and align with analogous behaviors known for k-means/Lloyd’s with random seeding.  
- Scope limited to equally weighted, isotropic Gaussians with fixed variance; main counterexamples are in 1D but extendable to higher dimensions by embedding.  
- Negative results depend critically on random initialization, a standard but important modeling choice.  
- Technical construction and hierarchical generalization are clear, plausible, and well-motivated.  
- Recommends adding explicit finite-sample statements/rates or a brief corollary quantifying sample complexity vs. approximation error.  
- Requests clarification of separation conditions and constants (dependence on M and d) and robustness of bad maxima to small perturbations in weights/variances.  
- Asks for precise stepsize/smoothness conditions required for the saddle-avoidance result and how they relate to practical settings.  
- Paper is generally well-written with helpful intuition for M = 3; suggests a schematic figure for the local trap and recursive construction.  
- Presentation of EM vs. gradient EM is clear; requests standardized reference formatting and a short discussion contrasting population vs. finite-sample landscapes.  
- Novelty: first to establish such negative likelihood-landscape phenomena for GMMs and EM/gradient EM at the population level, despite precedents in k-means.  
- Significance: highlights that initialization—not saddle points—causes EM failure even with infinite data and well-separated components, motivating principled initialization methods.  
- Practical implication: exponential failure probability implies naive multi-start is computationally prohibitive as M grows.  
- Suggests stronger comparison with Lloyd’s/k-means++ and spectral/moment initializations, and discussion of whether constructions also defeat Lloyd’s and advanced seeding methods.  
- Encourages exploration of practical initialization strategies (distance-squared sampling, pruning-based EM) that might avoid the traps or bypass the lower bounds.  
- Pros: resolves open problem, provides clear counterexamples, gives strong negative algorithmic guarantees, and clarifies the role of bad local maxima.  
- Cons: constructions are tailored and mostly 1D, lacks finite-sample theorems/experiments, limited discussion of positive results/alternative initializations, and needs more detail on stepsize/robustness.  
- Overall recommendation: strong theoretical contribution and lean toward acceptance, contingent on improved contextualization with clustering/initialization literature and clearer finite-sample and robustness discussions.
