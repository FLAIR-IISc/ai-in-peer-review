

This paper addresses Srebro's conjecture [2007] regarding the absence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors prove two main results: (1) for M ≥ 3 components, they construct a well-separated, isotropic GMM whose population log-likelihood contains local maxima arbitrarily worse than global optima, and (2) they demonstrate that EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to these bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)).

The work builds on foundational contributions by Srebro [2007], Balakrishnan et al. [2015], and Dasgupta and Schulman [2007], extending theoretical understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes beyond the two-component case. The technical contributions are significant, as they resolve an open question about the behavior of likelihood surfaces in the population setting. The proofs introduce novel techniques for analyzing population likelihood structures and provide precise probabilistic guarantees for algorithmic behavior.

The paper's strengths include strong theoretical rigor, clear exposition of the key constructions, and valuable algorithmic insights. The authors successfully distinguish between bad local maxima and strict saddle points using recent results on gradient methods, providing evidence that the failure of local search methods stems primarily from poor local maxima rather than saddle points.

However, several concerns limit the paper's practical significance. The counterexample constructions for bad local maxima require highly artificial configurations of centers (e.g., two centers clustered together while others are well-separated), raising questions about their relevance to real-world applications. The authors acknowledge this limitation but fail to demonstrate that such configurations become more probable as M or d increases. For instance, with M=3, the construction requires two centers to be very close together while the third is far away—this seems unlikely to arise naturally in practical clustering problems where data would typically exhibit more balanced separation.

The random initialization scheme analyzed (sampling centers from the data) may also be unrealistic in practice. When data clearly form distinct clusters (as in the paper's example), practitioners would likely use more informed initialization methods like k-means++ or spectral clustering rather than purely random sampling. The paper's claim that "careful initialization is necessary even in highly favorable settings" lacks convincing empirical support for realistic scenarios.

The paper contains minor notational issues that could be easily addressed: "well-separated constant" at L218 is unclear, Ω(M) at L225 should be defined, and several typos appear throughout. The M=2 case discussion (L74-81) fails to convincingly demonstrate why Srebro's conjecture might hold for M=2, which weakens the contrast with their main results.

While the theoretical contribution is novel and important for understanding GMM likelihood landscapes, the paper's practical significance is limited by its focus on artificial counterexamples and an initialization scheme that may not reflect common practice. The authors should strengthen their argument by showing that the problematic configurations are not pathological but could arise in practical applications, perhaps by analyzing the probability of such configurations under natural distributions of centers.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces, but its practical relevance needs strengthening. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the concerns about practical significance and to clarify the relationship between the theoretical constructions and real-world applications. Specifically, the authors should provide evidence that their counterexamples are not merely pathological but could emerge in natural settings, and they should discuss how their results might change under more realistic initialization schemes.
