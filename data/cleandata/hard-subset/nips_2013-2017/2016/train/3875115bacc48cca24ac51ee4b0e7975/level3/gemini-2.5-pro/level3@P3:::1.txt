This paper analyzes the population log-likelihood landscape for mixtures of spherical Gaussians, focusing on the performance of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The primary contribution is a definitive negative resolution to a conjecture by Srebro (2007), which posited that for well-behaved GMMs, all local maxima of the population likelihood are global maxima. The authors construct examples to show this is not the case. Specifically, they demonstrate that even for a well-separated mixture of three spherical Gaussians, the population likelihood function can have bad local maxima whose objective values are arbitrarily worse than the global optimum.

The paper then explores the algorithmic consequences of this structural property. It proves that for a mixture of M components, the EM algorithm (and a first-order variant) with random initialization—drawing initial centers from the mixture distribution—converges to a suboptimal critical point with a probability of at least 1 - exp(-Ω(M)). Finally, connecting to recent work in non-convex optimization, the authors show that a first-order variant of EM (equivalent to gradient ascent) almost surely avoids strict saddle points. This elegantly argues that the observed algorithmic failures are due to convergence to poor local maxima, not getting stuck at saddle points. Overall, the paper presents a coherent set of theoretical results demonstrating that EM can systematically fail to find the global maximum likelihood solution, even with infinite data and in favorable settings.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental, long-standing, and important open question in the theory of mixture models. By formally refuting the Srebro conjecture, it provides a crucial piece of understanding about the optimization landscape of one of the most widely used models in statistics and machine learning. The results provide strong theoretical justification for the practical wisdom that careful initialization is not just a heuristic but a necessity for EM.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound and the results are rigorously proven. The progression from a structural result (existence of bad local maxima) to an algorithmic one (high probability of failure for random initialization) is logical and compelling. The inclusion of the result on avoiding saddle points is a modern touch that connects this classical problem to contemporary optimization theory.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out, the problem setting is formally defined, and the main theorems are stated upfront with clear intuition provided for the constructions and proofs.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The primary weakness of the paper is that the results, while novel in their formal proof for the GMM likelihood, may not be entirely surprising to researchers familiar with clustering algorithms. The techniques and failure modes shown are highly analogous to well-understood phenomena in k-means clustering.

*   **Analogy to k-means:** The construction used to demonstrate a bad local optimum—two components close together and one far away—is a classic difficult instance for clustering. The resulting spurious local maximum, where one estimated center is wasted trying to model the space between the two close true centers while another true center is modeled by two estimated centers, is precisely the kind of failure mode known for Lloyd’s algorithm. Similarly, the exponential failure rate for random initialization mirrors known results for k-means, which motivated the development of improved initialization strategies like k-means++ (based on distance-squared sampling) or running multiple random trials. The paper would be strengthened by explicitly discussing this strong parallel. Acknowledging this connection would help frame the results as a formal confirmation that EM, as a soft-assignment generalization of k-means, inherits these fundamental landscape challenges.

*   **Comparison with Provable Algorithms:** The paper demonstrates a failure of EM under large separation conditions (implicitly requiring separation ≫ √d for the bad optima to be well-defined). This is precisely the regime where other algorithms, such as spectral methods or the one by Kumar & Kannan, provide provable guarantees. A direct comparison would add significant context. For instance, the authors could contrast the conditions under which EM fails with the separation guarantees required by these other methods to succeed. This would highlight the fundamental gap between local search on the likelihood function and other algorithmic approaches, providing a more complete picture of the GMM estimation problem.

*   **Scope of the Negative Result:** The constructions rely on very large separations between clusters of components. It would be valuable to discuss whether these bad local maxima are pathological artifacts of this asymptotic regime or if they persist under more moderate separation conditions relevant to practice.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that provides a clear and definitive answer to an important open problem. The main results are significant and well-supported. The primary critique is that the phenomena described are analogous to known issues in related problems like k-means, a connection the paper could explore more deeply to better contextualize its contributions. Adding a more explicit comparison to algorithms with positive guarantees under similar conditions would further enhance the paper's impact.
