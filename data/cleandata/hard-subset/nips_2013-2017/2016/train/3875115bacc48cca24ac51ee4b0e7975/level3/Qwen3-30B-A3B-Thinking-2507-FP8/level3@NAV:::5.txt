

This paper addresses a fundamental open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors provide a negative answer by constructing a general class of counterexamples for GMMs with M ≥ 3 components, demonstrating that the log-likelihood of these bad local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than global optima. This resolves an important theoretical question that had remained open for nearly a decade.

The paper makes two major contributions: first, it establishes structural properties of GMM likelihood surfaces showing the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima; second, it demonstrates algorithmic consequences by proving that EM and gradient EM with random initialization converge to these bad critical points with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)). The authors further show that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, indicating that poor performance stems from bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The manuscript is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with a careful balance between technical content and intuitive explanation. The detailed proof for the simple case (k=3, d=1) provides valuable intuition for the more general results. The results are highly significant for understanding non-convex optimization in the asymptotic limit and have direct implications for the practical use of EM algorithms in GMM estimation.

The primary weakness is the lack of estimates for the constants in the probability bounds, which would be valuable for understanding the phase transition and when bad-solution convergence appears for realistic values of M. While the authors provide a detailed theoretical argument, the absence of concrete constant estimates or supporting simulations limits the practical interpretation of the exponential probability bounds. However, as noted in the key points, this does not detract from the paper's novelty or impact.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs and careful treatment of the problem. It makes a clear, significant contribution to the understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces and the behavior of optimization algorithms. The work is original and addresses a well-defined open problem with important theoretical and practical implications. The authors properly contextualize their work within the NIPS literature, particularly referencing Srebro (2007) and Balakrishnan et al. (2015).

Overall, this is a high-quality, significant contribution that resolves an important open question with clear theoretical and practical implications. The minor weakness regarding constant estimates does not diminish the paper's substantial impact, and I recommend acceptance.
