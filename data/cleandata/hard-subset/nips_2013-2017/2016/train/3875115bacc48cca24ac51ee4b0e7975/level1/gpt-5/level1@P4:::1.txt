Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the infinite-sample (population) log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 equally weighted, unit-variance spherical components. It establishes two main results. First, even for well-separated mixtures, the population likelihood can have suboptimal local maxima whose values are arbitrarily worse than the global maximum, thereby resolving a conjecture posed by Srebro (2007) in the negative. The construction is explicit in d = 1: for M = 3, two true centers are close, one is far, and a configuration that merges the close pair and duplicates the far one forms the basis of a neighborhood containing a strict local maximum with arbitrarily large likelihood gap. The argument proceeds by exhibiting a neighborhood that excludes any global optimum and showing the likelihood on its boundary is strictly lower than at the suboptimal configuration, forcing an interior local maximum.

Second, the paper shows that EM and a first-order EM (gradient EM) with random initialization (i.i.d. seeds from the population mixture, the limit of choosing initial centers uniformly from the data) converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). The proof uses a recursively clustered construction of component means: at each level, centers split into two well-separated subclusters. With high probability, random initialization assigns the wrong number of seeds to subclusters at some level, trapping the dynamics in a suboptimal basin; success would require a perfectly balanced assignment across all levels, which occurs with exponentially small probability.

Finally, using tools related to Lee et al. (2016) and Panageas and Piliouras (2016), the paper proves that gradient EM with a constant stepsize s ∈ (0, 1) almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, so failures arise from bad local maxima rather than saddle points. The results contrast with settings (e.g., Loh and Wainwright, 2013; Wang et al., 2014) where nonconvex objectives lack poor local optima, and complement analyses of EM that require good initializations (Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015). They also motivate careful initializers such as moment/spectral methods (Moitra and Valiant, 2010; Hsu and Kakade, 2013).

Strengths:
- Clear negative resolution of a recognized open question (Srebro, 2007), with simple, interpretable constructions in d = 1 for all M ≥ 3.
- Strong algorithmic consequences: rigorous probability bounds showing that random restarts of EM/gradient EM require exponentially many trials in M to succeed on the constructed instances.
- Insightful separation between saddle-point issues and local maxima: formal guarantee that gradient EM avoids strict saddles almost surely.
- Results are stated for the population likelihood, focusing discussion on geometry rather than finite-sample idiosyncrasies; the setup (equal weights, identity covariance) isolates the core nonconvexity.
- Techniques (neighborhood “barrier” arguments; hierarchical clustering construction) are potentially reusable for other nonconvex likelihoods.

Weaknesses:
- Scope restricted to population setting, d = 1 constructions, equal weights, and known spherical covariances. While the authors note finite-sample extensions via empirical process arguments, these are not detailed; care is needed due to noncompactness unless one restricts parameter domains.
- The “well-separated” condition is invoked, but exact constants and how separation scales with M and d in the constructions could be made more explicit. The arbitrarily bad gap relies on particular configurations; practical prevalence is not quantified.
- The negative results rely on carefully designed recursive instances. While valid, they do not delineate regimes where random initialization might still work (e.g., two components, or certain separation geometries), beyond a discussion in the introduction.
- No empirical illustrations. Simple simulations demonstrating EM’s behavior on the constructed instances would aid clarity and confirm the geometry.
- The result on avoiding strict saddles for gradient EM is plausible given smoothness of L for GMMs; the paper claims to avoid a smoothness argument but could instead directly cite analyticity of L as a function of means to simplify assumptions.

Quality:
- Technical claims are well-motivated and appear sound from the provided sketches. Theorems are precisely stated; proof strategies (barrier argument for Theorem 1; hierarchical trapping argument for Theorem 2; dynamical systems reasoning for Theorem 4) are standard and appropriate. The dependence on stepsize s ∈ (0, 1) for gradient EM is reasonable and aligns with prior analyses (Balakrishnan et al., 2015). A more explicit treatment of the finite-sample extension (covering number bounds, uniform convergence over a compact ball) would strengthen completeness.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable. Background and notation are clear; the M = 3 geometry helps intuition. Suggestions:
  - Specify the precise “well-separated” constants used in each theorem and how they scale with M and the desired gap Cgap.
  - State explicitly the compact parameter set used to avoid degeneracies when transferring to finite samples.
  - In Section 3.2, separate randomness of initialization and sample more systematically; a figure illustrating the hierarchical construction would help.
  - Fix minor reference formatting issues.

Originality:
- Resolves a longstanding conjecture about absence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood for GMMs; to my knowledge, this is new and important. The exponentially small success probability for random initialization on well-separated mixtures is also novel and informative. The strict-saddle avoidance for gradient EM builds on known theory, but its application to this setting is useful.

Significance:
- High. EM remains a central tool for mixture estimation; clarifying when and why it can fail even in favorable regimes matters for both theory and practice. The work motivates principled initialization schemes and refines our understanding of nonconvex likelihood geometry in mixtures. While the construction is specialized, the negative result settles an influential conjecture and will likely influence future algorithm design and analysis.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves an open problem with clean, rigorous results.
- Provides sharp, quantitative algorithmic consequences for widely used methods (EM/gradient EM).
- Clear conceptual message: failures due to bad local maxima, not saddles, in this setting.
- Techniques are broadly relevant to nonconvex statistical estimation.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Population-level, 1D constructions with strong modeling simplifications limit immediate practical scope.
- Lack of empirical validation or finite-sample guarantees spelled out in detail.
- Results are negative and instance-specific; limited guidance on typical-case behavior beyond the constructed families.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add simulations showing EM trajectories and basins on the constructed instances, including likelihood values and responsibilities.
- Provide explicit constants and separation requirements in the main text for Theorems 1–3.
- Outline the finite-sample extension with a precise compactness assumption and a uniform law of large numbers argument.
- Discuss whether analogous constructions exist in higher dimensions without relying on 1D embeddings, and clarify the status of the M = 2 case.
