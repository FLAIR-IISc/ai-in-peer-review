This paper investigates the population likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components, focusing on the existence of bad local maxima and the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors build upon the conjecture by Srebro [2007] that the population likelihood has no bad local maxima, proving this conjecture to be false. They demonstrate that such bad local maxima exist and that the EM algorithm, with random initialization, converges to these suboptimal points with high probability. They further show that first-order EM does not converge to saddle points, reinforcing the idea that bad local maxima are the primary reason for poor performance.

The paper is well-motivated, as EM is widely used and theoretical guarantees are limited. The work connects to a rich body of research on GMMs, including identifiability (Allman et al. [2009]), convergence rates (Chen [1995], Ho and Nguyen [2015]), and algorithmic analysis (Dasgupta and Schulman [2007], Balakrishnan et al. [2015]).  The authors clearly position their work within this context.  The presented results address a fundamental open problem raised by Srebro and contribute significantly to the understanding of the optimization landscape of GMMs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a clear and rigorous disproof of Srebro’s conjecture, which is a significant contribution. The analysis of the EM algorithm's convergence behavior to bad local maxima is also novel.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proofs appear rigorous and well-structured, with detailed explanations and appropriate mathematical formalism. The theorems are clearly stated and the consequences are well-explained.
*   **Significance:** The results have practical implications for the use of EM in GMM estimation, highlighting the necessity of careful initialization. The work advances our understanding of the challenges in optimizing non-convex likelihood functions.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear overview of the problem and contributions. The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are relatively easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of the Model:** The analysis focuses on a specific case: equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians. While this simplifies the problem, it’s unclear how generalizable the results are to more complex GMMs with varying weights, covariance structures, or higher dimensions. This limitation could be highlighted more explicitly.
*   **Initial Setup for Theorem 2:** The specific construction in the proof of Theorem 2, while mathematically sound, feels somewhat contrived. A discussion about the likelihood of encountering such a configuration in a real-world scenario would be helpful.
*   **Step Size Restriction in Theorem 3:** The requirement of a step size within (0,1) for Theorem 3 feels potentially restrictive in practice. Further analysis might suggest more flexible bounds on step sizes for the first-order EM algorithm.
*   **Discussion of Practical Initialization Strategies:** While the paper stresses the need for careful initialization, it doesn't delve into concrete strategies beyond random initialization. Mentioning and briefly comparing other initialization methods (e.g., k-means++) would enhance the practical relevance.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper resolves a long-standing open problem in the field.
*   The technical results are rigorous and well-supported.
*   The findings have important implications for the practical application of the EM algorithm.
*   The work is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The limited scope of the analysis (specifically, the assumption of equally weighted, spherical Gaussians) reduces the generalizability of the findings.
*   The theoretical results may not directly translate into immediate improvements in real-world performance without further investigation of more practical initialization strategies.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important theoretical question and provides valuable insights into the behavior of the EM algorithm for GMM estimation. While the limitations in scope should be acknowledged, the novelty, technical soundness, and significance of the results warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS 2016. I would suggest that the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above in a revision if possible, particularly by discussing the generalizability of their results and suggesting avenues for future research on more practical initialization methods.
