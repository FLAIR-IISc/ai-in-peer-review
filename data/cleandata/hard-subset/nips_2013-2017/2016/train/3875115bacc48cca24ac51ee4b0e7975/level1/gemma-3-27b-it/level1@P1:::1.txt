This paper tackles a fundamental question regarding the landscape of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs): are there bad local maxima, even in well-behaved settings? The authors provide a strong negative answer to a conjecture by Srebro [2007], demonstrating the existence of such bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 components. Furthermore, they show that the EM algorithm, with random initialization, is likely to converge to these suboptimal points. The results are theoretically strong and have clear implications for the practical use of EM.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon a significant body of work concerning GMMs, including identifiability (Allman et al. [2009]), rate of convergence (Chen [1995], Ho & Nguyen [2015]), and the analysis of EM (Dasgupta & Schulman [2007], Balakrishnan et al. [2015]). It appropriately positions itself within the existing literature, referencing key contributions and acknowledging the challenges associated with GMM estimation. The connection to broader questions regarding non-convex optimization, and the robustness of maximum likelihood estimation (Donoho & Liu [1988]), adds further context.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Result:** The main result—proving the existence of bad local maxima—resolves an important open problem and provides a foundational understanding of the GMM likelihood landscape.
* **Strong Algorithmic Implications:** The analysis of the EM algorithm’s convergence behavior, coupled with the structural result, provides a compelling reason to be cautious about using EM with random initialization.
* **Rigorous Proofs:** The authors provide detailed proofs for their claims, indicating a solid understanding of the underlying mathematical principles.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, and the technical details are presented in a logical manner.
* **Novelty:** The work introduces novel techniques to analyze the population log-likelihood, promising future applications in other non-convex optimization problems.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Practical Recommendations:** While the paper clearly demonstrates the *potential* for EM to get stuck in bad local maxima, it doesn’t offer concrete, readily implementable solutions for mitigating this issue beyond emphasizing careful initialization.  Discussing practical strategies (e.g., k-means++ initialization, multiple restarts) and their theoretical guarantees (or lack thereof) would strengthen the paper.
* **Focus on a Specific Setting:** The analysis is largely restricted to uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussians with fixed variance.  Extending the results to more general GMMs (e.g., varying weights, non-spherical covariances) would significantly increase the paper’s impact, though this is acknowledged as a direction for future work.  However, it would be helpful to discuss the likely extent to which these results generalize.
* **Step Size Restriction:** Theorem 3 requires a stepsize s ∈ (0,1) for the first-order EM algorithm. While reasonable, it would be beneficial to discuss how sensitive the results are to the choice of stepsize, and if this restriction impacts the practical utility of the first-order method.
* **Density of the Proofs:** While rigorous, the proofs are quite dense and may be challenging for readers not intimately familiar with the technical details. Adding more intuitive explanations or breaking down the proofs into smaller, more manageable steps would improve accessibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental and important theoretical question.
* The results are mathematically rigorous and contribute significantly to our understanding of GMM estimation.
* The findings have clear implications for the practical use of EM and motivate further research into robust initialization strategies.
* The paper is well-written and positioned within the broader literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical recommendations are limited.
* The analysis is restricted to a specific GMM setting.
* The proofs are dense and may hinder accessibility for some readers.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical results are compelling, and the implications for the use of EM are important. The paper meets the criteria for acceptance to NIPS, particularly regarding quality, originality, and significance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting the authors to:

1.  Discuss practical strategies for mitigating the risk of getting stuck in bad local maxima, and potentially analyze their theoretical properties.
2.  Provide a brief discussion of the likely extent to which the results generalize to more general GMM settings.
3.  Elaborate on the sensitivity of the results to the step size chosen in the first-order EM analysis.
4.  Consider adding more intuitive explanations or breaking down the dense proofs into smaller, more digestible steps.
