- Paper proves existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population log-likelihood for M≥3 equally-weighted, well-separated spherical GMMs, refuting Srebro (2007).
- Shows EM (and a first-order variant) with standard random initialization converges to these bad local optima with probability ≥ 1 − e⁻ᶜᴹ, implying exponential random restarts are needed.
- Demonstrates first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, indicating poor local maxima—not saddles—are the primary algorithmic barrier.
- Technical contributions are rigorous, clever, and self-contained, with insightful geometric and recursive constructions (notably Theorems 1 and 2).
- Main weakness: analysis is in the population setting only; finite-sample extension is claimed but not formally sketched or sample-complexity quantified.
- Clarity is excellent: well-written, well-motivated, and provides helpful high-level intuition before technical proofs.
- Original and significant: first formal disproof of Srebro (2007), first proof of arbitrarily bad local maxima in this setting, and a canonical example where local optima—rather than saddles—dominate.
- Practical significance: gives theoretical backing for EM’s unreliability even in favorable GMM settings, underscoring need for better initialization.
- Theoretical significance: advances understanding of non-convex optimization landscapes in latent variable models and will likely spur further research.
- Arguments for acceptance: resolves major open problem, provides fundamental algorithmic insights, and is technically exemplary.
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on population-level analysis and a specific hierarchical construction that may limit claims about prevalence of failure modes.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — strengths substantially outweigh minor weaknesses.
