

**Peer Review**

This paper resolves an open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors demonstrate that for M ≥ 3 components, well-separated, equally-weighted mixtures of spherical Gaussians can have local maxima whose log-likelihood values are arbitrarily worse than global optima. They further show that both the EM algorithm and a first-order variant with random initialization converge to these bad critical points with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)). The paper also establishes that first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, indicating that the poor performance stems from bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

This work builds on foundational GMM literature, including Teicher (1963) on identifiability, Chen (1995) on convergence rates, and Dasgupta and Schulman (2007), Balakrishnan et al. (2015) on EM convergence. While previous work had established local convergence properties for EM under strong separation assumptions, this paper fundamentally challenges the assumption that well-separated GMMs would have well-behaved likelihood surfaces.

**Quality**: The paper is technically rigorous with carefully constructed proofs. The counterexample for M ≥ 3 components is elegant and directly resolves Srebro's conjecture. The authors provide a clear structural analysis of the likelihood surface and carefully distinguish between bad local maxima and saddle points. The theoretical results are well-supported, and the paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the necessity of M ≥ 3). The proof techniques are novel and could inform future work on non-convex optimization.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide strong intuition for their constructions (e.g., the M=3 case), making complex technical content accessible. The notation is consistent and well-defined, and the progression from problem statement to results is logical. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of finite-sample implications, though the authors note these can be established via standard empirical process arguments.

**Originality**: This is a highly original contribution that resolves a long-standing open question. The paper provides the first known counterexample to Srebro's conjecture in the population setting. While Améndola et al. (2015) showed finite-sample bad local maxima exist, this work is the first to establish their existence in the idealized population setting for M ≥ 3. The connection between structural properties of the likelihood surface and algorithmic behavior of EM represents a significant theoretical advance.

**Significance**: The results have substantial theoretical and practical implications. They explain why careful initialization is necessary for EM in practice, even for well-separated mixtures, and motivate the use of alternative initialization schemes (e.g., method of moments). The paper demonstrates that random initialization of EM can fail with exponentially high probability in M, which is a strong theoretical justification for practitioners to avoid this approach. The findings also provide important context for the theoretical analysis of non-convex optimization methods.

**Strengths**: 
- Resolves a fundamental open question in GMM theory
- Provides rigorous counterexample to Srebro's conjecture
- Establishes strong algorithmic guarantees for EM behavior
- Carefully distinguishes between bad local maxima and saddle points
- Well-written and technically sound with novel proof techniques

**Weaknesses**: 
- Results are limited to the population setting (though finite-sample extensions are straightforward)
- The construction is specific to one-dimensional cases (though the authors suggest extension to higher dimensions)
- The paper doesn't provide concrete guidance for improving initialization schemes beyond noting their necessity

**Pro/Con for Acceptance**:
- **Pro**: This is a significant theoretical contribution that resolves an important open question. The results have clear implications for both theory and practice, and the paper is exceptionally well-written with rigorous proofs.
- **Con**: The results are limited to a specific parameter configuration (well-separated, equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians), though this represents a favorable setting where one might hope for good behavior.

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs and non-convex optimization. It provides definitive evidence that bad local maxima can exist in favorable settings, explaining why random initialization of EM often fails in practice. The results are important for guiding both theoretical research and practical algorithm development for GMMs. The paper should be accepted as it advances the field through rigorous, original theoretical work that addresses a long-standing open question.
