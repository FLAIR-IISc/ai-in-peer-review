This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 equally weighted spherical components and delivers two main results. First, it disproves Srebro’s 2007 conjecture by constructing well-separated mixtures whose population log-likelihood admits spurious local maxima with arbitrarily large suboptimality gaps. Second, it shows that EM (and a first-order/gradient-ascent variant) with random initialization converges to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-Ω(M)}, while the first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points—implicating bad local maxima, not saddles, as the primary algorithmic obstacle. The work situates well within NIPS traditions on nonconvex optimization and mixture learning, connecting to analyses of EM’s local convergence (Balakrishnan et al., 2015), separation-based algorithms (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007), moment-based methods (Hsu & Kakade, 2013; Moitra & Valiant, 2010), and recent theory on gradient dynamics and saddle avoidance (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas & Piliouras, 2016).

Quality: The results are technically strong and carefully argued. The construction yielding arbitrarily bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 is clear and resolves an open question. The exponentially small success probability under random initialization leverages a recursive clustering structure; the argument appears sound and insightful. The strict-saddle avoidance statement is well justified via gradient dynamics, with attention to technical conditions.

Clarity: The manuscript is mostly well-organized and readable, with helpful intuition (e.g., the M = 3 geometry). Some proofs are deferred; that is appropriate, but a compact sketch/figure illustrating the recursive construction for Theorem 2 would aid comprehension. Reference formatting needs cleanup.

Originality: High. The paper settles a long-standing conjecture and strengthens our understanding of EM’s failure modes in a canonical model. The probabilistic failure bound under random initialization is new and sharp in its dependence on M.

Significance: The results meaningfully affect how we think about likelihood-based estimation for mixtures: random restarts can be exponentially inadequate, emphasizing principled initialization. While the setting is stylized (equal weights, spherical, d = 1 constructions), the conceptual consequences are broad.

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves a notable open problem; clean, structural insights.
- Strong algorithmic implications with quantitative probability bounds.
- Bridges nonconvex geometry and algorithmic behavior; relevant to practice.

Cons against acceptance:
- Population-level analysis; finite-sample translation is only discussed informally.
- Constructions rely on equal weights/spherical covariances and often d = 1; robustness to mild misspecification is not explored.
- No empirical validation or comparison with modern initializers (e.g., k-means++, spectral/moment pilots).

Suggestions: Add finite-sample guarantees via empirical process bounds, test robustness to slight weight/covariance perturbations, and include experiments illustrating the phenomena and mitigations.
