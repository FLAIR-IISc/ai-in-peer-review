

This paper addresses a fundamental open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding whether Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components can exhibit "bad" local maxima in their population likelihood function. The authors provide two key contributions: first, they construct a counterexample showing that even for well-separated, equally-weighted mixtures of spherical Gaussians with M ≥ 3 components, the population likelihood function can have local maxima with arbitrarily worse log-likelihood values than the global optimum, thereby resolving Srebro's conjecture in the negative. Second, they demonstrate that the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization will converge to such bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^{-Ω(M)}, indicating that careful initialization is essential even in highly favorable settings.

The paper excels in technical quality, providing rigorous proofs of both structural results and algorithmic consequences. The authors carefully distinguish between bad local maxima and saddle points, showing through Theorem 4 that first-order EM avoids strict saddle points almost surely, thus attributing poor performance to bad local maxima rather than saddle points. The clarity is excellent, with well-organized presentation and sufficient mathematical detail for reproducibility. The originality is strong, as the paper resolves a longstanding open question in a setting where previous work (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) had only addressed two-component cases. The significance is high, as the findings have direct implications for practitioners using EM in GMM estimation, demonstrating that random initialization can fail with exponentially high probability in M.

Strengths: Rigorous theoretical contribution resolving an important open question; clear distinction between local maxima and saddle points; significant practical implications for EM algorithm usage.

Weaknesses: The focus on the idealized case of spherical Gaussians with fixed variances, while theoretically clean, may limit direct applicability to more complex GMM settings. The paper could better discuss how these results connect to recent work on non-convex optimization (e.g., Lee et al., 2016).

Pro: Resolves a long-standing open question with significant theoretical and practical implications.  
Con: Limited to the idealized spherical case; the practical relevance for more complex GMMs needs further exploration.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances our theoretical understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes and has clear implications for algorithmic practice. The paper meets all NIPS criteria and should be accepted.
