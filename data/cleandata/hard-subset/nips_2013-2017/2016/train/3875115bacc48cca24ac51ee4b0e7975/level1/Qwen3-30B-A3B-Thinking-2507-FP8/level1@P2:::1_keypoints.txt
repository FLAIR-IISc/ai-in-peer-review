- Paper resolves Srebro (2007) conjecture by showing population GMM likelihood can have bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 even for equally-weighted, well-separated spherical Gaussians.
- Demonstrates local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than global maxima in the population log-likelihood.
- Shows EM and a first-order EM variant with random initialization converge to bad critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{-Ω(M)}.
- Proves first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, attributing failures to bad local maxima instead.
- Presents an elegant M=3 construction and geometric intuition: two true centers collapse while the third is placed correctly, creating a suboptimal local maximum.
- Technical proofs are rigorous, with careful constructions and detailed likelihood landscape analysis.
- Authors clearly distinguish population vs finite-sample settings and relate work to prior identifiability, EM convergence, and method-of-moments literature.
- Algorithmic results have practical implications, explaining why many EM restarts are often necessary.
- Paper addresses the distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points using recent gradient-method results.
- Limitation: results only cover M ≥ 3, leaving the important M=2 case unresolved.
- Limitation: focus is on population (infinite-sample) setting, though authors argue extension to large finite samples is standard.
- Limitation: lacks experimental validation to complement theoretical findings.
- Arguments for acceptance: resolves a fundamental open question, rigorous proofs, practical relevance, and clear distinction from prior work.
- Arguments against acceptance: omission of M=2 case and primary focus on population likelihood may reduce perceived direct applicability to finite-sample practice.
- Overall recommendation: high-quality theoretical contribution that should be accepted (meets NIPS criteria: quality, clarity, originality, significance).
