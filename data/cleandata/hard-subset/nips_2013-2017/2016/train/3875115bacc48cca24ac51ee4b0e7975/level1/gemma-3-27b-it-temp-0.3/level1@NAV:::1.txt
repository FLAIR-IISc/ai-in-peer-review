## Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper investigates the challenging problem of local optima in the likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The authors provide two key results: first, they demonstrate the existence of bad local maxima even in well-separated, equally-weighted mixtures of Gaussians, resolving a conjecture by Srebro [2007]. Second, they show that the EM algorithm, with random initialization, converges to these bad critical points with high probability. This work builds upon a long line of research into the theoretical properties of GMMs, including early work on identifiability (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009) and more recent investigations into convergence rates (Chen, 1995; Ho & Nguyen, 2015). It also connects to the broader literature on the EM algorithm’s convergence properties (Dempster et al., 1977; Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015).

The paper’s strengths lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and clear presentation. The proof of the existence of bad local maxima is a significant contribution, directly addressing a long-standing open question. The probabilistic analysis of the EM algorithm’s convergence is also compelling, highlighting the sensitivity of the algorithm to initialization. The authors carefully establish the conditions under which the results hold and acknowledge limitations. The connection drawn between the existence of bad local maxima and the performance of first-order EM, supported by Theorem 4, is particularly insightful. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, and the paper provides sufficient detail to understand the main ideas.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of these findings. While the authors mention the need for careful initialization, a more concrete discussion of effective initialization strategies (beyond those already in the literature) would be valuable. Furthermore, the focus is entirely on the idealized setting of fixed covariance matrices. Exploring the impact of estimating covariance alongside the means could reveal additional complexities. While the proofs appear sound, the technical details are relegated to the appendices, potentially hindering accessibility for readers less familiar with the specific mathematical tools employed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a fundamental open problem in GMM theory.
*   Provides rigorous theoretical results with clear implications for algorithm design.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.
*   Contributes to a deeper understanding of the non-convexity of the GMM likelihood.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of practical initialization strategies.
*   Focuses on a simplified setting (fixed covariance).
*   Technical details are largely confined to appendices.

**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs and the EM algorithm. The results are important and well-supported, and the paper is of high quality. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
