This paper provides a fundamental analysis of the population log-likelihood surface for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The authors present two main results. First, they show that for mixtures with M≥3 components, the likelihood function contains bad local maxima, even in the idealized setting of well-separated, spherical Gaussians. This definitively resolves a long-standing conjecture by Srebro (2007) to the contrary. Second, they demonstrate a significant algorithmic consequence: the EM algorithm, when started from a common random initialization scheme, converges to these suboptimal critical points with a probability approaching 1 exponentially in M. The work is well-contextualized, building upon prior analyses of EM that focused on local convergence (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and contrasting with alternative methods that provide global guarantees (e.g., Hsu & Kakade, 2013).

The primary strength of this paper is its significance. Resolving the Srebro conjecture is a major theoretical contribution that clarifies our understanding of the GMM likelihood landscape. The originality of the constructive proofs is also a highlight; the GMM instances built to demonstrate these pathological properties are insightful. The paper’s quality is excellent; the results appear technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by clear proof sketches. Furthermore, the finding that a first-order EM variant avoids strict saddle points adds a crucial insight to the ongoing discussion in non-convex optimization, suggesting that for GMMs, poor local maxima are indeed the primary algorithmic barrier. The paper is also very clearly written and well-organized.

The paper has few weaknesses. The analysis is confined to the population setting, and a brief discussion of the sample complexity required for these phenomena to appear in practice would be a welcome addition, though its absence does not detract from the core results.

Arguments for acceptance are numerous. The paper is a complete and impactful piece of work that solves an open problem, provides strong negative results for a widely used algorithm, and introduces novel analytical techniques. The results are highly likely to be built upon by researchers studying both mixture models and non-convex optimization more broadly. The main argument against might be that the analysis is in the idealized population setting, but the authors correctly note that the results can be extended to the finite-sample regime. Overall, this is a landmark paper in the theoretical understanding of GMMs and represents a clear and significant contribution to the field.
