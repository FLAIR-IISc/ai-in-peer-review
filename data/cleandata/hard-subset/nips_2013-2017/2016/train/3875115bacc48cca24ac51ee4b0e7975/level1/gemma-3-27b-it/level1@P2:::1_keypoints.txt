- Tackles whether bad local optima exist in the population likelihood for GMMs and their impact on EM.
- Proves bad local optima do exist, contradicting Srebro (2007), even for well-separated Gaussian components.
- Shows EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to these suboptimal points with high probability.
- Demonstrates failures are due to local maxima rather than saddle points (Theorem 4).
- Provides a concrete counterexample (Theorem 1) and rigorous, technically strong proofs for all main results.
- Proofs are lengthy but appear sound and build logically, connecting to recent gradient-method results.
- Paper is exceptionally well-motivated, situating the work within relevant literature on identifiability and EM analysis.
- Clarity and organization are high: precise writing, consistent notation, helpful background and definitions.
- Original contributions include the construction of the GMM instance with bad optima and analysis distinguishing maxima vs. saddles.
- Results are significant: implications for practical EM usage and the need for robust initialization strategies.
- Advances understanding of non-convex optimization and challenges in escaping local optima.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical results, high originality, clear presentation, and substantial impact.
- Arguments against acceptance: offers limited immediate practical remedies and contains technically complex proofs that may limit accessibility.
- Overall recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance.
