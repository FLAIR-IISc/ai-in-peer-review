- Addresses Srebro (2007) conjecture and gives a definitive negative answer: even well-behaved GMMs can have spurious local maxima.

- Theorem 1 (structural): For any M ≥ 3, there exist GMMs whose population log-likelihood has local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global maximum.

- Construction idea: arrange true Gaussian centers so that merging two nearby components and splitting a distant one yields a stable, suboptimal local maximum.

- Theorem 2 & 3 (algorithmic): Standard EM and a first-order/gradient-ascent variant, when initialized by sampling centers from the true distribution, converge to a bad critical point with probability ≥ 1 − e^(−cM).

- High-probability failure proof: uses a novel recursive GMM construction that makes the set of “good” initializations exponentially small in M.

- Theorem 4: First-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points (via Lee et al., 2016), implying observed failures stem from bad local maxima, not saddles.

- Key implication: algorithmic failures of EM are due to bad local maxima, contrasting with other non-convex problems where saddles dominate.

- Quality: technically strong, rigorous proofs, novel and self-contained theoretical contributions.

- Minor weakness: analysis limited to the population setting; a brief finite-sample discussion (sample complexity bounds) would improve practical relevance.

- Clarity: exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear statements and intuitive proof outlines.

- Originality: first formal refutation of Srebro’s conjecture; novel geometric and recursive techniques.

- Significance — theory: establishes a surprising, fundamental property of GMM likelihood landscapes even under idealized conditions.

- Significance — algorithmic: formalizes why random restarts are inefficient (exponential trials) and motivates advanced initialization methods (method-of-moments, spectral).

- Broader impact: provides an important counterexample in non-convex optimization by showing bad local maxima can be the main barrier.

- Acceptance arguments: resolves a long-standing open problem, offers deep insights and practical implications, and is technically sound and well-presented; main cons are population-only analysis and specificity of constructions.
