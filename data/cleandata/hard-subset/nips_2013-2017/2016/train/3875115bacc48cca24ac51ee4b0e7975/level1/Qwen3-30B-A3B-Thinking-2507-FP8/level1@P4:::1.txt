

This paper addresses an open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding whether Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components have bad local maxima in their population likelihood function. The authors provide two fundamental results: first, they construct a GMM with M ≥ 3 well-separated, equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians whose population log-likelihood contains bad local maxima (with log-likelihood arbitrarily worse than global maxima); second, they show that with random initialization, the EM algorithm and its first-order variant converge to these bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)).

The paper is well-motivated by the widespread use of GMMs and the dominant role of the EM algorithm for parameter estimation. The authors carefully contextualize their work within previous theoretical advances on GMMs, including identifiability results (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009), convergence rates (Chen, 1995; Ho and Nguyen, 2015), and algorithmic analyses (Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015).

The technical contributions are significant and well-executed. Theorem 1 provides a concrete counterexample to Srebro's conjecture by constructing a GMM with arbitrarily bad local maxima. The authors carefully analyze the structure of the likelihood surface, showing that the log-likelihood value at these bad local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than that at global optima. Theorem 2 establishes the algorithmic consequence: with random initialization, the EM algorithm converges to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability. Theorem 3 extends this result to the first-order EM algorithm, and Theorem 4 provides a key distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points by showing that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely.

The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The proofs are rigorous and the mathematical arguments are well-constructed. The authors carefully distinguish between the different types of critical points (local maxima vs. saddle points) and provide a clear theoretical explanation for the poor performance of random initialization. The paper also acknowledges the limitations of their results (e.g., the construction requires M ≥ 3, and the results apply to the population setting rather than finite samples).

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The writing is precise, the organization is logical, and the main results are clearly stated and explained. The authors provide sufficient mathematical detail for experts to follow the proofs while maintaining readability. The paper effectively uses geometric intuition to explain the construction of bad local maxima.

Regarding novelty, this paper resolves a long-standing open question that has been discussed in the community since Srebro (2007). While previous work has established local convergence guarantees for EM under certain separation conditions (Balakrishnan et al., 2015), the authors provide a negative result showing that even in favorable settings, random initialization can fail with high probability. The distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points is particularly novel and insightful.

The significance of this work is high. The findings have important practical implications for the use of EM algorithms in GMM estimation, suggesting that careful initialization strategies are necessary even in well-separated settings. The results provide a theoretical foundation for understanding the limitations of random initialization in non-convex optimization for GMMs. The paper also opens up new research directions, such as the necessity of at least three components for the existence of bad local maxima.

One minor limitation is that the results are restricted to the population setting (infinite samples) rather than finite samples. While the authors note that their results can be extended to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments, the paper does not provide these extensions explicitly. Additionally, the paper focuses on the specific case of spherical Gaussians with equal weights, so the results do not apply to all GMMs.

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of Gaussian mixture models and the EM algorithm. The results are technically sound, clearly presented, and address an important open question in the field. The paper provides strong evidence that careful initialization is necessary even in well-separated settings, which has important implications for practical applications of GMMs.

Strengths: Resolves an important open question, technically sound proofs, clear distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points, strong practical implications, excellent writing and organization.

Weaknesses: Limited to population setting and specific GMM configurations (spherical, equal weights, M ≥ 3).
