Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood of equally-weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). It resolves an open conjecture by Srebro (COLT’07) by proving that for M ≥ 3 components there exist well-separated, 1D GMMs whose population likelihood has bad local maxima with arbitrarily large suboptimality gaps relative to the global maximum. Algorithmically, the authors show that EM and a first-order EM (gradient EM) initialized by sampling centers from the mixture (the asymptotic analogue of choosing random data points) converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^−Ω(M). They also prove that gradient EM with a suitable step size almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that failures arise from bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

These results complement prior positive guarantees for EM under good initialization and local convergence (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., Annals of Statistics 2015), as well as algorithmic methods based on clustering and moments for well-separated GMMs (e.g., Vempala & Wang ’02; Arora et al. ’05; Dasgupta & Schulman ’07; Moitra & Valiant ’10; Hsu & Kakade ’13). The insights also connect to recent work on nonconvex landscapes and saddle-point avoidance by first-order methods (e.g., Ge et al. ’15; Lee et al. ’16; Panageas & Piliouras ’16).

Strengths:
- Resolves a natural and visible open conjecture, sharpening our understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes in the most favorable spherical, equal-weight setting.
- Clean structural construction for M = 3 (two close centers and one far) that yields arbitrarily bad local maxima; the proof strategy—exclude global optima from a neighborhood and show boundary likelihood is lower—seems robust and insightful.
- Clear algorithmic implications: random-initialized EM/gradient EM can fail with exponentially high probability in M via a recursive clustering construction.
- Clarifies that gradient EM avoids strict saddles almost surely, focusing attention on local maxima as the culprit—an important conceptual contribution amid ongoing discussions about saddles vs. local minima in nonconvex optimization.
- Results are at the population level but are plausibly transferrable to large finite samples via standard empirical process arguments.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Population-to-sample translation: The paper asserts that results “carry over in a straightforward way.” It would strengthen the contribution to provide explicit uniform deviation bounds (e.g., covering number or Rademacher complexity for log-sum-exp likelihood) quantifying n needed so that empirical EM exhibits the same failure with high probability.
- Separation and gap quantification: The definition of “well-separated” (Ω(√d)) is standard, but the theorems allow arbitrary Cgap. More guidance on how the suboptimality gap scales with separation and dimension, and explicit choices of centers realizing a given Cgap, would improve clarity and reproducibility. In particular, in 1D, specifying concrete positions and a figure illustrating the landscape would help.
- Initialization models: The negative results target random seeding from the mixture/data. Many practitioners use k-means++, spectral/moment-based pilots, or pruning/over-seeding heuristics (e.g., two-round EM). A discussion of whether the recursive “cluster-within-cluster” failure mode persists under these initializations (or if they provably mitigate it) would add practical context.
- First-order EM convergence: The claim of almost-sure avoidance of strict saddles with constant step size s ∈ (0,1) is appealing. Please clarify the precise regularity conditions used in lieu of global smoothness/Lipschitz gradient assumptions typically required to invoke stable manifold arguments, and whether the update map is a diffeomorphism on the relevant domain.
- Scope of model: The focus is equal weights and identity covariances. A short discussion on whether analogous constructions extend to unequal weights/diagonal covariances (likely easier) and to full covariances would broaden impact.
- Experiments/illustrations: While theory-focused, small numerical experiments on the constructed instances (e.g., 1D M=3) showing the likelihood surface and EM trajectories would greatly aid intuition.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: Theorems are stated clearly with plausible proof strategies; the constructions are well-motivated. Providing more explicit constants/parameters and a finite-sample translation would further solidify the technical contribution.
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized and situates results in prior literature. The geometric intuition for M=3 is helpful; adding explicit constructions/figures and clarifying the step-size and smoothness aspects in Theorem 4 would improve accessibility.
- Originality: High. The paper answers an open question, introduces a recursive failure construction for EM, and connects saddle-avoidance results to the EM setting.
- Significance: High. EM/GMMs are ubiquitous; showing that even in benign settings random-initialized EM fails with exponentially high probability underscores the necessity of careful initialization and informs both theory and practice.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves a notable conjecture and fills a gap in understanding the GMM likelihood landscape.
- Strong, general negative results with clear algorithmic consequences for widely used methods.
- Conceptually clarifies the role of bad local maxima vs. saddles for gradient EM.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Results are at the population level; finite-sample instantiations and explicit quantitative bounds are not fully developed.
- Practical initialization schemes beyond naive random seeding are not analyzed; lack of empirical demonstrations on the constructed instances.

Minor comments:
- Clarify how “well-separated” coexists with “two centers much closer than distances to the third”; i.e., all pairs can be large in absolute terms while exhibiting a strong imbalance.
- State constants (e.g., c in 1 − e^−cM) and dependence on separation if possible.
- Typographical polish in the references and ensure consistent citation formats.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper, timely and relevant to NIPS, with compelling results and clear implications. I recommend acceptance, conditional on addressing the clarifications above.
