- Paper proves existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in population log-likelihood for equally-weighted, spherical GMMs with M ≥ 3, resolving Srebro’s conjecture negatively.
- Provides intuitive M=3 construction and rigorous neighborhood/boundary argument showing a suboptimal local maximum can be forced.
- Shows EM and first-order EM with random initialization (centers i.i.d. from true mixture) converge to suboptimal critical points with probability 1−e^−Ω(M), implying e^Ω(M) restarts needed.
- Uses a recursive hierarchical clustering of true means to argue random initial centers become trapped within clusters and never reallocate across large gaps.
- Proves first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, so failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.
- Technical construction and probability arguments are plausible and well-motivated; detailed constants and probability calculations are deferred to the appendix.
- Assumption that EM cannot cross large inter-cluster gaps is standard and justified by responsibility decay under strong separation.
- Results are population-level; finite-sample analogues are claimed to follow by standard tools but explicit sample-complexity/separation bounds are not provided.
- Paper is mostly clear with good intuition for M=3, but has minor issues: malformed citations, a cross-reference typo, and imprecise quantification of “well-separated” and Cgap dependence.
- Recommends adding a schematic/figure for the hierarchical construction and EM trapping dynamics to aid intuition.
- Originality: novel negative resolution of Srebro’s conjecture and new exponential failure characterization for random initialization.
- Significance: strong implications for practice—random EM seeding is unreliable even with infinite data; motivates careful initialization methods.
- Suggestions: provide explicit finite-sample statements, analyze common initializations (k-means++, PCA), include empirical demonstrations, clarify separation/scale choices and dependence of exponential constant, and discuss advanced EM variants (split-merge, annealing).
- Arguments for acceptance: resolves an open problem, gives sharp algorithmic consequences, and connects to nonconvex optimization theory.
- Arguments against acceptance: main results are population-level without quantified finite-sample instantiation, and lacking experimental validation or practical mitigation advice.
- Overall recommendation: Accept—fundamental, well-executed contribution with clear theoretical and practical implications.
