- Paper resolves Srebro (2007) open question by showing GMMs with M ≥ 3 can have bad local maxima in the population likelihood.  
- Theorem 1: constructs M ≥ 3 well-separated, equal-weight spherical Gaussians whose population log-likelihood has arbitrarily bad local maxima.  
- Theorem 2: with random initialization, the EM algorithm converges to these bad local maxima with probability ≥ 1 - e^{-Ω(M)}.  
- Theorem 3: the same high-probability failure holds for the first-order EM algorithm.  
- Theorem 4: first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, distinguishing bad local maxima from saddles.  
- Technical contributions are rigorous, with careful analysis of the likelihood surface and clear mathematical proofs.  
- Authors clearly distinguish local maxima vs. saddle points and explain why random initialization performs poorly.  
- Paper is well-motivated and well-contextualized within prior identifiability and algorithmic GMM literature.  
- Novelty: resolves a long-standing open problem and provides a negative result even in favorable, well-separated settings.  
- Practical significance: shows careful initialization is necessary for EM in GMMs, even with separation.  
- Limitations: results are in the population (infinite-sample) setting and restricted to spherical, equal-weight mixtures with M ≥ 3.  
- Presentation is clear and readable, with strong organization and useful geometric intuition.
