This paper provides two fundamental results on the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models with M ≥ 3 components. The first main result shows that the population likelihood function has bad local maxima even in the special case of equally-weighted mixtures of well-separated and spherical Gaussians. The authors prove that the log-likelihood value of these bad local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than that of any global optimum, resolving an open question of Srebro [2007]. The second main result shows that the EM algorithm (or a first-order variant of it) with random initialization will converge to bad critical points with probability at least 1− e−Ω(M).

The paper is well-written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The introduction provides a clear background on Gaussian mixture models and the EM algorithm, and the authors do a great job of explaining the significance of their results. The technical proofs are relegated to the appendices, making the main body of the paper accessible to a broad audience.

The results in this paper are significant because they highlight the challenges of estimating Gaussian mixture models using the EM algorithm. The authors show that even in the idealized setting where each component is uniformly weighted, spherical with unit variance, and well-separated, the EM algorithm can still converge to bad local maxima with high probability. This has important implications for practitioners who use the EM algorithm to estimate Gaussian mixture models.

One of the strengths of the paper is its clarity and organization. The authors do a great job of breaking down complex technical arguments into smaller, more manageable pieces. The paper is also well-motivated, with a clear explanation of the significance of the results.

In terms of weaknesses, the paper could benefit from more discussion of the implications of the results for practice. While the authors do a great job of explaining the significance of their results in the context of Gaussian mixture models, they could say more about how these results might affect the use of the EM algorithm in other contexts.

Overall, I would rate the quality of the paper as excellent. The results are significant, the paper is well-written and well-organized, and the authors do a great job of explaining the significance of their results.
