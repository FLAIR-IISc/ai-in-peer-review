Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper gives structural and algorithmic negative results for maximum-likelihood estimation in Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) in the population (infinite-sample) setting, focusing on equally-weighted, spherical, unit-variance mixtures. The first result resolves a conjecture of Srebro (COLT’07) by constructing, for any M ≥ 3, well-separated 1D mixtures with bad local maxima of the population log-likelihood whose value can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. The construction is intuitive for M=3: two true means relatively closer to each other than to the third, and an assignment with two hypothesized centers collapsed onto one true mean and the remaining hypothesized center between the other two, around which a suboptimal local maximum exists. The proof proceeds by identifying a neighborhood excluding global optima and showing the boundary has strictly lower likelihood, forcing a local maximum inside.

The second result studies EM and a first-order/gradient variant under a practical random initialization scheme that, in the population limit, is equivalent to drawing initial centers i.i.d. from the true mixture. The authors show that with probability at least 1 − e^−Ω(M), EM and first-order EM converge to suboptimal critical points with gap Cgap > 0, so successful recovery would require e^Ω(M) restarts. The construction uses a recursively clustered arrangement of true means to argue that, with high probability, random initial centers become “trapped” within clusters and never reallocate across large gaps under EM dynamics. A final result shows first-order EM avoids strict saddle points almost surely (building on Lee et al. 2016; Panageas & Piliouras 2016), so the failure is due to bad local maxima, not saddles.

These results complement prior theoretical analyses of EM’s local convergence under good initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., Annals’15) and algorithmic alternatives (two-round EM: Dasgupta & Schulman ’07; method-of-moments/spectral: Hsu & Kakade ’13; Moitra & Valiant ’10). The paper also relates to finite-sample pathologies (Améndola et al. ’15) and recent viewpoints on saddle points (Pascanu et al. ’14).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The structural construction is plausible and well-motivated. The neighborhood/boundary likelihood argument for existence of a suboptimal local maximum is standard and appropriate. The “arbitrarily bad gap” follows by separating clusters farther. The EM failure proof leveraging hierarchical clustering of means to drive an exponentially small favorable initialization probability is convincing at a high level; a careful probability calculation is critical (e.g., multinomial occupancy across clusters at multiple levels), and placing all constants and separation scales in the appendix is reasonable. The assumption that EM dynamics cannot move centers across large inter-cluster gaps in the population setting is standard and justified by responsibility decay under strong separation. The first-order EM non-convergence to strict saddles is consistent with gradient dynamics theory; the claim that the proof avoids extra smoothness assumptions is interesting, though the objective here is indeed smooth, so clarifying what is technically avoided would help.
- Completeness: The results are at the population level; the paper states that finite-sample analogues follow by standard empirical-process tools, but providing explicit sample complexity/separation conditions would strengthen the contribution.

Clarity:
The paper is mostly clear and well-organized, with good intuition for M=3. A few minor issues:
- Some citation entries are malformed/incomplete.
- A cross-reference typo: random initialization is in Section 2.3 (referred to as 2.2 once).
- It would help to quantify “well-separated” concretely in the constructions (exact constants vs. Ω(√d)) and to explicitly state how Cgap depends on separation.
- A small figure/visualization of the hierarchical construction and EM trapping dynamics would aid intuition.

Originality:
Resolving Srebro’s conjecture negatively for well-separated, spherical, equally-weighted mixtures is new and important. The exponential failure probability with random initialization and the strict-saddle avoidance result for first-order EM together offer a coherent picture of why naive local search fails in this benign regime.

Significance:
The results have clear implications for practice: random seeding of EM is unreliable in general even with infinite data in simple GMMs when M≥3, so careful initialization (e.g., spectral/moment-based or k-means++-style seeding) is essential. The constructions delineate the limits of local-search heuristics and will likely inform both theory (nonconvex landscape analyses) and practice (robust initialization protocols). The two-component case remains open and is practically meaningful; clarifying that frontier would be impactful.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide explicit finite-sample statements (n vs. separation vs. M, with high-probability bounds) to make the transfer from population to sample concrete.
- Discuss how widely used initializations (e.g., k-means++ seeding or PCA-based spreads) fare on the constructed instances; can they avoid the exponential failure?
- Consider empirical demonstrations on finite samples showing the likelihood gap and convergence behavior as M grows.
- Clarify the exact separation and scale choices that yield a given Cgap, and the dependence of the exponential constant c on these parameters.
- Expand on whether split-merge EM, deterministic annealing EM, or continuation methods can escape the bad basins in your constructions.
- Clean up references and cross-references.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves a well-known open conjecture with clean constructions.
- Sharp and informative algorithmic consequences (exponential failure under standard random initialization).
- Solid connection to broader nonconvex optimization theory (strict saddle avoidance).

Arguments against acceptance:
- Results are purely population-level in the main text; finite-sample instantiation is deferred and not quantified.
- Experimental/empirical validation is absent; even small-scale demos would strengthen the message.
- Practical guidance on mitigations (beyond “careful initialization”) could be expanded.

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a fundamental and well-executed contribution to our understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes and EM’s limitations, with clear implications for both theory and practice.
