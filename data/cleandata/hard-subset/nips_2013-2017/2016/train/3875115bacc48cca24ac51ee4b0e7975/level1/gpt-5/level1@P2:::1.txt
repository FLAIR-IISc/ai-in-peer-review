Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper establishes fundamental negative results about the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 equally weighted, spherical components (unit variance). The first result constructs well-separated instances in which the population likelihood admits suboptimal local maxima whose value can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, thereby resolving an open conjecture of Srebro (COLT’07) that suggested benign landscapes in this idealized setting. The construction is explicit in 1D and relies on placing components in a way that creates neighborhoods with strictly lower boundary likelihood, guaranteeing a local (but poor) maximum.

The second set of results concerns algorithmic consequences for EM and a first-order EM (gradient ascent on the population likelihood). Under a widely used and realistic random initialization scheme (sampling initial centers from the mixture), the authors prove that both EM and first-order EM converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^−Ω(M) on the constructed instances. They further show first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points (adapting ideas from Lee et al. COLT’16 and Panageas & Piliouras ’16), implying the failures are attributable to bad local maxima rather than saddles. These results complement prior local analyses of EM that required good initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. Annals’15) and strengthen finite-sample constructions showing many local maxima (Améndola et al. 2015) by working at the population level. They also contrast with algorithmic guarantees for moment/spectral and clustering-based approaches under separation (Arora & Kannan ’05; Vempala & Wang ’02; Hsu & Kakade ’13; Moitra & Valiant ’10).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The theorems are clearly stated and the proof strategies are plausible and well-motivated. The construction for bad local maxima uses a careful neighborhood argument around a suboptimal assignment (e.g., merging two nearby true components and duplicating a far component), and the “boundary lower than interior” argument yields a local maximum. The probability bound for random initialization uses a recursive, multi-level clustering construction that forces exponentially many favorable allocation events; the trapping argument (once centers initialize into cluster neighborhoods they remain there) is intuitive under large separation and aligns with EM’s soft assignments. The strict-saddle non-convergence for first-order EM leverages contemporary dynamical systems results; the authors explain how they avoid a blanket smoothness assumption by exploiting structure of the EM gradient map.
- Assumptions and scope: Results are in the population setting with equal weights and known, identical covariances; they emphasize well-separated means. The authors note that standard empirical process arguments can carry conclusions to large finite samples, but explicit sample complexity statements are not provided. The bad-local-maxima construction is in 1D; while one can embed 1D pathologies into higher dimensions, making this explicit would strengthen the generality claim. The gradient-EM step-size restriction s ∈ (0,1) seems mild, but a brief discussion of sensitivity to s (e.g., whether larger s could avoid traps via overshooting) would be useful.
- Rigor: Proof sketches are convincing; full proofs are deferred to the appendix. Citing and positioning relative to prior results is appropriate.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The background on EM and first-order EM is self-contained, and the population vs sample objectives are clearly delineated. The geometric intuition for M=3 helps. Suggestions: include a figure illustrating the hierarchical cluster construction used in Theorem 2, and state explicit separation scales where possible to aid reproducibility. Minor typos in the references could be cleaned.

Originality:
- High. The paper resolves a notable conjecture about the landscape of GMM likelihoods, provides new lower bounds on the success probability of widely used local-search heuristics under realistic initialization, and tightens the narrative around saddle points vs bad local maxima in nonconvex learning.

Significance:
- Conceptual significance is strong. EM is ubiquitous; showing that even in the favorable, population, well-separated, equal-weight, spherical case the landscape can be arbitrarily deceptive—and that random initialization leads to failure with exponentially high probability—has clear implications for both theory and practice. It motivates principled initialization (moments/spectral) and cautions against naive multi-restart strategies that scale poorly with M. The strict-saddle result helps refine our understanding of failure modes in nonconvex objectives.

Pros:
- Resolves Srebro’s conjecture with a clean counterexample.
- Sharp, quantitative failure probability for EM/first-order EM under common initialization.
- Careful connection to modern saddle-point avoidance results clarifies the mechanism of failure.
- Clear exposition and relevant context to prior work.

Cons and suggestions:
- Population focus: The finite-sample translation is mentioned but not quantified. Providing a theorem that specifies n as a function of separation and M under which the sample likelihood inherits the bad local maxima and the EM failure would strengthen practical relevance.
- Dimensionality: Theorem 1 is explicitly 1D. An explicit statement that the construction embeds into higher dimensions (and whether separation needs to scale with √d) would preempt scope concerns.
- Initialization model: The negative results target random-from-data initialization. A brief empirical or theoretical discussion of popular alternatives (e.g., k-means++, spectral/moment initializers) would contextualize how practitioners can avoid the pitfalls.
- Two-component case: Since M=2 remains open and practically important, any partial progress (e.g., a no-bad-local-maxima guarantee under certain separations) would be valuable, or at least a more detailed discussion of obstacles.

Recommendation:
Strong accept. The paper is technically solid, original, and impactful. It clarifies a long-standing conjecture and provides crisp algorithmic consequences that will influence how EM and related methods are analyzed and used. With minor improvements addressing finite-sample translation and multidimensional embedding, the paper would be even stronger.
