This paper provides two fundamental results on the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components. The first main result shows that the population likelihood function has bad local maxima even in the special case of equally-weighted mixtures of well-separated and spherical Gaussians. The authors prove that the log-likelihood value of these bad local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than that of any global optimum, thereby resolving an open question of Srebro [2007]. The second main result shows that the EM algorithm (or a first-order variant of it) with random initialization will converge to bad critical points with probability at least 1− e−Ω(M). The authors further establish that a first-order variant of EM will not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, indicating that the poor performance of the first-order method can be attributed to the existence of bad local maxima rather than bad saddle points.

Strengths:
- The paper provides a clear and concise introduction to the problem of estimating Gaussian mixture models, including the challenges associated with the non-convexity of the log-likelihood function.
- The authors provide a thorough review of the existing literature on GMMs, including the identifiability of parameters, the rates of convergence for maximum likelihood estimation, and the algorithmic guarantees for the EM algorithm.
- The paper presents two main results that provide new insights into the behavior of the population likelihood function of GMMs. The first result shows that the population likelihood function has bad local maxima, while the second result shows that the EM algorithm with random initialization will converge to bad critical points with high probability.
- The authors provide a detailed proof of their results, including a geometrical description of their construction for M = 3.

Weaknesses:
- The paper assumes that the mixture components are equally weighted and spherical with unit variance, which may not be realistic in practice.
- The authors do not provide any empirical results to support their theoretical findings.
- The paper does not discuss the implications of their results for other optimization algorithms, such as gradient descent or quasi-Newton methods.
