This paper addresses fundamental questions about the optimization landscape of the population log-likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The central theme is an investigation of a conjecture by Srebro (2007) which posited that for well-behaved GMMs (e.g., equally weighted, spherical, well-separated), the population likelihood function has no spurious local maxima. The authors provide a definitive negative answer to this question.

The paper’s main contributions are twofold. First, it presents a structural result (Theorem 1) demonstrating that for any number of components M ≥ 3, there exist GMMs whose population log-likelihood possesses local maxima with values that can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. The construction cleverly arranges true Gaussian centers such that a suboptimal configuration of estimated centers (e.g., merging two nearby true components into one estimated component while splitting a distant true component into two) becomes a stable local maximum.

Second, the paper explores the algorithmic consequences of this structural property. It shows that both the standard EM algorithm (Theorem 2) and a first-order variant equivalent to gradient ascent (Theorem 3), when initialized randomly by drawing centers from the true data distribution, will converge to a bad critical point with probability at least 1 - e^(-cM). The proof of this high-probability failure relies on a novel recursive construction of the GMM, which makes the set of "good" initializations exponentially small. Finally, by leveraging recent results on gradient methods (Lee et al., 2016), the paper shows that the first-order EM algorithm will almost surely not converge to a strict saddle point (Theorem 4). This final result is crucial, as it implies that the observed algorithmic failure is due to the presence of bad local maxima, not the proliferation of saddle points, which contrasts with arguments made for other high-dimensional non-convex problems.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs, which appear to be sound. The constructions used to establish the existence of bad local maxima and the high probability of algorithmic failure are both novel and insightful. This is a complete and self-contained piece of theoretical work that resolves a significant open question. The authors are careful to delineate the idealized setting (population likelihood, known identity covariance) and discuss the implications clearly.

A minor point for improvement would be a brief discussion on the extension to the finite-sample case. While the authors state this can be done via standard empirical process arguments, adding a sentence about the required sample complexity (e.g., polynomial in d and M, logarithmic in 1/epsilon) would further strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the problem, placing it in the context of both classical GMM literature (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007) and modern non-convex optimization theory (e.g., Loh & Wainwright, 2013). The main results are stated clearly in Section 3, accompanied by valuable intuition behind the proofs before the full technical details are presented in the appendix. The separation of structural results from algorithmic consequences makes the paper's narrative easy to follow.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. It provides the first formal refutation of the Srebro (2007) conjecture, a well-known open problem in the learning theory community. The techniques developed, particularly the geometric construction for Theorem 1 and the recursive GMM structure for Theorems 2 and 3, are novel contributions. Furthermore, the connection drawn to the recent literature on the geometry of non-convex optimization, specifically by showing that bad local maxima rather than saddle points are the primary obstacle here, is both timely and original. This work clearly distinguishes itself from prior analyses of EM, which typically focused on local convergence properties (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) or required strong initialization.

**Significance:** The results are of major significance to the machine learning community.
1.  **Fundamental Theory:** The paper establishes a fundamental, and perhaps surprising, property of the likelihood function for one of the most common unsupervised learning models. It shows that even in highly idealized and favorable settings, the GMM likelihood landscape is treacherous.
2.  **Algorithmic Implications:** The work provides strong theoretical justification for the folk wisdom that initialization is critical for EM. It formally shows that the simple and widely used approach of random restarts is computationally inefficient for GMMs, requiring an exponential number of trials in the worst case. This underscores the necessity of more sophisticated initialization procedures, such as those based on the method of moments (Hsu & Kakade, 2013) or spectral methods.
3.  **Broader Impact on Non-Convex Optimization:** The paper makes an important contribution to the ongoing discussion about the nature of non-convex optimization landscapes. By demonstrating that bad local maxima are the primary issue for GMMs, it provides a compelling counterpoint to the narrative that saddle points are the only real barrier in many high-dimensional problems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:**
    *   Resolves a significant, long-standing open problem in learning theory.
    *   Provides fundamental new insights into the optimization landscape of GMMs.
    *   The algorithmic results have direct and important implications for practitioners, highlighting the computational intractability of random initialization and motivating the need for careful initialization strategies.
    *   The paper is technically sound, highly original, and exceptionally well-written.
    *   Contributes an important case study to the broader field of non-convex optimization.

*   **Con:**
    *   The analysis is confined to the population setting. While this is standard for this line of theoretical inquiry, a brief discussion of finite-sample implications would be beneficial.
    *   The constructions used to prove the results are specific. It remains an open question how "common" these pathological landscapes are among all possible GMMs, though the existence proof is a critical first step.

In summary, this is an outstanding theoretical paper that makes significant and lasting contributions to our understanding of Gaussian mixture models and non-convex optimization. It is well-executed, clearly presented, and addresses a problem of high interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
