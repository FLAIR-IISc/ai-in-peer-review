This paper addresses a fundamental question regarding the landscape of the population likelihood function for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs): are there bad local maxima? The authors demonstrate, counter to a conjecture by Srebro (2007), that such bad local maxima *do* exist, even for well-separated mixtures. Furthermore, they show that the EM algorithm, initialized randomly, is likely to converge to these suboptimal points. This is a significant result, as it explains why careful initialization is often necessary in practice and casts doubt on the inherent robustness of EM.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon a rich body of literature concerning GMM estimation, identifiability, and the theoretical properties of EM. It correctly positions the problem within the broader context of non-convex optimization, referencing related work on high-dimensional regression and saddle points. The related work section is comprehensive, covering foundational papers (Teicher, Dempster et al.) to more recent advances (Balakrishnan et al., Ho & Nguyen). The paper clearly articulates the open problem and the limitations of existing approaches.

The primary strength of the paper is the rigorous theoretical results. The construction of a 3-component GMM with a demonstrably bad local maximum is clever and well-justified. The proof that EM converges to these bad points with high probability is also a substantial contribution, providing a concrete explanation for the observed sensitivity of EM to initialization. The result regarding convergence to saddle points (or rather, *not* convergence to strict saddle points) is insightful, clarifying that bad local maxima are the primary culprit for EM's shortcomings, rather than the complexity of the likelihood surface beyond simple local minima. The use of techniques from dynamical systems theory to prove Theorem 4 is also noteworthy. 

However, the paper is not without weaknesses. While the mathematical arguments are sound, the presentation can be dense and challenging to follow, even for experts in the field. The notation is sometimes cumbersome and could benefit from simplification. While the motivation is strong, it would be beneficial to have some numerical experiments (even simple ones) demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima and the performance of EM with random initialization on the constructed example. Showing a visualization of the likelihood surface for the 3-component case would dramatically improve intuition. 

Furthermore, the focus on equally weighted, isotropic Gaussians is a simplifying assumption. While this allows for a clean theoretical treatment, it’s important to acknowledge the limitations of this setting. Real-world data often involves unequal weights and non-spherical covariances. It is unclear how the results extend to these more general cases. The discussion could benefit from some speculation on this point. The claim that the success probability is exponentially small in M feels rather strong; quantifying the dependence of the constant *c* would be helpful.

Finally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of potential remedies. While the authors emphasize the need for careful initialization, they don’t offer concrete guidance on how to achieve it. A brief exploration of alternative initialization strategies (e.g., k-means++, spectral methods) and their theoretical properties would strengthen the paper’s impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significant Theoretical Result:** The existence of bad local maxima in the GMM likelihood is a fundamental finding with implications for the understanding and application of EM.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The proofs are mathematically sound and provide strong support for the claims.
*   **Clear Connection to Practice:** The results explain a common observation in practice – the sensitivity of EM to initialization.
*   **Addresses an Open Problem:** The paper resolves a longstanding open question posed by Srebro (2007).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Dense Presentation:** The mathematical arguments are challenging to follow and could benefit from greater clarity and simplification.
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of numerical experiments limits the intuitive appeal of the results.
*   **Simplified Setting:** The focus on equally weighted, isotropic Gaussians restricts the generality of the findings.



**Overall assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMM estimation. The results are novel, rigorously proven, and address an important open problem.  I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the clarity and empirical validation concerns in a revised version. A more accessible presentation and the inclusion of numerical experiments would greatly enhance the paper's impact and broaden its appeal.
