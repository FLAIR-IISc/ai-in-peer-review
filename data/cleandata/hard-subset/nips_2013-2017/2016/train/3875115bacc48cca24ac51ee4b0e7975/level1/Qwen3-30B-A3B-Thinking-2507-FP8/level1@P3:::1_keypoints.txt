- Resolves Srebro (2007) conjecture by proving arbitrarily bad local maxima exist in the population likelihood for GMMs with M ≥ 3.  
- Constructs elegant geometric example for M=3 (two centers collapse, one stays), and a novel recursive construction for the general result.  
- Shows random initialization of EM (and first-order EM) converges to these bad local maxima with probability ≥ 1 − e^(−Ω(M)).  
- Proves first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, distinguishing failure due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Practical implication: random EM can fail catastrophically even for well-separated GMMs, justifying advanced initialization methods.  
- Technical sophistication and originality: novel construction and proof techniques, significant theoretical advance resolving a long-standing open question.  
- Quality and clarity: rigorous proofs, clear writing, good geometric intuition for M=3, and reproducible appendix.  
- Weakness: does not settle the M = 2 case and would benefit from discussion on why M=2 might differ.  
- Weakness: results are for the population likelihood; translation to finite-sample behavior is not fully elaborated.  
- Weakness: lacks explicit analysis of how the exponential failure probability scales with sample size for practical settings.  
- Recommendation: strong accept — high significance and immediate relevance to theory and practice, despite noted limitations.
