

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper resolves a long-standing open question posed by Srebro [2007] regarding the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors demonstrate that for M ≥ 3 components, even in the favorable setting of equally-weighted, well-separated, spherical Gaussians with unit variance, the population log-likelihood function can contain local maxima whose likelihood values are arbitrarily worse than global maxima. This directly contradicts Srebro's conjecture that all local maxima would be global maxima in the population setting. The paper further shows that the EM algorithm (and a first-order variant) with random initialization converges to bad critical points with probability at least 1 - e^{-Ω(M)}, and that first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely.

The paper makes two significant contributions: first, the structural result demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima, and second, the algorithmic result showing the high probability of convergence to suboptimal solutions with random initialization. The construction for M=3 is particularly elegant, with the authors providing a clear geometric intuition: when the true centers are arranged such that two are close together and far from the third, a local maximum occurs when the algorithm "collapses" two centers onto the pair and places the third at the location of the single true center.

Strengths:
- The paper provides a rigorous resolution to an important open problem in GMM theory that has been discussed in the community since Srebro's 2007 paper.
- The technical proofs are sound, with careful construction of examples and detailed analysis of the likelihood landscape.
- The authors clearly distinguish between population and finite-sample settings, and appropriately relate their results to previous work on identifiability (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009), EM convergence (Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and method-of-moments approaches (Moitra and Valiant, 2010; Hsu and Kakade, 2013).
- The algorithmic results have significant practical implications, showing that random initialization for EM is likely to fail with exponentially high probability as M increases, which explains why practitioners often need many restarts.
- The paper carefully addresses the distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points, using recent results on gradient methods to show that the failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

Weaknesses:
- The results are limited to M ≥ 3 components, leaving open the question of whether the same phenomenon occurs for M=2, which is the most commonly used setting in practice. The authors acknowledge this as an interesting open question.
- The paper focuses on the population setting (infinite samples), though the authors note that their results extend to large finite samples via standard empirical process arguments.
- The paper lacks experimental validation to complement the theoretical analysis, though this is common for theoretical papers in this vein.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper resolves a fundamental open question in GMM theory with important theoretical and practical implications.
- The results provide strong theoretical justification for why careful initialization is necessary in practice, even for well-separated mixtures.
- The proofs are rigorous and well-constructed, with clear explanations of the constructions.
- The paper makes a clear distinction from previous work, particularly by focusing on the population likelihood function rather than finite-sample behavior.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The M=2 case remains unaddressed, which is the most common practical scenario.
- The paper's focus on population likelihood might be seen as less directly applicable to finite-sample settings, though the authors provide appropriate caveats.

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes. The results have both theoretical importance and practical relevance for practitioners using EM for GMM estimation. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong case for its claims. The resolution of Srebro's conjecture is particularly noteworthy, as it corrects a widely held intuition about GMMs. The algorithmic results about the failure of random initialization with high probability are especially valuable for practitioners who often rely on this approach.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), addresses an important open question (Originality), and provides results with significant theoretical and practical implications (Significance). The authors have made a clear contribution to the field that will inform both future theoretical work and practical applications of GMMs. This paper should be accepted to NIPS 2016.
