## Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper investigates the landscape of the population likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components. The core contribution is a proof that, contrary to a conjecture by Srebro [2007], bad local maxima *do* exist even for well-separated, spherical Gaussians. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that the EM algorithm (and a first-order variant) will converge to these suboptimal critical points with high probability when initialized randomly. The paper builds on a long line of work exploring the difficulties of GMM estimation, including identifiability studies (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009) and convergence rate analysis (Chen, 1995; Ho & Nguyen, 2015). It also connects to the broader theoretical analysis of EM (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and recent interest in the role of saddle points in non-convex optimization (Pascanu et al., 2014).

**Strengths:** The paper addresses a fundamental theoretical question regarding the optimization landscape of GMMs, which has significant implications for practical algorithm design. The mathematical results are strong and provide a clear resolution to Srebro’s conjecture. The connection between the existence of bad local maxima and the performance of the EM algorithm is convincingly established through a careful probabilistic analysis. The authors are also careful to distinguish their findings from issues related to saddle points, providing a nuanced understanding of the algorithmic challenges. The writing is generally clear and the motivation for the work is well-established.

**Weaknesses:** While mathematically rigorous, the paper leans heavily on theoretical results and lacks concrete empirical validation. It would be valuable to see simulations illustrating the identified phenomena, even in simplified settings. The discussion of random initialization could be more detailed regarding practical implementations and potential mitigation strategies. While the authors acknowledge the issue of infinite likelihood solutions (existing when both location and scale are estimated), they fix the scale parameters. Discussing the interplay between scale parameter estimation and the observed local maxima could strengthen the work. Finally, the paper could benefit from a slightly more extensive discussion of related work, specifically recent advances in initialization techniques for non-convex optimization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
* Resolves a long-standing open question in the field.
* Provides strong theoretical justification for the challenges in GMM estimation.
* Highlights the importance of initialization strategies for EM and related algorithms.
* Demonstrates a clear connection between theoretical properties and algorithmic performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
* Lack of empirical validation.
* Limited discussion of practical implications and potential remedies.
* Could benefit from a more comprehensive literature review.



**Overall:** Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of GMM estimation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above.
