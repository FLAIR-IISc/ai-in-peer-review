This paper tackles a fundamental question about the EM algorithm for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs): are there bad local optima in the population likelihood function? The authors rigorously demonstrate that, contrary to a conjecture by Srebro (2007), such bad local optima *do* exist even for well-separated Gaussian components. Furthermore, they show that with random initialization, the EM (and first-order EM) algorithm converges to these suboptimal points with high probability. Finally, they establish that the failure is likely due to local maxima, not saddle points.

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The problem of efficiently and reliably estimating GMMs is central to many areas of statistics and machine learning. The existence of bad local optima directly impacts the practical applicability of the EM algorithm, the most common method for estimation. The authors clearly situate their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers on identifiability, convergence rates, and theoretical analysis of EM. They meticulously explain the related work and highlight the open questions their research addresses.

The main results (Theorems 1, 2, 3, and 4) are strong and technically impressive. Theorem 1 provides a concrete counterexample to Srebro’s conjecture, which is a significant contribution. Theorems 2 and 3 establish a pessimistic yet realistic assessment of the EM algorithm’s behavior with random initialization – a high probability of converging to a suboptimal solution. Theorem 4 is particularly interesting as it rules out saddle points as the primary cause of the observed failure. The proofs, while lengthy (as expected for this type of result), appear sound and build logically. The connection to recent results on gradient methods is insightful.

The clarity of the paper is very high. The writing is precise and well-organized. The authors carefully define all relevant terms and provide clear intuition behind the technical results. The use of mathematical notation is consistent and easy to follow. The paper is well-structured, with a logical flow from introduction to conclusion. The inclusion of a detailed background section and careful definitions are particularly helpful.  The authors do a good job explaining the implications of their results and suggesting directions for future research.

The originality of this work is high. The demonstration of bad local optima in the population likelihood function is a novel and significant result. The analysis of the EM algorithm's convergence behavior with random initialization, and the differentiation between local maxima and saddle points, are also original contributions. While the paper builds on prior work (Srebro, Balakrishnan et al.), it significantly advances the state of the art in understanding the theoretical limitations of EM. The construction of the specific GMM instance exhibiting bad local optima is also a clever and original approach.

The significance of the results is substantial. The findings have important implications for the practical use of the EM algorithm and motivate the development of more robust initialization strategies. The paper also contributes to the broader understanding of non-convex optimization problems and the challenges of escaping local optima. The results are likely to influence future research in this area.  The work addresses a long-standing open question and provides a fundamental insight into the behavior of a widely used algorithm. The conclusions regarding the necessity of careful initialization are particularly relevant in practical applications.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong theoretical results:** The paper provides rigorous mathematical proofs for significant claims about the population likelihood function and the behavior of the EM algorithm.
*   **High originality:** The results are novel and address a long-standing open question.
*   **Clear and well-written:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and easy to follow.
*   **Significant impact:** The findings have important implications for the practical use of the EM algorithm and the broader field of optimization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited scope of practical remedies:** While the paper identifies a problem, it doesn't offer immediate practical solutions beyond suggesting better initialization strategies. However, identifying the problem is a crucial first step towards finding solutions, and is a significant contribution in itself.
*   **Technical complexity:** The proofs are mathematically involved, potentially making the paper inaccessible to a wider audience. However, this is unavoidable given the nature of the problem and the rigor required.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a very strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of Gaussian Mixture Models and the EM algorithm. The results are technically sound, clearly presented, and highly relevant to the field. Despite the technical complexity, the clarity of the writing makes the paper accessible to researchers with a background in statistical learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
