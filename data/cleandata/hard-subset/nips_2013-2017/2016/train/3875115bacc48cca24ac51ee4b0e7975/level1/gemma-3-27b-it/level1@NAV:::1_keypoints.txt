- Main contribution: Proves bad local maxima exist for GMMs with M ≥ 3, even for well-separated spherical Gaussians, contradicting Srebro (2007).  
- Algorithmic consequence: Shows EM and a first-order variant can converge to these suboptimal critical points with high probability under random initialization.  
- Context: Builds on prior identifiability and convergence-rate work and connects to theoretical analyses of EM and saddle-point literature.  
- Strength: Addresses a fundamental theoretical question with strong, rigorous mathematical results resolving a long-standing conjecture.  
- Strength: Convincingly links the existence of bad local maxima to practical algorithmic behavior of EM via probabilistic analysis.  
- Strength: Carefully distinguishes issues from saddle points and provides clear motivation and generally clear writing.  
- Weakness: Paper is heavy on theory and lacks concrete empirical simulations to illustrate the phenomena.  
- Weakness: Discussion of random initialization is limited; practical implementation details and mitigation strategies are underdeveloped.  
- Weakness: Authors fix scale parameters; interplay between scale estimation and local maxima is not explored and could strengthen the work.  
- Weakness: Literature review could be expanded, especially regarding recent initialization techniques for non-convex optimization.  
- Argument for acceptance: Resolves an open question, offers strong theoretical justification for GMM estimation difficulties, and highlights importance of initialization.  
- Argument against acceptance: Missing empirical validation, limited practical implications and remedies, and incomplete literature coverage.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address empirical validation, initialization discussion, and related-work coverage.
