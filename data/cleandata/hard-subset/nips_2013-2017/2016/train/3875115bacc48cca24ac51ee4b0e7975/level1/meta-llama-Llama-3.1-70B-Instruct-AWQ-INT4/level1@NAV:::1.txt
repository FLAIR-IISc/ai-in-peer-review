This paper, "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences," presents a significant contribution to the field of machine learning and statistics. The authors investigate the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components and provide two fundamental results. 

Firstly, they show that the population likelihood function has bad local maxima even in the special case of equally-weighted mixtures of well-separated and spherical Gaussians. This result resolves an open question of Srebro [2007] and highlights the necessity of careful initialization when using the EM algorithm in practice.

Secondly, the authors demonstrate that the EM algorithm (or a first-order variant of it) with random initialization will converge to bad critical points with probability at least 1− e−Ω(M). This result indicates that repeated random initialization followed by local search (via either EM or its first-order variant) can fail to produce useful estimates under reasonable constraints on computational complexity.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their results. The introduction provides a thorough background on GMMs, the EM algorithm, and the motivation for the research. The main results are presented in a logical and easy-to-follow manner, and the authors provide a detailed proof of their theorems.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a negative answer to the open question of Srebro [2007]. The authors' construction of a GMM with bad local maxima is innovative and provides a significant contribution to the field. Additionally, the paper's results on the convergence of the EM algorithm and its variants are important and have implications for the practice of using these algorithms in machine learning.

The paper's weaknesses are minor. One potential criticism is that the authors' results are limited to the population setting, and it is unclear how their results generalize to finite samples. However, the authors do provide some discussion on how their results can be extended to finite samples using empirical process arguments.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel construction of a GMM with bad local maxima and provides new results on the convergence of the EM algorithm and its variants. The paper's results are not a straightforward extension of previous work, and the authors provide a significant contribution to the field.

The significance of the paper's results is high. The authors' work has implications for the practice of using EM algorithms in machine learning and highlights the importance of careful initialization. The paper's results also provide a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs and the EM algorithm.
