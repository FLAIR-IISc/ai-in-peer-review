- Main result: The population log-likelihood for GMMs with M ≥ 3 has bad local maxima even for equally-weighted, well-separated, spherical Gaussians; these can be arbitrarily worse than any global optimum, resolving Srebro (2007).  
- Main result: EM (and a first-order variant) with random initialization converges to bad critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{−Ω(M)}; first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, so failures stem from bad local maxima.  
- Strength: Clear, concise introduction explaining GMM estimation and non-convexity challenges.  
- Strength: Thorough literature review covering identifiability, MLE convergence rates, and EM algorithmic guarantees.  
- Strength: Detailed, well-organized proofs including a geometric construction for M = 3.  
- Weakness: Restrictive assumptions — equally weighted, spherical components with unit variance may be unrealistic.  
- Weakness: No empirical results provided to support theoretical claims.  
- Weakness: No discussion of implications for other optimization methods (e.g., gradient descent, quasi-Newton).  
- Originality: New proof of existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood, answering an open question.  
- Originality: New high-probability negative result on random-initialization EM convergence to bad critical points.  
- Significance: Advances understanding of the GMM likelihood landscape and non-convex optimization behavior.  
- Significance: Highlights importance of careful initialization and has implications for designing optimization algorithms for GMMs.  
- Quality: Well-written, accessible, and logically organized presentation.  
- Clarity: Explanations and proofs are clear and easy to follow.  
- Argument for acceptance: Novel theoretical insights with important implications for algorithm design and initialization strategies.  
- Argument for rejection: Limited practical realism, absence of experiments, and lack of broader algorithmic discussion.
