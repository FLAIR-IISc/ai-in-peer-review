This paper addresses fundamental questions about the optimization landscape of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The authors make two primary claims. First, they show that the population log-likelihood function for GMMs with M ≥ 3 components possesses bad local maxima, even in idealized settings (equally-weighted, spherical, well-separated Gaussians). The quality of these maxima can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, thereby negatively resolving a well-known conjecture by Srebro (2007). Second, they demonstrate a critical algorithmic consequence: the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and its first-order variants, when randomly initialized, will converge to these suboptimal critical points with a probability that is exponentially high in the number of components, M.

The paper’s strengths are numerous and significant.

**Pros for acceptance:**
*   **Significance:** The resolution of a decade-old open question is a major theoretical contribution. The results provide a definitive characterization of the GMM likelihood surface, explaining the long-observed practical need for careful initialization or multiple restarts for the EM algorithm.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with clear and rigorous theoretical arguments. The constructions used to prove the existence of bad local maxima and to analyze the failure probability of EM are insightful and elegant. The analysis correctly identifies that failure is due to local maxima rather than saddle points, connecting the work to the broader literature on non-convex optimization.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While prior work has studied the local convergence of EM, this paper is the first to prove the existence of arbitrarily bad local optima in the population setting and to quantify the high probability of convergence to them under a standard initialization scheme.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides clear motivation, situates the work within the relevant literature, and offers valuable geometric intuition for its main results before diving into the formal proofs.

The paper has very few weaknesses.

**Cons for acceptance:**
*   The extension of these population-level results to the finite-sample regime is mentioned only briefly in the conclusion. While the claim that this follows from standard empirical process arguments is plausible, a slightly more detailed comment or a specific reference could strengthen this connection for the reader.

Overall, this is an outstanding paper that makes a foundational contribution to our understanding of a cornerstone model in machine learning. It is technically deep, clearly communicated, and will be of high interest to both theoreticians and practitioners. I strongly recommend acceptance.
