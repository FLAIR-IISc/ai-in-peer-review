**Summary of the Paper**

The paper "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences" resolves an open problem of Srebro (2007) by demonstrating the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima for the population log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models, even in the idealized situation where each component is uniformly weighted, spherical with unit variance, and well-separated. The authors also provide evidence that random initialization schemes for the population EM algorithm are likely to fail with high probability.

**Claims**

The main claims of the paper are:

1. The population log-likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models with M ≥ 3 components has bad local maxima, even in the special case of equally-weighted mixtures of well-separated and spherical Gaussians.
2. The EM algorithm, as well as a variant thereof known as the first-order EM algorithm, with random initialization, converges to a bad critical point with an exponentially high probability.
3. The first-order EM algorithm with a suitable stepsize does not converge to a strict saddle point with probability one.

**Support for Claims**

The authors provide theoretical results and proofs to support their claims. They construct a Gaussian mixture distribution with a recursive structure, which allows them to demonstrate the existence of bad local maxima and the failure of the EM algorithm to converge to a global maximum. They also provide a detailed analysis of the first-order EM algorithm and show that it does not converge to strict saddle points.

**Usefulness**

The results of the paper have important implications for the use of EM algorithms in practice. They suggest that careful initialization is required for local search methods, even in large-sample settings, and even for extremely well-behaved mixture models. The authors also highlight the importance of understanding the robustness of algorithms to the assumption that the data exactly follows the generative model.

**Field Knowledge**

The paper demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature in the field, including the work of Srebro (2007), Balakrishnan et al. (2015), and others. The authors also demonstrate a deep understanding of the theoretical foundations of Gaussian mixture models and EM algorithms.

**Novelty**

The paper presents a significant improvement over existing approaches by providing a negative answer to the open question of Srebro (2007) and by demonstrating the failure of the EM algorithm to converge to a global maximum. The authors also introduce new techniques to reason about the structure of the population log-likelihood, which will aid in developing a better understanding of the behavior of algorithms for non-convex optimization.

**Completeness**

The paper provides sufficient details for reproducibility, including a detailed description of the Gaussian mixture model, the EM algorithm, and the first-order EM algorithm. The authors also provide a clear explanation of their theoretical results and proofs.

**Limitations**

The paper acknowledges that the results are limited to the idealized situation where each component is uniformly weighted, spherical with unit variance, and well-separated. The authors also note that the necessity of at least three mixture components in their constructions is still an open question.

**Constructive Criticism**

One potential criticism of the paper is that the authors do not provide a clear solution to the problem of finding a good initialization scheme for the EM algorithm. However, the authors do suggest that pilot estimators utilizing the method of moments may be a promising approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance**

* The paper presents a significant contribution to the field by resolving an open problem of Srebro (2007).
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the EM algorithm and its variants, which will aid in understanding the behavior of these algorithms in practice.
* The paper demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature and the theoretical foundations of Gaussian mixture models and EM algorithms.

**Arguments against Acceptance**

* The paper is limited to the idealized situation where each component is uniformly weighted, spherical with unit variance, and well-separated.
* The authors do not provide a clear solution to the problem of finding a good initialization scheme for the EM algorithm.

Overall, I believe that the paper makes a significant contribution to the field and provides a thorough analysis of the EM algorithm and its variants. While there are some limitations to the paper, I believe that it is a valuable addition to the literature and should be accepted for publication.
