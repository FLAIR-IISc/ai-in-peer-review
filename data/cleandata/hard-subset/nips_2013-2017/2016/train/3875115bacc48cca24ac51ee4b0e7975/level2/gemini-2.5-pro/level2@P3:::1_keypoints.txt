- Refutes Srebro (2007): for any M ≥ 3, there exist well-separated, simple GMMs whose population log-likelihood has arbitrarily bad local maxima.  
- Provides geometric intuition and constructions showing why bad local optima exist.  
- Shows EM (and a first-order variant) initialized by standard random sampling converges to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1−e−Ω(M), implying exponential random restarts are needed.  
- Proves first-order EM (gradient ascent) converges to strict saddle points with probability zero, indicating failures stem from bad local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Analysis is at the population (infinite-sample) level, appropriate for studying fundamental likelihood landscape properties.  
- Technical contributions are rigorous, proofs appear sound, and the work is complete.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, with precise statements and strong intuition for proofs.  
- Original: resolves a decade-old open question with a simple, insightful counterexample.  
- Novel algorithmic result: demonstrates exponential failure rate of random initialization for EM via a clever recursive construction.  
- Connects optimization failure specifically to bad local maxima, providing a problem-specific counterpoint to saddle-focused narratives.  
- Significant for theorists: fundamentally characterizes GMM likelihood landscapes as more complex than conjectured.  
- Significant for practitioners: formally justifies careful EM initialization and motivates moment- or spectral-based methods.
