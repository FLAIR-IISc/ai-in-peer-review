## Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper investigates the challenging problem of local optima in the likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The authors address a long-standing conjecture by Srebro [2007] and demonstrate the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima even in well-separated, equally-weighted mixtures of Gaussians. Furthermore, they show that the EM algorithm, with random initialization, converges to these bad critical points with high probability, and importantly, that this failure isn’t due to saddle points.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon a significant body of work on GMM estimation and the difficulties associated with non-convex optimization. The authors clearly articulate the problem and its relevance to both theoretical understanding and practical algorithm design. The core contribution – proving the existence of bad local maxima – is a significant step forward and resolves an open question. The subsequent analysis of the EM algorithm’s behavior, demonstrating its tendency to get trapped in these local optima, is equally compelling. The theoretical results are supported by intuitive explanations and a clear connection to the practical implications for initialization strategies.

A strength of the paper is its rigorous mathematical treatment. The proofs appear sound and the authors carefully define their terms and assumptions. The discussion of related work is thorough, citing key papers in the field. The acknowledgement of limitations, particularly regarding the step size constraint for the first-order EM algorithm, is also appreciated.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of their findings. While they suggest careful initialization is necessary, exploring specific initialization techniques that might mitigate the issue would strengthen the work. Additionally, the focus is heavily on the theoretical aspects; a small-scale empirical validation demonstrating the observed behavior would add further weight to the claims. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Resolves an open problem in GMM theory.
*   Provides strong theoretical justification for the difficulties in EM optimization.
*   Rigorous mathematical analysis and clear presentation.
*   Addresses a practically relevant issue in statistical modeling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical validation.
*   Could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of practical initialization strategies.

**Overall:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of GMM estimation. While some practical considerations could be further explored, the core results are significant and well-supported. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above.
