This paper investigates the theoretical properties of the population likelihood function for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components. The core contribution is demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima, resolving a conjecture by Srebro [2007], and showing that the EM algorithm, with random initialization, converges to these suboptimal points with high probability. The paper also establishes that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points, attributing the algorithm’s poor performance to bad local maxima.

The work is well-motivated, building upon a long line of research concerning the estimation of GMMs, starting with identifiability studies [Teicher, 1963, Allman et al., 2009] and progressing through convergence rate analysis [Chen, 1995, Genovese & Wasserman, 2000, Ghosal & Van Der Vaart, 2001, Ho & Nguyen, 2015] and algorithmic analysis [Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007, Balakrishnan et al., 2015].  The problem is clearly framed, and the connection to the practical challenges of EM algorithm convergence is explicitly stated. The introduction provides a thorough background on the relevant literature.

A significant strength is the rigorous mathematical treatment. The proofs appear sound and well-structured (though detailed inspection is limited given the review constraints).  The results are substantial – not merely pointing out the *possibility* of bad local optima, but demonstrating their existence and quantifying the probability of converging to them. The analysis of the first-order EM algorithm distinguishing between saddle points and bad local maxima is also a noteworthy contribution. The paper also appropriately acknowledges limitations, such as avoiding infinite likelihood solutions by fixing scale parameters. 

The clarity of the presentation is generally good, though the density of mathematical notation in Section 2 could be challenging for some readers.  While the authors provide extensive background and motivation, the core mathematical arguments leading to Theorem 1 could benefit from more intuitive explanation alongside the formal proof. The notation is consistent throughout.

The originality of the work is high. Addressing the open question from Srebro [2007] directly and providing a strong negative answer is a significant contribution. The analysis of random initialization and the probability of converging to suboptimal points are also novel and important. The distinction between saddle points and local maxima in the context of the first-order EM algorithm is a nuanced and insightful observation.

The significance of the results is considerable. The paper’s findings have clear implications for the practical use of the EM algorithm and related optimization methods for GMM estimation.  The need for careful initialization, even in seemingly favorable settings, is strongly highlighted. This is particularly relevant given the widespread use of GMMs in various applications. The results also point towards the importance of developing more robust initialization schemes or alternative optimization algorithms. The insights into the structure of the likelihood surface could be valuable for understanding similar phenomena in other non-convex optimization problems. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Resolves an important open problem in GMM theory.
*   Provides strong theoretical justification for the challenges in EM algorithm optimization.
*   Demonstrates novel and insightful results regarding the convergence properties of EM and first-order EM.
*   Well-written and rigorously proven.
*   Significant implications for practical applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The mathematical density in certain sections might hinder accessibility for some readers.
*   The paper focuses on a specific setting (uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussians). While the authors acknowledge this, the extent to which the results generalize to more complex GMMs is unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a substantial contribution to the understanding of Gaussian mixture models and the EM algorithm. The results are significant, the analysis is rigorous, and the writing is generally clear.  The limitations are appropriately acknowledged. This reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.

**Minor suggestions for improvement:**

*   Consider adding more intuitive explanations to accompany the formal proofs, particularly for Theorem 1.
*   Discuss potential avenues for generalizing the results to more complex GMM settings in the conclusion.
*   Adding a visual representation of the likelihood surface demonstrating a bad local maxima would greatly improve the paper.
