- Main claim: Proves existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population log-likelihood of GMMs for M ≥ 3, refuting Srebro’s 2007 conjecture.  
- Secondary claim: Shows EM and its first-order variant, with random initialization, converge to suboptimal critical points with high probability, implying careful initialization is necessary.

Strengths
- Novelty/significance: Resolves a long-standing open question and challenges assumptions about the GMM likelihood surface.  
- Theoretical rigor: Introduces new analysis techniques and a recursive construction of GMMs with bad local maxima.  
- Practical relevance: Highlights limitations of EM/gradient methods and the importance of initialization in practice.  
- Clarity of results: Clearly distinguishes bad local maxima from strict saddles and provides strong theoretical foundation.

Weaknesses
- Limited experiments: Lacks empirical validation comparing EM with random vs. alternative initializations.  
- Idealized setting: Analysis restricted to equally weighted, spherical, well-separated Gaussians, limiting generalizability.  
- Open questions: Leaves unresolved whether bad local maxima exist for <3 components and why EM often works well in practice.

Evaluation
- Quality: Technically sound with complete, rigorous proofs.  
- Clarity: Well-written and organized, though technical sections may challenge non-experts.  
- Originality: Highly original, resolving an open problem.  
- Significance: Important for theory and practice in non-convex optimization and mixture models.

Recommendation
- Accept, with encouragement to include empirical evaluations and address remaining open questions in future work.
