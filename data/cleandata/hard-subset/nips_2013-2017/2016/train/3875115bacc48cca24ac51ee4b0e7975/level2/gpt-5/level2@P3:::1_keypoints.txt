- Proves population log-likelihood for GMMs with M ≥ 3 can have arbitrarily bad local maxima, even for well-separated mixtures (construction appears already for d=1, M=3).  
- Shows EM and a first-order EM (gradient-ascent equivalent) initialized by sampling centers from the mixture converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e−Ω(M).  
- Establishes first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, attributing failures to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.  
- Resolves Srebro (2007)’s conjecture by demonstrating existence of bad local maxima at the population level.  
- Connects to classical EM analyses and alternative consistent methods (clustering/moments), complementing recent work on saddles in nonconvex optimization.  
- Theoretical claims are rigorous with carefully designed constructions and a boundary argument certifying large likelihood gaps.  
- Algorithmic result uses a hierarchical cluster-within-cluster construction to show random initialization concentrates on bad basins with high probability.  
- Smoothness and stable-manifold arguments are adapted carefully to justify saddle avoidance for first-order EM.  
- Paper is well written and organized; model, algorithms, and initialization are precisely defined and M=3, d=1 intuition is helpful.  
- Needs clearer presentation of explicit separation scales, constants, and precise stepsize/condition ranges for the first-order EM analysis.  
- Original resolution of a longstanding open problem and novel hierarchical/stable-manifold proof techniques likely of broader interest.  
- Results are significant for practice: random restarts of EM may require exponentially many restarts in M, highlighting importance of informed initialization.  
- Pros: settles open question on bad local maxima; shows EM’s exponential failure probability with random initialization; clarifies saddles vs. local maxima; introduces potentially general techniques; clear exposition.  
- Cons: population-level focus limits finite-sample implications; constructions are one-dimensional and adversarial, unclear typicality; assumes equal weights and spherical covariances; lacks empirical demonstrations or practical guidance; M=2 case remains open.  
- Essential suggestion: state explicit parameter regimes (separation scales, cluster sizes) and summarize constants driving the likelihood gap bound in the main text.  
- Essential suggestion: provide a short finite-sample corollary with concrete sample-size requirements via standard concentration arguments.  
- Optional suggestion: discuss robustness to unequal weights and mild covariance anisotropy and identify fragile proof elements.  
- Optional suggestion: add a brief synthetic experiment showing convergence to bad local maxima under random initialization and improvement with informed seeding.  
- Optional suggestion: expand discussion or partial results for the M=2 case to guide future work.  
- Optional suggestion: comment on high-dimensional scaling (e.g., separation Ω(√d)) and whether dimension qualitatively changes basin structure.  
- Overall assessment: strong, rigorous theory paper that settles an important question and provides clear algorithmic insights; recommended for acceptance.
