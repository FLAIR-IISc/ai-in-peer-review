

This paper addresses an important open question in the theoretical analysis of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically resolving Srebro's conjecture (2007) regarding the absence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function. The authors demonstrate that for M ≥ 3 components, even in the idealized setting of equally-weighted, well-separated, spherical Gaussians, the population log-likelihood function can contain local maxima whose log-likelihood values are arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. This is a significant theoretical contribution that challenges a widely held intuition in the field.

The paper's main strengths lie in its clear theoretical framework, rigorous proofs of the structural results, and well-motivated algorithmic consequences. The construction for M=3 is particularly elegant, showing how a configuration where two centers are clustered at the midpoint of two closely spaced true centers while the third is at the third true center can form a local maximum. The authors further demonstrate that the EM algorithm (and a first-order variant) with random initialization converges to these bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)), which has important practical implications for the use of EM in practice. The distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points is particularly well-handled, with Theorem 4 showing that first-order EM avoids strict saddle points almost surely, suggesting the failure is due to local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough introduction that properly situates the work within the existing literature. The authors carefully distinguish between the population and sample settings, and the theoretical results are presented with appropriate mathematical precision. The connection between the theoretical results and practical algorithmic behavior is well-motivated and compelling.

However, the paper would benefit from addressing a few limitations. First, while the authors mention that their results carry over to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments, this connection is not explicitly developed. Given that the practical relevance of the population results depends on this connection, a brief discussion of how the finite-sample behavior relates to the population results would strengthen the paper.

Second, the paper does not provide empirical validation of its theoretical claims about the EM algorithm's behavior. While the theoretical arguments are strong, some experimental results demonstrating the phenomenon in practice would provide additional support for the claims and help contextualize the severity of the issue for practitioners.

Third, the paper does not sufficiently address whether the constructed bad local maxima are "typical" or only exist in highly pathological cases. The authors note that the construction requires M ≥ 3, but do not provide a clear explanation for why M=2 might be fundamentally different (beyond mentioning it's an open problem). A more detailed discussion of the geometric conditions under which bad local maxima arise would help readers assess the practical significance of the results.

The paper's significance is substantial, as it provides a fundamental theoretical limitation for the widely used EM algorithm. The results suggest that careful initialization (beyond simple random initialization) is essential for GMM estimation, even in favorable settings. This has clear implications for practitioners and provides a theoretical foundation for the development of better initialization schemes, such as those based on the method of moments.

The paper's originality is strong, as it directly addresses and resolves an open question from NIPS 2007 (Srebro). The results are novel and not covered in previous NIPS proceedings. The authors properly cite related work, including the relevant Srebro paper and recent theoretical work on EM convergence (Balakrishnan et al., 2015).

In summary, this is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes. The main weakness is the lack of empirical validation for the algorithmic claims, which would strengthen the practical relevance of the results. The paper would also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the typicality of the constructed bad local maxima.

The paper should be accepted. The theoretical results are important, well-supported, and resolve a long-standing open question. The practical implications for the use of EM in practice are significant, and the paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for understanding the limitations of local search methods for GMMs. The minor weaknesses identified do not detract from the paper's strong theoretical contribution.
