Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies the infinite-sample (population) log-likelihood surface for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 equally weighted, spherical components and fixed variances. It establishes two main results: (1) the population log-likelihood can have bad local maxima, even under well-separated, spherical mixtures in one dimension; moreover, the suboptimality gap can be made arbitrarily large, resolving an open conjecture of Srebro (2007). (2) With random initialization (sampling initial means i.i.d. from the mixture), both EM and a first-order EM variant converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − exp(−Ω(M)); additionally, first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, implying that bad local maxima, not saddles, are the dominant obstruction.

The work is well positioned relative to the EM literature and nonconvex optimization: it complements local convergence analyses for two-component mixtures (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015), algorithmic alternatives via clustering and moments (Arora et al., 2005; Vempala & Wang, 2002; Hsu & Kakade, 2013; Moitra & Valiant, 2010), and the “strict saddle” viewpoint (Pascanu et al., 2014; Lee et al., 2016; Panageas & Piliouras, 2016). It also connects with finite-sample pathologies (Améndola et al., 2015) and contrasts with benign landscapes in some nonconvex problems (Loh & Wainwright, 2013).

Strengths:
- Resolves a long-standing open question by exhibiting arbitrarily bad local maxima at the population level for well-separated, spherical, equally weighted GMMs with M ≥ 3.
- Provides an algorithmic consequence: random-initialized EM/gradient ascent fail with exponentially high probability in M on concrete instances, sharpening folklore about the difficulty of random restarts.
- Clarifies the nature of critical points: first-order EM avoids strict saddles almost surely, pointing squarely to local maxima as the culprit.
- The geometric construction and hierarchical “trap” argument are insightful and likely reusable.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Population vs finite-sample: The paper asserts that results “carry over” via standard empirical process arguments; it would be valuable to state an explicit high-probability sample-size requirement (as a function of separation, M, and d) under which (a) the bad local maximum persists approximately, and (b) the EM failure probability remains exp(−Ω(M)). Even a theorem sketch with conditions would strengthen impact.
- Separation regime and constants: The construction is 1D, well-separated in the sense of Ω(1). Please quantify the separation and Cgap trade-off in Theorem 1 and how the hierarchical construction scales constants in Theorem 2. Explicit constants would clarify the boundary between “benign” and “adversarial” regimes.
- Initialization model: The negative results are for uniform-from-data initialization. Many practitioners use k-means++ or splitting heuristics. Can the failure extend to k-means++-style seeding under your instances (e.g., because seeds overconcentrate within top-level clusters)? Even a discussion would help.
- First-order EM stepsize: Theorem 3 assumes s ∈ (0,1) and notes equivalence to gradient ascent on the population likelihood. Please make the Lipschitz/smoothness assumptions explicit to ensure ascent and convergence to a critical point, and clarify whether the same failure holds for line-search or diminishing-step variants.
- Scope in M = 2: The paper conjectures that two components are benign; any partial progress or negative evidence (e.g., nonexistence of strict bad local maxima under certain separation) would be of high interest.
- Empirical illustration: A small synthetic demonstration (population approximation with large n) showing the bad local maxima and the exp(−Ω(M)) dependence (even over modest M) would make the results more tangible.

Quality:
The results are clearly stated and, given the proof sketches, appear technically sound. The geometric argument for constructing a strict suboptimal local maximum in M = 3 and its generalization are plausible, and the hierarchical initialization-trapping argument explains the exp(−Ω(M)) success probability. The avoidance of strict saddles by first-order EM is consistent with the stable manifold arguments in recent literature; your proof avoids global smoothness assumptions, which is a nice technical contribution. The main limitation is the lack of explicit finite-sample statements and constants.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and well organized. The intuition for the M = 3 construction is helpful. Suggestions for clarity:
- State the precise separation parameters and neighborhood definitions used in Theorem 1, perhaps with a figure illustrating the local maximum neighborhood and boundary argument.
- Summarize the hierarchical construction for Theorem 2 in the main text with a schematic, and provide an explicit bound like c = c(separation) in the 1 − e−cM probability.
- Clarify the equivalence between first-order EM and gradient ascent on L, including the required conditions and references.

Originality:
High. The negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture in a favorable setting is novel, as is the exponential failure probability under natural random initialization. The strict-saddle non-convergence statement tailored to first-order EM in this context is also original.

Significance:
High. The results recalibrate expectations for EM and other local-search methods on GMMs, even in very benign regimes, and underscore the importance of principled initialization (e.g., via moments or spectral methods). The structural insights about the likelihood landscape are likely to influence both theory and practice in mixture modeling and nonconvex learning.

Questions for the authors:
1. Can you provide a finite-sample analogue of Theorem 1: for n ≥ n0(M, separation, δ), with probability ≥ 1 − δ, Ln has a local maximum within ε of the population bad local maximum with a comparable gap?
2. Does k-means++ initialization reduce the failure probability on your hierarchical instances, or does it suffer the same exp(−Ω(M)) behavior?
3. Are there extensions to unequal weights and/or higher dimensions via embedding the 1D construction?
4. Can your techniques say anything about the absence of bad local maxima for M = 2 under standard separation?

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves an open problem with clean, interpretable constructions.
- Strong, general algorithmic consequences for widely used methods.
- Clear positioning within and contribution to current theory of nonconvex optimization.

Cons for acceptance:
- No finite-sample theorems with explicit rates/conditions.
- Limited empirical evidence to illustrate the theory.
- Practical initialization baselines beyond uniform-from-data are not analyzed.

Minor comments/typos:
- Please standardize references (several placeholders like “? Q2015 E”).
- Define “well-separated” with explicit constants when d = 1 to avoid ambiguity.
- In Definition 1, for maximization, a strict saddle should have at least one strictly positive eigenvalue and at least one strictly negative (depending on convention); please ensure consistency with your usage.
