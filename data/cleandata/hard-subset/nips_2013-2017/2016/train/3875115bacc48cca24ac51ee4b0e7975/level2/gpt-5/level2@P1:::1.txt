Summary
This paper studies the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components under very favorable conditions: equal weights, spherical unit covariance, and well-separated means. The authors prove two main results. (1) Structural: Even in this benign setting, the population log-likelihood can have bad local maxima whose objective value is arbitrarily worse than that of the global optimum, settling a conjecture of Srebro (2007) in the negative. The construction is explicit (1D, M ≥ 3) and extends to well-separated mixtures. (2) Algorithmic: With random initialization (drawing initial means i.i.d. from the population), both EM and a first-order EM (gradient ascent on the population likelihood) converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). The paper further shows first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, indicating failures are due to poor local maxima rather than saddle points. The proofs combine a geometric construction of bad basins and a hierarchical “cluster-within-cluster” instance to make success probability exponentially small. The authors discuss implications for initialization and robustness, and outline how the population results translate to large finite samples.

Relation to prior work
The paper contrasts with prior positive results on EM’s local convergence with good initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and algorithmic guarantees under strong separation via clustering or moment methods (Arora et al., Vempala and Wang, Hsu and Kakade). It complements recent nonconvex optimization results where all local minima are near-global (e.g., Loh and Wainwright; Wang et al.) by providing a counterexample in the GMM likelihood landscape. The avoidance of strict saddles ties to and leverages ideas in Lee et al. (2016), Panageas and Piliouras (2016), and Ge et al. (2015). It directly resolves Srebro’s (2007) conjecture for the population setting.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The theorems are clearly stated; the constructions and arguments are plausible and well-motivated. The 1D construction for M ≥ 3 with arbitrarily bad local maxima is crisp, and the hierarchical construction for exponentially small success probability is compelling. The use of first-order EM and stable-manifold-style reasoning to rule out convergence to strict saddles appears careful (with appropriate step-size conditions).
- Scope and assumptions: Results are for population likelihood with fixed unit variance and equal component weights and do not require adversarial sampling. The translation to finite-sample settings is mentioned via standard empirical process arguments; providing explicit sample-size conditions would strengthen applicability.
- Completeness: The algorithmic negative results cover EM and gradient ascent (first-order EM), the most commonly used local search heuristics for likelihood maximization. It would be helpful to more explicitly delineate dimension requirements in the multi-level construction and the exact notion of “well-separated” used therein.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized with intuitive explanations (e.g., for the M=3 construction) and precise statements.
- Suggestions to improve clarity:
  - Explicitly reconcile “well-separated” (ξ = Ω(√d)) with the intuition that two means are “much closer” than the third: emphasize that “closer” is relative while all pairwise distances remain Ω(√d).
  - State the dimension for each construction (the structural example is 1D; is the hierarchical construction also 1D?).
  - For Theorems 2–3, specify the dependence of constants (c, Cgap) on separation, dimension, and step size, and give a brief sketch of how the population results transfer to finite samples (e.g., uniform convergence conditions on L and its gradient).
  - Clarify the exact random initialization model at population level: drawing initial means i.i.d. from the mixture versus from the sample; explain equivalence in the limit.

Originality
- Resolves a long-standing open conjecture on the absence of spurious local maxima in the population likelihood for well-separated, equally weighted spherical GMMs.
- Provides a new hierarchical instance demonstrating exponentially small success probability for random restarts.
- Establishes that first-order EM avoids strict saddle points in this setting, strengthening the interpretation that bad local maxima are the bottleneck.

Significance
- Conceptual impact: The results significantly update our understanding of the GMM likelihood surface and the limitations of local search with naive initialization, even in highly favorable regimes.
- Practical implications: Motivates the use of informed initialization (e.g., method-of-moments or spectral pilots) in EM for GMMs and cautions against relying on a small number of random restarts.
- Broader relevance: Techniques and insights are pertinent to nonconvex statistical estimation and the saddle-vs-local-max debate.

Weaknesses and questions
- Finite-sample instantiation: While it is reasonable to defer details, explicit finite-sample corollaries (with n scaling in M, d, separation) would improve practical relevance.
- Generality of model: Results assume equal weights and known unit variances; discussion of extensions to unequal weights or unknown/anisotropic covariances would be valuable. Do the constructions and negative results persist with mild weight imbalance or slight covariance misspecification?
- Basin geometry: Some intuition/visualization of the basins of attraction under EM/gradient ascent for the constructed instances would help readers internalize the mechanism.
- M=2 case: The paper mentions this remains open; adding partial progress or a sharper conjecture would strengthen the narrative.

Minor comments
- Provide guidance on step-size ranges for first-order EM beyond s ∈ (0,1), and comment on robustness to diminishing step sizes.
- Clean up a few reference formatting issues.

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves a notable open problem with clear, rigorous results.
- Strong, surprising negative results that are relevant to common practice.
- Novel constructions and proof ideas; good connection to contemporary nonconvex optimization theory.
- Clear implications for algorithm design and initialization strategies.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical validation; no finite-sample experiments illustrating the constructions.
- Assumes equal weights and fixed unit variances; extensions are not developed.
- Finite-sample consequences are stated informally without explicit rates.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a well-executed theoretical contribution that clarifies the geometry and algorithmic behavior of a central model in machine learning, with clear implications for both theory and practice. Adding brief finite-sample corollaries and a small synthetic experiment would further strengthen the paper but are not essential for acceptance.
