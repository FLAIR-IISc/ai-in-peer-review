Summary
The paper studies the population (infinite-sample) log-likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 equally weighted spherical components and establishes two main results. (1) It proves that the population likelihood can have bad local maxima—even for well-separated mixtures—whose values can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, resolving an open question posed by Srebro (2007). The construction already appears in d = 1 with M = 3 by clustering components at multiple scales and showing a neighborhood that traps local search but excludes global optima. (2) It shows that EM, as well as a first-order variant equivalent to gradient ascent on the population likelihood, initialized by sampling centers from the underlying mixture (the large-sample analogue of seeding from data), converges to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−Ω(M). Furthermore, the first-order EM variant almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, leveraging results in dynamical systems/optimization (e.g., Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016), thus attributing failures to bad local maxima rather than saddles. The results underscore the necessity of careful initialization for EM, even in highly favorable settings.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to classical EM (Dempster et al., 1977) and its analyses in special regimes (Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and to consistent alternative approaches via clustering and moments (Vempala and Wang, 2002; Arora et al., 2005; Moitra and Valiant, 2010; Hsu and Kakade, 2013). It resolves the population-level conjecture of Srebro (2007) that, in favorable settings, all local maxima might be global. It also complements recent perspectives on the role of saddles in nonconvex optimization (Pascanu et al., 2014; Ge et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016) by showing that in this domain bad local maxima are the dominant obstruction.

Quality
The theoretical claims are clearly stated and supported by rigorous arguments. The structural result relies on a carefully designed family of well-separated mixtures and a boundary argument that certifies the existence of strictly suboptimal local maxima with arbitrarily large likelihood gaps. The algorithmic result uses a hierarchical “cluster-within-cluster” construction to show that random initialization concentrates on basins of bad critical points with probability 1 − e−Ω(M). The avoidance of strict saddles for first-order EM is justified via recent gradient-dynamics theory, with attention to technicalities (e.g., smoothness) and a proof tailored to the EM setting. While empirical illustrations are absent, the theoretical focus and completeness of proofs are adequate for a theory paper.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The model, algorithms, and initialization scheme are defined precisely. The informal explanation accompanying the M = 3, d = 1 construction is helpful. Some aspects could be clarified: explicit separation scales and constants used in the constructions; a more detailed statement of the conditions under which the first-order EM analysis applies (e.g., stepsize range) and how these map to practical choices; and a more explicit, short sketch of the recursive construction yielding the e−Ω(M) success probability before deferring to the appendix.

Originality
The resolution of the Srebro (2007) conjecture is novel and important. The demonstration that even in the population and well-separated regime there exist arbitrarily bad local maxima is a sharp negative result. The exponentially small success probability of random initialization for EM/first-order EM, together with the strict-saddle avoidance result, provides a distinctive and conceptually clarifying contribution. The proof techniques—especially the hierarchical construction and the adaptation of stable manifold ideas—are original in this context and likely to be of broader interest.

Significance
The results have clear consequences for the practice and theory of likelihood-based estimation for mixtures. They explain why random restarts of EM may fail in principle unless the number of restarts is exponential in M, highlighting the criticality of informed initialization (e.g., via moments or careful seeding). By ruling out strict saddles as the culprit for gradient-based failures, the paper sharpens where to focus algorithmic effort (initialization and landscape shaping) and informs interpretation of empirical success stories. The findings are relevant to a core NeurIPS audience working on nonconvex optimization, statistical learning, and probabilistic modeling.

Pros
- Resolves a long-standing open question on the existence of bad local maxima in population GMM likelihoods.
- Provides strong algorithmic consequences showing EM/first-order EM with standard random initialization often fails with exponentially high probability.
- Clarifies the role of saddles vs. local maxima for gradient-based methods in this setting.
- Techniques are clean and may generalize to other mixture/nonconvex models.
- Clear exposition of the model and algorithms.

Cons
- Results are population-level; finite-sample implications are only discussed at a high level.
- Constructions are one-dimensional and adversarial; discussion of how typical such pathologies are in practice is limited.
- Assumes equal weights and spherical covariances; the impact of mild model misspecification (unequal weights, anisotropy) is not analyzed.
- No empirical demonstrations or practical guidance beyond advocating careful initialization.
- The favorable two-component case remains open, with only heuristic evidence.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- State explicit parameter regimes (e.g., separation scales, cluster sizes) for the constructions in the main text, and summarize the constants driving the Cgap bound.
- Provide a short finite-sample corollary with concrete sample-size requirements via standard concentration/empirical process arguments to illustrate practical reach.

Optional but valuable:
- Discuss robustness of the negative results to unequal weights and mild covariance anisotropy; identify which proof elements break and what might still hold.
- Include a brief empirical illustration (synthetic) demonstrating convergence to bad local maxima under random initialization and the benefit of a simple informed initializer (e.g., method-of-moments or k-means++ seeding).
- Expand the discussion on M = 2 by offering partial structural lemmas or conjectures that could guide future work.
- Comment on high-dimensional scaling: how separation Ω(√d) interacts with the constructions, and whether dimension affects the basin structure qualitatively.

Overall assessment
A strong, well-executed theory paper that settles a notable open question and delivers clear algorithmic insights about EM for GMMs. The contribution is timely, rigorous, and of broad interest to the NeurIPS community. Recommended for acceptance.
