

This paper addresses a fundamental question in the theoretical understanding of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) by resolving an open problem posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function. The authors demonstrate two key results: (1) for any M ≥ 3, they construct a well-separated mixture of equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians whose population log-likelihood function contains local maxima with arbitrarily worse log-likelihood values than the global optimum; (2) they show that the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to bad critical points with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)), while proving that first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely.

The paper makes significant contributions to the understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces and algorithmic behavior. The structural result (Theorem 1) is particularly noteworthy as it provides a concrete counterexample to Srebro's conjecture, resolving a long-standing open question. The algorithmic consequence (Theorem 2) has important practical implications, demonstrating that random initialization for EM in well-separated GMMs fails with exponentially high probability in M. The authors' construction of a recursive mixture structure to achieve exponentially small success probability is elegant and technically sophisticated. The connection between the structural results and algorithmic behavior is well-motivated and rigorously established.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical exposition. The authors carefully distinguish between the population and finite-sample settings, and the proofs are presented with appropriate technical detail. The introduction provides excellent context for the problem, thoroughly reviewing relevant prior work on GMM identifiability, estimation rates, and EM convergence. The authors properly acknowledge related work, including the results of Balakrishnan et al. (2015) on EM convergence for two-component mixtures.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical results are technically sound and rigorously proven. The authors provide careful analysis of both the structural properties of the likelihood function and the algorithmic behavior of EM, addressing the limitations of their approach (e.g., the restriction to equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians). The paper's claims are well-supported by the mathematical arguments presented.

The originality is high. The paper resolves an open question that has been the subject of speculation in the community since Srebro (2007). The construction of the counterexample for M ≥ 3 components is novel, as is the analysis of the algorithmic consequences of bad local maxima. The connection between the structural properties and the failure of random initialization for EM is particularly insightful and contributes to the broader understanding of non-convex optimization in statistical models.

The significance of the paper is substantial. The results have direct implications for the practical use of EM in GMM estimation, highlighting the necessity of careful initialization even in highly favorable settings. This work provides theoretical justification for why practitioners often need to use multiple restarts or more sophisticated initialization schemes. The techniques developed for analyzing the likelihood surface may also be applicable to other non-convex optimization problems in statistics and machine learning.

The paper could be strengthened by addressing a few minor points. The focus on equally-weighted, spherical Gaussians with fixed variance is somewhat restrictive, and it would be valuable to briefly discuss how the results might extend to more general GMMs. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the proposed solution (repeating random initialization exponentially many times) in practical settings. Additionally, while the paper states that the construction requires M ≥ 3, a more thorough exploration of why two components might be different would strengthen the main result.

The paper's contribution is significant within the context of NIPS, addressing a fundamental theoretical question with clear algorithmic implications. The results provide a crucial theoretical foundation for understanding the behavior of EM in GMM estimation, filling an important gap in the literature. The work advances the state of the art in understanding non-convex optimization for statistical models and provides a clear warning about the limitations of simple random initialization in practice.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a substantial contribution to the field. The results are novel, significant, and well-supported by rigorous analysis. The work resolves an important open question and has clear practical implications for the use of EM in GMM estimation. The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS.
