

This paper addresses an important open question regarding the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 components, as posed by Srebro (2007). The authors provide two main theoretical results: (1) they construct a GMM with equally-weighted, well-separated, spherical Gaussians that has local maxima whose log-likelihood values can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, and (2) they show that the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to these bad local maxima with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)). The authors further establish that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, suggesting that the poor performance of local search methods is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving an open question that has been the subject of interest for over a decade. The construction of bad local maxima for GMMs with M ≥ 3 components is novel and addresses a gap in the literature. The authors provide careful and rigorous proofs for their main results, with clear geometric intuition (particularly for M=3). The distinction between the effects of bad local maxima versus saddle points is particularly valuable, as it clarifies the nature of optimization challenges in GMMs.

The paper is well-written, well-organized, and clearly explains the main results and their implications. The authors appropriately contextualize their work within the literature, citing relevant prior work on GMM identifiability, theoretical properties of EM, and related optimization challenges. The technical arguments are sound, and the proofs are detailed and accessible.

One minor suggestion for improvement would be to briefly discuss how the results might extend to more general GMM settings (e.g., non-spherical Gaussians or unequal weights), which would help readers understand the broader applicability of the findings. While the paper focuses on an idealized setting, acknowledging the boundaries of their results would strengthen the discussion.

The significance of this work is substantial. The paper provides theoretical justification for the empirical observation that careful initialization is crucial for the EM algorithm in GMM estimation, even when applied to highly favorable settings. The results demonstrate that random initialization can fail with high probability, requiring exponentially many restarts to find a good solution. This has clear practical implications for the design of algorithms for GMM estimation, suggesting that initialization strategies based on method of moments or other techniques are essential.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the understanding of the optimization landscape for GMMs. The results resolve an important theoretical question and provide valuable insights into the practical limitations of commonly used algorithms. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and represents a significant advancement in our understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS 2016. The paper addresses a fundamental question in a widely used statistical model with important theoretical and practical implications, and the results are rigorously established. The writing is clear, the arguments are compelling, and the work is well-positioned to influence future research on GMM estimation and non-convex optimization.
