- Main structural result: Even for equal-weight, spherical, well-separated GMMs with M ≥ 3, the population log-likelihood can have arbitrarily bad local maxima (constructive 1D example), refuting Srebro (2007).
- Main algorithmic result: With i.i.d. random initialization from the population, EM and first-order EM converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e−Ω(M); failures stem from bad local maxima, not strict saddles.
- Proof approach: Combines geometric “bad basin” constructions and a hierarchical cluster-within-cluster instance to make success probability exponentially small; shows first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles under step-size conditions.
- Relation to prior work: Complements positive local-convergence and global-recovery results by providing a counterexample in the GMM likelihood landscape and connecting to saddle-avoidance literature.
- Technical soundness: Theorems are clearly stated and proofs are plausible; 1D construction and hierarchical argument are compelling and step-size conditions are addressed.
- Scope and assumptions: Results are for population likelihood with unit variances and equal weights; translation to finite samples is discussed but lacks explicit sample-size conditions.
- Completeness: Covers EM and gradient ascent; would benefit from clearer dimension requirements and precise “well-separated” definition in multi-level constructions.
- Clarity suggestions: Reconcile “well-separated” notion with the “closer vs. farther” intuition, state dimensions for constructions, quantify constants (c, Cgap) dependencies, and clarify random initialization model.
- Originality: Resolves a long-standing conjecture, introduces a hierarchical instance for exponentially small success probability, and shows first-order EM avoids strict saddles in this setting.
- Significance: Changes understanding of GMM likelihood geometry, advises informed initialization for EM, and has broader implications for nonconvex statistical estimation.
- Weaknesses/questions: Lacks explicit finite-sample corollaries and empirical validation; unclear robustness to unequal weights or covariance misspecification; M=2 case remains open.
- Minor comments: Provide concrete step-size guidance for first-order EM, discuss robustness to diminishing step sizes, and fix reference formatting.
- Arguments for acceptance: Rigorous resolution of an open problem, surprising negative results with practical implications, and novel proof techniques tied to modern nonconvex theory.
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited empirical support, restrictive model assumptions (equal weights/unit variances), and informal finite-sample implications without rates.
- Recommendation: Accept, with suggestion to add finite-sample corollaries and a small synthetic experiment if possible.
