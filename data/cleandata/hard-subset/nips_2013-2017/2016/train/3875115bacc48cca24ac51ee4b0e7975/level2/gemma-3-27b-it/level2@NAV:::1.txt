## Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper investigates the challenging problem of local maxima in the likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The authors rigorously demonstrate the existence of bad local maxima, even in the well-separated, equally-weighted case, resolving a long-standing conjecture by Srebro [2007]. Furthermore, they show that the EM algorithm, with random initialization, converges to these suboptimal points with high probability, and importantly, argues that this failure is attributable to bad local maxima, not saddle points.

The core contribution is a strong negative result regarding the landscape of the GMM likelihood. The proof, constructing a specific 3-component GMM with demonstrably bad local optima, is a significant technical achievement. The algorithmic consequences are equally important, highlighting the potential for EM to get stuck in poor solutions and necessitating careful initialization strategies. The analysis of the first-order EM algorithm further reinforces this point, specifically showing a lack of convergence to saddle points. 

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, building logically from background to main results. The authors carefully relate their work to prior research in mixture models, identifiability, and EM algorithm analysis, providing a comprehensive context. The thorough referencing of relevant literature demonstrates a strong understanding of the field. 

However, the paper’s focus is almost entirely theoretical. While the results are impactful for understanding the *possibility* of failure, it would be beneficial to include some empirical validation. For instance, even a small set of simulations demonstrating the observed behavior of the EM algorithm on similar GMMs would strengthen the claims.  Additionally, while the paper acknowledges the need for careful initialization, it doesn't explore potential practical initialization schemes beyond the random approach analyzed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and important theoretical result resolving an open problem.
*   Strong technical analysis and rigorous proofs.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Addresses a fundamental challenge in GMM estimation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks empirical validation to support theoretical claims.
*   Limited discussion of practical initialization strategies beyond the analyzed random scheme.

**Overall:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of GMM estimation. While the lack of empirical results is a minor weakness, the novelty and rigor of the work outweigh this concern. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting the inclusion of some illustrative simulations.
