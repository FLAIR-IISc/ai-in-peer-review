

This paper addresses a fundamental question in Gaussian mixture model (GMM) theory: whether the population likelihood function of GMMs has "bad" local maxima (i.e., local maxima with significantly worse log-likelihood than global maxima). The authors resolve an open question posed by Srebro (2007) by proving two main results: (1) for M ≥ 3 components, there exist well-separated, equally-weighted GMMs with arbitrarily bad local maxima in their population likelihood; (2) the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to bad critical points with probability at least 1 - e^(-Ω(M)). 

The paper's strength lies in its elegant theoretical framework and rigorous proofs, which build upon recent advances in non-convex optimization (e.g., Lee et al., 2016). The authors demonstrate that the poor performance of local search methods stems from bad local maxima rather than saddle points, as they prove first-order EM avoids strict saddle points almost surely. This clarifies an important theoretical question with direct practical implications for GMM estimation.

The work is well-positioned within the field, correctly contextualizing its contribution against prior work on GMM identifiability (Allman et al., 2009), EM convergence (Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and non-convex optimization (Ge et al., 2015). The paper's theoretical results are sound, with detailed proofs in the appendices, and the implications for practitioners are clear: careful initialization is essential even in well-separated settings.

However, the paper's focus on the population setting (infinite samples) limits its immediate practical applicability, though the authors note this can be extended to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments. The paper also leaves open the question of whether M=2 components behave differently, which would be valuable to explore.

The paper's significance is high as it resolves a long-standing theoretical question with important consequences for algorithmic design. The results provide a strong theoretical foundation for understanding EM's limitations and motivate the need for better initialization strategies in practice. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental question in the field with significant implications for both theory and practice. The paper should be accepted as it makes a clear, well-supported advance in understanding the behavior of EM for Gaussian mixture models.
