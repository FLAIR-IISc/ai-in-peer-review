This paper investigates the landscape of the population likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and its implications for the widely used Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors prove that, counter to a conjecture by Srebro [2007], bad local maxima exist even for well-separated spherical Gaussian mixtures. Furthermore, they demonstrate that with high probability, the EM algorithm (and a first-order variant) initialized randomly will converge to these suboptimal points. They also show that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that poor performance stems from local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question about the theoretical properties of GMM estimation. The problem of non-convexity in GMM estimation is well-established (Chen 1995, Allman et al. 2009), and the question of whether the likelihood surface becomes "nicer" in the limit of infinite samples is a natural one. This work provides a definitive negative answer and connects it to the practical performance of EM. The results are technically strong, building on recent advances in optimization theory (Lee et al. 2016, Panageas and Piliouras 2016) to draw conclusions about the behavior of gradient-based methods.  The connection to existing work on related problems like sparse recovery and high-dimensional regression (Loh and Wainwright 2013, Wang et al. 2014) is also nicely drawn, highlighting a contrast in the behavior of objective functions. The writing is generally clear, and the organization is logical.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Negative Result:** Demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima for well-separated GMMs is a substantial contribution. It resolves an open question and highlights a fundamental challenge in GMM estimation.
* **Strong Theoretical Analysis:** The proofs appear rigorous and are supported by references to relevant work in optimization and statistical learning. The use of recent results on gradient descent is particularly commendable.
* **Practical Implications:** The analysis of the EM algorithm's behavior with random initialization has clear implications for practitioners. It emphasizes the need for careful initialization strategies.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making the technical details accessible.
* **Connection to Prior Work:** The authors provide a good overview of the related literature and appropriately situate their work within the broader context of GMM estimation and non-convex optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Initialization Schemes:** While the paper convincingly demonstrates the failure of random initialization, it could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of alternative initialization schemes and their theoretical guarantees. Referencing work like Moitra and Valiant (2010) and Hsu and Kakade (2013) is helpful, but expanding on the practical considerations of these approaches (computational cost, robustness to model misspecification) would strengthen the paper.
* **Focus on Specific Setting:** The results are established for uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussians. While this is a tractable setting, it’s important to acknowledge the limitations of this assumption. A discussion of how the results might generalize (or fail to generalize) to more complex GMMs (e.g., different weights, non-spherical covariance matrices) would be valuable.
* **Proof Complexity:**  The proofs are quite involved. While rigor is appreciated, some steps could be presented with more clarity or moved to the appendix. 
* **Minor Typographical Errors:**  A few minor typos and grammatical errors were observed throughout the manuscript. A careful proofread would be beneficial. (e.g.,  "th Conference" in abstract)

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental and important problem in statistical estimation.
* The results are technically sound and provide a definitive answer to a longstanding open question.
* The work has clear implications for the practical use of the EM algorithm.
* The presentation is generally clear and well-organized.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The focus on a specific setting limits the generalizability of the results.
* The complexity of the proofs may make the paper inaccessible to a wider audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of GMM estimation. The existence of bad local maxima, even in a well-behaved setting, is a surprising and important finding. While the limited discussion of initialization schemes and the focus on a specific setting are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the identified weaknesses. Specifically, expanding the discussion on alternative initialization schemes and conducting a thorough proofread would improve the paper’s impact.
