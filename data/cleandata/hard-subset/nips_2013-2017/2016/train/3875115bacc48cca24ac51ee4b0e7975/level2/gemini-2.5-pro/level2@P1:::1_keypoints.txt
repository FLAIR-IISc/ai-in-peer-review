- Studies the population log-likelihood geometry of GMMs and consequences for EM, centered on Srebro (2007) conjecture.  
- Main result 1: disproves Srebro’s conjecture by constructing GMMs (for M ≥ 3) with arbitrarily bad local maxima.  
- Main result 2: shows population EM and a first-order variant with random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − exp(−cM), implying exponentially many restarts needed.  
- Shows the first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, indicating failures are due to poor local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Significance/originality: resolves a long-standing open problem and gives a novel, important negative result motivating better initialization methods.  
- Technical quality: proofs and constructions (merging/duplicating components, hierarchical clustering) are clever and technically sound.  
- Links to optimization theory: demonstrates convergence to local maxima (not saddles), connecting to recent gradient-method results.  
- Clarity: paper is very well-written, well-organized, with precise theorems and good intuition.  
- Weakness: constructions feel adversarial; unclear how prevalent such pathological landscapes are in typical GMMs (e.g., equidistant means).  
- Weakness: analysis is in the population (infinite-sample) regime; paper should discuss finite-sample sample complexity for practical impact.  
- Question 1: clarify key technical challenges and differences for the unresolved M = 2 case.  
- Question 2: is the step-size restriction s ∈ (0,1) essential or just technical, and could larger steps escape basins?  
- Question 3: why doesn’t the Lee et al. (2016) technique apply directly to standard EM, and can standard EM also avoid strict saddles?  
- Overall: an excellent, fundamental contribution with significant theoretical and practical implications.
