Summary: The paper proves two structural and algorithmic results about the population likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with M ≥ 3 equally weighted spherical components. (1) It constructs well-separated mixtures whose population log-likelihood admits spurious local maxima whose value can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, resolving an open question of Srebro (2007). (2) It shows that EM (and a first-order EM/gradient variant) with random initialization converges to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-Ω(M)}, and almost surely avoids strict saddle points. These results contrast with positive local-convergence analyses for special cases (e.g., two-component models, Balakrishnan et al.) and with the perspective that saddle points are the main obstacle (e.g., Pascanu et al., Lee et al.).

Quality: The claims are fully theoretical and clearly stated. Proof ideas are sketched in the main text; detailed arguments are deferred to the appendix. The construction for bad local maxima and the recursive instance used to obtain exponentially small success probability are plausible and technically interesting. The avoidance of strict saddles leverages modern dynamical-systems arguments for gradient flows. One limitation is the population setting and reliance on adversarially constructed, albeit well-separated, mixtures; empirical or finite-sample corroboration is absent. Minor issues: reference formatting is inconsistent; some constants/ranges (e.g., separation scales, step-size regimes) could be made more explicit.

Clarity: The paper is well organized, with intuitive geometric explanations (M = 3 construction) and a careful positioning relative to prior work. Some figures/illustrations beyond the M = 2 case could further aid intuition. The bibliography needs cleanup.

Originality: High. Resolving the Srebro conjecture at the population level and proving exponential failure probabilities for random-init EM are substantive and new. The structural techniques and recursive construction appear novel.

Significance: High for both theory and practice. The work rigorously explains widely observed sensitivity of EM to initialization and shifts attention from saddles to bad local maxima in GMMs, informing the design of initialization schemes or alternative estimators.

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves a longstanding open question with clean, sharp results.
- Strong, broadly relevant algorithmic implications for EM and gradient ascent.
- Clear connections to contemporary nonconvex optimization theory.

Cons against acceptance:
- Population-level focus; no finite-sample or empirical validation.
- Results target spherical, equal-weight mixtures and a specific random-initialization regime.
- Bibliographic and presentation polish needed.

Suggestions: Add finite-sample analogs or simulations to illustrate practical impact; clarify separation/constant regimes; discuss extensions to unequal weights/covariances and principled polynomial-time initializations; improve references.
