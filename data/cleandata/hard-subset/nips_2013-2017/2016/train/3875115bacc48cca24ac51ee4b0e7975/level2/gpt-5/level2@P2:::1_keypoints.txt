- Paper studies population log-likelihood for equally weighted spherical GMMs (M ≥ 3), proving bad local maxima exist even in well-separated 1D mixtures and gaps can be arbitrarily large (resolving Srebro 2007).
- Shows random initialization (sampling means i.i.d. from the mixture) leads EM and first-order EM to converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − exp(−Ω(M)).
- Proves first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, indicating bad local maxima (not saddles) are the main obstacle.
- Positions work relative to EM/local convergence, algorithmic methods (clustering/moments), strict-saddle literature, and finite-sample pathologies.
- Strength: Resolves a long-standing open question by constructing arbitrarily bad population-level local maxima for M ≥ 3.
- Strength: Gives algorithmic consequence that random-initialized EM/gradient ascent fail with exponentially high probability in M.
- Strength: Clarifies nature of critical points by showing first-order EM avoids strict saddles.
- Strength: Geometric construction and hierarchical “trap” argument are insightful and likely reusable.
- Weakness: No explicit finite-sample theorems or sample-size requirements stated to transfer population results to empirical data.
- Weakness: Separation regime and constants are not quantified; need explicit trade-off between separation and gap and scaling of constants in the hierarchical construction.
- Weakness: Negative results assume uniform-from-data initialization; impact on practical initializers like k-means++ is not analyzed.
- Weakness: Theorem 3 assumes stepsize s ∈ (0,1) but lacks explicit Lipschitz/smoothness conditions and discussion of line-search/diminishing-step variants.
- Weakness: Scope for M = 2 is unclear; no partial progress or negative evidence provided for two-component mixtures.
- Weakness: Lacks empirical illustration demonstrating bad local maxima and the exp(−Ω(M)) dependence.
- Quality: Results are clearly stated and proofs/sketches appear technically sound; stable-manifold style argument for saddle avoidance is convincing.
- Clarity: Paper is generally well written; suggestions include stating precise separation parameters, adding figures/schematics, and clarifying equivalence between first-order EM and gradient ascent.
- Originality: High — novel negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture and the exponential failure probability under natural initialization.
- Significance: High — recalibrates expectations for EM and local-search on GMMs and highlights need for principled initialization.
- Key questions for authors: provide finite-sample analogue, analyze k-means++ behavior, extend to unequal weights/higher dimensions, and say anything about M = 2.
- Pros for acceptance: resolves open problem, strong algorithmic consequences, and clear contribution to nonconvex optimization theory.
- Cons for acceptance: missing finite-sample rates, limited empirical evidence, and lack of analysis for practical initialization baselines.
- Minor comments: fix reference placeholders, define “well-separated” with explicit constants in d=1, and clarify strict-saddle eigenvalue/sign convention in Definition 1.
