The paper "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences" provides two fundamental results on the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models with M ≥ 3 components. The first main result shows that the population likelihood function has bad local maxima even in the special case of equally-weighted mixtures of well-separated and spherical Gaussians. The authors prove that the log-likelihood value of these bad local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than that of any global optimum, resolving an open question of Srebro [2007]. The second main result shows that the EM algorithm (or a first-order variant of it) with random initialization will converge to bad critical points with probability at least 1− e−Ω(M). The authors further establish that a first-order variant of EM will not converge to strict saddle points almost surely, indicating that the poor performance of the first-order method can be attributed to the existence of bad local maxima rather than bad saddle points.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, background, and related work. The main results are clearly stated and proved, and the authors provide a detailed discussion of the implications of their results.

The paper's contributions are significant, as they provide a negative answer to the open question of Srebro [2007] and demonstrate the necessity of careful initialization when using the EM algorithm in practice, even when applied in highly favorable settings. The authors' results also highlight the importance of understanding the behavior of algorithms for non-convex optimization and provide new techniques for reasoning about the structure of the population log-likelihood.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the authors do not provide a comprehensive comparison of their results with existing work on Gaussian mixture models. While they do discuss some related work, a more thorough comparison would be helpful in understanding the significance of their contributions.

Another potential area for improvement is in the presentation of the proofs. While the authors provide a detailed proof of their main results, some of the technical aspects are deferred to the appendices. It would be helpful to provide a more detailed explanation of the proofs in the main body of the paper, as this would make the paper more self-contained and easier to follow.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide significant contributions to the field of Gaussian mixture models. The paper's results have important implications for the use of the EM algorithm in practice, and the authors' techniques for reasoning about the structure of the population log-likelihood are likely to be useful in future work on non-convex optimization.
