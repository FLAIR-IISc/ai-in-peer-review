This paper investigates the landscape of the population likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and its implications for the EM algorithm. The authors demonstrate, contrary to a conjecture by Srebro [2007], that bad local maxima exist even for well-separated Gaussian components. They further show that with high probability, EM initialized with random points converges to these suboptimal solutions. Finally, they establish that the first-order EM method is unlikely to converge to saddle points, suggesting that bad local maxima are the primary reason for the algorithm’s poor performance.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question about the reliability of EM for GMMs. The problem is clearly stated, and the connection to existing work (Srebro’s conjecture, identifiability issues, convergence rates, and alternative algorithms) is well-established. The contribution is significant, as it provides a rigorous explanation for the observed sensitivity of EM to initialization. The proofs, while not fully detailed within the paper, appear well-structured and build upon established techniques in statistical learning theory.

**Strengths:**

*   **Important Result:** The demonstration of bad local maxima resolves a long-standing open question and provides a theoretical foundation for understanding the challenges of GMM estimation.
*   **Strong Algorithmic Implications:** The result on the convergence of EM to suboptimal solutions with high probability is a powerful statement about the limitations of this widely used algorithm.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction provides a good overview of the problem and its context.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The authors employ a rigorous mathematical approach, grounding their claims in solid theoretical results.
*   **Connections to Related Work:** The authors situate their work within the broader landscape of GMM estimation and optimization, providing a comprehensive overview of the relevant literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Practical Guidance:** While the paper clearly identifies a problem, it doesn't offer immediately applicable solutions. It emphasizes the need for careful initialization, but doesn’t suggest specific strategies beyond avoiding random initialization. Further discussion on practical initialization schemes beyond those mentioned would be beneficial.
*   **Theoretical Focus:** The paper is heavily theoretical and may be less accessible to practitioners. Some intuitive explanations of the mathematical arguments could make the results more approachable.
*   **Proof Details:** The paper refers to proofs in the appendices, but the level of detail may not be sufficient for all readers to fully follow the arguments. Expanding certain key steps within the main text could improve clarity.
*   **Scope of Generality:** The results are primarily established for uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussians. It would be valuable to briefly discuss the potential extension (or limitations) of these findings to more general GMMs (e.g., with different weighting schemes or covariance structures).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental and important question in statistical learning theory.
*   The results are novel and significant, resolving an open problem and providing a theoretical justification for observed phenomena.
*   The analysis is rigorous and well-supported by mathematical arguments.
*   The paper is clearly written and logically organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of immediate practical solutions may limit the paper’s impact.
*   The theoretical nature of the work may make it less accessible to a broad audience.
*   The generality of the results is somewhat limited by the specific assumptions on the GMM structure.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMM estimation and the behavior of the EM algorithm. Addressing the weaknesses related to practical guidance and proof details would further strengthen the paper’s impact. Specifically, elaborating on possible initialisation schemes and adding more intuition behind key theoretical arguments would be beneficial. The theoretical contribution is strong enough to outweigh the minor limitations.
