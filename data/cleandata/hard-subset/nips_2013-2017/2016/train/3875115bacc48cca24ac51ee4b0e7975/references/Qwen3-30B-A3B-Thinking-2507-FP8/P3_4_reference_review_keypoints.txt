- Resolves Srebro's 2007 conjecture by constructing counterexamples showing arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood for M ≥ 3 well-separated, equally-weighted isotropic Gaussians.  
- Shows random initialization of EM and first-order EM converges to these bad local maxima with probability that decays exponentially in M.  
- Complements recent non-convex optimization work by proving the issue is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.  
- Technical contributions are novel and rigorous, with a clever recursive construction amplifying failure probabilities (Theorem 2).  
- Distinguishes clearly between bad local maxima and strict saddle points (Theorem 4).  
- Main counterexample is constructed in d = 1, and the claimed generalization to high dimensions is not rigorously justified.  
- Finite-sample implications are asserted to follow from empirical process arguments but lack explicit sample complexity bounds.  
- Practical relevance is unclear: the paper does not quantify how large M must be for the exponential failure probability to matter in real applications.  
- Focus is limited to isotropic Gaussians with fixed variance, leaving the non-isotropic case unexplored.  
- Correctly situates the work within prior literature (Srebro, Améndola et al., recent non-convex optimization papers) and distinguishes population vs. finite-sample results.  
- Significant theoretical contribution with clear algorithmic implications, emphasizing the need for careful initialization (e.g., method-of-moments) over random restarts.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance (NIPS) despite limitations, citing the paper's rigor and importance.  
- Minor suggested improvements: discuss extension to non-isotropic Gaussians, provide more detailed finite-sample implications, and clarify extension of M=3 counterexamples to higher dimensions.
