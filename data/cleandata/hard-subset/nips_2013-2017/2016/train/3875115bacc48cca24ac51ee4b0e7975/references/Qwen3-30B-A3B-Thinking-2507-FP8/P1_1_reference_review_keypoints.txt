- Paper disproves Srebro’s conjecture by constructing a counterexample showing bad local maxima in the population log-likelihood for GMMs.  
- Counterexample: M=3 well-separated, uniform spherical Gaussians in d=1; bad local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum.  
- EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability in M.  
- First-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points, implying bad local maxima are the main cause of algorithmic failure.  
- Technical contribution is rigorous, with careful distinction between population and finite-sample settings and detailed proofs.  
- Results are original, resolving a decade-old open question with a novel, non-trivial construction.  
- Findings are significant for practitioners, highlighting the need for careful initialization (e.g., method-of-moments) when using EM.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written, with useful geometric intuition for the M=3 construction.  
- Primary weakness: the counterexample is somewhat artificial, and its prevalence in practical or higher-dimensional settings is unclear.  
- Lack of empirical finite-sample validation; discussion of translation to finite samples would strengthen practical relevance.  
- Connection to recent non-convex optimization work (e.g., saddle-point literature) could be developed more thoroughly.  
- Evaluation scores: Quality 9/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 8/10.  
- Recommendation: accept — significant theoretical contribution despite minor practical limitations.  
- Specific suggestions: include the missing Figure 1 (likelihood visualization), better relate to "no bad local minima" literature, discuss implications for initialization schemes, and highlight novel proof techniques for broader non-convex optimization interest.
