

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses Srebro's conjecture (2007) that for uniform mixtures of isotropic Gaussians, all local maxima of the population likelihood are global maxima. The authors claim to resolve this open question by constructing a counterexample for M ≥ 3 components showing that arbitrarily bad local maxima exist. They further demonstrate that with random initialization, both EM and first-order EM algorithms converge to these suboptimal points with high probability (exponentially small success probability in M).

The paper makes two main contributions: (1) a structural result showing the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood for M ≥ 3, and (2) algorithmic results showing that random initialization leads to failure with high probability for both EM and first-order EM.

**Strengths:**
The paper is technically rigorous, with careful proofs of the main results. The construction of the counterexample is mathematically sound and appears to correctly demonstrate the existence of bad local maxima. The algorithmic results are well-motivated and provide important insights into the limitations of random initialization for GMM estimation. The connection to recent literature on gradient methods and saddle points (Theorem 4) adds theoretical depth.

**Weaknesses:**
The most significant weakness is the paper's failure to adequately contextualize its results within the practical landscape of GMM estimation. The authors construct a highly pathological example for M = 3 (with two centers very close together and one far away) that leads to bad local maxima. However, this construction is extremely specific and does not represent typical GMM scenarios where components are well-separated (as required by the authors' own definition of "well-separated"). In fact, for the standard case where all components are well-separated by a constant margin (not exponentially small), the authors' construction does not apply.

The paper's claim that "random initialization followed by local search can fail to produce useful estimates" is overstated. The authors acknowledge in their conclusion that "effective initialization schemes, such as those based on pilot estimators utilizing the method of moments" are critical, which directly contradicts their main algorithmic claim. This is a significant oversight in the paper's narrative.

The paper also lacks empirical validation. It makes strong claims about the failure of EM with random initialization, but provides no empirical evidence to support these claims on either synthetic or real datasets. Without empirical demonstration, the theoretical results remain abstract and disconnected from practical concerns.

Furthermore, the paper's main result (Theorem 1) for M = 3 appears to contradict the extensive empirical evidence supporting Srebro's conjecture. While the authors provide a mathematical construction, they do not address why this construction is not observed in practice. The paper would have been stronger had it addressed this discrepancy.

The paper's focus on the population likelihood (infinite samples) rather than finite-sample behavior is also problematic. The authors note this limitation, but the implications for practical applications are not sufficiently discussed. The claim that "random initialization is likely to fail with high probability" is only valid in the population setting, which is not how practitioners use EM.

**Originality:**
While the specific construction of bad local maxima is novel, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from related work. The paper cites Balakrishnan et al. (2015) who studied local convergence of EM for two Gaussians, and the authors' results for M ≥ 3 can be seen as a natural extension. However, the paper does not clearly articulate how their work fundamentally advances beyond this prior literature.

**Significance:**
The significance of the paper is limited by the pathological nature of their counterexample. The results apply to a very specific construction that is not representative of typical GMMs used in practice. The paper does not demonstrate that this pathological behavior occurs in any standard datasets or common applications of GMMs. Without such evidence, the practical implications of the results are questionable.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents technically sound mathematical results that resolve Srebro's conjecture. However, the significance of these results is greatly diminished by the highly pathological nature of the counterexample and the lack of empirical validation. The paper's main algorithmic claim about the failure of random initialization is overstated, as the authors themselves acknowledge that better initialization schemes exist. 

The paper would be significantly stronger if it:
1) Provided empirical evidence of the pathological behavior on synthetic or real data
2) Clearly distinguished between the pathological construction and typical GMM scenarios
3) Demonstrated why this behavior would be expected in practical applications

As it stands, the paper presents an interesting theoretical result but fails to convincingly demonstrate its practical relevance. While the mathematical contribution is valid, the lack of connection to practical GMM estimation and the overstatement of the algorithmic implications make the paper's significance questionable for NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject

The paper has solid theoretical results but fails to demonstrate their practical significance or provide sufficient context for why these results matter to practitioners. The counterexample is too pathological to be meaningful in real-world applications, and the paper's algorithmic claims are contradicted by the authors' own acknowledgment of effective alternative initialization schemes.
