Summary
The paper resolves a longstanding conjecture (Srebro, 2007) by proving that the population log-likelihood of uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) can have bad local maxima as soon as M ≥ 3, even when components are well separated. The authors construct 1D, M=3 instances where local maxima have arbitrarily worse likelihood than the global optimum (Theorem 1). They then show that EM and first-order EM (gradient EM), when initialized by sampling centers from the mixture (equivalently, from the data in the infinite-sample limit), converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−cM (Theorems 2 and 3). Finally, they prove first-order EM avoids strict saddle points with probability one (Theorem 4), indicating failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles. The key ideas include a geometric construction that creates a suboptimal local basin and a recursive “cluster-of-clusters” instance yielding exponentially small success probability for random initialization. The paper argues that careful initialization is essential for likelihood-based methods, even in this favorable setting.

Relation to prior work
The work complements analysis of EM’s local convergence (Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and counters the empirical belief (and Srebro’s conjecture) that benign landscapes might hold in simple GMMs. It contrasts with algorithms with global guarantees under separation or method-of-moments approaches (e.g., Arora et al., Dasgupta & Schulman, Vempala & Wang; Moitra & Valiant; Hsu & Kakade). The saddle-avoidance result leverages recent gradient dynamics theory (Lee et al., Panageas & Piliouras) to pinpoint the failure mode as local maxima rather than saddles.

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria

Quality
- Technical soundness: The constructions and arguments are plausible and well motivated. The local maximum existence proof follows a clear geometric/topological scheme (define a neighborhood around a suboptimal configuration and show the boundary has strictly lower likelihood), and the recursive initialization trap yields the e−Ω(M) success probability. The strict-saddle avoidance for gradient EM is consistent with stable manifold-style results.
- Rigor: Full proofs are deferred to the appendix; the main text states assumptions (equal weights, unit variances, well-separated means) and clarifies population vs. sample. The extension to finite samples is asserted via standard empirical process arguments—reasonable but not demonstrated in detail in the main body.
- Scope and honesty: The paper is explicit about limitations (does not resolve M=2, population focus, specific initialization scheme). Claims are appropriately qualified.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The motivating 1D two-component visualization and the intuitive explanation for M=3 help. Definitions (EM, first-order EM, random initialization) are precise.
- Suggestions: It would help to state one fully explicit parameter setting (e.g., exact spacings and separation scales) for Theorem 1 in the main text; and to summarize finite-sample implications with a self-contained theorem statement. The reference list contains formatting artifacts that should be cleaned.

Originality
- The refutation of Srebro’s conjecture in this canonical GMM setting is original and important. The exponentially small success probability for EM/gradient EM under practical random initialization at the population level is new and sharp. The combination with strict-saddle avoidance crisply separates the landscape issue (bad local maxima) from algorithmic artifacts (saddles).

Significance
- Conceptual impact: High. The results reshape our understanding of the likelihood landscape for even the “nicest” GMMs and provide a rigorous caution against naive random initialization for EM/gradient ascent.
- Practical implications: While the hard instances are constructed (with hierarchical separation scales), they are within the usual spherical, equal-weight model. The message—that robust performance requires principled initialization (e.g., moments/spectral methods, k-means++ variants, or careful seeding)—is valuable to both theorists and practitioners.
- Limitations: No empirical illustrations; does not analyze common heuristics like k-means++ seeding or pruning variants in finite samples. The exponential failure rate is in M; for small M the practical risk may be modest.

Pros
- Resolves an open conjecture with a clean counterexample.
- Strong, quantified algorithmic consequences for EM and gradient EM under realistic random initialization.
- Clear identification of failure due to bad local maxima, not saddles.
- Techniques likely to inform broader nonconvex landscape analyses.

Cons
- Population focus; finite-sample guarantees sketched but not proved in the main text.
- No experiments to illustrate geometry, failure modes, or dependence on M.
- Scope of negative results is tied to a specific (though common) initialization; less is said about smarter initializations used in practice (k-means++, method-of-moments warm starts).
- M=2 case remains open.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide an explicit, concrete instance (numerical values) realizing Theorem 1 in the main text, and a small simulation demonstrating the local-basin trapping.
- Add a finite-sample theorem with conditions under which the empirical likelihood inherits the bad local maximum and EM failure with high probability.
- Discuss how k-means++-style seeding or overfitting+pruning interacts with your constructions; do your negative results extend?
- Clarify step-size conditions for first-order EM beyond s ∈ (0,1) and whether backtracking line-search changes conclusions.

Overall recommendation
Accept. This is a strong theoretical contribution that resolves an open problem and delivers clear, consequential insights about EM’s behavior in GMMs. Despite being primarily theoretical and focused on population analysis, the results are well motivated, original, and significant for the NeurIPS community.
