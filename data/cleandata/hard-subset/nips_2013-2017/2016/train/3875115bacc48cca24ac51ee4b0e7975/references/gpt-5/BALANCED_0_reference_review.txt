Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies the population log-likelihood landscape of uniformly weighted, spherical (unit-variance) Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), with a focus on the existence of spurious local maxima and the algorithmic implications for EM and gradient-based variants. It resolves a conjecture attributed to Srebro (2007), which posited the absence of bad local maxima in the infinite-sample setting for such “well-behaved” GMMs. The main structural result (Theorem 1) constructs, for any M ≥ 3 in 1D, a well-separated uniform mixture whose population likelihood has local maxima with arbitrarily large suboptimality gaps relative to the global maximum. Algorithmically, the authors prove that EM and first-order EM (gradient ascent on the population likelihood) with a standard random initialization scheme converge to suboptimal critical points with high probability 1 − e−cM on carefully constructed instances (Theorems 2–3). They also prove that first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points under constant step size s ∈ (0,1) (Theorem 4), leveraging recent “strict saddle avoidance” results (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016). The constructions use hierarchical clustering of true means to create “basins of attraction” that trap local search unless initialization matches the cluster cardinalities at each level, which happens with exponentially small probability.

The work connects to several strands: identifiability and asymptotics for mixtures (Teicher, 1963; Chen, 1995; Ghosal & van der Vaart, 2001; Ho & Nguyen, 2015), algorithmic analyses of EM (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and globally consistent alternatives via clustering or moments (Vempala & Wang, 2002; Arora et al., 2005; Moitra & Valiant, 2010; Hsu & Kakade, 2013). It provides a negative complement to positive results showing large basins of attraction under favorable separation and initialization.

Strengths:
- Resolves an open conjecture of broad interest: the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood for uniform, spherical, well-separated GMMs when M ≥ 3.
- Clear algorithmic consequence: standard random initialization for EM/gradient ascent fails with exponentially high probability on explicit instances, highlighting the necessity of careful initialization.
- Conceptual contribution: separates “strict saddle proliferation” from “bad local maxima” as the main obstacle in this setting; Theorem 4 strengthens the narrative by ruling out convergence to strict saddles for first-order EM.
- The geometric construction and recursive clustering argument are elegant and likely to influence further analysis of nonconvex likelihood surfaces.
- Relevance to the NeurIPS community is high: nonconvex optimization, statistical estimation, and the empirical practice of EM.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
Quality/technical soundness:
- The paper primarily treats the population likelihood. While the authors claim transfer to finite samples via “standard empirical process arguments,” the strength of these transfers (uniform convergence over gradients/Hessians needed to preserve critical points and their stability) should be quantified. Suggest adding explicit sample complexity bounds as a function of (M, d, separation, desired gap) ensuring that with high probability the empirical landscape inherited from n samples has corresponding bad local maxima and that EM/gradient ascent mimic population dynamics.
- The “well-separated” condition is defined as pairwise distances ≥ Ω(√d). The main 1D constructions involve two nearby centers relative to a distant third. Please clarify how the construction satisfies the stated separation, and quantify constants so that both “well-separated” and “arbitrarily bad gap” can hold simultaneously.
- Theorems 2–3 depend on a recursive, carefully designed instance. While that is appropriate for a negative result, readers would benefit from tighter characterization of when failure holds beyond this worst case. For example, can one state a condition on the true cluster tree under which random initialization succeeds with at most polynomially small probability vs exponentially small?
- Theorem 4: the paper states it avoids standard smoothness arguments via specific properties of first-order EM. Please make those properties explicit and clearly state the minimal regularity assumptions (e.g., continuity/Lipschitzness of the update map, Jacobian properties) under which the stable manifold argument goes through. Is s ∈ (0,1) essential or merely sufficient? How sensitive are the results to step size schedules?

Clarity:
- The geometric intuition around the M=3 construction is helpful; consider adding a figure analogous to Fig. 1 for the 3-center case and for the hierarchical instance used in Theorem 2 to convey the trapping mechanism visually.
- It would help to more explicitly contrast “bad local maxima” versus “flat saddle regions” in the GMM likelihood to prevent conflating phenomena.
- Some reference entries are inconsistent/incomplete (years as placeholders, venue mislabels). Please clean and standardize the bibliography.

Originality:
- The negative resolution of the conjecture and the exponential failure probability under random init are, to my knowledge, new and substantial. The use of recursive cluster structures to amplify failure probability is a neat idea.

Significance:
- These results recalibrate expectations around EM and gradient ascent even in favorable “well-specified, spherical, uniform” GMMs. They should motivate more careful initialization schemes (e.g., moment-based pilot estimates, k-means++/farthest-first, hierarchical splitting) and spur work on characterizing benign regimes. The findings are likely to influence both theory and practice.

Additional questions/suggestions for improvement:
- Two-component case: the paper flags it as open; any partial progress? For example, can you prove absence of bad local maxima under a minimal separation in 1D for M=2? Even a conditional or restricted result would be valuable.
- Unequal weights and unknown/anisotropic covariances: do your constructions extend to these more general, practically relevant settings? A brief discussion would broaden impact.
- Empirical illustration: a small synthetic experiment at large n validating the trapping phenomenon and the exponential-decay trend in success probability (as M grows) would strengthen the message.
- Discuss robustness: do small model misspecifications or minor deviations from sphericity alter the conclusions?

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves a notable open question and clarifies the nonconvex landscape of GMM likelihoods.
- Strong, clear negative results with practical implications for EM and gradient ascent.
- Technically interesting constructions and a useful connection to strict-saddle theory.

Cons against acceptance:
- Lack of explicit finite-sample guarantees and quantitative uniform convergence statements.
- Failure instances are somewhat stylized; broader characterization of failure regimes would improve generality.
- Minor clarity and bibliography issues; no empirical illustration.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a substantive, well-argued theoretical contribution with clear relevance to NeurIPS, and provides constructive guidance for future algorithm design and analysis.
