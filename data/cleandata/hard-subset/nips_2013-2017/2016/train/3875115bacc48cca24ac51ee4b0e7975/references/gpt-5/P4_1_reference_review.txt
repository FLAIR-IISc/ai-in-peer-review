Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the population log-likelihood landscape of uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and the behavior of EM and first-order EM under random initialization. The main structural result (Theorem 1) disproves a conjecture of Srebro (2007) by exhibiting, for any M ≥ 3, a one-dimensional, well-separated, unit-variance GMM whose population log-likelihood has suboptimal local maxima, with arbitrarily large gap from the global maximum. The construction is intuitive for M = 3: two nearby true centers and one far-away center; placing two model centers on the far-away component and one at the midpoint of the nearby pair yields a neighborhood containing a suboptimal local maximum.

The algorithmic consequences are twofold. First, EM with random initialization (equivalently, drawing initial centers i.i.d. from the mixture distribution in the population limit) converges to a suboptimal critical point with probability at least 1 − e−cM (Theorem 2). The proof uses a hierarchical, well-separated clustering of the true centers across log M levels; unless the initialization matches the exact per-cluster counts at each level, the iterates become trapped in suboptimal basins. Second, analogous results hold for first-order EM (gradient ascent on the population likelihood) with step size s ∈ (0, 1) (Theorem 3). Finally, using recent results on gradient methods and dynamical systems (e.g., Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016), the paper shows that first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points with probability one (Theorem 4), so failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.

These results complement extensive work on EM and GMM learning: positive algorithmic guarantees under strong separations or moment methods (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Arora et al., 2005; Vempala & Wang, 2002; Moitra & Valiant, 2010; Hsu & Kakade, 2013), analyses of local convergence for two-component mixtures (Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and identifiability and rates for mixture estimation (Teicher, 1963; Chen, 1995; Ghosal & van der Vaart, 2001; Ho & Nguyen, 2015). The work differs from Améndola et al. (2015), which showed many local maxima for finite-sample likelihoods, by establishing bad local maxima in the population setting.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The claims are clearly stated and supported by plausible constructions and proof sketches, with full proofs deferred to an appendix. The geometric construction for M = 3 and the recursive hierarchical construction driving the e−Ω(M) failure probability are coherent. The link between first-order EM and gradient ascent on the population likelihood is standard (Balakrishnan et al., 2015). The use of stable manifold ideas to rule out convergence to strict saddles aligns with recent theory.
- Assumptions and completeness: Results are at the population level, with unit covariance, equal weights, and 1D (for the explicit local-maxima construction). The extension to finite samples is asserted via standard empirical process arguments, but no quantitative finite-sample bounds are provided. The step-size condition s ∈ (0, 1) is termed “weak”; more precise conditions in terms of smoothness/Lipschitz constants would strengthen rigor. The theorem statements could specify explicit separation requirements and constants to make “well-separated” and “arbitrarily bad” fully concrete.
- Reproducibility: Proof details are relegated to supplemental sections; the main text provides sufficient intuition but not enough to reproduce all the arguments without the appendix.

Clarity:
- Organization is clear: background, main results, and implications are well structured. The intuitive explanations for the constructions are helpful.
- Minor issues: some typographical errors (e.g., M-step indexing uses sum over i instead of ℓ in the sample formula); reference formatting contains placeholders and inconsistencies (years missing, escaped characters). The definition of “well-separated” as ξ = Ω(√d) could be linked explicitly to the 1D constructions used in Theorem 1. The precise smoothness/regularity assumptions needed for Theorem 4 could be stated more explicitly.

Originality:
- High. The paper settles an open conjecture (Srebro, 2007) by constructing arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood of well-separated GMMs for M ≥ 3 and shows exponentially small success probability for random-initialized EM/first-order EM. The hierarchical construction yielding e−Ω(M) behavior is novel. The strict-saddle avoidance result in this setting connects to, but is distinct from, prior general results.

Significance:
- The results have substantial conceptual and practical implications. They undermine the hope that population likelihoods of benign GMMs are free of bad local maxima, and they rigorously caution against naive random initialization for EM/gradient ascent. This motivates careful initialization (e.g., via moments/spectral methods) even in large-sample, well-specified regimes. The negative results are likely to influence how practitioners think about EM pipelines and how theorists design initialization schemes.

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves a notable open question by exhibiting bad local maxima in population GMM likelihoods for M ≥ 3.
- Clear algorithmic consequence: exponentially small success probability for random-initialized EM/first-order EM.
- Solid connection to modern nonconvex optimization theory (strict saddle avoidance).
- Intuitive constructions and implications; likely to stimulate further work on initialization and landscape analysis.

Cons for acceptance:
- Scope limited to 1D construction for Theorem 1; higher-dimensional generalizations are not developed.
- Population-level focus; finite-sample implications are only sketched without explicit rates.
- Assumptions (equal weights, unit covariance) are restrictive; robustness to mild misspecification or heteroskedasticity is not addressed.
- Theorem 4 relies on nontrivial dynamical systems arguments; assumptions needed for convergence could be stated more precisely.
- Presentation issues: typos and reference formatting need correction; some constants and separation requirements are not explicit.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Specify explicit separation thresholds and constants for the constructions and theorems.
- Provide a finite-sample corollary quantifying when the negative phenomena persist (sample size vs. separation).
- Discuss extensions to higher dimensions and to non-uniform weights/variances.
- Clarify the precise regularity conditions and step-size requirements for first-order EM convergence and saddle avoidance.
- Add small-scale simulations illustrating the constructions and failure modes.
- Fix typographical and bibliographic issues (years, author names, indices in equations).
