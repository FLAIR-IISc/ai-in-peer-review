

This paper addresses an important open question regarding the structure of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), specifically challenging Srebro's conjecture (2007) that all local maxima of the population likelihood function are global maxima for uniformly weighted mixtures of spherical Gaussians. The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture by constructing a counterexample for M ≥ 3 components, demonstrating that arbitrarily bad local maxima exist in the population likelihood. They further analyze the algorithmic implications, showing that the EM algorithm and its first-order variant with random initialization converge to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability as a function of M.

The paper builds on foundational work in mixture models (Titterington, 1985; Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009), recent theoretical advances on GMM estimation (Chen, 1995; Ho and Nguyen, 2015), and algorithmic analyses of EM (Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015). It relates to broader literature on non-convex optimization (Loh and Wainwright, 2013; Wang et al., 2014) and saddle point avoidance (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016).

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical results with well-constructed counterexamples. The proof techniques for establishing the existence of bad local maxima are novel and well-executed. The analysis of algorithmic behavior under random initialization is comprehensive, with the key insight that success probability decays exponentially with M.

2. **Novelty:** The paper resolves an important open question in the field, directly challenging a widely cited conjecture. The construction of counterexamples for M ≥ 3 (while leaving open the M=2 case) is a significant theoretical contribution. The distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points is particularly valuable, as it clarifies the fundamental reason for EM's failure in certain settings.

3. **Significance:** The results have clear implications for practical algorithm design, demonstrating that even in well-separated settings, random initialization can fail catastrophically for EM and first-order EM. This explains why practitioners often use more sophisticated initialization schemes (e.g., method of moments) and provides theoretical justification for these practices.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear exposition of the problem, results, and their implications. The geometric intuition for the counterexample construction is particularly helpful for understanding the main insight.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Scope Limitations:** The results are restricted to uniform mixtures of spherical Gaussians with fixed variance, which is a special case. While the authors note that their results extend to large finite samples via standard empirical process arguments, the paper does not address whether the counterexample persists for more general GMMs (e.g., with varying variances or non-uniform weights).

2. **Algorithmic Practicality:** While the paper establishes that random initialization fails with exponentially high probability, it does not provide concrete guidance on better initialization strategies. The connection to existing methods (e.g., method of moments) is mentioned but not elaborated upon in the context of the counterexample.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses on population likelihood and theoretical guarantees, without providing empirical evidence to support the theoretical findings in finite-sample settings. While the authors note that their results extend to finite samples, a brief empirical demonstration would strengthen the practical relevance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Resolves a significant open question in GMM theory that has been widely discussed since Srebro (2007).
- Provides rigorous theoretical justification for the empirical observation that careful initialization is critical for EM.
- Contributes to the broader understanding of non-convex optimization in statistical learning.
- The distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points is a valuable theoretical insight with implications beyond GMMs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The results are limited to a specific special case of GMMs, which may reduce their general applicability.
- The paper does not provide concrete evidence of how common these bad local maxima are in practical settings.
- The theoretical results on failure probability are strong, but the paper does not offer constructive alternatives for initialization.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces and their algorithmic implications. The results are technically sound, well-justified, and address a long-standing open question. While the scope is somewhat limited to a specific GMM setting, the insights have broad relevance for non-convex optimization in statistical learning. The paper advances the field by clarifying why certain optimization strategies fail for GMMs, providing theoretical grounding for established empirical practices. The quality of the theoretical analysis, novelty of the results, and significance of the findings make this a strong contribution to the NIPS community.
