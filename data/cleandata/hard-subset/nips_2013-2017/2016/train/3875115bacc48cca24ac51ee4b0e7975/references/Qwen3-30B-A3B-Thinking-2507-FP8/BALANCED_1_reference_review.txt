

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses an important open question in the theoretical analysis of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) - whether the population log-likelihood for well-separated GMMs has no "bad" local maxima (Srebro, 2007). The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture, demonstrating that for M ≥ 3 components, there exist well-separated uniform mixtures of spherical unit-variance Gaussians whose population log-likelihood contains arbitrarily bad local maxima. They further show that the EM algorithm with random initialization converges to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability as a function of M.

**Strengths:**

1. **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper resolves a long-standing open question (Srebro, 2007) that has been discussed in the literature since 2007. The construction of bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 is elegant and general, providing a clear counterexample to the conjecture. This is a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs.

2. **Strong Theoretical Results:** The paper establishes three main results: (1) existence of bad local maxima, (2) high probability of convergence to bad local maxima with random initialization, and (3) characterization of the types of critical points reached by first-order EM. The proofs are rigorous and introduce new techniques for analyzing likelihood surfaces, which could be valuable for future work on non-convex optimization.

3. **Relevance to Practice:** The results have important practical implications for the widespread use of EM in GMM estimation. The paper explains why multiple random restarts are often necessary in practice, providing theoretical justification for this common empirical practice.

4. **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions, good motivation, and a logical flow from problem statement to results. The geometric intuition for the construction is particularly helpful for understanding the main technical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Results:** The paper focuses exclusively on the idealized case of uniform weights and spherical Gaussians with fixed variance. While this is a natural starting point, the authors should discuss how their results might extend to more general cases (e.g., non-uniform weights, different variances, or non-spherical Gaussians). This limitation reduces the immediate applicability of the results to more complex GMMs used in practice.

2. **Finite-Sample Extension:** The authors claim that their results "carry over in a straightforward way" to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments, but this is not rigorously established. A more detailed discussion of the finite-sample implications would strengthen the paper significantly.

3. **Lack of Practical Guidance:** While the paper explains why random initialization fails, it doesn't provide concrete guidance on how many restarts might be necessary in practice (beyond the exponential dependence on M). A more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the required restarts would be valuable for practitioners.

4. **Comparison to Recent Work:** The paper mentions Balakrishnan et al. (2015) as related work, but doesn't fully contextualize how their results complement or differ from this recent work on EM convergence. A more thorough comparison would help situate this contribution within the broader literature.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving an important open question in the GMM literature. The results are technically sound, well-motivated, and have clear implications for the practical use of EM algorithms. The paper meets the quality criterion with rigorous proofs, and addresses the significance criterion by providing important theoretical justification for common empirical practices.

The main weaknesses are related to the scope of the results and the lack of detailed discussion about finite-sample implications and practical guidance. However, these are relatively minor concerns given the paper's strong theoretical contribution. The paper is clearly original and makes a substantial advance in understanding the landscape of the GMM likelihood function.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that resolves an important open question in the field. While the scope is somewhat limited to the idealized case, this is a natural starting point for the theoretical investigation, and the results are robust and generalizable in spirit. The paper provides important insights into why EM with random initialization can fail, which is valuable for both theoretical understanding and practical applications of GMMs.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Add a brief discussion of how the results might extend to more general GMMs (non-uniform weights, different variances).
2. Provide more detail on the finite-sample implications and how the results scale with sample size.
3. Include a more thorough comparison with recent work on EM convergence for GMMs (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015).
4. Consider adding a small empirical illustration (even if just a 1D visualization) to help readers intuitively grasp the likelihood surface structure.

This paper is a strong contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs and has significant implications for both the theory and practice of mixture model estimation. The results are important enough that they should be widely disseminated at NIPS.
