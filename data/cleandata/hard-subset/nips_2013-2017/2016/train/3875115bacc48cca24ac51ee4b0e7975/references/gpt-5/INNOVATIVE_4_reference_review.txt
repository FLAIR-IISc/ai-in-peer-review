Summary
This paper overturns a long-standing conjecture (Srebro, 2007) by constructing well-separated, uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussian mixtures whose population log-likelihood possesses spurious local maxima with arbitrarily large suboptimality gaps. The construction is explicit for M ≥ 3 in 1D. Building on this structural insight, the authors prove algorithmic consequences: EM and first-order (gradient) EM with random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{−cM} for a universal constant c, implying that exponentially many random restarts are needed to reach a global optimum with constant probability. They further show that first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, so failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles. The proofs use geometric reasoning about the population likelihood landscape and a recursive mixture construction to obtain exponentially small success probabilities; the avoidance-of-saddles result leverages recent dynamical-systems analyses of gradient methods while avoiding global smoothness assumptions for EM.

Relation to prior work
- Clarifies and refutes Srebro’s conjecture about benign local maxima for population GMM likelihoods in the uniform, spherical, well-separated case.
- Complements analyses of EM that establish local convergence under good initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) by showing random initialization can be provably ineffective even in large-sample, well-specified settings.
- Relates to lines of work ensuring global guarantees via clustering/moments (Arora et al., Vempala & Wang, Hsu & Kakade, Moitra & Valiant), underscoring the importance of informed initialization for likelihood-based methods.
- Connects to optimization theory on saddle points (Pascanu et al.; Lee et al.; Panageas & Piliouras) by demonstrating that strict saddles are not the culprit here.

Strengths
- Novel and impactful: Resolving a prominent conjecture and demonstrating strong algorithmic consequences in a classic model family is a substantial conceptual advance.
- Clean, minimal assumptions: The negative results hold for population likelihoods of uniform, spherical, unit-variance, well-separated mixtures—arguably the most favorable GMM setting.
- Clear algorithmic message: Random-restart EM/gradient EM can fail with exponentially high probability; careful initialization is necessary.
- Technical contributions: New geometric arguments to certify existence of bad local maxima; a recursive mixture construction to amplify failure probability; an elegant application of gradient-flow arguments to rule out convergence to strict saddles for first-order EM.

Weaknesses and questions
- Dimensionality: The core structural counterexample is given in d = 1 (though well-separatedness in 1D is already meaningful). An explicit statement about extension to higher dimensions (e.g., via embedding or product constructions) would strengthen the generality claims.
- Finite-sample instantiation: While the authors state that results carry over via standard empirical process arguments, the paper would benefit from a brief finite-sample theorem (rates and conditions) or at least a precise sketch.
- Practical scope: The negative results target random initialization; it would be informative to discuss empirically common initializations (k-means++, hierarchical clustering, spectral/moment-based seeds) and whether the recursive construction defeats them or not.
- Tightness and constants: The existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima is compelling; adding intuition on how the separation and cluster geometry control the gap would aid understanding. Similarly, giving a concrete c in 1 − e^{−cM} (or at least its dependence on construction parameters) would help.
- EM vs first-order EM: The saddle-avoidance result is established for first-order EM; a discussion of whether analogous behavior holds for full EM (and what technical obstacles prevent a proof) would be useful.

Quality
The theoretical claims are clearly stated and supported by rigorous arguments (as outlined). The high-level proof ideas are explained well in the main text, with technical details deferred. The work is complete as a theory paper; empirical validation is not necessary but could broaden impact.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with helpful intuition (e.g., the M=3 geometry). Some reference formatting is inconsistent; tightening notation early (e.g., explicit definition of “well-separated” in 1D vs general d) and summarizing the recursive construction with a figure would improve accessibility.

Originality
High. Disproving a widely believed conjecture in a canonical model and pinpointing algorithmic consequences is a significant and original contribution. The recursive construction and the combination of structural and algorithmic results are fresh.

Significance
High. These results reshape how we think about likelihood landscapes for GMMs and the limits of EM with naive initialization, even in the infinite-data, well-specified regime. They argue for principled initialization and may influence both theory and practice in mixture modeling and nonconvex optimization.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concise finite-sample corollary with explicit rates/conditions.
- Clarify extension to d > 1 and whether analogous constructions exist with equal pairwise separations.
- Empirically illustrate the phenomenon on synthetic data (small M), and test whether k-means++ or spectral seeds mitigate failure.
- Expand on whether the recursive instance thwarts multi-start heuristics that enforce diversity among initial centers.
- Clean up and complete reference entries.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a bold, novel, and consequential set of results that advance our understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes and the behavior of EM. Minor limitations (1D construction, finite-sample formalities, and reference polish) do not detract from the core conceptual contribution.

Confidence
High.
