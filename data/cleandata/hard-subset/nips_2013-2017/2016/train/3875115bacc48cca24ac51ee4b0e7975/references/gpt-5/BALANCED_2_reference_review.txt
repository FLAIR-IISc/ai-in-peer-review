Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper investigates the optimization landscape of the population log-likelihood for uniformly weighted mixtures of spherical Gaussians with fixed variances. It resolves a conjecture of Srebro (2007), which posited that in the infinite-sample (population) setting there are no spurious local maxima for such GMMs. The authors construct, for any M ≥ 3, a one-dimensional, well-separated mixture with arbitrarily bad local maxima (Theorem 1). They then analyze algorithmic implications: EM and first-order EM (gradient ascent on the population log-likelihood) with a practical random initialization scheme converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-cM} for a universal constant c (Theorems 2–3). Finally, leveraging ideas from Lee et al. (2016) and Panageas and Piliouras (2016), they show first-order EM cannot converge to strict saddle points with positive probability (Theorem 4), suggesting failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.

This work connects to several strands: classical identifiability and rates for mixtures (Teicher 1963; Chen 1995; Genovese and Wasserman 2000; Ghosal and van der Vaart 2001; Ho and Nguyen 2015), algorithmic guarantees for learning GMMs via clustering and moments (Vempala and Wang 2002; Arora et al. 2005; Dasgupta and Schulman 2007; Belkin and Sinha 2010; Moitra and Valiant 2010; Hsu and Kakade 2013), and recent analyses of EM’s local convergence under good initialization (Balakrishnan et al. 2015). The paper also dialogues with the “benign nonconvexity” literature (e.g., Loh and Wainwright 2013; Wang et al. 2014) and the saddle-point perspective (Pascanu et al. 2014; Lee et al. 2016).

Strengths:
- Clear, impactful negative result refuting a widely held conjecture for a canonical model class, even under favorable assumptions (equal weights, spherical, unit variance, well-separated).
- Algorithmic consequences are convincing: random initialization plus EM/first-order EM fails with exponentially high probability via an elegant recursive clustering construction.
- Theorem 4 strengthens the interpretation: failures are not due to strict saddles but to genuinely bad local maxima.
- The problem is central to practice, where EM is ubiquitous; the results motivate careful initialization and alternative methods (e.g., moments-based).
- The intuition behind the bad local maximum for M = 3 is well explained, and the high-level proof strategy is transparent.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Scope is limited to population objectives, equal weights, and fixed spherical variances; it would help to discuss robustness of the constructions to mild deviations (unequal weights, small covariance perturbations, higher dimensions).
- The constructions are one-dimensional and somewhat contrived (two close centers, recursion across scales). How representative are these of practical instances? What is the measure/volume of the basins of attraction in parameter space?
- Finite-sample consequences are only sketched (“standard empirical process arguments”). Concrete sample-size requirements relative to separation and M would improve practical relevance.
- Theorem 4 part (a) claims convergence of first-order EM iterates to a critical point with constant step size s ∈ (0,1) without invoking smoothness/Lipschitz assumptions. This is stronger than standard results; more conditions or a clearer statement of assumptions would be helpful.
- The probability bounds depend on a worst-case instance. It would be useful to quantify success probability under simpler or more natural spatial arrangements of component means.
- The analysis focuses on random initialization by sampling from the mixture. Many practitioners use k-means++-style seeding or moment/spectral initializers; an empirical or theoretical comparison would be valuable.
- Some clarity and presentation issues: references have formatting glitches; some figures are referenced but not shown; exact separation constants (beyond Ω(√d)) and “well-separated” parameters in the theorems are not explicit.

Quality:
The results are technically plausible and align with known theory of EM and nonconvex optimization. The high-level proof ideas are sound, though some arguments (finite-sample translation; convergence conditions for first-order EM) should be made more precise. Deferring proofs to the appendix is fine, but the main text could better highlight key lemmas and constants.

Clarity:
Generally well organized with helpful intuition. The construction for M = 3 is clearly motivated. Some editing is needed: clean up the references, ensure all figures are present, and state assumptions (e.g., step size, smoothness) crisply. Explicit constants and a precise definition of “well-separated” used in the theorems would help.

Originality:
High. The paper overturns a conjecture and establishes strong negative algorithmic consequences, complementing prior positive results that required good initialization.

Significance:
High for theory of mixture models and EM. It sharpens our understanding of when EM can/cannot be expected to work and motivates principled initialization schemes. Even as a negative result, it is likely to influence algorithm design and analysis.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide explicit separation thresholds and constants in Theorems 1–3; quantify the gap Cgap in terms of separation.
- Add a finite-sample corollary with concrete n as a function of M, separation, and d to ensure the sample likelihood inherits the population’s bad landscape with high probability.
- Discuss robustness to slight deviations: unequal weights, near-spherical covariances, higher-dimensional embeddings of the 1D construction.
- Clarify the assumptions behind Theorem 4(a) (e.g., smoothness or KL-smoothness of L, step size conditions ensuring stable gradient ascent).
- Include empirical illustrations on the constructed instances: failure rate vs M, basin plots for M = 3, sensitivity to step size, and comparison to k-means++/moment-based initializers.
- Clean up reference formatting and ensure all cited works are properly listed.

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves an open conjecture with a crisp counterexample.
- Strong, relevant algorithmic implications for widely used methods.
- Timely and well connected to current nonconvex optimization literature.

Cons for acceptance:
- Population, worst-case focus; limited discussion of robustness and finite-sample thresholds.
- Constructions are somewhat stylized; practical prevalence is not quantified.
- Some technical claims (first-order EM convergence) need clearer assumptions.

Recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions).
