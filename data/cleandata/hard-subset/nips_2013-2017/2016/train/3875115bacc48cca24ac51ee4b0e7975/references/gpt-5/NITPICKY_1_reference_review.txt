Summary
The paper studies the population log-likelihood landscape of uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with known variance, focusing on the existence of spurious local maxima and the algorithmic behavior of EM/first-order EM under random initialization. It claims three main results: (i) for any M ≥ 3, there exist well-separated 1D mixtures whose population likelihood has suboptimal local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global optimum (negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture); (ii) EM and first-order EM, initialized by drawing centers from the mixture distribution, converge with probability at least 1 − e−cM to suboptimal critical points; (iii) first-order EM (gradient ascent on L) almost surely does not converge to strict saddles, so failures are due to bad local maxima, not saddles. The paper relates to prior work on EM analysis (Dasgupta & Schulman; Balakrishnan et al.), identifiability and rates for mixtures (Teicher; Chen; Ho & Nguyen), method-of-moments alternatives (Hsu & Kakade; Moitra & Valiant), and gradient methods’ avoidance of strict saddles (Lee et al.; Panageas & Piliouras).

Strengths
- Conceptually important: resolves a long-standing conjecture (Srebro 2007) in the negative for M ≥ 3 and shifts focus from saddle points to genuinely bad local maxima in GMM likelihoods.
- Algorithmic implications are crisply stated: random restarts+local search can fail with exponentially small success probability, motivating careful initialization.
- The hierarchical “cluster within cluster” construction for exponentially small success probability is intriguing and, if made precise, would be a valuable contribution.
- The population setting isolates likelihood geometry without finite-sample noise and is analytically tractable.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
1) Theorem 1 existence argument lacks necessary topological conditions. The sketch relies on “L is bounded from above” and “L on the boundary is strictly smaller than at a suboptimal configuration µ,” and concludes a local maximum exists in the neighborhood. This is not sufficient: one needs continuity and compactness to invoke Weierstrass on the closure of a neighborhood (e.g., a compact ball). Please explicitly construct a compact neighborhood, state continuity of L, and argue that the maximizer lies in the interior. As written, the step “bounded from above ⇒ maximum exists” is incorrect.

2) Precision of “well-separated” vs. construction. You define well-separated as min pairwise distance ≥ Ω(√d) but later require two true centers to be “much closer” to each other than to a third. In d=1 this can still satisfy Ω(1)-separation, but the text should make explicit that the “close” pair remains ≥ c > 0 apart (consistent with your definition). State explicit separation constants used in the proofs.

3) The “arbitrarily worse” phrasing vs. Cgap. Theorem 1 states L(µ′) ≤ L(µ∗) − Cgap for any fixed Cgap > 0. That’s a fixed additive gap, not “arbitrarily worse” in the sense of unbounded degradation. Please either (a) clarify the intended meaning (arbitrary fixed additive gaps), or (b) prove an unbounded multiplicative or additive deterioration if claimed.

4) Theorem 2/3 probability calculation needs formalization. The hierarchical construction suggests e−Ω(M) success probability, but the main text omits: (i) the exact combinatorial/probabilistic argument (independence or negative correlation across levels?), (ii) how the separation scales with M to ensure the EM dynamics remain trapped (invariance of clusters under E/M-steps), and (iii) for EM (not gradient ascent), why the mapping is trapping under the hard-to-control denominator E[wi(X)] and soft assignments. Please provide explicit constants, assumptions on separations between every scale, and a self-contained lemma that shows once initialized within a cluster, all iterates stay within it.

5) First-order EM as gradient ascent: scope and step-size. You state (6) “is equivalent to gradient ascent on L with step size s > 0,” citing Balakrishnan et al. That equality holds for the gradient, but global convergence/avoidance results typically require Lipschitz gradients and step-size constraints. In Theorem 3 you restrict s ∈ (0,1); justify this range formally (in terms of smoothness constants of L), or make the dependency explicit.

6) Strict saddle definition and applicability. The definition (“Hessian has at least one strictly positive eigenvalue” for a maximization problem) departs from standard “has at least one direction of strictly negative curvature” in minimization; for gradient ascent the symmetric condition is “at least one strictly negative eigenvalue for minimization” ↔ “at least one strictly positive eigenvalue for maximization,” but most avoidance results are framed for descent, not ascent. Please (i) clearly translate the Lee et al./Panageas & Piliouras conditions to gradient ascent, (ii) verify that L is C2 with Lipschitz gradient (it is analytic for spherical GMMs), and (iii) state the stable manifold theorem hypotheses you use. The text says you “avoid such a smoothness argument,” which is puzzling since L is smooth; better to leverage standard results.

7) Population-to-sample transfer is hand-wavy. The claim that results “carry over in a straightforward way” via “standard empirical process arguments” is nontrivial: persistence of critical points/local maxima under uniform convergence requires more than pointwise convergence—typically, uniform convergence of function and gradient/Hessian on compact sets and a quantitative stability argument (Morse theory/perturbation). Please provide a theorem stating conditions under which the bad local maxima and their basins persist with high probability at finite n.

8) Reproducibility and completeness. All proofs are deferred, and key technical lemmas (trapping of EM iterates, basin measure) are absent from the main text. Given the paper’s purely theoretical nature, more proof detail in the main body would greatly improve clarity.

9) Presentation and formatting issues:
- The stray “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.” appears mid-paragraph.
- Numerous reference formatting errors (escaping characters, missing years/venues/pages; e.g., “Allman et al\.,? \Q2009\E”). Please fix and standardize.
- Footnote-like sentence mid-text about infinite likelihoods is awkwardly placed.
- Inconsistent spacing/notation (e.g., [M ] with a trailing space; “µ : =”).
- Minor typos (e.g., “one needs repeat” → “needs to repeat”).

Evaluation
- Quality: Promising ideas with potentially solid constructions, but critical steps are only sketched and some arguments (Theorem 1 existence, Theorem 2/3 trapping and probability) lack necessary rigor in the main text. Several claims need tightening.
- Clarity: Moderate. High-level narrative is clear, but the technical presentation is under-specified. Numerous formatting and citation issues distract.
- Originality: High. A negative resolution of Srebro’s conjecture for M ≥ 3 and the exponentially small success probability for random-initialized EM are novel and impactful if fully justified.
- Significance: High potential impact on our understanding of EM and nonconvex likelihood landscapes; also practically relevant for initialization strategies.

Pros
- Addresses an important and long-standing question.
- Clear algorithmic message with practical implications.
- Likely reusable proof ideas about likelihood geometry.

Cons
- Key technical steps insufficiently justified in main text.
- Probability and dynamical arguments for EM need precise constants and conditions.
- Population-to-sample transfer assertion is unsupported.
- Numerous presentation/citation issues.

Recommendation: Reject (encourage resubmission)
While the contributions are significant, the current manuscript has multiple technical gaps and presentation problems. Strengthening the proofs (or at least the main lemmas) in the body, tightening definitions/assumptions, providing a rigorous probability calculation for e−Ω(M), and fixing the strict saddle discussion and all formatting/references would make this a strong paper.
