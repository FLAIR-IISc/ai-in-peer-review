- Resolves Srebro's conjecture by providing a counterexample showing not all local maxima are global for GMMs.  
- Constructs an explicit M=3, d=1 counterexample and shows arbitrarily bad local maxima exist for M ≥ 3.  
- Theorem 1: rigorous construction proving existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood.  
- Theorem 2: random initialization for EM fails with probability 1 − e^(−cM), implying exponentially many restarts needed.  
- Theorem 4: failure arises from bad local maxima (not saddle points), leveraging recent non-convex optimization results.  
- Shows first-order EM converges to suboptimal points with high probability; standard EM results are not established.  
- Strength: elegant, geometrically intuitive counterexample with novel, rigorous proof techniques.  
- Strength: clear distinction between population and finite-sample settings and careful theoretical analysis.  
- Practical implication: fundamentally alters understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes and explains EM failures.  
- Connects to prior work (Srebro 2007, Balakrishnan et al. 2015, Lee et al. 2016) and motivates method-of-moments style initializations.  
- Limitation: analysis restricted to isotropic, unit-variance, equal-weight GMMs, limiting immediate practical applicability.  
- Limitation: lacks concrete numerical examples quantifying how “arbitrarily worse” local maxima are in practice.  
- Limitation: does not establish analogous negative results for the standard (full) EM algorithm.  
- Minor concern: paper does not quantify how often these pathological cases arise in real finite-sample datasets; empirical validation is suggested.  
- Recommendation: Accept — significant theoretical contribution with important implications for algorithm design and initialization strategies.
