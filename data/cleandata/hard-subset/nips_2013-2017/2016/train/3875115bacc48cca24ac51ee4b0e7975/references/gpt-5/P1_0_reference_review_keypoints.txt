- Paper studies population log-likelihood landscape of uniformly weighted spherical GMMs and shows bad local maxima exist for M ≥ 3 in d = 1, causing EM/first-order EM with random initialization to fail with high probability.  
- Resolves Srebro (2007) conjecture in the negative for the infinite-sample limit.  
- Theorem 1: constructs geometric M = 3 example (two close centers, one far) yielding bad local maxima with arbitrarily large suboptimality gaps via scaling separations.  
- Theorems 2–3: hierarchical, recursively separated construction shows EM and first-order EM initialized from the mixture converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{-cM}, so exponentially many random restarts are needed.  
- Theorem 4: first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, so algorithmic failures are due to bad local maxima, not saddle points.  
- Connects to prior work on identifiability, density estimation/rates for mixtures, algorithmic EM analyses, spectral/moment methods, and nonconvex optimization landscape results.  
- Technical contributions are substantial and appear sound; constructions and arguments are plausible and well sketched.  
- Suggestion: state precise separation conditions and constants of Theorem 1 in the main text rather than deferring to the appendix.  
- Suggestion: formalize contraction/monotonicity arguments showing cluster confinement for population EM updates.  
- Suggestion: clarify what extra arguments beyond standard smoothness are required for the stable-manifold avoidance of saddles in first-order EM.  
- Suggestion: provide a quantitative finite-sample corollary (explicit n scaling) to demonstrate persistence of the constructions with high probability.  
- Clarity: well-written and organized; recommend moving key construction details to main text, clarifying “well-separated” definition, discussing advanced initializations, and fixing reference formatting.  
- Originality: novel hierarchical construction and combined landscape/algorithmic analysis that settles a notable open question.  
- Significance: important theoretical and practical implications—sharp limits on EM with naive random restarts even in favorable settings; directs future focus to M = 2 and better initialization strategies.  
- Pros for acceptance: resolves open conjecture, elegant constructions, clear algorithmic consequences, shows failures due to local maxima.  
- Cons/weaknesses: results are population-level only, limited to equal-weight spherical known-variance mixtures, do not analyze advanced initializations, and relegate technical details to the appendix.  
- Questions for authors: can you give explicit finite-sample n scaling? do results extend to unequal weights or mild variance heterogeneity? do k-means++ or data-dependent inits get trapped? any definitive statements for M = 2?  
- Recommendation: Accept, high confidence.
