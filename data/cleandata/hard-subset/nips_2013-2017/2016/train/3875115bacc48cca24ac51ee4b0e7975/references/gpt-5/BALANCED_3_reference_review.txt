Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the nonconvex landscape of the population log-likelihood for isotropic, equally weighted Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and the algorithmic behavior of EM and first-order EM under random initialization. It resolves a long-standing conjecture attributed to Srebro (2007), which posited that in the population limit for well-specified, uniform, spherical GMMs there are no spurious local maxima. The authors construct, for any M ≥ 3, one-dimensional, well-separated mixtures with arbitrarily bad local maxima (Theorem 1). They further show that EM and gradient EM (first-order EM) with a natural random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{−cM} (Theorems 2–3), implying that achieving good solutions by repeated random restarts requires exponentially many trials in M. Finally, they apply recent “strict-saddle avoidance” theory to prove that first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points (Theorem 4), suggesting that the main algorithmic obstacle in this setting is the presence of bad local maxima rather than saddles.

This work connects to classical identifiability and estimation theory for mixtures (Teicher 1963; Genovese & Wasserman 2000; Ghosal & van der Vaart 2001; Chen 1995; Ho & Nguyen 2015), modern finite-sample analyses and landscape results for EM (Dasgupta & Schulman 2007; Balakrishnan et al. 2015), and to recent nonconvex optimization results on saddle-avoidance (Lee et al. 2016; Panageas & Piliouras 2016; Ge et al. 2015). It complements algorithmic work on learning GMMs under separation using clustering or moments/spectral methods (Vempala & Wang 2002; Arora et al. 2005; Chaudhuri & Rao 2008; Belkin & Sinha 2010; Moitra & Valiant 2010; Hsu & Kakade 2013). The paper’s negative results contrast with benign landscape results in other nonconvex problems (e.g., Loh & Wainwright 2013; Wang et al. 2014) and with empirical heuristics for EM.

Strengths
- Resolves an open conjecture with a clean counterexample: existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima for population likelihood of well-separated, uniform, spherical GMMs with M ≥ 3.
- Algorithmic consequences are strong and precise: EM and gradient EM converge to bad critical points with exponentially high probability under widely used random initialization, via a clever hierarchical mixture construction.
- The strict-saddle analysis for first-order EM is timely and informative, sharpening the source of difficulty.
- Results are cleanly stated at the population level; the paper argues extensions to large finite samples via standard concentration arguments.
- Broad relevance: informs both theory (nonconvex landscapes) and practice (necessity of careful initialization or alternative methods for GMMs).

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Dependence on separation and geometry: “well-separated” is defined as Ω(√d), but the constructions are in d=1. Please quantify the required separations and how C_gap scales with separations. Is the bad-gap construction robust to additive perturbations? Can you provide explicit numeric parameters for the M=3 example?
- Probability bound details: Theorems 2–3 state 1 − e^{−cM}. The proof sketch relies on a multilevel clustering structure; a concrete combinatorial lower bound (with constants) for success probability would help, and a brief comparison to the polynomially small bound obtainable without recursion would clarify the gain.
- First-order EM convergence claims: Theorem 4(a) states convergence to a critical point with constant step size s ∈ (0,1). Please state the precise regularity assumptions (e.g., boundedness of iterates, L-smoothness, or coercivity) under which the map is a diffeomorphism or the stable manifold theorem applies. The text mentions avoiding global smoothness; a crisp statement of the conditions actually used is needed.
- Scope of initialization: The results address “random initialization by sampling from the data/mixture.” How do they extend to common practical schemes (k-means++ seeding, mixture splitting/merging, or spectral/moments-based initialization followed by EM)? A short discussion or corollary delineating which strategies can provably avoid the exponential failure would increase practical impact.
- Finite-sample transfer: While standard, the claim that results carry over “in a straightforward way” would benefit from a brief theorem specifying sample size requirements (as a function of d, M, separation) ensuring the empirical likelihood inherits the bad local maxima and that EM behavior concentrates on its population counterpart.
- Two-component case: The paper highlights M=2 as open and empirically benign. Any partial progress (e.g., ruling out bad local maxima under certain separations, or characterizing all critical points) would strengthen the narrative, even if deferred to an appendix.

Quality
The conceptual arguments are convincing and align with contemporary techniques in nonconvex analysis. Given that proofs are in the appendix, adding more intuition for the key lemmas (e.g., the boundary argument establishing a local basin near the misassigned configuration, and the “no-crossing” trapping argument for EM across clusters) would aid readability.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. Minor issues:
- Reference formatting needs cleanup (years, venues, author lists). Ensure consistent citation style.
- Figure 1 is referenced; ensure high-resolution plots with clear axes and labels.
- Define all constants (e.g., c, C_gap) when introduced, and be explicit about “universal.”

Originality
High. The counterexample and the probabilistic failure results under random initialization are, to my knowledge, new and resolve a visible conjecture. The strict-saddle angle for gradient EM in this context is also novel.

Significance
High. These results recalibrate expectations about EM (and gradient ascent) even in the friendliest GMM setting, and will influence algorithm design (initialization via moments/spectral, smarter seeding, or alternative objectives). The negative landscape insight is of broad interest to the NeurIPS community.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concrete, parameterized M=3 construction (locations and separations) with an explicit lower bound on the likelihood gap.
- State a finite-sample corollary with sample complexity requirements.
- Discuss implications for and limitations of k-means++ initialization and pruning-based EM variants.
- Clarify Theorem 4’s assumptions and proof dependencies, and note whether a similar saddle-avoidance guarantee is plausible for exact EM.
- Add a small synthetic experiment illustrating the constructions and the observed EM failures.

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves an open conjecture with crisp, impactful negative results.
- Strong, general algorithmic consequences for widely used methods.
- Clear connection to nonconvex optimization theory and to practice.

Arguments against acceptance
- Some technical conditions and constants are left implicit; finite-sample instantiation is not formalized.
- Limited empirical illustration; practical initialization strategies beyond pure random seeding are not analyzed.

Recommendation
Accept (strong). This is a well-executed and significant theoretical contribution that will interest a broad subset of the community and guide future work on mixture learning and nonconvex optimization.

Reviewer confidence
High.
