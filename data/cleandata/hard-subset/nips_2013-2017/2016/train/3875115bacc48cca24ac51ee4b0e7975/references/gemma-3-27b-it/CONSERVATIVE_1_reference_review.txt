This paper addresses a fundamental question regarding the EM algorithm for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs): whether the likelihood surface possesses bad local optima, even in the large-sample, well-separated case. The authors provide a negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture, demonstrating the existence of such bad local optima for M ≥ 3 components. Furthermore, they show that the standard EM and first-order EM algorithms, initialized by randomly sampling from the data, converge to these suboptimal points with high probability. The theoretical results are supported by a compelling illustrative example for M=3.

The strengths of this work lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and clear demonstration of a concerning property of EM—its susceptibility to bad local optima even under seemingly favorable conditions. The proof techniques appear novel and may prove useful in analyzing other non-convex optimization problems. The connection to prior work on the robustness of maximum likelihood estimation is well-motivated. The authors are careful to specify the conditions under which their results hold (e.g., fixed scale parameters, uniformly weighted mixtures) and to discuss the limitations of their findings.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, while the theoretical results are strong, the construction demonstrating the bad local optima relies on a specific, somewhat contrived, configuration of Gaussians. It’s unclear how prevalent such configurations are in practice, and whether the observed behavior generalizes to more realistic data distributions.  The authors acknowledge this, but a further discussion of the practical implications of this construction would be beneficial.  Relatedly, while the exponential failure rate for random initialization is concerning, the authors don’t provide a practical assessment of *how* bad the local optima are that the algorithm converges to, beyond stating they are arbitrarily worse than the global maximum.  Quantifying this performance degradation would be valuable. 

Second, the focus on fixed scale parameters is a notable limitation. Estimating both location and scale introduces additional complexity and potential for ill-conditioning, and the results may not directly translate to scenarios where the scale is also estimated. This should be more explicitly discussed. While acknowledged in the introduction, its impact on the broader applicability of the results isn’t sufficiently emphasized.

Third, while the paper convincingly argues that saddle points are less of a problem than bad local optima, the analysis of the first-order EM algorithm’s convergence properties feels somewhat separate from the core result concerning bad local maxima. It adds complexity without significantly strengthening the overall argument.  

Finally, the writing, while generally clear, is occasionally dense and could benefit from streamlining. More intuitive explanations of the mathematical arguments would be helpful for a broader audience.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous and novel theoretical results addressing a fundamental question in GMM estimation.
*   Clear demonstration of a potentially serious issue with the EM algorithm.
*   Well-motivated work with strong connections to existing literature.
*   Strong mathematical foundations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The construction demonstrating bad local optima relies on a specific configuration that may not be representative of real-world data.
*   The practical implications of the exponential failure rate are not fully quantified.
*   The restriction to fixed scale parameters limits the applicability of the results.
*   The analysis of the first-order EM algorithm feels somewhat tangential to the main contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound and theoretically interesting paper. However, its practical significance is somewhat unclear, and the limitations (fixed scale, specific Gaussian configuration) are substantial.  I lean towards **rejecting** this paper, primarily due to the concern that the demonstrated issue, while mathematically rigorous, may not be frequently encountered in practice. While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the lack of a compelling discussion of the practical implications weakens the overall impact.  I recommend the authors consider expanding their analysis to address the limitations identified above before resubmission. A more detailed exploration of the structure of these bad local optima and their prevalence in realistic datasets would significantly strengthen the paper.
