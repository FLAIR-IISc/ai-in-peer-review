

# Peer Review: "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental question about Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) - whether the population likelihood function has only global maxima (no "bad" local maxima), a conjecture by Srebro (2007) that has remained open for over a decade. The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture by constructing a counterexample with M=3 well-separated components in dimension d=1. They further demonstrate that standard EM and first-order EM algorithms with random initialization converge to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Significance:** The paper resolves a long-standing open question in the GMM literature with a rigorous construction. The counterexample for M=3 components is particularly noteworthy as it contradicts the widely held intuition that well-separated GMMs would have well-behaved likelihood surfaces. This is a major theoretical contribution that should impact the understanding of non-convex optimization in mixture models.

2. **Algorithmic Consequences:** The authors go beyond just proving existence of bad local maxima by showing that practical algorithms (EM and first-order EM) with standard random initialization are highly likely to converge to these suboptimal points. The exponential dependence on M (1 - e^{-cM}) is a strong quantitative result that has clear practical implications.

3. **Technical Sophistication:** The proof techniques for establishing the existence of bad local maxima are novel and sophisticated, involving careful geometric constructions and neighborhood arguments. The analysis of the first-order EM algorithm's behavior with respect to strict saddle points (Theorem 4) is particularly elegant and leverages recent advances in non-convex optimization theory.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dimensional Limitation:** The counterexample is constructed in dimension d=1, while most practical applications of GMMs occur in higher dimensions. The authors don't address whether their construction can be extended to higher dimensions, which weakens the practical relevance of their negative result. The paper states "well-separated" as ξ = Ω(√d), but their construction is only for d=1.

2. **Overly Strong Claim About Two-Component Case:** The authors claim that "at least three mixture components are necessary" for poor behavior of the likelihood, but they don't provide a proof for the two-component case. While they cite empirical evidence for two components, a formal proof that two-component GMMs don't have bad local maxima would strengthen their argument and better address the Srebro conjecture.

3. **Algorithmic Implications Overstated:** The paper claims that "repeated random initialization followed by local search... can fail to produce useful estimates under reasonable constraints on computational complexity." However, they don't provide concrete evidence of how often this failure occurs in practice, nor do they compare with alternative initialization schemes (e.g., k-means++ or method-of-moments based initialization) that are known to work better in practice.

4. **Missing Connection to Recent Work:** The paper cites recent work on non-convex optimization (Lee et al., 2016), but doesn't explicitly connect their results to the growing literature on "benign non-convexity" where local search algorithms can find global optima (e.g., Loh and Wainwright, 2013; Wang et al., 2014). This would have strengthened their theoretical positioning.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes. The core result (Theorem 1) is a major advancement that resolves an open question in the field. The algorithmic consequences (Theorems 2-4) are well-motivated and provide important insights into why standard EM implementations can fail.

The paper is well-written and clear, with precise mathematical statements. The construction for M=3 components is elegant and convincing. The connection between the structural properties of the likelihood surface and the algorithmic behavior is particularly strong.

The main limitation is the focus on d=1, which somewhat restricts the practical impact of the results. However, the authors explicitly state that their results generalize to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments, and the core theoretical insight about the existence of bad local maxima should hold in higher dimensions.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental question in the GMM literature. The results are rigorous, well-constructed, and have clear implications for both theory and practice. While the d=1 limitation is noted, the core insight about the existence of bad local maxima for M≥3 is compelling and likely to have lasting impact. The paper demonstrates deep understanding of the problem and provides a convincing resolution to the Srebro conjecture.

The authors have made a significant contribution to the field, and this paper would be an excellent addition to NIPS. The results should prompt researchers to reconsider assumptions about the behavior of EM and other local search algorithms for GMMs, and to develop more sophisticated initialization schemes.

**Key Points for Authors:**
1. Consider explicitly addressing whether the counterexample extends to higher dimensions.
2. Provide a more detailed discussion of the two-component case to strengthen the "at least three components" claim.
3. Add a brief comparison to recent work on benign non-convexity to better position their results in the broader optimization literature.
