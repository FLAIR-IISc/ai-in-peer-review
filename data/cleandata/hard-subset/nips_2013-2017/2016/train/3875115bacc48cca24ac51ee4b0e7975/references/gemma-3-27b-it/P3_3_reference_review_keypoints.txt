- Proves existence of bad local maxima in GMM likelihood, resolving Srebro (2007) conjecture.  
- Focuses on isotropic Gaussians with fixed variances; setting is restrictive but yields useful insights.  
- Strong, rigorous theoretical proofs with clear geometric intuition (notably the two-component example).  
- Shows EM and first-order EM often converge to suboptimal points under random initialization.  
- Proves first-order EM avoids strict saddle points, implying failures are primarily due to bad local optima.  
- Well-written and logically organized presentation.  
- Positions work appropriately within identifiability, convergence-rate, and non-convex optimization literature.  
- Main weakness: results are limited to isotropic, fixed-variance GMMs; generalization is unclear.  
- Initialization analysis is specific (uniform sampling from data); other initialization strategies are not analyzed.  
- Some proofs (e.g., Section A.4) are complex and could use simplification or clearer exposition.  
- Limited empirical validation; small synthetic experiments would strengthen practical relevance.  
- Request for elaboration on generalizing Theorem 1 to higher dimensions and more components.  
- Question about whether the exponential dependence on M in Theorems 2 & 3 can be improved or is inherent.  
- Ask for characterization of the types of critical points reached by first-order EM beyond strict saddle avoidance.  
- Suggest discussing the two-component case, and citing recent work on sharpness of local minima for broader context.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions — discuss relaxing assumptions, acknowledge initialization specificity, add small empirical validation, and clarify/simplify proofs.
