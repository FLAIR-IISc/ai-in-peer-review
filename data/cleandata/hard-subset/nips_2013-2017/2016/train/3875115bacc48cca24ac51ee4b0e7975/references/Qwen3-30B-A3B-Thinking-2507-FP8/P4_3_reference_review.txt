

This paper addresses a long-standing open question regarding the likelihood landscape of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically challenging Srebro's conjecture (2007) that all local maxima of the population likelihood are global maxima. The authors prove that this conjecture is false for mixtures with M ≥ 3 components by constructing explicit examples where the population log-likelihood contains arbitrarily bad local maxima (with log-likelihood arbitrarily worse than the global maximum). They further demonstrate that the EM algorithm and its first-order variant, when initialized via random sampling from the data, converge to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability (in M). The paper also shows that first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that bad local maxima—not saddle points—are the primary obstacle to successful optimization.

The paper builds upon foundational work in GMM theory (e.g., Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009; Chen, 1995) and recent advances in understanding EM convergence (Balakrishnan et al., 2015; Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007). It connects to broader literature on non-convex optimization (e.g., Loh and Wainwright, 2013; Wang et al., 2014) by demonstrating that even in well-behaved settings (uniformly weighted, isotropic, well-separated Gaussians), local search algorithms can fail catastrophically due to bad local maxima.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving an open question in the GMM literature with a concrete counterexample.
2. The proofs are rigorous and well-structured, with detailed technical arguments provided in the appendix.
3. The algorithmic results are highly relevant to practitioners, as they provide a precise characterization of EM's failure modes under standard random initialization.
4. The distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points (via Theorem 4) is insightful and addresses a key question in non-convex optimization.
5. The paper carefully contextualizes its results within the broader literature, citing relevant work from NIPS and other venues.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper does not fully resolve the two-component case (M=2), leaving open whether Srebro's conjecture holds for two Gaussians. The authors acknowledge this as an open question but provide no evidence for or against it.
2. The construction of bad local maxima is specific to one-dimensional settings (d=1), though the authors note the results extend to higher dimensions via standard empirical process arguments. A brief discussion of the dimensional dependence would strengthen the paper.
3. The paper does not address whether their counterexamples are robust to slight perturbations of the model parameters or data distribution, which could be important for practical applications.
4. While the theoretical results are strong, the paper lacks empirical validation of the counterexamples in finite-sample settings, which would help bridge the gap between theory and practice.

**Novelty:** The paper's central contribution—proving the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 components—represents a substantial advance over previous work. While the existence of multiple local maxima for finite samples was known (Améndola et al., 2015), this paper establishes the first rigorous result for the population likelihood in the well-separated case. The algorithmic consequences (exponentially low success probability with random initialization) are also novel and significant.

**Significance:** The findings have profound implications for the use of EM and related algorithms in GMM estimation. The paper demonstrates that even in idealized settings (uniform weights, spherical Gaussians, well-separated components), standard random initialization schemes can fail catastrophically with high probability. This explains why practitioners often require specialized initialization methods (e.g., method of moments) and provides theoretical justification for these approaches. The results also advance the understanding of non-convex optimization landscapes in statistical models.

**Overall Assessment:** This paper makes a strong, technically sound contribution to the theory of GMMs and non-convex optimization. The results are novel, significant, and well-supported by rigorous proofs. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of local search methods for GMMs under standard initialization schemes. While the one-dimensional construction and the unresolved two-component case are minor limitations, they do not detract from the paper's overall impact. The work is clearly relevant to NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental question in statistical learning theory with direct implications for widely used algorithms. The paper is technically excellent, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
