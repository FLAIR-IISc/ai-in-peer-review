**Summary**

This paper investigates the optimization landscape of the population log-likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The primary focus is on a conjecture by Srebro (2007) which posited that for uniformly weighted, isotropic, and well-separated GMMs, the population likelihood function has no "bad" local maxima—that is, all local maxima are global maxima.

The authors make three main contributions:
1.  **Structural Result:** They provide a negative answer to Srebro's conjecture. By constructing a simple GMM with M ≥ 3 components, they prove the existence of local maxima whose log-likelihood values can be arbitrarily worse than that of the global maximum.
2.  **Algorithmic Consequence for EM:** They show that the existence of these bad local maxima has severe algorithmic consequences. For the specific GMMs they construct, the standard EM algorithm (and a first-order variant), when initialized randomly by drawing starting centers from the true data distribution, will converge to a suboptimal critical point with a probability that approaches 1 exponentially fast in the number of components, M.
3.  **Nature of Failure Points:** Leveraging recent results in non-convex optimization, they show that for the first-order EM algorithm (gradient ascent), the suboptimal points to which the algorithm converges with random initialization are indeed local maxima, not strict saddle points.

In essence, the paper demonstrates that even for idealized, well-separated GMMs, the likelihood surface can be treacherous, and naive but common algorithmic approaches like EM with random starts are provably likely to fail.

**Review**

This is an excellent theoretical paper that resolves a long-standing open question and provides profound insights into the challenges of GMM estimation. It is technically strong, clearly written, and highly significant.

**Quality: (Excellent)**
The paper is of very high technical quality. The claims are supported by rigorous theoretical analysis, and the proof sketches provided in the main body are both intuitive and compelling.

*   **Strengths:** The construction used to disprove Srebro's conjecture (Theorem 1) is elegant and insightful. The idea of clustering two true means close together and showing that a single estimated mean can "capture" both of them in a locally optimal way is simple to grasp yet powerful. The subsequent construction for Theorem 2, which uses a recursive structure to establish the exponential failure probability, is a very clever way to amplify the core negative result into a strong algorithmic statement. The final connection to the literature on saddle points (Theorem 4) shows a command of the broader context of modern non-convex optimization and strengthens the paper's conclusion that bad local maxima are the primary obstacle, not saddle points. The work is presented as a complete and self-contained theoretical contribution.

*   **Weaknesses:** I could not identify any significant technical weaknesses in the paper. The arguments appear sound.

**Clarity: (Excellent)**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do a fantastic job of motivating the problem, situating it within the existing literature, and clearly stating their contributions.

*   **Strengths:** The introduction provides a superb overview of GMM estimation, covering identifiability, rates of convergence, and algorithmic approaches. It clearly articulates Srebro's conjecture and its importance before methodically outlining how the paper will disprove it and explore the consequences. The "geometrical description of our construction for M=3" is a model of clarity, allowing the reader to build strong intuition before diving into the formal results. The distinction between the structural results (Theorem 1) and the algorithmic consequences (Theorems 2-4) provides a logical and easy-to-follow structure.

*   **Weaknesses:** None. The paper is a pleasure to read.

**Originality: (Excellent)**
The paper is highly original and makes a fundamental contribution to the theory of mixture models.

*   **Strengths:** The primary contribution—a definitive negative resolution to a well-known, decade-old conjecture—is by definition highly original. While there is a rich literature on the analysis of the EM algorithm (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. 2015), much of that work focuses on characterizing the local basin of attraction around the *global* optimum. This paper takes a different and arguably more challenging approach by characterizing the global properties of the likelihood surface and proving the existence and danger of *spurious* optima. This shifts the focus from "when does EM work if started well?" to "why can EM with random starts fail so badly?". The paper also clearly distinguishes its contributions from prior work, providing adequate citations.

**Significance: (Excellent)**
The results presented in this paper are of great importance to both the machine learning theory community and practitioners who use GMMs.

*   **Strengths:**
    1.  **Theoretical Impact:** Resolving an open problem is a significant achievement in itself. This result fundamentally changes our understanding of the GMM likelihood landscape. The prior belief (or hope) was that for well-behaved GMMs, non-convexity might not be a practical issue in the large-sample limit. This paper shows that this is false; the non-convexity is pernicious and creates traps even in simple settings.
    2.  **Practical Impact:** The paper provides strong theoretical justification for the necessity of sophisticated initialization techniques for EM, such as those based on the method of moments (e.g., Hsu and Kakade, 2013) or spectral methods. It sounds a clear warning against relying on the common heuristic of "run EM from many random starts and pick the best result," showing that for large M, an exponential number of starts may be required, rendering the approach computationally infeasible. This is a crucial insight for anyone using mixture models in practice.
    3.  **Broader Impact:** The work connects beautifully to the burgeoning field of non-convex optimization in machine learning. By showing that local maxima, and not saddle points, are the cause of failure, it provides a canonical example of a problem where "escaping saddles is not enough." This will likely make it a key reference paper in future discussions of optimization landscapes.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Resolves a significant and long-standing open problem in the theory of mixture models.
*   Provides deep and surprising insights into the failure modes of the EM algorithm.
*   The results have strong practical implications, arguing for the necessity of careful initialization.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and technically sound.
*   It connects the specific problem of GMMs to the broader, very active field of non-convex optimization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   None. This is a first-rate theory paper that is a perfect fit for NIPS.

**Recommendation:**
This paper is a clear and enthusiastic accept, and I would argue strongly for its inclusion as a spotlight or oral presentation. It represents a major advance in our understanding of a fundamental machine learning model.
