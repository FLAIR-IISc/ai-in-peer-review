

# Peer Review: "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental question about Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) that has been open since Srebro's 2007 conjecture: whether the population likelihood function for GMMs has "bad local maxima" (local maxima with significantly lower likelihood than the global maximum). The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture, demonstrating that for M ≥ 3 well-separated components, the population log-likelihood function can have arbitrarily bad local maxima. They further show that the EM algorithm and its first-order variant with random initialization converge to these bad critical points with high probability (exponentially small success probability in M). The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the landscape of GMM likelihood functions and the limitations of common optimization approaches.

The work builds on prior theoretical analyses of GMMs (Chen, 1995; Ho and Nguyen, 2015; Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and connects to recent advances in non-convex optimization (Ge et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016). The authors' results provide a theoretical foundation for the empirical observation that careful initialization is crucial for GMM estimation.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Quality**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and technically sound. The counterexample construction for M=3 is elegant and well-motivated. The proofs of Theorems 1-4 are rigorous and thorough, with detailed constructions and arguments that hold up to scrutiny.

2. **Originality**: The paper resolves a specific, long-standing open question in the GMM literature. While there has been work on GMM identifiability (Allman et al., 2009) and convergence of EM (Balakrishnan et al., 2015), this is the first to demonstrate the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood. The construction of the counterexample for M ≥ 3 is novel and insightful.

3. **Significance**: The results have profound implications for both theory and practice. The finding that random initialization fails with exponentially high probability (in M) explains why practitioners often need sophisticated initialization schemes. The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for the empirical success of method-of-moments approaches (Hsu and Kakade, 2013; Moitra and Valiant, 2010) for GMM estimation.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The authors provide excellent intuition for their constructions, and the flow from structural results to algorithmic consequences is logical and compelling. The proofs are well-structured and accessible to experts in the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of the counterexample**: While the paper focuses on uniformly weighted, spherical, unit-variance GMMs, it would be valuable to briefly discuss whether the results extend to more general settings (e.g., non-uniform weights, non-spherical covariances). This limitation is acknowledged by the authors, but a short discussion would strengthen the paper.

2. **Practical implications**: While the paper establishes theoretical results for the population likelihood, it would be helpful to provide some intuition about how large the "arbitrarily bad" gap might be in practical settings with finite samples. The authors note that their results carry over to finite samples via empirical process arguments, but a brief discussion of the sample size requirements would be useful.

3. **Comparison to related work**: The paper references Améndola et al. (2015) who showed that for finite samples, configurations can be constructed with unbounded local maxima. The authors could more explicitly contrast their infinite-sample result with this finite-sample phenomenon.

## Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes. The results are surprising, well-supported, and have clear implications for both theory and practice. The paper addresses a specific, important question in a novel way, providing a definitive answer to Srebro's conjecture.

The quality of the paper is outstanding - the technical content is rigorous, the writing is clear, and the arguments are compelling. The significance of the results is substantial, as they provide theoretical justification for why careful initialization is crucial in GMM estimation, explaining why simple random initialization schemes often fail in practice.

The paper is highly original, resolving a specific open problem that has been mentioned in the literature since 2007. The results are clearly distinct from previous NIPS work on GMMs, which has largely focused on identifiability, convergence rates, or algorithmic guarantees under strong assumptions.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It is a high-quality, theoretically rigorous contribution that advances our understanding of a fundamental problem in statistical modeling. The results have important implications for practitioners and researchers working with GMMs, and the paper is well-written and well-structured.

The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field, resolving a long-standing open question and providing important insights into the behavior of local search algorithms for GMMs. The results are likely to influence future work on GMM estimation and non-convex optimization more broadly.

## Specific Suggestions

1. Consider adding a brief discussion about how the "arbitrarily bad" gap might manifest in practical settings with finite samples.

2. In the introduction, more explicitly contrast the infinite-sample result with the finite-sample results of Améndola et al. (2015).

3. The paper could briefly mention whether the results extend to non-spherical Gaussians (though this might be beyond the scope of the paper).

This paper represents a major theoretical advance in understanding GMM likelihood functions and the limitations of common optimization approaches. It is deserving of publication at NIPS.
