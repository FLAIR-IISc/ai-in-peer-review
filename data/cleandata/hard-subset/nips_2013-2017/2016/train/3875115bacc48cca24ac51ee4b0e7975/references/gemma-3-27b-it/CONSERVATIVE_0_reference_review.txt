This paper investigates the local optima of the likelihood function for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), a fundamental problem in statistical modeling. The authors demonstrate the existence of bad local optima, challenging a conjecture by Srebro [2007], and show that the EM algorithm, with a reasonable random initialization scheme, converges to these suboptimal points with high probability. This is a significant result with implications for the practical use of EM and related algorithms.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon a long line of work concerning the theoretical limitations of GMM estimation. The authors clearly articulate the problem and its relevance to both theoretical understanding and practical algorithm design. The technical approach, while requiring substantial mathematical machinery, appears sound. The proofs seem rigorous, and the theorems are stated precisely. The connection to prior work on non-convex optimization, particularly the discussion of saddle points and the use of results from Lee et al. [2016] and Panageas & Piliouras [2016], is insightful. 

However, I have several significant concerns. First, while the theoretical result regarding the existence of bad local optima is mathematically elegant, its practical relevance is not fully established. The construction relies on a specific configuration of well-separated Gaussians. It's unclear how frequently such configurations arise in real-world datasets. A discussion of the robustness of these results to deviations from the idealized setting (e.g., overlapping Gaussians, non-spherical covariances) would strengthen the paper.

Second, the analysis of the EM algorithm’s convergence focuses heavily on a specific random initialization scheme – uniformly sampling initial centers from the data. While this is a common practice, it's not the only possible initialization strategy. It's possible that other initialization methods (e.g., k-means++) might mitigate the issue of convergence to bad local optima. The authors should address this possibility and discuss the limitations of their analysis in this regard.

Third, the paper focuses on a fixed covariance structure (isotropic Gaussians). While simplifying the analysis, this significantly restricts the scope of the results. Real-world applications often involve full covariance matrices, where the problem of local optima is likely to be even more severe. Extending the results to this more general setting would be a valuable contribution, although admittedly challenging. 

Finally, the presentation, while generally clear, occasionally suffers from dense mathematical notation. Providing more intuition and illustrative examples could enhance readability, particularly for readers less familiar with the technical details.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental theoretical question in GMM estimation.
*   The results are mathematically rigorous and provide a negative answer to a long-standing conjecture.
*   The analysis of the EM algorithm's convergence is insightful and highlights a potential issue with random initialization.
*   The paper is well-connected to related work in optimization and statistical learning.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The practical relevance of the theoretical results is unclear, given the specific assumptions made in the construction.
*   The analysis is limited to a specific initialization scheme and a simplified covariance structure.
*   The presentation could be improved with more intuition and illustrative examples.
*   While theoretically sound, the paper lacks a strong empirical component to support its claims.

**Overall assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of the challenges in GMM estimation. However, the limitations in terms of practical relevance, scope, and presentation prevent me from giving it a strong recommendation for acceptance. I believe the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of its limitations and a more compelling argument for its impact on real-world applications. I recommend **rejection** with the encouragement to address the concerns outlined above. The work is valuable, but needs further refinement before being suitable for NIPS. A minor revision will not be sufficient.
