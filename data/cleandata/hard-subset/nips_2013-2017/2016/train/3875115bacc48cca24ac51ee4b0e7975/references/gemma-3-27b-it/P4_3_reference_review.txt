This paper investigates the local maxima of the likelihood function for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), focusing on the impact of these local maxima on the performance of estimation algorithms like EM. The authors rigorously demonstrate, through a carefully constructed example, that the conjecture of Srebro [2007] – that any local maximum of the likelihood function is a global maximum in the limit of infinite samples – is false. They then prove that the EM and first-order EM algorithms, with random initialization, converge to suboptimal critical points with high probability. Finally, they show that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting the algorithmic failures are primarily due to bad local optima.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question about the theoretical guarantees of widely used algorithms for GMM estimation. The problem is clearly stated, and the authors position their work well within the existing literature on mixture models, identifiability, convergence rates, and algorithmic guarantees. The paper builds upon prior work by Teicher, Allman et al., Chen, Dasgupta & Schulman, Balakrishnan et al., Srebro, and others, and clearly articulates how it differs from and extends those contributions. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core result – demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima in the GMM likelihood – is a significant contribution and disproves a long-standing conjecture.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The proofs appear mathematically sound and employ sophisticated techniques. The arguments presented for the algorithmic consequences are well-developed and supported by the theoretical results.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The authors clearly define the problem, assumptions (uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians), and goals of the paper.
*   **Algorithmic Relevance:** The results have direct implications for the practical use of algorithms like EM, highlighting the importance of careful initialization.
*   **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper provides a thorough review of relevant literature, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The results are restricted to uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussian mixtures. While this simplifies the analysis, it’s unclear how the findings generalize to more complex scenarios (e.g., non-isotropic covariances, unequal weights). The authors acknowledge this limitation.
*   **Complexity of Proofs:** The proofs, while rigorous, are dense and may be challenging for readers not intimately familiar with the technical details. More intuitive explanations or intermediate steps could improve accessibility.
*   **Practicality of Initialization:** While the analysis focuses on random initialization from the dataset, it doesn’t fully explore other initialization strategies. The authors briefly mention moment-based methods, but a more detailed discussion of their potential benefits would be valuable.
* **Limited Discussion of High Dimensions**: While the authors reference work on high-dimensional regression, a dedicated discussion of how their findings translate to high-dimensional Gaussian mixtures is missing. The separation requirements for ensuring good optimization behavior are likely to become more stringent in higher dimensions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental theoretical question with significant implications for GMM estimation.
*   The disproof of Srebro’s conjecture is a substantial contribution.
*   The rigorous analysis and clear presentation of results are commendable.
*   The work is likely to stimulate further research on initialization strategies and alternative algorithms for GMM estimation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The scope is limited to a specific class of GMMs, potentially diminishing the broader impact.
*   The technical complexity of the proofs may make the paper inaccessible to a wider audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The results are rigorously established and have clear implications for the practical use of EM and related algorithms. While the limited scope and technical complexity are minor drawbacks, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work.  The paper meets the criteria for NIPS acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
