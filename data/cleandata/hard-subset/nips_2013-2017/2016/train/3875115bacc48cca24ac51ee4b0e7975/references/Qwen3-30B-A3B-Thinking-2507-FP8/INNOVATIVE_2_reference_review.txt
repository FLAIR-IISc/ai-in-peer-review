

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental question in Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) by resolving Srebro's conjecture (2007), which posited that for uniformly weighted mixtures of isotropic Gaussians, all local maxima of the population likelihood are global maxima. The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture by constructing a mixture of three well-separated Gaussians in one dimension where the population log-likelihood contains local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global maximum.

The paper's main contributions are threefold. First, it establishes the existence of "bad" local maxima for GMMs with M ≥ 3 components, directly contradicting Srebro's conjecture. Second, it demonstrates that the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) with random initialization converges to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability as a function of M. Third, it proves that the first-order EM algorithm avoids strict saddle points, suggesting that the algorithmic failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

This work is exceptionally novel and bold in its approach. It directly challenges a widely accepted assumption in the field that has persisted since 2007, providing a clear counterexample that resolves a long-standing open question. The construction of the bad local maxima and the recursive design of the Gaussian mixture to achieve exponential failure probability are particularly clever and demonstrate deep theoretical insight.

The significance of this work is substantial. It fundamentally changes our understanding of the likelihood landscape for GMMs, showing that even for well-separated mixtures (which are often considered "easy" cases), local search algorithms can fail catastrophically with random initialization. This has important practical implications for the use of EM and similar algorithms in practice, as it suggests that repeated random initialization may be necessary to find good solutions, which could be computationally prohibitive for large M.

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs that introduce new techniques for analyzing population likelihood surfaces. The theoretical results are well-supported, and the authors carefully distinguish between the population and finite-sample settings. The construction for M=3 provides geometric intuition that helps readers understand the nature of the bad local maxima.

I particularly appreciate how the authors connect their theoretical results to practical algorithmic consequences. The demonstration that the failure probability is exponentially small in M (requiring e^Ω(M) repetitions of random initialization) is a powerful insight that challenges common practice in the field. The proof technique for showing that the first-order EM avoids strict saddle points is elegant and leverages recent advances in optimization theory.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear statements of results and helpful geometric intuition. The connection to related work (such as the work of Balakrishnan et al. on local convergence of EM) is appropriate and demonstrates the authors' thorough understanding of the field.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold theoretical contributions over methodological perfection, I find this paper exceptionally strong. It doesn't merely provide incremental improvements to existing algorithms or theoretical results—it fundamentally challenges a core assumption in the field and provides a resolution to a long-standing open question. The authors' willingness to tackle this difficult theoretical question directly and provide a negative answer is exactly the kind of bold approach I value.

While the focus on the idealized setting (uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians) might raise questions about applicability to more general settings, the authors appropriately note that their results carry over to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments. The paper also acknowledges the open question of whether three components are necessary for this behavior, suggesting fruitful directions for future work.

In conclusion, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that resolves a long-standing open problem in the field. The results have both theoretical importance and practical implications for the use of EM algorithms in Gaussian mixture modeling. The work is novel, bold, and well-executed, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

Strengths:
- Resolves a long-standing open question (Srebro's conjecture)
- Provides a negative answer to a widely held assumption
- Demonstrates practical implications for EM algorithm usage
- Introduces novel proof techniques for analyzing likelihood landscapes
- Shows exponential failure probability for random initialization

Weaknesses:
- Focus on idealized setting (though this is appropriate for the theoretical question)
- Some readers might find the recursive construction challenging to follow without careful reading

Overall, this paper represents a significant theoretical advance that will influence both the theoretical and practical understanding of Gaussian mixture models. The novelty and boldness of the approach, combined with the rigorous theoretical foundation, make it an excellent candidate for publication at NIPS.
