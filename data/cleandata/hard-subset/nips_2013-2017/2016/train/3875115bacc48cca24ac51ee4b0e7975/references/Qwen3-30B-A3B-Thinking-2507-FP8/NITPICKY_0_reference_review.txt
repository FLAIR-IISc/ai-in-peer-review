

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper investigates the structure of likelihood surfaces in Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), specifically addressing Srebro's conjecture that all local maxima are global in the population limit. The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture by constructing examples with arbitrarily bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 components, and demonstrate that random initialization of EM and first-order EM algorithms with high probability converges to these suboptimal points. The results are significant for understanding the limitations of local search methods in GMM estimation.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by resolving an open problem posed by Srebro [2007].
2. The construction of bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 components is novel and well-executed.
3. The analysis of algorithmic consequences, particularly the exponential failure probability of random initialization, is rigorous and insightful.
4. The distinction between bad local maxima and strict saddle points is well-motivated and supported by Theorem 4.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Formatting inconsistencies in references:** The references exhibit inconsistent citation styles, with some using "et al." and others not, and some containing malformed entries like "Allman et al\.,? \Q2009\E" instead of standard "[2009]" or "2009." This detracts from the paper's professionalism.

2. **Technical imprecision in definitions:** The paper defines "well-separated" as "ξ = Ω(√d)" (Equation 3), but ξ is a constant parameter, not a function of d. This should be corrected to "ξ = Ω(√d)" to properly indicate that the separation scales with dimension. The current phrasing is confusing and mathematically imprecise.

3. **Inconsistent likelihood inequality:** In Theorem 1, the inequality L(µ′) ≤ L(µ∗) - Cgap is stated, but this implies L(µ′) < L(µ∗), which contradicts the fact that L(µ∗) is the global maximum (by KL divergence properties). The correct inequality should be L(µ′) ≤ L(µ∗) - Cgap, but the phrasing is confusing. The paper should clarify that L(µ∗) is the population likelihood at the true parameters, which is the global maximum.

4. **Ambiguous notation in proof sketch:** In the discussion of Theorem 1, the authors describe "µ1 = (µ∗1 + µ∗2)/2; the points µ2 and µ3 are both placed at the true center µ∗3." This is confusing because it's unclear whether these are the estimated parameters or the true parameters. The notation needs clarification to avoid reader confusion.

5. **Misstated probability in Theorem 2:** Theorem 2 states "P [ ∀t ≥ 0, L(µt) ≤ L(µ∗)− Cgap ] ≥ 1− e−cM." This should be L(µt) ≤ L(µ∗) - Cgap, but the phrasing implies the likelihood is bounded below, whereas it should be bounded above (since it's a suboptimal solution). This is a critical typo that could mislead readers.

6. **Missing figure reference:** The paper references "Figure 1" in the introduction but provides no figure. This is a serious omission as the visualization is claimed to support the conjecture for M=2.

7. **Inconsistent treatment of initialization:** The paper states "in the limit of infinite samples, the initialization scheme we consider is equivalent to selecting M initial centers i.i.d from the underlying mixture distribution." This is inaccurate - for the population setting, initialization is from the true distribution, but for finite samples, it's from the empirical distribution. The distinction should be clarified.

8. **Reference formatting error:** The entry for "Améndola et al. [2015]" contains a typo: "Améndola et al\.,? \Q2015\E" instead of "Améndola et al. [2015]."

**Minor issues:**
- The paper states "the mere existence of bad local maxima is not a practical concern unless it turns out that natural algorithms are frequently trapped in these bad local maxima." This is vague - "frequently" should be quantified mathematically.
- The constant c in Theorem 2 is called "universal" but isn't defined. While this is acceptable for a high-level paper, it's worth noting that the value of c could impact the practical implications.
- In Section 3.2, the phrase "this observation alone only suffices to guarantee that the success probability is polynomially small in M" is confusing - polynomially small would mean 1/poly(M), but the paper later claims it's exponentially small.

**Conclusion:**
This is an interesting paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution by resolving Srebro's conjecture. However, the technical imprecision in definitions, critical typos in key theorems, inconsistent notation, and formatting errors in references significantly undermine the paper's quality. These are not major conceptual flaws, but they are serious enough to raise concerns about the paper's rigor and presentation. For a NIPS submission, which demands exceptional precision and clarity, these issues make the paper difficult to evaluate fully.

Given the paper's significant contribution but the presence of multiple technical and formatting issues that could confuse readers and reviewers, I recommend rejection. The authors should address these concerns before resubmission.
