

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental question in Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) by resolving Srebro's conjecture (2007) regarding the absence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood. The authors construct a counterexample with M=3 well-separated spherical Gaussians showing the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima, and demonstrate that EM and first-order EM algorithms with random initialization converge to these suboptimal points with high probability. This represents a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the limitations of likelihood-based methods for GMMs.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear, important theoretical contribution by resolving an open problem in the GMM literature. The construction of counterexamples for M≥3 is well-motivated and the proofs of the main theorems are technically sound. The connection between the existence of bad local maxima and the failure of random initialization schemes is particularly insightful. The paper also makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of saddle points versus bad local maxima in non-convex optimization.

## Weaknesses
Despite the significant contribution, the paper contains multiple minor but pervasive issues that undermine its presentation and technical precision:

1. **Figure 1 is missing**: The introduction references "Figure 1" showing the population log-likelihood for a two-component model, but no figure is included in the submission. This is a critical omission that prevents readers from verifying the empirical evidence supporting the authors' claims.

2. **Notational inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout:
   - "µ" vs "µi" for mean vectors
   - "GMM(µ∗)" vs "GMM(µ∗)model"
   - "Cgap" (non-standard notation) instead of a standard constant symbol
   - Inconsistent spacing in mathematical expressions (e.g., "log ( 1 M M∑ i=1 φ(x` | µi, I) )" should be "log( (1/M) Σ_{i=1}^M φ(x'|µ_i,I) )")

3. **Reference formatting issues**: The references contain inconsistent formatting with strange punctuation like "et al\.,? \Q2009\E" and missing punctuation. For example, the reference for Allman et al. (2009) appears as "Identifiability of parameters in latent structure models with many observed variables, Elizabeth S Allman, Catherine Matias, John A Rhodes, Annals of Statistics,, Allman et al\.,? \Q2009\E, Allman et al\.,", which is not standard.

4. **Mathematical notation errors**:
   - Equation (1) has a missing closing parenthesis: "det(Σ)"
   - Equation (4b) has improper formatting: "Eµ∗ log ( 1 M M∑ i=1 φ(X | µi, I) )"
   - The probability statement in Theorem 2 uses "P [ ... ]" instead of standard "P( ... )"
   - The phrase "ξ = Ω(√d)" in the definition of well-separated mixtures should be "ξ = Ω(√d)" for clarity

5. **Minor grammatical issues**:
   - "This combinatorial structure also creates challenges for statistical and computational theory, and there are many problems associated with estimation of finite mixtures that are still open." (Should be "estimation of finite mixture models")
   - "The only remaining critical point is (0, 0), which is a saddle point." (This should be "a saddle point" without the "the")

6. **Theoretical clarification needed**: The paper states "the log-likelihood of these local maxima can be arbitrarily worse than that of the global maxima" but doesn't define what "arbitrarily worse" means in the context of the paper (e.g., the difference L(µ*) - L(µ') can be made arbitrarily large).

7. **Formatting inconsistencies**: The paper uses "isotropic" in the abstract but "spherical" throughout the rest of the paper. These terms are equivalent in this context, but the paper should be consistent with terminology.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving Srebro's conjecture, the numerous minor technical and formatting issues throughout the paper are substantial enough to warrant rejection. The paper would be much stronger with careful attention to these details. The missing Figure 1 is particularly problematic as it undermines the empirical support for the authors' claims.

The paper is technically sound in its main results, but the presentation issues suggest a lack of careful proofreading that might indicate other, more serious issues in the manuscript. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field, but the current presentation does not meet the standards expected for publication at NIPS.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper's significant contribution is overshadowed by numerous minor but pervasive technical and formatting issues that undermine its presentation. While the theoretical results are important, the paper would need substantial revisions to address the notational inconsistencies, missing figure, and reference formatting before it could be considered for publication.
