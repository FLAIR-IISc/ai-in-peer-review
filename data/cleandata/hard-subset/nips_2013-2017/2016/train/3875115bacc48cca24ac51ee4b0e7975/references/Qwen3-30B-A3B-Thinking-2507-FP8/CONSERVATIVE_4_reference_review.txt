

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses an open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding whether Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) have "bad" local maxima in the population likelihood. The authors claim to provide a negative answer to Srebro's conjecture by constructing a GMM with M≥3 well-separated components whose population log-likelihood contains local maxima that are arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. They further show that random initialization of the EM algorithm leads to convergence to these bad local maxima with high probability, requiring exponentially many restarts to find a good solution.

The paper makes two main contributions: (1) a structural result demonstrating the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima for M≥3, and (2) algorithmic results showing that random initialization of EM and first-order EM converges to these bad local maxima with probability at least 1-e^{-cM}. The authors support their claims with rigorous proofs and carefully constructed examples.

**Strengths:**
The paper is technically sound and well-organized. The main results are clearly stated and the proofs appear to be complete. The authors provide a detailed geometric intuition for their constructions, particularly for M=3, which helps clarify the counterexample to Srebro's conjecture. The paper properly situates itself within the literature, citing relevant work on GMMs, EM algorithm convergence, and non-convex optimization. The connection between the existence of bad local maxima and the failure of random initialization is well-justified.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the technical quality, I have significant concerns about the paper's significance and practical relevance. The construction of bad local maxima requires an extremely pathological configuration of the true centers: for M=3, the authors require two centers to be very close together while the third is far away. This is not a realistic scenario for most applications where mixture components are reasonably separated. In fact, the paper's own Theorem 1 specifies "well-separated" Gaussians, but fails to quantify how well-separated they must be for the bad local maxima to exist. Without this quantitative bound, it's unclear whether the pathological configuration is relevant to practical GMM estimation.

The algorithmic results (Theorems 2-4) claim that random initialization fails with high probability, but this is only shown for the specific pathological construction. The paper does not demonstrate that this failure mode occurs in any realistic data-generating process. In practice, GMMs are often initialized using k-means or other methods that are more likely to yield reasonable starting points than random sampling from the data. The paper's claim that "random initialization followed by local search can fail to produce useful estimates under reasonable constraints on computational complexity" is overstated, as practitioners rarely use the exact random initialization scheme described.

Most critically, the paper's main claim contradicts the widespread empirical success of EM for GMMs in practice. While the authors acknowledge that EM works well in practice, they dismiss this as "empirical evidence" without addressing why the pathological counterexample doesn't reflect real-world behavior. The paper fails to provide evidence that the bad local maxima they construct actually occur in typical GMM estimation problems, which weakens the practical significance of their results.

**Originality:**
The paper makes a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces, resolving Srebro's conjecture in the negative. However, the construction of bad local maxima for M≥3 is not entirely novel in the sense that Améndola et al. (2015) already showed that for finite samples, configurations can be constructed with unbounded numbers of local maxima. The paper's contribution is limited to the population setting, but the construction is still highly specialized.

**Significance:**
The significance of this work is questionable. While the theoretical result is interesting, it appears to be limited to a very narrow class of pathological GMMs that do not reflect typical data structures. The paper does not address whether the bad local maxima they construct would be encountered in practice, nor does it provide guidance for practitioners on how to avoid them in real applications. The claim that "careful initialization is required" is not new, and the paper does not provide any new practical initialization methods.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound and addresses a well-known open problem, the results are limited to a highly specialized pathological case that does not reflect realistic GMM estimation scenarios. The paper overstates the practical implications of its results, claiming that random initialization fails with high probability, when in fact this is only true for their specific construction. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the conditions under which bad local maxima actually occur in practice, and how these conditions compare to typical GMM applications.

The paper makes a technically interesting contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces, but the results are too narrow in scope and lack practical relevance to justify publication at NIPS. The paper would be more compelling if it could demonstrate that the pathological behavior they describe occurs in realistic settings, or if it provided new practical algorithms to address the issue. Without this, the paper's contribution is limited to a theoretical curiosity rather than a meaningful advance in the field.
