- Overall: Significant theoretical result showing bad local maxima in GMM likelihoods and that EM/first-order EM often converge to them; recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.

- Problem addressed: Characterizes local maxima of GMM likelihoods, refutes Srebro’s conjecture that all local maxima are global in the infinite-sample limit.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on identifiability and convergence literature and contrasts with benign optimization landscapes observed in other high-dimensional problems.

- Core contribution: Rigorously proves existence of bad local maxima even for well-separated, uniformly weighted isotropic Gaussians.

- Algorithmic consequence: Shows EM and first-order EM converge to these suboptimal points with high probability under common initializations.

- Technical quality: Proofs are sound, use insightful geometric constructions (1D M=3) and dynamical-systems tools for first-order EM.

- Presentation: Exceptionally clear, well-organized, and thoroughly situated in related work.

- Limitation 1: Results are restricted to uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussian components, raising questions about generalization.

- Limitation 2: Paper does not quantify how much worse the suboptimal solutions are in practice.

- Limitation 3: Theorem 3’s step-size requirement lacks guidance on how step-size affects convergence probability.

- Suggested improvements: Discuss impact on confidence intervals, add a figure for the M=3 bad local maximum, explicitly note computational cost of exponentially many EM restarts, clarify Theorem 4’s scope, and expand future-work directions (non-isotropic Gaussians, better initializations).

- Acceptance arguments: Novel, practically relevant, technically rigorous, well-written, and resolves an open problem.

- Rejection arguments: Restricted setting and missing quantification of practical performance loss.
