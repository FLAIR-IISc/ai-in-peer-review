- Main contribution: Constructs uniform, spherical GMMs (M ≥ 3) with well-separated centers whose population log-likelihood has spurious local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global optimum, disproving Srebro’s conjecture (Theorem 1).  
- Algorithmic consequence: Shows EM and first-order EM with practical random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{-cM}, implying exponentially many restarts are needed (Theorems 2–3).  
- Saddles vs maxima: Proves first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points w.p.1, so failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles (Theorem 4).  
- Technical soundness: Claims are precise with formal theorems and plausible constructions; multiscale clustering argument justifies the exponential failure probability.  
- Assumptions/scope: Results are for the population objective with equal weights, identity covariances, and 1D constructions; extensions to finite samples are asserted but not quantified.  
- Caveat on instances: The examples are adversarially constructed, so practical prevalence of such configurations is unclear and acknowledged by the authors.  
- Missing result: No rigorous no-strict-saddle convergence guarantee is provided for classic EM (only for first-order EM).  
- Clarity strengths: Paper is well-organized with clear model/algorithm statements and helpful intuitive sketches (e.g., M = 3 geometry, recursive construction).  
- Clarity issues: Lacks explicit constants and separation scales in the main text, finite-sample carryover is not quantified, reference formatting glitches, and a referenced figure is missing.  
- Originality: Novel negative resolution of Srebro’s conjecture for M ≥ 3 and novel demonstration of exponential failure probability under random initialization.  
- Significance: Results materially impact how EM/gradient ascent on GMMs are viewed—bad local maxima can occur even in benign, population settings—motivating better initialization and landscape study.  
- Pros: Resolves an open conjecture, ties likelihood geometry to algorithmic consequences, separates roles of maxima vs. saddles, and provides clear formulation and proof sketches.  
- Cons: Population-level focus without explicit finite-sample bounds, reliance on tailored adversarial instances, restrictions to equal weights/identity covariances/1D, and gap for classic EM.  
- Suggestions: Provide finite-sample conditions (n vs. separation, d, M), clarify separation constants and suboptimality scaling, test common initializers (k-means++, spectral) empirically, discuss extensions to unequal weights/non-identity covariances, and explore no-strict-saddle arguments for EM.  
- Overall evaluation: Strong, original, and significant theoretical work; recommendation leans toward acceptance pending improved finite-sample discussion and minor clarifications.
