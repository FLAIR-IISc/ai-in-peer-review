Summary
The paper studies the optimization landscape of the (infinite-sample) population log-likelihood for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) under a particularly favorable setting: equally weighted, isotropic (unit-variance) components. It resolves a conjecture by Srebro (2007), which informally suggested that the population likelihood has no spurious local maxima in this setting, by constructing counterexamples with M ≥ 3 components in d = 1. Specifically:

- Structural result (Theorem 1): There exist well-separated, uniformly weighted mixtures of spherical Gaussians whose population likelihood admits suboptimal local maxima. The suboptimality gap can be made arbitrarily large.
- Algorithmic consequences (Theorems 2–3): EM and first-order EM, when initialized by sampling M centers i.i.d. from the mixture (the population analog of “pick M data points uniformly at random”), converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−cM for a universal c > 0. The construction uses a recursively clustered arrangement of means to argue that a favorable random initialization has exponentially small probability.
- Saddle-point avoidance (Theorem 4): First-order EM (gradient ascent on the population likelihood) with a suitable constant step size converges to critical points but almost surely not to strict saddle points. Hence the algorithmic failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper connects to prior work in several ways. It refutes Srebro’s (2007) conjecture about the benign landscape of the population likelihood for GMMs; complements finite-sample pathologies (e.g., Améndola et al., 2015) with a population-level negative result; contrasts with positive algorithmic guarantees for moment/clustering methods under separation (Arora et al., 2005; Vempala and Wang, 2002; Hsu and Kakade, 2013; Moitra and Valiant, 2010); and relates to recent analyses of EM’s local convergence (Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and of saddle-point avoidance in gradient methods (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016; Pascanu et al., 2014).

Strengths
- Clarity of core contributions: The paper clearly states the structural counterexample to Srebro’s conjecture and the algorithmic consequences for EM/first-order EM with random initialization.
- Technical significance: Demonstrating arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood for well-separated, uniform, spherical GMMs is a strong negative result that closes a long-standing question and impacts how EM-based methods are interpreted theoretically.
- Algorithmic insight: The exponentially small success probability for random initialization provides a sharp quantitative barrier, motivating principled initialization (e.g., via moments).
- Conceptual positioning: The separation between failure due to bad local maxima (not saddle points) adds nuance to the broader narrative that saddles dominate nonconvex difficulties.
- Scope: The topic is squarely within NeurIPS areas (nonconvex optimization, probabilistic modeling, unsupervised learning).

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Assumptions and regularity for Theorem 4: The claim that first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points is said to “avoid smoothness arguments.” Standard stable manifold results typically require at least C2 smoothness and a diffeomorphic update map. Please state precise regularity assumptions on L and the EM map, and explain how these conditions are verified for GMM likelihoods (e.g., treatment of degenerate regions, step-size constraints, and global vs local properties).
- Exact notion of “well-separated”: The paper defines well-separated as pairwise squared distances ≥ ξ with ξ = Ω(√d). The construction’s intuition uses one pair much closer than the others but still satisfying the threshold. It would help to specify explicit constants and show that the bad-local-maxima construction satisfies the stated separation quantitatively.
- From population to finite sample: The conclusion references “standard empirical process arguments” to carry results to finite n. Given the practical importance, a short formal finite-sample corollary with explicit sample complexity and approximation error would strengthen the paper.
- Initialization model: The negative results are for i.i.d. sampling from the mixture (or from data uniformly). Many practitioners use more structured initializations (e.g., K-means++, spectral/moment-based seeds). While it is not necessary to cover all schemes, a brief discussion of whether the same hard instances defeat K-means++-style seeding, or why they might not, would help contextualize the practical impact.
- Scope limitation to M ≥ 3: The paper conjectures tractability for M = 2 and notes empirical support. Can any partial progress be made (e.g., structural lemmas, verified properties of the landscape) that would further differentiate the two-component case?
- Presentation details: The reference list appears to have formatting placeholders and inconsistencies. Cleaning this up and ensuring standard citation formats would improve clarity. Including figures (e.g., the 1D landscape for M = 2) and possibly a schematic for the recursive construction would help readers.

Quality
- Technical soundness appears strong given the outlined constructions and use of dynamical systems tools; however, the precise conditions for Theorem 4 and the finite-sample extension deserve more explicit statements.
- Claims are aligned with the arguments sketched; full proofs in the appendix should make constants and regularity conditions explicit.

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally well-organized and readable. Some sections would benefit from tighter definitions (well-separated, neighborhoods used in the local-maximum construction) and explicit constants. The algorithmic discussion is clear and well-motivated.

Originality
- The work provides a clear and novel resolution to an open conjecture at the population level, and new quantitative failure probabilities for EM/first-order EM with random initialization under strong separation.

Significance
- The results materially alter the theoretical understanding of EM and likelihood landscapes for GMMs, even in favorable regimes, and they motivate careful initialization strategies. This is likely to influence both theory and practice.

Pros for acceptance
- Resolves a notable open question with crisp counterexamples.
- Strong and relevant negative results for widely used algorithms.
- Clear implications for algorithm design (initialization).
- Adds conceptual clarity about local maxima vs saddle points.

Cons for acceptance
- Dependence on a specific (albeit common) random initialization; limited discussion of other practical initializations.
- Some technical conditions (e.g., for saddle-point avoidance) need clearer, self-contained statements.
- No explicit finite-sample theorem despite claiming straightforward extension.

Suggestions for improvement
- State Theorem 4 with explicit assumptions (smoothness class, step-size range, properties of the update map).
- Provide a finite-sample corollary with sample complexity and approximation error bounds.
- Briefly discuss K-means++/spectral/moment-based seeding vis-à-vis the hard instances.
- Include figures illustrating the landscape and the hierarchical construction.
- Fix citation formatting and ensure related work coverage is complete and consistent.

Overall, this is a strong, impactful theoretical paper with clear contributions and relevance to the NeurIPS community.
