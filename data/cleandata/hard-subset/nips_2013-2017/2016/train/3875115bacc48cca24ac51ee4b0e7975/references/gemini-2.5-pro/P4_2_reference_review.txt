This paper investigates the structure of the population log-likelihood surface for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and the resulting consequences for local search algorithms like Expectation-Maximization (EM). The work is motivated by a conjecture from Srebro (2007) which posited that for a uniformly weighted mixture of isotropic Gaussians, the population likelihood function (i.e., in the infinite sample limit) has no spurious local maxima. If true, this conjecture would provide strong justification for using EM in the large-sample regime, as any stationary point found would be globally optimal. This work provides a definitive negative answer to this conjecture.

The paper's main contributions are fourfold. First, it constructs a counterexample to Srebro's conjecture, showing that for any number of components M ≥ 3, there exist well-separated, unit-variance GMMs whose population log-likelihood possesses local maxima that can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. Second, it analyzes the algorithmic consequences of this structural property, demonstrating that the standard EM algorithm, initialized by drawing M points from the true mixture distribution, will converge to a suboptimal critical point with a probability of at least 1 - exp(-cM). This implies that an exponential number of random restarts are required to find the global optimum. Third, this high-probability failure result is extended to the first-order EM algorithm (equivalent to gradient ascent on the likelihood). Finally, leveraging recent results from the optimization literature (Lee et al., 2016), the paper shows that the first-order EM algorithm with random initialization does not converge to strict saddle points with probability one. This final result strongly suggests that the algorithmic failure is due to convergence to poor local maxima rather than getting trapped at saddle points.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are significant and appear to be well-supported by the proof sketches provided. The central construction of a GMM with bad local optima is insightful, based on a hierarchical clustering of the true mean parameters. The subsequent analysis that shows EM with random initialization gets trapped with exponentially high probability is a strong result that follows from this construction. The application of recent theoretical results on gradient descent dynamics to rule out convergence to strict saddle points is a solid and relevant addition, strengthening the paper's conclusions about the nature of the optimization landscape. The authors are careful to frame their results in the population setting and discuss the idealized assumptions (uniform weights, known identity covariance), which is appropriate for a theoretical contribution of this nature.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the problem, relevant prior work, and the paper's contributions. The motivation, starting with the Srebro (2007) conjecture and the visual example for M=2 components, is very effective at setting the stage. The main theorems are stated precisely, and each is followed by an intuitive explanation of the result and the core proof ideas. This clear exposition makes a complex theoretical argument accessible to a broad audience within the NIPS community. The separation of the paper into structural properties and algorithmic consequences is logical and helps the reader follow the narrative.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original and represents a significant theoretical advance. It resolves a well-known and important open question that has persisted for nearly a decade. While the general problem of local optima in non-convex optimization is well-studied, this work provides a definitive, and somewhat surprising, negative result for a specific, fundamental, and widely used statistical model (GMMs) in a setting that was conjectured to be well-behaved. The subsequent results on the high-probability failure of EM with random initialization are also novel and go beyond a mere existence proof of bad local optima to demonstrate their practical relevance for a common algorithmic paradigm. The connection made to the modern literature on saddle points further demonstrates a novel synthesis of ideas.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are of high significance to both the machine learning theory and practitioner communities.
1.  **Theoretical Impact:** By refuting Srebro's conjecture, the paper fundamentally alters our understanding of the GMM likelihood landscape. It tempers the optimistic view, emerging from work on other high-dimensional models, that non-convexity in statistical estimation might often be benign. It formally establishes that even for well-separated mixtures, the likelihood surface can be treacherous.
2.  **Practical Impact:** The algorithmic results provide a strong cautionary tale. They give a formal theoretical basis for the empirical wisdom that initialization is critical for the EM algorithm. The finding that a natural random initialization scheme fails with exponentially high probability in M strongly motivates the use of more sophisticated initialization procedures, such as those based on the method of moments or spectral techniques (e.g., Hsu & Kakade, 2013), which come with theoretical guarantees. The paper provides a compelling argument that simply relying on many random restarts is not a computationally feasible strategy for finding the global MLE in GMMs as the number of components grows.

A potential minor weakness is that the GMM instances constructed to demonstrate the negative results are specific and could be viewed as adversarial. However, for an existence proof and a worst-case analysis, this is entirely appropriate and does not diminish the validity or importance of the conclusions. The paper's goal is to show what *can* go wrong, and it does so convincingly.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   **Pro:** The paper resolves a significant, long-standing open problem in the theory of mixture models.
*   **Pro:** The results provide a fundamental characterization of the GMM likelihood landscape, showing it can contain arbitrarily bad local maxima even in simple, well-separated cases.
*   **Pro:** It presents strong, novel algorithmic results, proving that EM and gradient ascent with a standard random initialization fail with high probability, thus providing a firm theoretical grounding for the importance of advanced initialization schemes.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and places its contributions effectively within the context of prior work.
*   **Pro:** The findings are of broad interest and significance to researchers working on optimization, statistical theory, and latent variable models.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

*   None. The paper is a strong contribution that meets and exceeds the standards for acceptance at NIPS.
