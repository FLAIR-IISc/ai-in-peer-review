## Peer Review: Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the landscape of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), specifically focusing on the existence of bad local maxima. Challenging a conjecture by Srebro (2007), the authors demonstrate that for a mixture of three Gaussians, the population log-likelihood *does* contain suboptimal local maxima, arbitrarily far from the global optimum.  Furthermore, they demonstrate that standard initialization schemes (randomly selecting initial means from the data) lead the EM and first-order EM algorithms to converge to these suboptimal points with high probability. Crucially, they also show that these failures aren't due to saddle points, solidifying the problem as one of bad local optima. The paper provides theoretical justification for the difficulties often experienced in practice when applying EM to GMMs, and suggests the need for more sophisticated initialization strategies.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on a long line of work studying GMM estimation. It connects to early identifiability work (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009) and more recent density estimation results (Genovese & Wasserman, 2000; Ghosal & Van Der Vaart, 2001).  It positions itself within the algorithmic landscape dominated by EM (Dempster et al., 1977), acknowledging existing guarantees for modified EM variants (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and alternative methods (Arora et al., 2005; Belkin & Sinha, 2010).  Importantly, the work frames its contribution in the context of the robustness of maximum likelihood estimation (Donoho & Liu, 1988) and recent advances in understanding non-convex optimization landscapes (Loh & Wainwright, 2013; Wang et al., 2014).  The references are thorough and well-placed.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Result:** The proof of the existence of bad local maxima for a three-component Gaussian mixture is a significant and surprising result.  It directly refutes a long-standing conjecture and provides a fundamental understanding of the likelihood surface.
* **Algorithmic Implications:**  The demonstration that EM and first-order EM algorithms are likely to get stuck in these suboptimal points with random initialization is highly relevant and has practical implications for the use of these algorithms.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The paper employs a mathematically rigorous approach, clearly defining the problem setting and proving its results with careful analysis.  The introduction of a recursive structure to demonstrate the exponential failure probability of random initialization is particularly clever.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the authors provide sufficient context to understand the key concepts.
* **Connections to Broader Field:**  The discussion connecting the results to the broader literature on non-convex optimization and the saddle point problem is valuable.
* **Demonstrates limitation of common practice:** Showing that the common practice of random initialization of EM fails in many cases sheds light on why EM can be difficult to use in practice.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Dimensionality:** The existence of bad local maxima is demonstrated in 1D. While the authors state the result generalizes, the proof relies heavily on the simplicity of the 1D case.  Extending this result to higher dimensions remains an open question, and the authors acknowledge this.
* **Specific Model Assumptions:** The results are obtained under specific assumptions about the GMM (uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians).  While these are simplifying assumptions that allow for a rigorous analysis, it's unclear how robust the results are to deviations from these assumptions.  Discussing the potential impact of these assumptions would be beneficial.
* **Practical Remedies:** While the paper clearly identifies a problem, it doesn’t offer concrete solutions. Mentioning directions for future research focused on developing robust initialization schemes or alternative algorithms would strengthen the conclusion.
* **Proof Complexity:** While rigorous, the proofs are dense and require significant mathematical background to fully appreciate.  Providing more intuition behind the key steps in the proofs, even in the form of high-level explanations, would make the paper more accessible.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a fundamentally new and surprising theoretical result that challenges a widely held belief about the behavior of GMM likelihoods.
* The algorithmic implications are significant, providing a theoretical explanation for the difficulties experienced in practice when using EM.
* The paper is rigorously written, mathematically sound, and well-connected to the existing literature.
* The work addresses an important and open problem in the field of statistical machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The results are currently limited to a specific 1D setting.
* The assumptions of the model (uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians) may limit the generality of the findings.
* The paper doesn't offer immediate practical solutions to the identified problem.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a **strong acceptance** candidate. The paper’s theoretical contribution is significant, the analysis is rigorous, and the implications are important.  While the limitations regarding dimensionality and model assumptions are acknowledged, they don’t diminish the value of the results. The paper makes a substantial contribution to our understanding of GMM estimation and highlights the challenges of optimization in non-convex landscapes. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically encouraging a discussion of the robustness of the results to deviations from the model assumptions and a brief mention of potential directions for future research.

**Confidence:** 9/10
