## Peer Review: Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the landscape of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), specifically focusing on the existence of bad local maxima. Challenging a conjecture by Srebro (2007), the authors demonstrate the existence of such maxima even for well-separated mixtures of three Gaussians. They further analyze the consequences of this finding for the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and its first-order variant, showing that with high probability, these algorithms converge to suboptimal solutions when initialized randomly from the sample set.  The paper provides strong theoretical justification for the need for careful initialization strategies when applying local search methods to GMM estimation.  The results are supported by rigorous analysis and build upon existing work in statistical estimation and optimization.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a rich history of work on GMM estimation. It correctly positions itself within the context of identifiability problems (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009), convergence rate analysis (Chen, 1995; Ho & Nguyen, 2015), and algorithmic guarantees (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015).  Crucially, it addresses the limitations of existing guarantees, which often rely on strong assumptions about model specification. The work connects to the broader literature on non-convex optimization, referencing recent results on the landscape of high-dimensional objective functions (Loh & Wainwright, 2013; Wang et al., 2014) and the prevalence of saddle points (Pascanu et al., 2014; Lee et al., 2016). The paper appropriately cites relevant work and clearly distinguishes its contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significant Theoretical Result:**  The demonstration of bad local maxima in the GMM likelihood, even for well-separated components, is a crucial and surprising result. This directly contradicts the intuitive belief and Srebro’s conjecture.
*   **Algorithmic Implications:** The analysis of EM and first-order EM provides a concrete explanation for the practical difficulties encountered when using these algorithms.  The showing that random initialization leads to suboptimal convergence with high probability is a strong and impactful finding.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The proofs appear thorough and employ sophisticated techniques from statistical learning theory and optimization.  The use of recent advances in gradient descent analysis (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas & Piliouras, 2016) to rule out convergence to saddle points is particularly insightful.
*   **Clear Presentation (Generally):** The paper is generally well-organized, and the authors provide clear definitions and notation.  The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the work within the existing literature.
* **Well-defined Problem Setting:** Focusing on uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians allows for a focused and rigorous analysis, while still addressing a practically relevant scenario.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technicality:** The paper is highly technical and requires a strong background in statistical learning theory and optimization to fully appreciate the proofs. This may limit its accessibility to a broader audience within NIPS. While rigor is important, certain sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the underlying ideas.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the theoretical results are compelling, the paper lacks any empirical validation. It would be valuable to see some simulations demonstrating the practical impact of the identified local maxima and the failure of random initialization.
*   **Scope of Results:** The results are specific to the setting of uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians. While the authors acknowledge this, it would be useful to discuss the potential for extending these results to more general GMMs with varying weights and covariance structures.
*   **Initialization Scheme Nuance:** The description of the initialization scheme in Section 2.3, while technically correct, could be phrased more clearly. Describing the population equivalent immediately helps to ground the intuition.
* **Typos/Minor Issues:** There were a few minor typographical errors in the provided text (e.g., "µ∗i , . . . , µ∗M" repeated a lot, inconsistent use of notation).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in GMM estimation with a surprising and significant theoretical result.
*   The analysis has clear algorithmic implications, providing a theoretical explanation for the observed difficulties in using EM and related algorithms.
*   The proofs are rigorous and build upon recent advances in the field.
*   The paper is well-written (overall) and clearly positions itself within the existing literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is highly technical and may be inaccessible to a broad audience.
*   The lack of empirical validation weakens the practical impact of the findings.
*   The results are limited to a specific GMM setting, raising questions about their generalizability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and important theoretical contribution to the field of GMM estimation.  The demonstration of bad local maxima and the analysis of EM’s behavior are significant results.  While the technicality and lack of empirical validation are weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns.  I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS, with the caveat that the authors consider adding some empirical validation to strengthen the impact of their findings.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions – suggest adding simulations and potentially clarifying some technical explanations)

**Confidence:** 8/10
