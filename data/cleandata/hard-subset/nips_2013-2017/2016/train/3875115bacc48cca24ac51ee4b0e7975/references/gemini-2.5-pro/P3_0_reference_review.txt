### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses a fundamental and long-standing question regarding the optimization landscape of the log-likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The central focus is on the population log-likelihood for the idealized setting of a uniformly weighted mixture of isotropic Gaussians. The authors investigate a well-known conjecture by Srebro (2007) which posits that for this setting, in the infinite-sample limit, all local maxima of the likelihood function are also global maxima. The truth of this conjecture would provide strong theoretical justification for the widespread use of local search heuristics like the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

The paper makes several key contributions:
1.  It provides a definitive negative answer to Srebro's conjecture. The authors construct an explicit example of a well-separated, 1-D mixture of three Gaussians (M=3) whose population log-likelihood possesses a "bad" local maximum. Furthermore, they show this local maximum can be arbitrarily worse in likelihood value than the global maximum.
2.  It demonstrates severe algorithmic consequences of this structural property. The authors prove that for a specific class of GMMs, the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant, equivalent to gradient ascent), when initialized by sampling from the underlying distribution (a common practical heuristic), converges to a suboptimal critical point with probability approaching 1 exponentially fast in the number of components, M.
3.  It clarifies the nature of these failure points. By leveraging recent results on the dynamics of gradient descent (Lee et al., 2016), the paper argues that for the first-order EM algorithm, convergence to strict saddle points occurs with probability zero. This strongly suggests that the algorithmic failures are due to convergence to poor local maxima, not getting stuck at saddle points.

This work fits into a rich literature on GMM estimation. It contrasts sharply with the line of work on provably correct algorithms based on the method of moments (e.g., Hsu & Kakade, 2013; Moitra & Valiant, 2010) or clustering (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007), which provide global guarantees but under strong separation assumptions. This paper tackles the more widely used likelihood-based approach and reveals its inherent computational difficulty, even under similar strong separation conditions. It also complements local convergence analyses (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015), which show that EM converges quickly from a good initialization. This paper's results underscore the criticality of the initialization phase, showing that a "natural" random initialization strategy is provably insufficient. Finally, it provides a compelling counterpoint to recent findings in other areas of high-dimensional non-convex optimization (e.g., Loh & Wainwright, 2013) where spurious local optima are provably absent.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **High Significance:** The paper resolves a well-known and important open problem in the learning theory community. The conjecture of Srebro (2007) has lingered for nearly a decade, and its resolution has significant implications. It tempers the optimism that the GMM likelihood landscape might be benign in the population setting and provides a formal basis for the folk wisdom that EM for GMMs can fail badly.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Results:** The results are not merely existential; they are quantitative and powerful. The construction of a GMM whose likelihood has arbitrarily bad local maxima (Theorem 1) is a strong negative result. Even more impressively, the proof that EM with random initialization fails with exponentially high probability (Theorems 2 and 3) is a profound statement about the practical difficulty of the problem. It implies that the "repeat and pick the best" heuristic requires an exponential number of trials to succeed, rendering it computationally infeasible.

3.  **Novel and Insightful Proof Techniques:** The construction used to prove the existence of bad local maxima is clever and intuitive. The idea of placing one estimated center to cover two nearby true centers, while "wasting" two estimated centers on a single distant true center, provides a clear geometric picture of the failure mode. The recursive, hierarchical construction used to prove the exponential failure rate of EM is particularly elegant and is a powerful tool for analyzing the global behavior of the algorithm under random initialization.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the existing literature, and states its main contributions upfront. The intuition behind the main theorems is explained clearly before delving into the formal statements, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses (Points for discussion/improvement):**

1.  **Specificity of the Counterexample:** The GMM instances constructed to show the negative results are highly structured and somewhat pathological (e.g., the recursive clustering of means). While perfectly valid for disproving a general conjecture, it leaves open the question of how prevalent such bad local maxima are for "typical" or "natural" GMMs. The paper proves that a hard instance *exists*, but does not characterize the class of GMMs that are susceptible to this failure mode.

2.  **Finite Sample Regime:** The paper focuses entirely on the population setting (infinite samples). It briefly claims that the results extend to the large finite-sample setting via "standard empirical process arguments." While this is likely true, a more formal statement or a brief discussion on the required sample complexity (`n` as a function of `M`, `d`, and the separation) for the empirical likelihood landscape to inherit the properties of the population landscape would substantially strengthen the practical relevance of the claims.

3.  **The M=2 Case:** The paper notes that resolving the M=2 case remains an open problem and conjectures that it is well-behaved. It would be helpful to include a sentence or two explaining why their construction for M≥3 fundamentally fails for M=2. This would provide the reader with a deeper insight into the geometric nature of the problem.

### Overall Evaluation

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a significant and lasting contribution to our understanding of mixture models and non-convex optimization in machine learning. It elegantly resolves a major open question with a clear and definitive negative result. The algorithmic consequences derived from this structural result are profound and provide strong theoretical caution against the naive use of EM with random initialization. The strengths of the paper—its significance, the novelty of its techniques, and its clarity—far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily avenues for future research. This paper is of high quality and will undoubtedly be of great interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
