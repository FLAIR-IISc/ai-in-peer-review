- Resolves Srebro's conjecture by constructing a counterexample showing bad local maxima exist for GMM likelihoods.  
- Counterexample uses M=3 well-separated components in d=1, contradicting intuition about well-separated GMMs.  
- Shows standard EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to bad local maxima with exponentially high probability.  
- Provides strong quantitative algorithmic consequence: failure probability scales like 1 - e^{-cM}.  
- Uses novel, technically sophisticated geometric and neighborhood-based proofs; analyzes first-order EM via strict-saddle arguments.  
- Limitation: the counterexample and main construction are confined to dimension d=1, reducing practical relevance.  
- Claim that at least three components are necessary is not formally proven for the two-component case.  
- Algorithmic implications may be overstated—no empirical frequency estimates or comparisons to better initialization schemes (k-means++, method-of-moments).  
- Misses explicit connections to recent "benign non-convexity" literature (e.g., Loh & Wainwright, Wang et al.).  
- Overall assessment: high-quality, rigorous theoretical contribution; recommendation to accept.  
- Key suggestions for authors: address extension to higher dimensions, provide formal or deeper discussion for M=2, and relate results to benign non-convexity and initialization methods.
