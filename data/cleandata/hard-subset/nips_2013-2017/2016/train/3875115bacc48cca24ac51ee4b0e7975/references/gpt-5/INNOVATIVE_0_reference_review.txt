Summary
This paper settles a long-standing conjecture by Srebro (2007) by showing that the population log-likelihood of uniformly weighted, spherical, unit-variance Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) can indeed possess spurious local maxima once the number of components M ≥ 3, even when components are well-separated. The authors construct explicit one-dimensional, well-separated mixtures with three or more components whose population likelihood has bad local maxima with arbitrarily large suboptimality gaps relative to the global maximum (Theorem 1). They then study algorithmic consequences: EM and first-order EM (gradient EM) with random initialization (sampling initial centers i.i.d. from the mixture distribution) converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − exp(−cM) for a universal constant c, implying exponentially many restarts are needed to succeed with constant probability (Theorems 2–3). Finally, they show that first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, so failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles (Theorem 4), leveraging recent results on gradient flows and stable manifold arguments.

Relation to prior work is clear: it contrasts with positive local convergence results for EM under good initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and with algorithmic approaches based on clustering or moment methods (Arora et al., Hsu and Kakade, Moitra and Valiant). It also connects to broader nonconvex optimization insights on saddles vs. local optima (Pascanu et al., Lee et al.).

Strengths
- Novel and impactful negative result: a clean, population-level counterexample in a surprisingly benign regime (uniform weights, isotropic, well-separated, even in d=1) that overturns a widely believed conjecture.
- Clear algorithmic implications: formalizes the practical risk of random initialization for EM/gradient EM and quantifies the failure probability as exponentially high in M.
- Thoughtful structural insights: geometric intuition for M=3 and a recursive clustering construction to drive exponential failure probability are compelling and likely reusable.
- Clarifies the saddle vs. local maxima debate by showing first-order EM avoids strict saddles almost surely, making a precise statement about the source of failures.
- The results are likely to influence how practitioners think about initialization schemes and when to prefer moment/spectral methods.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Population setting only: while the authors claim carryover to large finite samples via standard arguments, explicit finite-sample bounds (rates, dependence on n and separation) are not provided.
- Scope of initialization: analysis focuses on random initialization from the mixture distribution; it would be valuable to compare with common practical alternatives (e.g., k-means++, spectral/moment-based pilots).
- Generality: results are shown for uniform weights, spherical covariances, and known variance; it would strengthen the paper to discuss explicitly how the constructions extend (or fail to extend) to unequal weights, unknown variances, or anisotropic covariances.
- Quantification and visualization: no empirical demonstrations or visualizations of the basins of attraction; even simple 1D/2D plots would aid intuition and broaden impact.
- Presentation issues: some references have formatting inconsistencies; a few constants and separation thresholds in statements could be made explicit.

Quality
Technically solid overall. The constructive arguments for bad local maxima and the hierarchical clustering construction for exponential failure probability are convincing at the population level. The avoidance of strict saddles by first-order EM is timely and well-grounded in recent theory, with care taken to avoid global smoothness assumptions. The main caveat is the lack of explicit finite-sample instantiations and empirical corroboration.

Clarity
Well-written and well-organized. The intuitive M=3 construction is helpful. Background on EM and first-order EM is concise. I recommend tightening theorem statements with explicit separation parameters and clarifying the exact random initialization measure. Cleaning up citation formatting would improve polish.

Originality
High. The counterexample to Srebro’s conjecture in such a favorable regime is a significant conceptual advance. The exponential failure probability for random restarts under a natural scheme, via a recursive mixture construction, is also novel. The strict-saddle avoidance for first-order EM in this setting adds a useful nuance.

Significance
High. This work reframes how we think about EM for GMMs: even under generous assumptions, random initialization can be fundamentally unreliable. It motivates principled initialization strategies and strengthens the case for alternative estimators (spectral/moment methods) or carefully designed restarts. The structural insights should catalyze further work on characterizing benign regimes (e.g., M=2) and on provably good initializations.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you provide explicit finite-sample guarantees showing, with high probability over data, that local maxima persist and EM fails under random initialization, with sample complexity as a function of separation and M?
- How sensitive are your constructions to slight deviations from uniform weights or to mild covariance anisotropy?
- Do your results change if one uses k-means++ or split-and-merge EM for initialization?
- Can you quantify the measure of the basin of attraction of the bad local maxima beyond the high-level exponential-in-M statement?
- A small empirical section (even synthetic, 1D/2D) visualizing landscapes and EM trajectories would greatly increase accessibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Resolves an influential conjecture with a clean, surprising counterexample.
- Provides sharp algorithmic consequences with clear practical implications.
- Bridges structural nonconvex analysis with learning-theoretic concerns in a core model.
- Likely to stimulate substantial follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance
- Population-only results; no empirical validation.
- Limited discussion of non-uniform weights, unknown variances, or higher-dimensional constructions beyond the recursive idea.
- Some presentation polish needed.

Recommendation: Accept (strong). This is a bold, conceptually important contribution that will influence both theory and practice around EM and GMMs, despite the absence of experiments and finite-sample instantiations.

Confidence: High.
