Summary
This paper settles a long-standing conjecture by Srebro (2007) by constructing well-separated, uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) for which the infinite-sample (population) log-likelihood has spurious local maxima that are arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. The construction works already in 1D with M ≥ 3 components. Algorithmically, the paper shows that EM and first-order EM (gradient ascent on the population likelihood) with standard random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{−cM} on a carefully constructed family of mixtures; hence, to succeed with constant probability under this initialization one needs exponentially many restarts. Finally, using tools from dynamical systems (stable manifold ideas), the paper proves first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points with probability one, suggesting failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles. These results complement and contrast prior positive analyses of EM under strong separation and good initialization, and inform the practical need for careful initializers (e.g., moment-based).

Relation to prior work
- Addresses Srebro’s conjecture on the absence of bad local maxima in population GMM likelihoods, giving a definitive negative answer.
- Complements analyses of EM’s local convergence and basins of attraction (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman, Balakrishnan et al.) by showing that random initialization often fails even in well-separated, equal-weight, unit-variance settings.
- Uses and adapts insights from work on nonconvex landscapes and saddle points (e.g., Lee et al., Ge et al.) to the GMM likelihood.
- The recursive hard instance echoes hierarchical constructions used in algorithmic lower bounds and makes the exponentially-small success probability sharp and intuitive.

Quality
- Theoretical claims are clearly stated as population-level theorems with precise probability statements over random initialization. The construction for bad local maxima (Theorem 1) is well motivated: two nearby true centers and one far-away center, together with a neighborhood argument (exclude global optima; show boundary likelihood is lower), imply a suboptimal local maximum. The algorithmic negative results (Theorems 2–3) follow from a recursive cluster/mini-cluster arrangement that traps iterates within initialization “regions,” yielding e^{−Ω(M)} probability of a “perfect” allocation across all levels.
- The strict-saddle avoidance (Theorem 4) is carefully scoped to first-order EM with step size s ∈ (0,1), consistent with conditions needed for applying stable manifold-type arguments. The paper notes why the same argument doesn’t immediately apply to EM and flags this as conjectural.
- Limitations are acknowledged: population (infinite-sample) analysis; equal weights and fixed, identical covariances; main structural construction in 1D; no full resolution of the M=2 case.
- While the paper asserts that finite-sample analogues follow via standard empirical process arguments, it would help to outline the precise mode of convergence and sample complexity needed to transfer each theorem rigorously.

Clarity
- The paper is well organized: background, main results, proof ideas, and algorithmic implications are presented cleanly. The intuition behind each theorem is explained before deferring details to the appendix.
- Notation is consistent; the link between EM and first-order EM is clearly described. Minor issues: the definition of “well-separated” is given, but constants matter for the constructions—explicit ranges (even as examples) would help reproducibility. Some reference formatting appears corrupted; please fix citations.
- Suggestions: add a concise, explicit parameterized example (numerical values for means and separations) illustrating Theorem 1; include a simple finite-sample simulation to visualize EM trajectories getting trapped; clarify precisely how “random initialization from data” maps to the population setting.

Originality
- Resolving Srebro’s conjecture with a simple 1D counterexample for M ≥ 3 is a notable and original contribution.
- The exp-small success probability for EM/first-order EM under natural random restarts on a carefully crafted but well-separated family is new and impactful.
- The strict-saddle non-convergence result tailors general dynamical systems insights to the GMM population likelihood and includes a proof strategy that avoids stronger smoothness assumptions; while related in spirit to known results, the application and argument are novel in this context.

Significance
- This work materially changes the community’s understanding of the GMM likelihood landscape: even in benign, well-separated, equal-weight, spherical cases, bad local maxima exist and trap standard local methods with high probability under common initialization schemes.
- It provides a rigorous rationale for the widespread practice of using careful initializers (moments, spectral, clustering) before EM, and tempers overly optimistic interpretations of EM’s empirical behavior.
- The techniques for constructing hard instances and analyzing trapping basins are likely to influence subsequent work on nonconvex likelihoods and initialization strategies.

Pros
- Resolves an open conjecture with clean, interpretable constructions.
- Strong, well-motivated negative algorithmic results with explicit probabilities.
- Clarifies the role of saddle points vs local maxima for first-order EM.
- Clear exposition and useful intuition.

Cons
- Population-level focus; finite-sample implications are only sketched.
- No experiments or empirical illustrations.
- Results are largely for equal weights and fixed unit covariances; scope to broader settings is not explored.
- The success/failure landscape for M=2 remains open (though appropriately flagged).

Recommendation
Accept. The paper meets the quality, originality, and significance criteria strongly and is sufficiently clear. Adding a brief finite-sample discussion and a small illustrative experiment would further strengthen the submission, but the theoretical contributions alone warrant acceptance. Confidence: high.
