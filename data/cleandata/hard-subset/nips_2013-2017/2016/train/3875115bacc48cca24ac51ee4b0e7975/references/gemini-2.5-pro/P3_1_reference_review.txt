**Summary:**
This paper investigates the optimization landscape of the population log-likelihood for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The authors make three primary contributions. First, they provide a negative answer to a well-known conjecture by Srebro (2007), which posited that for uniformly weighted, spherical, well-separated GMMs, the population likelihood has no spurious local maxima. The authors construct an explicit counterexample for any number of components M ≥ 3, showing the existence of local maxima whose likelihood can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. Second, they analyze the algorithmic consequences of this structural result. They prove that for a specific, yet well-behaved, GMM construction, the standard EM algorithm and its first-order variant (gradient ascent), when initialized by sampling from the data, will converge to a suboptimal critical point with a probability that is exponentially high in M (i.e., at least 1 - exp(-cM)). This implies that a naive random-start strategy requires an exponential number of restarts to find the global optimum. Third, the paper shows that for the first-order EM algorithm, convergence to a strict saddle point occurs with probability zero. This result, leveraging recent advances in non-convex optimization, compellingly argues that the failure of local search is due to the presence of bad local maxima, not getting stuck at saddle points.

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are substantial and well-supported by the described proof techniques. The analysis is performed at the population level, which allows for clean, fundamental insights into the geometry of the GMM likelihood function, unencumbered by finite-sample noise. The proof sketches provided are intuitive and elegant. The construction of the counterexample in Section 3.1 is clever, relying on a "misallocation" of estimated centers to true component clusters. The construction used to prove the high-probability failure of EM is particularly insightful, using a recursive structure of clusters to show that a "correct" initialization is an exponentially rare event. The application of recent results on gradient descent dynamics (Lee et al., 2016) to the GMM context is both timely and appropriate.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides an excellent overview of the problem, its history, and the context of related work, clearly motivating the paper's contributions. The main theorems are stated precisely, and the authors provide clear intuition behind their results before diving into technical details. The geometric description of the M=3 counterexample is particularly helpful for building intuition. The distinction between structural results (Theorem 1) and algorithmic consequences (Theorems 2-4) provides a logical flow to the paper. The writing is clear, concise, and accessible to an expert audience.

**Originality and Significance:**
The originality and significance of this work are outstanding.

1.  **Resolving an Open Conjecture:** The refutation of Srebro's (2007) conjecture is a major theoretical result. For nearly a decade, the question of whether the GMM likelihood landscape was benign in this idealized setting has been open. This paper definitively settles the question in the negative, fundamentally altering our understanding of this classical estimation problem.

2.  **Bridging Theory and Practice:** While the existence of local maxima might be a known issue, the paper's second result—that EM with a very natural random initialization scheme fails with overwhelming probability—is a powerful and sobering statement. It provides a rigorous theoretical explanation for why EM can be highly sensitive to initialization. This result strongly reinforces the necessity of more sophisticated initialization procedures, such as those based on the method of moments (e.g., Hsu & Kakade, 2013), and demonstrates that their utility is not merely theoretical but essential for avoiding catastrophic failures.

3.  **Connecting to Modern Optimization Theory:** The paper adeptly connects the classical problem of GMM estimation to the modern literature on non-convex optimization. By showing that strict saddle points are not the primary obstacle (Theorem 4), the authors pinpoint the problem squarely on the existence of poor local optima. This is a significant finding, as much recent work has focused on developing algorithms that can escape saddle points, with the implicit hope that this is sufficient. This paper provides a canonical example where this is not enough.

The results are of high importance to both the machine learning theory community and practitioners using GMMs. The insights will likely spur further research into the optimization landscapes of other latent variable models and provide stronger motivation for the development of provable initialization and optimization algorithms.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is very strong, and weaknesses are minor.

1.  **Population vs. Finite Sample:** The analysis is restricted to the population setting. While the authors correctly state that the results can be extended to the finite sample regime via empirical process arguments, a brief discussion of what sample complexity `n` would be required for these phenomena to manifest would be valuable. Does `n` need to be exponential in M or d, or are more modest sample sizes sufficient?

2.  **Generality of the GMM:** The analysis focuses on the simplified setting of uniform weights and spherical identity covariances, which is the setting of the original conjecture. This is perfectly justified. However, a brief discussion on whether the authors expect these negative results to hold (or worsen) for more general GMMs (unequal weights, general covariances) would be a welcome addition to the conclusion.

3.  **The M=2 Case:** The paper conjectures that for M=2, the likelihood landscape is well-behaved. This is an intriguing loose end. While solving it is beyond the scope of this paper, slightly more discussion on why they believe the M≥3 construction is necessary would be interesting. For instance, is it related to the inability to "misallocate" centers in the same way with only two components?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Resolves a significant and long-standing open problem in the field.
*   Provides a strikingly strong negative result on the performance of the widely used EM algorithm with a standard random initialization.
*   The proof techniques, particularly the recursive construction of the GMM for analyzing EM, are novel and elegant.
*   The work provides a crucial bridge between classical statistical estimation and the modern theory of non-convex optimization.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and the results are of high significance to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   None. This is a top-tier paper.

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept (Oral Presentation)
