

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses an important question regarding the likelihood surface of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) by resolving Srebro's conjecture (2007) that for well-specified GMMs, all local maxima are global maxima. The authors construct counterexamples for M ≥ 3 components showing the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima, and prove that random initialization for EM and first-order EM algorithms converges to these suboptimal points with high probability. This is a significant theoretical contribution that provides important insights into the limitations of local search methods for GMMs.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper resolves an important open problem that has been discussed in the literature for over a decade.
2. The construction of bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 is novel and well-motivated.
3. The theoretical results have clear algorithmic implications for practitioners using EM.
4. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good connections to prior work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Formatting and citation inconsistencies:** The paper contains numerous inconsistent citation formats (e.g., "Allman et al., 2009" vs "Allman et al\.,? \Q2009\E" in references). The reference list contains multiple formatting errors that undermine the paper's professionalism.

2. **Technical gaps in the main result:** Theorem 1 claims to show the existence of bad local maxima for any M ≥ 3, but the geometric intuition is only provided for M = 3. The generalization to M > 3 is not clearly explained or justified.

3. **Critical typo in EM algorithm:** The M-step formula (equation 5) contains a typo: "µnewi = ∑n i=1 wi(x`)x`∑n `=1 wi(x`)" should be "µnewi = (∑_{`=1}^n wi(x`)x`) / (∑_{`=1}^n wi(x`))" with the summation index correctly using ` instead of i.

4. **Ambiguous definition of "success":** Theorem 2 claims "the probability of success for the EM algorithm is exponentially small as a function of M" but does not define what constitutes "success" (finding the global maximum? within ε of it?).

5. **Missing justification for key claims:** The paper states "the first-order EM updates are identical to gradient ascent updates" but provides no proof or citation. This is a critical claim that should be supported.

6. **Incomplete technical justification:** The paper claims "the log-likelihood function is bounded from above" but does not provide a proof or citation for this basic property.

7. **Redundant phrasing:** The paper repeatedly states "for any M ≥ 3 and any constant Cgap > 0" when the latter part is redundant since Cgap is a constant.

8. **Missing figure:** The paper references "Figure 1" but the figure is not included in the provided text, making it difficult to verify the geometric intuition.

9. **Inconsistent use of "well-separated":** The paper defines "well-separated" as ξ = Ω(√d) but later uses this term without explicit reference to this definition.

10. **Unjustified constant:** The paper states "for a universal constant c > 0" without specifying its value or derivation, which is problematic for the precision expected in theoretical work.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving Srebro's conjecture, the numerous technical inconsistencies, formatting errors, and missing justifications undermine its quality. The critical typo in the EM algorithm description alone would prevent replication of the algorithm. The paper would benefit from careful proofreading, clearer definitions, and more complete technical justifications for its key claims. The significance of the work is substantial, but the execution contains multiple avoidable errors that prevent the paper from meeting NIPS's high standards for technical rigor.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's contribution is important, but the technical flaws are too numerous and fundamental to be overlooked. The authors should address these issues before resubmission.
