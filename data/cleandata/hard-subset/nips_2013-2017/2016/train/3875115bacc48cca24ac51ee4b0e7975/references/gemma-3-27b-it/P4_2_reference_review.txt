This paper investigates the local optima of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), specifically focusing on uniformly weighted mixtures of isotropic Gaussians. It builds upon the conjecture of Srebro [2007] that, in the limit of infinite samples, there are no bad local maxima. The authors disprove this conjecture by constructing a 3-component GMM with demonstrably bad local maxima.  Furthermore, they show that the EM and first-order EM algorithms, initialized by uniformly random selection of component means from the data, converge to these suboptimal points with exponentially high probability. This work connects to a large body of literature on GMM estimation, including identifiability (Allman et al. [2009], Teicher [1963]), convergence rates (Chen [1995], Ho and Nguyen [2015]), and algorithmic analysis (Dasgupta & Schulman [2007], Balakrishnan et al. [2015]). Recent work on non-convex optimization (Loh & Wainwright [2013], Wang et al. [2014]) relating to the vanishing of bad local optima in high dimensions provides relevant context, as does research into the role of saddle points (Pascanu et al. [2014], Ge et al. [2015]).

**Strengths:**

*   **Important Negative Result:** The disproof of Srebro’s conjecture is a significant contribution. It reveals a fundamental limitation of local search methods for GMM estimation, even in a relatively well-behaved setting.
*   **Strong Algorithmic Implications:**  The demonstration that EM and first-order EM converge to bad local optima with high probability under a reasonable initialization scheme is a compelling result, highlighting the need for careful initialization strategies. The exponential dependence on the number of components is particularly striking.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper employs rigorous mathematical analysis and proofs, particularly in demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima and characterizing the convergence behavior of the algorithms.
*   **Connections to Related Work:** The paper is well-situated within the existing literature, clearly referencing and building upon previous work in statistical estimation, optimization, and machine learning.
*   **Clear Presentation (with caveats - see weaknesses):** The overall structure is logical, and the authors attempt to provide intuition alongside the formal mathematical results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technical Density:** The paper is mathematically dense and may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the specific techniques used in the proofs. The notation is often heavy, and greater effort could be made to present the core ideas in a more accessible manner.
*   **Limited Scope of GMMs:** The results are restricted to uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussian mixtures.  While this simplification allows for a more thorough analysis, it's unclear how much of the observed behavior extends to more general GMMs (e.g., unequal weights, arbitrary covariance matrices).  The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not extensively discuss it.
*   **Initialization Assumption:** The random initialization scheme, while practical, is not thoroughly justified beyond its connection to sampling from the underlying mixture distribution. A more detailed discussion of the properties of this initialization scheme and its impact on the likelihood surface would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Discussion of Alternatives:** While the paper highlights the failure of EM-style algorithms, it offers relatively little discussion of potential alternative initialization or optimization strategies that might mitigate these issues. Briefly discussing potential solutions (even if outside the scope of the paper) would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Figure 1 quality:** The figure showing the log-likelihood and gradient norm in the 2D case is helpful for intuition, but its resolution is low, making it difficult to discern details.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in the theory of GMM estimation.
*   The negative result regarding Srebro’s conjecture is significant and has implications for the design and analysis of GMM algorithms.
*   The rigorous mathematical analysis and proofs are convincing and contribute to our understanding of the landscape of the GMM likelihood function.
*   The paper is well-written (despite its technicality) and clearly articulates its contributions and limitations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The restricted scope of the results (uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians) limits their generalizability.
*   The technical density of the paper may make it inaccessible to a broad audience.
*   The lack of discussion of potential alternative solutions leaves the reader with a somewhat pessimistic outlook.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant theoretical result regarding the local optima of GMM likelihoods. The disproof of Srebro’s conjecture is important, and the analysis of the EM algorithm's convergence behavior is compelling. While the paper’s scope is limited and it is technically challenging, the contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity (e.g., more intuitive explanations, higher-resolution figures) and to briefly discuss potential avenues for future research exploring alternative algorithms or initialization strategies.
