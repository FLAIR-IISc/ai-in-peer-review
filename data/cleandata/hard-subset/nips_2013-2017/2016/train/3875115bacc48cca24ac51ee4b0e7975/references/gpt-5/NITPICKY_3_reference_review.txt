Summary
The paper studies the population likelihood landscape of uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with fixed unit variance. It disproves Srebro’s conjecture by constructing, for any M ≥ 3 (in d = 1), well-separated GMMs whose population log-likelihood has suboptimal local maxima (Theorem 1), with arbitrarily large likelihood gaps to the global optimum. It then shows that EM and first-order EM (gradient EM) with a practical random initialization (drawing centers i.i.d. from the data, which in the population coincides with i.i.d. draws from the mixture) converge with high probability to suboptimal critical points—specifically, the success probability is exponentially small in M (Theorems 2–3). Finally, using stable-manifold-type arguments, it shows first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points with probability one (Theorem 4), suggesting that the algorithmic failures arise from bad local maxima rather than saddles. The paper emphasizes structural insights and algorithmic consequences; finite-sample implications are claimed to follow via standard empirical process arguments.

Strengths
- Resolves a well-circulated conjecture for a canonical model by an explicit, low-dimensional, well-separated counterexample; conceptually important.
- Clear articulation of algorithmic consequences: even in idealized settings, EM/gradient EM with common random initializations can fail with exponentially high probability.
- The recursive construction underlying Theorem 2 is elegant and informative; it cleanly explains why failure probability compounds across levels.
- The strict-saddle non-convergence result for first-order EM clarifies the type of critical points responsible for failure.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope of constructions: All structural counterexamples are 1D and rely on very tailored clusterings. It would help to show robustness to small perturbations and generalization to d > 1 beyond trivial embeddings. “Well-separated” is defined via Ω(√d), but the theorems do not state explicit separation constants (especially in d = 1), making the results hard to instantiate.
- Finite-sample claims are deferred to “standard empirical process arguments” without any rates. Given the practical angle, at least a theorem with explicit n scaling (uniform deviation bounds for L_n vs. L over the parameter class) would be valuable.
- Random initialization model: Only “sample-from-data” is analyzed. Many practical initializations (k-means++, overclustering-and-pruning, split-merge heuristics) are not discussed; a brief analysis or at least a counterexample to common alternatives would strengthen the algorithmic message.
- EM result statements: Theorem 2 is phrased as “∀t ≥ 0, L(µ_t) ≤ L(µ∗) − C_gap.” This does not explicitly assert convergence to a bad critical point (although EM is monotone). Please clarify whether convergence is proven and identify the limit set; as written, it guarantees persistent suboptimality, not convergence.
- First-order EM: The requirement s ∈ (0,1) is asserted “not aggressive” but no Lipschitz/smoothness constants or stability region are provided. Since L is analytic, a simple statement tying s to the gradient Lipschitz constant would improve rigor.
- Two-component case: The paper repeatedly alludes to M = 2 being open. Given its centrality, a stronger partial result (e.g., structural properties ruling out bad local maxima under explicit separation) would enhance impact.
- Clarity/rigor of Theorem 1: The proof idea argues via a neighborhood with lower boundary likelihood to imply an interior maximum. This guarantees existence of a maximum, but not strictness; more detail on the Hessian or curvature at the suboptimal point would solidify the “bad local maximum” claim (vs. non-strict local max/plateau).
- Assumptions: All results fix equal weights and equal known variances. The paper should discuss whether the constructions extend (or how they change) under unknown weights/variances—especially since unknown variances are standard in applied EM.
- No empirical illustrations: Simple population-level plots (as for M = 2) for M = 3 (constructed instance) would aid intuition and help verify basins of attraction.

Clarity and presentation issues (numerous and should be fixed)
- Several formatting glitches and stray text (e.g., “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.” appears in the middle of the intro).
- In the M-step formula, the numerator uses ∑n i=1 instead of ∑n ℓ=1; likely a typo.
- Inconsistent notation between µ as an ordered vector vs. a multiset {µ_i}; sometimes spacing “M -component,” “[M ]”.
- Definition (3) uses squared norm but the text calls it “distance”; be precise.
- References are poorly formatted, with placeholders like “?, \Q2015\E,” missing venues/pages, inconsistent author names, and duplicates. This must be corrected to meet NeurIPS standards.
- Figure 1 is referenced but not included; the discussion about gradient norms at (0,R) is informal—consider adding a precise argument or removing speculation.
- Minor grammatical issues and hyphenation inconsistencies.

Relation to prior work
- The positioning relative to known EM analyses (local convergence with good init) is good. However, please better differentiate from earlier constructions of spurious maxima for finite-sample or non-spherical/unknown-variance mixtures. A brief taxonomy of what was known (finite-sample bad local maxima, degeneracies with unknown variances) versus what is new here (population, equal weights, isotropic, well-separated) would help.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide explicit separation constants and a precise parameterization for the M = 3 construction (and for the recursive instance in Theorem 2), along with a robustness statement under small perturbations?
- Can you state a finite-sample theorem with explicit n that preserves the bad local maxima and the exponentially small success probability?
- Do your negative results persist for k-means++ initialization or overcomplete initializations followed by pruning?
- Is Theorem 1’s local maximum strict? If so, provide second-order conditions.
- Any partial progress for M = 2 under explicit separation?

Pros
- Resolves a notable conjecture; conceptually important negative results.
- Clean, insightful constructions; useful perspective on EM failure modes.
- Clarifies the role of saddles vs. local maxima for gradient EM.

Cons
- Many presentation and reference issues; several technical claims underspecified.
- Lack of finite-sample, higher-dimensional, and alternative-initialization analyses.
- Some theorem statements lack precise conditions and explicit constants.

Overall assessment
The core idea is strong and significant, but the paper needs substantial polishing and stronger, more explicit statements to meet NeurIPS standards. Given the numerous clarity/formatting issues and missing technical details, I lean toward rejection in its current form, with the expectation that a revised version could be very impactful.

Recommendation: Reject
