

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental open question in Gaussian mixture model (GMM) theory: whether all local maxima of the population likelihood are global maxima, as conjectured by Srebro (2007). The authors provide a negative answer by constructing a GMM with M ≥ 3 well-separated components that contains arbitrarily bad local maxima. They further demonstrate that the EM algorithm and its first-order variant, when initialized randomly, converge to these suboptimal critical points with exponentially high probability in M.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on a rich line of work on GMMs, including identifiability results (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009), convergence rates (Chen, 1995; Ho and Nguyen, 2015), and algorithmic approaches (Arora et al., 2005; Vempala and Wang, 2002; Hsu and Kakade, 2013). It specifically addresses Srebro's conjecture (2007) about the absence of bad local maxima in population likelihood for well-specified GMMs, which has been a topic of interest since the early 2000s. The paper connects to recent advances in non-convex optimization (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016), particularly regarding the role of saddle points versus bad local maxima.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Significance**: The paper resolves an important open question in GMM theory that has remained unanswered for over a decade. The construction of a GMM with arbitrarily bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 components is elegant and well-justified.

2. **Rigorous Analysis**: The proofs are technically sound and well-structured. The authors carefully distinguish between population and sample settings, and provide a detailed geometric intuition for their construction (especially for M=3).

3. **Algorithmic Implications**: The paper provides strong theoretical justification for the empirical observation that careful initialization is crucial for EM in practice. The exponentially small success probability for random initialization (Theorem 2) is a significant contribution with direct practical implications.

4. **Novel Connection to Non-Convex Optimization**: The paper's analysis of the first-order EM algorithm's behavior regarding saddle points (Theorem 4) provides a useful theoretical perspective on why certain optimization failures occur in GMMs.

## Weaknesses

1. **Restrictive Assumptions**: The paper focuses exclusively on uniformly weighted, isotropic GMMs with fixed covariance (identity matrix). While this is a reasonable starting point, the results do not immediately extend to more general GMMs with varying weights or covariances, which are common in practice.

2. **Incomplete Treatment of M=2 Case**: The authors note that the conjecture may hold for M=2, but they do not provide a thorough analysis of this case. This is significant because many practical applications use two-component mixtures, and the paper's main conclusion (that random initialization fails for M≥3) leaves open the question of whether it also fails for M=2.

3. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications**: While the paper discusses the theoretical implications for EM, it could better address how practitioners might overcome these limitations (beyond referencing method-of-moments approaches). A brief discussion of real-world applications where these results would be particularly relevant would strengthen the paper.

4. **Finite-Sample Extension**: The paper mentions that results extend to finite samples via empirical process arguments but provides no details. A more thorough discussion of how the population results translate to finite samples would be valuable.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to understanding the optimization landscape of GMMs. The resolution of Srebro's conjecture is a major achievement, and the algorithmic results provide important theoretical justification for the practical challenges in GMM estimation.

The paper meets all the key criteria for NIPS: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written and organized (Clarity), resolves a long-standing open question (Originality), and has significant implications for both theory and practice (Significance).

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The results are important, the theoretical contributions are substantial, and the paper is well-executed. The weaknesses noted are primarily limitations of the specific setting considered rather than flaws in the paper's execution.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. The authors should briefly discuss whether their results can be extended to non-isotropic GMMs, perhaps by noting the technical challenges involved.

2. A more detailed analysis of the M=2 case would strengthen the paper's position on the threshold for when bad local maxima appear.

3. The paper could include a short discussion of practical applications where the M≥3 case is relevant, to better illustrate the significance of the results.

4. A few sentences on how the population results translate to finite samples would help bridge the theoretical and practical aspects of the work.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs and the behavior of optimization algorithms for these models. It provides a strong theoretical foundation for the empirical observation that careful initialization is critical for successful GMM estimation.
