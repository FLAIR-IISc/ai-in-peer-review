- Paper resolves Srebro’s 2007 conjecture by constructing explicit well-separated, uniformly weighted spherical GMMs (1D, M ≥ 3) with spurious local maxima and arbitrarily large gap to the global optimum.
- Authors show EM and first-order EM with standard random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e−cM via a hierarchical “cluster-of-clusters” construction.
- First-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, implicating bad local maxima rather than saddle proliferation as the cause of failures.
- Work complements prior positive local-convergence results for EM and contrasts with benign nonconvex landscapes where local optima are near-global.
- Strength: Clean, sharp 1D construction that gives a clear negative resolution of the open conjecture under very favorable settings (uniform weights, spherical, separable).
- Strength: Concrete algorithmic consequence—exponentially high failure probability of random restarts for EM and first-order EM.
- Strength: The saddle-avoidance result aligns the analysis with recent dynamical-systems literature and sharpens blame on local maxima.
- Strength: Conceptual clarity—warns against naive random restarts and motivates careful initialization or non-likelihood algorithms.
- Weakness: No explicit finite-sample theorem—claim that results transfer via standard empirical-process arguments is asserted but not proved.
- Weakness: Need precise treatment of transfer from population to sample likelihoods, including uniform approximation, persistence of critical points, and tracking iterates.
- Weakness: Scope of separation needs clarification—embedding to higher d should be stated explicitly and how separation scales with d and hierarchical levels quantified.
- Weakness: Quantitative dependence of the “arbitrarily bad” gap on separation and cluster spacings, and conditions ensuring strict local maximality (Hessian negative definiteness), are not fully specified.
- Weakness: Theorem 4(a) on first-order EM convergence needs clearer assumptions (e.g., analyticity, KŁ property, Lipschitz gradient) and clarification whether convergence is to a point or a connected set.
- Weakness: Avoidance of strict saddles is shown only for first-order EM; analogous statements for EM (a non-gradient map) are missing and would strengthen the algorithmic picture.
- Weakness: No empirical validation—synthetic experiments showing EM failure probability scaling with M would improve practical impact.
- Weakness: Limited discussion on robustness to alternative initializers (e.g., K-means++) and to nonuniform weights or covariance misspecification.
- Quality: Constructions and probability bounds appear technically sound at the population level but finite-sample and convergence claims need more formal statements or assumptions.
- Clarity: Generally well-written with helpful geometric intuition for M = 3, though separation scales, recursive construction, and some references need tightening.
- Originality: High—resolves a conjecture and introduces a hierarchical construction for exponential failure; saddle-avoidance argument is timely.
- Significance: High—shows fundamental limitations of likelihood-based local search for GMMs even in favorable regimes, impacting algorithm design and theory.
- Pros: Clear counterexample, strong algorithmic consequences, and clarification of saddles vs. maxima in EM failures.
- Cons: Missing finite-sample results and empirical evidence; some convergence claims need sharper assumptions; limited robustness discussion.
- Recommendation: Accept, subject to addressing finite-sample and convergence clarifications.
- Reviewer confidence: High.
