## NIPS 2016 Review: Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the challenging problem of local optima in the likelihood function of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). Challenging a conjecture by Srebro (2007), the authors demonstrate the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima for a 3-component Gaussian mixture. Crucially, they go beyond a purely theoretical result by showing that the EM and first-order EM algorithms, when initialized with samples drawn randomly from the mixture, converge to these suboptimal critical points with exponentially high probability. They further demonstrate that these issues aren’t merely due to saddle points. This work provides a strong theoretical justification for the difficulties often encountered in practice when using local search methods like EM for GMM estimation, even in seemingly well-behaved scenarios.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The existence of bad local optima is a fundamental issue hindering reliable GMM estimation, and this work provides a rigorous analysis of the problem.
* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The proof of the existence of bad local maxima for a 3-component mixture is a significant contribution, directly contradicting a longstanding conjecture. The bounds on the probability of converging to suboptimal solutions are particularly compelling.
* **Algorithmic Relevance:** Connecting the theoretical results to the performance of practical algorithms like EM and its first-order variant is crucial.  The analysis directly informs our understanding of why EM can fail in practice, despite its widespread use.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction provides excellent context and motivation.  The background section is thorough.
* **Technically Sound:** The proofs, though complex, appear rigorous and well-motivated. The connection to recent work on gradient descent and saddle points is appropriately made.
* **Novelty:** The construction of a specific GMM instance demonstrating bad local maxima and the subsequent analysis of EM’s convergence behavior are highly original.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:** The results are presented for uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussian mixtures with fixed variances. While these simplifying assumptions are necessary for the analysis, it would be valuable to discuss the potential generalization (or lack thereof) to more complex GMMs (e.g., with different weights, covariances, or dimensionality).  The authors do acknowledge this limitation.
* **Proof Complexity:** The proofs, while rigorous, are quite involved. While understandable to experts, they may be difficult for a broader audience to fully grasp. Providing more intuitive explanations alongside the technical details could improve accessibility.
* **Practical Mitigation Discussion:** While the paper effectively demonstrates the *problem*, it offers limited discussion of potential *solutions*.  Briefly outlining potential strategies for avoiding these bad local optima (e.g., better initialization schemes beyond random sampling, global optimization techniques) would enhance the practical impact of the work.
* **Connection to Existing EM Analysis:** While related work is cited, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of *why* existing analysis of EM (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman 2007) doesn't readily resolve the issues identified in this work.  Specifically, explaining why their scenario differs from those considered in prior analyses would strengthen the argument.



**Originality:**

The work is highly original. The counterexample to Srebro's conjecture is novel, and the subsequent analysis linking this to the behavior of EM algorithms with random initialization is a significant contribution. The careful application of recent results on gradient descent to rule out convergence to saddle points is also novel.

**Significance:**

The results are significant.  They provide a theoretical underpinning for the practical challenges encountered with EM for GMM estimation. The exponential dependence of the success probability on the number of components highlights the inherent difficulty of the problem and underscores the need for more robust initialization or optimization strategies.  This work will likely influence future research on GMM estimation and non-convex optimization.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally clear, but the complexity of the proofs requires careful reading. More intuitive explanations of the key ideas would be beneficial.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound and provides a rigorous analysis of a challenging problem. The claims are well-supported by theoretical arguments. It represents a complete and well-executed piece of work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental problem in statistical estimation.
* Presents strong theoretical results with clear implications for practical algorithms.
* Is highly original and contributes significantly to the understanding of GMM estimation.
* Is well-written and technically sound.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  The limited scope (fixed variances, isotropic Gaussians) may restrict its direct applicability to real-world problems.
* The complexity of the proofs might limit accessibility for some readers.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMM estimation. The results are important and have clear implications for the design and analysis of algorithms.  Despite the limited scope, the novelty and theoretical rigor justify acceptance.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions - suggesting a brief discussion of potential mitigation strategies and a more detailed connection to existing EM analyses)
