## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the landscape of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), specifically focusing on the existence of bad local maxima. Challenging a conjecture by Srebro (2007), the authors demonstrate that for a mixture of three well-separated Gaussians, the population log-likelihood *does* contain local maxima inferior to the global maximum. Furthermore, they show that standard initialization schemes (randomly selecting initial means from the data) combined with the EM and first-order EM algorithms converge to these suboptimal points with exponentially high probability.  They complement this by proving that convergence is not hindered by saddle points, reinforcing the role of bad local optima in the failure of these algorithms.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a long line of research concerning the difficulties in estimating GMMs. It acknowledges early work on identifiability (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009) and density estimation (Genovese & Wasserman, 2000; Ghosal & Van Der Vaart, 2001).  It connects to recent work characterizing convergence rates (Chen, 1995; Ho & Nguyen, 2015) and analyzing EM variants (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015).  The authors explicitly position themselves within the context of maximizing likelihood, recognizing its robustness (Donoho & Liu, 1988) and seeking to understand when efficient algorithms can approximate it.  They differentiate their work from previous analyses focusing on well-specified models by investigating robustness to model misspecification. The connections to high-dimensional regression where non-convex objectives sometimes have “good” local optima (Loh & Wainwright, 2013; Wang et al., 2014) are also aptly noted.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Negative Result:** The proof of the existence of bad local optima, even for well-separated Gaussians, is a strong and surprising result. This directly refutes a previously held conjecture and provides a fundamental insight into the optimization landscape of GMMs.
* **Strong Algorithmic Implications:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that a commonly used initialization strategy (random sampling from data) leads to a high probability of converging to suboptimal solutions with EM and first-order EM. This has practical ramifications for the reliability of these algorithms.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The proofs appear mathematically sound and well-constructed, employing techniques tailored to the specific structure of the likelihood function. The careful analysis of the initialization scheme is particularly impressive.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the structure follows a natural progression from background to results to implications.
* **Completeness:** The authors address potential issues such as infinite likelihoods and the need for careful initialization, demonstrating a thorough understanding of the field.
* **Distinction from Saddle Points:** The work importantly demonstrates that the failure of algorithms is due to *bad local optima* and not saddle points, adding nuance to the understanding of the optimization challenge.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Dimensionality:** The existence of bad local optima is shown for d=1.  While a significant result, it leaves open the question of whether these bad optima persist in higher dimensions. The authors acknowledge this limitation.
* **Specific Gaussian Setting:** The analysis is focused on uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians with fixed variances.  The results might not generalize to more general GMMs with different weights, covariance structures, or variable selection.  This is inherent in the chosen scope but should be explicitly stated as a limitation.
* **Computational Cost of Avoiding Bad Optima:** While the paper demonstrates the *existence* of bad optima and the likelihood of converging to them, it doesn't provide concrete guidance on how to avoid them efficiently beyond suggesting the need for careful initialization. The statement about needing to repeat the EM algorithm exponentially many times is discouraging.
* **Discussion of Empirical Verification:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper lacks any empirical validation of the claimed phenomena. Showing these bad local optima and the EM's tendency to get stuck in them through simulations would strengthen the paper.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical result that challenges a prior conjecture and provides a fundamental understanding of the optimization landscape of GMMs.
* The algorithmic implications are substantial, highlighting the potential unreliability of commonly used algorithms with standard initialization.
* The analysis is rigorous and well-presented.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The results are limited to a specific, simplified Gaussian setting and a single dimension for the existence proof.
* The lack of empirical validation weakens the practical impact of the findings.
* While a negative result is valuable, the paper doesn't offer immediate practical solutions to avoid the identified problem.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of the challenges in estimating Gaussian mixture models. The existence of bad local optima, particularly in light of the widespread use of EM, is a crucial finding. While the limitations regarding dimensionality and model generality are valid concerns, the paper’s rigorous analysis and clear presentation outweigh these weaknesses. I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, despite these limitations.

**Rating:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)

**Recommendations for the Authors:**

* Consider adding simulations to visually demonstrate the existence of bad local optima and the EM algorithm's behavior in this landscape.
* Discuss the potential for extending the results to higher dimensions, even if only speculatively.
* Briefly address the computational cost of repeated EM runs and explore potential alternative initialization strategies that might mitigate the problem.
* Explicitly state the limitations of the results regarding the specific Gaussian setting.
