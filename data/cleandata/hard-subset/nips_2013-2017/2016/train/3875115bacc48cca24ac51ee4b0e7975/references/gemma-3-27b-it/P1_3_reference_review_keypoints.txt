- Paper shows population log-likelihood for a 3-component GMM can have arbitrarily bad suboptimal local maxima, refuting Srebro (2007).
- EM and first-order EM with data-based random initialization converge to these suboptimal points with high probability.
- Failures are due to bad local optima, not saddle points, highlighting a fundamental optimization issue for GMMs.
- Results provide theoretical explanation for practical difficulties with EM and imply need for better initialization strategies.
- Builds on identifiability and density estimation literature and connects to algorithmic analyses of EM and non-convex optimization.
- References are thorough and the work is well-placed within prior research on GMM estimation and optimization landscapes.
- Strong theoretical contribution: surprising existence proof of bad local maxima for a simple GMM setting.
- Algorithmic implications are practically relevant, showing exponential failure probability for standard random initialization.
- Rigorous, careful mathematical analysis with a clever recursive construction supporting the claims.
- Writing and notation are clear, with useful connections to broader non-convex optimization issues.
- Demonstrates a concrete limitation of common EM initialization practice in many cases.
- Limitation: existence proof is demonstrated in 1D, and extension to higher dimensions is not shown.
- Limitation: relies on specific assumptions (uniform weights, isotropic Gaussians), so robustness to model deviations is unclear.
- Paper lacks concrete practical remedies or algorithms to address the identified failures.
- Proofs are dense and could benefit from more high-level intuition to improve accessibility.
- Overall: strong acceptance candidate; recommends acceptance with minor revisions to discuss robustness and future research directions.
- Reviewer confidence: 9/10.
