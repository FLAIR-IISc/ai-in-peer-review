This paper investigates the local maxima of the likelihood function for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), focusing on the structural properties and algorithmic consequences. The authors demonstrate that, contrary to a conjecture by Srebro [2007], bad local maxima exist even for well-separated Gaussian mixtures. Furthermore, they show that the EM and first-order EM algorithms, initialized randomly from the data, converge to these suboptimal points with high probability, highlighting the challenges of local search methods in this setting.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question about the landscape of the likelihood function for GMMs. The problem is clearly stated, and the connection to existing work (Teicher [1963], Allman et al. [2009], Genovese & Wasserman [2000], Chen [1995], Dasgupta & Schulman [2007], Balakrishnan et al. [2015], Srebro [2007], Donoho & Liu [1988], among others) is thoroughly established. The authors effectively build on previous results, particularly those concerning the convergence properties of EM, and pinpoint a significant limitation. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the proofs appear carefully constructed. The exploration of both structural properties (existence of bad local maxima) and algorithmic consequences (convergence of EM) is a strength of the paper. The introduction of a recursive construction for demonstrating the high probability of convergence to suboptimal points is clever. The acknowledgement that saddle points are also a concern and the attempt to rule them out (Theorem 4) is commendable.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the theoretical results are strong, the practical implications beyond the idealized setting of uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians are not fully explored. The setting is deliberately simplified to isolate the issue of local maxima, but the extent to which these findings generalize to more realistic scenarios (non-isotropic covariances, unequal weights, finite sample sizes) remains unclear. While the authors mention empirical process arguments for extending the results to finite samples, a more detailed discussion or even preliminary experiments would strengthen the argument. The random initialization scheme is stated clearly, but a discussion of its prevalence in practice and how it compares to other common initialization methods would be useful. The paper focuses heavily on the *failure* of the algorithms; a brief discussion of potential mitigation strategies or alternative approaches that might overcome these limitations would be valuable.  Furthermore, while Theorem 4 attempts to address the saddle point issue, the complexity of the proof and the specific assumptions might limit its broad applicability. The notation is generally clear, but some equations could benefit from more descriptive labels.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The paper provides a negative answer to a longstanding conjecture and demonstrates a fundamental limitation of standard algorithms for GMM estimation.
* **Rigorousness:** The theoretical analysis is sound, and the proofs appear meticulously crafted.
* **Significance:** The findings have important implications for understanding the behavior of EM and related algorithms, even if the setting is idealized.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough literature review.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited Scope:** The results are specific to a simplified setting, and the generalization to more realistic scenarios is not fully explored.
* **Lack of Practical Guidance:** The paper primarily focuses on the negative results (failure of algorithms) without offering concrete suggestions for improvement.
* **Complexity:** Some of the theoretical arguments and proofs are quite intricate, potentially making the paper less accessible to a broader audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of GMM estimation. The results are novel, rigorously proven, and address an important open question. However, the limited scope and lack of practical guidance prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance at this time. 

**Recommendation:** Reject with encouragement to revise and address the weaknesses mentioned above. Specifically, the authors should consider:

1. **Discussing the generalization of their results to more realistic GMM settings.**  Even a qualitative discussion of the impact of non-isotropic covariances or unequal weights would be valuable.
2. **Including a brief discussion of potential mitigation strategies.**  What initialization schemes might be more robust to bad local maxima? Are there alternative optimization algorithms that could perform better?
3. **Adding some illustrative examples or simulations.** Showing the existence of these bad local maxima and the convergence of EM to these points in practice would strengthen the paper.
4. **Simplifying the presentation of some of the more complex proofs or providing more intuition behind the key steps.**
