Summary
The paper settles a long-standing conjecture about the landscape of the population log-likelihood for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). For uniformly weighted, spherical, unit-variance GMMs with M ≥ 3 components, it constructs well-separated instances whose population log-likelihood has spurious local maxima that can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum (Theorem 1). Algorithmically, it shows that EM and first-order EM (gradient ascent on the population likelihood) with practical random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-cM} for a universal c > 0 (Theorems 2–3), implying that exponentially many restarts are needed to succeed on these instances. Finally, it proves that first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points with probability one (Theorem 4), so the observed failures are attributable to bad local maxima rather than saddles.

Relation to prior work is well-situated. Earlier analyses either assumed favorable separation and focused on local convergence given good initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) or proposed alternatives to EM (moment/spectral or clustering-based methods). The paper disproves Srebro’s conjecture that population likelihood has no bad local maxima for equal-weight spherical mixtures (previously supported empirically at least for M = 2). It leverages ideas from the dynamical-systems view of gradient methods (Lee et al., Panageas & Piliouras) to rule out convergence to strict saddles.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The claims are precise and supported by formal theorems, with proofs deferred to the appendix. The structural construction behind Theorem 1 is plausible and carefully outlined: create a configuration where two true centers are close and the third is far; show a neighborhood where the boundary likelihood is strictly lower than a specific suboptimal configuration, ensuring a local maximum exists away from the global solution. The exponential failure probability (Theorems 2–3) is justified by a recursive, multiscale clustering of true centers: even if random initialization places the right number of centers in the two top-level clusters, one must also match counts within each subcluster across Ω(log M) levels, making success exponentially unlikely.
- Assumptions and scope: The main results are for the population objective with equal weights, identity covariances, and 1D (for the construction), albeit with well-separated centers. The authors argue the results extend to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments, which is reasonable but not quantified in the main text (e.g., explicit sample complexity ensuring preservation of bad basins). The step-size restriction s ∈ (0,1) for first-order EM is mild and encompasses natural choices.
- Potential caveats: The paper relies on adversarially constructed instances. While this is appropriate for a structural impossibility result, the practical frequency of such configurations in applications remains an open question the authors acknowledge. The no-strict-saddle convergence result is for first-order EM; the analogous statement for classic EM is conjectured but not proved.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized: background, clear statement of the model and algorithms, precise formulation of random initialization at the population level, and a clean separation between structural and algorithmic results. The intuitive sketches are helpful (e.g., the M = 3 geometry and the recursive construction).
- Some clarity issues: constants and separation scales are not explicit in the main text; the finite-sample carryover is mentioned but not quantified; reference formatting has many glitches; and a figure is referenced but not provided here. Minor notation choices are standard and reproducible.

Originality
- The negative resolution of Srebro’s conjecture for M ≥ 3 under such a favorable setting (uniform weights, spherical, well-separated) is new and impactful. The exponential failure probability with random initialization (at the population level) is conceptually strong and, to my knowledge, novel. The application of strict-saddle avoidance to first-order EM in this context strengthens the interpretation of failures.

Significance
- The results materially affect how the community should view EM and gradient ascent on GMM likelihoods: even in benign regimes and in the population limit, bad local maxima exist and random restarts can be exponentially unlikely to help. This pushes toward careful initialization (e.g., moment/spectral pilots) and motivates further study of likelihood landscapes and initialization strategies. While the constructions are adversarial, the conceptual takeaway is important and likely to influence both theory and practice.

Pros
- Resolves an open conjecture by explicit counterexamples.
- Connects structural geometry of the likelihood to concrete algorithmic consequences.
- Separates the roles of local maxima vs. saddle points via a rigorous no-strict-saddle convergence result for first-order EM.
- Clear problem formulation and insightful proof sketches.

Cons
- Population focus with only qualitative discussion of finite-sample implications; no explicit n vs. d, M bounds ensuring the phenomena persist empirically.
- Results rely on tailored adversarial instances; practical prevalence is not empirically assessed.
- Restriction to equal weights, identity covariances, and 1D constructions; generality to more realistic mixtures is not established.
- Theorem 4 covers first-order EM but not standard EM.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide finite-sample conditions (n as a function of separation, d, M) guaranteeing that the sample likelihood inherits the bad local maxima and the EM failure probabilities up to small slack.
- Clarify the separation constants used in the constructions and how “arbitrarily bad” suboptimality scales with separation.
- Discuss whether common initializers (k-means++, spectral methods) avoid the bad basins on your instances; small empirical illustrations would strengthen the message.
- Comment on extensions to unequal weights and non-identity covariances; do the constructions adapt?
- For EM (not first-order EM), is there a path to a no-strict-saddle convergence guarantee (perhaps via a generalized stable-manifold argument)?

Overall evaluation
This is a strong theoretical paper with clear, original, and significant contributions to our understanding of nonconvex likelihood landscapes and EM for GMMs. Despite some limitations (population setting and adversarial nature), the results are important and timely. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on improved discussion of finite-sample implications and minor clarifications.
