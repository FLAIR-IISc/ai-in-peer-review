- Provides a rigorous counterexample showing Srebro’s (2007) conjecture is false: well-separated GMMs can have bad local maxima (M=3, 1D, unit-variance spherical Gaussians).  
- Constructs arbitrarily bad local maxima and proves their existence (Theorem 1).  
- Shows random initialization for EM and first-order EM fails with exponentially high probability as M grows (Theorems 2–3).  
- Proves failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points (Theorem 4).  
- Results are in the population setting but can be extended to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments.  
- Technical arguments are rigorous and connect appropriately to recent optimization literature (e.g., Lee et al., 2016).  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written with helpful geometric intuition for the counterexample.  
- Formal theorem statements and structured proofs (with appendix for technical details) enhance clarity.  
- Distinguishes contributions from related work, notably Balakrishnan et al. (2015) on two-component GMMs.  
- Novel and non-trivial resolution of a long-standing open question in mixture model theory.  
- Algorithmic consequences are important: even well-specified, well-separated settings can defeat random EM initialization.  
- Significant implications for non-convex optimization: bad local maxima (not just saddle points) can be the main obstacle.  
- Strengths: rigorous resolution of open problem, clear construction, important algorithmic insights, careful distinction between maxima and saddles.  
- Weaknesses: focus on population setting with limited finite-sample discussion; construction presented in 1D with only brief comments on higher-dimensional extensions.  
- Recommendation: strong accept — technically sound, significant, well-written, and of broad interest to the community.
