Summary
The paper tackles a long-standing conjecture due to Srebro (2007): that in the population (infinite-sample) limit for uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), the likelihood has no spurious local maxima. The authors give a sharp negative result. For any M ≥ 3, they construct a 1D, well-separated, uniform mixture of spherical Gaussians whose population log-likelihood has bad local maxima with arbitrarily large suboptimality gaps. They further show algorithmic consequences: EM and first-order EM (a gradient-ascent variant) with the widely used random initialization scheme converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{−cM}. Conversely, achieving a good solution via random restarts requires exponentially many initializations. Finally, they prove gradient EM does not converge to strict saddle points with probability one, strengthening the conclusion that failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles. Proofs use geometric arguments and a recursive clustering construction to drive the exponential failure probability.

Relation to prior work
- Refutes Srebro’s conjecture in the population setting, even under favorable assumptions (equal weights, isotropic, well-separated).
- Complements local convergence analyses of EM with “good” initializations (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. 2015) by showing that random initialization is typically bad on explicit instances.
- Aligns with recent theory on gradient methods avoiding strict saddles (Lee et al. 2016; Panageas & Piliouras 2016), leveraging those ideas to argue failures must be local maxima.
- Connects to method-of-moments/spectral approaches that provide provable initialization or estimation guarantees under separation (Arora et al.; Moitra & Valiant; Hsu & Kakade).

Strengths
- High novelty and conceptual impact: a clean, definitive counterexample in an archetypal model class overturns a widely held belief about the benign nature of the GMM likelihood landscape at the population level.
- Algorithmic bite: the exponential failure probability for EM and first-order EM under natural random initialization is a strong and practically relevant statement that cautions against blind multi-start EM.
- Clarity of the underlying ideas: the M=3 geometric construction is intuitive; the recursive cluster construction that yields e^{-Ω(M)} success probability is elegant.
- Useful diagnostic of landscape vs. algorithm dynamics: by ruling out convergence to strict saddles, the paper isolates the culprit (bad local maxima).
- Broad implications: reinforces the need for careful initialization or alternative estimators if one wants MLE’s robustness properties.

Weaknesses and questions
- Population vs. finite-sample: while the authors state the extension to finite samples is straightforward via empirical process tools, the paper would be stronger with explicit uniform convergence bounds, sample complexity requirements (n as a function of M, separation, and d), and a theorem connecting the sample EM dynamics to the population basins.
- Scope of constructions: all results are shown for 1D, equal-weight, identity covariance mixtures. This already refutes the conjecture, but it would be valuable to know how far the negative results extend (unequal weights? varied isotropic variances? low-dimensional multivariate d > 1?).
- Initialization model: the random initialization is data-subsampling (equivalently sampling from the mixture at population level). Many practitioners use k-means++/farthest-first, PCA-based, or moment/spectral initializations. Either a brief analysis or experiments evaluating those schemes on the constructed instances would sharpen practical conclusions.
- Empirical validation: simple simulations demonstrating (i) existence of bad local maxima; (ii) EM’s failure probability scaling with M; and (iii) gradient EM avoiding strict saddles while still failing, would make the work more persuasive to a broad NeurIPS audience.
- Constants/separation: “well-separated” is used in the statements; providing concrete separation thresholds and the exact dependence of the gap C_gap on separations would improve readability and reproducibility.
- First-order EM details: the step-size condition s ∈ (0,1) is said to be “weak”; clarifying whether this depends on Lipschitz constants of ∇L near the constructed basins would help practitioners.
- Bibliography/formatting: several citations have placeholders or inconsistent formatting; please clean for the camera-ready.

Quality
Technically solid and complete at the population level. The constructions and probability calculations appear sound, and the use of modern results on strict saddles is appropriate. The finite-sample claims are currently informal; formalizing them would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
Generally well-organized and clearly written. The geometric intuition for M=3 is helpful. Suggestions: explicitly state the separation condition and constants in Theorem 1; include a figure for the M=3 bad local maximum configuration; tighten the exposition for Theorem 2’s recursive construction with a schematic; fix references.

Originality
High. A crisp negative answer to a known conjecture, plus nontrivial algorithmic consequences and a strict-saddle non-convergence result tailored to first-order EM.

Significance
High. This paper resets expectations about the likelihood landscape for GMMs and the reliability of EM with random restarts. It will influence both theory (landscape analysis, initialization design) and practice (discouraging naive EM initializations; encouraging methods-of-moments pre-initialization).

Pros
- Resolves an open conjecture with a simple, compelling counterexample.
- Strong, quantitative algorithmic consequences (exponential failure probability).
- Clarifies the role of saddles vs. local maxima for EM-like methods.
- Likely to stimulate follow-up work on initialization and landscape shaping.

Cons
- No experiments; practical impact would be clearer with simulations.
- Finite-sample extensions are not formalized.
- Results are for 1D, equal-weight, isotropic mixtures; generalization left open.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add finite-sample theorems with explicit n scaling and separation requirements, and a coupling argument showing sample EM tracks population basins with high probability.
- Provide small-scale simulations corroborating the theory and comparing initializations (random vs. k-means++ vs. spectral/moments).
- Discuss extensions to unequal weights and d > 1; even partial progress or conjectures would be useful.
- Tighten constants and clarify “well-separated” thresholds in the main text; add a schematic for the recursive construction.
- Clean up references.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper provides a novel and important conceptual advance with clear algorithmic implications. Minor shortcomings (finite-sample formalization and lack of experiments) do not outweigh the contribution. Confidence: high.
