Summary
This paper studies the population likelihood landscape of uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with fixed variances. It resolves a conjecture by Srebro (2007) by constructing, for any M ≥ 3 (in d = 1), well-separated mixtures whose population log-likelihood has spurious local maxima that can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. Algorithmically, the paper shows that EM and first-order EM (gradient ascent on the likelihood) with a standard random-initialization scheme converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{−cM}, implying that exponentially many restarts are required to find a good solution under this scheme. Finally, it shows that first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points under random initialization, suggesting that bad local maxima—not saddles—are the dominant obstruction.

The work builds on and contrasts with prior analyses: convergence of EM under good initialization and separation (e.g., Dasgupta & Schulman 2007; Balakrishnan et al. 2015), computationally efficient alternatives (spectral/moment methods: Belkin & Sinha 2010; Moitra & Valiant 2010; Hsu & Kakade 2013), and recent nonconvex optimization theory about strict saddles (Lee et al. 2016; Panageas & Piliouras 2016; Pascanu et al. 2014). It also relates to identifiability and rate results in mixtures (Teicher 1963; Chen 1995; Ho & Nguyen 2015).

Strengths
- Clear, conceptual resolution to an open conjecture: existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population GMM likelihood for M ≥ 3, even with well-separated components.
- Strong algorithmic consequence: standard random initialization for EM/first-order EM leads to bad critical points with exponentially high probability, via an elegant hierarchical “trapping” construction.
- Clarifies the role of saddles: first-order EM avoids strict saddles almost surely, focusing the blame on bad local maxima.
- Well-motivated population analysis; arguments plausibly transfer to finite samples.
- Timely and impactful: cautions against naive local search for MLE in GMMs and motivates principled initialization.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope of constructions: The main negative example is in d = 1; it would help to explicitly state how/if these constructions embed into higher dimensions without degeneracy and whether separation scales with √d (the paper mentions well-separatedness generally).
- Finite-sample implications: While the authors state that results carry over via empirical process arguments, concrete statements (sample sizes, deviation bounds, stability of basins) are not provided. Even a sketch or theorem with rates would strengthen the practical message.
- Initialization model: The negative result targets “random from data” initialization. Many practitioners use k-means++, multi-stage seeding, splitting/merging, or spectral/tensor-based initializations. It would be useful to discuss whether the hierarchical trapping construction also defeats such schemes, or to provide conditions under which they succeed.
- Step-size dependencies: Theorem 3 holds for s ∈ (0, 1); could larger or adaptive step-sizes escape the bad basins? A brief treatment (or impossibility statement) would close a gap.
- Empirical illustration: While the work is primarily theoretical, a small synthetic experiment visualizing the 3-component 1D trap and the hierarchical construction’s effect on EM would be very helpful for readers.
- Clarity of assumptions for Theorem 4: The proof claims to avoid smoothness arguments typical of stable manifold approaches. Given that mixture likelihoods are real-analytic, clarifying the exact regularity assumptions used would improve trust.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The structural construction and the probabilistic argument for failure with probability 1 − e^{−cM} are convincing and novel. The local maximality argument using neighborhoods and boundary comparisons is clear in spirit. The saddle-avoidance result leverages modern nonconvex analysis appropriately.
- Experimental support is absent (not strictly necessary) but could help.
- Evaluation of limitations: The paper candidly discusses the likely success for M = 2 and lists open problems.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The intuition for the M = 3 construction is helpful; a figure would aid understanding.
- Minor notation issues and occasional reference formatting glitches can be cleaned up.
- Explicit constants/separation scales for “well-separated” in the constructions would improve readability.

Originality
- Provides the first rigorous counterexample to the “no bad local maxima” conjecture for GMMs in the population setting.
- The hierarchical initialization-trapping idea is neat and seems potentially reusable for other nonconvex likelihood landscapes.

Significance
- Important for theory and practice of mixture estimation: it tempers optimism about EM’s landscape even in benign, correctly specified, separated cases and motivates better initialization.
- The exponential failure probability result is particularly sobering and likely to influence algorithm design and benchmarking.

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves a notable open question with clean constructions.
- Offers strong, broadly relevant algorithmic implications.
- Technically solid and conceptually insightful; likely to spur follow-up work on initialization and landscape design.

Arguments against acceptance
- Primarily negative results; limited exploration of remedies beyond recommending moment-based seeds.
- Lack of finite-sample theorems and empirical demonstrations.
- Constructions are somewhat adversarial and 1D; broader demonstrations would strengthen generality.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an explicit finite-sample corollary with sample size requirements ensuring that the bad basins persist with high probability.
- Discuss or analyze k-means++/spectral initializations; characterize conditions under which they avoid the hierarchical traps.
- Add figures illustrating the 3-component local maximum and the hierarchical construction’s basin structure.
- Clarify dimensionality and separation scaling; give explicit constants.
- Elaborate on the regularity assumptions and stepsize requirements in Theorems 3–4; comment on line-search/annealing/tempered EM.

Recommendation: Accept
This paper delivers a clear, rigorous, and impactful negative result with meaningful algorithmic consequences, addresses an open problem, and clarifies the saddle-versus-local-maxima debate for GMMs. While extensions and empirical illustrations would enhance it, the core contributions merit acceptance.
