

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental question about Gaussian mixture models (GMMs): whether the population likelihood function has only global maxima (no "bad local maxima"), as conjectured by Srebro (2007). The authors provide a negative answer by constructing a 3-component GMM with arbitrarily bad local maxima, and demonstrate that standard EM and first-order EM algorithms with random initialization fail to find global maxima with high probability. The work makes a significant contribution to understanding the optimization landscape of GMMs and the limitations of local search methods.

**Strengths:**
- The paper resolves an important open question posed by Srebro (2007) with a concrete counterexample.
- The theoretical results are well-motivated and provide important algorithmic implications.
- The construction of the counterexample is clever and well-justified.
- The analysis of the EM algorithm's failure with random initialization is thorough and insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Terminology and notation inconsistencies:** The paper uses inconsistent terminology regarding the "population likelihood." The conjecture of Srebro (2007) is about the population likelihood (defined as the limit of the sample likelihood as n→∞), not "the likelihood function in the limit of infinite samples" as stated in the introduction. This subtle but important distinction is consistently blurred throughout the paper.

2. **Reference formatting errors:** Multiple references contain incorrect formatting (e.g., "Allman et al\.,? \Q2009\E" instead of "Allman et al. [2009]"). The paper also has a line break in "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)" that should read "The Conference."

3. **Ambiguous construction details:** The paper states that for M=3, "the distance between µ*1 and µ*2 is much smaller than the respective distances from µ*1 to µ*3, and from µ*2 to µ*3" but fails to quantify "much smaller." Without specifying how the separation scales with M or d, it's impossible to verify the well-separation condition (3) for their construction. The paper also lacks a precise definition of the "neighborhood" used to prove the existence of a local maximum in Theorem 1.

4. **Probability statement misinterpretation:** Theorem 2 states "P [ ∀t ≥ 0, L(µt) ≤ L(µ*)− Cgap ] ≥ 1− e−cM" but this probability is over the random initialization, not the algorithm's behavior. The statement suggests the EM algorithm always converges to a bad local maximum, which is not what the probability describes. The authors should clarify whether they mean "with high probability over the random initialization, the EM algorithm converges to a bad local maximum" or "with high probability over the random initialization, the EM algorithm converges to a point with log-likelihood ≤ L(µ*)− Cgap."

5. **Misattribution of related work:** The paper cites Améndola et al. (2015) as evidence that "configurations of the samples can be constructed which lead to the likelihood function having an unbounded number of local maxima." However, Améndola et al. (2015) is about the algebraic properties of maximum likelihood estimates, not about the number of local maxima. This misattribution undermines the paper's credibility.

6. **Inconsistent treatment of "well-separated":** The paper defines a GMM as well-separated if the distance between centers is Ω(√d) (equation 3), but in the construction for Theorem 1, it's unclear how the separation scales with M. For the counterexample to be valid, the separation must scale appropriately with M to maintain well-separation as M increases.

7. **Missing technical detail in Theorem 4:** The paper claims "the first-order EM algorithm cannot converge to strict saddle points" but doesn't explain why this doesn't apply to the standard EM algorithm, which the authors acknowledge is different from the first-order EM.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to understanding the optimization landscape of GMMs, the numerous technical inconsistencies, ambiguous constructions, and misattributions of related work significantly undermine the paper's credibility. These are not trivial issues but rather fundamental problems that would require substantial revision to resolve. The paper would benefit from a careful reworking of the terminology, precise definition of all technical concepts, and accurate citation of related work.

Given the importance of this topic to the machine learning community, I believe the paper has the potential to be a strong contribution if these issues are addressed. However, as currently written, the technical inconsistencies and ambiguities prevent me from recommending acceptance.

The paper's main contribution is important, but the execution contains too many minor but critical flaws that prevent me from recommending acceptance at NIPS. The authors should carefully revise the paper to address these issues before resubmission.
