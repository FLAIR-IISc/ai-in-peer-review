Summary
The paper studies the population log-likelihood landscape of uniformly weighted mixtures of isotropic Gaussians, with fixed covariances. The main claims:
- Theorem 1: For any M ≥ 3, there exist well-separated 1D mixtures with arbitrarily bad local maxima of the population log-likelihood, refuting a conjecture (Srebro, 2007) that no bad local maxima exist in this favorable setting.
- Theorem 2: With random initialization (drawing M initial means i.i.d. from the mixture), EM converges with probability at least 1 − e−cM to a suboptimal critical point whose likelihood is worse than optimal by at least a user-specified constant Cgap.
- Theorem 3: The same negative result holds for the first-order EM (gradient ascent on the population likelihood) with stepsize s ∈ (0, 1).
- Theorem 4: First-order EM avoids strict saddle points with probability one under random initialization and converges to a critical point, implying the failures above are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.

The construction intuition: arrange true means so two are close and far from the third, and show a neighborhood around an assignment that “double-covers” the far component and “single-covers” the close pair contains a suboptimal local maximum; extend to a recursive clustering structure to drive failure probability to e−Ω(M).

Relation to prior work: This complements analyses of EM’s local convergence under good initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and algorithmic alternatives (clustering/moments). The strict-saddle avoidance leverages recent gradient method theory (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas & Piliouras, 2016; Ge et al., 2015).

Strengths
- Conceptually important negative result: even in the most benign GMM setting (uniform weights, spherical, fixed variance), the population objective has bad local maxima for M ≥ 3.
- Algorithmic consequence is sharp: random initialization plus EM/gradient ascent fails with exponentially high probability for a natural hard instance.
- Clear intuition for the geometric construction; the recursive clustering idea to amplify failure probability is elegant.
- The strict-saddle result refines the diagnosis: failures are due to local maxima, not saddle proliferation.

Weaknesses and detailed issues
Technical correctness/precision:
- EM lower bound (Sec. 2.2): The inequality as written is wrong. The standard EM bound is log P(x|θ′) ≥ E_{Z∼P(·|x;θ)}[log P(x,Z|θ′)] + H(P(·|x;θ)); the second term should not depend on θ′. Your expression subtracts E log P(Z|x;θ′), which breaks the bound and the interpretation of Q(θ′|θ). Please correct and align with Wu (1983) or standard EM references (e.g., McLachlan & Krishnan).
- Existence of a local maximum (proof sketch of Thm 1): “Since the log-likelihood is bounded from above, this neighborhood must contain at least one maximum.” Boundedness is insufficient; you need continuity and compactness with a boundary that has strictly smaller function value than some interior point. Please explicitly define a compact neighborhood and invoke the extreme value theorem (and continuity of L) to make the argument rigorous.
- Norm notation ambiguity: You use “‖·‖2” both as the ℓ2-norm and seemingly as the squared norm (e.g., “−‖∇L(µ)‖2” called the “negative 2-norm” versus separation condition “‖µ∗i − µ∗j‖2 ≥ ξ”). If the separation is in Euclidean norm it should be ‖·‖2 ≥ ξ with ξ = Ω(√d); if it is squared norm, it should be ‖·‖22 ≥ ξ with ξ = Ω(d). Please disambiguate consistently throughout.
- Theorem 2 phrasing vs statement: The text claims EM/first-order EM “converges to a bad critical point,” yet the theorem statement only shows that for all t, L(µt) ≤ L(µ∗) − Cgap, which is a likelihood bound and does not imply convergence or criticality for EM. For first-order EM you later prove convergence to a critical point (Thm 4(a)); for EM, either add a convergence-to-stationary-point guarantee (under stated regularity) or soften the language.
- First-order EM stepsize (Thm 3 and Thm 4): Stating s ∈ (0,1) without a Lipschitz constant is nonstandard. Gradient ascent convergence/avoidance of strict saddles typically assumes s < 1/L where L is a Lipschitz constant of ∇L. Please specify smoothness of L and the dependence of s on L. The claim in Thm 4(a) that iterates converge for any spherical GMM with any s ∈ (0,1) seems too strong without such conditions.
- Population-to-sample transfer: You assert that results “carry over” via standard empirical process arguments. This is nontrivial; e.g., preserving presence and basins of bad local maxima requires uniform control on function and gradient and some topological stability. Please provide a formal finite-sample theorem with explicit n, d, M, separation, and confidence dependencies, or at least a precise sketch citing appropriate uniform convergence/stability results.
- Random initialization model: You sample M initial centers i.i.d. from GMM(µ∗), whereas practical initialization from the dataset is without replacement and often followed by k-means++ or pruning. While your intent is clear, please (i) clarify with/without replacement, and (ii) discuss whether the e−Ω(M) failure persists under common heuristics (k-means++, oversampling+pruning). Currently, the negative result may be seen as specialized to a contrived instance and a specific init.

Clarity/organization:
- Several typographical/indexing errors:
  - M-step formula uses the index i in the summation over samples: “µnew_i = ∑^n_i w_i(x`) x` / ∑^n_` w_i(x`)”. Should be ∑_{`=1}^n.
  - “one method is to to initialize...” (duplicated “to”).
  - “This result strongly suggests that that effective initialization...” (duplicated “that”).
  - “one needs repeat” → “one needs to repeat”.
- The header “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain.” appears mid-paragraph in the Introduction and should be removed.
- Figure 1 is referenced but absent. Either include it or remove the reference.
- “Well-separated” is defined as ξ = Ω(√d); please give constants in the theorems and ensure your 1D constructions satisfy the definition with explicit gaps.
- The recursive construction underlying Theorem 2 is only sketched. A precise, formal construction with separation scales, counts per cluster/level, and the exact combinatorial calculation would improve readability and verifiability.

Related work:
- Missing/appropriate references: Please add Wu (1983) on EM convergence. For EM monographs, McLachlan & Krishnan would help. Clarify how your negative results relate to other landscape results in mixture models beyond two-component settings.
- The reference list has numerous formatting issues: inconsistent years (“? \Q2015\E”), missing venues, duplicated punctuation, inconsistent author formats. Please standardize to NeurIPS style.

Significance/originality
The core negative results are significant and timely, clarifying that even the population GMM landscape can be treacherous for M ≥ 3 and that random initialization is provably inadequate on certain instances. The strict-saddle avoidance strengthens the message. The results will likely influence how practitioners initialize EM and how theorists think about mixture model landscapes. That said, the reliance on a tailored hard instance and the lack of a formal finite-sample transfer theorem limit immediate practical impact.

Pros
- Resolves an open conjecture negatively with a clean construction.
- Strong, quantitative algorithmic consequences for random initialization.
- Clear identification of bad local maxima (not saddles) as the culprit.

Cons
- Several technical imprecisions (EM bound, compactness argument, stepsize conditions).
- Population-only results; “standard arguments” claim is not formalized.
- Reference/typo/notation issues hinder clarity.
- Limited discussion of robustness to practical inits (e.g., k-means++).

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The contributions are important, but the manuscript needs significant polishing in rigor, presentation, and finite-sample formalization. I would be inclined to support acceptance after a careful revision addressing the issues above.

Confidence: High.
