

This paper addresses a long-standing conjecture by Srebro (2007) regarding the absence of "bad" local maxima in the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors provide a definitive negative answer to this conjecture by constructing counterexamples for M ≥ 3 components, demonstrating that arbitrarily bad local maxima exist in the population likelihood for well-separated uniform mixtures of unit-variance spherical Gaussians. The paper makes three main contributions: (1) a structural result showing the existence of bad local maxima, (2) algorithmic consequences demonstrating that EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability in M, and (3) a proof that first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points, indicating that the failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs for all main claims. The construction of the counterexample for M=3 provides clear geometric intuition, and the extension to M ≥ 3 components is well-justified. The proofs of Theorems 2 and 3, establishing the high probability of convergence to suboptimal critical points with random initialization, are particularly strong. Theorem 4 distinguishes between bad local maxima and saddle points through a novel analysis that avoids the smoothness assumptions required by prior work on gradient methods. The authors properly contextualize their results within relevant literature, including foundational work on GMM identifiability (Allman et al., 2009), EM convergence analysis (Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and recent advances in non-convex optimization (Lee et al., 2016).

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The background section establishes necessary definitions and context without unnecessary detail. The main results are stated precisely, with the geometric intuition for the M=3 case making the construction accessible. The connection between theoretical results and practical implications for EM algorithm usage is well-articulated, explaining why repeated random initialization is often necessary in practice. The manuscript properly references related work, including the Srebro conjecture and previous results on EM convergence for two-component mixtures.

The novelty is substantial. While Srebro's conjecture had been widely accepted, the authors provide the first definitive counterexample, resolving a significant open question in the field. The construction of bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 components is novel, as is the demonstration of the exponentially small success probability for random initialization schemes. The distinction between bad local maxima and saddle points in the GMM context represents a new theoretical contribution to the understanding of non-convex optimization for mixture models.

The significance of this work is high. It directly addresses a fundamental question about the behavior of likelihood surfaces for GMMs, with clear practical implications for the use of EM and related algorithms. The results explain why repeated random initialization is often necessary in practice, providing theoretical justification for a common empirical strategy. The work also opens new research directions, such as understanding the necessity of at least three mixture components for poor likelihood behavior, and reconciling the theoretical failure with the empirical success of EM-style algorithms.

Strengths: The paper resolves a major open question with definitive results, provides strong theoretical guarantees with rigorous proofs, clearly connects theory to practical implications, and properly situates the work within the literature. The counterexample construction is elegant and well-explained, and the algorithmic consequences are significant for practitioners.

Weaknesses: The focus on the idealized setting of uniform mixtures with fixed covariance (unit-variance spherical Gaussians) limits immediate applicability to all GMM settings. The paper does not fully resolve the case for M=2 components, though it provides evidence suggesting the conjecture might hold in this case. The extension to finite samples is mentioned but not elaborated in detail.

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of Gaussian mixture models and non-convex optimization. It resolves an important open question with rigorous results, provides clear algorithmic implications, and advances the state of the art in the analysis of likelihood surfaces for mixture models. The work is of high quality, clearly written, and makes a novel contribution that will be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers in machine learning.
