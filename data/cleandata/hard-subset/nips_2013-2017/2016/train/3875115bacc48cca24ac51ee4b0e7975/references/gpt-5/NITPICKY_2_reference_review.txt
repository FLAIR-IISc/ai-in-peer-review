Summary
The paper studies the population log-likelihood landscape of uniformly weighted, unit-variance spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). It gives a negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture by constructing, for any M ≥ 3, a 1D well-separated mixture with arbitrarily bad local maxima (Theorem 1). Algorithmically, the paper shows that EM and first-order EM (gradient ascent on the population likelihood) with random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-cM} on a worst-case instance (Theorems 2–3), using a recursive clustering construction. Finally, it argues that first-order EM does not converge to strict saddles with probability one (Theorem 4), suggesting bad local maxima, not saddles, are the main obstacle.

Strengths
- Resolves a natural and visible open question (Srebro 2007) by an explicit worst-case population-level counterexample, even under favorable modeling assumptions (equal weights, isotropic covariances, “well-separated”).
- The construction for exponentially small success probability under random initialization is conceptually clean (recursive cluster structure) and highly relevant for practical heuristics relying on random restarts.
- The insight that first-order EM avoids strict saddles aligns with the recent strict-saddle literature and clarifies the failure mode.
- The results are likely to influence thinking about initialization strategies and the limits of local search for GMMs.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness/precision
- Theorem 4(a) claims convergence of first-order EM iterates to a critical point with a fixed stepsize s ∈ (0,1). Gradient ascent convergence typically requires smoothness with a known Lipschitz constant L and stepsize s < 2/L (or diminishing stepsizes), or at least guarantees only that limit points are critical. The paper explicitly avoids a smoothness argument, yet population GMM likelihood is smooth; please either (i) invoke L-smoothness with an explicit bound on L (depending on M, separation, dimension) and restrict s accordingly, or (ii) weaken to “every limit point is critical.” As written, (a) appears overstated.
- The definition of “strict saddle” for maximization (Def. 1) as “∇²f has at least one strictly positive eigenvalue” is unconventional but acceptable if you clarify that both minima and saddles satisfy it in the maximization setting; otherwise, the statement (4b) could be misread.
- The “well-separated” condition is defined as pairwise distances ≥ ξ with ξ = Ω(√d). The main negative construction is in d = 1; for higher d the paper should explicitly state how to scale the construction to preserve the Ω(√d) separation (e.g., embed on a line and scale positions by √d), and confirm that the proofs are dimension-agnostic under this scaling.
- Theorems 2–3 quantify that with high probability “for all t ≥ 0, L(µ_t) ≤ L(µ*) − Cgap.” For EM, monotonic ascent and boundedness imply convergence; for first-order EM this requires step-size control. Please make the stepsize condition explicit (e.g., s ≤ 1/L) to ensure monotonicity or at least boundedness and existence of limit points.
- The extension from population to finite-sample is asserted to follow from “standard empirical process arguments,” but no rates or conditions (n vs d, separation, M) are provided. This is important for practical relevance; please supply a theorem with sample complexity and stability of the landscape under sampling.

Clarity and exposition
- Heavy reliance on appendices without enough intuition in the main text for the core geometric argument proving local maxima (Theorem 1). The “neighborhood and boundary” argument is reasonable but should be made more concrete (exact neighborhood definition, quantitative likelihood gap on the boundary).
- The recursive construction for Theorem 2 is sketched; a visual or a small worked example (say M = 8) would aid understanding.
- The treatment of first-order EM vs. EM is intermixed. Please separate assumptions and guarantees cleanly, especially stepsize restrictions and monotonicity.

Relation to prior work
- The paper positions itself relative to EM local analysis (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. 2015) and bad finite-sample landscapes (Améndola et al. 2015). It would help to note earlier negative landscape results in other models and strict-saddle literature (e.g., Jin et al., 2017; Ge et al., 2015 for minimization) to better contextualize Theorem 4.

Experimental/empirical
- No empirical illustrations are provided. While this is a theory paper, a small population-level visualization for M = 3 (Figure 1 is referenced but not shown) and a simple numerical EM trajectory would strengthen clarity.

Formatting/technical nits (numerous)
- EM M-step formula has an indexing typo: numerator uses “∑n i=1” but should sum over samples ℓ: µ_i^{new} = (∑_{ℓ=1}^n w_i(x_ℓ) x_ℓ)/(∑_{ℓ=1}^n w_i(x_ℓ)).
- In 2.1, the set of means is written “{µ∗i , . . . , µ∗M}”; should be {µ∗1, …, µ∗M}.
- Several grammatical issues: missing commas (“In practice, however”), agreement (“one needs repeat” → “one needs to repeat”), capitalization, and inconsistent use of italics/math fonts.
- Equation references and footnotes: misplaced “1” footnote marker in the introduction; “th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems” line fragment.
- References are in very poor shape: missing years, inconsistent venues, stray TeX markers (\Q2015\E), author truncations, punctuation, capitalization. This must be fixed for camera-ready.
- Figure 1 is referenced but absent.

Questions for authors
1. Precisely state separation and stepsize conditions under which Theorems 2–4 hold, including any Lipschitz constants or smoothness assumptions.
2. Provide a finite-sample theorem quantifying how many samples are needed to preserve the bad local maxima and the failure probability under random initialization.
3. Are the negative results robust to mild weight imbalances and/or slight variance misspecification?
4. Can you characterize the M- and separation-dependence of basin volumes for the good vs. bad local maxima?

Pros
- Resolves an open conjecture at the population level with clean constructions.
- Illuminates the failure mode of EM/gradient ascent under random initialization.
- Likely to influence both theory and practice regarding initialization strategies.

Cons
- Overstatement/lack of conditions for convergence of first-order EM (Theorem 4(a)).
- Missing finite-sample guarantees and dependence on problem parameters.
- Significant clarity and presentation issues; broken references; missing figure.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The core ideas are strong and potentially impactful, but the paper in its current form has technical gaps (notably Theorem 4(a) stepsize/convergence), lacks finite-sample validation, and contains numerous clarity/formatting problems. With these addressed, I would lean toward acceptance.
