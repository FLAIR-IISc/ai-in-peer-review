

# Peer Review: "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental question in Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) estimation: whether the population likelihood function for GMMs has "bad local maxima" (local maxima that are significantly worse than the global maximum). The authors resolve an open problem posed by Srebro (2007), who conjectured that for well-separated GMMs, any local maximum of the population likelihood is also a global maximum. The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture by constructing a 3-component GMM in 1D with arbitrarily bad local maxima. They further demonstrate that with random initialization, the EM algorithm and its first-order variant converge to these suboptimal points with exponentially high probability. The paper also establishes that the first-order EM algorithm avoids convergence to strict saddle points, suggesting that the failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

This work connects to several important threads in the literature: the identifiability of mixture models (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009), the statistical properties of GMMs (Chen, 1995; Ho and Nguyen, 2015), and the theoretical analysis of the EM algorithm (Dempster et al., 1977; Balakrishnan et al., 2015). It also relates to recent work on non-convex optimization (Loh and Wainwright, 2013; Lee et al., 2016).

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs of all main results. The authors provide a constructive proof of Theorem 1, carefully analyzing the population likelihood surface. The probabilistic arguments in Theorem 2 are compelling, demonstrating that random initialization fails with exponentially high probability. The connection to recent results on saddle points (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016) strengthens the theoretical foundation.

**Originality:** The paper resolves an important open question that has been discussed in the community (Srebro, 2007; Améndola et al., 2015). The construction of GMMs with arbitrarily bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 components is novel, and the proof technique (using recursive clustering structures) is particularly elegant. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work on GMMs (Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015) by focusing on the population likelihood and the negative consequences for optimization algorithms.

**Significance:** The findings have important practical implications for GMM estimation. They demonstrate that random initialization for EM-based methods is insufficient for good performance, even in well-separated settings, and that careful initialization (e.g., using moment-based methods) is critical. This provides theoretical justification for the empirical success of initialization schemes like those based on the method of moments (Moitra and Valiant, 2010; Hsu and Kakade, 2013).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, and the technical results are presented with sufficient detail. The geometric intuition for the construction in Theorem 1 is particularly helpful for understanding the main insight.

## Weaknesses

**Limited scope:** The results are limited to uniform mixtures of isotropic Gaussians with fixed variance. While this is a natural starting point, the authors do not address whether similar phenomena occur for non-uniform mixtures or when covariance matrices are estimated. This limitation is acknowledged in the paper, but it would be valuable to discuss how the results might extend to more general settings.

**Theoretical focus:** The paper focuses on population-level analysis rather than finite-sample behavior. While the authors note that their results extend to large finite samples via standard empirical process arguments, the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of how the probability of failure scales with sample size.

**Algorithmic perspective:** The paper focuses on EM and first-order EM, but does not discuss whether other optimization methods (e.g., stochastic gradient descent) might avoid the bad local maxima. This is a minor limitation given the paper's focus, but might be worth mentioning.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs and optimization algorithms for mixture models. The resolution of Srebro's conjecture is a major theoretical result, and the algorithmic implications are both surprising and important for practitioners. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a fundamental question in the field.

The main limitation is the restricted scope to uniform mixtures of isotropic Gaussians, but this is a reasonable starting point for the theoretical analysis. The results have clear implications for practical GMM estimation, suggesting that random initialization is insufficient and that careful initialization schemes are essential.

**Recommendation: Accept**

## Final Comments

The paper makes a strong contribution to the understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces and the behavior of optimization algorithms. The results challenge the common intuition that well-separated GMMs are easy to optimize, and provide important theoretical justification for careful initialization schemes in practice. The writing is clear, the proofs are rigorous, and the results are significant for both theory and practice.

The authors should consider briefly discussing how their results might extend to more general GMMs (e.g., with non-uniform weights or non-isotropic covariances) in the conclusion. Additionally, a brief mention of how their results relate to the finite-sample behavior would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of a fundamental problem in statistical learning. It is well-suited for publication at NIPS.
