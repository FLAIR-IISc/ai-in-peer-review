- Paper constructs a 3-component 1D GMM counterexample showing arbitrarily bad population log-likelihood local maxima, refuting Srebro (2007)'s conjecture.
- The construction generalizes to M≥3 and has a clear geometric explanation for M=3.
- The authors prove EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to these bad local maxima with probability exponentially close to 1 in M.
- Theoretical results are technically rigorous with complete proofs and careful distinction between local maxima and saddle points.
- Theorem 4 separates saddle-point issues from true local maxima, clarifying why local search methods can fail.
- The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and accessible while providing sufficient mathematical detail.
- This work is original, resolving a long-standing open question and extending beyond finite-sample counterexamples to the population setting.
- The algorithmic consequences are novel, particularly the exponential failure probability of random initialization.
- The findings are significant for practice, explaining why standard initialization can fail and motivating more sophisticated methods (e.g., method of moments).
- Strengths: resolves an open problem, links structural and algorithmic results, gives precise probabilistic guarantees, and connects to non-convex optimization literature.
- Weaknesses: focuses only on equally-weighted, isotropic GMMs with fixed variance and does not explore extensions to more general settings.
- Weaknesses: Theorem 4's proof depends on properties of first-order EM that may not hold for standard EM.
- Weaknesses: does not establish how common the constructed bad local maxima are in practical datasets.
- Recommendation: strong accept — important, rigorous, and practically relevant contribution.
- Future work: open question whether three components are necessary for bad local maxima, delineating the boundary of well-behaved GMM likelihood surfaces.
