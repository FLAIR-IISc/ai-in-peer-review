Summary
The paper studies the landscape of the (population) log-likelihood for uniformly weighted mixtures of isotropic Gaussians and the algorithmic behavior of EM and first-order EM under random initialization. It resolves a conjecture of Srebro (2007) by constructing, for any M ≥ 3, a 1D, well-separated, uniform spherical GMM whose population likelihood has spurious local maxima with arbitrarily large suboptimality gap relative to the global optimum. Algorithmically, it shows that EM and first-order EM (with constant step size s ∈ (0,1)) initialized by sampling centers from the mixture converge to a suboptimal critical point with probability at least 1 − e−cM, unless one performs exponentially many random restarts. Finally, it proves that first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, indicating that failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points. The authors give constructive proofs and outline how the population results transfer to large-sample regimes via standard arguments.

Relation to prior work
The work complements analyses of EM and variants that either assume good initialization or strong separation (e.g., Dasgupta–Schulman 2007; Balakrishnan et al. 2015) and algorithmic approaches based on moments/spectral methods (Moitra–Valiant 2010; Hsu–Kakade 2013). It directly refutes Srebro’s conjecture about benign landscapes for uniform spherical GMMs in the population limit, a conjecture that, if true, would have justified EM asymptotically. The saddle-point result leverages recent theory on gradient methods avoiding strict saddles (Lee et al. 2016; Panageas–Piliouras 2016), applied here to first-order EM.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The statements are precise and the high-level proof ideas are plausible. The construction for bad local optima (coalescing two true centers while duplicating another) is intuitive, and the recursive clustering argument underpinning the e−Ω(M) failure probability is convincing. The strict-saddle avoidance for first-order EM is well aligned with the cited dynamical systems results, and the paper emphasizes that extending this to EM would require additional machinery.
- Rigor and completeness: The core claims are theoretical and appear to be fully proved in the appendices. The authors carefully state assumptions (uniform weights, identity covariance, population setting, step size range).
- Limitations acknowledged: The authors clearly state that the two-component case remains open and that failure is established under a particular, but common, random initialization scheme. They also note the population-to-sample transfer.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear and well-organized: background, main theorems, and proof sketches are accessible. The motivating 2-component visualization is helpful. Some details would benefit from sharpening:
  - Precisely quantify “well-separated” for each theorem (e.g., the ξ in terms of d and M for the constructions).
  - Be explicit about how constants in the e−cM bounds arise from the recursive construction.
  - The references section has numerous formatting issues; please fix for readability.
- Including a small synthetic experiment that illustrates convergence to suboptimal solutions under random initialization would help readers internalize the constructions, even though the contributions are theoretical.

Originality
- The paper provides a clear negative resolution of a recognized open problem and nontrivial algorithmic consequences. The recursive construction driving the exponentially small success probability with random initialization is novel (to my knowledge). The strict-saddle avoidance result for first-order EM in this context is also a useful clarification.

Significance
- Conceptual impact: This work changes how we think about the GMM likelihood landscape in a canonical, “benign” setting, and it tempers optimism about EM with naive random initialization, even in the population limit.
- Practical implications: While the constructed instances are adversarial, the results rigorously justify the community’s emphasis on careful initialization and alternative estimators (e.g., method-of-moments, spectral, or k-means++-style seeding).
- Scope: The results are strongest in the population, uniform-weight, identity-covariance setting. The lack of experiments is acceptable for a theory paper; still, more discussion of finite-sample constants and dimension dependence would increase practical relevance.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope of assumptions: The landscape result is shown in d = 1 for M ≥ 3. It would be helpful to discuss whether analogous constructions extend cleanly to higher dimensions and to what separation. The paper focuses on identity covariance and fixed scale to avoid degeneracy; a brief discussion of how the phenomena change with unknown covariances would be valuable.
- Initialization model: The negative algorithmic results hinge on sampling initial centers from the mixture. Many practical pipelines use k-means++, splitting heuristics, or method-of-moments-based initializations. Can the recursive failure construction be adapted to defeat k-means++ seeding, or can the authors clarify which commonly used heuristics are provably safe/unsafe?
- Quantitative aspects: Provide more explicit constants or scaling for the required separation and the gap Cgap in Theorem 1; and outline the sample size needed for the empirical likelihood to inherit the same bad local maxima with high probability.
- Two-component case: Since this is the most practically relevant special case, any partial progress or additional intuition beyond Figure 1 would be appreciated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a brief empirical illustration (even in 1D/2D) showing EM converging to the constructed bad local maxima under random seeding.
- Clarify dimension and separation requirements in each theorem.
- Discuss whether stronger step sizes for first-order EM can escape the bad maxima (e.g., nonconstant schedules) or whether the constructions remain robust.
- Tighten and correct the references.

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves a notable open question; strong theoretical contribution.
- Clear algorithmic consequences with sharp probability bounds.
- Well written and likely to influence future work on initialization and nonconvex landscapes.

Arguments against acceptance
- Negative results in somewhat specialized setting; limited empirical validation.
- Algorithmic failure relies on specific random initialization; unclear implications for more sophisticated initializations.

Overall recommendation
Accept. This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes an original and significant contribution by characterizing the nonconvex landscape of GMM likelihoods and the failure modes of EM with random initialization, with clear implications for practice and future research.
