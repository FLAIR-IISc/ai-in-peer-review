

# Review: Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models

This paper addresses a long-standing open question in the theory of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) regarding the structure of the likelihood function. Specifically, it provides a negative answer to Srebro's (2007) conjecture that for well-separated GMMs, all local maxima of the population likelihood are global maxima. The authors construct a counterexample with M=3 well-separated unit-variance spherical Gaussians in one dimension that exhibits arbitrarily bad local maxima, and demonstrate that random initialization for both the EM algorithm and its first-order variant fails with exponentially high probability.

## Quality

The paper makes strong theoretical contributions with rigorous proofs that address a fundamental question in mixture model theory. The construction of the counterexample (Theorem 1) is elegant and well-justified, providing a concrete demonstration that Srebro's conjecture is false. The algorithmic results (Theorems 2-4) are particularly compelling, showing that even in the favorable setting of well-separated, equally weighted, isotropic Gaussians, random initialization for EM fails with exponentially high probability as M grows. 

The authors carefully consider the limitations of their results, noting that their counterexample requires M ≥ 3 components and that the failure of random initialization is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points (Theorem 4). The paper also correctly notes that their results apply to the population setting but can be extended to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments. The technical arguments are sound, with appropriate connections to recent results on optimization (e.g., Lee et al., 2016) for the saddle point analysis.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from the introduction of the problem to the main results. The geometric intuition for the counterexample (for M=3) is particularly helpful for understanding the construction. The formal statements of the theorems are precise, and the proofs are well-structured (with technical details deferred to the appendix). 

The paper effectively distinguishes its contributions from related work, particularly from Balakrishnan et al. (2015) who studied local convergence for two-component GMMs. The authors correctly acknowledge that the two-component case may still satisfy Srebro's conjecture, which is an important nuance. The discussion of the first-order EM algorithm and its relation to gradient ascent is clear and well-motivated.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution to a long-standing open problem in mixture model theory. Srebro's conjecture had been discussed in the literature for over a decade (with a 2007 conference paper), and the authors provide a definitive answer. The construction of the counterexample with M=3 components is novel and non-trivial, as the authors correctly note that the two-component case appears to satisfy the conjecture.

The algorithmic consequences are also original and important: the authors demonstrate that even in a well-specified, well-separated setting, random initialization for EM fails with exponentially high probability, which is a strong theoretical justification for why careful initialization (e.g., using method of moments) is necessary in practice. This connects to recent work on optimization for non-convex problems (e.g., Loh and Wainwright, 2013), but provides a concrete example in the GMM context.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. The paper resolves an important theoretical question that has implications for the practical use of the EM algorithm for GMMs. The finding that random initialization fails with exponentially high probability (Theorem 2) provides a theoretical explanation for why practitioners often need to use multiple restarts or more sophisticated initialization schemes. This is particularly relevant given that EM is the dominant practical method for GMM estimation.

The paper also has important implications for the broader field of non-convex optimization. It provides a concrete example where bad local maxima (not just saddle points) are the main obstacle to convergence, which challenges the prevailing view (e.g., Pascanu et al., 2014) that saddle points are the primary challenge in high-dimensional optimization.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of Gaussian mixture models. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses an important open question. The results are both surprising and well-justified, with careful consideration of the limitations and connections to related work.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, as it addresses fundamental theoretical questions in machine learning with implications for a widely used algorithm. The results are significant for both theoretical understanding and practical applications of mixture models.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Resolves a long-standing open question in mixture model theory
- Provides rigorous counterexample to Srebro's conjecture
- Demonstrates important algorithmic consequences for EM and first-order EM
- Carefully distinguishes between local maxima and saddle points
- Well-written with clear geometric intuition for the counterexample

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper focuses on the population setting; while the authors note finite-sample extensions are possible, a more detailed discussion of how the results scale to finite samples would be valuable
- The construction is specific to one-dimensional Gaussians; while the authors note the construction generalizes, a brief discussion of higher-dimensional implications would strengthen the paper

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of Gaussian mixture models and the behavior of the EM algorithm. It resolves an important open question with rigorous results, and the algorithmic implications are substantial. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a topic of broad interest in machine learning.

I recommend acceptance. The paper meets all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level, and the results have important implications for both theory and practice. The counterexample to Srebro's conjecture is particularly compelling, and the demonstration that random initialization fails with exponentially high probability provides a strong theoretical justification for more careful initialization strategies in practice.

The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of how the results might extend to higher-dimensional settings, but this is not essential to the core contribution. The work represents a substantial advance in understanding the non-convex optimization landscape of GMMs, and I believe it will be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
