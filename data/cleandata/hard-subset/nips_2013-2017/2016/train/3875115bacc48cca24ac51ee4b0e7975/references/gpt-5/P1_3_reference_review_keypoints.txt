- Main result: Constructs 1D, well-separated GMMs for any M ≥ 3 whose population likelihood has arbitrarily bad suboptimal local maxima, resolving Srebro (2007).
- Algorithmic consequence: EM and first-order EM with i.i.d. random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e^{-cM}.
- Construction idea: Uses a hierarchical clustering of true centers to trap local search unless an exponentially unlikely initialization event occurs.
- Dynamics insight: First-order EM avoids strict saddles (w.p.1 under random init), implying bad local maxima — not saddles — cause failures.
- Relation to prior work: Complements strong-initialization analyses and contrasts with benign-landscape results in other nonconvex problems.
- Contrast with alternative methods: Shows random-restart EM may need exponentially many restarts compared to clustering/moment-based approaches that succeed under separation.
- Strength: Clear, simple explicit construction (already in 1D, M=3) that settles an open conjecture.
- Strength: Strong quantitative algorithmic negative results with exponential-in-M failure bounds via recursive instances.
- Strength: Clean separation of failure source (bad maxima) and good exposition with intuitive explanations.
- Strength: Results likely transfer to finite samples via standard concentration arguments.
- Weakness: Quantitative separation unclear — need explicit separation as function of M and desired likelihood gap, and whether constant separation suffices in 1D.
- Weakness: Finite-sample implications are claimed but lack explicit sample-size requirements (n as function of M, gap, separation).
- Weakness: Initialization model limited to i.i.d. sampling — sensitivity to other initializations (k-means++, splitting, balanced seeding) is unclear.
- Weakness: Step-size questions — does failure persist under line-search, adaptive/diminishing steps, and do EM proper results mirror first-order EM strict-saddle avoidance?
- Weakness: Robustness to model misspecification (unequal weights, variance mismatch) and the special M=2 case remain open.
- Quality: Technical arguments are plausible and compelling; sharper constants and a cleaner counting argument in the main text would improve verifiability.
- Completeness: Population-level theory is self-contained but would benefit from a finite-sample corollary/theorem.
- Clarity: Well written and organized; recommend moving a distilled hierarchical-construction statement/figure into main text and consistently defining “bad critical point.”
- Originality: Novel and significant — explicit well-separated counterexamples and exponential failure probability are original contributions.
- Significance: High — reshapes understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes and cautions against naive random-restart EM.
- Pros for acceptance: Resolves an open problem, strong negative algorithmic results, clear writing, and broad NIPS relevance.
- Cons against acceptance: Deferred finite-sample/quantitative details, limited discussion of alternative inits and robustness, and no empirical illustrations.
- Suggestions: Add a finite-sample corollary with explicit n scaling, quantify separations vs. M and gap, analyze k-means++ on hard instances, and clarify strict-saddle scope for EM and adaptive step sizes.
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept.
