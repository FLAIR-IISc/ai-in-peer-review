This paper tackles a fundamental question regarding the landscape of the likelihood function in Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs): are there bad local optima? The authors provide a negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture, demonstrating the existence of such optima even in well-separated mixtures. Furthermore, they show that the ubiquitous EM algorithm, with a reasonable random initialization, converges to these suboptimal solutions with high probability. This is a significant contribution, as it provides theoretical grounding for the often-observed sensitivity of EM to initialization.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core result—proving the existence of bad local optima in a well-defined GMM setting—is genuinely novel and addresses a long-standing question.  This goes beyond incremental improvements; it challenges a prevailing intuition about the behavior of likelihood surfaces.
* **Theoretical Rigor:**  The proofs appear rigorous and leverage clever constructions to demonstrate the existence of problematic local optima and analyze the convergence properties of EM. The connection to recent work on gradient descent and the avoidance of saddle point arguments are also strong.
* **Algorithmic Implications:** The analysis of EM's convergence under random initialization has direct practical implications. The exponential dependence on the number of components (M) highlights the need for more sophisticated initialization strategies.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper is well-situated within the existing literature on GMMs, EM, and non-convex optimization. The authors diligently cite relevant work and position their contributions accordingly.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized, presenting the concepts and results in a logical manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Specific Setting:** The results are currently restricted to uniformly weighted, isotropic GMMs. While this simplifies the analysis, it raises questions about the generalizability to more complex GMMs (e.g., varying weights, full covariance matrices).  The authors acknowledge this, but exploring the limitations is crucial.
* **Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical results are compelling, the lack of empirical validation weakens the overall impact. Demonstrating the existence of bad local optima and EM's convergence to them through simulations would strengthen the claims significantly. The figure in the introduction is helpful for the 2-component case, but doesn’t scale to higher dimensions/more components.
* **Computational Cost of Avoiding Bad Optima:** The paper states that exponentially many runs of the EM algorithm are needed to find a good solution.  While true, it doesn't offer any *practical* alternatives beyond more intelligent initialization. Discussing potential avenues for escaping these bad local optima (e.g., stochastic gradient methods, different initialization schemes) would be valuable.
* **Technical Density:** Some sections are quite technically dense, potentially making them inaccessible to readers less familiar with the intricacies of GMM theory and optimization.  While rigor is important, streamlining some of the derivations could improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a fundamentally new understanding of the likelihood landscape of GMMs, resolving a long-standing open problem.
* The theoretical results are strong and rigorously proven.
* The implications for the widely-used EM algorithm are significant and raise important practical considerations.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and appropriately situated within the existing literature.
* The novelty and potential impact outweigh the limitations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The results are currently limited to a specific GMM setting.
* The lack of empirical validation weakens the claims.
* The exponential dependence on M, while theoretically interesting, doesn't offer immediate practical solutions.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a significant new insight into the theoretical properties of Gaussian Mixture Models.  The novelty of the results and the rigorous analysis are particularly commendable. While the limitations regarding the specific GMM setting and the lack of empirical validation are concerns, I believe that the fundamental contribution of this work outweighs these weaknesses. I am willing to overlook the lack of extensive experimentation because the theoretical insight is so strong.  I **recommend acceptance** with a request for the authors to address the following in a camera-ready version:

1. **Discuss the limitations:**  More thoroughly discuss the implications of restricting the analysis to uniformly weighted, isotropic GMMs. What are the expected effects when these assumptions are relaxed?
2. **Add some simulation results:** Even simple simulations illustrating the existence of bad local optima and EM's convergence behavior would greatly strengthen the paper.
3. **Briefly discuss potential mitigation strategies:** Offer a few potential ideas for escaping these bad local optima in practice, even if they are just preliminary suggestions.
