This paper investigates the local optima landscape of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), a fundamental problem in statistical estimation and machine learning. The authors demonstrate, counter to a conjecture by Srebro, that bad local optima *do* exist, even for well-separated Gaussians. They further show that standard initialization schemes, like random selection from the data, can lead the EM algorithm to converge to these suboptimal solutions with high probability. The theoretical results are supported by a clear illustration with a two-dimensional example.

The paper is technically sound and addresses an important, long-standing question in the field. The mathematical proofs appear rigorous, and the connection to existing work (Teicher, Chen, Dasgupta & Schulman, Balakrishnan et al., Srebro) is well-established. The analysis of the EM algorithm’s convergence behavior given random initialization is particularly insightful. The authors convincingly argue that naive application of local search methods can be unreliable without careful initialization.

However, while the results are mathematically interesting, the practical significance isn’t fully convincing. The construction demonstrating bad local optima relies on a specific, somewhat contrived, configuration of three Gaussians. The extension of this result to a general setting, or more realistic data distributions, isn't immediately clear. While the theoretical analysis of EM’s convergence with random initialization is strong, the constants involved (and thus the practical impact of the exponential failure rate) aren't well-characterized. The paper doesn’t offer concrete guidance on *how* to achieve more robust initialization schemes—it primarily points out the failings of a common practice. The focus on isotropic Gaussians and fixed covariance parameters is a significant limitation; real-world GMM estimation often involves estimating both location and shape, where the likelihood surface is considerably more complex and potentially more prone to difficulties.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, but the density of notation in the proofs can be challenging. While the explanations are detailed, readers less familiar with the theoretical underpinnings of mixture models might struggle to follow all the arguments. The organization is logical, building from background material to the main results and their implications. The related work section is comprehensive.

The originality of the work is high. Demonstrating the existence of bad local optima in the population likelihood function, resolving Srebro’s conjecture, is a significant contribution. The analysis of random initialization and its consequences for EM convergence is also novel and valuable.

The significance of the paper is moderate. While the results highlight a fundamental limitation of local search methods for GMM estimation, they don't immediately translate into a practical solution. The theoretical insights are more valuable to researchers studying optimization landscapes and algorithm design than to practitioners seeking a more reliable GMM estimator. The results underscore the need for better initialization strategies, but the paper doesn’t provide one.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Resolves a long-standing open question.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear proofs.
*   Novel insights into the behavior of EM with random initialization.
*   Addresses a fundamental problem in statistical estimation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The existence of bad local optima is demonstrated for a specific, potentially contrived, case.
*   The practical impact of the exponential failure rate of EM with random initialization is unclear.
*   The paper doesn't offer a solution to the identified problem.
*   The focus on isotropic Gaussians is a limiting factor.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid theoretical contribution to the field, but falls short of being a truly impactful NIPS paper. The results are interesting and mathematically sound, but their practical implications are limited. The paper feels more like a foundational result that opens up avenues for future research, rather than a self-contained contribution that significantly advances the state of the art.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. While the paper is not fundamentally flawed, its limited practical relevance and the lack of concrete solutions make it a less compelling fit for NIPS. I encourage the authors to consider focusing future work on identifying robust initialization strategies that mitigate the risk of converging to bad local optima.
