## Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the landscape of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), specifically focusing on the existence of bad local maxima. Challenging a conjecture by Srebro (2007), the authors demonstrate the existence of such maxima even for well-separated Gaussian components.  They further show that standard initialization schemes for the EM algorithm (and its first-order variant) are likely to converge to these suboptimal points, highlighting a fundamental issue with practical GMM estimation. The work combines theoretical analysis with a clear exposition of the problem and its implications.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a foundational question about the behavior of a widely used statistical model.  Understanding the likelihood surface of GMMs is crucial for developing robust and reliable estimation algorithms.
* **Negative Result with Impact:**  Disproving Srebro’s conjecture is a significant contribution. It establishes that even in relatively well-behaved settings (isotropic Gaussians, uniform weights), local optima can be problematic.
* **Algorithmic Implications:** The demonstration that random initialization, a common practice, can lead to convergence to bad local optima is a practical and concerning result. This motivates the need for more sophisticated initialization or alternative algorithms.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The proofs appear rigorous and well-structured (although a detailed assessment requires more time). The introduction of techniques to analyze the population log-likelihood is promising for future work.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the key results are presented in a concise and accessible manner.
* **Connections to Existing Work:**  The paper effectively places its contribution within the broader context of GMM estimation, referencing relevant literature on identifiability, convergence rates, and the EM algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:** The analysis is restricted to uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussian mixtures. While this simplifies the problem and allows for rigorous results, it's unclear how much of these findings generalize to more realistic scenarios (varying weights, non-diagonal covariance matrices).  The authors acknowledge this, but a discussion of potential extensions would be beneficial.
* **Practicality of the Constructed Bad Local Maximum:** While theoretically interesting, the constructed bad local maximum feels somewhat contrived. It would be valuable to explore whether similar bad local maxima arise in more natural data distributions.  How representative is this construction of the general landscape?
* **Exponential Dependence on M:** The exponential dependence of the failure probability on the number of components *M* in Theorem 2 is quite strong.  While mathematically precise, it might make the result less immediately relevant for practical applications with moderate *M*.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:**  The paper is primarily theoretical. While the theoretical results are compelling, some supporting empirical evidence, even on simulated data, could strengthen the conclusions and provide intuition.  Visualizations of the likelihood surface in higher dimensions would be insightful.




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported. The rigorous proofs and careful analysis demonstrate a high level of scientific quality.
* **Clarity (8/10):** The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The notation is standard and the explanations are easy to follow.  Some parts could benefit from further elaboration.
* **Originality (9/10):** Disproving a long-standing conjecture and providing a rigorous analysis of the likelihood surface is highly original.
* **Significance (8/10):** The results are important for the GMM estimation community, particularly those interested in the theoretical foundations of the EM algorithm.  The algorithmic implications are significant and could motivate research into more robust initialization schemes.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Disproves a key conjecture regarding the likelihood surface of GMMs.
* Provides a theoretical justification for the difficulties encountered when using standard algorithms for GMM estimation.
* Offers a rigorous analysis with strong theoretical foundations.
* Clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The results are limited to a specific GMM configuration (uniformly weighted, isotropic).
* The constructed bad local maximum may not be representative of typical scenarios.
* Lack of empirical validation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and significant paper.  The theoretical results are compelling and address a fundamental question in the field of GMM estimation.  While the limitations regarding the scope and practical relevance of the constructed bad local maximum are worth noting, the overall contribution is substantial. 

**Recommendation:** **Strong Accept**.  I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016. I believe it will be a valuable contribution to the literature and stimulate further research in this area.  I encourage the authors to consider adding some empirical validation and a discussion of potential extensions to more general GMM configurations in a future revision.
