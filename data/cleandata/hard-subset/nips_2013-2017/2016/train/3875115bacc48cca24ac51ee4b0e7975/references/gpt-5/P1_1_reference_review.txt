Summary
The paper studies the population log-likelihood landscape of uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and the algorithmic behavior of EM and first-order EM under random initialization. It resolves a conjecture (Srebro, 2007) by constructing, for any M ≥ 3, a 1D well-separated mixture whose population likelihood has spurious local maxima that are arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. It then shows that EM and first-order EM (gradient ascent on the population likelihood) with the practical random-initialization scheme (sampling initial centers i.i.d. from the mixture) converge to such suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − exp(−cM), unless one performs an exponential number of restarts. Finally, leveraging ideas from gradient dynamics, it proves that first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that bad local maxima—not saddles—drive the failure.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to classical identifiability and asymptotic theory of mixtures (Teicher; Chen; Ghosal & van der Vaart), analyses of EM and its local convergence (Dasgupta & Schulman; Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and algorithmic alternatives via clustering/moments (Arora et al.; Moitra & Valiant; Hsu & Kakade). It contrasts with “benign nonconvexity” results elsewhere (e.g., Loh & Wainwright; Wang et al.), and builds on recent insights that gradient methods avoid strict saddles (Lee et al., Panageas & Piliouras). To my knowledge, the negative resolution of Srebro’s conjecture in this very favorable, population setting is new and significant.

Strengths
- Clear, sharp structural result: existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood for well-separated, uniform, spherical GMMs with M ≥ 3 in 1D.
- Strong algorithmic consequence: with natural random initialization, EM and gradient ascent fail with exponentially high probability; construction with recursive clustering is elegant and informative.
- Conceptual clarity on saddles vs local maxima: first-order EM avoids strict saddles, so failures are due to suboptimal local optima.
- Relevance: EM is ubiquitous; these results caution against naive random restarts and motivate careful initialization or moment-based pilot estimates.
- Proof techniques may generalize to other latent variable likelihoods and nonconvex objectives.

Weaknesses and questions
- Dimensionality of the counterexample: Theorem 1 is stated in d = 1. While one can embed 1D pathologies into higher dimensions, it would be useful to state explicitly whether the construction extends to d > 1 (e.g., by placing means on a line with identity covariance) and whether separation scaling remains Ω(√d).
- “Well-separated” vs pathological clustering: The negative examples rely on hierarchical grouping at multiple scales. While still well-separated, they may be atypical in practice. A discussion/experiment quantifying how often such pathologies arise under more “generic” configurations would strengthen the practical message.
- Sample vs population: The results are at the population level; the claim that they “carry over” via empirical process arguments is plausible, but concrete finite-sample statements (uniform convergence bounds specifying n as a function of M, separation, and d) would be valuable.
- First-order EM convergence: Part (a) of Theorem 4 asserts convergence to a critical point for constant step size s ∈ (0,1). Gradient ascent need not converge without additional assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz gradient and diminishing step sizes or Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz-type conditions). The paper says it avoids smoothness arguments; more detail is needed to justify monotonicity and convergence, or to state the precise regularity used by the dynamical systems argument.
- Scope of initialization: The negative results focus on i.i.d. draws from the mixture or uniform-from-data. Practical initializations (e.g., k-means++, farthest-first, or moment-based pilots) can materially change the outcome. A brief empirical comparison or theorem indicating that some practical nonrandom initialization can avoid the exponential failure would contextualize the impact.
- No empirical illustrations: Small simulations/visualizations (even in 1D/2D) demonstrating the bad local maxima and the trapping behavior of EM would greatly aid intuition.
- References formatting and minor issues: Several citations are malformed; please fix. Define “bad local maximum” more formally the first time it appears, and clarify the step-size requirements in Theorem 3/4.

Quality
- The core theorems are plausible and appear technically sound from the outlines provided. The recursive construction for exponentially small success probability is convincing. Proof details are deferred; the paper would benefit from sketching key lemmas (e.g., the trapping lemma showing centers remain in their initial cluster).
- Care is taken to separate EM vs first-order EM arguments and to justify the saddle-point claim. Clarify the regularity assumptions needed for convergence in Theorem 4.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and well-organized, with good intuition (e.g., the M=3 construction). Add a figure or schematic for the hierarchical instance used in Theorem 2.
- Clearly delineate assumptions: equal weights, unit covariance, separation definition, step-size ranges, and exact random init.
- Clean up references and notation (some typos; ensure consistent use of separation scaling, and that µ denotes the vector of centers vs a single component).

Originality
- High. The negative resolution of Srebro’s conjecture in this setting and the exponential failure probability for EM with random initialization seem new and important.

Significance
- High. The results directly impact how practitioners think about EM for GMMs, emphasizing the necessity of informed initialization. They also inform broader discussions on nonconvex landscapes and the limits of local search.

Pros for acceptance
- Resolves a well-known open conjecture; strong, general negative algorithmic consequences.
- Clean theoretical framing and broad relevance.
- Techniques and insights likely to influence future work on nonconvex MLE landscapes.

Cons against acceptance
- Population-level focus without concrete finite-sample guarantees.
- No empirical demonstrations.
- Some technical claims (convergence of first-order EM) need clearer assumptions or proofs in the main text.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a brief finite-sample corollary with explicit rates.
- Add a figure and a small experiment illustrating bad local maxima and EM trapping.
- Clarify extension to d > 1 and the exact separation scaling.
- State key lemmas used in Theorems 2–4 in the main body.
- Discuss practical initializations (k-means++, spectral/moment pilots) and whether they can succeed on your hard instances.

Recommendation: Weak accept. Important theoretical contribution with clear message; adding finite-sample and empirical corroboration and clarifying some technical details would further strengthen the paper.

Reviewer confidence: High.
