**Summary**

This paper investigates the structure of the population log-likelihood surface for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). The primary contribution is a negative resolution to a conjecture by Srebro (2007), which posited that for uniformly weighted, isotropic GMMs, the population likelihood has no spurious local maxima. The authors construct a simple, well-separated GMM with M=3 components in d=1 for which the population likelihood possesses a local maximum whose value can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum.

Building on this structural result, the paper analyzes the algorithmic consequences for the widely used Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and a first-order variant (equivalent to gradient ascent). The authors show that when initialized by drawing centers from the true data-generating distribution (a common practice), both algorithms converge to a suboptimal critical point with a probability that is exponentially high in the number of components, M. Finally, leveraging recent results from the optimization literature, the paper argues that for the first-order EM algorithm, these failure points are indeed local maxima and not strict saddle points, as convergence to the latter occurs with probability zero.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper addresses a theoretically significant problem. The refutation of Srebro's conjecture is a notable result that deepens our understanding of the GMM likelihood landscape. The core theoretical claims, if correct, are strong and impactful. The construction of the GMM with a bad local maximum (sketched for M=3) is intuitive yet powerful. The subsequent analysis connecting this structural property to the high-probability failure of EM with random initialization provides a compelling, if pessimistic, narrative.

However, the paper suffers from a number of issues that detract from its quality:
1.  **Missing Proofs:** The paper repeatedly states that proofs are deferred to Section A and the appendices. However, no such sections are present in the submitted manuscript. This is a critical omission that makes it impossible to verify the central claims of the paper (Theorems 1-4). The review is therefore based on the claims and proof sketches, which cannot be fully assessed.
2.  **Unsupported Claims:** The authors claim in the conclusion that their results "carry over in a straightforward way, via standard empirical process arguments, to settings where a large finite sample is provided." This is a highly non-trivial extension. The geometry of the sample likelihood can be much wilder than its population counterpart (as the authors' own citation of Améndola et al. [2015] suggests), and a hand-wavy appeal to "standard arguments" is insufficient.
3.  **Technical Imprecision:**
    *   Theorem 4(a) claims that the first-order EM iterates (gradient ascent) converge to a critical point for any constant stepsize `s` in (0, 1). This is a very strong claim for a general non-concave function. Convergence guarantees for gradient ascent typically require either a sufficiently small step size or further assumptions on the objective function, which are not stated or verified here.
    *   The term "well-separated" is defined as having `||µ*_i - µ*_j||_2 >= Ω(sqrt(d))`, but the main construction for Theorem 1 is presented in `d=1`, where this scaling is less meaningful. The role of dimensionality in the results is not clearly articulated.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written, but clarity could be improved in several areas.
1.  The intuitive description of the bad local maximum for M=3 is helpful, but the wording is slightly ambiguous. Stating `µ2` and `µ3` are "placed at the true center `µ*3`" and then analyzing a "neighborhood of this configuration" is less precise than defining the candidate point `µ'` explicitly.
2.  The organization of the introduction is adequate, but the flow feels somewhat disjointed. The transition from general challenges in GMM estimation to the specific focus on Srebro's conjecture could be smoother.
3.  Equation (1) presents the Gaussian PDF `φ(x | µ, Σ)` with a transpose symbol `>` instead of `T`. While perhaps a minor typo, it indicates a lack of careful proofreading.
4.  The footnote on page 1, "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)," appears to be an unedited template artifact.

**Originality:**
The work is highly original. It directly confronts and resolves a known open problem in the field. While it builds upon a rich literature of GMM analysis and leverages recent advances in non-convex optimization theory, the core constructions and the synthesis of these ideas to analyze the failure modes of EM are novel and insightful.

**Significance:**
The results are significant and potentially have a broad impact. They provide a strong theoretical justification for the necessity of careful, non-random initialization for EM-type algorithms in the context of GMMs, even in asymptotically large sample settings and for well-behaved models. This challenges a common folk wisdom that with enough data, the likelihood landscape should be benign. The finding that bad local maxima, rather than saddle points, are the primary obstacle for gradient-based methods in this setting is a valuable contribution to the broader discussion on the challenges of non-convex optimization.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper resolves a long-standing and well-known conjecture about the GMM likelihood function.
2.  The algorithmic consequences presented are of high practical and theoretical importance, cautioning against the naive use of random initializations for EM.
3.  The main ideas are novel and provide a deeper understanding of a fundamental statistical model.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **The complete absence of proofs (Section A) is a fatal flaw for a theoretical paper of this nature.**
2.  The paper exhibits a notable lack of polish, suggesting a rushed preparation. The bibliography is improperly formatted to the point of being unreadable (e.g., `Allman et al\.,? \Q2009\E, Allman et al\.,`). This, combined with minor typos and template artifacts, undermines confidence in the care taken with the technical details.
3.  Several key technical claims (e.g., extension to finite samples, unconditional convergence of gradient ascent) are stated without adequate justification, appearing as overstatements.

**Decision:**
While the ideas and claimed results presented in this paper are of high quality and significance, the execution of the manuscript is severely lacking. The absence of proofs makes proper verification impossible, and the numerous formatting errors and instances of technical imprecision fall far short of the standards expected for a top-tier conference like NIPS. I must therefore recommend **rejection**. The paper has the potential to be a landmark contribution, but it requires a thorough revision to address the critical issues of proof inclusion, technical precision, and overall presentation.
