## Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the landscape of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), specifically addressing the conjecture by Srebro (2007) that, in the infinite sample limit, there are no "bad" local maxima. The authors demonstrate a counterexample – a 3-component GMM in 1D exhibiting bad local maxima – and then rigorously analyze the behavior of the EM and first-order EM algorithms initialized randomly from the data. They prove that both algorithms converge to a suboptimal critical point with high probability, necessitating an exponential number of restarts to achieve a reasonable likelihood.  They further show that the failure is likely due to the existence of bad local optima, rather than saddle points.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Theoretical Contribution:** The paper resolves a longstanding open question regarding the properties of GMM likelihood surfaces.  Demonstrating the existence of bad local optima, even in a well-behaved setting (isotropic Gaussians), is a significant theoretical result with practical implications.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The authors provide a thorough mathematical treatment, including proofs for the existence of bad local maxima and the convergence properties of EM algorithms.  The use of recent results from dynamical systems (Lee et al., 2016, Panageas and Piliouras, 2016) is particularly commendable.
* **Algorithmic Implications:** The practical consequences of the theoretical results are clearly articulated. The exponential restart requirement highlights the sensitivity of EM to initialization and underscores the need for more robust initialization schemes.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The introduction provides sufficient context and motivation, and the presentation of the results is clear.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately contextualizes its contributions within the broader literature on GMM estimation, optimization, and statistical learning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of the Counterexample:** While the counterexample effectively disproves Srebro’s conjecture, it is specific to a 1D GMM with 3 components.  While the authors claim the result generalizes, the construction is tailored to this specific case.  A broader understanding of conditions that lead to bad local maxima would be valuable.
* **Strong Assumptions:** The analysis focuses on uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussian mixtures. This simplification, while enabling the theoretical results, limits the direct applicability to more realistic scenarios where component weights and covariances vary.
* **Proof Complexity:** The proofs are quite involved and may be inaccessible to readers without a strong background in optimization and statistical theory. More intuitive explanations or intermediate steps could be beneficial.
* **Practical Evaluation Missing:** The paper is entirely theoretical.  While the authors discuss the practical implications, empirical validation demonstrating the high probability of convergence to bad local optima using the described random initialization on synthetic or real datasets would strengthen the claims.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  Excellent. The paper presents a technically sound and rigorous analysis of a fundamental problem in GMM estimation. The claims are well-supported by the proofs provided.  It's a complete piece of theoretical work.
* **Clarity:** Good. The writing is generally clear, though the proofs are dense.  More intuitive explanations and intermediate steps in the proofs would improve clarity.
* **Originality:** Excellent.  The result resolving Srebro’s conjecture is highly original. The analysis of EM's convergence properties under random initialization adds a valuable perspective.
* **Significance:** Excellent.  The results are significant because they challenge a long-held intuition about GMM estimation and highlight the crucial role of initialization in practice.  The work is likely to influence future research on GMM algorithms and optimization methods.




**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Resolves a significant open problem.
* Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with clear implications.
* Offers valuable insights into the behavior of EM algorithms.
* Well-written and logically organized.

**Against:**

* The counterexample is limited in scope.
* The analysis relies on strong simplifying assumptions.
* Lacks empirical validation.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**

Despite the limitations mentioned above, the paper presents a significant and original contribution to the field. The theoretical results are compelling, and the analysis is rigorous.  The limitations regarding scope and empirical validation are not fatal, as the paper’s primary contribution lies in its theoretical insights. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program and warrants acceptance.  I encourage the authors to consider incorporating empirical results in future work to further strengthen their claims.
