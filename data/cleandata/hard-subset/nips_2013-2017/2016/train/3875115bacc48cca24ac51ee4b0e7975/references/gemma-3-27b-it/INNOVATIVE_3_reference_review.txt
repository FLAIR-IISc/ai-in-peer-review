This paper tackles a fundamental question about the EM algorithm for Gaussian Mixture Models: are there “bad” local optima? The authors provide a negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture, demonstrating the existence of such optima, and crucially, show that the EM algorithm, initialized with a reasonable (data-driven) scheme, *converges* to these suboptimal points with high probability. This is a bold and significant result. The technical work appears solid, and the implications are important – even with large datasets, simple local search methods can be deeply unreliable for GMM estimation.

**Summary:** The paper rigorously demonstrates the existence of bad local optima for the likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models. It then provides a strong theoretical justification for why the standard EM algorithm, even with a sensible initialization strategy, will frequently get stuck in these suboptimal solutions, especially as the number of components increases.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** This is the primary strength. The result definitively answers a long-standing open question and challenges the common intuition about EM's behavior. The existence of bad local optima *and* the demonstration that EM converges to them with high probability is a powerful combination.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The proofs appear to be carefully constructed and leverage recent advances in optimization theory (stable manifold theorem connection).  The recursive construction used to prove Theorem 2 is particularly clever.
* **Significance:** The findings have practical implications for anyone using EM or related local search methods for GMM estimation. It highlights the critical importance of initialization strategies and suggests that simply increasing sample size doesn't guarantee convergence to a good solution.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the motivation is clearly laid out. The notation is standard, and the key ideas are explained with sufficient detail. The inclusion of a 1D example (Figure 1) is helpful.
* **Connection to Related Work:** The paper is well-situated within the existing literature on GMM estimation, EM analysis, and optimization landscapes.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:** The analysis is restricted to uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussian mixtures. While this simplification is necessary to obtain strong theoretical results, it limits the direct applicability to more general GMMs.  The authors acknowledge this, but it’s worth reiterating.
* **Proof Complexity:** While I believe the proofs are likely correct, they are complex and require a significant effort to fully verify.  More detailed explanations within the main text (rather than deferring everything to the appendices) might be beneficial.
* **Practical Alternatives:** The paper doesn't offer concrete alternatives to EM. While it points to the need for better initialization, it doesn't explore specific strategies beyond acknowledging the work on moment-based methods. Exploring potential remedies, even briefly, would strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in a rigorous and novel way.
* The results have significant theoretical and practical implications.
* The technical work is substantial and appears to be sound.
* The paper is well-written and clearly motivated.
* The potential impact on the field outweighs the limitations of the scope.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The restricted scope might limit the immediate applicability of the results.
* The proofs are complex and require careful scrutiny.
* The paper doesn't offer immediate practical solutions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a highly impactful paper. While the limited scope and complexity of the proofs are minor concerns, the novelty and significance of the results are overwhelming. This paper introduces a crucial warning about the reliability of EM for GMMs and opens up new avenues for research into more robust estimation algorithms. I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper meets the NIPS criteria of quality, clarity, originality, and significance. It's a paper that will be cited and discussed for years to come.



**Score:** 9/10. (Strong Accept)
