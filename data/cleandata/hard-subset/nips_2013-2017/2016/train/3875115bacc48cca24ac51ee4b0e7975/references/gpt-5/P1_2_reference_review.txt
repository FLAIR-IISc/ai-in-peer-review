Summary
The paper studies the population log-likelihood landscape of uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) and the behavior of EM-type algorithms under random initialization. It settles a conjecture due to Srebro (2007) by constructing, for any M ≥ 3, a well-separated 1D GMM with unit variances whose population likelihood has spurious local maxima, with likelihood gap arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. Algorithmically, it shows that EM and first-order EM (gradient EM) with the common random initialization scheme (drawing M initial centers i.i.d. from the mixture) converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − exp(−cM), so exponentially many restarts are needed to find a good solution on these instances. Finally, leveraging stable manifold ideas, the paper shows first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting bad local maxima (not saddles) are the main obstruction.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on extensive theory of identifiability and rates in mixtures (Teicher; Chen; Ho & Nguyen; Genovese & Wasserman; Ghosal & van der Vaart).
- Connects to algorithmic analyses of EM (Dasgupta & Schulman; Balakrishnan et al.) that give local convergence under separation and good initialization, and to moment/spectral methods (Moitra & Valiant; Hsu & Kakade; Vempala & Wang; Arora et al.) that provide global guarantees under separation in the well-specified setting.
- Contrasts with positive “benign landscape” results seen in some nonconvex problems (e.g., sparse M-estimation), by giving a negative landscape result for a canonical model.
- The strict-saddle non-convergence result relates to recent results on gradient methods avoiding strict saddles (Lee et al.; Panageas & Piliouras; Ge et al.).

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: definitively answers an open conjecture about the population likelihood landscape for GMMs by exhibiting arbitrarily bad local maxima even in a very favorable setting (uniform weights, spherical, unit variance, well-separated).
- Algorithmic implications: shows EM and gradient EM with widely used random initialization are exponentially unlikely to succeed on constructed instances. This is a clear message about the necessity of careful initialization, even asymptotically.
- Technical novelty: clever geometric construction for M = 3 and a recursive, multiscale clustering construction to drive the success probability down to exp(−Ω(M)). The argument that gradient EM avoids strict saddles strengthens the interpretation that failures are due to local maxima.
- Clarity and organization: well-motivated, with clear background and formalization. The high-level proof sketches help intuition, with full proofs deferred to the appendix.

Weaknesses and questions
- Population-only and adversarial: All results are at the population level and on carefully constructed instances. While the authors note that empirical process arguments extend to large-sample regimes, finite-sample rates and explicit sample complexities would strengthen the practical message. Empirical illustrations would also help.
- Model restrictions: Results assume equal weights and identical spherical covariances. Are the constructions robust to slight weight perturbations or mild covariance mismatches? It would be valuable to discuss how the arguments adapt.
- Separation and geometry: The bad local maxima example for M = 3 relies on two means being “much closer” than the third, yet the model remains well-separated (in the sense of min pairwise distance ≥ Ω(1) in 1D). It would help to state explicit parameter choices (numerical placements and separation constants) and quantify the likelihood gap Cgap as a function of separation.
- EM trapping claim: The key idea that EM updates never move centers across far-apart clusters is intuitively plausible, but the formal conditions (exact thresholds, dependence on separation and number of centers per cluster) should be stated more explicitly in the main text.
- First-order EM convergence: Theorem 4(a) claims convergence to a critical point under constant step size s ∈ (0,1). For gradient ascent on nonconvex smooth functions, convergence (as opposed to approaching a limit cycle or a set) can require additional conditions (e.g., Lipschitz gradient, step size bounds tied to smoothness). Please clarify the precise smoothness assumptions, handling of label symmetry (non-isolated critical manifolds), and why convergence (not just convergence of the gradient to zero) holds here.
- Scope of negative result vs practice: The paper emphasizes failures under random initialization. It would be helpful to discuss whether common variants (e.g., k-means++-like seeding, overparameterization followed by pruning, or spectral/moment-based initialization) avoid the bad basins on these instances, and to what extent the recursive construction defeats such schemes.
- Two-component case: The authors flag M = 2 as open and conjecture benign behavior. Any partial progress (e.g., special cases, additional intuition or plots) would be welcome.

Quality
- The claims are theoretically well-founded with clear theorems and proof sketches; the main technical burden is in the appendix. The landscape counterexample and the exponentially small success probability under random init are persuasive. Some assumptions and constants could be made more explicit in the main text to improve reproducibility.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. Consider adding explicit parameter settings for the illustrative M = 3 construction, a figure for that example (alongside the M = 2 visualization), and a short pseudocode box tying FO-EM to gradient ascent.
- Minor: bibliography formatting is inconsistent; several entries have placeholders or missing details.

Originality
- High. The paper settles a conjecture, provides new structural insights about the GMM likelihood, and connects algorithmic behavior (EM) with landscape features in a precise, negative way.

Significance
- High theoretical significance. This work will influence how the community reasons about EM, initialization, and “benign landscape” hopes in mixture models. It motivates using moment/spectral initializers or more sophisticated seeding in practice.

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves an open problem with a clean construction.
- Strong, crisp theorems with algorithmic consequences.
- Bridges optimization landscape theory and practical algorithms (EM).
- Likely to stimulate follow-up work on initialization and on the M = 2 case.

Arguments against acceptance
- No empirical demonstrations; all results are population-level and adversarial.
- Restricted model class (uniform weights, spherical covariances).
- Some technical conditions and constants are deferred; convergence claims for FO-EM could be stated more precisely.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide explicit numerical example(s) for the M = 3 construction and quantify separation/gap.
- Add a brief empirical section illustrating EM’s failure probability scaling vs M on your constructed instances (even in 1D).
- Discuss robustness to unequal weights/covariances and to alternative seeding (k-means++, overspecification).
- Clarify assumptions and proof outline for Theorem 4(a), especially handling label symmetry and smoothness/step-size conditions.

Overall recommendation
This is a strong theoretical paper with clear novelty and significance. Despite the lack of experiments and the model restrictions, I recommend acceptance, with the above clarifications to enhance impact and readability.
