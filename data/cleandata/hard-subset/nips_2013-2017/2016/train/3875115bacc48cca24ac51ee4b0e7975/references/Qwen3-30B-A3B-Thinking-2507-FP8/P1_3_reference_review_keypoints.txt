- Main result: Provides a counterexample to Srebro (2007), showing well-separated GMMs with M ≥ 3 can have arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood.  
- Algorithmic implication: EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability in M.  
- Optimization insight: First-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points, indicating failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Model scope: Results are proven for equally weighted, spherical GMMs with unit variance only.  
- Theoretical significance: Resolves a long-standing open conjecture and advances understanding of non-convexity in GMMs.  
- Technical quality: Proofs are rigorous and leverage recent advances in non-convex optimization.  
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized.  
- Practical relevance: Highlights the need for careful initialization in GMM estimation and exposes limitations of standard methods.  
- Limitation — generality: Unclear whether results extend to unequal weights, non-spherical components, or varying variances.  
- Limitation — empirical validation: Lacks experiments or finite-sample demonstrations of the claimed bad local maxima.  
- Limitation — practical context: Does not fully discuss implications for common GMM applications like clustering or density estimation.  
- Open case: The M = 2 case remains unresolved and the paper does not explain why the construction fails for M = 2.  
- Recommendation: Strongly recommended for acceptance; strengths outweigh the relatively minor weaknesses.  
- Pro arguments: Resolves an important theoretical question, gives strong justification for careful initialization, and is technically rigorous and well-written.  
- Con arguments: Restricted model assumptions, no empirical validation, and limited discussion of real-world impacts.
