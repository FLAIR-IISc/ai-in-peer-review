This paper investigates the local optima landscape of the likelihood function for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), focusing on the setting of uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians. The authors demonstrate that, contrary to a conjecture by Srebro, bad local maxima *do* exist even with well-separated components, and crucially, show that the EM and first-order EM algorithms, initialized randomly from the data, converge to these suboptimal solutions with high probability. They further prove that the first-order EM algorithm avoids strict saddle points with probability one, strengthening the conclusion that the issue is genuinely bad local optima.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question in GMM estimation and relating to the broader literature on non-convex optimization. The problem is clearly stated, and the paper builds upon a strong foundation of relevant prior work (Teicher, Chen, Genovese & Wasserman, Dasgupta & Schulman, Balakrishnan et al., Srebro, Améndola et al., etc.). The theoretical results are significant: demonstrating the existence of bad local optima, and showing the high probability of convergence to them from a natural initialization scheme represent a substantial contribution. The authors' meticulous analysis of the EM and first-order EM algorithms, and their careful consideration of the role of saddle points, are commendable. 

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The existence of bad local optima in GMM estimation is a long-standing question with significant practical implications.
* **Strong Theoretical Results:** The negative answer to Srebro's conjecture and the high-probability analysis of EM convergence are substantial achievements.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The proofs appear technically sound and rely on sophisticated techniques.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written, with clear definitions and a logical flow of ideas.  The introduction does a good job of contextualizing the work.
* **Connections to Broader Literature:** The paper thoughtfully connects its results to related work in optimization and statistical learning.
* **Thorough Discussion:** The conclusion points out open questions and potential directions for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:** The results are largely restricted to the specific setting of uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussian mixtures.  While this simplifies the analysis, it raises questions about the generalizability of the findings to more realistic scenarios (e.g., different weighting schemes, non-spherical Gaussians).  The paper acknowledges this, but a discussion of how the results *might* extend (or not) would be beneficial.
* **Practical Impact:** While the theoretical results are significant, the paper doesn't offer immediate practical solutions. The implication is that careful initialization is crucial, but doesn't prescribe *how* to achieve effective initialization in practice beyond repeating the random initialization many times, which is computationally expensive.  A discussion of potential initialization strategies (e.g., based on spectral methods or moment estimation) that could mitigate the problem would enhance the paper's impact.
* **Technical Density:** The mathematical details can be quite dense, and may make the paper less accessible to readers not deeply familiar with the relevant mathematical tools. While thoroughness is appreciated, some intuitive explanations alongside the formal proofs could broaden the audience.
* **Figure 1:** The figure illustrating the two-component case is helpful for intuition, but could benefit from clearer labeling of axes and potentially a visual representation of the gradient norm.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a fundamental and important problem in statistical estimation.
* The theoretical results are novel, rigorous, and contribute significantly to our understanding of GMM estimation.
* The analysis is thorough and insightful, providing a deeper understanding of the challenges associated with local optimization in this context.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making the complex technical details accessible to experts in the field.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The results are limited to a specific setting, raising questions about generalizability.
* The lack of immediate practical solutions limits the paper's immediate impact.
* The technical density might make the paper inaccessible to a broader audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMM estimation. The existence of bad local optima, coupled with the high probability of convergence to them under a natural initialization scheme, is a concerning result that highlights the challenges of local optimization in this context.  While the limited scope and lack of immediate practical solutions are weaknesses, the theoretical significance and rigor of the analysis outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Discuss Generalizability:**  Expand the discussion on how the results might (or might not) extend to more general GMM settings (e.g., non-isotropic covariances, different weighting schemes).
* **Explore Initialization Strategies:** Discuss potential initialization schemes that could mitigate the problem of bad local optima and improve the practical performance of EM.
* **Provide More Intuition:** Add intuitive explanations alongside the formal proofs to make the paper more accessible.
* **Enhance Figure 1:** Improve the clarity of Figure 1 with better labeling and potentially a visual representation of the gradient norm.
