- Resolves Srebro (2007) conjecture by constructing a counterexample showing non-global local maxima in the population likelihood of GMMs.  
- Counterexample: uniform mixture of three well-separated isotropic Gaussians in d=1 with local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global maximum.  
- Shows both standard EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability in M.  
- Proves first-order EM avoids strict saddle points, so failures arise from bad local maxima rather than saddles.  
- Technical contributions are rigorous, carefully proving Theorems 1–4 and distinguishing population vs finite-sample settings.  
- Provides clear geometric intuition (Section 3.1) that helps explain why bad local maxima exist for M≥3.  
- Exceptionally well-organized and clearly written with precise definitions and logical progression from structure to algorithmic consequences.  
- Novel and significant: resolves a long-standing open problem and links likelihood landscape structure to EM behavior.  
- Experimental evidence (population-level) supports theoretical claims, though limited in scope.  
- Practical implication: random initialization success probability decays exponentially with M, underscoring need for better initialization methods.  
- Main weakness: analysis focuses on a specific setting (uniform isotropic Gaussians, fixed variance, d=1), with limited extensions and no proposed practical algorithms to fix the issue.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept — strong theoretical contribution with broad implications despite some setting restrictions.
