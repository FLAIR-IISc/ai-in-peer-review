Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the population log-likelihood landscape of uniformly weighted mixtures of M isotropic Gaussians with fixed unit covariance (GMMs). It resolves a conjecture of Srebro (2007), which suggested that the infinite-sample (population) likelihood has no spurious local maxima in this favorable setting. The main structural result (Theorem 1) constructs, for any M ≥ 3, a 1D well-separated mixture whose population log-likelihood admits a suboptimal local maximum with arbitrarily large likelihood gap from the global optimum. Algorithmically, the paper shows that EM and first-order EM (gradient EM) with random initialization (drawing M initial centers i.i.d. from the underlying mixture) converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-cM} (Theorems 2 and 3), implying that exponentially many random restarts are needed to succeed with constant probability on these instances. Finally, using tools inspired by recent results on the avoidance of strict saddles by gradient methods (e.g., Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016), the paper shows that first-order EM with a constant step size in (0, 1) converges to a critical point and almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points (Theorem 4), suggesting that failure is due to bad local maxima, not saddles.

These results complement prior positive analyses of EM under separation and good initialization (e.g., Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and algorithmic methods beyond EM (clustering-based and moment methods: Vempala and Wang, 2002; Arora et al., 2005; Moitra and Valiant, 2010; Hsu and Kakade, 2013). They refine the landscape picture for GMMs by giving explicit counterexamples even in the most benign equal-weight, spherical, population setting.

Strengths
- Clear, sharp negative resolution of a standing conjecture (Srebro, 2007) with explicit 1D constructions for M ≥ 3.
- Algorithmic implications are quantified: EM/gradient-EM with random initialization fail with probability 1 − e^{-cM} on a recursively clustered construction, not just at adversarial initializations.
- The avoidance-of-saddles result for first-order EM isolates bad local maxima as the culprit, aligning with and adapting recent dynamical systems perspectives on gradient methods.
- The proofs introduce useful structural arguments about the population likelihood (geometric neighborhood barriers, trapping phenomena under EM updates, recursive cluster tree), which may transfer to other nonconvex models.

Weaknesses
- Population-level and existential: the constructions are tailored and the negative probability bounds are for a specific (though well-separated) family; practical instances often use more sophisticated initializations (k-means++, methods-of-moments) not covered here.
- Finite-sample translation is asserted to follow “straightforwardly,” but no explicit finite-sample rates, covering numbers, or uniform deviation bounds are provided; this weakens immediate applicability.
- The two-component case (M = 2) remains open; the paper conjectures benign behavior but does not provide partial progress or conditions under which the conjecture holds.
- The assumptions ensuring convergence of first-order EM to a critical point (e.g., smoothness/Lipschitz gradient and step-size constraints) are not fully specified; the claim that the proof “avoids smoothness” deserves more precise conditions.
- Scope is restricted to equal weights and unit covariances. It is unclear whether analogous results hold (or become easier/harder) with unequal weights or known/unknown variances.

Quality
- Technical claims are plausible and consistent with related literature. The geometric intuition for Theorem 1 and the recursive cluster construction for Theorem 2/3 are sensible. The saddle-avoidance result follows the spirit of Lee et al. (2016) and Panageas and Piliouras (2016), though the exact regularity assumptions for first-order EM should be stated precisely (e.g., differentiability, local Lipschitz continuity of ∇L, and step-size bounds).
- Algorithmic conclusions are careful: failure is shown under random initialization; other initializations may succeed (and are suggested as remedies).

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with clear statements of models and algorithms. The intuition for key constructions is helpful.
- References need cleanup (incomplete years/punctuation). Some claims (finite-sample carryover; smoothness avoidance) would benefit from explicit conditions and brief lemmas in the main text.
- Defining “well-separated” with explicit constants used in constructions would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- Resolving the Srebro conjecture negatively for M ≥ 3 in the uniform, spherical, population setting is novel and nontrivial.
- The exponentially small success probability for random initialization via a recursive mixture construction is a strong and original quantitative statement.
- The saddle-avoidance application to first-order EM in this model is a useful specialization of recent general theory.

Significance
- Scientifically significant: clarifies that even extremely benign GMMs can have bad local maxima at the population level; informs the design and analysis of EM initializations.
- Practical implications: cautions against naive random initialization and supports pilot estimators (e.g., spectral/moment methods) before local search. The impact is moderated by the tailored nature of the counterexamples and the population focus.

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves a notable open question; strong, clean negative results with algorithmic consequences.
- Introduces techniques that may generalize to other models.
- Clarifies the role of local maxima vs. saddles for EM-like methods in GMMs.

Arguments against acceptance
- Primarily existential/population results with limited empirical or finite-sample substantiation.
- Restricted model class (equal weights, fixed covariances) and random initialization; does not analyze standard practical initializations (k-means++, splitting).
- Missing precise regularity assumptions for the gradient-EM convergence claim in the main text.

Suggestions and questions
- Provide explicit finite-sample guarantees transferring Theorems 1–3 to Ln with n large (uniform concentration, rates, and how the exponential failure probability degrades with n).
- State the exact assumptions (smoothness/Lipschitz constants) and step-size range needed for Theorem 4, and clarify whether similar conclusions hold for EM (not just first-order EM).
- Discuss robustness of the constructions to unequal weights or known non-unit covariances.
- Elaborate on the M = 2 case: any partial results or regimes where the no-bad-local-maxima conjecture can be proved?
- Include a brief experiment illustrating the constructions and the exponential failure trend with M.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a clear, technically sound, and novel contribution by resolving an open conjecture and quantifying algorithmic consequences, with broadly relevant insights for nonconvex statistical estimation, despite limitations in scope and finite-sample development.
