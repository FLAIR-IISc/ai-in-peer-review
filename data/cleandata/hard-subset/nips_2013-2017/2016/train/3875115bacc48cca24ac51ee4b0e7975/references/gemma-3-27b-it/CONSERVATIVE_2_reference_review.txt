This paper investigates the local optima of the likelihood function for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), a fundamental problem in statistical modeling. The authors demonstrate the existence of bad local optima, challenging a conjecture by Srebro [2007], and analyze the convergence properties of the EM algorithm under random initialization. While the problem is well-motivated and the results are technically sound, I find the contribution incremental and the presentation overly dense, hindering a clear understanding of the core ideas.

The strength of the paper lies in the formal proof of the existence of bad local optima for a 3-component Gaussian mixture. This is a valuable theoretical result, clearly demonstrating a limitation of local search methods. The accompanying analysis of the EM algorithm, showing its tendency to converge to these suboptimal points under random initialization, further reinforces this concern. The rigorous treatment of the first-order EM algorithm and the result distinguishing between bad local optima and saddle points are also positive contributions.

However, several weaknesses detract from the overall impact. First, the motivation, while standard, feels somewhat disconnected from practical concerns. The focus on uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians, while simplifying the analysis, limits the applicability of the results. Real-world data rarely adheres to such strict assumptions. The discussion of robustness to model misspecification feels tangential and doesn’t fully integrate with the main findings.

Second, the presentation is exceptionally dense and relies heavily on mathematical notation without sufficient intuitive explanation. The extended background section, while covering relevant literature, becomes overwhelming. Figures 1 (mentioned in the introduction) is not included in the submitted version. The jump between the general discussion and the specific technical results is abrupt. A more gradual build-up of the ideas, with illustrative examples, would significantly improve clarity.

Third, the experimental validation is entirely theoretical. While this is acceptable for a theoretical paper, a small-scale empirical demonstration—even using simulated data—could strengthen the claims and provide a more concrete understanding of the phenomenon. The authors acknowledge the difficulties of evaluating EM's behavior, but some attempt to visualize the likelihood surface or demonstrate the convergence issues would be beneficial.

Fourth, the claim of "new techniques" for reasoning about the likelihood surface feels overstated. The approach leverages established tools from optimization and statistical learning, albeit applied in a specific context. While the combination of these tools is novel, the authors don’t adequately highlight the unique aspects of their methodology.

Finally, while the paper identifies a significant limitation of EM, it doesn’t offer concrete solutions. The discussion of careful initialization is generic and doesn't point to specific strategies for overcoming the problem.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous proof of the existence of bad local optima in GMMs.
*   Analysis of EM convergence under random initialization.
*   Clear distinction between bad local optima and saddle points.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited applicability due to strong assumptions (isotropic Gaussians).
*   Overly dense and difficult-to-follow presentation.
*   Lack of empirical validation.
*   Incremental contribution—the existence of bad local optima, while important, isn't entirely surprising.
*   Offers no practical remedies for the identified problem.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the technical results are sound and address an important theoretical question, the limited practical relevance, challenging presentation, and lack of experimental validation outweigh the strengths. The paper requires significant revisions to improve clarity, broaden its scope, and provide a more compelling demonstration of its significance. It currently falls short of the standards expected for a NIPS publication. I encourage the authors to consider presenting their work at a more theoretically focused conference.
