Summary
The paper studies the optimization landscape of the population log-likelihood for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) with equal weights and known, spherical covariances, and the algorithmic behavior of EM and gradient-EM (first-order EM) under random initialization. It resolves a conjecture of Srebro (2007) by showing that even in this favorable setting there exist spurious local maxima of the population likelihood for M ≥ 3 components, and that these can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. The authors then demonstrate that EM and gradient-EM, when initialized by sampling centers from the data (equivalently, from the mixture in the population limit), converge to suboptimal critical points with high probability—indeed, the success probability is at most e−Ω(M), necessitating exponentially many random restarts to achieve constant success probability. Finally, they show that gradient-EM (with fixed step size s ∈ (0, 1)) converges to critical points and avoids strict saddle points almost surely, suggesting that the observed failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.

The main construction underlying Theorem 1 is an explicitly well-separated 1D mixture where two true centers are close while a third is far away; a configuration where two estimated centers co-locate at the far-away component and one sits between the close pair is shown to be a strict local maximum with arbitrarily bad likelihood gap. The algorithmic failure results use a hierarchical “cluster-of-clusters” arrangement of true centers over Θ(log M) levels to show that random initialization very likely assigns an incorrect number of centers to at least one (sub)cluster, and that EM/gradient-EM remain trapped within their initial topological partition due to negligible cross-responsibilities under strong separation. The strict-saddle avoidance result leverages ideas from dynamical systems and recent nonconvex optimization results (Lee et al., Panageas and Piliouras), with a tailored argument that avoids imposing global smoothness assumptions on L.

Strengths
- Clear resolution of a well-known open conjecture: the existence of spurious local maxima in the population likelihood for well-separated, uniform, spherical GMMs with M ≥ 3.
- Strong algorithmic consequences: rigorous evidence that EM and gradient-EM with common random initialization schemes fail with high probability unless one uses exponentially many restarts.
- Conceptually clean constructions and intuitions (two-near-one-far example; hierarchical clustering of components) that illuminate why and how local search gets trapped.
- Useful insight separating roles of local maxima vs strict saddles: gradient-EM almost surely avoids strict saddles yet still fails, pinpointing bad local maxima as the culprit.
- Results are presented at the population level but plausibly carry to finite samples via standard uniform convergence arguments, making them relevant in practice.
- Good coverage of related work on EM analysis, mixture identifiability, moment methods, and saddle-point theory.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope limitations: Main structural example is in d = 1. While it likely embeds trivially into higher dimensions, a statement or lemma formalizing extension to d ≥ 2 (beyond the definition of “well-separated” as Ω(√d)) would strengthen the contribution.
- Practical initialization: The negative results target random initialization by sampling from the mixture/data. Many practitioners use k-means++ or other heuristics; it would be helpful to discuss whether the hierarchical construction also causes those schemes to fail with non-negligible probability.
- Finite-sample guarantees: The paper states that results “carry over” via empirical process arguments. It would be valuable to include explicit finite-sample statements (sample size scaling in M, separation, and d) and a brief proof outline to substantiate this claim.
- Quantitative separation: Theorems assert existence for “well-separated” mixtures and step size s ∈ (0, 1) for gradient-EM. Precise dependencies of constants (e.g., how large ξ needs to be relative to d and M for trapping and decoupling arguments) would help reproducibility.
- Local maxima characterization: Theorem 1 provides an additive likelihood gap Cgap. Is there a multiplicative or KL gap characterization? Also, are the bad local maxima isolated or could there be plateaus/continuums in some parameterizations?
- Strict-saddle avoidance: The proof sketch claims to avoid general smoothness assumptions. It would help to clearly state the minimal regularity conditions on L and the EM map, and whether the result extends beyond constant step sizes or to line-search schemes.
- Experiments: Even small-scale synthetic experiments illustrating the constructions and EM’s failure probability as a function of M would strengthen the empirical relevance.

Quality
The results are technically sound and well-motivated. The proof ideas are plausible and align with known behavior of responsibilities under strong separation and with stable manifold arguments for gradient methods. The claims are carefully stated for the population setting with appropriate caveats; however, more explicit constants and finite-sample translations would improve completeness.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem setup, EM and gradient-EM updates, and the random initialization scheme are clearly described. The geometric intuition for the constructions is helpful. Minor suggestion: define “well-separated” precisely where it is invoked in each theorem and explicitly note the dimension of the constructions. The references list appears to have some formatting issues (likely due to draft conversion).

Originality
The paper provides new and definitive negative results that overturn a long-standing conjecture, and sharp algorithmic consequences for widely used methods (EM and gradient-EM). The hierarchical construction for e−Ω(M) failure probability and the strict-saddle avoidance argument tailored to gradient-EM are novel.

Significance
The results matter: they caution against naive random initialization in EM, even under benign modeling assumptions, and motivate the use of principled initializers (moments/spectral, k-means++ variants) or global methods. They also refine our understanding of nonconvex likelihood landscapes in mixture models and will likely influence subsequent theoretical and algorithmic work.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add explicit finite-sample theorems with sample complexity and approximation parameters.
- Discuss behavior under k-means++ or other distance-aware initializers.
- Provide quantitative separation thresholds and constants in the main text.
- Include a brief experimental section validating the constructions and scaling of failure probability.
- Clarify whether the results extend to unequal weights or to estimating variances.

Pros for acceptance
- Resolves a notable open problem with clean, rigorous results.
- Strong, general algorithmic implications for widely used methods.
- Clear exposition and insightful constructions.
- Likely to stimulate follow-up work on initialization and robust likelihood optimization.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited discussion of alternative practical initializers and finite-sample instantiations.
- Constructions and theorems are primarily 1D; higher-dimensional formalization is implicit.
- No empirical validation.

Recommendation: Accept
