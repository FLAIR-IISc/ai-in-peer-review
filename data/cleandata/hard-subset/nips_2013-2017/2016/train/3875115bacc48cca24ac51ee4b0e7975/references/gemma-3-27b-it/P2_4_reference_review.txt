## NIPS 2016 Review: Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the landscape of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), specifically focusing on the existence of bad local maxima. It builds upon Srebro's conjecture that, in the infinite sample limit, the likelihood function has no spurious local optima.  The authors rigorously demonstrate that this conjecture is *false* – they construct a 3-component GMM where bad local maxima exist, and show they can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum.  Further, they prove that the standard EM algorithm, initialized randomly from the data, converges to these suboptimal points with high probability.  They also show that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting bad local optima are the primary obstacle.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Result:** The paper provides a definitive negative answer to a long-standing conjecture regarding the shape of the GMM likelihood surface. This is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs.
* **Algorithmic Implications:** The results have direct and important implications for the practical use of the EM algorithm. Demonstrating that random initialization is likely to fail, even in a relatively well-behaved setting, highlights the critical need for more robust initialization strategies.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The proofs appear rigorous and well-structured. The authors make a clear connection between the structural properties of the likelihood surface and the behavior of the EM algorithm.
* **Well-Motivated:** The introduction effectively contextualizes the work within the broader literature on GMM estimation, highlighting the challenges and open problems.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistent and the key concepts are explained adequately.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:**  The results are largely confined to the specific case of uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussian mixtures.  While a good starting point, this limits the direct applicability to more general GMMs (varying weights, covariance structures). The paper acknowledges this, but a discussion of the challenges in extending these results would be helpful.
* **Construction Complexity:** The construction demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima is somewhat complex. While mathematically sound, it could benefit from a more intuitive explanation.  A visualization beyond the 2D example in the introduction would be beneficial.
* **Practical Severity:** While the paper demonstrates *high probability* of convergence to a bad local maximum, it doesn't quantify *how bad* these optima typically are in practice, or how often alternative initialization methods are sufficient to avoid them.  Empirical validation, even on a limited scale, would strengthen the impact.




**Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by rigorous mathematical analysis. The construction of the counterexample to Srebro’s conjecture is a significant achievement.  It's a complete piece of work, though further investigation into generalization might be pursued in future work.
* **Clarity (8/10):** The paper is generally well-written. However, the complexity of the construction and proofs makes it challenging for a non-expert to fully grasp the details.  More intuitive explanations would improve clarity.
* **Originality (10/10):** The paper presents a genuinely original contribution, resolving a significant open problem. The connection between the likelihood surface structure and the EM algorithm's convergence is novel and insightful. The authors demonstrate excellent awareness of related work.
* **Significance (9/10):** The results are important. They challenge a fundamental assumption about GMMs and have direct consequences for practical algorithm design. The paper offers valuable guidance on the limitations of standard approaches and motivates the development of more robust initialization schemes.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Presents a significant theoretical breakthrough by disproving a long-held conjecture.
* Provides a rigorous and insightful analysis of the GMM likelihood surface.
* Has clear implications for the practical use of the EM algorithm.
* Is well-written and technically sound.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The results are currently limited to a specific case of GMMs.
* Lack of empirical validation or quantification of the severity of the bad optima.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** Despite the limitations regarding scope and empirical evaluation, the theoretical contribution of this paper is substantial and justifies acceptance to NIPS.  The results are likely to stimulate further research in this area and improve our understanding of GMM estimation.  I recommend the authors consider expanding the scope of their work and providing some empirical validation in future iterations.
