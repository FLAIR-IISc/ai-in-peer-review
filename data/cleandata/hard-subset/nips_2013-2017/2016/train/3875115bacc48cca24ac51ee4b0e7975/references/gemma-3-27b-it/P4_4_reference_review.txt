This paper investigates the likelihood surface of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and the convergence properties of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors demonstrate the existence of bad local maxima for the population log-likelihood of a well-separated mixture of three Gaussians, resolving a conjecture by Srebro (2007). They then show that the EM algorithm, initialized by uniformly sampling means from the dataset, converges to these suboptimal points with high probability. A similar result is established for the first-order EM algorithm. Furthermore, they prove that the first-order EM algorithm is unlikely to converge to a strict saddle point, suggesting that bad local optima are the primary cause of failure.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work on GMMs, including identifiability studies (Teicher, 1963; Allman et al., 2009), convergence rates (Chen, 1995; Ghosal & Van Der Vaart, 2001; Ho & Nguyen, 2015), and algorithmic analysis (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015). It connects to the broader literature on non-convex optimization, including the robustness of maximum likelihood estimation (Donoho & Liu, 1988) and the behavior of gradient descent (Loh & Wainwright, 2013; Lee et al., 2016; Panageas & Piliouras, 2016). The work is particularly relevant given the widespread use of EM and its variants in practice, despite the lack of strong theoretical guarantees.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The main result – the existence of bad local maxima in a well-separated GMM – is a significant contribution. It directly contradicts a previously held conjecture and provides a fundamental limitation on local search methods.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proofs appear rigorous and well-structured. The construction of the counterexample to Srebro’s conjecture is cleverly designed, and the analysis of the EM algorithm’s convergence behavior seems careful. The connection to the recent literature on gradient descent is well-motivated.
*   **Significance:** The results have practical implications for GMM estimation. They underscore the importance of careful initialization and suggest that repeated random restarts are often necessary to achieve good performance.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a good overview of the problem and related work. The notation is consistent, and the key concepts are clearly defined. 

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The results are established for a specific, albeit idealized, setting – uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussians with fixed variances. While this simplifies the analysis, it is unclear how well the findings generalize to more complex GMMs (e.g., with different weights, covariance structures, or dimensionality).  The paper acknowledges this limitation, but a discussion of potential extensions would be beneficial.
*   **Proof Complexity:** The proofs, while seemingly rigorous, are quite involved and rely on intricate arguments. While detailed proofs are essential, a more intuitive explanation of the key ideas might enhance accessibility.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The paper focuses entirely on theoretical results. Including some numerical experiments to corroborate the findings would strengthen the conclusions. Demonstrating the empirical failure rate of EM with random initialization would be particularly compelling.
* **Initialization Dependency**: The analysis hinges heavily on the specific random initialization scheme. Other common initialization techniques (e.g., k-means++) aren't considered, and it's unclear if the same negative results would apply to them.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result that addresses a long-standing open problem in the field of GMM estimation.
*   The analysis is technically sound and builds upon a strong foundation of related work.
*   The findings have practical implications for the design and analysis of GMM algorithms.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The results are limited to a specific and idealized setting.
*   The proofs are complex and may be difficult for some readers to follow.
*   The lack of empirical validation weakens the conclusions.
* The analysis is narrowly focused on the chosen initialization method.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper represents a solid contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMM estimation. The discovery of bad local maxima is a significant result, and the analysis of the EM algorithm’s convergence behavior is compelling. While the limitations regarding scope and empirical validation are noteworthy, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more intuitive explanation of the key proof ideas and a discussion of potential directions for generalizing the results to more complex GMMs. Adding some initial empirical results would also greatly improve the paper.
