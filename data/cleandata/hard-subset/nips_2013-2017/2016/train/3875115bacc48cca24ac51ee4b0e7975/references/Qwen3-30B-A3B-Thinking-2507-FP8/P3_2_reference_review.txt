

# Peer Review: "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental open question in Gaussian mixture modeling: Srebro's conjecture (2007) that for infinite samples, all local maxima of the likelihood function for a well-separated GMM are global maxima. The authors provide a negative answer by constructing a counterexample with M=3 components in dimension d=1, demonstrating that arbitrarily bad local maxima exist for M≥3. They further establish that with random initialization, EM and first-order EM algorithms converge to these suboptimal points with exponentially high probability in M.

The paper makes significant contributions to both theoretical understanding and practical algorithm design. Theorem 1 provides a rigorous counterexample to Srebro's conjecture, resolving a long-standing open question. Theorem 2 demonstrates that random initialization for EM fails catastrophically with probability 1 - e^(-cM), showing that achieving good solutions requires exponentially many restarts. Theorem 4 cleverly shows that the failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points, leveraging recent advances in non-convex optimization.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The construction of the counterexample is elegant and well-justified with clear geometric intuition. The algorithmic results are theoretically rigorous and have important practical implications for practitioners of GMMs. The authors carefully distinguish between the population setting and finite samples, and their proof techniques for establishing the existence of bad local maxima introduce novel analytical approaches. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong case for its claims.

However, some limitations merit consideration. The focus on isotropic unit-variance Gaussians with equal weights, while theoretically clean, limits the immediate applicability to more general GMMs. The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently discuss how their results might extend to more complex settings. The claim that "bad local maxima" can be "arbitrarily worse" lacks concrete numerical examples to illustrate the severity in practical terms. While Theorem 4 is compelling for first-order EM, the standard EM algorithm remains the most widely used variant, and the authors don't establish similar results for it (though they acknowledge this limitation).

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work. It builds on the foundational work of Srebro (2007), Balakrishnan et al. (2015) on EM convergence, and recent advances in non-convex optimization (Lee et al., 2016). It connects to the method-of-moments approaches (Hsu & Kakade, 2013; Moitra & Valiant, 2010) by suggesting that better initialization strategies are needed for EM. The paper's resolution of Srebro's conjecture is particularly significant given its prominence in the literature.

The significance of this work is high. It fundamentally changes our understanding of the optimization landscape for GMMs, showing that even in the idealized setting, the likelihood surface can be highly non-convex with bad local maxima. This explains why random initialization for EM often fails in practice and provides a theoretical justification for the need for more sophisticated initialization schemes (like those based on the method of moments). The exponential failure probability result (Theorem 2) is particularly striking and has important implications for computational complexity.

I have one minor concern about the interpretation of the results. While the paper demonstrates that random initialization fails with high probability, it doesn't quantify how often practitioners actually encounter such pathological cases in real data. The paper focuses on the idealized population setting, but the authors might consider discussing how their theoretical results might manifest in finite-sample settings through empirical validation.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that resolves an important open question and has significant implications for both theory and practice. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear, novel contribution to the field. While the focus on a highly idealized setting limits some practical applicability, the core insights about the likelihood landscape and algorithmic behavior remain valuable.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that resolves a long-standing open question in GMM theory. The results have important implications for practitioners and provide a foundation for future work on initialization strategies and optimization algorithms for mixture models. The paper is well-written and the results are rigorously established.

**Strengths:**
- Resolves Srebro's conjecture (2007) with a counterexample
- Demonstrates catastrophic failure of random initialization for EM with exponential probability
- Distinguishes between bad local maxima and saddle points
- Novel proof techniques for analyzing likelihood surfaces

**Weaknesses:**
- Focus on idealized isotropic unit-variance GMMs limits immediate practical applicability
- Lacks concrete numerical examples illustrating the severity of "arbitrarily worse" local maxima
- Results for standard EM algorithm not established (only for first-order variant)
