## NIPS 2016 Review: Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and theoretically rigorous contribution to our understanding of the non-convexity inherent in Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) estimation. It definitively answers a longstanding conjecture by Srebro (2007) regarding the absence of bad local optima, and provides compelling evidence for the practical difficulties of using local search methods like EM, even with well-separated mixtures. The results are clearly explained and supported by strong theoretical arguments. While the setting (isotropic Gaussians, fixed variances) is somewhat restrictive, the insights gained are broadly applicable and highly relevant to the NIPS community. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, outlined below.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper investigates the landscape of the likelihood function for GMMs, specifically focusing on the existence of bad local maxima. It builds upon a body of work addressing the challenges of estimating GMMs, including identifiability studies (Allman et al. 2009), convergence rate analyses (Chen 1995, Ho & Nguyen 2015), and algorithmic analyses of EM (Dasgupta & Schulman 2007, Balakrishnan et al. 2015).  The authors correctly position their work within this context, highlighting the limitations of existing guarantees, which often rely on strong assumptions about data separation. They also appropriately connect their work to broader themes in non-convex optimization, referencing recent results on the benign nature of landscapes in certain high-dimensional regression settings (Loh & Wainwright 2013, Wang et al. 2014). The motivation, linking theoretical robustness of maximum likelihood estimation to computational tractability, is well articulated.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Definitive Negative Result:** The core result – demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima even for well-separated, isotropic GMMs – is a significant contribution. It resolves an important open question and fundamentally alters our understanding of the optimization landscape.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The proofs are rigorous and well-structured, demonstrating a deep understanding of the problem. The use of geometric intuition (illustrated in the two-component example) is particularly helpful.
* **Algorithmic Implications:** The analysis of the EM and first-order EM algorithms, demonstrating their high probability of converging to suboptimal points with random initialization, is highly impactful.  This provides a theoretical justification for the practical observation that careful initialization is crucial for successful GMM estimation.
* **Connection to Gradient Descent Landscape:**  The result showing that the first-order EM algorithm doesn't converge to *strict* saddle points is a compelling argument that the failures of local search are primarily due to bad local optima, aligning with recent trends in understanding gradient-based optimization.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the work, and the technical sections are presented in a digestible manner.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Restricted Setting:** The assumption of isotropic Gaussians with fixed variances is a limitation. While justified as a starting point for theoretical analysis, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss the potential impact of relaxing these assumptions.  Are the core findings likely to generalize?
* **Initialization Scheme Specificity:** The analysis focuses on a specific random initialization scheme (uniform sampling from the data). While realistic, it’s worth acknowledging that other initialization strategies exist, and discussing potential differences in performance.
* **Proof Complexity:** While rigorous, some of the proofs (especially in Section A.4) are quite involved and might benefit from further simplification or more detailed explanations.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper is primarily theoretical. While not strictly *required*, some limited empirical validation, even on synthetic data, could strengthen the claims about the practical relevance of the findings.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Theorem 1:** The intuition behind the construction of the bad local maximum is clear from the 2D example. However, the generalization to higher dimensions and more components could be elaborated on slightly.
* **Theorem 2 & 3:** The exponential dependence on *M* is striking. Is there any hope for improving this bound, or are these exponential rates inherent to the problem?
* **Theorem 4:** The result regarding the avoidance of strict saddle points is valuable.  Are there any insights into the nature of the critical points that *are* reached by the first-order EM algorithm?
* **Future Work:** The authors mention addressing the two-component case. Is there a reason to believe that the conjecture holds in this simpler setting?
* **Related Work:** Consider mentioning recent work on sharpness of local minima in non-convex optimization, which may provide additional context for the results.



**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Resolves a long-standing open problem.
* Provides strong theoretical justification for practical challenges in GMM estimation.
* Well-written and rigorous.
* Offers valuable insights into the optimization landscape of GMMs.

**Con:**

* Limited to a specific GMM setting.
* Primarily theoretical with limited empirical validation.
* Proofs can be complex.




**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should:

* Briefly discuss the potential impact of relaxing the assumptions of isotropic Gaussians and fixed variances.
* Acknowledge the specificity of the analyzed initialization scheme and briefly comment on other potential approaches.
* Consider adding a small-scale empirical validation experiment to demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.
* Review the proofs for potential simplifications or clarifications.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field.  The insights gained will be valuable to researchers and practitioners working on GMMs and non-convex optimization more broadly.
