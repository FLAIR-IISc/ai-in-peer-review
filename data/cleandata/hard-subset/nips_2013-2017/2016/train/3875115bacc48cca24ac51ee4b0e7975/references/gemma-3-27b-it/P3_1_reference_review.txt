## NIPS 2016 Review: Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a significant and surprising theoretical result regarding the landscape of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs).  It rigorously demonstrates the existence of bad local maxima, even in well-separated mixtures, thus refuting a long-standing conjecture. Furthermore, it provides strong evidence that standard initialization schemes (random sampling from the data) lead the EM and first-order EM algorithms to these suboptimal points with high probability. This work is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a fundamental question in statistical estimation. While the setting is relatively restricted (uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians), the insights gained are important and have broader implications for understanding the behavior of likelihood-based methods. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of identifying and characterizing the local maxima of the likelihood function for GMMs. This is a classic problem in statistics with connections to identifiability (Teicher 1963, Allman et al. 2009) and convergence rates (Chen 1995, Ho & Nguyen 2015). While theoretical work has focused on convergence rates and alternative algorithms (Arora et al. 2005, Belkin & Sinha 2010), and the EM algorithm remains the dominant practical method (Dempster et al. 1977), guarantees on the quality of EM’s solution have been limited. Recent analyses (Dasgupta & Schulman 2007, Balakrishnan et al. 2015) provide some insight but rely on strong separation assumptions. The paper builds upon Srebro’s (2007) conjecture that, in the infinite sample limit, all local maxima are global maxima, providing a counterexample and extending the analysis to explore the implications for algorithms like EM.  The authors cleverly connect their work to recent results in high-dimensional regression (Loh & Wainwright 2013, Wang et al. 2014) demonstrating a similar phenomenon of benign optimization landscapes, and effectively argue that the GMM setting does *not* exhibit such behavior.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Result:** The core result – the existence of bad local maxima even in well-separated GMMs – is a substantial contribution. It settles a long-standing open question and demonstrates that the likelihood landscape can be surprisingly complex.
* **Strong Algorithmic Implications:**  The analysis showing that EM and first-order EM converge to bad local optima with high probability under reasonable initialization is compelling and has direct practical relevance.  This provides a clear justification for the need for careful initialization strategies.
* **Technical Rigor:** The proofs appear sound and are supported by insightful geometric arguments (the construction in 1D for M=3 is particularly illuminating).  The authors employ sophisticated tools from dynamical systems theory (in Theorem 4) to further understand the behavior of the first-order EM algorithm.
* **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent context, the technical details are presented clearly, and the conclusions are well-motivated.
* **Thorough Related Work:**  The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of the relevant literature and appropriately position their work within the broader context of GMM estimation and non-convex optimization.



**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Restricted Setting:** The results are currently limited to uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussian components. While this simplification allows for rigorous analysis, it's unclear how well these results generalize to more realistic scenarios with varying weights and covariance structures.  Acknowledging this limitation in the conclusion is important, but exploring potential avenues for generalization would be valuable future work.
* **Practical Impact Quantification:** While the paper shows EM converges to bad optima *with high probability*, quantifying the magnitude of the performance loss (i.e., how much worse is the suboptimal solution) could strengthen the practical impact of the findings.
* **Step-size Sensitivity:** Theorem 3 requires a step-size `s` in (0,1) for the first-order EM, but doesn't provide guidance on how this choice affects the probability of convergence to a bad local optimum. A discussion of step-size selection would be useful.



**4. Specific Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Introduction:** Could briefly mention that the existence of bad local maxima impacts the reliability of confidence intervals or uncertainty quantification based on the likelihood.
* **Theorem 1:**  It would be beneficial to visually illustrate the bad local maximum for M=3 in a figure, complementing the existing visualization for M=2.
* **Section 3.2:** When discussing the exponential repetition of EM runs, mention computational cost explicitly. E.g. “This necessitates running EM e^Ω(M) times, making this approach computationally intractable for even moderately sized M.”
* **Theorem 4:**  Clarify that the result regarding saddle points applies specifically to the first-order EM algorithm, as noted by the authors.
* **Conclusion:**  Expanding on potential future research directions, such as exploring generalization to non-isotropic Gaussians or developing more robust initialization schemes, would enhance the paper's impact.



**5. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel and significant theoretical result.
* Strong algorithmic implications with practical relevance.
* Technically sound and rigorously proven.
* Exceptionally well-written and organized.
* Addresses a fundamental open problem in the field.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Results are limited to a specific GMM setting.
* Quantification of performance loss in suboptimal solutions would be helpful.




**Overall:** I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the theory of GMM estimation. The demonstrated existence of bad local maxima and the associated algorithmic implications are important and warrant acceptance to NIPS.  The limitations of the setting are acknowledged, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
