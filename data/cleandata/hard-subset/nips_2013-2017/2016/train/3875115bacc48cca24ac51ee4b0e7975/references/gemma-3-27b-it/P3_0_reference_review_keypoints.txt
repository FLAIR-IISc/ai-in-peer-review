- Demonstrates existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima for a 3-component GMM, contradicting Srebro (2007).
- Shows EM and first-order EM with random-sample initialization converge to these suboptimal points with exponentially high probability.
- Proves issues are not merely due to saddle points, using recent gradient-descent results.
- Addresses a fundamental problem in GMM estimation with rigorous theoretical analysis.
- Constructs a specific GMM instance that yields bad local maxima and analyzes algorithmic consequences.
- Provides strong bounds on the probability of converging to suboptimal solutions.
- Connects theoretical findings to practical algorithm behavior, explaining common EM failures.
- Paper is generally well-written, logically organized, and provides thorough background and motivation.
- Proofs are technically sound and appear rigorous, though complex.
- The work is highly original and applies novel arguments to link counterexamples to algorithmic behavior.
- Results are significant, highlighting exponential dependence on number of components and motivating better methods.
- Scope is limited to uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians with fixed variances, restricting generality.
- Proof complexity may hinder accessibility for non-experts; more intuitive explanations would help.
- Limited discussion of practical mitigation strategies (e.g., improved initialization or global methods).
- Could better clarify why prior EM analyses do not resolve the issues shown here.
- Overall technically strong and impactful despite scope limitations.
- Recommendation: Accept, with minor revisions to discuss mitigation strategies and connections to prior EM analysis.
