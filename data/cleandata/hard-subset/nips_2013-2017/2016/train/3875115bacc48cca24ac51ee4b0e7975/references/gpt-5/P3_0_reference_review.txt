Summary and relation to prior work
The paper resolves Srebro’s 2007 conjecture by constructing explicit, well-separated, uniformly weighted, spherical GMMs (unit covariance, fixed variances) for which the population log-likelihood has spurious local maxima, even in 1D and for M ≥ 3 components. Moreover, the gap to the global optimum can be made arbitrarily large. Algorithmically, the authors show that EM and first-order EM (gradient ascent on the population likelihood) with a standard random initialization (drawing initial centers from the data distribution) converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−cM, i.e., exponential failure probability in M. Finally, they show first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points with probability one, suggesting failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddle proliferation.

This work complements a line of results that provide local convergence guarantees for EM under good initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and computationally efficient, statistically consistent alternatives based on clustering or moments (Arora et al.; Dasgupta and Schulman; Vempala and Wang; Belkin and Sinha; Moitra and Valiant; Hsu and Kakade). It also contrasts with empirical intuition and the open nature of the M = 2 case. The construction builds on ideas in nonconvex landscapes where all local optima can be near-global in some problems (e.g., Loh and Wainwright; Wang et al.), showing that GMM likelihoods do not share that benign structure.

Strengths
- Clear negative resolution of a visible open conjecture with a simple, sharp construction (1D, M ≥ 3, uniform weights, spherical, arbitrarily large separation).
- Algorithmic consequences are concrete: under a standard random initialization, both EM and first-order EM fail with exponentially high probability via a hierarchical “cluster-of-clusters” instance.
- The result that first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles focuses blame on local maxima, aligning with recent dynamical systems-based analyses of gradient methods (Lee et al.; Panageas and Piliouras).
- Conceptual clarity and relevance: cautions against naive random restarts and motivates careful initialization or non-likelihood-based algorithms even in very favorable settings.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Finite-sample implications are claimed to follow “straightforwardly via standard empirical process arguments,” but no explicit finite-sample theorem is given. Because finite-sample likelihoods can have many additional local maxima (Améndola et al., 2015), the transfer from population constructions to Ln needs care: (i) uniform approximation of L by Ln on relevant neighborhoods; (ii) persistence of critical points/basins; and (iii) tracking of EM/first-order EM iterates from sample to population dynamics. Providing an explicit theorem with n scaling would strengthen the practical message.
- Scope of separation: the construction is in d = 1. Although one can embed the 1D instance into higher dimensions, it would help to state this explicitly (e.g., place means on a line and use spherical noise) and clarify how “well-separated” scales with d. The stated “well-separated” condition is ξ = Ω(√d); in d = 1 this is Ω(1), but the hierarchical construction likely demands separations that grow across levels—quantify this.
- Quantitative details: The “arbitrarily bad” gap result would benefit from an explicit dependence of Cgap on separation and cluster spacings, and the exact conditions ensuring the suboptimal configuration is a strict local maximum (e.g., verifying the Hessian is negative definite in the specified neighborhood).
- First-order EM convergence: Theorem 4(a) asserts convergence of iterates to a critical point for stepsize s ∈ (0, 1). For general smooth nonconvex objectives, gradient ascent with fixed step size need not converge (limit cycles can occur if the step is too large). Please clarify the exact mode of convergence (to a point vs. to a connected set) and the assumptions on L (e.g., real-analytic plus Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz property, Lipschitz gradient) that ensure convergence rather than mere accumulation to the critical set. If you leverage monotonic ascent or KŁ, state it explicitly.
- EM vs. first-order EM: The avoidance of strict saddle points is only shown for first-order EM. Given that EM is not a gradient method, can any analogous statement be proved (perhaps via a generalized stable manifold theorem or KŁ analysis of the EM map)? Even a partial result would improve the algorithmic picture.
- Practical impact: While the theoretical consequences are strong, a small empirical illustration (even synthetic) showing EM’s failure probability scaling with M on your constructed instances would help ground the message for practitioners.
- Initialization model: In practice, K-means++ or method-of-moments seeding is common. It would be useful to discuss whether the negative results extend to common practical initializers beyond uniform data sampling, and to what extent the exponential failure persists under mild improvements to initialization.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The constructions and probability bounds are plausible and align with known tools. However, the finite-sample transfer and convergence details in Theorem 4(a) would benefit from more formal statements and proofs (or at least precise assumptions).
- Completeness: The paper is mostly self-contained at the population level and careful about assumptions (fixed variances to avoid singularities).

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The geometric intuition for M = 3 is helpful. Some references have formatting issues; tightening the exposition around separation scales, the recursive construction, and the proof sketches would aid readability.

Originality
- Resolves a conjecture and provides a new hierarchical construction for exponential failure of random initialization. The strict-saddle avoidance argument in this specific EM context is also timely.

Significance
- High. Establishes fundamental limitations of likelihood-based local search for GMMs even in favorable regimes, influencing algorithm design (initialization, alternative estimators) and theoretical understanding of nonconvex landscapes.

Pros
- Resolves a notable open question with a clean counterexample.
- Strong, interpretable algorithmic consequences with exponential failure probabilities.
- Clarifies the role of saddles vs. local maxima in EM’s failures.

Cons
- Lacks explicit finite-sample results and empirical validation.
- Some technical claims (gradient convergence) need sharper assumptions and statements.
- Limited discussion of robustness to nonuniform weights or slight covariance misspecification.

Recommendation
- Accept. The paper delivers a clear, well-motivated, and impactful theoretical contribution with direct algorithmic implications. Addressing the finite-sample and convergence clarifications would further strengthen it.

Confidence
- High.
