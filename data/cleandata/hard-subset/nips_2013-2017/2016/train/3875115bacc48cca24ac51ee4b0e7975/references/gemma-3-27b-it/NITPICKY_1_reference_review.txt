This paper investigates the local optima of the likelihood function for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), a fundamental problem in statistical estimation. The authors demonstrate the existence of bad local optima even in well-separated mixtures, challenging a conjecture by Srebro [2007]. They further show that the EM and first-order EM algorithms, with random initialization, converge to these suboptimal points with high probability. The work is technically rigorous and addresses a significant question regarding the reliability of widely used estimation methods.

However, despite the undeniable importance of the question addressed, and the demonstrated theoretical results, I have serious reservations regarding the paper’s clarity, presentation, and the practical relevance of the established results. My assessment leans towards rejection due to the accumulation of numerous, albeit individually minor, flaws.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The theoretical result demonstrating bad local optima in well-separated GMMs is genuinely novel and provides a counterexample to a long-standing conjecture.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The proofs appear mathematically sound, building on existing literature but extending the understanding of the likelihood surface’s structure.
* **Relevance:** The question of local optima is crucial for understanding the behavior of EM and related algorithms.

**Weaknesses & Detailed Comments:**

* **Clarity and Organization (Major Concern):** The paper suffers from a significant lack of clarity in its presentation. The introduction, while outlining the motivation, is overly dense and relies heavily on citations without sufficient explanation. Key concepts, such as the specific random initialization scheme, are introduced without a clear rationale for its choice beyond being “practical.” The organization within sections is often disjointed, jumping between theoretical results and their implications without smooth transitions.
* **Notational Inconsistency:** Numerous minor notational inconsistencies appear throughout. For instance, the use of both `µ` (Greek mu) and `mu` to represent mean vectors is confusing. Similarly, the definition of `L(µ)` in Section 2.1 (population log-likelihood) appears to be missing the normalizing constant (integral of the density function). This omission, although potentially corrected in subsequent derivations, diminishes the reader’s confidence in the technical details.
* **Missing Definitions/Context:** Several crucial definitions and background elements are missing or lack sufficient context. The definition of a “strict saddle point” (Definition 1) is introduced abruptly, without mentioning its relevance to the broader discussion. The rationale for focusing on uniformly weighted mixtures and fixed covariance parameters is stated, but the implications of relaxing these assumptions are not discussed.
* **Overly Assertive Language:** The authors frequently employ overly assertive language (“Our proofs introduce new techniques…”, “This result strongly indicates…”). While the results are significant, the claims of broad impact are not fully substantiated. For example, claiming the techniques will “aid in developing a better understanding of the behavior of algorithms for non-convex optimization” is a strong statement that requires more justification.
* **Lack of Discussion on Practical Impact:** While the theoretical results are compelling, the paper lacks a nuanced discussion of their practical implications. The exponential dependence on `M` in the convergence probability of the EM algorithm is acknowledged, but the authors do not adequately discuss how this translates into real-world scenarios with typical dimensionality and sample sizes. It's unclear if this exponential slowdown is truly prohibitive in practice, or whether the conditions leading to this behavior are rarely encountered.
* **Figure 1 Annotation:** The annotation for Figure 1 is somewhat cryptic. While the figure illustrates the log-likelihood and gradient norm for a 2D GMM, the significance of the observed patterns is not fully explained. A more thorough analysis of the figure’s implications would be beneficial.
* **Minor Typographical Errors:** Numerous minor typographical errors and grammatical inconsistencies exist throughout the paper. While individually insignificant, they detract from the overall polish and readability. For example, "Thorem 1" instead of "Theorem 1" appears multiple times.
* **Reference Formatting:** There are inconsistencies in the reference formatting. Some references are abbreviated (e.g., “Ho and Nguyen, 2015”), while others are not.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The theoretical result on the existence of bad local optima is a significant contribution to the field.
* The rigorous analysis and proofs are commendable.
* The topic is relevant to a broad audience of researchers working on mixture models and optimization algorithms.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of clarity and organization significantly hinders comprehension.
* The numerous minor technical flaws and inconsistencies erode confidence in the details.
* The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the practical implications of the findings.
* The overly assertive language and lack of nuanced arguments weaken the overall impact.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a novel and theoretically important result, its lack of clarity, numerous minor flaws, and insufficient discussion of practical relevance outweigh its strengths. I believe that a substantial revision, addressing the issues outlined above, is necessary before this paper would be suitable for NIPS. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors should focus on improving the paper’s clarity, ensuring technical accuracy, and providing a more nuanced discussion of the practical implications of their findings. A more conservative tone in presenting the results would also be beneficial.
