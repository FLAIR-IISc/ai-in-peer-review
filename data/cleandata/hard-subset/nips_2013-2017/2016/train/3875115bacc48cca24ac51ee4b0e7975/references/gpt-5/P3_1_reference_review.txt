Summary
The paper resolves a long-standing conjecture (Srebro, 2007) by showing that the population log-likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) can have spurious local maxima even in the benign setting of uniformly weighted, spherical, unit-variance mixtures. Specifically: (i) For any M ≥ 3, there exist well-separated 1D mixtures with arbitrarily bad local maxima (Theorem 1). (ii) EM and first-order EM (gradient EM) with random initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−cM for some universal c > 0 (Theorems 2–3), implying an exponential number of random restarts are needed to reliably hit a global maximum. (iii) First-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points (Theorem 4), suggesting that bad local optima—not saddles—are the main algorithmic obstacle in these instances. The constructive proof uses a geometric argument to certify a local maximum near a configuration that “splits” responsibility across clusters, and a recursive, hierarchical mixture construction to drive the success probability of random initialization to be exponentially small. The results are at the population level and the authors argue that standard empirical process techniques transfer the phenomena to large finite samples.

Relation to prior work
This work complements analyses of EM’s local behavior under favorable initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and algorithmic alternatives with provable guarantees under separation from clustering or moment methods (Arora et al., 2005; Moitra and Valiant, 2010; Hsu and Kakade, 2013). It goes beyond finite-sample examples of many local optima (Améndola et al., 2015) by establishing spurious local maxima in the infinite-sample limit. The non-convex optimization angle builds on recent insights that gradient methods avoid strict saddles (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016), clarifying that in this model class bad local maxima persist and dominate failure modes.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: A clean, definitive negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture in perhaps the most favorable GMM setting. This closes an open question of broad interest.
- Algorithmic implications: A sharp, exponential lower bound on the success probability of EM/first-order EM under a widely used random seeding scheme; the hierarchical construction is elegant and convincing.
- Technical novelty: The geometric “barrier” argument that certifies a local maximum near an intuitively suboptimal configuration is insightful; the recursive instance construction is well-motivated and carefully described.
- Clarity of message: The paper clearly separates structural and algorithmic results and explains why saddles are not the culprit for gradient EM.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope of models: Results apply to equal weights and known identity covariances. While well-justified to avoid degeneracies, these assumptions are restrictive relative to typical EM practice (unknown variances, unequal weights). A brief discussion of whether the constructions extend (or become even easier) in these more general settings would be valuable.
- Quantitative separation and gap: Theorems assert “well-separated” with Ω(√d) and “arbitrarily bad” local maxima via a tunable Cgap. Please make explicit: (a) how the required separation scales with Cgap and M; (b) whether the local maxima are strict and isolated (e.g., Hessian definiteness at the local maximizer).
- Population-to-sample transfer: The paper states that standard empirical process arguments transfer the results to finite samples. Given the significance of the claim, providing a concrete finite-sample corollary with explicit sample complexity (e.g., uniform sup-norm control of L̂ over the relevant neighborhood to preserve the basin and critical points) would strengthen the contribution.
- Initialization model: The random initialization draws centers i.i.d. from the mixture distribution (population analog of uniformly sampling data). In practice, k-means++ or farthest-first seeding are common. Do the negative results extend to these simple heuristics? Even a qualitative discussion would help position the implications.
- First-order EM step sizes: Theorems require s ∈ (0, 1). Is the phenomenon robust to line-search or diminishing step sizes? Since first-order EM is equivalent to gradient ascent on L, can the exponential failure probability be shown for any step size ensuring convergence to a critical point?
- Two-component case: The paper correctly emphasizes that M = 2 remains open and empirically benign. Any partial progress (e.g., nonexistence of suboptimal local maxima under certain separations) or barriers would be valuable to include.
- Exposition and references: Some references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted. The paper refers to Figure 1 but it is not included here; more figures illustrating the recursive construction would aid intuition.

Quality
The results appear technically sound and carefully argued at the level presented; proofs are deferred. The structural proof strategy and the probabilistic argument for exponentially small success probability are plausible and align with known behaviors of EM. The strict-saddle avoidance result leverages well-established theory. I encourage the authors to add explicit finite-sample corollaries and more quantitative constants.

Clarity
Overall well-written and organized, with clear statements of the main theorems and intuition for constructions. Minor clarity issues include precise definitions of “well-separated” versus the needed constants, and more detail on the neighborhoods and boundary arguments used in Theorem 1.

Originality
High. Establishing spurious local maxima for the population likelihood of uniform spherical GMMs and quantifying the exponential failure of EM under random seeding are new and resolve an open conjecture.

Significance
High. The results recalibrate expectations about EM and likelihood-based optimization for GMMs even in favorable regimes and provide concrete guidance: careful initialization (e.g., via moments/spectral methods) is essential. The techniques may inform analyses of other non-convex latent variable models.

Pros for acceptance
- Resolves an open problem with clean, general negative results.
- Strong and practically relevant algorithmic implications.
- Technically novel constructions and analyses.
- Clear, impactful message likely to influence practice and theory.

Cons for acceptance
- Restricted model class; limited discussion of extensions.
- No empirical illustration; finite-sample guarantees are only sketched.
- Limited discussion of alternative initializations common in practice.

Suggestions
- Add a finite-sample corollary with explicit sample complexity.
- Quantify separation vs. gap trade-offs and certify strictness of the local maxima.
- Discuss robustness to other seeding schemes (e.g., k-means++).
- Improve reference formatting and add missing details.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with clear significance to the NeurIPS community. I recommend acceptance after addressing the above clarifications.
