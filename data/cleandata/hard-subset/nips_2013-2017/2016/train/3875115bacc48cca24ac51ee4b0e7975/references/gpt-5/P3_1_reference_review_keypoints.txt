- Paper resolves Srebro (2007) by showing population log-likelihood of uniform spherical GMMs can have spurious local maxima even in benign settings.
- Main results: (i) For any M ≥ 3 there are well-separated 1D mixtures with arbitrarily bad local maxima (Theorem 1).
- Main results: (ii) EM and first-order EM with random init converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e−cM, implying exponentially many restarts needed (Theorems 2–3).
- Main results: (iii) First-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddles, so bad local maxima—not saddles—are the primary algorithmic obstacle (Theorem 4).
- Proof approach: geometric “barrier” to certify local maxima near cluster-splitting configurations and a recursive hierarchical mixture construction to drive failure probability exponentially small.
- Results are at the population level and authors claim standard empirical-process techniques transfer phenomena to large finite samples.
- Complements prior work analyzing EM under favorable initialization and provable alternatives (moment/spectral methods); goes beyond finite-sample examples by proving population-level spurious maxima.
- Builds on nonconvex optimization literature showing gradient methods avoid strict saddles, clarifying that persistent bad local maxima dominate here.
- Strength: Conceptual advance—clean negative resolution of a long-standing conjecture in a favorable GMM setting.
- Strength: Algorithmic implication—sharp exponential lower bound on success probability for random seeding; elegant hierarchical construction.
- Strength: Technical novelty—the geometric barrier argument and recursive construction are insightful and well-motivated.
- Strength: Clear separation of structural and algorithmic results and explanation of why saddles are not the issue.
- Weakness: Restrictive model assumptions (equal weights, known identity covariances); unclear extension to unknown variances or unequal weights.
- Weakness: Need explicit quantitative scaling: how required separation depends on Cgap and M, and whether local maxima are strict/isolated (Hessian definiteness).
- Weakness: Population-to-sample transfer is only sketched; a concrete finite-sample corollary with sample complexity is requested.
- Weakness: Initialization model uses i.i.d. draws from the mixture; unclear if negative results extend to practical seeding heuristics like k-means++ or farthest-first.
- Weakness: First-order EM results assume step size s ∈ (0,1); robustness to line-search or diminishing step sizes is unclear.
- Weakness: Two-component (M=2) case remains open and empirically benign; partial results or barriers for M=2 would be valuable.
- Weakness: Exposition issues—missing figure references and some inconsistent/incomplete references; more figures to illustrate constructions would help.
- Quality: Results appear technically sound and proofs plausible though deferred; strict-saddle avoidance leverages established theory.
- Clarity: Well-written and organized overall, but need precise constants for “well-separated” and more detail on neighborhoods used in proofs.
- Originality: High—new population-level spurious maxima result and exponential EM failure under random seeding resolving an open problem.
- Significance: High—recalibrates expectations about EM for GMMs and emphasizes the need for careful initialization; techniques likely broadly relevant.
- Pros for acceptance: resolves an open problem, strong algorithmic implications, technically novel, clear and impactful message.
- Cons for acceptance: restricted model class, no empirical illustration, finite-sample guarantees only sketched, limited discussion of practical initializations.
- Suggestions: add a finite-sample corollary with explicit sample complexity; quantify separation vs. gap and certify strictness of maxima; discuss robustness to other seeding schemes; fix references and add illustrative figures.
