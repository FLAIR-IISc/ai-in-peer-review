

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses an open question posed by Srebro (2007) regarding the absence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). Previous work by Balakrishnan et al. (2015) and Dasgupta and Schulman (2007) established local convergence guarantees for EM under certain separation conditions, while Améndola et al. (2015) showed that finite samples can exhibit unbounded local maxima. The authors resolve Srebro's conjecture by constructing a counterexample for M ≥ 3 components, demonstrating that bad local maxima can exist in the population likelihood, even for well-separated mixtures.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a theoretical construction showing that for M ≥ 3, there exist well-separated uniform mixtures of spherical Gaussians with arbitrarily bad local maxima; (2) a proof that EM and first-order EM with random initialization converge to bad critical points with probability at least 1 - e^(-cM); and (3) a demonstration that the first-order EM algorithm avoids strict saddle points, suggesting that bad local maxima—not saddle points—are the primary obstacle to convergence.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs establishing the existence of bad local maxima and the algorithmic failure probability. The counterexample for M=3 is well-constructed and intuitively explained through geometric reasoning. The authors carefully distinguish between different types of critical points (local maxima vs. saddle points), and their analysis of the first-order EM algorithm's behavior with respect to strict saddle points is particularly strong. The results are well-supported by theoretical arguments that build upon recent advances in non-convex optimization (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors provide clear motivation for their work by situating it within the existing literature, and they carefully explain the intuition behind their counterexample construction. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout, and the paper includes a helpful figure (Figure 1) that illustrates the population likelihood surface for the two-component case. The theoretical results are presented with precise statements and clear implications.

**Originality:** This work directly addresses a longstanding open question in GMM theory that has been the subject of speculation (Srebro, 2007) and empirical observation. The counterexample construction for M ≥ 3 is novel and resolves the conjecture in the negative. The algorithmic consequences—showing that random initialization fails with exponentially high probability—are significant and go beyond previous work on EM convergence. While there is related work on the behavior of EM (Balakrishnan et al., 2015; Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007), this paper provides the first rigorous demonstration of the failure of random initialization for GMMs.

**Significance:** The results have substantial theoretical and practical significance. Theoretically, they provide a definitive resolution to an open question about the structure of GMM likelihood surfaces. Practically, they explain why local search methods like EM often fail in practice for GMM estimation, and they suggest that careful initialization (beyond random sampling from data) is essential. The finding that the failure probability is exponentially small in M provides a concrete theoretical explanation for the empirical difficulty of GMM estimation with random initialization.

**Strengths:**
- Resolves a significant open question in GMM theory
- Provides rigorous theoretical guarantees with clear algorithmic implications
- Distinguishes between bad local maxima and saddle points in the optimization landscape
- Demonstrates that the failure is due to the presence of bad local maxima, not saddle points
- Strong theoretical foundation built on recent advances in non-convex optimization

**Weaknesses:**
- The results are specific to uniform mixtures of spherical Gaussians with unit variance; extension to more general cases (e.g., non-uniform weights, non-spherical covariances) is not addressed
- The construction is limited to one dimension, though the authors state the results extend to higher dimensions
- The paper does not provide concrete guidance for improved initialization schemes beyond mentioning method of moments

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* This is a significant theoretical contribution that resolves an important open question in a well-studied area of machine learning. The results are rigorous, well-motivated, and have clear implications for both theory and practice. The paper is exceptionally well-written and makes a substantial contribution to our understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces.

*Con:* The results are somewhat restricted to a specific setting (uniform weights, spherical Gaussians), though the authors argue that the results extend to more general cases. The paper does not address how to construct better initialization schemes, though this is not the primary focus of the work.

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. It makes a clear theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental question in GMM estimation, provides rigorous proofs of novel results, and has significant implications for both theory and practice. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and thoroughly situates its contributions within the existing literature. The results represent a definitive resolution to an open problem that has been the subject of speculation for over a decade.

Given the paper's technical soundness, novelty, and significance, I recommend acceptance. The results have the potential to influence both theoretical understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces and practical approaches to GMM estimation.
