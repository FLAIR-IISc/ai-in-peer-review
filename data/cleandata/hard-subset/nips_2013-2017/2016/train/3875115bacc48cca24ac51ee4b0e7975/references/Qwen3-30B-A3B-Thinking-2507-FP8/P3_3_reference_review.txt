

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental question in the theory of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs): whether all local maxima of the population likelihood are globally optimal, as conjectured by Srebro (2007). The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture by constructing a counterexample with M=3 well-separated spherical Gaussians in 1D dimension, whose population log-likelihood contains local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. They further demonstrate that the EM algorithm with random initialization converges to these bad local maxima with high probability (exponentially small success probability in M), and show that the first-order EM algorithm avoids strict saddle points.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical significance:** The paper resolves a long-standing open question in the GMM literature with a clean, elegant construction. This is a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of non-convex optimization for mixture models.

2. **Algorithmic implications:** The results have important practical consequences, demonstrating that random initialization for EM can fail catastrophically even for well-separated mixtures. This explains why practitioners often use more sophisticated initialization schemes (e.g., method of moments) despite the theoretical appeal of EM.

3. **Rigorous analysis:** The proofs are technically sound, with careful construction of the counterexample and rigorous analysis of the algorithmic behavior. The authors distinguish clearly between population and sample settings, which is crucial for the problem.

4. **Novel insights into optimization landscape:** The work provides valuable insights into why certain optimization methods fail for GMMs, distinguishing between bad local maxima and saddle points (which the first-order EM algorithm avoids).

**Weaknesses and concerns:**

1. **Limited scope on component count:** The paper only resolves the conjecture for M≥3, leaving open the question of whether bad local maxima exist for M=2. While the authors conjecture that M=2 is different, they don't provide evidence or analysis for this case. This is a significant gap since the two-component case is the most commonly studied in practice.

2. **1D construction:** The counterexample is constructed in 1D, which might not generalize to higher dimensions where GMMs are most commonly applied. The authors acknowledge this but don't discuss how their results might extend to higher dimensions, where the geometry of the likelihood surface could be more complex.

3. **Finite sample implications:** While the authors note that their results can be extended to finite samples via empirical process arguments, they don't provide concrete bounds or discuss how quickly the population behavior translates to finite samples. For practitioners, the finite-sample behavior is more relevant than the population limit.

4. **Algorithmic practicality:** The paper demonstrates that random initialization leads to failure with high probability, but doesn't quantify how large M would need to be for the failure probability to become practically significant. For example, for M=10, the failure probability is 1-e^(-c·10) ≈ 0.9999, which is devastating, but for M=3, it's 1-e^(-c·3) ≈ 0.95, which is still bad but might be more manageable in practice.

**Comparison to related work:**

The paper builds well on prior work by Srebro (2007), Balakrishnan et al. (2015), and others. It correctly situates itself in the literature by addressing a specific gap in the theoretical understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces. The authors properly cite the relevant literature, including the conjecture by Srebro and previous work on EM convergence.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that resolves an important open question in the GMM literature. The results have clear implications for the design and understanding of algorithms for GMM estimation. While the scope is limited to M≥3 and 1D, these limitations are acknowledged by the authors and represent natural directions for future work.

The paper should be accepted for NIPS. The theoretical contribution is significant, the results are well-supported, and the implications for both theory and practice are clear. The minor limitations noted above don't detract from the paper's main contribution, which is a clear negative resolution of the Srebro conjecture.

**Suggestions for improvement:**

1. The authors could briefly discuss the M=2 case in more detail, even if they don't provide a proof, to better contextualize their result.

2. A short discussion of how their population results translate to finite samples would strengthen the practical relevance.

3. Including a figure showing the likelihood surface for the M=3 counterexample would greatly enhance clarity.

In summary, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the optimization landscape for Gaussian mixture models. The results provide a rigorous explanation for the empirical observation that random initialization for EM often fails, and they highlight the necessity of careful initialization schemes for practical GMM estimation.
