

# Peer Review: "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental open question in Gaussian mixture model (GMM) theory: whether the population log-likelihood function for GMMs has no "bad" local maxima (i.e., local maxima that are not global maxima), as conjectured by Srebro [2007]. The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture by constructing a counterexample for M=3 well-separated, uniform spherical Gaussians in dimension d=1. They show that for this construction, the population log-likelihood contains local maxima whose log-likelihood values can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. The paper further demonstrates that the EM algorithm (and a first-order EM variant) with random initialization converges to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability in M. The authors also prove that first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that bad local maxima—not saddle points—are the primary cause of algorithmic failure.

This work directly relates to the extensive literature on GMMs, including foundational identifiability work by Teicher [1963], theoretical results on convergence rates by Chen [1995], and recent algorithmic analyses of EM by Balakrishnan et al. [2015]. It also connects to broader themes in non-convex optimization, such as the "no bad local minima" phenomenon observed in some high-dimensional problems.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving an open question in GMM theory. The construction of the counterexample is rigorous, and the proofs are technically sound. The authors carefully distinguish between population and finite-sample settings and provide detailed analysis of both structural properties and algorithmic consequences. The paper is complete and carefully evaluates both strengths (e.g., the connection to non-convex optimization theory) and limitations (e.g., the specific dimensionality of the counterexample).

**Originality:** The paper directly addresses a conjecture that has been open for over a decade, making it highly original. The counterexample construction is novel and non-trivial, and the connection between bad local maxima and the failure of random initialization schemes for EM is a significant theoretical contribution. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly the work on the "no bad local minima" phenomenon in other non-convex optimization settings.

**Significance:** The results have important implications for practitioners using EM for GMMs, demonstrating that random initialization can fail with exponentially high probability. This provides a theoretical justification for the need for careful initialization schemes (e.g., those based on the method of moments), which is a practical concern in the field. The paper advances the state of the art in understanding the optimization landscape of GMMs and contributes to the broader understanding of non-convex optimization in machine learning.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The main results are stated formally and supported by detailed proofs. The authors provide a geometric intuition for their counterexample construction (for M=3), which helps readers understand the intuition behind the result. The paper would benefit from visualizations of the likelihood surface (as mentioned in the introduction), but the text is otherwise clear and well-organized.

## Weaknesses

The primary weakness is the artificiality of the counterexample construction. While the authors provide a valid counterexample for M=3 in d=1, it's not clear how common such bad local maxima are in practical applications or higher-dimensional settings. The paper would benefit from more discussion about the practical relevance of these findings.

The paper focuses on the population setting and does not provide empirical evidence to support the theoretical results for finite samples. While this is expected for a theoretical paper, a brief discussion of how the results might translate to finite samples would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

The connection to recent advances in non-convex optimization (e.g., the work by Ge et al. [2015] on saddle points) could be more thoroughly developed. While the authors mention the work of Pascanu et al. [2014], they could better position their results within the broader context of non-convex optimization theory.

## Evaluation

**Quality:** 9/10 - The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs and careful analysis. The results are well-supported by theoretical arguments.

**Clarity:** 8/10 - The paper is well-written and organized, but could benefit from more visualizations to illustrate the likelihood surface.

**Originality:** 9/10 - The paper directly addresses a long-standing open question with a novel counterexample construction.

**Significance:** 8/10 - The results have important implications for understanding the limitations of EM for GMMs, though the practical relevance of the specific counterexample could be strengthened.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to a fundamental open problem in GMM theory, resolving a conjecture that has been open for over a decade. The results have important implications for understanding the limitations of the EM algorithm and highlight the need for careful initialization schemes in practice. While the counterexample is somewhat artificial, the theoretical insights are sound and provide valuable new understanding of the optimization landscape of GMMs.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a problem of significant importance to the machine learning community. The connection to broader themes in non-convex optimization strengthens its significance. The minor weaknesses in the paper (artificiality of counterexample, lack of empirical validation) are expected for a theoretical paper and do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

## Specific Comments

1. The paper mentions Figure 1 in the introduction but doesn't include it. Including a visualization of the likelihood surface for the M=2 case would help readers understand the intuition behind the construction.

2. The authors should discuss how their results relate to the "no bad local minima" phenomenon observed in other settings (e.g., Loh and Wainwright [2013]). While they mention this briefly, a more thorough discussion would strengthen the paper.

3. For practical relevance, the authors could briefly discuss how their results might inform the design of better initialization schemes for GMMs.

4. The proof techniques for the main results (particularly Theorem 1) are novel and could be of interest to the broader non-convex optimization community.

This paper makes a clear and important contribution to the theoretical understanding of GMMs and the behavior of the EM algorithm, and I believe it would be valuable to the NIPS community.
