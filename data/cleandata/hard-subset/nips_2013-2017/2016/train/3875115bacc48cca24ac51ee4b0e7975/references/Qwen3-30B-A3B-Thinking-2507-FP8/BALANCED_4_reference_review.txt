

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses a fundamental question in Gaussian mixture model (GMM) theory: whether all local maxima of the population likelihood function are global maxima, as conjectured by Srebro (2007). The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture by constructing a GMM with three well-separated spherical unit-variance Gaussians whose population log-likelihood contains arbitrarily bad local maxima. They further demonstrate that both the EM algorithm and its first-order variant with random initialization converge to these suboptimal critical points with exponentially high probability. This work builds on prior theoretical analyses of GMMs (Chen, 1995; Ho and Nguyen, 2015; Balakrishnan et al., 2015) and addresses a key open question that has been widely cited in the literature.

**Strengths:**

1. **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper resolves an important open problem that has been discussed in the GMM literature since Srebro (2007). The construction of a GMM with arbitrarily bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 components is a major theoretical advance that provides a rigorous foundation for understanding the limitations of local search algorithms for GMMs.

2. **Rigorous Analysis:** The authors provide detailed theoretical results with careful proofs, establishing both the structural properties of the likelihood surface (Theorem 1) and the algorithmic consequences (Theorems 2-4). The proof techniques for establishing the existence of bad local maxima are novel and could be applicable to other non-convex optimization problems.

3. **Clear Distinction Between Structural and Algorithmic Results:** The paper effectively separates the theoretical properties of the likelihood surface from the behavior of optimization algorithms, providing a comprehensive understanding of the problem. The distinction between local maxima and saddle points (Theorem 4) is particularly valuable.

4. **Practical Implications:** The results have clear implications for practitioners, demonstrating that random initialization for EM and gradient-based methods is insufficient for GMM estimation in the population setting, and suggesting that more sophisticated initialization schemes (e.g., based on method of moments) are necessary.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the paper provides theoretical results for the population likelihood, it lacks empirical evidence demonstrating the behavior of these algorithms on finite samples. The connection between the population results and finite-sample behavior could be strengthened with some experimental validation.

2. **M=2 Case Not Fully Resolved:** The paper acknowledges that their construction requires M ≥ 3, but does not provide evidence or intuition about why the conjecture might hold for M=2. While the authors mention this as an open question, a more thorough discussion of the M=2 case would strengthen the paper.

3. **Technical Density:** The paper is highly technical, which may limit accessibility for some readers. More intuitive explanations of why the bad local maxima exist (beyond the geometric construction) would make the results more widely understandable.

4. **Limited Discussion of Alternative Approaches:** While the paper mentions that method-of-moments approaches might be more robust, it doesn't explore how these methods specifically address the limitations of local search methods identified in the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the limitations of local search methods for Gaussian mixture models. The results are technically sound, the proofs are rigorous, and the implications for algorithm design are clear. The paper successfully resolves an important open question in the field, demonstrating that bad local maxima exist for GMMs with three or more components.

The paper is highly relevant to NIPS, addressing a fundamental theoretical question with practical implications for a widely used statistical model. The work advances the state of the art in understanding non-convex optimization in latent variable models, which is a key area of interest for the conference.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that resolves an important open question. While the lack of empirical validation and limited discussion of the M=2 case are weaknesses, the theoretical results are compelling and well-supported. The paper's significance and originality outweigh these limitations.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Add a brief empirical section demonstrating the behavior of EM on a finite sample version of the constructed GMM, showing convergence to suboptimal solutions.

2. Expand the discussion of why the M=2 case might differ, perhaps including a simple geometric argument for why bad local maxima cannot exist for M=2.

3. Include a visualization of the likelihood surface for the M=3 case (as referenced in Figure 1) to help readers intuitively understand the construction of bad local maxima.

4. Briefly discuss how the method-of-moments approaches (e.g., Moitra and Valiant, 2010; Hsu and Kakade, 2013) specifically overcome the limitations identified in this paper.

This paper represents an important advance in our understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces and the limitations of local search algorithms. The results provide a theoretical foundation for why careful initialization is crucial in practice, and the techniques developed could be applicable to other non-convex optimization problems in statistics and machine learning.
