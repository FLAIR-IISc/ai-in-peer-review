- Paper proves existence of bad local maxima in the population log-likelihood for mixtures of three well-separated Gaussians, refuting Srebro (2007).
- Shows that EM and first-order EM with data-based random initialization converge to these suboptimal points with exponentially high probability.
- Demonstrates that saddle points are not responsible for convergence failures; bad local optima are the main issue.
- Analysis and proofs are rigorous, mathematically sound, and tailored to the likelihood structure.
- Careful analysis of common initialization schemes is a notable strength.
- Writing and organization are clear, with a well-motivated introduction and logical progression.
- Addresses technical issues like infinite likelihoods and initialization concerns, showing completeness.
- Builds appropriately on prior work in identifiability, density estimation, EM analyses, and nonconvex optimization literature.
- Major limitation: existence proof is shown only for d = 1, leaving higher-dimensional behavior unclear.
- Scope is narrow: uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians with fixed variances, which may limit generalization.
- Paper lacks empirical validation or simulations to illustrate bad optima and algorithmic failures.
- Does not provide practical, efficient strategies to avoid bad local optima beyond noting potentially exponential retries of EM.
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical contribution, strong algorithmic implications, rigorous analysis, and clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: restricted setting (dimension and Gaussian assumptions), absence of empirical support, and no immediate practical remedies.
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical paper worthy of acceptance despite limitations; reviewer rating 8/10 (Strong Accept).
- Recommendations: add simulations, discuss possible extensions to higher dimensions, address computational costs and alternative initializations, and explicitly state result limitations.
