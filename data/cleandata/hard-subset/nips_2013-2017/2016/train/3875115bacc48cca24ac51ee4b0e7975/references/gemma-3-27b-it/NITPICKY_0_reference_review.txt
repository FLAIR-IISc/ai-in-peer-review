This paper investigates the local optima of the likelihood function for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), a fundamental problem in statistical estimation. The authors demonstrate the existence of bad local maxima, contradicting a conjecture by Srebro, and rigorously show that the EM and first-order EM algorithms, initialized with points drawn uniformly at random from the data, are likely to converge to these suboptimal points. The results are significant, as they cast doubt on the practical utility of local search methods, even with large sample sizes, and highlight the need for careful initialization strategies. 

However, while the core ideas are valuable, the paper suffers from a multitude of small, yet pervasive, flaws that detract from its overall quality and readability. My assessment is that, despite the potential significance, the density of issues necessitates a rejection. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Important Problem:** The existence of bad local optima in GMM estimation is a crucial question, directly impacting the reliability of widely used algorithms like EM.
*   **Rigorous Results:** The authors provide formal proofs for their claims, demonstrating the existence of bad local optima and the failure of common initialization schemes.
*   **Clear Connection to Practice:** The work directly addresses the practical limitations of EM and related algorithms, motivating the development of more robust initialization strategies.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The proofs introduce new techniques for analyzing the likelihood surface of GMMs, potentially useful for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Formatting and Typos:** The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent spacing after periods, varying font sizes within equations) and typographical errors (e.g., “M -component” vs. “M-component”, incorrect equation numbering). While individually minor, their sheer number creates a sense of carelessness.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** The notation is occasionally inconsistent. For example, both *µ* and *µ*** are used to represent the true parameters. Also, the use of both *L(µ)* and *Ln(µ)* without sufficient clarification of the distinction between population and sample likelihoods is confusing.
*   **Lack of Intuition:** While the authors state the implications of their results, the intuition behind *why* these bad local optima arise is not thoroughly explained, particularly for M > 2. The one-dimensional example with M=2 is helpful, but it doesn’t adequately generalize to higher dimensions or more components. The explanation relating the recursive structure to the exponentially small success probability is also difficult to follow.
*   **Overly Strong Claims:** The claim that the first-order EM algorithm *never* converges to a strict saddle point (Theorem 4) seems remarkably strong. The authors acknowledge the reliance on recent work and the need for a smoothness argument, but this raises concerns about the generality of the result.
*   **Redundancy:** Certain sections, particularly the introduction, repeat information unnecessarily. The introduction contains several sentences stating the obvious (e.g., “finite mixture models are widely used”).
*   **Unclear Motivation for Assumptions:** The focus on uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians feels somewhat arbitrary. While simplifying the analysis, the authors do not adequately justify why these assumptions are crucial or how the results might generalize to more realistic scenarios.
*   **Missing Related Work:** A more detailed discussion of existing work on landscape analysis of non-convex optimization problems (beyond those already cited) would be beneficial.
*   **Proof Details:** Some proof sketches lack sufficient detail, particularly regarding the application of the stable manifold theorem in Theorem 4. The reader is left to fill in substantial gaps.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamentally important problem in statistical estimation.
*   The theoretical results are novel and rigorously proven.
*   The findings have significant implications for the practical use of EM and related algorithms.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The numerous minor flaws (formatting, typos, notational inconsistencies) indicate a lack of attention to detail.
*   The lack of intuitive explanations and the overly strong claims limit the impact of the results.
*   The redundancy and lack of clarity detract from the overall readability of the paper.
*   The limited discussion of related work and the lack of justification for key assumptions weaken the broader significance of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially important contribution to the theory of GMM estimation. However, the pervasive minor flaws, coupled with issues of clarity and justification, outweigh the strengths. While the theoretical results are valuable, the paper is not polished enough for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the aforementioned concerns. The authors should prioritize a thorough proofreading, clarification of notation, and a more intuitive explanation of their findings. A more detailed discussion of related work and a more nuanced presentation of their claims would also significantly strengthen the paper.
