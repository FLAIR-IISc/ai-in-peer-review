This paper investigates the local optima of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), a fundamental problem in statistical estimation. The authors demonstrate, counter to a conjecture by Srebro [2007], the existence of arbitrarily bad local maxima even in well-separated mixtures of three Gaussians. They further show that the EM and first-order EM algorithms, initialized randomly from the data, converge to these suboptimal points with high probability, necessitating an exponential number of restarts to achieve a reasonable solution. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, building upon recent work in optimization and statistical learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Important Problem:** The question of local optima in GMM estimation is a critical one, impacting the practical reliability of widely used algorithms like EM.
*   **Negative Result:** The demonstration of bad local optima is a significant theoretical contribution, disproving a longstanding conjecture.
*   **Algorithmic Implications:** The analysis of EM and first-order EM algorithms under random initialization is insightful and provides a clear explanation for the algorithm’s practical limitations. The exponential restart requirement is a stark warning.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The proofs appear mathematically sound and build upon a strong foundation of relevant literature. The connection to recent results on gradient methods for non-convex optimization is well-motivated.
*   **Clear Structure:** The paper is logically organized, with a clear introduction, background section, and statement of results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The analysis is restricted to uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians. While this simplifies the problem, it raises questions about the generalizability of the results to more realistic settings. The authors acknowledge this but a discussion on potential extensions or the expected behavior under different assumptions (e.g., varying weights, non-isotropic covariances) would be valuable.
*   **Construction Specificity:** The construction of the bad local maxima, while mathematically elegant, feels somewhat contrived. It’s not immediately obvious whether similar pathological cases arise frequently in practice. A more intuitive explanation for the existence of these bad optima, beyond the specific geometric construction, would be helpful.
*   **Initialization Details:** The random initialization scheme is described as drawing points uniformly from the sample set. This isn’t precisely the same as drawing from the underlying distribution GMM(µ∗) as claimed, especially for finite samples. This discrepancy, while likely minor, should be clarified.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** The notation is generally clear, but there are minor inconsistencies. For example, using both `µi` and `µ∗i` to denote the true and estimated means can be confusing. Consistent use of superscripts for true parameters would improve readability.
*   **Overly Dense Notation:** The use of multiple subscripts and superscripts in equations, such as (5) and (6), makes them difficult to parse quickly. Simplifying the notation where possible would enhance clarity.
*   **Minor Typographical Errors:** There are a few minor typographical errors throughout the paper (e.g., "Thorem 1" instead of "Theorem 1"). A careful proofreading pass is recommended.
* **Lack of empirical evaluation:** It would be very helpful to see some visualizations or simple empirical results to support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental question in statistical estimation with a rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   The negative result regarding the existence of bad local optima is significant and challenges a prevailing assumption.
*   The analysis of the EM algorithm's convergence properties provides valuable insights into its practical limitations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The results are limited in scope to a specific, idealized setting.
*   The construction of the bad local maxima feels somewhat contrived.
*   Several minor issues regarding notation, clarity, and typographical errors detract from the overall quality of the presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of local optima in GMM estimation. However, the limitations in scope, the contrived nature of the construction, and the minor flaws in presentation prevent me from wholeheartedly recommending acceptance. While the theoretical results are strong, the lack of broader applicability and the areas for improvement in clarity diminish the overall impact. I believe the paper could be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing a more intuitive explanation for the existence of bad local optima and discussing potential generalizations to more realistic settings. 

**Recommendation:** Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The core ideas are important, but the paper requires substantial polishing and broadening to reach the quality expected of a NIPS publication.
