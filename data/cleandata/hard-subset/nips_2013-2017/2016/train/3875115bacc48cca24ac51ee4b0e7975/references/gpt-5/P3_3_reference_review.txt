Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the geometry of the population log-likelihood for uniformly weighted mixtures of isotropic Gaussians (fixed unit variance) and the algorithmic implications for EM and first-order EM (gradient ascent on the likelihood). It resolves Srebro’s 2007 conjecture by constructing, for any M ≥ 3 in d = 1, well-separated GMMs whose population likelihood has spurious local maxima with arbitrarily large suboptimality gaps. It further shows that EM and first-order EM, under a practical random initialization (drawing initial means i.i.d. from the mixture), converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e−cM, implying exponentially many random restarts are necessary to succeed with constant probability. Finally, leveraging ideas from stable manifold arguments, it shows first-order EM almost surely does not converge to strict saddle points; thus, failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddles.

The work is well-situated relative to: EM analyses (Dasgupta & Schulman ’07; Balakrishnan et al. ’15), moment/spectral methods (Belkin & Sinha ’10; Moitra & Valiant ’10; Hsu & Kakade ’13), identifiability (Teicher ’63; Allman et al. ’09), nonconvex landscapes with benign local optima (Loh & Wainwright ’13; Wang et al. ’14), results on saddle points (Pascanu et al. ’14; Lee et al. ’16; Panageas & Piliouras ’16), and on finite-sample likelihood pathologies (Améndola et al. ’15). The contribution is complementary and largely negative: nonexistence of a benign landscape in the population setting once M ≥ 3.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The core construction for M = 3 is plausible and clearly motivated: two true means relatively close to one another and far from a third, with a suboptimal configuration placing two estimated centers on the far component and one between the close pair. The “barrier” argument—defining a neighborhood excluding global optima and showing boundary likelihood is lower than the suboptimal interior configuration—ensures the existence of a local maximum. Extending to arbitrary M and achieving an arbitrarily large likelihood gap by tuning separations is reasonable in the 1D, unit-variance, uniform-weights setting.
- The algorithmic consequence uses a recursive, hierarchical cluster-of-clusters construction that forces an exponentially shrinking set of “balanced” initializations to avoid getting trapped, leading to success probability e−Ω(M). The intuition that EM (and gradient ascent) will not move centers across large between-cluster gaps is standard and believable in the population setting with strong separation.
- The strict-saddle avoidance result for first-order EM aligns with recent gradient-flow arguments. Given the analyticity of the population likelihood for GMMs, smoothness assumptions are natural; the authors claim a proof avoiding full-strength smoothness conditions by exploiting structure in first-order EM, which is a nice touch.

That said, several aspects would benefit from sharpening:
- Clarity on “well-separated”: earlier defined as ξ = Ω(√d). In 1D this permits two means to be closer than the third, yet all pairwise distances exceed a fixed constant. It would help to state explicitly in Theorem 1 the exact scaling of separations used to achieve arbitrary gaps and to confirm that all pairwise distances still satisfy the stated well-separated condition.
- Theorem 2 claims EM converges to a suboptimal critical point with probability ≥ 1 − e−cM. It would be helpful to make precise the required separation schedule and whether c is universal or depends on those choices, and to clarify whether convergence is to a local maximum (as opposed to potentially non-strict saddles) for EM itself. The paper later shows first-order EM avoids strict saddles; a matching statement (or discussion) for EM would strengthen the message.
- The finite-sample extension is only sketched. Given the practical importance, a theorem quantifying the sample size needed (as a function of M, separations, and the desired gap) so that empirical EM inherits the same failure probability with high probability would make the results more actionable.

Clarity:
The exposition is generally clear, with good motivation and connections to prior work. The geometric intuition for M = 3 is helpful. Suggestions:
- Move a concise but complete statement of the 1D construction (parameter values and the neighborhood/barrier argument) into the main text.
- Provide a figure illustrating the hierarchical instance used in Theorem 2 and a short calculation showing why the balanced initialization probability is e−Ω(M).
- Minor editorial issues exist in the references (incomplete bibliographic data); these should be cleaned up.

Originality:
The paper provides a definitive negative answer to a well-known conjecture in a canonical model class, and gives new algorithmic insights on EM’s failure modes with random restarts. The hierarchical construction underpinning the exponentially small success probability is, to my knowledge, novel in this context. The strict-saddle avoidance for first-order EM in this setting is also a timely contribution building on recent theory.

Significance:
The results significantly impact our understanding of likelihood landscapes for GMMs and the limits of EM with naive initialization, even in very favorable conditions (uniform weights, unit variance, strong separation, population setting). This informs both theory and practice: it justifies the need for careful initialization (e.g., via moments/spectral methods) and cautions against over-relying on random restarts. The open problem of M = 2 is clearly articulated and important.

Pros:
- Resolves a long-standing conjecture with a clean counterexample.
- Strong algorithmic consequences: exponentially many restarts needed under random initialization.
- Clear conceptual message: bad local maxima, not saddles, drive failures.
- Techniques (barrier argument; hierarchical instance) may be useful more broadly.

Cons:
- Finite-sample guarantees are not formalized.
- Dependence on separation constants and explicit constants in probabilities are not exposed.
- No empirical illustration; given NIPS audience, small simulations could strengthen impact.
- EM’s behavior vis-à-vis converging to saddles is left as a conjecture.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a finite-sample theorem with explicit n scaling and a brief simulation confirming the theory for moderate M.
- Discuss alternative initializations (e.g., k-means++, spectral/moment initializers) and whether they evade the negative results on the constructed instances.
- Clarify constants and separation regimes in Theorems 1–3, and state whether the construction trivially embeds into higher dimensions.

Recommendation:
Strong accept. The paper delivers an important, well-argued negative result with clear algorithmic implications, resolving an influential conjecture and shaping future work on initialization and robust algorithms for mixture models.
