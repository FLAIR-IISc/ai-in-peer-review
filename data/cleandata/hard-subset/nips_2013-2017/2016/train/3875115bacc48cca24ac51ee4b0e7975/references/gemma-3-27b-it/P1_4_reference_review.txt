## NIPS 2016 Paper Review: Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the landscape of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), specifically focusing on the existence of bad local maxima. Building on Srebro’s conjecture that well-separated GMMs have no bad local maxima, the authors *disprove* this conjecture by constructing a 3-component, well-separated GMM in 1D exhibiting such maxima. They then demonstrate that the EM and first-order EM algorithms, initialized by uniformly sampling from the data, converge to these suboptimal points with exponentially high probability. Crucially, they show this isn't due to saddle points but to the presence of these local maxima.  The work provides theoretical justification for the need for careful initialization strategies in GMM estimation.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds directly upon the line of work exploring the theoretical properties of GMM estimation, referencing foundational papers by Teicher, Chen, Genovese & Wasserman, and Ghosal & Van Der Vaart. It contrasts with work showing convergence guarantees for specific algorithms (Dasgupta & Schulman, Balakrishnan et al.) which often rely on strong separation assumptions or specific initializations.  The authors position their work as complementary to the broader study of robustness in maximum likelihood estimation (Donoho & Liu). It also connects to recent work on the landscape of non-convex optimization, particularly results showing that even in non-convex problems, local optima can be near-global (Loh & Wainwright, Wang et al.).

**Strengths:**

* **Important Negative Result:** Disproving Srebro’s conjecture is a significant theoretical contribution.  It provides a clear limitation to the theoretical justification of the EM algorithm.
* **Strong Algorithmic Implications:**  Demonstrating the high probability of convergence to suboptimal points under a natural initialization scheme (random sampling from data) is a compelling and practically relevant result.  This isn’t just a theoretical curiosity; it explains why EM can struggle in practice.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The proofs appear thorough and technically sound. The introduction of new techniques for reasoning about the population log-likelihood is promising.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and the introduction provides excellent context and motivation.  The notation is generally clear.
* **Distinction from Saddle Points:** The clear separation of the issue of bad local maxima from saddle points is valuable. The work convincingly argues that the problem stems from the shape of the likelihood surface, not just stationary points.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Dimensionality:** The initial construction demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima is limited to 1D. While conceptually clear, it feels somewhat restrictive.  The authors acknowledge this and focus on more general algorithmic results, but a more general structural result would be stronger.
* **Specific Initialization Scheme:** The analysis focuses on a particular random initialization scheme. While a natural choice, it’s not the only possible initialization strategy. Further exploration of the impact of different initialization methods would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Infinite Sample Setting:**  The theoretical results are largely focused on the infinite sample setting. While the authors mention the applicability to large finite samples via empirical process arguments, a more detailed exploration of the finite-sample effects would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Empirical Validation:**  While the theoretical results are compelling, the paper lacks any empirical validation.  Some simulations demonstrating the behavior of the EM algorithm on synthetic data, consistent with the theoretical predictions, would greatly enhance the impact.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically sound, the claims are well-supported (though mostly theoretically), and represents a complete, well-executed piece of work.  The construction of the example, while limited in dimensionality, is rigorous.
* **Clarity (8/10):** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized.  However, the mathematical derivations in the appendix could be more thoroughly explained.
* **Originality (9/10):**  Disproving a longstanding conjecture and providing strong algorithmic implications makes this a highly original contribution.
* **Significance (9/10):** The results are significant because they identify a fundamental limitation of standard GMM estimation methods, even in idealized settings. This has implications for both theoretical understanding and practical algorithm design.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Provides a strong theoretical justification for the difficulties often encountered in GMM estimation.
* Disproves a key conjecture in the field.
* Offers a compelling explanation for why EM can fail, even with seemingly reasonable initialization strategies.
* High-quality technical analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited dimensionality of the initial structural result.
* Lack of empirical validation.
* Focus on a specific initialization scheme.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  Despite the minor weaknesses, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to our understanding of GMM estimation.  The disproof of Srebro’s conjecture and the associated algorithmic implications are important and well-supported. The lack of empirical validation is a drawback, but the theoretical results are compelling enough to warrant acceptance. I would encourage the authors to consider including some simulations in a future version to further strengthen their findings.
