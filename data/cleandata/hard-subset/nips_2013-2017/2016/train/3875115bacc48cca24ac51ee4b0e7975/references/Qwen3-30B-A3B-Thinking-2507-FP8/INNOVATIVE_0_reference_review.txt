

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving an open question regarding the structure of likelihood surfaces for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors directly challenge Srebro's (2007) conjecture that for population GMMs, all local maxima of the likelihood function are global maxima. Through a novel construction, they demonstrate that for M ≥ 3 well-separated, equally weighted, unit-variance spherical Gaussians, the population log-likelihood can contain local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. This is a bold and surprising result that overturns a long-held intuition in the field.

The paper's main contributions are threefold: (1) a structural result showing the existence of bad local maxima for M ≥ 3; (2) an algorithmic result demonstrating that random initialization of EM and first-order EM leads to convergence to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability as M increases; and (3) a result showing that first-order EM avoids strict saddle points, suggesting the failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The significance of this work is substantial. It fundamentally changes our understanding of the behavior of EM for GMMs, explaining why repeated random initialization is often necessary in practice. The paper provides theoretical justification for the empirical observation that EM often requires multiple restarts, and it suggests that more sophisticated initialization schemes are critical for effective GMM estimation. This has direct implications for both theoretical understanding and practical algorithm design.

The paper excels in originality. The counterexample construction for M ≥ 3 is novel and elegant, and the connection between the structural result and algorithmic failure is compelling. The authors clearly distinguish their work from previous literature on GMMs (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. 2015, Ho and Nguyen 2015) and properly contextualize their contribution relative to Srebro's conjecture.

The quality of the paper is high. The theoretical results are rigorously proven, with detailed arguments provided in the appendix. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making the complex theoretical arguments accessible to readers. The authors are careful to note the limitations of their results (e.g., the construction requires M ≥ 3) and properly contextualize their findings.

The paper's main limitation is that it focuses on the idealized case of well-separated, equally weighted, spherical Gaussians with unit variance. However, the authors correctly note that their results extend to large finite samples via standard empirical process arguments, and their main insight about the failure of random initialization would likely hold in more general settings.

This paper is a clear example of the kind of bold theoretical work that advances our fundamental understanding of statistical methods. While previous work on GMMs has focused on convergence rates or algorithmic guarantees under strong separation assumptions, this paper addresses a more foundational question about the landscape of the likelihood surface itself.

The paper's contribution is not incremental—it directly challenges a long-standing conjecture and provides deep theoretical insights with practical implications. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this work highly valuable. The results are surprising, significant, and well-supported, and they represent a meaningful advance in our understanding of GMMs.

I recommend acceptance. This paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS, as it provides a fundamental theoretical insight that will shape future research in mixture models and non-convex optimization. The results are not just of theoretical interest—they directly explain a common practical challenge in GMM estimation and suggest new directions for algorithm design.

The paper's main strength is its bold theoretical contribution that overturns a widely held intuition. The main weakness is the restricted setting of the results (well-separated, equally weighted, spherical Gaussians), but this is a reasonable starting point for a fundamental theoretical question, and the authors properly acknowledge the limitations.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field. It deserves to be published at NIPS as a novel and impactful theoretical work.
