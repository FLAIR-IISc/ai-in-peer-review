Summary
The paper studies the population log-likelihood landscape of uniformly weighted mixtures of isotropic Gaussians and its algorithmic consequences for EM and gradient-based variants. It resolves a conjecture (Srebro, 2007) by constructing, for any M ≥ 3, a 1D, well-separated, unit-variance GMM whose population likelihood admits suboptimal local maxima that can be arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. It then shows that EM and first-order EM (gradient ascent on the population likelihood) with standard random initialization (drawing initial centers i.i.d. from the mixture) converge to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-cM}. The construction uses a hierarchical clustering of true centers that traps local search unless an exponentially unlikely combinatorial event occurs at initialization. Finally, using tools inspired by recent results on gradient dynamics, the paper shows first-order EM does not converge to strict saddle points (with probability 1 under random initialization), suggesting that bad local maxima—not saddles—explain failures.

Relation to prior work
This work complements analyses of EM under strong initialization or separation (Dasgupta & Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and stands in contrast to positive “benign landscape” results in other nonconvex problems (e.g., Loh & Wainwright, 2013; Wang et al., 2014). It sharpens the informal conjecture that the population GMM likelihood might be well-behaved in the infinite-sample limit by providing explicit counterexamples with strong separation and uniform weights. Algorithmically, it contrasts with clustering/moment-based approaches that succeed under separation or identifiability assumptions (Arora et al., 2005; Moitra & Valiant, 2010; Hsu & Kakade, 2013), by showing random-restart EM requires exponentially many restarts in M on natural instances. The strict-saddle avoidance follows the line of work of Lee et al. (2016) and Panageas & Piliouras (2016).

Strengths
- Clear resolution of a well-known open conjecture via an explicit and conceptually simple construction (already in 1D, M=3).
- Strong algorithmic implications: EM and gradient EM provably fail with high probability under standard random initialization, with quantitative exponential bounds in M via a recursive hierarchical instance.
- The strict-saddle result cleanly separates the source of failure (bad local maxima rather than saddles).
- The paper is well written, with intuitive explanations (e.g., two-close-one-far intuition for M=3) and a coherent storyline from geometry to algorithmic consequences.
- Results seem to transfer to finite samples via standard concentration arguments.

Weaknesses and questions
- Quantitative separation: “Well-separated” is defined as Ω(√d); the main counterexample is in d=1. Please quantify the explicit separation required as a function of M and the desired likelihood gap Cgap, and whether constant separation suffices in 1D. A statement of minimal separation conditions would strengthen the result.
- Finite-sample implications: The paper claims straightforward extensions to finite n. Can the authors provide sample-size requirements (as functions of M, Cgap, separation) under which the empirical landscape inherits the bad local maxima and the EM failure probability is ≥ 1 − e^{-cM}? This would aid practitioners.
- Initialization model: The negative results hinge on initialization by i.i.d. samples from the mixture (equivalently, uniform-from-data). How sensitive are the conclusions to other common initializations (e.g., k-means++, splitting, or balanced seeding)? Do the hierarchical hard instances also foil k-means++ seeded EM with high probability?
- Step size in gradient EM: Theorems for first-order EM assume s ∈ (0,1). Does the failure persist under line-search or adaptive steps (e.g., diminishing step sizes)? For EM proper (not gradient EM), are similar strict-saddle avoidance statements expected? The paper conjectures yes; clarifying technical barriers would be valuable.
- Model misspecification and weight imbalance: The counterexamples use uniform weights and unit variances. Do the constructions extend to unequal weights or mild variance mismatch? Are there regimes (e.g., balanced clusters with large separation, M=2) where benign landscapes can be proved?
- Two-component case: The paper notes the M=2 case remains open; given its practical importance, any partial progress (e.g., in 1D or with separation scaling) would be impactful. Even a conjecture with stronger evidence would be useful.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The structural constructions and probabilistic arguments are plausible and well-motivated. The hierarchical argument for e^{-Ω(M)} success probability is compelling; still, providing sharper constants or a clean counting argument in the main text would improve verifiability without diving into appendix details. The strict-saddle result builds on established dynamical systems arguments; outlining the key lemmas in the main paper would enhance accessibility.
- Completeness: For a theory paper, the work is self-contained at the population level. A brief finite-sample corollary or theorem would round out the contribution.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. Suggestions:
  - Move a distilled statement of the hierarchical construction (with a small illustrative figure) into the main text.
  - Define “bad critical point” consistently (sometimes used interchangeably with “local maximum” in the narrative).
  - Provide an explicit discussion of constants and separation in Theorems 1–3.

Originality
- The resolution of Srebro’s conjecture with explicit, well-separated counterexamples and the exponential failure probability for random-restart EM are original and significant advances.

Significance
- High. These results reshape our understanding of likelihood landscapes for GMMs and caution against naive random-restart EM even in favorable regimes, motivating principled initialization or alternative estimators.

Pros for acceptance
- Resolves a notable open problem with clean constructions.
- Strong, general algorithmic negative results with clear practical implications.
- Clear writing, good intuition, and broad interest to the NIPS community.

Cons against acceptance
- Finite-sample guarantees and quantitative separation thresholds are deferred/implicit.
- Limited discussion of alternative initializations and robustness beyond uniform weights/unit variance.
- No empirical illustrations, which could help ground the theory.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a finite-sample corollary with explicit n scaling.
- Quantify required separations as functions of M and Cgap.
- Discuss k-means++ (or other) seeding on the hard instances.
- Clarify the scope of the strict-saddle result for EM (not only first-order EM), and comment on adaptive step sizes.

Overall recommendation
- Strong accept. This is a well-executed theoretical contribution with clear impact on how we view EM and likelihood-based methods for GMMs, and it will interest both theory and practice audiences.
