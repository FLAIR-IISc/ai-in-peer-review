Summary
The paper delivers a sharp negative answer to Srebro’s conjecture about the benign landscape of the population log-likelihood for uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The authors construct, for any M ≥ 3, 1D well-separated mixtures whose population likelihood admits spurious local maxima that can be arbitrarily worse than the global optimum. Building on this, they prove that EM (and first-order/gradient EM) initialized by drawing M centers i.i.d. from the underlying mixture converges to suboptimal critical points with probability at least 1 − e^{-cM}, implying exponentially many restarts are needed to attain a good solution. They also show gradient EM with constant stepsize avoids strict saddles almost surely, so the failures are due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points. Techniques include a geometric construction for M = 3 and a hierarchical/recursive cluster construction to drive the exponentially small success probability.

Relation to prior work
The paper sits squarely in a line of work on the optimization and statistical properties of GMMs and EM: it complements positive local convergence results for EM under good initialization (e.g., Balakrishnan et al. 2015) and algorithmic results under separation or method-of-moments assumptions (e.g., Dasgupta-Schulman 2007; Hsu-Kakade 2013; Moitra-Valiant 2010). It directly addresses Srebro’s 2007 conjecture on the absence of bad local maxima in the population setting. The discussion of escaping saddle points connects to contemporary results on gradient dynamics (Lee et al. 2016; Panageas-Piliouras 2016). To my knowledge, no prior work established arbitrarily bad local maxima for such a favorable GMM setting, nor exponential failure probability for EM with the most common random initialization.

Strengths
- High-novelty structural result: a clean, population-level counterexample in arguably the most favorable GMM regime (equal weights, spherical, unit variance, well-separated) and already in 1D. This is a new conceptual pivot for how we think about the EM landscape.
- Strong algorithmic consequences: the exponential-failure probability under natural random initialization is both surprising and practically relevant, challenging a pervasive heuristic.
- Clarity of the message: the geometric intuition (two close centers vs. a far third; recursive clusterings) provides a crisp picture of why and how basins trap EM.
- Technical depth: the hierarchical construction to amplify failure probability to e^{-Ω(M)} is clever, and the strict-saddle avoidance result for gradient EM isolates bad local maxima as the culprit.
- Broader impact: the results motivate nontrivial initialization schemes (moments/spectral pilots, careful clustering) and caution against blind EM restarts even in large-sample/population regimes.

Weaknesses / questions
- Scope of assumptions:
  - The constructions are in d = 1 with equal weights and fixed unit variances. While this makes the negative result stronger in some sense, it leaves open how the phenomena scale with dimension and whether heteroscedastic or mis-specified weights exacerbate or mitigate the issue. A brief discussion of robustness to slightly unequal weights/variances would help.
  - EM with random initialization is modeled by sampling i.i.d. from the mixture. In practice, initialization from the dataset without replacement and/or k-means++-style seeding is common. How sensitive are the results to these variants? Do k-means++-type schemes escape the bad basins on your constructions?
- Quantitative details:
  - The separation requirement is described informally as “well-separated”; in the main text, please state the explicit scale used in the constructions and how constants (including c in 1 − e^{-cM} and the gap C_gap) depend on separation.
  - For Theorem 3, how restrictive is s ∈ (0,1)? Could larger steps (or line search/momentum) theoretically jump basins in your construction, or does the argument extend?
- Empirical illustration: Although the results are population-level and self-contained theoretically, a small synthetic experiment (e.g., 1D, M = 3) visualizing the landscape and showing EM’s attraction regions would greatly aid readers.
- Presentation:
  - The M = 3 construction is central: include more of the concrete details and a figure in the main text rather than deferring almost entirely to the appendix.
  - The recursive construction for Theorem 2 is elegant; a worked-out probability calculation in the main text would make the e^{-Ω(M)} scaling more transparent.
  - Minor: polish references and formatting inconsistencies; a few citations have placeholders or typos.

Quality
The theoretical claims are well-motivated and, as presented, appear technically sound. The intuition and proof sketches align, and the consequences are carefully stated. I appreciate the authors’ care in separating EM versus first-order EM and in attributing the failure to local maxima rather than saddles.

Clarity
Overall clearly written and well-organized. The paper would benefit from moving key construction details and an illustrative figure into the main body, explicitly stating separation scales/constants, and tightening reference formatting.

Originality
High. The counterexample in the population setting for such a benign GMM class and the exponential failure probability for EM with random initialization are, to my knowledge, new and important. The use of hierarchical cluster structure to amplify failure is particularly neat.

Significance
High. This work changes how we should think about the EM landscape for mixtures: even in the best-specified, well-separated regime, bad local maxima and exponentially small success under standard random initialization are real. It will influence both theory (landscape analyses, initialization schemes) and practice (discouraging naive EM restarts, encouraging pilot estimators).

Suggestions
- Add a finite-sample corollary with explicit rates via standard empirical process arguments to make the practical impact concrete.
- Discuss robustness to slight weight/covariance perturbations and to common initialization heuristics (e.g., k-means++, splitting, deterministic annealing).
- Include a simple 1D visualization for M = 3 and a brief computation illustrating the hierarchical e^{-Ω(M)} probability.
- Clarify whether your bad local maxima are strict local maxima (negative-definite Hessian) and, if so, state it explicitly.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a bold, novel, and impactful theoretical contribution that overturns a widely held belief and has clear algorithmic implications, even if some presentation details could be improved.
