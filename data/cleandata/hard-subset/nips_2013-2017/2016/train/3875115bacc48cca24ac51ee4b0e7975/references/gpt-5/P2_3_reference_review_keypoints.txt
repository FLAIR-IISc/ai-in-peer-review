- Constructs 1D, well-separated uniform spherical GMMs for M ≥ 3 with spurious local maxima, refuting Srebro (2007).
- Shows EM and first-order EM with random mixture-based initialization converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e−cM unless exponentially many restarts are used.
- Proves first-order EM almost surely avoids strict saddle points, so failures stem from bad local maxima, not saddles.
- Provides constructive proofs and argues population results transfer to large-sample regimes via standard arguments.
- Complements prior work that assumes good initialization or strong separation and contrasts with moment/spectral methods.
- Technical claims are precise, proof ideas plausible, and the recursive clustering argument for the e−Ω(M) failure probability is convincing.
- Core theoretical claims appear rigorously proved in appendices with clear stated assumptions (uniform weights, identity covariance, population setting, step-size range).
- Authors acknowledge limitations: two-component case remains open and results depend on a specific random initialization scheme.
- Exposition is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful 2-component visualization.
- Needs sharper quantification of “well-separated” (ξ), explicit derivation of constants in the e−cM bounds, and fixing reference formatting.
- Recommends adding a small synthetic experiment to illustrate convergence to suboptimal solutions under random initialization.
- Originality: novel negative resolution of a recognized open problem and a new recursive construction yielding exponentially small success probability.
- Conceptually significant: changes understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes and tempers optimism about naive EM initialization.
- Practical impact: justifies careful initialization or alternative estimators (method-of-moments, spectral, k-means++-style seeding), though constructed instances are adversarial.
- Scope limitations: strongest results in population, uniform-weight, identity-covariance, d = 1 setting; finite-sample constants and dimension dependence need more discussion.
- Open questions: extensions to higher dimensions and unknown covariances, whether construction defeats k-means++ or other heuristics, explicit separation/gap constants, and sample-size requirements.
- Suggested improvements: add 1D/2D empirical illustration, clarify dimension/separation requirements, discuss variable/nonconstant step sizes, and tighten references.
- Acceptance arguments: resolves notable open question, strong theoretical contribution with sharp probability bounds, and likely to influence future work.
- Rejection arguments: negative results in a specialized setting with limited empirical validation and reliance on a specific random initialization.
- Recommendation: Accept.
