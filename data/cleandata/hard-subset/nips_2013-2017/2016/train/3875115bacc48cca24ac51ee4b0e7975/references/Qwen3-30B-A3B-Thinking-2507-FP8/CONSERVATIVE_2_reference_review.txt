

# Review for "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

This paper addresses Srebro's conjecture (2007) that for Gaussian mixture models (GMMs), any local maximum of the likelihood function is a global maximum in the limit of infinite samples. The authors claim to provide a negative answer by constructing a counterexample with M=3 well-separated spherical unit-variance Gaussians that contains arbitrarily bad local maxima. They further prove that EM and first-order EM algorithms with random initialization converge to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by resolving an open problem in the GMM literature. The construction for M=3 is carefully developed, and the proofs appear technically sound (though deferred to the appendix). The connection between the structural properties of the likelihood surface and the algorithmic behavior of EM is well-motivated. The authors also provide a clear distinction between bad local maxima and strict saddle points, which is relevant to recent work on non-convex optimization (e.g., Lee et al., 2016).

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is its focus on the population setting (infinite samples), which has limited relevance to practical applications where finite samples are used. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not adequately address how their theoretical results translate to finite samples. The claim that "random initialization schemes for the population EM algorithm are likely to fail with high probability" does not directly translate to the finite-sample setting where EM is actually used.

The paper's most significant claim—that EM with random initialization fails with exponentially high probability for well-separated mixtures—relies on a very specific construction. The counterexample requires the true centers to be arranged in a particular geometric configuration (two centers close together and one far away), which is not representative of typical GMM applications. The authors do not demonstrate how common this configuration is in practice or whether it affects real-world GMM estimation.

Perhaps most concerning is the lack of empirical validation. While the paper provides theoretical results for the population likelihood, it does not include any experiments on finite datasets to demonstrate that the claimed failure modes actually occur in practice. The paper's title suggests "algorithmic consequences," yet there is no evidence that these theoretical results translate to practical difficulties with EM.

The paper also overstates the implications for the field. The authors claim that "repeated random initialization followed by local search... can fail to produce useful estimates under reasonable constraints on computational complexity," but this is only shown to hold for their specific construction, not for general GMMs. Their Theorem 2 requires a carefully constructed mixture with recursive clustering structure, which is not typical in real-world applications.

The paper's focus on M=3 components is also problematic. As the authors themselves note in the conclusion, it's an open question whether at least three components are necessary for bad local maxima to exist. If the conjecture holds for M=2 (which is a common case in practice), then the paper's results would have limited practical relevance.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the understanding of GMM likelihood surfaces, its practical significance is severely limited by its focus on the population setting and the highly specific construction used. The lack of empirical validation and the overstatement of practical implications make this paper unsuitable for NIPS, which typically values both theoretical rigor and practical relevance.

The authors should be commended for addressing an important open problem, but their claims about algorithmic consequences are not sufficiently supported. To strengthen this paper, the authors would need to:
1. Extend their results to finite samples using standard empirical process arguments
2. Demonstrate that their counterexample is representative of common GMM scenarios
3. Provide empirical evidence showing that the claimed failure modes occur in practice

Without these, the paper's contribution is primarily theoretical with minimal practical impact, which does not align with NIPS' focus on impactful machine learning research. The field would benefit more from a paper that demonstrates how to overcome these theoretical limitations in practice, rather than merely demonstrating their existence.
