

This paper addresses an open question posed by Srebro [2007] regarding the absence of "bad" local maxima in the population likelihood function of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). Srebro conjectured that for uniformly weighted mixtures of isotropic Gaussians, all local maxima of the population likelihood are global maxima. The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture by constructing a counterexample for M ≥ 3 components in 1D, demonstrating that arbitrarily bad local maxima exist. They further prove that the EM algorithm and its first-order variant with random initialization converge to such suboptimal critical points with exponentially high probability in M, and that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points with probability one.

The paper makes three significant contributions: (1) a rigorous construction of a GMM with bad local maxima contradicting Srebro's conjecture, (2) algorithmic results showing that random initialization fails with exponentially high probability for EM and first-order EM, and (3) theoretical evidence that failure stems from bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The proofs are rigorous, with clear construction of the counterexample and careful analysis of the EM algorithm's behavior. The authors provide excellent context by connecting their work to previous literature on GMM identifiability (Allman et al. [2009]), EM convergence (Balakrishnan et al. [2015]), and non-convex optimization (Lee et al. [2016]). The paper carefully distinguishes its contributions from prior work, particularly noting that the two-component case remains an open question.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and provides concrete examples (including a helpful 1D visualization of the two-component case). The technical arguments are logically organized, with clear definitions and precise statements of theorems. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results.

The novelty is high. The resolution of Srebro's conjecture is a significant theoretical advance. The counterexample construction is novel, and the algorithmic results about the failure of random initialization with exponentially high probability represent new insights into the practical limitations of EM. The paper explicitly addresses the gap between population and finite-sample settings, noting that results extend to finite samples via standard empirical process arguments.

The significance is substantial. The findings have direct implications for practitioners using EM for GMM estimation, demonstrating that random initialization can fail with high probability even for well-separated mixtures. This explains why careful initialization schemes (e.g., method of moments-based approaches) are necessary in practice. The results also contribute to the broader understanding of non-convex optimization landscapes in statistical models.

The paper's main limitation is its focus on the population setting, though the authors note that the results extend to finite samples. The counterexample construction is specific to 1D, though the generalization to M ≥ 3 components is provided. These limitations are well-justified and do not diminish the paper's theoretical contribution.

Strengths:
- Resolves a long-standing open problem in the field
- Provides rigorous proofs for all claims
- Clear exposition with helpful examples and figures
- Strong connection to previous work in GMM theory and optimization
- Practical implications clearly articulated

Weaknesses:
- Population setting focus (though finite-sample extension is noted)
- Counterexample construction limited to 1D (though generalization to higher dimensions is implied)

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solves an important open question with significant theoretical contribution
- Provides rigorous mathematical evidence for practical algorithmic failures
- Well-written, clear, and technically sound
- Has direct implications for the design of practical GMM estimation algorithms

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited to 1D case for the counterexample (though generalization is provided)
- Population setting focus (though finite-sample extension is noted)

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of GMM likelihood landscapes and optimization algorithms. The paper is technically rigorous, well-written, and addresses an important open question with significant implications for both theory and practice. The results provide a compelling explanation for the practical necessity of careful initialization schemes in GMM estimation, even for well-separated mixtures. The paper meets all NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
