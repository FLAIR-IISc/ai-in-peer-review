## Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the landscape of the likelihood function for Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), specifically focusing on the existence of bad local maxima and their implications for the convergence of common estimation algorithms like EM.  The authors challenge a conjecture by Srebro [2007] that, in the infinite sample limit, the GMM likelihood has no bad local maxima. They prove the existence of such maxima for a mixture of three Gaussians and, crucially, demonstrate that the EM and first-order EM algorithms, initialized randomly from the data, converge to these suboptimal points with high probability. This is a significant result, as it provides a theoretical justification for the observed difficulties in applying EM to GMMs and highlights the importance of careful initialization strategies.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical proof of the existence of bad local maxima, resolving an open question in the field and challenging a widely held belief. This is a substantial theoretical advance.
* **Algorithmic Implications:**  The authors don't just demonstrate the existence of these bad maxima; they also show that standard algorithms (EM and its first-order variant) are likely to get trapped in them under a reasonable initialization scheme. This connection between theory and practice is crucial.
* **Clear and Well-Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The introduction provides adequate context and motivation, and the subsequent sections logically build upon each other.
* **Rigorous Analysis:** The proofs appear careful and well-motivated. The authors leverage recent advances in gradient descent analysis to show that the issue is not saddle points but rather the presence of these bad local maxima.
* **Relevant to NIPS Audience:** The work bridges statistical modeling with optimization, a common theme within the NIPS community. The algorithmic concerns directly relate to the design and analysis of machine learning algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope:** The results are established for uniformly weighted, isotropic Gaussians. While this is a sensible starting point for theoretical analysis, it limits the direct applicability of the findings to more general GMMs with varying weights and covariance matrices. The extent to which the results generalize to these more complex settings needs further investigation.
* **Initialization Assumption:** The random initialization scheme, while practical, is specifically drawing points *from the data*. This differs from initializing with, for example, samples from a uniform distribution.  The effect of this choice on the probability of convergence to a bad local optima should be discussed more explicitly. It would be valuable to explore the performance under other reasonable initialization strategies.
* **Practical Impact Discussion:** While the paper clearly articulates the *theoretical* implications, the discussion of practical mitigation strategies is relatively brief.  While mentioning the need for careful initialization, a more detailed exploration of potential methods (e.g., k-means pre-initialization, spectral initialization, or moment-based approaches) would enhance the paper’s practical relevance.
* **Proof Complexity:** While rigorous, the proofs are quite involved and require significant background knowledge. A more intuitive, high-level explanation of the key ideas behind the proofs would benefit readers less familiar with the technical details.



**Originality:**

The work is highly original. While the problem of local optima in GMM estimation has been studied, the explicit construction of a problematic case and the demonstration of algorithmic failure under a practical initialization scheme are novel contributions. The connection to recent results on gradient descent analysis also adds to the originality.

**Significance:**

The results are significant. They provide a theoretical explanation for a long-standing practical challenge in GMM estimation.  The demonstration that random initialization can lead to poor solutions, even in large samples, underscores the importance of developing more robust initialization strategies and potentially rethinking the reliance on local search methods for GMMs.  The theoretical techniques developed in the paper are also likely to be valuable in analyzing other non-convex optimization problems.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical implications. The authors have addressed an important open problem and provided compelling evidence for the existence of bad local maxima in GMM likelihoods. While the scope is somewhat limited, the core results are well-supported and the analysis is rigorous. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Resolves an open problem with significant theoretical implications.
* Provides a theoretical justification for observed difficulties with EM algorithm.
* Demonstrates a connection between theory and practice.
* High-quality, rigorous analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope – results are specific to a constrained GMM setting.
* Could benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical mitigation strategies.
* Proofs are complex and may be inaccessible to some readers.



**Rating:**  **Strong Accept (8/10)**

**Recommendations:**

*  Discuss the potential generalization of the results to GMMs with varying weights and covariance structures.
*  Provide a more detailed discussion of practical initialization strategies and their potential effectiveness.
*  Consider adding a high-level, intuitive explanation of the key ideas behind the proofs.
*  Explicitly discuss the implications of the initialization scheme choice and potentially explore alternative schemes.
