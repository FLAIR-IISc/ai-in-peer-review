Summary
The paper resolves a conjecture of Srebro (2007) by showing that the population log-likelihood of uniformly weighted, spherical Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) can have spurious local maxima even in benign settings, and that EM-type local search with standard random initialization often converges to such bad critical points. Specifically:
- Structural result (Theorem 1): For any M ≥ 3, in d = 1 there exist well-separated unit-variance GMMs whose population likelihood has local maxima with arbitrarily worse value than the global maximum.
- Algorithmic consequences (Theorems 2–3): With random initialization (initial centers sampled from the mixture), both EM and first-order EM converge to suboptimal critical points with probability ≥ 1 − e−cM on a family of well-separated instances, unless one repeats exponentially many times.
- Landscape characterization (Theorem 4): First-order EM with a constant stepsize does not converge to strict saddle points with probability one; hence failure is due to poor local maxima rather than saddles.

The techniques hinge on constructing mixtures with hierarchical cluster structure that induce basins of attraction for suboptimal configurations, and on dynamical-systems arguments to rule out convergence to strict saddles.

Relation to prior work
This paper complements a long line of work on EM and MoM/spectral methods for GMMs. Unlike Dasgupta–Schulman (2007) and follow-ups that give positive guarantees under separation and specific initializations, this paper shows that naive random initialization generally fails at the population level. It contrasts with local convergence analyses for two-component mixtures (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and with “benign landscape” results for other nonconvex problems where all local optima are near-global. The strict-saddle non-convergence leverages ideas from Lee et al. (2016) and Panageas–Piliouras (2016). The work is well situated relative to identifiability (Teicher, Allman et al.) and robustness of MLE (Donoho–Liu) cited.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: A clean, definitive negative resolution of a widely discussed conjecture in a canonical model. The d=1, M≥3 construction is surprisingly simple yet impactful.
- Algorithmic relevance: The exponential failure probability for EM and gradient EM under widely used random initialization is a strong, practically meaningful statement. The recursive hierarchical construction is elegant and convincingly explains the exponential effect in M.
- Clarity of the message: The paper carefully distinguishes population vs. sample settings, and the claims are stated precisely with clear high-level proof ideas.
- Broader implications: Results underscore the critical role of initialization for likelihood-based methods, motivating hybrid pipelines (e.g., MoM + EM), and add to the literature on nonconvex landscapes by highlighting a regime where saddles are not the culprit.

Weaknesses and points to clarify
- Finite-sample guarantees: The paper asserts that results “carry over via standard empirical process arguments,” but no finite-sample rates are provided. Given practice relies on finite data, a theorem (even with conservative rates) showing how large n must be (as a function of M, separation, and dimension) to preserve the bad local maxima and EM failure would strengthen the contribution.
- Scope of separation: “Well-separated” is defined as Ω(√d). In the 1D constructions, the bad local maxima rely on relative spacing (two centers moderately separated and both far from a third). It would help to quantify explicitly the scaling of the likelihood gap Cgap with separations, and to state the minimal pairwise separation needed.
- Generality beyond equal weights and fixed covariances: The paper focuses on equal weights and known unit variance. While this is common, the landscape could plausibly be more pathological with unknown/unequal variances or weights. A discussion (or extension) clarifying which parts of the construction extend would be valuable.
- First-order EM technical conditions: Theorem 4 claims to avoid smoothness assumptions used in standard stable manifold arguments. Please make explicit the precise conditions under which the non-convergence-to-strict-saddles holds: measurability and absolute continuity of the initialization, invertibility/Lipschitz properties of the update map, and stepsize constraints. The stepsize s ∈ (0,1) is dimensionless; clarify how this choice relates to the smoothness/curvature constants of L for the equivalence to gradient ascent and for the diffeomorphism arguments to apply.
- Two-component case: The paper states M=2 remains open. Given Figure 1 and empirical folklore, even a partial theoretical result (e.g., no bad local maxima under a specified separation) would be impactful. Short of that, a more thorough discussion of obstacles would help.
- Practical validation: While this is a theory paper, small illustrative experiments (1D/2D mixtures demonstrating convergence to suboptimal basins under random init and the effect of hierarchical structure) would make the algorithmic consequences more concrete.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide explicit finite-sample bounds ensuring that the sample likelihood inherits the bad local maxima of the population likelihood with high probability? What are the dependencies on n, M, separation, and dimension?
2. How sensitive are Theorems 2–3 to the precise random initialization distribution (e.g., k-means++-style seeding, slight perturbations, or uniform in a bounding box covering the support)?
3. Does your hierarchical construction yield similar failure statements for mixtures in higher dimensions with centers arranged on a grid or along a tree-structured embedding?
4. For first-order EM, does a diminishing stepsize alter the strict-saddle non-convergence or failure results?

Minor comments and clarity
- A figure illustrating the M=3 construction and its basin of attraction would aid intuition.
- Please standardize references (years, venues are missing in several entries).
- Define “bad critical point” precisely on first use; clarify handling of permutation symmetries.
- In eq. (6), explicitly state the equivalence constant between the gradient of L and the first-order EM update.

Significance and originality
The results are original and significant. They decisively shift the understanding of the GMM likelihood landscape and the limits of EM with random initialization, even under favorable assumptions. The work will inform both theory (landscape analyses, initialization strategies) and practice (discouraging blind multi-start EM unless coupled to good seeding).

Recommendation
Strong accept. The paper is technically solid, clearly written, and delivers important, broadly relevant insights. Addressing the finite-sample discussion and clarifying technical conditions for Theorem 4 would further strengthen an already compelling contribution.
