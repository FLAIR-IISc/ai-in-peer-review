

# Peer Review: "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models"

This paper addresses a fundamental theoretical question regarding Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) that has been open since Srebro's 2007 conjecture. The authors resolve this conjecture by proving that for M ≥ 3 well-separated, equally weighted, isotropic Gaussian components, the population likelihood function can contain arbitrarily bad local maxima—meaning local maxima whose log-likelihood is arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. This is a significant theoretical contribution that has important algorithmic implications for the widely used EM algorithm.

**Summary and Context:**
The paper builds upon Srebro's conjecture (2007) that for GMMs, all local maxima of the population likelihood are global maxima. Previous work by Améndola et al. (2015) showed that for finite samples, bad local maxima can exist, but the population case remained open. The authors resolve this by constructing explicit counterexamples for M ≥ 3. They further demonstrate that random initialization schemes for EM and first-order EM algorithms converge to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability in M. Their work complements recent advances in understanding non-convex optimization landscapes (e.g., Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016), showing that the issue is specifically due to bad local maxima, not saddle points.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Significance:** This paper resolves a long-standing open problem in GMM theory that has been cited in multiple subsequent works (e.g., Balakrishnan et al., 2015; Ho and Nguyen, 2015). The authors provide a clear, constructive counterexample that is both elegant and rigorous.

2. **Algorithmic Implications:** The connection between the existence of bad local maxima and the failure of random initialization schemes is particularly compelling. Theorem 2 demonstrates that for well-separated mixtures, the success probability of EM with random initialization decays exponentially with M, which has profound practical implications.

3. **Technical Rigor:** The proof techniques are novel and well-executed. The recursive construction for Theorem 2 is especially clever and demonstrates deep understanding of how to amplify failure probabilities through hierarchical clustering structures.

4. **Clear Distinction:** The authors successfully distinguish between bad local maxima and strict saddle points (Theorem 4), which is crucial for understanding the optimization landscape. This distinction has been a key focus in recent non-convex optimization work (e.g., Ge et al., 2015).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dimensionality Limitation:** The main counterexample (Theorem 1) is constructed in dimension d=1, while the paper focuses on high-dimensional applications. The authors claim the construction generalizes to higher dimensions, but this isn't rigorously justified. For practical applications in high dimensions (d >> 1), the existence of bad local maxima would need to be established for d > 1.

2. **Finite Sample Implications:** While the authors mention that results carry over to finite samples via empirical process arguments, they don't provide specific sample complexity bounds. For practitioners, it's important to know how large n needs to be before these bad local maxima become relevant.

3. **Practical Relevance:** The paper doesn't address whether these bad local maxima are likely to be encountered in realistic applications with moderate M. For example, how large must M be before the exponential failure probability becomes practically relevant? The paper doesn't provide this context.

4. **Non-Isotropic Case:** The focus on isotropic Gaussians with fixed variance is somewhat restrictive. Real-world applications often involve non-isotropic Gaussians, and it's unclear whether the counterexamples would extend to this more general case.

**Comparative Analysis:**
The paper correctly situates itself within the literature on GMMs and EM algorithms. It properly references Srebro's conjecture and the related work by Améndola et al. (2015) on finite-sample bad local maxima. It also connects to the recent work on non-convex optimization (Lee et al., 2016; Panageas and Piliouras, 2016), which is highly relevant to understanding why the first-order EM algorithm avoids strict saddle points. The paper distinguishes itself from previous work by resolving the population case (infinite samples) rather than the finite-sample case.

**Significance:**
This paper is highly significant for the theoretical understanding of GMMs. It provides a definitive answer to a long-standing question and has clear implications for the design of better initialization schemes for EM. The results strongly suggest that careful initialization (e.g., using method-of-moments estimators) is essential for practical GMM estimation, rather than relying on random initialization. This is a crucial insight for both theoretical and applied researchers working with mixture models.

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental open question in the field. The results are technically sound, the construction is elegant, and the implications for algorithm design are clear. While there are some limitations in the dimensionality and finite-sample aspects, these don't undermine the core theoretical contribution.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS. The paper is well-written, makes a clear and important theoretical contribution, and addresses a problem that has been of interest to the community for over a decade. The results provide valuable theoretical justification for why certain initialization strategies (e.g., method-of-moments) might be necessary in practice, which has direct implications for the design of better algorithms.

**Minor Suggestions:**
1. The paper could briefly discuss how their results might extend to non-isotropic Gaussians.
2. A more detailed discussion of the finite sample implications would strengthen the practical relevance.
3. The authors could clarify whether their counterexamples for M=3 can be extended to higher dimensions with a simple argument.

In summary, this paper resolves a significant open question in GMM theory with important algorithmic implications. The theoretical contribution is substantial, the proofs are rigorous, and the results provide valuable insights for both theoretical and applied researchers working with mixture models.
