

# Peer Review: "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

## Summary

This paper addresses a long-standing conjecture by Srebro (2007) regarding the likelihood surface of Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). The conjecture posited that for population GMMs (infinite samples), any local maximum of the likelihood function is a global maximum. The authors provide a negative answer to this conjecture by constructing a counterexample: a uniform mixture of M ≥ 3 well-separated, spherical unit-variance Gaussians whose population log-likelihood contains local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. This is a significant theoretical contribution that resolves an open problem in the GMM literature.

The paper further analyzes algorithmic consequences, showing that with random initialization (a standard practical approach), both the EM algorithm and its first-order variant converge to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability in M. The authors also demonstrate that the failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points, as the first-order EM algorithm avoids strict saddle points with probability one. These results have important implications for practitioners, suggesting that random initialization is insufficient for GMM estimation and that careful initialization schemes are necessary.

This work builds on previous theoretical investigations of GMMs (Chen, 1995; Ho and Nguyen, 2015), EM algorithm analysis (Dasgupta and Schulman, 2007; Balakrishnan et al., 2015), and recent advances in non-convex optimization (Loh and Wainwright, 2013; Lee et al., 2016).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Significance**: The paper resolves a specific open question in GMM theory that has been unresolved since Srebro's conjecture (2007). The counterexample for M ≥ 3 is elegant and well-constructed, providing clear evidence that the conjecture is false.

2. **Rigorous Analysis**: The proofs of the main theorems are thorough and technically sound. The authors provide a detailed construction of the bad local maxima and a careful probabilistic analysis of the EM algorithm's behavior. The proof techniques for establishing the existence of bad local maxima are novel and potentially applicable to other non-convex optimization problems.

3. **Algorithmic Insight**: The paper makes a strong practical contribution by demonstrating that random initialization for EM is insufficient for GMMs, even in the idealized setting where the model is well-specified. The exponential dependence on M is particularly striking and has clear implications for practitioners.

4. **Comprehensive Treatment**: The authors address multiple facets of the problem: structural properties of the likelihood surface, convergence behavior of EM, and the nature of the critical points reached by the algorithm. The connection between the structural result (Theorem 1) and algorithmic consequences (Theorems 2-4) is well-motivated.

5. **Clear Organization**: The paper is well-structured, with clear separation of results, proofs, and implications. The technical details are appropriately relegated to the appendix while maintaining accessibility for the main text.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope**: The paper focuses exclusively on uniform mixtures of isotropic Gaussians with fixed variance. While this is a natural starting point, it would be valuable to discuss how the results might extend to more general GMMs (e.g., with varying variances or non-uniform weights). The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion.

2. **Dimensional Analysis**: The counterexample is constructed in dimension d = 1. While this is sufficient for the main theoretical result, it would be interesting to understand how the results scale with dimension. The paper mentions that well-separated mixtures require ξ = Ω(√d), but doesn't explicitly address how this affects the construction.

3. **Practical Implications**: While the paper demonstrates that random initialization fails with high probability, it doesn't provide concrete alternatives. The authors mention "pilot estimators utilizing the method of moments" but don't elaborate on how these might be implemented or their computational cost. A brief discussion of practical alternatives would strengthen the paper's impact.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and careful analysis. The authors are honest about the limitations of their results (e.g., the need for M ≥ 3) and provide clear connections to prior work. The construction of the counterexample is particularly strong.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The main contributions are clearly articulated, and the technical content is accessible to readers with a background in statistics or machine learning. The proofs in the appendix are sufficiently detailed for experts to verify the claims.

**Originality**: The resolution of Srebro's conjecture is a significant contribution. The construction of bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 is novel, and the analysis of EM's behavior with random initialization under these conditions is original. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous work on GMMs and non-convex optimization.

**Significance**: The results have important theoretical and practical implications. Theoretical advances in understanding the likelihood surface of GMMs are valuable for the field, while the practical insight about random initialization being insufficient is directly relevant to practitioners. The paper demonstrates that even in the "ideal" setting (well-separated, isotropic Gaussians), random initialization fails with high probability, which is a sobering result for the community.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of Gaussian mixture models and the behavior of the EM algorithm. The results resolve a specific open question in the literature, provide valuable insights into algorithmic limitations, and will likely influence future work on GMM estimation. The paper is technically rigorous, clearly written, and well-motivated.

The paper's main limitation is its restricted scope to uniform mixtures of isotropic Gaussians, but this is a natural starting point for resolving the specific conjecture. The authors appropriately acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, noting that extending the results to more general cases is an open problem.

This work is highly relevant to NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in statistical learning with implications for optimization and model estimation. The paper makes a clear contribution to the state of the art and will be valuable to both theoreticians and practitioners working with mixture models.

## Final Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of GMMs and EM. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important open question. The results have significant theoretical and practical implications. I believe this paper would be a strong addition to the NIPS proceedings.

Pro: Resolves a long-standing conjecture, provides rigorous proofs, demonstrates important algorithmic limitations, well-written and clear.

Con: Limited to uniform isotropic mixtures, could elaborate on practical alternatives to random initialization.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field and deserves acceptance.
