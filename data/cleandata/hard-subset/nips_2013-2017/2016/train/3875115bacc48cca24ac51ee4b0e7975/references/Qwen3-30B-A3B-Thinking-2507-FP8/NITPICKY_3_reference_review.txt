

# Review of "Local Maxima in the Likelihood of Gaussian Mixture Models: Structural Results and Algorithmic Consequences"

## Summary
This paper addresses a longstanding open question in Gaussian mixture models (GMMs) regarding the existence of bad local maxima in the population likelihood function. The authors provide a negative answer to Srebro's conjecture [2007] by constructing a GMM with three well-separated spherical unit-variance Gaussians whose population log-likelihood contains local maxima arbitrarily worse than the global maximum. They further show that random initialization of EM and first-order EM algorithms leads to convergence to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability in the number of components M. The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to understanding the optimization landscape of GMMs, particularly regarding the limitations of local search methods.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a significant open problem in the GMM literature, resolving Srebro's conjecture in the negative.
2. The theoretical results are non-trivial and provide important insights into the limitations of popular algorithms like EM.
3. The paper connects to broader literature on non-convex optimization, particularly regarding the role of local maxima versus saddle points.
4. The construction of bad local maxima for M ≥ 3 provides a concrete counterexample to a widely believed conjecture.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Citation Inconsistencies
The paper suffers from severe formatting inconsistencies in references and citations, which undermine its professional presentation. For example:
- Multiple instances of "Allman et al\.,? \Q2009\E" (with inconsistent backslashes and formatting)
- Inconsistent citation styles: "Srebro [2007]" in text vs. "Srebro.,? \Q2007\E" in references
- "Balakrishnan et al\.,? \Q2015\E" appears repeatedly with inconsistent formatting
- The reference list contains inconsistent citation formats throughout

### Technical Ambiguities
1. The paper states "for the setting when M = 2 remains an open problem" but then immediately discusses M = 3 in their construction, creating confusion about the scope of their contribution.

2. The definition of "well-separated" is inconsistent. In Section 2.1, it's defined as "ξ = Ω(√d)" but the paper doesn't specify the exact relationship between M, d, and ξ for the construction in Theorem 1. This is critical for understanding the scope of their negative result.

3. The paper claims "In the limit of infinite samples, the initialization scheme we consider is equivalent to selecting M initial centers i.i.d from the underlying mixture distribution." This is inaccurate - for infinite samples, the empirical distribution converges to the true distribution, but the initialization scheme (drawing from the sample set) is not exactly equivalent to sampling from the true distribution. The paper should clarify this distinction.

4. In the geometric description of the construction for M = 3, the paper states "µ1 = (µ*1 + µ*2)/2" but doesn't specify whether this is the mean of the two centers or something else. The notation is ambiguous.

5. Theorem 2 states "P [ ∀t ≥ 0, L(µt) ≤ L(µ*)− Cgap ] ≥ 1− e−cM" but doesn't specify what c is or whether it depends on M, d, or other parameters. This lack of detail makes it difficult to assess the practical implications of the result.

6. The paper claims "The first-order EM updates are identical to gradient ascent updates on the log-likelihood function" but this is only true for a specific step size, not for any step size. The paper should clarify this nuance.

### Proof and Explanation Gaps
1. In the geometric description of the M=2 case, the paper states "the only remaining critical point is (0, 0), which is a saddle point" without providing the mathematical basis for this claim. This is a significant gap in the exposition.

2. The paper states "we first define a neighborhood of this configuration ensuring that it does not contain any global maximum" but doesn't specify how this neighborhood is defined or why it doesn't contain global maxima.

3. Theorem 4 is mentioned but not clearly stated in the main text, with the proof details relegated to the appendix. A brief statement of Theorem 4 would be helpful for readers.

4. The paper claims "the probability of success for the EM algorithm is exponentially small as a function of M" but doesn't provide the exact rate or the constant c in the exponent, making it difficult to assess the practical significance.

### Minor but Notable Errors
1. Typo: "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016)" should be "The Conference"

2. Incomplete sentence: "Although points of the form (0, R), (R, 0) have small gradient when |R| is large, the gradient is not exactly zero for any finite R." This sentence is grammatically incomplete.

3. The paper states "For suitably small sample sizes, it is known [Améndola et al., 2015] that configurations of the samples can be constructed which lead to the likelihood function having an unbounded number of local maxima." This is an odd phrasing - "unbounded number" should be "unbounded number of" or "an unbounded number."

4. The paper claims "the EM algorithm is an ascent method for maximizing the likelihood, but is only guaranteed to converge to a stationary point" without clarifying the conditions under which this guarantee holds (e.g., when the likelihood is bounded above).

## Overall Assessment
While the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by resolving Srebro's conjecture in the negative, the numerous formatting inconsistencies, technical ambiguities, and incomplete explanations significantly undermine its quality. As a reviewer, I find the paper's presentation to be unprofessional and the technical details insufficiently explained for a NIPS submission. The formatting errors in references are particularly egregious for a venue like NIPS where such details matter.

The paper's main contribution is important, but the execution is flawed. The minor technical issues I've identified are not just trivial formatting problems but actually affect the reader's ability to understand and evaluate the paper's claims. For instance, the ambiguity in the definition of "well-separated" and the lack of specification about the constant c in the exponent make it difficult to assess the significance of the results.

Given the stringent standards of NIPS and the fact that these issues could easily be fixed with careful proofreading and clarification, I recommend rejection. The paper's contribution is valuable but its presentation and technical execution are insufficient for publication at NIPS.
