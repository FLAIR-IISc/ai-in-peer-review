## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental question in the theory of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs): whether the population likelihood function of a well-specified GMM has any "bad" local maxima (i.e., local maxima that are not global maxima). Srebro (2007) conjectured that for the population likelihood, all local maxima are global maxima. The authors provide a counterexample to this conjecture, showing that for M ≥ 3 components, there exist well-separated GMMs with arbitrarily bad local maxima in the population likelihood. Furthermore, they show that the EM algorithm and its first-order variant, when initialized randomly, converge to these bad local maxima with exponentially high probability in M. The paper also proves that the first-order EM algorithm does not converge to strict saddle points, suggesting that the failure is due to bad local maxima rather than saddle points.

This work builds on previous theoretical analyses of GMMs, including identifiability results by Teicher (1963), convergence results for EM by Balakrishnan et al. (2015), and moment-based methods for GMMs by Moitra and Valiant (2010) and Hsu and Kakade (2013). It also relates to broader literature on non-convex optimization, particularly Lee et al. (2016) and Panageas and Piliouras (2016) on gradient methods.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Theoretical Contribution**: The paper resolves an important open question in GMM theory that has been the subject of speculation for over a decade. The counterexample to Srebro's conjecture is elegant and surprising, directly contradicting a widely held belief in the community.

2. **Strong Algorithmic Implications**: The results provide clear theoretical justification for the need for careful initialization in GMM estimation. The exponential dependence on M in the success probability of random initialization is particularly significant for understanding practical limitations of current methods.

3. **Rigorous Proofs**: The proofs are technically sound and leverage recent advances in non-convex optimization. The authors successfully bridge theoretical analysis with practical algorithmic consequences.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The main results are presented in a logical sequence, with careful explanations of technical details.

5. **Relevance to NIPS**: The work addresses a fundamental problem in a widely used model class (GMMs) with clear implications for machine learning algorithms, making it highly relevant to the NIPS community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope**: The paper focuses exclusively on equally weighted, spherical GMMs with unit variance. While this is a natural starting point, the results may not generalize to more complex GMMs (e.g., with unequal weights, non-spherical components, or different variances). The authors should clarify the limitations of this assumption.

2. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper provides theoretical results but lacks empirical validation. Including small-scale experiments demonstrating the existence of bad local maxima in finite samples would strengthen the paper significantly.

3. **Practical Context**: The paper doesn't fully discuss how these theoretical findings impact real-world applications of GMMs. Providing context on how common GMM applications (e.g., clustering, density estimation) might be affected would add practical relevance.

4. **M=2 Case Discussion**: The paper mentions the M=2 case remains open but doesn't explain why their construction for M≥3 fails for M=2. A brief discussion would be valuable for completeness.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

*Quality*: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs that build on recent advances in non-convex optimization. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their assumptions.

*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The main results are presented logically, and the technical details are well-explained.

*Originality*: The work provides a novel counterexample to a long-standing conjecture, making it highly original. The connection between the structural properties of the likelihood function and algorithmic performance is particularly innovative.

*Significance*: The results have important implications for understanding the limitations of current GMM estimation methods. The exponential failure probability for random initialization is particularly significant for practitioners, as it suggests that standard approaches may fail even for well-separated mixtures.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant and resolves an important open question. The results have clear implications for both theory and practice, and the paper is well-written and technically sound.

The weaknesses noted are relatively minor and could be addressed in a camera-ready version. The limited scope is understandable given the complexity of the problem, and the authors have clearly delineated the specific conditions under which their results hold.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Resolves a long-standing open question in GMM theory with significant theoretical importance
- Provides strong theoretical justification for the need for careful initialization in GMM estimation
- Makes a substantial contribution to understanding non-convex optimization in statistical models
- Well-written, clearly organized, and technically rigorous

**Con:**
- Limited to equally weighted, spherical GMMs with unit variance
- Lacks empirical validation of the theoretical results
- Does not fully discuss practical implications for real-world applications

Overall, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of machine learning. The results will undoubtedly influence both theoretical and practical approaches to GMM estimation.
