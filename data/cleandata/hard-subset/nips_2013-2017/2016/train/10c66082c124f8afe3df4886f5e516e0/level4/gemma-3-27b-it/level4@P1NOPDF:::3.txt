The authors propose a novel method for learning the parameters of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which is essentially the Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) computed on text documents using word embeddings to determine bin-to-bin dissimilarities. They parameterize the EMD cost (i.e., ground metric) matrix using a Mahalanobis distance between vector representations, and further learn histogram coefficients for reweighting – an approach related to previous work by Lebanon (see comment below). The authors provide gradients for NCA-type costs in this context, and present compelling experimental results. Overall, the paper represents a strong combination of existing techniques and offers very convincing experimental validation, leading me to recommend acceptance. 

The strengths of this work lie in its simplicity and the relatively straightforward nature of the core idea, despite the non-trivial implementation and testing required. The experimental section is therefore a particularly strong component of the paper. 

However, there are several areas for improvement. The interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations could be clarified. The mathematical treatment would benefit from increased rigor – the computations and notations are currently somewhat sloppy. Finally, including an algorithmic box would help to more clearly illustrate the authors’ proposed approach.

A few minor comments: In Equation 1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used as a cost function. However, in Equation 6, when applied to Mahalanobis metric learning, this cost becomes the squared Euclidean metric (and therefore corresponds to the 2-Wasserstein distance). To avoid this inconsistency, it would be more accurate to state that WMD is a p-Wasserstein distance between clouds of word embeddings, regardless of the value of *p*.

The reweighting approach proposed in Equation 5 is not novel; it was previously introduced by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification (“metric learning for text documents”). Related work includes “Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations” by Le and Cuturi.

It would be valuable to gain experimental insights into the importance of the learned weight vector **w** in the metric. Does this innovation significantly impact performance? Is it demonstrably relevant?

Regarding related work, a clearer articulation of the differences between this work and previous approaches would be beneficial. For instance, the paper clearly builds upon the work of Cuturi and Avis, but utilizes entropy regularization, which demonstrably improves speed and scalability. The cost function also differs: whereas their work employs a discrete cost, this paper uses a continuous cost parameterized by linear maps. These distinctions are currently missing. The paper also draws from Wang et al., but that work is not readily reproducible in its current form; the one-time OT optimization they propose does not perform as described.
