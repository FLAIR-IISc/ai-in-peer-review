

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an efficient extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates label information to improve document classification performance. The authors build upon Kusner et al.'s WMD, which treats documents as distributions of word embeddings and computes distances via optimal transport. S-WMD extends this approach by learning both a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word-specific importance weights to better align document distances with class labels. The key innovation is the use of the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient proxy for the Wasserstein distance, avoiding the cubic complexity of exact WMD computation. The authors demonstrate S-WMD's effectiveness across eight text classification datasets, consistently outperforming 26 baselines including traditional document representations (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), and other metric learning approaches.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of document representation learning. It clearly situates its work within the broader context of metric learning for text classification, referencing key prior work on document distances, word embeddings, and optimal transport. The authors properly acknowledge the limitations of unsupervised approaches like WMD and demonstrate how their supervised variant addresses these limitations. They provide a thorough mathematical foundation for their approach, including a detailed derivation of the gradient computation that enables efficient optimization, and they properly contextualize their use of the Sinkhorn approximation within the broader literature on optimal transport.

The paper excels in clarity and technical rigor. The mathematical formulations are well-motivated and presented with exceptional clarity, making the complex optimization problem accessible to readers. The paper's organization is logical, moving from problem statement to background, methodological development, implementation details, and finally empirical evaluation. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including specific choices for hyperparameters like λ=10, and they carefully explain the trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational efficiency. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with results on eight diverse datasets across multiple text classification tasks, and the comparison to 26 baselines provides strong evidence of the method's effectiveness.

Regarding quality, the paper is technically sound and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach, noting that S-WMD performs best on datasets with shorter documents where the word vector space is more informative. They also acknowledge that initialization quality can affect performance, as seen in the CLASSIC dataset where WMD outperforms S-WMD. The authors are appropriately cautious about their claims, noting that S-WMD is not universally superior but shows consistent improvement across most datasets.

The significance of this work is substantial. Document distance metrics are fundamental to numerous text retrieval applications, and S-WMD provides a practical, efficient solution that leverages high-quality word embeddings while incorporating label information. The method's ability to consistently outperform a wide range of baselines across multiple datasets demonstrates its practical value. The authors' approach also offers a principled way to incorporate supervision into optimal transport-based document metrics, which has been a challenging problem in the literature.

For originality, the paper makes a meaningful extension to the WMD framework. While the core idea of learning a metric from labels is not entirely novel, the specific application to word embeddings and the efficient approximation using Sinkhorn distance represent a valuable contribution. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work on metric learning by focusing on the word embedding space rather than document representations, which allows the authors to maintain low-dimensional computations throughout. The authors provide a thorough review of related work, including the recent literature on Sinkhorn distances and metric learning.

Regarding the only notable weakness, the paper could provide more thorough discussion of the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn approximation. While the authors set λ=10 as a practical choice, they don't sufficiently address the theoretical relationship between λ and the approximation quality. It's true that λ determines whether the method approaches the pure Wasserstein distance (for large λ) or exhibits the entropic regularization behavior (for small λ), and the paper would benefit from more discussion of this trade-off. The authors should clarify whether their implementation effectively approximates the Wasserstein distance (with λ=10 providing a sufficiently accurate approximation) or whether they're deliberately using the entropic regularization behavior. This is particularly important given that the paper claims to improve upon WMD, which is based on the exact Wasserstein distance.

The paper also includes a potentially confusing reference to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) in the results section. While WCD is properly introduced as a baseline method, the authors' comparison of S-WMD to S-WCD (the supervised version of WCD) seems to conflate two different approaches. The paper would benefit from clearer distinction between these methods and more precise explanation of why S-WCD serves as an initialization for S-WMD.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document representation learning. The technical content is rigorous, the writing is clear, and the empirical results are compelling. The minor weaknesses regarding λ discussion and the WCD reference do not detract substantially from the paper's overall quality and contribution.

Pro: Strong technical contribution that extends WMD to a supervised setting; thorough mathematical development; excellent empirical evaluation across eight diverse datasets; clear writing; practical and efficient implementation; addresses important limitations of unsupervised approaches.

Con: Limited discussion of the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn approximation; potentially confusing reference to Word Centroid Distance in results section; S-WMD shows slightly worse performance than WMD on one dataset (CLASSIC), though this appears to be due to initialization rather than a fundamental limitation.

Given the paper's technical soundness, clear writing, and strong empirical results across multiple datasets, I recommend acceptance. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in document distance metrics.
