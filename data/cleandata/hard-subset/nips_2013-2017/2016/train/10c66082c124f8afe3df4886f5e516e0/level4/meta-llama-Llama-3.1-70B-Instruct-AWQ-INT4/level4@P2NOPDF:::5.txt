The authors provide a thorough analysis of the text classification problem, building upon previous work that introduced Word Mover's Distance (Wasserstein) by incorporating word-specific weights to create a supervised approach. A key contribution of this paper is the proposal to use Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for Wasserstein distance, aiming to improve computational efficiency. The authors evaluate their method on eight datasets, comparing it to 26 baselines and achieving the best average performance. 

The paper is well-written, presenting a clear and in-depth analysis of the mathematical concepts underlying the problem. However, two potential weaknesses are the limited novelty compared to the original Word Mover's Distance paper and the inclusion of references to World Centroid Distance, which may be confusing and lack relevance. Furthermore, the discussion of lambda, the regularizing factor, appears to be inadequate. In practice, the value of lambda can significantly impact the results, sometimes becoming the dominant factor in equation (2). 

Theoretically, with a sufficiently large lambda, the results should be similar to those obtained using the pure Wasserstein distance, albeit at the cost of slower convergence. However, in practice, a small lambda is often used to improve convergence rates, resulting in behavior that diverges from the Wasserstein distance. Therefore, it would be interesting to know whether the chosen value of lambda in this contribution leads to behavior akin to the pure Wasserstein distance or the Sinkhorn (entropic) distance.
