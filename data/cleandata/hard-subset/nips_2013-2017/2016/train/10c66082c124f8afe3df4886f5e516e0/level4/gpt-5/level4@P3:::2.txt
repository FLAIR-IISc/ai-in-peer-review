Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns task-specific document distances for kNN classification. WMD (Kusner et al., ICML 2015) defines document distance as the optimal transport cost of moving word mass between normalized bag-of-words histograms using pre-trained word embeddings as ground costs. S-WMD introduces supervision by learning (i) a linear transform A applied to word embeddings, effectively learning the ground metric, and (ii) a nonnegative per-word importance vector w that reweights document histograms before renormalization. The training objective follows the NCA (Neighborhood Components Analysis) framework: it minimizes the stochastic leave-one-out kNN error under the learned distance. To make training efficient, the authors approximate the WMD with Cuturi’s entropic-regularized optimal transport (Sinkhorn distance) and derive practical gradients for both A and w via the relaxed primal and dual solutions. They further accelerate training with an initialization based on NCA on word centroids (S-WCD), minibatch optimization, and neighbor pruning using fast WCD screening.

The work is well connected to prior art in metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML), entropic optimal transport (Cuturi), and EMD ground metric learning in vision. Relative to prior EMD metric learning (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.), this paper targets text, learns both the ground metric and word reweighting, and provides an efficient learning and inference pipeline suitable for high-vocabulary sparse data. Empirically, S-WMD is evaluated on eight text classification datasets against 26 baselines (unsupervised representations and their supervised metric-learned variants) and achieves the best average performance, with code publicly available.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is coherent. The NCA-style objective is standard for metric learning, and the use of entropic OT to avoid nested LPs is appropriate. Gradients for A and w are derived via the relaxed transport plan and its dual potentials, leading to efficient updates.
- Empirical support: The experiments are broad (8 datasets) and competitive (26 baselines), with consistent gains on most datasets and strong average rank. Training and initialization details, as well as complexity considerations, are discussed. Reported training times are reasonable.
- Caveats:
  - The reliance on the Sinkhorn approximation introduces a bias controlled by λ; while sensible, the paper would benefit from a sensitivity analysis of λ and its effect on accuracy and convergence.
  - The gradient for w depends on dual variables derived from Sinkhorn scaling; clarifying constraints/regularization on w (e.g., nonnegativity, sparsity, or smoothing) and reporting its sensitivity would strengthen claims about robustness.
  - The evaluation focuses on kNN. It would be informative to show how S-WMD performs for retrieval tasks or as a distance in nonparametric classifiers beyond kNN, and to compare against strong linear classifiers (e.g., SVM/logistic regression on TF-IDF) and simple supervised embedding methods prevalent in text classification at the time.
  - Some notation is overloaded (e.g., p as both power and dimension), and a few typos remain.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear motivation, derivations at the right level of detail, and helpful algorithmic descriptions. The high-level flow from WMD to S-WMD (objective, gradients, initialization, and acceleration) is easy to follow. Suggested clarifications:
- Disambiguate notation for p (power in the ground cost vs. dimensionalities); define all symbols at first use in Section 3.1 (e.g., dimensions of 1 and p in the dual expressions).
- State explicitly the constraints on w (nonnegativity) and any regularization used; discuss initialization’s role and convergence behavior.
- Fix minor typos (e.g., “word-imporance,” “converges slower,” “th Conference”), and ensure consistent naming (S-WMD init., S-WCD).

Originality
The combination of (i) learning a linear ground metric in the word embedding space, (ii) learning per-word importance weights, and (iii) efficient NCA-style training via Sinkhorn approximations appears novel in the context of text and WMD. While metric learning for EMD has precedents, applying it to word-level transport with learned histogram reweighting and providing an effective, scalable training procedure is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
S-WMD addresses a central limitation of WMD by incorporating labels to tailor distances to the task. The gains over a broad suite of baselines, coupled with efficiency and open-source code, suggest impact for practitioners in text classification and retrieval who favor distance-based methods. The approach also opens a path for supervised adaptation of OT-based distances in NLP more broadly.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated method that extends WMD with supervision through a principled objective.
- Efficient training via Sinkhorn and practical accelerations; code released.
- Strong, comprehensive empirical results across many datasets and baselines; state-of-the-art average performance for kNN.
- Conceptually simple and likely to be adopted; leverages pre-trained embeddings without heavy end-to-end models.

Cons and questions
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to λ, batch/neighbor sizes, and initialization; no ablations to disentangle the contributions of A vs. w.
- Focused on kNN; missing comparisons to competitive supervised text classifiers (e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression on TF-IDF, fast shallow neural baselines of the time).
- Some notational ambiguities and minor typos; details on constraints/regularization for w are under-specified.
- No theoretical guarantees on convergence or approximation error propagation from Sinkhorn to the learned metric (even a brief discussion would help).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablation studies: (1) learn A only, w fixed; (2) learn w only, A fixed; (3) both. Report effects on accuracy and interpretability of w.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for λ, neighbor set size N, batch size B, and the number of gradient iterations; include convergence plots.
- Compare against strong supervised baselines beyond kNN (e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression on TF-IDF) and, if feasible, a simple supervised embedding baseline.
- Report robustness across different pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe) and vocabulary sizes.
- Clarify constraints and any regularization on w; consider adding an L1/L2 or entropy regularizer to control overfitting and improve interpretability.
- Tighten notation and fix minor editorial issues.

Overall recommendation
A solid, well-executed contribution that advances supervised metric learning for document distances by building on WMD with principled and efficient techniques. The empirical gains and practicality make it a strong candidate for acceptance. With minor clarifications and additional analyses (especially ablations and sensitivity), the paper would be even stronger.
