The authors propose a method to learn the parameters of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD)—i.e., Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) applied to text documents—using word embeddings to compute bin-to-bin dissimilarities. They parameterize the EMD ground cost via a Mahalanobis distance between embedding vectors and, in addition, learn a reweighting of histogram coefficients (as in Lebanon’s work; see comment below). They derive gradients for NCA-type objectives and present compelling experimental evidence. Overall, the paper reads as a thoughtful combination of existing techniques with strong empirical results. I support acceptance.

Strengths include the simplicity and clarity of the main idea, which is conceptually straightforward yet nontrivial to implement and evaluate. The experimental section is a major asset of the paper.

Areas for improvement:
- Better address the interplay between regularized and unregularized formulations of OT in the method and analysis.
- Improve mathematical rigor: several computations and parts of the notation are somewhat sloppy.
- Provide an algorithm box or pseudocode to make the proposed procedure explicit and easier to reproduce.

Minor comments:
- In Eq. 1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used as the cost; in Eq. 6, for Mahalanobis metric learning, the cost becomes the squared Euclidean metric (which corresponds to 2-Wasserstein). To avoid such inconsistencies, it would be clearer to present WMD as the p-Wasserstein distance between point clouds of word embeddings, for a chosen p, and maintain that choice consistently throughout.
- The reweighting approach in Eq. 5 is not new; it was proposed by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification (“Metric Learning for Text Documents”). See also Le and Cuturi, “Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations.”
- Please provide experimental insights into the importance of the weight vector w in the learned metric. Does this innovation make a measurable difference? In what settings is it most relevant?
- Related work could be positioned more clearly. This paper builds on the work of Cuturi/Avis but differs in two important ways that are not sufficiently emphasized: (i) the use of entropy regularization, which substantially accelerates and scales computation, and (ii) a different ground cost—discrete in that work versus a continuous, linearly parameterized form here. The paper also draws from Wang et al.; however, that paper is not publishable in its current state. In particular, their OT optimization is run only once and does not function as proposed.

In summary, this is a solid, well-motivated contribution with strong empirical validation. Clarifying the regularized/unregularized treatment, tightening the mathematical presentation, and adding a concise algorithmic description would significantly strengthen the paper.
