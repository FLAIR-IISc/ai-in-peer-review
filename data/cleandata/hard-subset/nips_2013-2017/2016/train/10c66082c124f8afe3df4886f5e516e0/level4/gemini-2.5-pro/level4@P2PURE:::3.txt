This paper proposes a method for learning the parameters of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), an application of the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) to text documents where the ground metric is derived from word embeddings. The authors parameterize the EMD cost matrix using a Mahalanobis distance and additionally learn coefficients to reweight the document histograms. The paper provides derivations for the gradients of an NCA-style loss function and presents compelling experimental evidence for the method's effectiveness.

Overall, the paper presents a nice combination of existing techniques that yields very convincing results. The core idea is relatively straightforward, yet its implementation and empirical validation are non-trivial. The strong experimental section is a key strength of this work, and I support its acceptance.

Suggestions for improvement:
The paper would be strengthened by addressing the following points:
*   Clarify the interplay between the regularized and unregularized formulations of the proposed method.
*   Improve the mathematical rigor, as some computations and notations are slightly imprecise.
*   Include an algorithm box or pseudocode to provide a clearer, more consolidated view of the proposed procedure.

Minor comments:
*   There appears to be an inconsistency in the choice of ground metric. Equation 1 uses the Euclidean distance between word embeddings, whereas Equation 6, in the context of Mahalanobis metric learning, uses the squared Euclidean distance. This corresponds to a switch between what is commonly known as the 1-Wasserstein and 2-Wasserstein distances. To resolve this, the authors could frame WMD more generally as the p-Wasserstein distance between clouds of word embeddings.
*   The histogram reweighting approach proposed in Equation 5 is not novel. This technique was previously introduced by Guy Lebanon in "Metric Learning for Text Documents" for the same application and is also related to the work of Le and Cuturi in "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations." The authors should acknowledge this prior work.
*   The paper would benefit from experimental insights into the importance of the weight vector `w` in the learned metric. An ablation study demonstrating the relevance of this component would be valuable.
*   The related work section could more clearly differentiate this work from previous methods. For instance:
    *   While the paper builds on the work of Cuturi and Avis, it introduces two significant distinctions that are not highlighted: the use of entropy regularization, which enables faster and more scalable computation, and the use of a continuous, parameterized cost matrix instead of a discrete one.
    *   When comparing to Wang et al., it would be useful to note that the approach in that paper performs the OT optimization only once, which is a methodological flaw. Highlighting how the current paper overcomes such limitations would better position its contribution.
