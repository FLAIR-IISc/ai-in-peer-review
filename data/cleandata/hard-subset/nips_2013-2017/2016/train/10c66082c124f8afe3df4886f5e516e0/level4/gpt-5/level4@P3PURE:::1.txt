Summary
This paper proposes a supervised metric learning framework built on the recent Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) [19], i.e., an optimal transport distance applied to word2vec embeddings. The method applies to any transport distance with squared Euclidean ground costs after a learned linear projection. In addition to learning the projection that maximizes task performance, the approach learns per-word weights: it reweights each word’s frequency by a learned parameter followed by renormalization. The paper presents an efficient optimization procedure to learn the parameterized metric. Empirically, the method is evaluated thoroughly against many reasonable baselines on several document classification datasets, using a kNN classifier operating under the learned metric.

Strengths
- Extends the recent WMD in a technically sound and practically useful way, with demonstrated gains on multiple kNN text classification tasks.
- Broad applicability to transport distances with squared Euclidean costs after linear projection.
- Clear formulation and efficient optimization.
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation and strong writing quality.

Weaknesses and main concerns
- Potential impact may be limited: earth mover’s distance with metric learning via a “word centroid distance” initialization performs similarly to the full learned distance. The small difference between the last two rows of Table 3 suggests that the main technical contributions add little beyond this initialization based on existing techniques.
- Missing evaluation: Table 3 does not include the “word centroid embedding” (weighted mean of word embeddings for a document) combined with the standard metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA). Including these would clarify how much WMD-specific structure is needed versus simpler centroid-based baselines.
- Missing ablation: There is no assessment of the relative importance of the word-specific weights versus the projection matrix A. Results with each component enabled in isolation would help disentangle their contributions.

Originality
- The primary novelty lies in the optimization strategy (Lines 156–175). While incorporating linear metric learning within earth mover’s distance is an interesting idea, it constitutes a relatively limited contribution on its own given the strength of the centroid-based initialization.

Minor comments and clarifications
- Line 152: “The authors” — which specific paper is being referenced? [1], [7], [8]?
- Equation 11: Should α and β carry stars (α*, β*) here for consistency with the optimal dual variables?
- Around Line 170, it would be helpful to explicitly state that this is the Sinkhorn scaling referred to in the introduction (Line 56).

Overall
A well-written and carefully executed paper that meaningfully extends WMD and shows strong empirical performance. However, the incremental gains over a strong “word centroid distance” initialization raise concerns about the depth of the contribution. Additional baselines (centroid + ITML/LMNN/NCA) and ablations (word weights vs. projection matrix A) would substantially strengthen the paper’s claims.
