The authors study text classification and extend prior work on Word Mover’s Distance (Wasserstein) by making it supervised through learning word-specific weights. They further propose using the Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for the Wasserstein distance to accelerate computation, and evaluate on eight datasets against 26 baselines, achieving the best average performance. The paper is very well written, clearly presents the topic, and provides an in-depth yet clear mathematical analysis.

The main weaknesses are, perhaps, the limited novelty relative to the original Word Mover’s Distance paper, and the inclusion of references critiquing the Word Centroid Distance, which I find irrelevant and confusing. I also feel that the role of lambda (the regularization parameter) is insufficiently discussed. In my experience, the value of lambda has a very large impact—sometimes dominating Equation (2). Theoretically, results should approach those of the pure Wasserstein distance when lambda is large enough, albeit with slower convergence; however, in practice, many users of Sinkhorn adopt a very small lambda to speed up convergence, resulting in behavior that differs markedly from Wasserstein. I would therefore like clarification on whether, in this contribution, lambda induces behavior closer to the pure Wasserstein distance or to the Sinkhorn (entropic) formulation.
