This paper addresses the problem of text classification by proposing a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn word-specific weights to refine the distance metric for a given classification task, an approach the authors term Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD). To mitigate the computational expense of the exact Wasserstein distance used in WMD, the authors employ the Sinkhorn distance as a faster, differentiable approximation. The proposed method is thoroughly evaluated on eight benchmark datasets against a comprehensive suite of 26 baselines, where it achieves the best average performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The authors do an excellent job of introducing the topic, motivating the problem, and situating their work within the existing literature. The mathematical foundations are presented with both rigor and clarity, making the paper accessible and convincing.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental setup is extensive and robust. Comparing against 26 baselines across eight datasets provides strong empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed S-WMD method and its Sinkhorn-based variant.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Novelty:** While the paper presents a valuable and effective method, the conceptual novelty over the original Word Mover's Distance paper is somewhat limited. The main contribution is the adaptation of WMD to a supervised setting by learning word weights, which is an important but incremental step.

2.  **Relevance of Word Centroid Distance (WCD) Baseline:** The discussion and comparison against the Word Centroid Distance felt underdeveloped and potentially confusing. It would be helpful if the authors could further clarify the motivation for including this specific baseline and elaborate on the key insights drawn from the comparison.

3.  **Insufficient Discussion of the Regularization Parameter (lambda):** A significant concern is the limited discussion regarding the entropic regularization parameter, lambda, in the Sinkhorn distance computation (Eq. 2). The choice of lambda is critical, as it controls a fundamental trade-off between accurately approximating the Wasserstein distance and achieving fast convergence.
    *   In theory, a very large lambda ensures that the Sinkhorn distance closely approximates the true Wasserstein distance, but this often comes at the cost of slower convergence.
    *   In practice, many applications use a small lambda to accelerate computation. However, this results in a metric dominated by the entropic term, whose behavior can be markedly different from the unregularized Wasserstein distance.

    The paper does not explore this crucial trade-off. It is unclear from the text whether the chosen lambda values result in a metric that behaves like the pure Wasserstein distance or one that exhibits a distinct "Sinkhorn" (entropic) behavior. This ambiguity is important, as the motivation for using Sinkhorn is presented as a proxy for Wasserstein, but the practical behavior might be different.

    To strengthen the paper, I would ask the authors to address the following:
    *   Could you provide an analysis of the model's sensitivity to the value of lambda?
    *   Do the lambda values used in your experiments yield a behavior that is a close approximation of the Wasserstein distance, or is the entropic regularization a dominant factor?
    *   Could you offer more insight into the methodology used for selecting lambda for the experiments? An ablation study on this parameter would be highly beneficial.
