

This paper introduces a supervised extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) for measuring document similarity, building upon the unsupervised optimal transport framework. The authors propose learning (1) a linear transformation of word embeddings and (2) a reweighting of word distributions to maximize separation between documents of different labels. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and demonstrates a strong grasp of prior work in the field. Comprehensive experiments on multiple novel datasets further strengthen the presentation.

However, the paper's core contributions require stronger justification and exhibit limited novelty. The primary concerns are:

1.  **Motivation for Word Distribution Reweighting:** The use of reweighting vector `w` to modify the document's word distribution lacks sufficient motivation. The current explanation does not convincingly justify why this reweighting is necessary or how it preserves the document's original semantic content. Crucially, the reweighting process could fundamentally alter the document's meaning (e.g., by amplifying irrelevant words or suppressing key terms), potentially transforming it into a different semantic entity. The authors must explicitly demonstrate *why* this reweighting is beneficial *without* compromising the document's inherent meaning, or provide evidence that the learned `w` consistently reflects meaningful document characteristics rather than artifacts of the optimization.

2.  **Incremental Contribution:** The proposed approach—applying a linear transformation `A` and reweighting `w` within a Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) objective to the WMD framework—is a logical, albeit straightforward, extension for a supervised setting. While technically sound, this formulation does not represent a significant conceptual advance or novel technical insight. The core WMD framework remains unchanged, and the supervised adaptation follows a well-trodden path in metric learning. The contribution appears incremental rather than groundbreaking, which is a key consideration for NIPS.

3.  **Generalization to New Documents:** The paper does not address how the method handles *unseen* documents. Specifically, the reweighting vector `w` is learned per document during training. For a completely new document (not seen during training), the method lacks a clear mechanism to determine its appropriate `w`. Without a principled way to infer `w` for out-of-sample documents, the practical applicability of the learned model is severely limited. The authors must clarify the generalization strategy or acknowledge this as a significant limitation.

4.  **Ambiguity in "Semantic Difference":** The abstract claims the method captures "semantic differences" between documents. However, the paper does not clearly define what constitutes a "semantic difference" in this context. Given that the labels (e.g., sentiment, topic) are used for supervision, the observed differences primarily reflect label-related distinctions, not necessarily deeper semantic variations (e.g., authorship style or subtle topic shifts). The term "semantic difference" is misleading unless explicitly linked to the document content beyond the label. The authors should clarify the intended meaning of this phrase.

In summary, while the paper is well-written, presents thorough experiments, and demonstrates a good understanding of the literature, the core technical contribution lacks sufficient novelty and the motivation for key components (particularly reweighting) is underdeveloped. The lack of a clear generalization mechanism for new documents further weakens the practical impact. The significance of the work is therefore low for NIPS. Addressing the concerns regarding the motivation for reweighting, the novelty claim, the generalization strategy, and the precise meaning of "semantic difference" is essential before the paper can be considered for acceptance. The current formulation appears to be a minor extension rather than a substantial advancement in the field.
