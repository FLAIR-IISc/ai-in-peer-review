This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific distance metric for documents. The approach builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which frames the distance between two documents as an optimal transport (or Earth Mover's Distance) problem between their respective word embeddings. The authors' key contribution is to introduce supervision by parameterizing the ground metric of the transport problem. Specifically, they learn an affine transformation of the underlying word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector. The learning objective minimizes a stochastic version of the leave-one-out kNN classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). To make this computationally tractable, the authors propose an efficient gradient computation scheme by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized counterpart, the Sinkhorn distance, which avoids a nested optimization problem.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a logical and important extension of the WMD framework by incorporating supervision. The proposed method of jointly learning a linear transformation in the embedding space and re-weighting the word histograms is an elegant combination of existing ideas from metric learning and optimal transport, leading to a powerful new document distance.
*   **Technical Quality:** The use of the regularized Sinkhorn distance to enable efficient gradient computation is a crucial and well-executed technical contribution. This makes the method practical for real-world datasets, which would be infeasible with the exact WMD gradient.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** A major strength of this work is its extensive and compelling experimental evaluation. The authors compare S-WMD against a large suite of 26 competitive baselines across 8 diverse text classification tasks. The results are very strong, showing that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance on a majority of the datasets and outperforms all other methods on average.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity and Mathematical Rigor:** The mathematical presentation could be tightened. There is a slight inconsistency between the WMD formulation in Equation 1, which uses the Euclidean distance `||xi − xj||_p`, and the loss function in Equation 6, which uses a squared Euclidean distance `||A(xi − xj)||_2^2`. This corresponds to the difference between a p-Wasserstein and a 2-Wasserstein distance. Clarifying this and ensuring consistency throughout the paper would improve rigor. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a concise algorithm box that outlines the full training procedure, making the method easier to understand and reproduce.
*   **Relation to Prior Work:** The discussion of related work could more clearly delineate the contributions of this paper.
    *   The concept of re-weighting histograms (Equation 4) is not entirely new and has been proposed in the context of text document classification (e.g., Lebanon, "Metric Learning for Text Documents") and for learning on histograms more generally (e.g., Le & Cuturi, "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations"). Acknowledging this prior work would better situate the paper's contribution.
    *   The paper builds upon the idea of learning the EMD ground metric (e.g., Cuturi & Avis). It would be beneficial to explicitly highlight the key differences, such as the use of entropy regularization for significant speed-up and the continuous parameterization via linear maps, which are important advancements.
*   **Analysis:** The experimental results are impressive, but an ablation study would provide deeper insight. In particular, it is not clear how much of the performance gain comes from the learned linear transformation `A` versus the word-importance vector `w`. An experiment analyzing the impact of each component would strengthen the paper by quantifying the importance of this specific innovation.
