This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the recently introduced Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The original WMD provides a powerful unsupervised metric for document similarity by calculating the minimum cost to "transport" words from one document to another in a pre-trained word embedding space, an instance of the Earth Mover's or Wasserstein distance. The key limitation of WMD is its unsupervised nature, which this paper directly addresses.

The authors introduce supervision by learning two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation matrix `A` that reshapes the word embedding space to be more suitable for the classification task, and (2) a word-importance vector `w` that re-weights word frequencies to emphasize task-relevant terms. The learning objective is formulated to minimize a stochastic version of the k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) leave-one-out classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A significant technical challenge is that differentiating through the standard WMD optimization problem is computationally prohibitive. To overcome this, the authors propose a clever and practical approximation: they replace the Wasserstein distance with its entropy-regularized version, the Sinkhorn distance. This makes the objective function differentiable and allows for an efficient gradient computation, enabling optimization via batch gradient descent. The method is validated on eight text classification datasets, where it is shown to achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance against a comprehensive suite of 26 baseline methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of a highly influential method (WMD). By providing an effective mechanism to incorporate supervision, S-WMD significantly extends the applicability and performance of optimal transport-based document distances. The strong empirical results suggest that this could become a go-to method for kNN-based text classification when labeled data is available.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical contribution is sound and elegant. The core idea of using the regularized Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable and computationally efficient proxy for the WMD is the key insight that makes this entire framework practical. The formulation of the joint learning problem for the embedding transformation `A` and the word weights `w` is well-motivated, and the derivation of the gradients is clear. The proposed initialization strategy using a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) is a smart heuristic that likely improves convergence.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of introducing the necessary background on WMD, Sinkhorn distances, and NCA before detailing their own contribution. The mathematical formulations are presented with great clarity, and the overall narrative logically flows from the problem definition to the proposed solution and its empirical validation.

4.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The comparison against 26 baselines across eight diverse datasets provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD. The inclusion of multiple metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN) applied to various document representations further strengthens the authors' claims. The visualizations in Figure 1 and 2 effectively illustrate the improvements offered by the learned metric.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Originality:** While the results are impressive, the conceptual contribution can be viewed as an incremental, albeit highly effective, combination of existing ideas. The work builds directly upon WMD (Kusner et al., 2015), metric learning via NCA (Goldberger et al., 2005), and fast optimal transport via Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013). The primary novelty lies in the successful synthesis of these components into a practical and powerful algorithm for supervised document distance learning.

2.  **Discussion of the Regularization Parameter (λ):** The role of the hyperparameter `λ` in the Sinkhorn distance approximation is under-discussed. The paper states that `λ = 10` is used as a good trade-off between speed and accuracy, but provides no further analysis. The value of `λ` is critical, as it controls the degree of entropic regularization. A very large `λ` makes the Sinkhorn distance approximate the true Wasserstein distance more closely but can lead to slower convergence and numerical instability. Conversely, a small `λ` speeds up computation but yields a distance that behaves quite differently, emphasizing entropy over transport cost. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief sensitivity analysis or a more detailed discussion on how the choice of `λ` impacts the model's performance and training dynamics. Does the method's effectiveness depend on being in a "Wasserstein-like" regime (large `λ`) or an "entropic" regime (small `λ`)?

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   The paper provides a powerful and practical solution to a significant problem: adding supervision to the state-of-the-art WMD metric.
*   The technical approach of using the Sinkhorn distance to enable efficient gradient-based learning is novel in this context and well-executed.
*   The method is shown to achieve state-of-the-art results through an extensive and convincing empirical evaluation.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, making it a strong contribution to the conference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance**
*   The conceptual framework is an integration of several existing techniques, making the work more of a highly successful application and extension rather than a fundamentally new idea.
*   The analysis of the key hyperparameter `λ` is insufficient, leaving open questions about its influence on the behavior of the learned metric.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a very strong paper. It presents a well-motivated, technically sound, and practically useful extension to an important document distance metric. The weaknesses are minor in comparison to the strengths. The impressive empirical results, combined with the clarity of the presentation, make this a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
