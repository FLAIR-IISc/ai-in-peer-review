This paper presents a supervised learning approach to document distance, building upon the word mover’s distance (WMD) metric. WMD, originally inspired by the earth mover’s distance, calculates optimal transport between word distributions to determine document similarity. The initial formulation utilizes word2vec embeddings for representing words. This work extends WMD by learning (1) a linear transformation applied to word embeddings and (2) a reweighting of the original word distribution, with the goal of increasing separation between documents of different classes.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed methodology. The related work section demonstrates a strong understanding of existing literature and provides a comprehensive overview of relevant research. The authors evaluate their approach on a variety of datasets, including several novel ones.

However, several aspects of the paper require further clarification and raise concerns about the significance of the contribution. First, the motivation behind reweighting the word distribution with learnable weights, *w*, is not sufficiently explained. The potential for significant alteration of the document’s representation through this reweighting is a concern. The authors should more clearly articulate *why* this reweighting improves document distance without fundamentally changing the document's core meaning. Justification of this step is crucial. 

Second, the overall contribution appears incremental. Applying a Non-convex Amortized Coordinate Ascent (NCA) objective to learn a linear transformation (*A*) and reweighting (*w*) within the WMD framework represents a plausible extension to a supervised setting, but it does not present a particularly novel or impactful advance. The formulation, while sound, feels more like a natural extension than a groundbreaking development.

Third, the practical application of the algorithm to new, unseen documents requires clarification. Specifically, how is the reweighting vector *w* determined for a document outside of the training set? The paper lacks detail on this crucial aspect of deployment.

Finally, the use of the term "semantic difference" in the abstract is unclear. If this refers solely to document labels, it is a potentially misleading simplification. Differences in authorship or topic do not typically fall under the common understanding of "semantic difference." The authors should explicitly define what they mean by this term and ensure its consistency throughout the paper.

Despite these concerns, the paper is generally well-executed. I do not strongly object to its acceptance, contingent upon the authors adequately addressing the aforementioned questions and strengthening the justification for their approach, particularly regarding the reweighting scheme and the overall significance of the contribution. A clearer explanation of the algorithm’s applicability to unseen documents and a precise definition of “semantic difference” would also be beneficial.
