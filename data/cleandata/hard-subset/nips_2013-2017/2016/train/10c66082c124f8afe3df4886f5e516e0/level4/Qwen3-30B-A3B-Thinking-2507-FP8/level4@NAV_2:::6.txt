

The paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) framework [Kusner et al., 2015] to incorporate label information for document classification. Building on the NIPS 2015 WMD work, which leveraged word embeddings to compute document distances via optimal transport, S-WMD jointly learns a linear transformation matrix A of the word embedding space and a word importance weight vector w to minimize stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification error. The authors address computational challenges by approximating the WMD with a Sinkhorn distance [Cuturi, 2013], reducing complexity from O(q³ log q) to O(q²), and propose efficient batch gradient descent with a WCD-based initialization. The method is evaluated on 8 text classification datasets, consistently outperforming 26 baselines including BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, and supervised metric learning variants.

Strengths: The paper demonstrates exceptional empirical significance, achieving the lowest error on 5/8 datasets and outperforming all baselines on average. The technical quality is strong, with careful theoretical grounding in optimal transport and metric learning (NCA [Goldberger et al., 2005]). The dual contribution of learning both A and w provides clear advantages over prior unsupervised WMD and standard metric learning. The implementation details (e.g., Sinkhorn approximation, WCD initialization) are well-justified and contribute to the practicality of the approach. The significance is evident through state-of-the-art results across diverse tasks (news categorization, sentiment analysis), with the method's ability to handle syntactically diverse but semantically similar documents being particularly valuable.

Weaknesses: The intuition behind Eq. (5), which defines the weighted document representation (d̃ₐ = (w ◦ dₐ)/(wᵀdₐ)), requires clarification. It is unclear whether the improvements stem primarily from the linear transformation A, the word weights w, or their combination. The paper should include ablation studies to isolate these effects. While the authors address the need for kNN classification details in their response, the draft review correctly notes that k is not specified in Table 2 (it is k=1 for all experiments). The claim about "syntactically different but conceptually similar words" (line 316) lacks concrete evidence beyond the WMD's known properties; the authors should provide a specific example from their results to substantiate this. The relationship between λ in Eq. (2) and the λ used for approximation accuracy (line 316) requires explicit clarification to avoid confusion with the Sinkhorn regularization parameter.

The paper excels in quality (sound theory, rigorous experiments), clarity (well-structured, readable), and originality (novel integration of supervised learning with WMD), though minor improvements to the exposition of Eq. (5) and the supporting evidence for the semantic similarity claim would strengthen it. The significance is demonstrably high, with clear potential for adoption in text classification pipelines. The work is well-scoped for NIPS, directly advancing document representation learning. Pro: Strong empirical results, efficient computation, novel dual learning approach. Con: Limited mechanistic explanation of Eq. (5)'s contribution, insufficient evidence for key semantic claim. Accept.
