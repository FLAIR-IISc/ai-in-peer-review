

This paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) for document classification, addressing a key limitation of the original unsupervised metric. The authors introduce Supervised WMD (S-WMD), which jointly learns an affine transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to minimize leave-one-out kNN classification error under the WMD framework. The core innovation lies in efficiently approximating the gradient of the nested WMD optimization using Cuturi's Sinkhorn distance relaxation, enabling scalable training without sacrificing the interpretability of the optimal transport formulation. The method is evaluated across 8 diverse text classification tasks against 26 baselines, including classical representations (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), deep learning approaches (mSDA), and prior distance metrics (WCD, WMD).

The paper excels in **quality** and **significance**. The technical approach is sound: the Sinkhorn approximation (Section 3.1) correctly addresses the cubic complexity of WMD gradients, and the loss function (Equation 6) properly leverages stochastic neighborhood probabilities from NCA [15] to enable differentiable supervision. The experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough—covering multiple domains (news, sentiment, product review), 8 datasets, and 26 baselines with consistent hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian optimization. S-WMD achieves the lowest test error on 5/8 datasets and ranks first on average (Table 2), with statistically significant improvements over unsupervised WMD (e.g., 51% relative error reduction on OHSUMED vs. LSI). Crucially, the method preserves WMD's strength in handling semantically similar but syntactically distinct words (Section 6), while adding discriminative power through word weighting and embedding transformation. The training time is practical (Table 3), outperforming standard metric learning pipelines by leveraging the low-dimensional word space.

**Clarity** is generally strong, with well-structured sections that effectively contextualize the work within prior literature (e.g., contrasting S-WMD with metric learning approaches like LMNN/ITML [10, 36] and supervised EMD methods [35]). Minor improvements could be made to notation consistency (e.g., defining $D_{A,w}$ early) and clarifying the role of the initialization step (S-WCD). The t-SNE visualizations (Figure 1) compellingly demonstrate improved class separation over WMD, and the word weight analysis (Figure 2) provides intuitive evidence of the learned importance weights.

**Originality** is well-established: while metric learning for text has been explored [10, 36], this is the first work to integrate supervision directly into the WMD framework *without* learning a new document representation. The dual contribution—learning both a linear transformation of word embeddings *and* word-specific weights—distinguishes S-WMD from prior supervised EMD approaches [35] and addresses a gap in the WMD literature (Kusner et al. [19] note the need for supervision). The connection to Sinkhorn distance [6] is appropriately cited, and the paper avoids overclaiming novelty by clearly situating itself relative to related work (Section 5).

**Strengths**:  
- Practical solution to a well-motivated problem (incorporating supervision into WMD without incurring high computational cost)  
- Comprehensive empirical validation across diverse settings with rigorous baselines  
- Efficient training (O(q²) complexity) while retaining WMD's semantic advantages  
- Clear theoretical grounding in optimal transport and metric learning  

**Weaknesses**:  
- The reliance on pre-trained word2vec embeddings (Section 2) may limit adaptability to domain-specific contexts, though this is a common practice in the field.  
- The analysis of failure cases (e.g., CLASSIC dataset where WMD outperforms S-WMD) is brief; a deeper investigation into why squared Euclidean distance may be suboptimal for certain datasets would strengthen the discussion.  

The paper makes a significant contribution to text representation learning. It advances the state of the art in document distance metrics by successfully marrying the semantic richness of word embeddings with discriminative supervision, all while maintaining computational feasibility. The results are robust, the method is well-motivated, and the implementation details (including code availability) support reproducibility. This is a high-quality, impactful contribution to NIPS that addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. I strongly recommend acceptance.
