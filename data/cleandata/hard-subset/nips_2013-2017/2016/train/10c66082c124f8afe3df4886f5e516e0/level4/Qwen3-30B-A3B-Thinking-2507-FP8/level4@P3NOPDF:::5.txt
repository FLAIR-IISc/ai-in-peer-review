

This paper addresses text classification by extending the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) framework to a supervised setting through learned word-specific weights. The authors propose using the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient proxy for the Wasserstein distance, evaluating their approach on eight benchmark datasets against 26 baselines. The method achieves strong average performance, demonstrating significant improvements over prior work. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear mathematical exposition that balances technical depth and accessibility—particularly commendable for a topic involving complex optimal transport theory. The thorough analysis of the underlying mathematics and the structured presentation of experiments reflect high scholarly rigor.

The primary weakness lies in the novelty claim. While the integration of Sinkhorn distance for speed and the supervised weighting scheme represent incremental progress, the core idea of adapting WMD for classification is closely aligned with the authors' prior work (e.g., the 2015 WMD paper). The paper would benefit from a more precise articulation of how this work advances beyond the existing framework, particularly in distinguishing the supervised weighting component from the Sinkhorn approximation itself. A clearer comparison to the foundational WMD approach would strengthen the contribution statement.

Additionally, the comparison to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) baseline appears tangential and confusing. WCD is a simple centroid-based method that operates on a fundamentally different principle (e.g., bag-of-words averaging vs. optimal transport), making the comparison less relevant to the paper’s technical focus. The discussion would be more persuasive if comparisons were limited to methods sharing the same underlying paradigm (e.g., other optimal transport-based classifiers like WMD or its variants).

The most significant concern pertains to the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn algorithm. The paper does not adequately address how λ influences the results, despite its critical role in determining the behavior of the Sinkhorn distance relative to the Wasserstein distance. As is well known in the literature, λ controls the trade-off: large λ (e.g., λ → ∞) recovers the pure Wasserstein distance (albeit with slower convergence), while small λ (e.g., λ = 0.1) yields the entropic approximation characteristic of Sinkhorn, which is computationally efficient but statistically distinct. The authors’ results could be interpreted as either: (1) achieving Wasserstein-like behavior (if λ is sufficiently large), or (2) leveraging the entropic bias (if λ is small). The paper does not clarify which regime was used or justify the choice of λ. Given that λ often dominates the performance in practice (e.g., as noted in the Sinkhorn literature), this omission undermines the interpretation of the results. Specifically, the claim that the method "improves speed" without acknowledging that this speedup inherently comes at the cost of deviating from the Wasserstein metric requires explicit justification. The authors should clarify whether their λ values were chosen to approximate Wasserstein (with slower convergence) or to leverage the entropic bias (with faster convergence but a different metric), and how this choice impacts the observed performance gains.

In summary, this is a technically sound and well-structured paper that makes a modest but useful contribution to supervised text classification. The computational efficiency gains via Sinkhorn are compelling, and the empirical results are robust. However, the novelty claim needs sharpening, the WCD comparison should be omitted, and the treatment of λ requires substantial clarification to ensure the results are interpreted correctly. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution and impact. The work is suitable for acceptance, pending minor revisions to clarify the role of λ and refine the novelty discussion.
