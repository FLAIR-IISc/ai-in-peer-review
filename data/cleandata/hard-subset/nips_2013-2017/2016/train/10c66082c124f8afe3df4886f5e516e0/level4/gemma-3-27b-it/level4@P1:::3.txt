The authors propose a new method to learn a supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), effectively learning the parameters of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which is the Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) computed on text documents using word embeddings to define bin-to-bin dissimilarities. They parameterize the EMD cost matrix using a Mahalanobis distance between vector representations and, additionally, learn histogram coefficients for reweighting. The authors provide gradients, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), for this problem and present compelling experimental evidence. Overall, the paper represents a strong combination of existing techniques, yielding convincing experimental results, and I would support acceptance. The strengths of the paper lie in the simplicity and straightforwardness of the idea, despite the non-trivial implementation and testing. The experimental section is a particularly strong component. 

Areas for improvement include a clearer discussion of the interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations, increased mathematical rigor (the computations and notations are occasionally imprecise), and the inclusion of an algorithmic box to provide a clearer overview of the proposed method. 

A few minor comments:

*   In Eq. 1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used as a cost, while in Eq. 6, for Mahalanobis metric learning, this becomes the squared Euclidean metric (and thus the 2-Wasserstein distance). To avoid such inconsistencies, it might be clearer to state that the WMD is a *p*-Wasserstein distance between clouds of word embeddings, regardless of the value of *p*.
*   The reweighting approach proposed in Eq. 5 is not entirely novel, having been previously proposed by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification (“metric learning for text documents”). See also "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations" by Le and Cuturi.
*   It would be valuable to include experimental insights into the importance of the weight vector *w* in the learned metric. Does this innovation significantly impact performance, and if so, how?
*   The related work section could be strengthened by more explicitly highlighting the differences between this work and previous approaches. For example, this paper builds upon the work of Cuturi and Avis, but utilizes entropy regularization, which demonstrably improves speed and scalability. The distinction also lies in the nature of the cost: discrete in the previous work, now continuous but parameterized by linear maps. These arguments are currently missing. This paper also references Wang et al., however, that work encountered issues and remains unpublished. Their OT optimization, executed only once, did not perform as intended.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in document distance learning – incorporating supervision into the WMD framework.
*   The proposed method (S-WMD) demonstrates consistently strong performance across a diverse set of datasets, outperforming a large number of competitive baselines.
*   The technical contributions, particularly the efficient gradient computation and the combination of metric learning with word embeddings, are valuable.
*   The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The mathematical notation could be more rigorous.
*   The connection to prior work on histogram reweighting isn’t fully acknowledged.
*   The impact of the word importance weights (*w*) isn’t thoroughly investigated.
