

This paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) for text classification, introducing Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient proxy for the Wasserstein distance. The authors learn word-specific weights to adapt WMD for supervised tasks and evaluate their approach on 8 benchmark datasets against 26 baselines, reporting superior average performance. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a clear and thorough mathematical exposition that balances technical depth and accessibility.  

The primary contributions are well-motivated and technically sound, particularly the practical adoption of Sinkhorn to accelerate computation without significant accuracy loss. However, the novelty is somewhat limited relative to the foundational WMD work, as the core innovation (supervised adaptation via word weights) builds directly on prior ideas. Additionally, the comparison to World Centroid Distance appears tangential and confusing, as this baseline lacks clear relevance to the proposed method’s design or empirical claims.  

The most significant gap lies in the insufficient discussion of the regularization parameter λ. The paper does not clarify whether the chosen λ value aligns with the pure Wasserstein distance (requiring large λ for accuracy, albeit with slower convergence) or the entropic Sinkhorn approximation (using small λ for speed, but at the cost of deviating from Wasserstein behavior). In practice, λ critically determines the trade-off between approximation fidelity and computational efficiency—yet the paper provides no analysis of its impact on Equation (2) or the resulting model behavior. For instance, the authors do not address whether their implementation prioritizes Wasserstein-like properties (via large λ) or computational pragmatism (via small λ), which is a key consideration for reproducibility and interpretation. This omission is particularly notable given that λ often dominates performance in similar methods, as observed in prior work.  

Overall, the paper makes a solid empirical contribution with a clear technical foundation, but the lack of depth on λ’s role and the questionable reference to World Centroid Distance weaken its scholarly impact. Addressing these points would strengthen the paper’s claims and clarify its position within the broader landscape of distance-based text classification.
