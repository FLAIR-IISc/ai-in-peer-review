The authors present a novel method for learning the parameters of the Wasserstein distance for text documents (WMD), effectively an Earth Mover's Distance computed using word embeddings to define bin-to-bin dissimilarities. Their approach parameterizes the EMD cost matrix using a Mahalanobis distance based on vector representations, and further incorporates learned histogram coefficients, building upon prior work such as that of Lebanon. The paper provides gradients for Non-convex Optimization (NCA)-type costs within this framework and demonstrates strong experimental validation. Overall, the work represents a well-integrated combination of existing techniques, yielding compelling results and warranting acceptance. The paper’s strengths lie in the simplicity and clarity of the core idea, alongside the non-trivial implementation and comprehensive experimental evaluation. 

To further improve the manuscript, the authors should more clearly articulate the relationship between regularized and non-regularized formulations. A greater level of mathematical rigor would also be beneficial, addressing some imprecision in the computations and notations; an algorithmic box summarizing the proposed method would increase clarity. Specifically, the authors should address the following points:

*   The use of Euclidean distance in Eq. 1 and its transformation to a squared Euclidean metric (equivalent to the 2-Wasserstein distance) in Eq. 6 creates an inconsistency. Framing WMD as a p-Wasserstein distance between clouds of word embeddings, regardless of *p*, could resolve this.
*   The reweighting approach presented in Eq. 5 is not novel, having been previously proposed by Guy Lebanon for text document classification. Relevant work by Le/Cuturi on unsupervised Riemannian metric learning for histograms should also be acknowledged.
*   The importance of the learned weight vector *w* in the metric should be investigated experimentally. Insights into whether this innovation significantly impacts performance and its relevance to the overall approach would be valuable.
*   The related work section could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methodologies. For instance, the paper’s reliance on Cuturi/Avis' work should be highlighted, with explicit discussion of the advantages conferred by entropy regularization in terms of speed and scalability. The shift from a discrete cost function (Cuturi/Avis) to a continuous, linearly parameterized cost is a key distinction that deserves emphasis. The connection to Wang et al. should also be discussed, acknowledging the limitations of their unpublished work and the improvements offered by this approach – particularly the iterative OT optimization, as opposed to the single-run optimization in Wang et al. which proved ineffective.
