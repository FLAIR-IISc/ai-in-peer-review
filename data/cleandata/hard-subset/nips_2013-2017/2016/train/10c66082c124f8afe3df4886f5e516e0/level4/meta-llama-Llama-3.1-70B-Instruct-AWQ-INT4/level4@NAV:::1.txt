This paper introduces a supervised metric learning approach that builds upon the "word-movers distance" concept [19], a transport distance applied to word2vec word embeddings. The proposed method is versatile and can be applied to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances following linear projection. In addition to learning the linear projection to optimize performance, this approach also involves learning a word weighting scheme specific to document applications, which re-weights and re-normalizes word frequencies using learned weights. The authors present an efficient optimization technique to learn the proposed parameterized metric. The paper's experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach through comparisons with multiple baselines on document classification datasets using a kNN classifier based on the learned metric.

This work provides an interesting and technically useful extension of the recent word-movers distance, showcasing its effectiveness in kNN text classification tasks. However, the potential impact may be limited by the fact that the earth-movers distance with metric learning via "word centroid distance" initialization achieves similar performance to the full learning of the proposed distance, as seen in the last two rows of Table 3. This suggests that the main technical contributions of this paper may not significantly improve upon the initialization method based on existing techniques.

A few aspects are missing from the evaluation in Table 3, including an assessment of the "word centroid embedding" (weighted mean of word embeddings for the document) with all tested existing metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA). Furthermore, it would be beneficial to evaluate the relative importance of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A by examining performance with each component active in isolation.

The originality of this work primarily stems from the optimization method (Lines 156-175), and the concept of linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance is intriguing, albeit a limited contribution in itself. The paper is well-written and a pleasure to read, with a few minor issues that could be clarified, such as specifying the authors referred to in Line 152 ([1, 7, 8]?), adding stars to alpha and beta in Equation 11, and explicitly recalling the Sinkhorn scaling around Line 170, as mentioned in the introduction (Line 56).
