The paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel supervised metric learning approach extending the established Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD leverages word embeddings to define document distance as an optimal transport problem. This work addresses WMD’s limitation of being entirely unsupervised by learning both a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word-importance weights to incorporate supervision. The method minimizes leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error, utilizing an efficient approximation to overcome computational challenges associated with the optimal transport problem. The authors demonstrate strong performance across eight real-world text classification tasks, consistently outperforming a comprehensive set of 26 baselines.

The core idea of learning both a transformation matrix *A* and word-importance weights *w* is a compelling extension of WMD. The approach effectively integrates supervised information into the document distance calculation. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to approximate the original optimal transport problem is a key contribution, enabling efficient gradient computation and practical applicability. The empirical results are convincing, demonstrating the superiority of S-WMD across a diverse range of datasets and in comparison to numerous strong baselines.  The authors also provide insightful visualizations and a detailed analysis of training time.

A potential weakness lies in fully elucidating the individual contributions of the learned transformation *A* and the word-importance weights *w*. While both are learned simultaneously, further analysis exploring their independent effects could provide deeper understanding. Additionally, while the experimental setup is extensive, a comparison to a simple classifier operating directly on the word embeddings, without the WMD framework, would provide a stronger baseline for assessing the value of the proposed approach. The paper could benefit from explicitly stating the value of *k* used for k-nearest neighbors classification and whether this parameter is tuned per dataset.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed method presents a novel and significant extension to the widely used WMD metric, incorporating supervision in a principled manner.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is well-defined, and the efficient approximation using the Sinkhorn distance is a crucial contribution.
*   **Empirical Results:** The extensive experimental evaluation demonstrates consistently strong performance across diverse datasets and baselines.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the core concepts clearly.
*   **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is significant, as it offers a powerful new tool for document similarity and text classification.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Insight into Component Contributions:** The relative importance of the learned transformation *A* and word weights *w* isn’t thoroughly investigated.
*   **Missing Baseline:** Lacking a comparison with a simpler classifier trained directly on word embeddings limits a comprehensive assessment of the method's benefits.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of metric learning for text. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated. While some minor clarifications and additional baselines could strengthen the paper, the overall quality is high and warrants acceptance. The provided code will be valuable for the community.
