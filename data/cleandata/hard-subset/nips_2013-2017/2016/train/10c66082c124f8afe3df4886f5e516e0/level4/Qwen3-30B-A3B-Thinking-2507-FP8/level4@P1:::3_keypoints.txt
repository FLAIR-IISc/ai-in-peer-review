- Introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of WMD that learns a linear transform of word embeddings and word importance weights to minimize classification error.  
- Uses Cuturi's Sinkhorn distance to approximate WMD for computational efficiency.  
- Adopts a stochastic neighborhood approach inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) to enable differentiable optimization.  
- Evaluated on eight text classification tasks, outperforming 26 baselines (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA, WCD, WMD) and achieving lowest error on five datasets and best average performance.  
- Bridges unsupervised document-distance metrics and supervised learning by learning metrics directly in low-dimensional word embedding space, avoiding document-space dimensionality reduction.  
- Strong empirical contribution with comprehensive experiments, thorough baseline comparisons, and attention to reproducibility (initialization and batch optimization details).  
- Well-grounded theoretically, properly relating to optimal transport (EMD/Wasserstein), metric learning (NCA), and word embeddings (word2vec).  
- Novelty: first effective integration of supervision into optimal-transport–based document metrics at scale.  
- Practical significance: maintains computational efficiency (via Sinkhorn) with training times comparable to standard metric learning methods.  
- Writing and organization are clear, providing sufficient technical detail for reproduction.  
- Weakness: insufficiently distinguishes the reweighting approach from Lebanon’s prior metric-learning-for-text work.  
- Weakness: mathematical presentation and gradient derivations (Equations 8–10) lack rigor and have inconsistent notation (e.g., squared Euclidean vs. general p-norms).  
- Weakness: limited analysis of the learned weight vector w—unclear quantitative impact and which words are upweighted.  
- Meets NIPS criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance) and is strongly recommended for acceptance.  
- Pro/Con summary: strong empirical results and novelty, good reproducibility and motivation; cons are notation rigor, related-work distinction, and deeper analysis of word weights.
