This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel approach to learning a document distance metric by extending the recent Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) framework. WMD leverages word embeddings to compute distances between documents as an optimal transport problem, but is inherently unsupervised. S-WMD addresses this limitation by incorporating supervision to learn a metric that minimizes k-nearest neighbor classification error. The core idea is to simultaneously learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space *and* word-specific importance weights, enabling the model to emphasize words most relevant for distinguishing document classes. The authors also address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by utilizing a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering. Code is available.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in document distance metrics, starting from classical representations like bag-of-words and TF-IDF, through dimensionality reduction techniques like LSI and LDA, and culminating in the embedding-based approach of WMD. It also connects to the broader field of metric learning, acknowledging the quadratic scaling issues of many existing methods and positioning S-WMD as an efficient alternative, particularly suited for high-dimensional text data. The authors appropriately cite relevant work throughout.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of learning both a transformation of the word embedding space and word-specific importance weights within the WMD framework is a novel and promising approach.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results on eight datasets, consistently outperforming a wide range of 26 competitive baselines. This demonstrates the effectiveness of S-WMD.
*   **Efficiency:** The authors address a key challenge – the computational cost of incorporating supervision into WMD – with an efficient approximation based on the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, method, and experimental setup.
*   **Code Availability:** Providing code fosters reproducibility and encourages further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Intuition for Word Importance:** While the introduction of word importance weights (w) is conceptually appealing, the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the intuition behind Eq. (5) and a clearer discussion of how the learned weights contribute to the performance gains. Determining the relative contribution of the linear transformation (A) versus the word weights (w) would be insightful.
*   **Baseline Comparison Depth:** While a comprehensive set of baselines is included, the paper lacks an analysis of how the relative performance changes with different k values for kNN classification. Is the performance sensitive to k?
*   **Minor Clarifications:** The parameter λ appears in two different contexts (Eq. 2 and line 170), and the connection between them could be more explicitly stated. The claim regarding WMD being “forgiving” of syntactic variations (line 316) lacks supporting evidence or a more detailed explanation.
*   **Table Formatting:** In the original submission, there was a discrepancy regarding information referenced in the text that was not readily apparent in the tables (e.g., average rank). This has been clarified by the authors.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel method for supervised document distance learning.
*   The experimental results are strong and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing approaches.
*   The authors address a key practical challenge (computational efficiency) and provide a clear and well-written description of their work.
*   The availability of code enhances the reproducibility and impact of the research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of deeper insight into the learned word importance weights and their contribution to performance.
*   The absence of a more detailed ablation study or sensitivity analysis (e.g. varying k in kNN).

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document distance learning. The proposed S-WMD method is novel, effective, and efficiently implemented. While there are a few minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
