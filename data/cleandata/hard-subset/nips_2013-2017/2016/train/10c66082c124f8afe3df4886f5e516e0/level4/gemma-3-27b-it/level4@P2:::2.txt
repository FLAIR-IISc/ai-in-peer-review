This paper introduces a Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD) metric, extending the recent Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by incorporating supervised learning to refine word importance and embedding transformations. The authors address a key limitation of WMD – its unsupervised nature – and propose an efficient technique to minimize leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. The core idea is to learn an affine transformation of the word embedding space alongside word-importance weights, leveraging a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) for computational feasibility. The paper demonstrates strong empirical performance, consistently outperforming a comprehensive set of 26 baselines across eight real-world text classification tasks.

The motivation is well-articulated. Document distance metrics are fundamental to various NLP applications, and previous work, including WMD, has largely focused on unsupervised approaches. This paper directly tackles the need for task-specific adaptation. The connection to prior work on metric learning is appropriately discussed, noting the limitations of existing methods when applied to high-dimensional text data. The authors effectively position their contribution as leveraging the strengths of WMD – operating in a low-dimensional embedding space – while addressing its lack of supervision.

The technical approach is sound and clearly explained. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to approximate the WMD gradient is a crucial contribution, enabling efficient supervised training. The formulation of the loss function, based on stochastic leave-one-out classification error, is well-justified. The authors provide sufficient detail regarding the optimization process, including initialization strategies and gradient computation. The explanation of the relaxed optimal transport problem and its connection to the original WMD is particularly well done.

The experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough. The inclusion of eight diverse datasets and a large number of baseline methods demonstrates a strong commitment to rigorous evaluation. The consistent outperformance of S-WMD across these benchmarks is compelling. The visualization using t-SNE further supports the claim that S-WMD learns a more semantically meaningful distance metric. The ablation study, showing the benefit of the learned weights and transformations, is valuable. The discussion of training time and complexity provides practical insights.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** Consistently outperforms a large and diverse set of baselines across multiple datasets.
*   **Technical Soundness:**  The proposed method is well-motivated, clearly explained, and leverages existing techniques (WMD, Sinkhorn distance) in a novel and effective way.
*   **Significance:** Addresses a key limitation of WMD and provides a valuable tool for improving document classification performance.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:**  The extensive experimental setup and analysis provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with the details of WMD or metric learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The method's reliance on word embeddings means performance is inherently tied to the quality of those embeddings. While the authors use word2vec, exploring other embedding techniques could be beneficial.
*   While the complexity analysis is provided, further investigation into scalability for very large datasets may be warranted.




**Overall:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document distance metrics. The technical approach is sound, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results are compelling. I strongly recommend accepting this paper for presentation at NIPS.
