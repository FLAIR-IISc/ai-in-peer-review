This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach built on the "word-movers distance" [19], a transport distance applied to word2vec word embeddings. The proposed approach can be applied to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances after linear projection. In addition to learning the linear projection to maximize performance, the approach also learns a weighting of words, which is specific to the document application. This weighting involves re-weighting the frequency of each word by a learned weight and re-normalizing the word frequencies. The paper presents an efficient optimization method to learn the proposed parameterized metric.

The experimental evaluation thoroughly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach by comparing it to a large number of reasonable baselines on several document classification datasets using a kNN classifier based on the proposed learned metric. This work extends the recent word-movers distance in an interesting and technically useful way, as shown by its effectiveness on several kNN text classification tasks.

However, the potential impact of this work may be limited by the fact that earth-movers distance with a metric learning via a "word centroid distance" initialization performs similarly to the full learning of the proposed distance, as seen in the last two rows of Table 3. This suggests that the main technical contributions of this paper may not contribute significantly over this initialization method based on existing techniques.

One aspect missing in the evaluation is a comparison of the "word centroid embedding" (weighted mean of word embeddings for the document) with all tested existing metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA). Additionally, it would be useful to assess the relative importance of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A by evaluating performance with each of these components being active in isolation.

The originality of this work is mostly related to the optimization of the metric (Lines 156-175), and the idea of linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance is interesting but a limited contribution in itself.

The paper is well-written and a pleasure to read. However, there are a few minor issues that could be clarified. For instance, in Line 152, it is unclear which authors are being referred to ([1, 7, 8]?). Additionally, in Equation 11, alpha and beta should likely carry stars. It would also be useful to explicitly recall around Line 170 that this is the Sinkhorn scaling referred to in the introduction (Line 56).
