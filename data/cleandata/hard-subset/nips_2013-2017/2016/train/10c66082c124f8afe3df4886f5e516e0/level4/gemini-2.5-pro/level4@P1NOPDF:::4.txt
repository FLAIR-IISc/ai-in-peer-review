This paper proposes a supervised method for learning a document distance metric, building upon the Word Mover's Distance (WMD). WMD, introduced in prior work, is based on the Earth Mover's Distance. It measures the minimum cost to transform the word distribution of one document into another, where the ground distance between words is typically based on their proximity in a word embedding space like word2vec. The authors extend this by learning two components: (1) a linear transformation of the word embeddings and (2) a set of word importance weights. These parameters are optimized to increase the distance between documents with different labels while decreasing it for documents with the same label.

The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. It includes a thorough review of related work, demonstrating the authors' strong command of the literature. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering a variety of datasets. However, I have some concerns regarding the motivation for the proposed formulation and the overall significance of the contribution.

1)  The motivation for reweighting the word distribution using the vector `w` is not well-justified. This component seems powerful enough to fundamentally alter the content of a document, potentially transforming it into something semantically different. The paper would be strengthened by a clearer explanation of why this reweighting is necessary and how it avoids distorting the original document's meaning. For instance, an analysis of the learned weights or a justification for how this component helps would be beneficial.

2)  The significance of the technical contribution appears limited. The proposed method, which learns a linear transformation `A` and a weight vector `w` under a Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA)-style objective, is a natural and somewhat straightforward extension of WMD to a supervised setting. While effective, the formulation itself does not seem particularly novel or groundbreaking.

3)  The paper does not clearly explain how the proposed method generalizes to new documents at test time. Specifically, the reweighting vector `w` is learned over the vocabulary of the training set. How are weights determined for words in a new document that were not present during training? This is a critical point for the practical application of the method.

4)  The term 'semantic difference' used in the abstract appears to refer to documents having different class labels (e.g., differences in authorship or topic). This might be a slightly confusing use of the term, as 'semantic difference' usually implies a difference in meaning, which may not perfectly align with supervised labels like authorship. The authors should clarify their intended meaning of this term.

In summary, this is a well-written paper presenting a sensible extension to WMD. My main reservations concern the motivation for certain design choices and the overall novelty. However, I would be willing to support the paper's acceptance if the authors can adequately address the concerns raised above.
