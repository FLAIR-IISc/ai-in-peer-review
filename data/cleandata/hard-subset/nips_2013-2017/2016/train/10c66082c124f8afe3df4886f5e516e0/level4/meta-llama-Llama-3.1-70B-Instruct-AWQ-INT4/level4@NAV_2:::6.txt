The paper proposes a novel method for learning a supervised document distance metric, building upon the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) algorithm. The authors extend the original unsupervised WMD to the supervised setting, where the goal is to learn a distance metric that captures the semantic meaning of similarity between documents. The proposed method, called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), learns a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector to improve the document distance.

The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of S-WMD on eight real-world text classification tasks, comparing its performance to 26 competitive baselines. The results show that S-WMD consistently outperforms almost all baselines, demonstrating its effectiveness in learning a supervised document distance metric.

The key contribution of the paper is the proposal of a novel method for learning a supervised document distance metric. The authors' approach is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector, which allows for a more accurate capture of the semantic meaning of similarity between documents. The use of a linear transformation and word-importance weights is a novel approach that sets S-WMD apart from other metric learning methods.

The paper also provides a thorough evaluation of S-WMD, including a comparison to other state-of-the-art methods. The results show that S-WMD is a competitive method that can achieve state-of-the-art performance on text classification tasks. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the results, including a discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of S-WMD.

One potential weakness of the paper is the lack of a clear explanation of the intuition behind Equation (5), which defines the usage of the word-importance weights. The authors could provide more insight into why this equation is chosen and how it contributes to the overall performance of S-WMD.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship between S-WMD and other metric learning methods. The authors mention that S-WMD is related to other methods, such as Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), but a more detailed comparison would be helpful in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of S-WMD.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method. The evaluation is thorough, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of S-WMD in learning a supervised document distance metric. With some minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
