The authors address the problem of text classification, building upon prior work that utilized Word Mover's Distance (WMD) – a metric based on the Wasserstein distance – by introducing a supervised learning approach to determine word-specific weights. Their key contribution lies in employing the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient proxy for the Wasserstein distance, and they demonstrate strong performance on eight datasets, achieving state-of-the-art results when compared to 26 baselines. This is a clearly written and well-presented paper that thoroughly explains the underlying mathematical concepts. The primary weaknesses are a relatively incremental novelty compared to the original WMD paper, and the inclusion of comparisons to World Centroid Distance, which I found unhelpful and somewhat distracting. Furthermore, the discussion of the regularization parameter, lambda, feels incomplete. Based on my experience, lambda’s value significantly impacts performance, potentially dominating equation (2). While theoretically, a sufficiently large lambda should approximate the behavior of the pure Wasserstein distance (albeit with slower convergence), practitioners often employ small lambda values to accelerate convergence, resulting in behavior fundamentally different from that of Wasserstein distance. Therefore, I am interested in understanding whether the reported results exhibit behavior closer to the pure Wasserstein distance or the entropic relaxation inherent to the Sinkhorn distance, as dictated by the chosen lambda.
