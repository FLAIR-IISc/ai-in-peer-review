This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach built on top of the recent "word-movers distance" (WMD) [19], a transport distance applied to word2vec word embeddings. The proposed approach, called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), can be applied to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances after linear projection. On top of learning the linear projection to maximize performance, S-WMD also learns a weighting of words, which is specific to the document application. This weighting consists of re-weighting the frequency of each word by a learned weight and re-normalizing the word frequencies.

The paper presents an efficient optimization method to learn the proposed parameterized metric. A thorough experimental evaluation shows the effectiveness of the proposed approach by comparing it to a large number of reasonable baselines on several document classification datasets using a kNN classifier based on the proposed learned metric.

The work extends the recent WMD, which is an interesting and technically useful extension, shown to be effective on a number of kNN text classification tasks. However, the potential impact is perhaps limited by the fact that earth-movers distance with a metric learning via a "word centroid distance" initialization performs similar to the full learning of the proposed distance. This suggests that the main technical contributions of this paper may not contribute very much over this initialization method based on existing techniques.

One thing that is missing in the evaluation is an assessment of the relative importance of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A. It would be useful to see performance with each one of these components being active in isolation. Additionally, an evaluation of the "word centroid embedding" (weighted mean of word embeddings for the document) with all tested existing metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA) would be beneficial.

The originality of the paper is mostly related to the way to optimize the metric (Section 3.1), and the idea of a linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance is very interesting but a limited contribution in itself.

The paper is very well written and a pleasure to read. However, there are a few minor issues that could be clarified. For example, in Line 152, it would be helpful to specify which paper the authors refer to. Additionally, in Equation 11, alpha and beta should carry stars. It would also be useful to explicitly recall around Line 170 that this is the Sinkhorn scaling referred to in the introduction in Line 56.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The work is a complete piece of research, and the authors are careful about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work.

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and adequately informs the reader. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results.

**Originality**: The paper proposes a novel combination of familiar techniques, and it is clear how this work differs from previous contributions. The related work is adequately referenced.

**Significance**: The results are important, and other researchers or practitioners are likely to use these ideas or build on them. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

**Arguments for acceptance**:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective approach to supervised metric learning for document classification.
* The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance**:

* The potential impact of the paper may be limited by the fact that earth-movers distance with a metric learning via a "word centroid distance" initialization performs similar to the full learning of the proposed distance.
* The evaluation is missing some important comparisons, such as the assessment of the relative importance of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A.
