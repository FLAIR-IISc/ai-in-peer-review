This work addresses the task of categorical text classification. It extends a prior method based on the Word Mover's Distance (Wasserstein) by introducing a supervised framework for learning word-specific weights. The core contribution is the use of the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient proxy for the Wasserstein distance to improve scalability. The proposed approach is rigorously evaluated across eight datasets against 26 baselines, demonstrating superior average performance.

The paper is exceptionally well-written. The authors provide a lucid introduction to the topic and a thorough yet highly accessible mathematical analysis of the underlying problem.

However, a few points merit further consideration. The novelty appears somewhat incremental when compared to the original Word Mover's Distance publication. Furthermore, the comparisons made against the Word Centroid Distance felt more confusing than relevant to the central claims.

My primary concern relates to the insufficient discussion of the regularization factor, lambda. In practice, the value of lambda can have a substantial impact, at times dominating the objective function in eq(2). Theoretically, a large lambda should yield results that approximate the pure Wasserstein distance, albeit at the cost of slower convergence. Conversely, the common use of a small lambda in Sinkhorn applications accelerates convergence but results in a distinct, entropy-regularized behavior. It is therefore unclear whether the lambda value used in this work encourages a behavior closer to the true Wasserstein distance or one that is characteristically Sinkhorn (entropic). Clarifying this trade-off would significantly strengthen the paper's analysis.
