This paper introduces a supervised metric learning approach leveraging the recent word-movers distance (WMD) [19], a transport distance applied to word2vec embeddings. The method generalizes to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances following a linear projection. Importantly, the approach learns not only this projection to maximize performance, but also word-specific weights used to re-weight word frequencies within documents before normalization. The authors present an efficient optimization method for learning the parameters of this parameterized metric. A comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach against a strong set of baselines on several document classification datasets, utilizing a k-nearest neighbors (kNN) classifier based on the learned metric.

This work represents a valuable extension of WMD, offering a technically sound and practically effective improvement demonstrated through solid performance on kNN-based text classification tasks. The paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read. However, the potential impact of the contribution, while positive, appears somewhat limited. Specifically, the performance of the proposed full learning approach is very close to that achieved using earth-movers distance initialized with a “word centroid distance” (as seen in the last two rows of Table 3). This suggests that the primary technical contributions of this paper provide a marginal benefit over this initialization method, which relies on existing techniques.

The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including several additional baselines. In particular, the performance of a simple “word centroid embedding” – the weighted mean of word embeddings for each document – evaluated *with* each of the existing metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA) is missing from Table 3.  This would provide a clearer understanding of the contribution stemming specifically from the WMD and the learned metric, rather than simply from the embedding representation itself. 

Furthermore, an assessment of the relative importance of the learned word-specific weights and the linear projection matrix *A* would be highly informative. Presenting results with each component active in isolation would help to disentangle their individual contributions to performance.  Is the improvement primarily driven by the optimized projection, the refined word weighting, or a synergistic effect of both?

The originality of the work resides primarily in the optimization method (Lines 156–175). While the idea of linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance framework is interesting, its inherent contribution is relatively modest.

Several minor clarifications would further enhance the paper's readability:

*   Line 152: The reference to “the authors” is ambiguous. Please specify which paper is being referenced ([1,7,8]?).
*   Equation 11: It appears that α and β should be superscripted (e.g., α*, β*) to indicate that they are the optimal values obtained through optimization.
*   Around line 170, explicitly mentioning that the scaling being performed is the Sinkhorn scaling (referenced in the introduction, Line 56) would improve continuity and understanding for the reader.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a solid and well-executed paper presenting a practical improvement to a recent and promising technique. While the gains over a strong baseline are modest, the method is sound and the experimental evaluation is thorough. Addressing the suggested improvements regarding baseline comparisons and ablation studies would strengthen the paper and better highlight the value of the proposed contributions.
