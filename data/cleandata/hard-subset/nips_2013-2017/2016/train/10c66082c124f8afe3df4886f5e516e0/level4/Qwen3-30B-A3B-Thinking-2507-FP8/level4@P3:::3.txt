

The paper proposes a method to learn a supervised metric for the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), termed Supervised-WMD (S-WMD). The authors extend the unsupervised WMD by learning a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a word importance weight vector, with the goal of improving document classification performance. The key insight is that the WMD, while effective, is entirely unsupervised and lacks a mechanism to incorporate class information when available. The authors formulate this as a metric learning problem, directly minimizing a stochastic approximation of the leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric. To address computational challenges, they employ entropy regularization (Sinkhorn distance) to approximate the optimal transport problem, reducing the complexity from cubic to quadratic. The method is evaluated across eight text classification tasks, where it consistently outperforms 26 competitive baselines.

The paper builds upon several important lines of work: Kusner et al.'s WMD (2015) for document distances using optimal transport on word embeddings, Cuturi's entropy-regularized optimal transport (2013), and NCA (Goldberger et al., 2005) for metric learning. The authors correctly identify that prior work on metric learning typically operates on high-dimensional document representations, whereas their approach leverages low-dimensional word embeddings throughout, making it computationally more efficient for text. They also acknowledge the related work of Lebanon (2006) on histogram reweighting for text classification, though they do not fully address the novelty of this aspect.

The strengths of this paper are clear. First, the experimental evaluation is thorough, covering eight diverse text classification tasks with detailed comparisons against 26 baselines, including both unsupervised and supervised methods. The results consistently demonstrate S-WMD's superiority, with the method achieving the lowest classification error on five of the eight datasets and outperforming all baselines on average. Second, the paper provides a well-justified computational approach, using entropy regularization to make the optimization tractable while maintaining good approximation quality. Third, the implementation details are clear, and the authors provide an efficient training algorithm that incorporates clever initialization and batch gradient descent.

However, there are several areas for improvement. First, the paper contains some notational inconsistencies: in Equation (1), the authors use the Euclidean distance as the cost, but in Equation (6), they refer to it as "squared Euclidean," which is confusing. It would be clearer to consistently refer to the p-Wasserstein distance (with p=2) throughout. Second, the "reweighting" approach in Equation (5) is not novel; Lebanon (2006) proposed an identical technique for text classification, and Cuturi & Le (2014) have also explored similar histogram reweighting approaches. The authors should more thoroughly discuss this prior work and clarify how their approach differs or extends these earlier methods. Third, the paper lacks analysis on the importance of the weight vector w. It would be valuable to include ablation studies showing how much the learned weights contribute to the performance gains, particularly compared to using the linear transformation A alone.

Regarding originality, while the combination of WMD with metric learning is novel, the technical approach builds on well-established techniques (entropy-regularized optimal transport, NCA-style metric learning). The authors could strengthen their contribution by more clearly differentiating their work from Wang et al. (2015), which also explores metric learning for EMD but with several key differences: Wang et al. learn the ground metric (not the embedding transformation) and do not incorporate histogram reweighting. The authors' use of entropy regularization to make the optimization tractable is a significant practical contribution, but this should be more explicitly contrasted with previous work.

The significance of this work is strong. Document distance metrics are fundamental to many text processing tasks, and the authors demonstrate that incorporating supervision into the WMD framework leads to consistent improvements across multiple datasets. The approach is practical, efficient, and directly applicable to real-world text classification problems. The fact that S-WMD outperforms even the best unsupervised baselines (including WMD itself) on most datasets demonstrates its value for practitioners.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, though some mathematical details could be tightened. The authors provide sufficient information for reproducibility, including implementation details and a clear description of the optimization procedure. The experimental setup is rigorous, with multiple datasets, appropriate baselines, and careful hyperparameter tuning.

In conclusion, this is a solid contribution to the field of document representation and metric learning. The paper presents a practical, efficient method that improves upon the state-of-the-art for text classification. While some aspects of the technical approach are not entirely novel, the thorough experimental validation and clear presentation make this a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the notational inconsistencies and provide more thorough discussion of the histogram reweighting component relative to prior work.
