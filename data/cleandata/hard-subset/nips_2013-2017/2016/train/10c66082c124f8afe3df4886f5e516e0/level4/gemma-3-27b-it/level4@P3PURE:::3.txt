The authors propose a novel method for learning the parameters of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), effectively the Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) computed on text documents using word embeddings to define bin-to-bin dissimilarities. They parameterize the EMD cost matrix using a Mahalanobis distance between vector representations, and further learn histogram coefficients for reweighting, building upon prior work by Lebanon et al. Importantly, the authors derive gradients for NCA-type costs within this framework and support their approach with compelling experimental results. Overall, the paper presents a well-considered combination of existing techniques, yielding convincing improvements in performance. I recommend acceptance. The strengths of this work lie in its conceptual simplicity and the non-trivial implementation and experimental validation. The experimental section is particularly strong.

However, several areas could benefit from improvement. The interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations should be clarified. The mathematical treatment could be more rigorous; computations and notations are somewhat imprecise. Finally, including an algorithmic box would enhance clarity regarding the authors’ proposed method. 

Specifically: in Eq. 1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used as a cost, while in Eq. 6, for Mahalanobis metric learning, this becomes the squared Euclidean metric (equivalent to the 2-Wasserstein distance). To avoid this inconsistency, it would be clearer to frame the WMD as a p-Wasserstein distance between clouds of word embeddings, irrespective of the value of p. The reweighting approach in Eq. 5 is not novel, having been previously proposed by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification (“metric learning for text documents”). Relevant related work also includes “Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations” by Le and Cuturi. 

Further experimental insights would be valuable. What is the importance of the learned weight vector *w* in the metric? Does this innovation significantly impact performance, and if so, in what way? 

The related work section could be strengthened by more explicitly highlighting the distinctions between this work and previous approaches. This paper clearly builds upon the work of Cuturi and Avis, but utilizes entropy regularization, which demonstrably improves speed and scalability. The cost function also differs: whereas the previous work employs a discrete cost, this paper utilizes a continuous, linearly parameterized cost. These key differences are currently missing. While the paper also draws inspiration from Wang et al., that work remains unpublished and, as presented, is ineffective due to a single, non-iterative optimization of the OT problem.
