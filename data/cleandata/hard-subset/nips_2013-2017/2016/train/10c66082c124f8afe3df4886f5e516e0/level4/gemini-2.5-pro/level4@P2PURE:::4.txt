This paper presents a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a document distance metric based on optimal transport between word distributions. The original WMD uses a ground distance derived from pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec). The authors propose to learn this metric by simultaneously optimizing two components: (1) a linear transformation of the word embeddings and (2) a word-importance reweighting vector. The objective is to learn a distance function that better separates documents with different class labels.

The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The authors provide a thorough review of related work, demonstrating a strong command of the literature. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with results reported on multiple datasets.

However, the motivation for the proposed formulation requires further explanation, and the work's significance appears limited. My main concerns are as follows:

1.  The motivation for reweighting the word distribution via the vector `w` is not sufficiently explained. This component seems capable of fundamentally altering a document's representation, potentially to the point where it no longer reflects the original text. The authors should provide a stronger justification for this approach, perhaps by demonstrating that it improves discriminability without distorting the document's core meaning.

2.  The novelty of the proposed method appears limited. The approach of learning a linear transformation `A` and a reweighting vector `w` using a Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA)-style objective function is a straightforward extension of WMD to a supervised setting. While potentially effective, the formulation itself is not particularly groundbreaking.

3.  The paper does not clearly describe how the learned metric is applied to new, unseen documents at test time. Specifically, it is unclear how the reweighting vector `w` is determined for a document that was not part of the training set. This is a crucial detail for the practical application of the method.

4.  The use of the term "semantic difference" in the abstract is ambiguous. It appears to be used as a synonym for class label differences (e.g., topic or authorship), which is a non-standard usage of the term "semantic." The authors should clarify their intended meaning or use more precise language.

Overall, the paper is well-written and the proposed method is evaluated thoroughly. I would be willing to recommend acceptance if the authors can satisfactorily address the points raised above.
