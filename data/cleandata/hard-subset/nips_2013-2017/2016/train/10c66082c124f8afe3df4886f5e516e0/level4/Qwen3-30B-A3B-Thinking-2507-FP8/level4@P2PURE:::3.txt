

The authors propose a novel method for learning the parameters of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which computes Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) on text documents using word embeddings to define bin-to-bin dissimilarities. Specifically, they parameterize the EMD cost (i.e., ground metric) matrix via a Mahalanobis distance between vector representations and additionally learn histogram coefficients reweighting, building on Lebanon's work (see comment below). The authors derive gradients for NCA-style costs and present compelling experimental results. Overall, the paper synthesizes established techniques into a simple yet non-trivially implementable approach, with the experimental section serving as a strong contribution. I recommend acceptance.  

Key strengths include the method's simplicity and straightforward core idea, though implementation and testing present non-trivial challenges.  

Key improvements needed:  
1. Clarify the interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations.  
2. Enhance mathematical rigor (computations and notation are inconsistent).  
3. Include an algorithmic box to explicitly delineate the proposed procedure.  

Specific comments:  
- In Eq.1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used as the cost, while Eq.6 employs the squared Euclidean metric (typically associated with the 2-Wasserstein distance). To avoid inconsistency, the paper should uniformly define WMD as the *p*-Wasserstein distance between point clouds of word embeddings for any *p*.  
- The "reweighting" approach in Eq.5 is not novel; it was explicitly proposed by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification ("Metric Learning for Text Documents"). This should be acknowledged, as well as related work in "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations" by Le/Cuturi.  
- Provide experimental insights on the importance of the learned weight vector **w**—does this innovation contribute meaningfully to performance, or is it redundant?  
- Strengthen the related work section by clarifying distinctions:  
  - Unlike Cuturi/Avis (which uses discrete OT without regularization), this paper employs entropy regularization, enabling faster scaling.  
  - The cost structure differs: while Cuturi/Avis uses a discrete cost matrix, this work uses a continuous, linearly parameterized cost.  
  - The connection to Wang et al. is tenuous; their OT approach runs only once and is unpublishable due to fundamental flaws (as noted in their draft).
