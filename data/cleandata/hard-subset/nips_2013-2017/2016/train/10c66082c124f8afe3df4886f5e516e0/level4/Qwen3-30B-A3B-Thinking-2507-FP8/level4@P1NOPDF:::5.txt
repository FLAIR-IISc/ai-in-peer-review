

The authors address text classification into predefined categories by extending prior work on Word Mover's Distance (Wasserstein distance) to a supervised setting through learned word-specific weights. Their key contribution replaces the computationally intensive Wasserstein distance with the Sinkhorn distance as a faster proxy, evaluating the approach on 8 datasets against 26 baselines and achieving the best average performance. The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the topic and providing a thorough, accessible analysis of the underlying mathematics.  

However, two limitations warrant consideration. First, the novelty appears constrained relative to the foundational Word Mover's Distance (WMD) paper; the core extension to supervised learning via word weights does not significantly diverge from established directions. Second, the comparison against the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) is unclear and potentially misleading—WCD operates under fundamentally different assumptions (e.g., centroid-based, non-optimal transport), making the reference confusing rather than illuminating.  

Most critically, the discussion of the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn approximation is insufficient. Based on my experience, λ exerts a dominant influence on the model’s behavior, often overshadowing Equation (2)’s intended formulation. Theoretically, a sufficiently large λ should recover pure Wasserstein behavior (at the cost of slower convergence), yet practitioners routinely use small λ values to accelerate convergence, resulting in a distinct entropic-regularized regime. I am therefore uncertain whether the authors’ implementation aligns with pure Wasserstein behavior or deliberately adopts the Sinkhorn approximation. Clarifying this—particularly the chosen λ values and their implications—would strengthen the paper’s technical contribution.
