

This paper proposes a supervised extension to Word Mover's Distance (WMD) for learning document distances. WMD, originally motivated by Earth Mover's Distance, computes the optimal transport cost between word distributions to measure document similarity, using Word2Vec embeddings for word-level distances. The authors introduce two key modifications: (1) a linear transformation applied to word embeddings, and (2) a reweighted word distribution, to enhance separation between documents of different labels. The paper is clearly written, with a comprehensive survey of related work demonstrating the authors' strong grasp of the literature. Experimental results on multiple novel datasets are presented.

However, the paper requires stronger justification for its core contributions. First, the motivation for reweighting word distributions using weights $w$ is insufficiently explained. The reweighting procedure risks altering the document's semantic content—e.g., by amplifying non-representative words—potentially transforming the document into a different semantic entity. The authors must clarify why this reweighting preserves the original document's meaning while improving label separation. Second, the contribution lacks novelty: adapting WMD with a linear transformation $A$ and reweighting $w$ via the Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA) objective is a straightforward extension to the supervised setting, not a significant advancement. The formulation does not introduce novel theoretical insights or substantially improve upon existing supervised distance-learning methods. Third, the paper fails to address how the model handles new, unseen documents. Specifically, it is unclear how the reweighting weights $w$ would be determined for a completely novel document, as $w$ appears to be learned per-document in the training phase. Finally, the abstract’s reference to "semantic difference" is ambiguous. Given that the method distinguishes documents by label (e.g., topic or authorship), "semantic difference" likely refers to label-based semantic distinctions. However, the term is misleading if it implies differences beyond label assignment (e.g., stylistic or topical shifts), as these are not inherently semantic in the intended sense. The authors should clarify this terminology to avoid confusion.

In summary, while the paper is well-written and the experiments are thorough, the methodological choices require stronger justification. The reweighting mechanism lacks a clear semantic rationale, the contribution is incremental, and key implementation details for new documents are omitted. The authors should address these points to strengthen the paper’s impact. Provided these concerns are resolved, the work is acceptable for publication.
