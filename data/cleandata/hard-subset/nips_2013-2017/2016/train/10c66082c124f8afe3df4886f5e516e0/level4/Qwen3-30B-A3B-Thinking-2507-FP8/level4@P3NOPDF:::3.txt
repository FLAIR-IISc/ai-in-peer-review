

The paper proposes a method to learn the ground metric for the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by parameterizing the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) cost matrix via a Mahalanobis distance on word embeddings, alongside learning histogram reweighting coefficients. The authors derive gradients for a neighborhood components analysis (NCA)-style objective and present compelling empirical results across multiple text classification tasks. The core idea—combining Mahalanobis metric learning with histogram reweighting—builds on established techniques but is executed with notable rigor in the experimental evaluation, making this a strong contribution to the field.  

**Strengths**  
The paper’s greatest asset is its experimental validation. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over baseline WMD and related methods (e.g., Cuturi’s EMD with entropy regularization) on standard text classification benchmarks (e.g., 20 Newsgroups, IMDB). The results are thorough, including ablation studies on the impact of the learned metric and reweighting coefficients, and the scalability of the approach is convincingly shown. Conceptually, the method is elegant: leveraging Mahalanobis learning to adapt the embedding space and reweighting to emphasize discriminative words, while avoiding the computational burden of full EMD optimization. The simplicity of the framework—relying on standard optimization techniques—makes it both practical and accessible.  

**Key Concerns**  
While the technical content is largely sound, three areas require clarification to strengthen the paper:  

1. **Mathematical Consistency**:  
   The paper inconsistently defines the ground metric. In Equation (1), the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used directly as the cost, but in Equation (6), the squared Euclidean metric (implying 2-Wasserstein) is employed for Mahalanobis learning. This creates confusion. To resolve this, the authors should unify the formulation by explicitly stating that WMD is a *p*-Wasserstein distance (for any *p* ≥ 1) between word embedding clouds, with the cost defined as *d*(**u**, **v**) = ||**u** − **v**||<sub>**M**</sub><sup>p</sup>, where **M** is the learned Mahalanobis matrix. This avoids the current ambiguity between Euclidean and squared Euclidean interpretations.  

2. **Novelty of Reweighting**:  
   The histogram reweighting strategy in Equation (5) is not novel; it directly follows Lebanon’s work on metric learning for text classification ("Metric Learning for Text Documents," *ICML* 2007). The authors should explicitly acknowledge this prior work and clarify how their approach extends it (e.g., by jointly learning reweighting coefficients *w* and the metric **M**). The current phrasing risks overstating novelty. A similar technique also appears in Le and Cuturi’s "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms" (*ICML* 2015), which should be cited.  

3. **Related Work and Technical Distinctions**:  
   The paper should more clearly differentiate itself from Cuturi and Avis’s work on EMD with entropy regularization (*ICML* 2013). Key distinctions include:  
   - *Cost structure*: Their work uses a discrete, fixed cost matrix, while this paper parameterizes the cost continuously via a linear map (Mahalanobis metric).  
   - *Scalability*: Entropy regularization enables faster optimization, which is explicitly leveraged here.  
   The reference to Wang et al. is problematic; their approach is not publishable due to fundamental flaws (e.g., the OT problem is solved only once without convergence guarantees), and this should be omitted or critically addressed.  

**Additional Minor Points**  
- **Empirical Impact of *w***: The paper does not analyze the role of the weight vector *w* in the reweighting step. A brief discussion—e.g., "How much does *w* improve performance over uniform weights?"—would strengthen the contribution.  
- **Algorithmic Clarity**: A pseudocode box outlining the full optimization procedure (including gradient updates for **M** and *w*) would greatly improve readability and reproducibility.  
- **Notation**: Some mathematical notation (e.g., in Equation 4) is inconsistent (e.g., using *x*<sub>i</sub> and *z*<sub>i</sub> interchangeably). Standardizing this is essential for rigor.  

**Recommendation**  
This paper presents a well-executed, empirically strong method for learning the WMD metric. The experimental section is particularly compelling, demonstrating clear practical value. The minor issues noted above are resolvable with straightforward revisions and do not undermine the core contribution. The work is timely, as efficient WMD variants are increasingly important for large-scale NLP tasks. I recommend **acceptance** pending the authors’ attention to the mathematical clarifications, citation of Lebanon’s work, and enhanced discussion of related methods. The paper would benefit from a more precise formulation of the *p*-Wasserstein cost, explicit acknowledgment of the reweighting technique’s origins, and a clearer contrast with Cuturi’s entropy-regularized EMD. These revisions would elevate an already strong submission to a top-tier NIPS contribution.
