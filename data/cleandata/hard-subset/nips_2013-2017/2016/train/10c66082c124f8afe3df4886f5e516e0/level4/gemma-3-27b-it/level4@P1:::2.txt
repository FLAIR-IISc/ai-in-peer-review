This paper proposes a supervised extension to the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), learning weights to adapt the distance for improved performance on text classification tasks. The authors present a technically sound and well-explained method, accompanied by a remarkably comprehensive experimental evaluation. It is encouraging to see the WMD leveraged with discriminative training to learn a task-specific metric.

The core idea is to learn an affine transformation of the word embedding space alongside word-importance weights, minimizing leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. Addressing the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD, the authors provide an efficient approximation leveraging the Sinkhorn distance. The method is evaluated on eight real-world text classification datasets, consistently outperforming a broad range of 26 competitive baselines.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed approach is well-motivated and theoretically grounded, building upon existing work in optimal transport and metric learning. The use of the Sinkhorn distance for efficient approximation is a clever and effective solution to a significant computational bottleneck.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The technical details are explained in a concise and accessible manner, with sufficient detail to allow for reproduction of the results.
*   **Extensive Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is a major strength of this work. The authors thoroughly compare S-WMD to a very large number of baselines across diverse datasets, demonstrating its consistent superiority.
*   **Significance:** The results indicate that S-WMD represents a significant advance in document distance metrics, offering substantial improvements over existing methods. The method addresses a crucial limitation of the original WMD by effectively incorporating supervision.
* **Originality:** Extending WMD to a supervised setting is novel and insightful. The combination of learning a transformation of the word embeddings *and* re-weighting word frequencies provides a flexible and effective approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   While the paper mentions the potential limitations related to longer documents and the squared Euclidean distance in the CLASSIC dataset, a deeper discussion of these limitations and potential avenues for future work would be beneficial.
*   The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of the sensitivity of the method to the choice of hyperparameters (learning rates, regularization strength, etc.). Though Bayesian optimization is used, understanding the impact of these choices is valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The method presents a significant improvement over existing document distance metrics.
*   The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and extensively evaluated.
*   The proposed approach addresses a key limitation of the original WMD.
*   The work has the potential to impact a wide range of applications in text retrieval and natural language processing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   While the method performs well, the gains aren't uniformly large across all datasets.
*   The computational complexity, while addressed with approximations, remains a concern for extremely large datasets.



**Overall:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a substantial contribution to the field of document distance metrics. The technical innovations, combined with the comprehensive experimental evaluation, strongly support acceptance to NIPS. I am confident that this work will be of significant interest to the NIPS community and will inspire further research in this area.
