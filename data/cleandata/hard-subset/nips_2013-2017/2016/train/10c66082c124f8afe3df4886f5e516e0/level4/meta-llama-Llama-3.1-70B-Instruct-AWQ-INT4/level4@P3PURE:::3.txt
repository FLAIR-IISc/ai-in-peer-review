The authors propose a novel method for learning the parameters of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which is essentially the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) computed on text documents using word embeddings to calculate bin-to-bin dissimilarities. They parameterize the EMD cost (i.e., ground metric) matrix using a Mahalanobis distance between vector representations. Additionally, they propose learning histogram coefficients reweighting, a concept that has been explored in previous work, such as Lebanon's research (and as noted below). The authors provide the gradients of NCA-type costs for this problem and offer compelling experimental evidence. Overall, the paper presents a nice combination of existing techniques, yielding convincing experimental results. I would support acceptance.

The strengths of the paper lie in its simplicity and relatively straightforward idea, although its implementation and testing are not trivial. Consequently, the experimental section is a strong aspect of this paper. To improve the paper, the authors should better handle the interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations, be more rigorous with their mathematical computations and notation, and ideally provide an algorithmic box to clearly illustrate their proposed method. A few minor comments are as follows:

- In Eq. 1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used as a cost, whereas in Eq. 6, for the purpose of Mahalanobis metric learning, this cost becomes the squared Euclidean metric (and thus what is usually referred to as 2-Wasserstein). To avoid such inconsistencies, it would be easier to simply state that WMD is p-Wasserstein between clouds of word embeddings, regardless of the value of p.

- The "reweighting" approach proposed in Eq. 5 is not new, as it was already proposed by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification ("metric learning for text documents"). See also "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations" by Le/Cuturi.

- Any experimental insights on the importance of the weight vector w in the learned metric would be valuable. Does this innovation matter, and is it relevant in any way?

- In the related work section, it would be interesting to more clearly underline the differences with previous work. For instance, this paper builds on the work of Cuturi/Avis but uses entropy regularization, which clearly speeds up and scales up applications. The cost is also different: while discrete in that work, it is now continuous but parameterized with linear maps. These two arguments are missing. This paper also draws from Wang et al, but that paper is not publishable in its current form. The OT optimization is also run only once in their paper and clearly does not work as proposed.
