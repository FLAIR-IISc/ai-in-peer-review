This paper presents a supervised approach to learning document distances building on the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD, inspired by the Earth Mover’s Distance, computes the optimal transport cost to move one document’s word distribution to another within a pretrained embedding space; the ground distances are initially derived from word2vec. The authors propose to learn (1) a linear mapping of the word embeddings and (2) a per-word importance vector that reweights document histograms, so that documents sharing labels are pulled closer while those with different labels are pushed apart. The manuscript is clearly written and easy to follow, the related work coverage is careful and comprehensive, and the experimental evaluation spans multiple datasets. That said, the motivation for the specific formulation requires further justification, and the overall significance appears modest.

1) Rationale for histogram reweighting w: The motivation for reweighting the word distribution is insufficiently articulated. As presented, this mechanism could substantially alter a document’s effective content representation. A clearer argument (and empirical analysis) is needed to show why such reweighting is appropriate, how it improves discrimination without effectively turning a document into a different one, and under what conditions it preserves meaningful semantics.

2) Significance of the contribution: Introducing a linear transform A and a global reweighting vector w and training them with an NCA-style objective is a reasonable supervised extension of WMD, but it feels incremental rather than conceptually novel. The paper would benefit from a stronger case that this formulation materially advances the state of the art beyond a straightforward combination of known ingredients.

3) Generalization to unseen documents: It is unclear how the method applies at test time. For a new document, what is the procedure for obtaining its reweighted representation—does one use a fixed, globally learned w, or is there any document-specific adaptation? Please clarify the inference pipeline for previously unseen inputs and how distances are computed without retraining.

4) Meaning of “semantic difference”: The abstract mentions capturing “semantic differences,” yet the supervision appears to be based on labels (e.g., authorship or topic). Do the authors equate semantic difference with label difference, or is there a more nuanced notion at play? Clarifying what “semantic” means in this context would help align the terminology with the training signal.

Overall, the paper is well presented, and I would not oppose acceptance if the above concerns are convincingly addressed.
