This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach extending the recent “word-movers distance” [19], a transport distance applied to word2vec word embeddings. The proposed approach can be applied to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances after linear projection. Beyond learning the linear projection to maximize performance, the method learns a weighting of words—specifically, re-weighting word frequencies and re-normalizing them to be task-specific. The paper presents an efficient optimization method for learning this parameterized metric. A thorough experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach, comparing it to a large number of reasonable baselines on several document classification datasets using a kNN classifier based on the learned metric.

This work extends the recent word-movers distance, representing a technically interesting and useful extension demonstrated through effective performance on kNN text classification tasks. However, the potential impact is potentially limited because the earth-movers distance with a metric learning initialization via a "word centroid distance" achieves comparable performance to the full learning of the proposed distance (as seen in the final two rows of Table 3). In other words, the main technical contributions do not appear to provide a substantial advantage over this initialization method based on existing techniques. 

Further, the evaluation lacks a comparison of the “word centroid embedding” (weighted mean of word embeddings for the document) with all tested metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA). Finally, an assessment of the relative importance of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A is missing. It would be valuable to see performance with each component activated in isolation.

The originality primarily lies in the optimization method (Lines 156–175); the idea of linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance is interesting but a limited contribution in itself. The paper is very well-written and a pleasure to read. There are a few minor issues that could be clarified: Line 152: “The authors”: of which paper(s)? [1, 7, 8]? Equation 11: shouldn't α and β carry stars here? Additionally, explicitly recalling the Sinkhorn scaling referred to in the introduction (Line 56) around Line 170 would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound extension of a recent and impactful method (WMD).
*   Strong empirical results consistently outperform a large number of baselines.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Efficient optimization procedure enables practical application.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The performance gain over a simple initialization strategy (S-WCD) is modest.
*   Missing ablation studies to assess the individual contribution of the learned weights and projection matrix.
*   Lack of comparison with existing metric learning techniques applied to the word centroid embedding.
