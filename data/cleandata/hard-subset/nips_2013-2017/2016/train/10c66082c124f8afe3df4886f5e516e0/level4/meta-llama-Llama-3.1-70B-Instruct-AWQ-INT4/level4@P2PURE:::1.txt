This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach that builds upon the "word-movers distance" [19], a transport distance applied to word2vec word embeddings. The proposed approach can be generalised to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances after linear projection. In addition to learning the linear projection to maximise performance, the approach also learns a weighting of words, which is specific to document applications and involves re-weighting the frequency of each word by a learned weight, followed by re-normalising the word frequencies. The authors present an efficient optimisation method to learn the proposed parameterised metric. A thorough experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach by comparing it to a large number of reasonable baselines on several document classification datasets using a kNN classifier based on the proposed learned metric.

The work provides an interesting and useful extension of the recent word-movers distance, which is shown to be effective on several kNN text classification tasks. However, the potential impact of this work may be limited by the fact that the earth-movers distance with metric learning via a "word centroid distance" initialisation performs similarly to the full learning of the proposed distance, as evident from the last two rows in Table 3. This suggests that the main technical contributions of this paper may not significantly improve upon the initialisation method based on existing techniques.

One notable omission in Table 3 is an evaluation of the "word centroid embedding" (weighted mean of word embeddings for the document) with all tested existing metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA). Furthermore, the evaluation lacks an assessment of the relative importance of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A. It would be beneficial to see the performance of each of these components in isolation.

The originality of the paper is primarily related to the optimisation of the metric (Lines 156-175), and the idea of linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance is interesting, although it is a limited contribution in itself. On the other hand, the paper is very well-written and a pleasure to read. However, there are a few minor issues that could be clarified. For instance, it is unclear which paper the authors refer to in Line 152 ("The authors" [1,7,8]). Additionally, in Equation 11, it seems that alpha and beta should carry stars. It would also be helpful to explicitly recall around Line 170 that the method referred to is the Sinkhorn scaling, which was mentioned in the introduction (Line 56).
