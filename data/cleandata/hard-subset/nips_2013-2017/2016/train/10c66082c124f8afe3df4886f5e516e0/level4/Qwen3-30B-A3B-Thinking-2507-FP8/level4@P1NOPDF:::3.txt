

The authors introduce a novel approach for learning the parameters of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which computes Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) between text documents using word embeddings to define bin-to-bin dissimilarities. Specifically, they parameterize the EMD ground metric (cost matrix) via a Mahalanobis distance between word vector representations and additionally propose learning histogram reweighting coefficients, building on Lebanon's work for text classification. The paper derives gradients for NCA-style objective functions and presents compelling experimental validation. Overall, the work represents a well-executed synthesis of established techniques, delivering strong empirical results. I recommend acceptance.  

Major strengths include the method's simplicity and the clarity of its core idea, which—while not trivial to implement and validate—leads to a robust experimental section that significantly strengthens the contribution.  

Key areas for improvement:  
1. **Mathematical rigor**: The interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations requires clearer exposition. Current mathematical notation and derivations are occasionally imprecise (e.g., in the gradient computations). An algorithmic box would greatly enhance readability by explicitly outlining the proposed procedure.  
2. **Consistency in metric definition**: In Equation (1), the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used as the cost, but Equation (6) employs the *squared* Euclidean distance (implicitly defining the 2-Wasserstein metric). To avoid confusion, it would be preferable to state that WMD is the *p*-Wasserstein distance between word embedding clouds, with *p* left as a parameter (e.g., *p*=2).  
3. **Novelty clarification**: The "reweighting" approach in Equation (5) is not new; it was explicitly proposed by Lebanon (2006) for text classification ("Metric Learning for Text Documents") and further developed in Le & Cuturi (2015) using Aitchison geometry. The paper should explicitly acknowledge this prior work and clarify how the current method differs.  
4. **Experimental insights**: The paper should provide analysis on the practical impact of the learned weight vector *w*—e.g., how its values influence performance, whether it consistently improves results, and under what conditions it matters most.  
5. **Related work**: The connection to Cuturi’s entropy-regularized OT framework (Cuturi & Avis, 2013) requires stronger emphasis. While both papers use entropy regularization for scalability, this work differs by (a) parameterizing the ground metric via linear maps (making it continuous vs. discrete in Cuturi & Avis) and (b) incorporating histogram reweighting. Additionally, Wang et al.’s approach (cited) is not fully developed; their OT optimization runs only once and lacks validation, so the comparison should clarify why the current method overcomes these limitations.  

The experimental section is strong and well-justified, but these clarifications would elevate the paper from a solid technical contribution to a definitive advance in the field. The core idea is sound, the results are convincing, and the proposed improvements are feasible. I believe this work merits acceptance.
