The authors present a novel method for learning the parameters of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), effectively an Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) computed on text documents utilizing word embeddings to define bin-to-bin dissimilarities. They parameterize the EMD cost matrix using a Mahalanobis distance between vector representations and further learn histogram coefficients for reweighting, building upon prior work by Lebanon et al. A key contribution is the derivation of gradients for NCA-type costs within this framework, supported by compelling experimental results. Overall, the paper demonstrates a well-considered combination of existing techniques, and the strong experimental validation supports acceptance. The strengths of the work lie in its simplicity and the non-trivial implementation and experimental verification. The experimental section is a particularly strong component.

To improve the paper, the authors should more clearly delineate the interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations. Greater mathematical rigor is also needed, as the current computations and notations are somewhat imprecise; an algorithmic box outlining the proposed method would enhance clarity. Several minor points warrant attention: In Eq. 1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used as a cost, while in Eq. 6, this cost becomes the squared Euclidean metric – effectively the 2-Wasserstein distance – when applied to Mahalanobis metric learning. To avoid this inconsistency, framing the WMD as a p-Wasserstein distance between clouds of word embeddings, for any p, would be more consistent. Furthermore, the reweighting approach in Eq. 5 is not novel, having been previously proposed by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification, and also explored in “Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations” by Le/Cuturi. Additional experimental analysis investigating the importance of the learned weight vector *w* in the metric would be valuable; specifically, assessing the contribution of this innovation and its relevance. 

Finally, the related work section would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the differences between this work and prior approaches. For example, while building upon the work of Cuturi and Avis, this paper utilizes entropy regularization, which demonstrably improves speed and scalability. The cost function also differs: it is continuous and parameterized by linear maps, contrasting with the discrete cost in the prior work. These distinctions should be highlighted. The paper also draws from Wang et al., however, that work remains unpublished and suffered from limitations in its OT optimization procedure, performing only a single optimization run that proved ineffective.
