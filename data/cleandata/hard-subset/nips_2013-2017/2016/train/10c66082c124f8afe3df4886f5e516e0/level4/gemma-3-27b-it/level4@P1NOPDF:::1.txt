This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach built upon the recent “word-movers distance” [19] – a transport distance applied to word2vec word embeddings. The proposed approach is applicable to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances following a linear projection. Beyond learning this projection to maximize performance, the method also learns a weighting for each word. This weighting is specific to document applications and involves re-weighting word frequencies by learned values, followed by re-normalization. The paper presents an efficient optimization method for learning the proposed parameterized metric. A thorough experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach by comparing it to a large number of reasonable baselines on several document classification datasets, using a kNN classifier based on the learned metric. 

+ This work extends the recent word-movers distance, which is both technically interesting and provides a useful extension – demonstrated through its effectiveness on several kNN text classification tasks. - However, the potential impact may be limited by the observation that the earth-movers distance with metric learning initialized via a “word centroid distance” performs comparably to the full learning of the proposed distance, as seen in the last two rows of Table 3. In other words, the main technical contributions of this paper do not appear to offer substantial improvement over this initialization method based on existing techniques. - I also noticed a missing evaluation in Table 3: an assessment of the “word centroid embedding” (the weighted mean of word embeddings for a document) when used with all the tested existing metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA). - Furthermore, the evaluation lacks an assessment of the relative importance of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A. It would be beneficial to see performance results with each of these components active in isolation. o The originality primarily lies in the optimization method (Lines 156–175); the idea of linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance is interesting but constitutes a somewhat limited contribution in itself. + The paper is very well written and a pleasure to read.
