This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns both a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a vector of word-importance weights. The learning objective is a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss in the spirit of NCA, encouraging documents with the same label to be close under the induced optimal transport distance. Because backpropagating through exact WMD would be prohibitively slow and non-smooth, the authors adopt an entropic regularization of the transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) to obtain efficient and differentiable approximations of both transport plans and dual variables, enabling practical gradient updates. They further accelerate training with WCD-based neighbor filtering and minibatching, and initialize the metric via NCA on word centroids (S-WCD). Across eight datasets and 26 baselines (including TF-IDF/LSI/LDA/mSDA with and without metric learning), S-WMD achieves the best average kNN performance and often the lowest error per dataset. Visualizations and learned word-weight analyses illustrate interpretability gains. Code is released.

Relation to prior work: The method builds directly on WMD (Kusner et al.), entropic OT/Sinkhorn (Cuturi), and supervised metric learning (NCA/LMNN/ITML). The key novelty is to learn the ground metric and word weights specifically for WMD using an NCA-style objective while using Sinkhorn to make training scalable. Related work on learning ground distances for EMD in vision is acknowledged; the addition of histogram reweighting and the text domain make this a useful extension.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized exposition with careful mathematical derivations and practical training details.
- Sound optimization strategy: entropic OT yields unique solutions and efficient approximate gradients; neighbor filtering and minibatching make the approach scalable.
- Strong empirical study: eight diverse datasets, 26 competitive baselines, consistent average gains, and sensible ablations/initialization (S-WCD).
- Interpretability: word-importance weights and class-cluster visualizations support qualitative understanding.
- Practical significance: easy to adopt on top of off-the-shelf word embeddings; code availability enhances impact.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate: the approach combines known ingredients (WMD + Sinkhorn + NCA-style objective), though the integration is thoughtful and practically valuable.
- The role of the entropic regularization parameter λ is under-discussed. In practice λ strongly influences the behavior: small λ yields smoother, more “entropic OT” distances, while large λ approaches true WMD but slows convergence. The paper mostly fixes λ=10 without a sensitivity analysis.
- The discussion around Word Centroid Distance (WCD) can be confusing. While WCD is a useful lower bound and for initialization/neighbor filtering, the narrative occasionally blurs its role as an approximation versus a final metric.
- Some datasets favor TF-IDF+NCA (especially with long documents), suggesting S-WMD may be less competitive when IDF weighting dominates or when squared Euclidean ground costs are suboptimal. A deeper analysis of when S-WMD underperforms would help.
- Limited ablation on the contributions of A versus w (e.g., learning only A, only w, or different ground metrics p=1 vs p=2).

Quality
- Generally strong and careful. The approximation and gradients are well motivated; empirical results are thorough. Missing: sensitivity to λ, and a clearer characterization of how closely S-WMD tracks true WMD as λ increases versus runtime trade-offs.

Clarity
- Very well written overall. Clarify the WCD role (initialization vs evaluation) and expand the explanation of λ’s effect on both optimization and the induced metric.

Originality
- Incremental but nontrivial: applying supervised metric learning to the WMD with joint ground-metric and histogram reweighting, trained efficiently via Sinkhorn, is a meaningful extension.

Significance
- High practical relevance with competitive state-of-the-art performance and interpretability. Likely to be adopted for text classification, retrieval, and related tasks.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please provide a sensitivity analysis for λ (e.g., validation curves for performance, runtime, and transport sparsity) and discuss whether, empirically, the learned distance behaves closer to entropic OT or true WMD in your setting.
- Do you tune λ per dataset? If not, what guided the choice of λ=10?
- Report ablations isolating the effects of A vs w (and their combination), and potentially p=1 versus p=2 ground costs.
- Clarify the use of WCD: where it is used solely for initialization/neighbor filtering versus as a baseline metric, to avoid confusion.
- Comment on when TF-IDF+NCA outperforms S-WMD (e.g., document length, vocabulary density) and whether incorporating IDF-like priors into w helps.
- Discuss fairness of hyperparameter tuning across baselines and whether any are transductive (e.g., LDA).

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong empirical performance across many baselines and datasets.
- Clear, solid method with good theoretical grounding and practical efficiency.
- Interpretable outputs and released code increase usability and impact.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to combining established components.
- Insufficient analysis of λ, which is central to the behavior and efficiency of the method.
- Some ambiguity around the WCD discussion and conditions where S-WMD underperforms.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper is well executed, empirically convincing, and practically important; addressing the λ sensitivity and clarifying the WCD role would further strengthen it.
