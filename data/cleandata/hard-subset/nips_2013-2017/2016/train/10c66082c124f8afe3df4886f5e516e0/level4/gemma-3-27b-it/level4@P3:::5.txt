This paper addresses the problem of supervised text classification by extending the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), a recently proposed metric based on optimal transport. The authors aim to incorporate supervision into WMD by learning both a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word-specific importance weights. A key contribution is the use of the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient approximation to the Wasserstein distance underlying WMD, enabling practical gradient-based optimization. The method, termed Supervised-WMD (S-WMD), is evaluated on eight datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to 26 competitive baselines.

The paper builds upon Kusner et al.’s [19] impactful introduction of WMD, addressing its unsupervised nature. This is a natural and important extension, given the success of WMD in kNN-based classification. The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck in incorporating supervision—the cubic scaling of WMD—and propose a clever solution leveraging the Sinkhorn distance [6] to achieve efficient optimization. This work also connects to a broader body of research on metric learning [10, 15, 36, 37], but distinguishes itself by operating directly on word embeddings rather than learning document representations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is well-motivated and technically sound. The use of the Sinkhorn distance is a practical and effective approximation for handling the computational complexity of the optimal transport problem. The gradient derivation appears correct, and the optimization strategy (batch gradient descent with initialization) is sensible.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The extensive experimental evaluation across eight datasets, compared to a large number of baselines, convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of S-WMD. Consistently outperforming almost all baselines is a significant achievement.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts. The mathematical derivations are presented in a digestible manner, and the motivation behind each step is clearly articulated. The connection to related work is also well-explained.
*   **Novelty:** While building on WMD, the introduction of a learned linear transformation and word importance weights, combined with the efficient optimization strategy, represents a novel contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Incremental Novelty:** The core idea of applying metric learning to the WMD framework is relatively straightforward and, while effective, might be viewed as an incremental contribution rather than a groundbreaking advance. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the novelty relative to existing metric learning techniques applied to text data.
*   **Discussion of Lambda:** The role of the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn distance approximation deserves more detailed discussion. The authors acknowledge its potential impact, but a more thorough exploration of its influence on the learned metric (specifically, whether it favors a behavior closer to pure Wasserstein distance or a more entropic Sinkhorn distance) would strengthen the analysis. Investigating the sensitivity of the results to λ and providing guidance for its selection would be valuable.
*   **Relevance of WCD Comparison:** The comparison to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) feels somewhat tangential. While WCD serves as a baseline, the paper focuses on improving *WMD*, and the connection to WCD isn’t thoroughly explained or leveraged. Removing or clarifying this comparison would improve the focus.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and effective method for supervised text classification.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a clear improvement over numerous baselines.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand, making it accessible to a broad audience.
*   The use of the Sinkhorn distance is a clever and practical solution to a significant computational challenge.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The incremental nature of the novelty may limit its overall impact.
*   The lack of in-depth analysis of the regularization parameter λ is a minor weakness.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of text classification. The technical soundness, strong empirical results, and exceptional clarity of presentation make it a worthy candidate for acceptance to NIPS. While the novelty is incremental, the practical benefits and well-executed implementation outweigh this concern. I recommend **acceptance**.
