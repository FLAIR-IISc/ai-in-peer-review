The authors propose a novel method for learning the parameters of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which is essentially the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) computed on text documents using word embeddings to calculate bin-to-bin dissimilarities. By parameterizing the EMD cost matrix using a Mahalanobis distance between vector representations, the authors provide a new approach to this problem. Additionally, they propose learning histogram coefficients reweighting, a concept that has been explored in previous work, such as Lebanon's research (see comment below). The authors derive the gradients of Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA) type costs for this problem and provide compelling experimental evidence to support their method. Overall, the paper presents a nice combination of existing techniques and delivers convincing experimental results, making a strong case for acceptance.

The strengths of the paper lie in its simplicity and the relatively straightforward idea, which, although not trivial to implement and test, has been effectively executed. The experimental section is a notable aspect of this paper, and the authors' efforts to provide thorough evaluations of their method are commendable. However, there are a few areas that require improvement. Firstly, the authors should better handle the interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations. Secondly, the mathematical computations and notations could be more rigorous, as they appear somewhat sloppy in their current form. Finally, including an algorithmic box would provide a clearer understanding of the authors' proposed method.

A few minor comments are worth noting. In Equation 1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used as a cost, whereas in Equation 6, the squared Euclidean metric is employed for Mahalanobis metric learning, which is typically referred to as the 2-Wasserstein distance. To avoid inconsistencies, it would be more accurate to state that WMD is a p-Wasserstein distance between clouds of word embeddings, regardless of the value of p.

The "reweighting" approach proposed in Equation 5 is not a new concept, as it was previously introduced by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification ("metric learning for text documents"). A similar idea can also be found in "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations" by Le and Cuturi.

It would be beneficial to gain insight into the importance of the weight vector w in the learned metric. Specifically, does this innovation have a significant impact, and is it relevant in any way? Furthermore, the authors could provide a clearer distinction between their work and previous research. For instance, this paper builds upon the work of Cuturi and Avis but utilizes entropy regularization, which significantly improves scalability and speed. The cost function is also different, being continuous and parameterized with linear maps, whereas the cost in the previous work was discrete. These differences are worth highlighting.

Additionally, the authors draw inspiration from Wang et al., but that paper is not publishable in its current form. The OT optimization is only run once in their paper, and it is evident that their proposed method does not work as intended.

Overall, the authors have presented a solid paper with a well-executed idea and convincing experimental results. With some revisions to address the mentioned areas, the paper has the potential to become even stronger and more impactful.
