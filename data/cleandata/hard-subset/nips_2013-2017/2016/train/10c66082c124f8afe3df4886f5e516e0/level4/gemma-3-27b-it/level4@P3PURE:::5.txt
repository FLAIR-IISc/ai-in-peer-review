The authors analyze the problem of text classification and extend previous work leveraging Word Mover’s Distance (Wasserstein distance) by learning word-specific weights in a supervised manner. This contribution proposes using the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient proxy for the Wasserstein distance and demonstrates strong performance on eight datasets, achieving state-of-the-art average results against 26 baselines. This is a very well-written paper that clearly presents the topic and thoroughly analyzes the underlying mathematics. 

The primary weaknesses lie in the incremental novelty compared to the original Word Mover’s Distance paper, and the inclusion of references comparing against the World Centroid Distance feels irrelevant and somewhat confusing. Furthermore, the discussion of lambda (the regularization parameter) feels incomplete. In my experience, the value of lambda has a significant impact on performance, often becoming a dominant factor in equation (2). While theoretically, a sufficiently large lambda should yield results similar to those obtained with the pure Wasserstein distance (albeit with slower convergence), practitioners typically employ small lambda values to accelerate convergence, resulting in behavior distinct from the Wasserstein distance. Therefore, I am curious whether, in this work, lambda encourages behavior consistent with the pure Wasserstein distance or with the entropic relaxation inherent to the Sinkhorn distance.
