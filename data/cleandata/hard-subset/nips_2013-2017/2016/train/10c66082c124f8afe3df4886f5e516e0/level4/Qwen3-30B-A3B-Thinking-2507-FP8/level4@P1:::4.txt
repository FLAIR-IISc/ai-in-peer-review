

This paper introduces a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) framework, building upon the unsupervised document distance metric proposed by Kusner et al. (2015). The authors propose a method called Supervised WMD (S-WMD) that learns both a linear transformation of the underlying word embeddings and a word importance weight vector to improve document classification performance. The approach directly minimizes a stochastic approximation of the leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric, using an efficient Sinkhorn distance approximation to avoid the cubic computational complexity of the original WMD. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over 26 competitive baselines across eight text classification tasks.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough literature review that properly situates the work within the broader context of document representation, metric learning, and optimal transport. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of related work, particularly the connections to Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) and the recent advances in efficient Wasserstein distance computation. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering diverse text classification tasks and providing detailed comparisons against multiple strong baselines.

The primary strengths of this work include the elegant integration of supervised learning with the WMD framework, the practical computational improvements achieved through the Sinkhorn approximation, and the thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets. The authors successfully demonstrate that their supervised approach significantly outperforms both unsupervised baselines and other metric learning methods in most classification tasks.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that warrant careful consideration. First, the motivation for the word importance weight vector (w) is insufficiently justified. The current formulation reweights the bag-of-words representation of documents (d̃a = (w ◦ da)/(w>da)), which fundamentally alters the document representation rather than simply weighting existing words. The authors do not explain why this reweighting preserves the semantic meaning of the document while improving classification performance. Without this clarification, it's difficult to assess whether the method is truly learning a better document distance or merely creating a different document representation. A more detailed explanation of how the weights relate to document semantics and why they improve classification would be essential.

Second, the significance of the contribution appears limited. While the authors successfully apply standard metric learning techniques (specifically NCA) to the WMD framework, the resulting method is essentially a straightforward extension of existing approaches rather than a novel theoretical advancement. The paper does not clearly articulate how S-WMD differs from previous supervised metric learning methods applied to text, nor does it demonstrate a fundamental theoretical contribution to the field of optimal transport or metric learning.

Third, the paper fails to address how the method would handle new, unseen documents during inference. The word importance weights (w) are learned from the training data and depend on the specific document distribution in the training set. For a completely new document, the authors do not explain how the appropriate weights would be determined. Without this crucial detail, the practical applicability of the method is questionable.

Finally, the abstract's reference to "semantic difference" is ambiguous. In the context of document classification, this appears to refer to the label-based differences between documents, but "semantic difference" typically refers to meaning-based distinctions rather than label-based categorization. The authors should clarify whether they mean semantic similarity as captured by the document labels or actual semantic meaning.

While the paper presents a technically sound approach with strong empirical results, the contribution does not represent a significant advance over the state of the art. The method is a reasonable extension of existing techniques, but it lacks the novelty or theoretical depth expected for NIPS. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the motivation for the reweighting mechanism, clarify the practical implementation for new documents, and better articulate the theoretical significance of their approach to justify acceptance at NIPS.

The paper is well-written and the experiments are thorough, but the incremental nature of the contribution and the unanswered questions about the word importance weights and practical implementation suggest it does not meet the high standards for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend rejection, with the understanding that the authors could potentially revise the paper to address the concerns about the motivation for the reweighting mechanism, the significance of the contribution, and the practical implementation details.
