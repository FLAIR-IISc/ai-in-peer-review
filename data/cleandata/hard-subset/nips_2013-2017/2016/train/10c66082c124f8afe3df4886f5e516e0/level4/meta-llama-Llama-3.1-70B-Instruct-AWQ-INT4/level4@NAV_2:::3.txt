The authors propose a novel method to learn the parameters of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), essentially the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) computed on text documents using word embeddings to calculate bin-to-bin dissimilarities. The authors parameterize the EMD cost matrix using a Mahalanobis distance between vector representations. Additionally, they propose learning histogram coefficients reweighting, which is reminiscent of Lebanon's work. The authors provide the gradients of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) type costs for this problem and present compelling experimental evidence.

The paper's strengths lie in its simplicity and relatively straightforward idea, although the implementation and testing are not trivial. The experimental section is a strong part of this paper, showcasing the method's effectiveness. However, there are a few areas that require improvement. Firstly, the authors should better handle the interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations. Secondly, the mathematical computations and notations could be more rigorous. Ideally, the authors should provide an algorithmic box to clearly illustrate their proposed method.

A few minor comments are also in order. In Equation 1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used as a cost, whereas in Equation 6, the cost becomes the squared Euclidean metric for Mahalanobis metric learning. To avoid inconsistencies, it might be easier to simply state that WMD is p-Wasserstein between clouds of word embeddings, regardless of the value of p.

The "reweighting" approach proposed in Equation 5 is not new, as it was already proposed by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification ("metric learning for text documents"). See also "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations" by Le/Cuturi.

It would be interesting to gain experimental insights into the importance of the weight vector w in the learned metric. Does this innovation matter, and is it relevant in any way?

In the related work section, the authors should more clearly underline the differences with previous work. For instance, this paper builds on the work of Cuturi/Avis but uses entropy regularization, which clearly speeds up and scales up applications. The cost is also different: while discrete in that work, it's now continuous but parameterized with linear maps. These two arguments are missing. This paper also draws from Wang et al, but that paper is not publishable as it is. The OT optimization is also run once only in their paper, and clearly does not work as it was proposed.

The paper's quality is high, and the authors provide a nice combination of existing tricks, along with convincing experimental results. I would support acceptance.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper proposes a novel method to learn the parameters of the WMD.
- The authors provide compelling experimental evidence showcasing the method's effectiveness.
- The paper's simplicity and relatively straightforward idea make it accessible to a wide range of readers.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The paper could benefit from a more rigorous mathematical treatment.
- The authors should provide more insight into the importance of the weight vector w in the learned metric.
- The related work section could be improved to more clearly highlight the differences with previous work.

Overall, the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and I believe it is a valuable contribution to the field.
