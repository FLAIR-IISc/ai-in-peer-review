

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension to the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) framework. WMD, introduced by Kusner et al. (2015), computes document distances by solving an optimal transport problem between word embeddings, achieving state-of-the-art results in document classification. The authors recognize that WMD is entirely unsupervised and lacks a mechanism to incorporate task-specific supervision, which can be crucial for document classification tasks where semantic similarity may depend on the specific classification problem. S-WMD addresses this limitation by learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space and a word importance weight vector to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out classification error.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it proposes a practical approximation to the gradient of WMD, which would otherwise require prohibitively expensive nested optimization. This approximation leverages the Sinkhorn distance (Cuturi, 2013) to achieve O(q²) complexity instead of O(q³ log q). Second, it introduces a novel metric learning approach that simultaneously learns both a linear transformation of word embeddings and word-specific importance weights, allowing the distance metric to adapt to the specific classification task. Third, the authors provide a comprehensive empirical evaluation across eight diverse text classification datasets, comparing S-WMD against 26 baseline methods, including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) and metric learning approaches (NCA, ITML, LMNN).

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear explanations of the mathematical formulation and efficient approximation techniques. The authors properly contextualize their work within the broader landscape of metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and document representation methods (LSI, LDA, mSDA). The experimental design is thorough, with careful attention to hyperparameter tuning (using Bayesian optimization) and appropriate statistical reporting (averaging over five train/test splits for datasets without predefined splits). The results consistently show S-WMD outperforming all baselines across the majority of datasets, with statistically significant improvements in several cases.

The paper excels in clarity. The writing is precise and well-organized, with appropriate use of figures (t-SNE visualizations) and tables to convey results. The pseudo-code for S-WMD (Algorithm 1) clearly presents the optimization procedure, and the supplementary materials provide additional detail for reproducibility. The authors effectively explain the intuition behind their approach, particularly the importance of learning word-specific weights to capture task-relevant semantics.

The originality of the work is significant. While metric learning has been extensively studied, S-WMD represents a novel application of these techniques to the specific context of document distances based on word embeddings. The paper clearly articulates how it differs from prior work, particularly noting that most metric learning methods operate on document representations, whereas S-WMD operates directly on the word embedding space. This distinction is crucial for maintaining efficiency with high-dimensional text data.

The significance of this work is substantial. Document classification is a fundamental task with wide applications in information retrieval, search, and recommendation systems. S-WMD consistently outperforms all baselines across eight diverse datasets, demonstrating practical value. The method's efficiency (training times comparable to or better than baseline metric learning approaches) makes it particularly appealing for real-world applications. The paper's claim that S-WMD "may well be the best performing distance metric for documents to date" is well-supported by the empirical results.

The paper has minor weaknesses. The discussion of limitations could be strengthened, particularly regarding the sensitivity of the method to the choice of λ in the Sinkhorn approximation and its performance on extremely large-scale datasets. The initialization procedure (S-WCD) is well-motivated but could benefit from more detailed analysis of its impact on final performance. Additionally, while the paper mentions the use of word2vec embeddings, it would be valuable to discuss how the method might perform with other embedding techniques.

In terms of the four review criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical foundation and comprehensive empirical validation.
- Clarity: Well-written, well-organized, and effectively communicates complex concepts.
- Originality: Makes a clear contribution by extending WMD to a supervised setting with novel learning techniques.
- Significance: Demonstrates substantial improvements over prior work across multiple datasets, with clear practical value.

Arguments for acceptance: This is a strong contribution to the field of document representation and metric learning, with well-motivated technical approach, comprehensive empirical evaluation, and clear practical significance. The paper addresses a meaningful limitation in the WMD framework and provides a practical solution that consistently outperforms existing methods.

Arguments against acceptance: The paper could provide more detailed analysis of limitations, particularly regarding scalability to extremely large datasets and sensitivity to hyperparameters.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The method is technically sound, well-explained, and empirically validated across a diverse set of tasks. I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS.
