### Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space (e.g., word2vec). While WMD has shown impressive performance, it is entirely unsupervised.

The key contribution of this work is to incorporate supervision into the WMD framework. The authors propose learning two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation (`A`) of the word embedding space to warp the geometry for the specific task, and (2) a word-importance vector (`w`) that re-weights word frequencies to emphasize task-relevant words. To learn `A` and `w`, the authors formulate an objective function based on Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), which aims to minimize the stochastic k-Nearest Neighbors leave-one-out classification error.

A significant technical challenge is that computing the gradient of the WMD distance is computationally prohibitive, involving a nested optimization problem. The authors present a critical and clever solution by approximating the WMD with its entropy-regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance). This makes the objective function strongly convex and allows for an efficient and practical gradient-based learning algorithm. The authors validate their method with an extensive experimental evaluation on eight text classification datasets, demonstrating that S-WMD consistently outperforms a large suite of 26 competitive baseline methods.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of the original WMD: its unsupervised nature. By successfully incorporating supervision, the authors create a powerful and flexible document distance metric that can be adapted to specific tasks, such as topic or sentiment classification. The strong empirical results suggest that S-WMD could become a new state-of-the-art method for kNN-based text classification.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The technical contribution of this work is solid. While the high-level idea of combining metric learning with an existing distance may seem straightforward, making it work for the complex WMD metric is non-trivial. The main technical novelty lies in the efficient gradient computation. By leveraging the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance to create a differentiable and fast-to-compute approximation of the WMD, the authors make the entire learning framework computationally feasible. This is an elegant solution to a difficult problem.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare S-WMD against an extensive list of 26 baselines—including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with several metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN), as well as the unsupervised WMD and WCD. The consistent superior performance of S-WMD across eight diverse datasets strongly supports the paper's claims.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The background section provides all the necessary context on word embeddings, WMD, and NCA. The proposed method is explained logically, and the visualizations effectively illustrate the benefits of the learned metric.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Motivation for Word Re-weighting (`w`):** The motivation for learning the word-importance vector `w` could be further elaborated. While intuitively appealing, re-weighting word counts fundamentally alters the document's bag-of-words representation. This could potentially distort a document's meaning if not properly regularized or constrained. A more in-depth discussion on the role of `w` would be beneficial. Furthermore, an ablation study that isolates the performance gains from the linear transformation `A` versus the re-weighting `w` would provide valuable insight into the contributions of each component.

2.  **Generalization to New Documents:** To improve clarity for all readers, it would be helpful if the authors explicitly stated how the learned importance vector `w` is applied to unseen test documents. It is understood from the formulation that `w` is a global model parameter learned from the training data and then fixed, but a direct statement would prevent any potential ambiguity about the model's generalization process.

3.  **Choice of Terminology:** The paper uses the phrase "semantic difference" to describe the task-specific notion of similarity encoded by the labels (e.g., topic or authorship). While not incorrect, this phrasing could be interpreted more broadly. Using a more precise term, such as "task-specific similarity" or "label-based similarity," might more accurately reflect what the model is learning. This is a minor point but could improve the precision of the manuscript.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound method for supervised document metric learning. The core contribution—an efficient way to learn a supervised version of the powerful WMD metric—is significant and backed by very strong experimental results. The paper is clearly written and provides a valuable contribution to the field. The identified weaknesses are minor and can be addressed with small revisions. I strongly recommend accepting this paper.
