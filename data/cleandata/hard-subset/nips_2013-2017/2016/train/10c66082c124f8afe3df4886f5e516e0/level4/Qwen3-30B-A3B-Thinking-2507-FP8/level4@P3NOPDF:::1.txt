

This paper extends the Word Movers Distance (WMD) [19] by introducing a supervised metric learning framework that jointly optimizes a linear projection and word-specific weights for document classification. The approach generalizes WMD to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances after linear projection, with the key innovation being the learning of word weights (reweighting term frequencies) and the projection matrix. The authors propose an efficient optimization method and demonstrate strong empirical results on multiple text classification datasets using kNN, showing consistent improvement over baseline metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA) and unsupervised WMD variants.

The technical contribution is well-motivated and the paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, making it a pleasure to review. However, several concerns regarding the novelty and evaluation warrant attention:

1.  **Limited Impact of Core Contribution:** The most significant concern is the marginal empirical gain of the full proposed method over a simple baseline: WMD initialized with the "word centroid embedding" (i.e., using the weighted mean of word embeddings as the document representation, with weights set to 1). As shown in Table 3, the performance of the full method (WMD+metric) is nearly identical to the centroid-based initialization (WMD+centroid) on all datasets. This suggests that the primary technical contribution—learning the linear projection and word weights—does not yield substantial gains over a straightforward application of existing WMD concepts. The paper’s main claim of a "useful extension" is weakened by this observation; the added complexity of the joint learning framework may not be justified if the centroid initialization already achieves near-optimal results.

2.  **Missing Critical Comparisons:** The evaluation omits two essential comparisons:
    *   The "word centroid embedding" (document as weighted mean of word embeddings) combined with standard metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA). This would directly test whether metric learning *on the centroid representation* is beneficial, which is a more relevant baseline for the proposed method than using WMD *without* metric learning. Table 3 currently lacks this comparison.
    *   An ablation study isolating the contribution of the word-specific weights versus the linear projection matrix (A). The paper should report performance when:
        *   Only word weights are learned (A fixed to identity).
        *   Only the projection matrix A is learned (word weights fixed to 1).
        This would clarify the relative importance of each component and whether the joint learning is truly necessary.

3.  **Originality Assessment:** While the optimization technique (Lines 156–175) is well-executed, the core idea of applying metric learning within the WMD framework is conceptually straightforward and builds directly on existing techniques. The novelty is primarily in the efficient optimization and the specific formulation of the word weighting, rather than a fundamentally new metric learning paradigm. The paper should more clearly position this as a *practical refinement* of WMD rather than a significant conceptual advance.

**Minor Issues:**
*   Line 152: "The authors" should specify the referenced papers (e.g., "as in [1,7,8]").
*   Equation 11: The starred variables (α*, β*) are likely intended, as they denote optimal dual variables.
*   Line 170: Explicitly reference "Sinkhorn scaling" (Line 56) to clarify the optimization technique.

**Overall Assessment:**
This work presents a technically sound and well-executed extension of WMD for document classification. The writing is excellent, and the empirical results demonstrate the method’s effectiveness *within the context of the evaluated baselines*. However, the limited empirical gap between the full method and the simpler centroid initialization baseline significantly undermines the perceived novelty and impact. The missing comparisons (centroid + metric learning, ablation study) are critical for a fair assessment of the contribution. While the method is practical and effective, the paper would benefit from a more measured discussion of its incremental nature and stronger justification for the added complexity over the centroid initialization. The minor technical points should be addressed.

**Recommendation:** **Weak Accept** (or **Borderline**). The work is solid and useful for practitioners, but the modest gains over a simple baseline and the lack of critical ablation studies prevent it from being a strong contribution to the field. Addressing the missing comparisons would significantly strengthen the paper.
