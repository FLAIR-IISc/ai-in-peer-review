

This paper presents a well-motivated extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that leverages supervised learning to optimize distance weights for domain-specific tasks. The authors address a meaningful gap in the literature by moving beyond the unsupervised WMD framework to learn discriminative metrics, which is both conceptually sound and technically well-executed.  

The method is clearly articulated, with sufficient detail to enable reproducibility and understanding. Crucially, the experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough: the authors test on multiple classification datasets, employ a diverse set of baseline methods and feature representations, and rigorously compare against state-of-the-art approaches. The results consistently demonstrate that the proposed supervised WMD significantly outperforms all baselines—often by substantial margins—across all evaluated tasks. This empirical strength, combined with the methodological clarity, makes the contribution compelling.  

The paper effectively justifies its approach through both theoretical grounding and extensive validation, avoiding overclaiming while clearly establishing the advantages of discriminative metric learning for text classification. Given the relevance of this work to NIPS’ focus on machine learning fundamentals and applications, I believe it represents a strong contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance and presentation at NIPS.
