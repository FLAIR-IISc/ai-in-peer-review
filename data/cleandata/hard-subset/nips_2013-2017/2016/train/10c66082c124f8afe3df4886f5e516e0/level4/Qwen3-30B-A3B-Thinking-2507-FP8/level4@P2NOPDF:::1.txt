

This paper introduces a supervised metric learning framework extending the Word Movers' Distance (WMD) [19], leveraging word2vec embeddings. The approach learns a linear projection matrix and word-specific weights to optimize the transport distance, enabling application to any squared Euclidean-based transport metric after projection. The word weighting component re-scales document word frequencies via learned weights followed by re-normalization, tailored to document classification. The authors propose an efficient optimization method for the parameterized metric and demonstrate strong empirical results across multiple document classification datasets using kNN with the learned metric.  

**Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and technically clear. The core idea—integrating linear metric learning within WMD—is conceptually sound and well-motivated. The optimization procedure (Lines 156–175) is a notable contribution, offering a practical solution for learning the projection and weights. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against numerous baselines (including ITML, LMNN, NCA, and variants of WMD) across standard text classification tasks.  

**Key Concerns:**  
The primary technical contribution appears limited by the results in Table 3. Specifically, the "WMD + centroid initialization" baseline (last row) achieves performance nearly identical to the full proposed method ("WMD + projection + weights"). This suggests that the learned projection matrix and word weights provide minimal marginal gain over a simple initialization based on the standard word centroid embedding (weighted mean of word vectors). Consequently, the core innovation—metric learning within WMD—does not demonstrably outperform a straightforward initialization using existing techniques. This significantly weakens the paper's impact, as the proposed method’s advantage over established baselines is marginal.  

Two critical experiments are missing:  
1. **Baseline comparison for centroid embedding:** Table 3 omits performance of the standard word centroid embedding (without metric learning) when combined with existing metric learners (ITML, LMNN, NCA). Including this would clarify whether the centroid initialization alone (without the proposed learning) is competitive or if metric learning is truly necessary.  
2. **Component ablation study:** The relative importance of the word weights versus the projection matrix is unassessed. Ablation experiments isolating each component (e.g., using weights alone, projection alone, or both) are essential to determine which element drives performance gains. Without this, it remains unclear whether the word weighting (a novel aspect) or the projection (a standard metric learning technique) is the key contributor.  

**Minor Issues:**  
- Line 152: "The authors" is ambiguous—specify which paper(s) (e.g., [1,7,8]).  
- Equation 11: The variables α and β should carry stars (e.g., α*, β*) to denote optimized values.  
- Line 170: Explicitly connect the Sinkhorn scaling referenced here to the introduction (Line 56) for consistency.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
While the paper presents a technically coherent extension of WMD and is well-executed, the empirical findings undermine its significance. The marginal improvement over centroid initialization (Table 3) suggests the core contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The missing ablation studies and centroid-baseline comparisons prevent a clear assessment of the method’s novelty and necessity. If the authors can demonstrate substantial gains beyond the centroid initialization (e.g., through additional experiments or stronger baselines), the paper would be more compelling. As-is, the work extends WMD but does not convincingly justify its complexity over simpler, equally effective alternatives. The writing is excellent, but the technical contribution is limited by the evidence provided.  

**Recommendation:** Weak accept (or borderline reject). The method is sound, but the lack of clear superiority over a simple baseline and missing ablation studies weaken the case for publication. Addressing the missing experiments would be essential for acceptance.
