This paper addresses the problem of text classification by extending the Word Mover's Distance (WMD). The authors build upon this framework, which is based on the Wasserstein distance, by introducing a supervised learning component that learns word-specific weights. The core technical contribution is the use of the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient approximation of the Wasserstein distance. The method is evaluated on eight datasets against 26 baselines, demonstrating the best average performance.

The paper is exceptionally well-written. It provides a clear introduction to the topic and presents a mathematical analysis that is both deep and accessible.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses. First, the novelty over the original Word Mover's Distance paper is somewhat limited. Second, the comparisons to the Word Centroid Distance are confusing and their relevance is not well-established.

More importantly, the role and impact of the regularization parameter, lambda, are insufficiently discussed. The value of lambda can have a significant impact on performance, at times becoming the dominant term in Equation (2). Theoretically, for a sufficiently large lambda, the Sinkhorn distance should approximate the true Wasserstein distance, albeit with slower convergence. In practice, however, a small lambda is often chosen to accelerate convergence, which can result in behavior that is qualitatively different from the Wasserstein distance. This raises a crucial question: does the choice of lambda in this work lead to behavior that closely approximates the true Wasserstein distance, or does it reflect the distinct properties of the entropically regularized Sinkhorn distance? The paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of this trade-off.
