

The authors introduce a novel method for learning parameters of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), reformulating the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) computed on text documents using word embeddings to measure bin-to-bin dissimilarities. The approach parameterizes the EMD cost matrix (ground metric) through a Mahalanobis distance between word vector representations while also learning histogram reweighting coefficients to emphasize important words for classification. The paper provides gradients for NCA-type loss functions and presents compelling experimental validation across eight text classification tasks. Overall, this work represents a well-executed synthesis of existing techniques with strong empirical results, supporting its acceptance.

The paper's strengths include its conceptual simplicity, the straightforward yet non-trivial nature of the proposed approach, and particularly its thorough experimental evaluation, which serves as a strong foundation for the claims made. The authors demonstrate consistent performance improvements over 26 competitive baselines across multiple datasets.

Areas for improvement include: (1) a more careful treatment of the interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations; (2) greater mathematical rigor in the derivations and notation, which currently appears somewhat imprecise; and (3) inclusion of a clear algorithmic pseudocode box to elucidate the proposed method. Specific technical points to address: - Equation (1) uses Euclidean distance between word embeddings as the cost, while Equation (6) employs the squared Euclidean metric (effectively 2-Wasserstein). To avoid confusion, it would be preferable to consistently refer to WMD as p-Wasserstein between word embedding clouds, with p as a hyperparameter. - The histogram reweighting approach in Equation (5) is not novel; it was previously proposed by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification ("Metric Learning for Text Documents") and further developed in "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations" by Le and Cuturi. - The authors should provide experimental insights on the contribution of the weight vector w to the final metric performance - does this innovation significantly impact results, and if so, why? - The related work section would benefit from clearer differentiation from prior literature. Specifically, while the paper builds on Cuturi and Avis' work, it introduces entropy regularization (significantly improving scalability) and parameterizes the cost as a continuous linear map rather than discrete, aspects that are currently not emphasized. The connection to Wang et al.'s work should be clarified, noting that their approach is not publishable in its current form and differs substantially in that their OT optimization is run once rather than iteratively.
