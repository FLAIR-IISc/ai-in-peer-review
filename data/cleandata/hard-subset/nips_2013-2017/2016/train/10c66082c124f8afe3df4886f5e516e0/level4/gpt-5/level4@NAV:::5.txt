The authors address the task of categorizing text documents. They build on prior work that introduced Word Mover’s Distance (Wasserstein) by converting it into a supervised method through learning word-specific weights. Their contribution proposes using the Sinkhorn distance as a surrogate for the Wasserstein distance to gain computational speed, and they benchmark on 8 datasets against 26 baselines, reporting the best average performance. This paper is very well written, clearly introduces the topic, and provides a deep yet lucid mathematical analysis. The main shortcomings are, perhaps, the modest novelty relative to the original Word Mover’s Distance paper, and I find the references comparing to the Word Centroid Distance not particularly relevant and somewhat confusing. I also feel that lambda (the regularization parameter) is insufficiently discussed. In my experience, the choice of lambda has a substantial impact, sometimes dominating eq(2). In theory, results should approach pure Wasserstein for sufficiently large lambda at the cost of slower convergence, whereas most practitioners using Sinkhorn select a very small lambda to speed up convergence, leading to behavior that is distinctly different from Wasserstein. Hence, I am wondering whether, in this work, lambda induces a pure Wasserstein regime or a Sinkhorn (entropic) regime.
