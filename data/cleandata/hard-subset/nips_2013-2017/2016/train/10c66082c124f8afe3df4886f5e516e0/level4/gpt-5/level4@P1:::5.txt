Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles supervised text classification by extending the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) to incorporate label information. The proposed Supervised WMD (S-WMD) learns (i) a linear transformation A of the underlying word embedding space and (ii) a word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. Training minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective, in the spirit of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). Because direct differentiation through the original WMD is computationally expensive, the authors use an entropy-regularized transport (Sinkhorn distance) to obtain efficient, differentiable approximations for the transport plan and dual potentials. They further speed training with a word-centroid-based initialization (S-WCD), mini-batch optimization, and neighbor filtering. Across eight datasets and 26 baselines (including TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA, WCD, WMD, and supervised metric learners like NCA/LMNN/ITML on PCA-reduced features), S-WMD achieves the best average performance.

This work builds directly on (1) WMD for document distance via optimal transport using pretrained embeddings, (2) entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) for fast, differentiable transport, and (3) classic metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The main contribution is combining these strands to learn the ground metric and word weights for text classification efficiently.

Strengths:
- Clear and well-organized presentation, with intuitive motivation and solid mathematical development. The gradient derivations via the Sinkhorn-fixed point and dual variables are clearly explained.
- Practical and efficient training procedure: initialization via S-WCD, batch gradients, and neighbor pruning reduce costs significantly.
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation on diverse datasets with many strong baselines; consistent improvements in average rank. Code availability is a plus.
- Sensible design choices (learning both A and w) that increase flexibility relative to prior metric-learning-on-documents approaches.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Novelty is somewhat incremental: the paper combines known components—WMD, Sinkhorn regularization, and NCA-style objectives—rather than introducing a fundamentally new idea. Nonetheless, the integration is thoughtful and practically important.
- The discussion of the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn relaxation is too brief. In many applications, λ has a dominant impact on both speed and the degree of entropic smoothing. Large λ approaches the true Wasserstein distance but can slow convergence; small λ induces behavior that can differ substantially from WMD. The paper uses λ = 10 but does not offer a principled rationale or sensitivity analysis. Does S-WMD’s performance reflect a “near-Wasserstein” or a distinctly entropic metric? How sensitive are results to λ across datasets? Is λ tuned per dataset?
- The comparison and referencing around the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) were occasionally confusing. Some cited references seemed tangential, and the role of WCD as a lower bound and as an initialization could be clarified.
- On datasets with long documents (BBCSPORT, OHSUMED), NCA on TF-IDF can outperform S-WMD. The paper notes this but gives limited analysis. Are there adjustments (e.g., different ground cost p, regularization of A, or alternative initializations) that help S-WMD on long, dense texts?
- The method fixes the ground cost to squared Euclidean distance between word vectors. Results on CLASSIC suggest this may be suboptimal there. An ablation on other costs (e.g., L2 vs squared L2) or constraints on A could be informative.

Quality:
- The method is technically sound, and the approximations are well-motivated. However, more guidance on the approximation error induced by the Sinkhorn relaxation (as a function of λ) and its effect on gradients would strengthen the theoretical underpinnings.
- The empirical study is thorough and fair, with strong baselines tuned via Bayesian optimization.

Clarity:
- Overall very clear and well written. Areas to improve: expand the λ discussion; streamline and clarify the WCD comparisons and references; briefly justify the choice of squared Euclidean ground cost.

Originality:
- Moderate. The integration and application to supervised document distances are useful, but the components are known. Learning both A and w within the WMD framework is a meaningful extension.

Significance:
- High practical impact. The approach is efficient, easy to apply with pretrained embeddings, and yields strong improvements across multiple benchmarks. It will likely be used and extended by practitioners.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong empirical results and efficiency.
- Clear, well-executed integration of metric learning with OT-based document distances.
- Public code and reproducibility.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited novelty relative to prior WMD, Sinkhorn OT, and NCA.
- Insufficient analysis of λ and its effect on behavior/performance.
- Some confusion in the WCD discussion and lack of ablations on cost choices.

Suggestions for the authors:
- Provide a sensitivity study for λ (accuracy vs. λ, runtime vs. λ) and clarify whether the learned metric behaves more like true WMD or an entropically smoothed variant in practice.
- Add ablations isolating the contributions of A vs. w, and squared vs. unsquared Euclidean ground costs.
- Include a small-scale comparison to exact WMD gradients (or higher λ) on short documents to quantify the approximation gap.
- Analyze failure cases on long documents and explore remedies (e.g., regularizing A, alternative initializations, or document-length-aware weighting).
- Tighten the WCD discussion and ensure references are directly relevant.

Overall assessment:
A strong, well-written paper with solid empirical gains and a practical training scheme. While the novelty is incremental, the contribution is meaningful and timely. Addressing the λ issue and clarifying the WCD discussion would further strengthen the work.
