The authors analyze the problem of text classification into categories, expanding on previous work that suggested the Word Mover's Distance (Wasserstein) to make it supervised by learning word-specific weights. This contribution suggests using the Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for the Wasserstein distance to improve its speed and evaluates it on eight datasets against 26 baselines, showing the best average performance.

The paper is well-written, presenting the topic clearly and analyzing the math behind the problem in depth. However, the novelty of the contribution is somewhat limited compared to the previous Word Mover's Distance paper. The references against the World Centroid Distance may be confusing and are not particularly relevant.

One notable weakness is the limited discussion of lambda, the regularizing factor. In the reviewer's experience, the value of lambda has a significant impact, sometimes being the dominant factor in equation (2). Theoretically, results should be similar to the pure Wasserstein distance given a large enough lambda at the cost of slower convergence. However, most people using Sinkhorn use a very small lambda to improve the convergence rate, achieving a behavior plainly different from Wasserstein. Therefore, it would be interesting to know whether lambda dictates a pure Wasserstein behavior or a Sinkhorn (entropic) behavior in this contribution.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a clear and well-written analysis of the problem and the math behind it.
* The evaluation on eight datasets against 26 baselines shows the best average performance.
* The use of Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for the Wasserstein distance improves speed.

Weaknesses:

* Limited novelty compared to the previous Word Mover's Distance paper.
* Confusing and irrelevant references against the World Centroid Distance.
* Poor discussion of lambda, the regularizing factor, and its impact on the results.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a well-written and clear analysis of the problem and the math behind it.
* The evaluation on eight datasets against 26 baselines shows the best average performance.
* The use of Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for the Wasserstein distance improves speed.

Arguments against acceptance:

* Limited novelty compared to the previous Word Mover's Distance paper.
* Poor discussion of lambda, the regularizing factor, and its impact on the results.

Recommendation:

* Accept the paper, but encourage the authors to address the limited discussion of lambda and its impact on the results.
* Suggest that the authors provide more insight into the behavior of the Sinkhorn distance with different values of lambda and how it compares to the pure Wasserstein distance.
