The authors analyze the problem of text classification into categories, building upon a previous work that introduced Word Mover's Distance (Wasserstein) and expanding it to a supervised setting by learning word-specific weights. This contribution proposes using the Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for the Wasserstein distance to improve computational efficiency and evaluates its performance on eight datasets against 26 baselines, achieving the best average performance. 

This paper is exceptionally well-written, presenting the topic clearly and providing an in-depth analysis of the underlying mathematics. The authors strike a great balance between technical rigor and clarity, making the paper accessible to a broad audience. However, the paper has a few weaknesses. One potential drawback is the limited novelty of the approach compared to the original Word Mover's Distance paper. Additionally, the discussion of World Centroid Distance as a baseline seems unnecessary and may confuse readers. 

Furthermore, I found the treatment of lambda (the regularizing factor) to be somewhat lacking. In my experience, the value of lambda can significantly impact the results, sometimes becoming the dominant factor in equation (2). Theoretically, a large enough lambda should yield results similar to the pure Wasserstein distance, albeit at the cost of slower convergence. However, in practice, many users of Sinkhorn distance employ a very small lambda to improve convergence rates, resulting in behavior that deviates from the Wasserstein distance. I am curious to know whether, in this contribution, lambda is chosen such that it dictates a behavior akin to the pure Wasserstein distance or the Sinkhorn (entropic) distance.
