This paper presents a supervised metric learning approach that extends the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD). The method, termed Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), learns a distance metric tailored for specific tasks, such as document classification. The key contributions are twofold: learning a linear projection matrix to transform the underlying word2vec embeddings, and learning a set of word-specific weights to adjust the importance of individual words within a document. The authors propose an efficient optimization algorithm for learning these parameters and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through a comprehensive experimental evaluation on several document classification benchmarks, using a k-NN classifier.

**Strengths**

The paper has several notable strengths:
*   The core idea of extending the unsupervised WMD with a supervised learning framework is both technically interesting and practically useful.
*   The empirical results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed learned metric outperforms WMD and numerous other baselines on several k-NN text classification tasks.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and a pleasure to read.

**Weaknesses**

Despite these strengths, there are some weaknesses that temper the paper's overall impact:

1.  **Marginal Gain Over Simpler Initialization:** The most significant concern is the limited performance gain of the fully-learned S-WMD over a simpler baseline. As shown in Table 3, the proposed method initialized with a "word centroid distance" (WCD) already achieves performance that is very close to the final, fully optimized model. This suggests that much of the performance improvement comes from a good initialization using established metric learning techniques on a simpler document representation, rather than from the complex optimization of the full transport-based objective. This observation diminishes the perceived value of the paper's main technical contribution—the optimization procedure for the S-WMD.

2.  **Missing Experiments:** The evaluation could be strengthened by including two key experiments:
    *   **Ablation Study:** The paper introduces two learned components: the projection matrix A and the word-specific weights. An ablation study is needed to disentangle their respective contributions. It would be highly informative to see the performance when learning only the projection matrix, and separately when learning only the word weights, to understand the importance of each component.
    *   **Stronger Centroid-Based Baselines:** Given the strong performance of the WCD initialization, it would be valuable to see how well standard metric learning algorithms (e.g., ITML, LMNN, NCA) perform when applied directly to the word centroid document embeddings. This would provide a more direct comparison and help to clarify whether the complexity of the transport-based approach is truly necessary for achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Originality**

While the idea of applying metric learning within the Earth Mover's Distance framework is compelling, the primary novel contribution appears to be the specific optimization procedure. As discussed above, the limited empirical gains from this procedure suggest that the practical impact of this contribution may be modest.
