The authors analyze the problem of text classification into categories, expanding on previous work that suggested using Word Mover's Distance (Wasserstein) in a supervised manner by learning word-specific weights. This contribution proposes using the Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for the Wasserstein distance to improve its speed, and evaluates its performance on 8 datasets against 26 baselines, demonstrating the best average performance.

This is a well-written paper that effectively presents the topic and provides an in-depth analysis of the underlying mathematics with great clarity. However, the paper has a couple of weaknesses. Firstly, the novelty of the contribution is somewhat limited compared to the previous Word Mover's Distance paper. Secondly, the comparison to the World Centroid Distance is not particularly relevant and may be confusing to readers. Furthermore, the discussion of lambda (the regularizing factor) is lacking. In my experience, the value of lambda has a significant impact on the results, sometimes being the dominant factor in equation (2).

Theoretically, the results should be similar to those obtained using the pure Wasserstein distance, given a large enough lambda, albeit at the cost of slower convergence. However, in practice, most users of Sinkhorn distance employ a very small lambda to improve convergence rates, resulting in behavior that is distinctly different from the Wasserstein distance. I am therefore curious to know whether, in this contribution, lambda dictates a behavior that is more akin to the pure Wasserstein distance or the Sinkhorn (entropic) distance.
