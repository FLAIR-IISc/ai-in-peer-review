The authors present a novel method for learning the parameters of the Wasserstein distance for text documents (WMD), effectively an Earth Mover’s Distance computed using word embeddings to define bin-to-bin dissimilarities. Their approach parameterizes the EMD cost matrix using a Mahalanobis distance based on vector representations, and further incorporates learned histogram coefficients, building upon prior work such as that of Lebanon. The paper provides gradients for Non-commutative Attraction (NCA) type costs within this framework and demonstrates strong experimental validation. Overall, the work represents a well-integrated combination of established techniques, yielding compelling results and supporting acceptance. The paper’s strengths lie in the simplicity and clarity of the core idea, despite the non-trivial implementation and experimental verification. The experimental results are a particularly strong component of this submission.

To further strengthen the paper, the authors should more clearly articulate the interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations. Increased mathematical rigor is also recommended, as the current computations and notations contain some imprecision. Finally, including an algorithmic box would improve clarity regarding the proposed methodology. 

Several minor points warrant attention: In Eq. 1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings serves as the cost, while Eq. 6 utilizes the squared Euclidean metric (equivalent to the 2-Wasserstein distance) for Mahalanobis metric learning. To avoid this inconsistency, framing WMD as a *p*-Wasserstein distance between clouds of word embeddings, regardless of the value of *p*, may be more appropriate. The histogram reweighting approach in Eq. 5 is not novel, having been previously proposed by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification, and also explored in “Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations” by Le/Cuturi.  Further experimental analysis exploring the impact of the learned weight vector *w* in the metric would be valuable – does this component contribute significantly, and if so, in what way? 

The related work section could benefit from a more explicit discussion of the differences between this work and existing approaches. For instance, the paper builds upon the work of Cuturi and Avis, but leverages entropy regularization to improve scalability. The cost function also differs; it is continuous and parameterized by linear maps, in contrast to the discrete cost in the prior work. These distinctions should be highlighted. The paper also references Wang et al., but it is important to acknowledge the limitations and unpublished status of that work, particularly regarding the single optimization run and its demonstrated ineffectiveness.
