Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD computes document distance as the optimal transport cost between word distributions using pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec). S-WMD introduces supervision by learning (i) a linear transformation of the word embeddings and (ii) a global word-importance weight vector that rescales document histograms. Training minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss (in the spirit of NCA), and uses an entropy-regularized optimal transport (Sinkhorn) approximation to obtain efficient gradients. Experiments on eight text classification datasets compare S-WMD to 26 baselines, reporting consistent improvements on most tasks.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on WMD (Kusner et al.), optimal transport with entropy regularization (Cuturi), and supervised metric learning (NCA/LMNN/ITML). The contribution is to learn a supervised ground metric and word weights specifically for WMD, trained via an NCA-style objective with Sinkhorn-based approximations.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized writing with a thorough discussion of related work.
- Practical and scalable training via Sinkhorn relaxation and batching.
- Broad empirical evaluation across diverse datasets with many competitive baselines.
- The approach is conceptually simple and easy to implement.

Weaknesses and questions
1) Motivation for word reweighting w: The introduction of a learned word-importance vector is not well justified. Since w is applied directly to the normalized word histograms, it could substantially alter document content. Please clarify:
- Why this reweighting is necessary beyond transforming the embedding space with A.
- Whether w is constrained (e.g., positivity, regularization) to prevent degenerate or semantically distorting solutions.
- Empirical evidence that w improves performance without changing document meaning (e.g., ablations with/without w, qualitative analyses).

2) Incremental contribution: The main design—learning a linear transform A and weights w under an NCA-like objective—feels like a natural, but not particularly novel, extension of WMD combined with standard metric learning ideas and Sinkhorn approximations. Please better articulate the conceptual novelty beyond prior work on learning ground metrics for EMD and generalized Euclidean metric learning.

3) Inference on new documents: How is w applied at test time?
- Is w a single global vector learned once and then used for all new documents, or is it document-specific?
- If global, please state this clearly and describe the inference pipeline for unseen documents.
- If document-specific, explain how w is estimated for a new document without labels.

4) “Semantic difference” terminology: The abstract suggests S-WMD learns distances that reflect “semantic difference,” yet supervision is provided by labels (topic, sentiment, authorship). Please clarify what is meant by “semantic difference” and how that relates to possibly heterogeneous label semantics across tasks.

Evaluation against review criteria
- Quality: The method appears technically sound, and the use of Sinkhorn makes optimization practical. However, the role and constraints of w require clearer justification and analysis.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. Providing more intuition and ablations for w would further improve clarity.
- Originality: The combination is sensible but largely incremental, integrating known components (WMD, Sinkhorn, NCA) with a learned word-weighting scheme.
- Significance: Empirical results are strong across many datasets, suggesting practical value. Conceptual novelty is modest.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical performance across diverse benchmarks.
- Efficient and practical training procedure with publicly available code.
- Clear exposition and solid positioning within the literature.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited conceptual novelty; the formulation largely combines established techniques.
- Insufficient motivation and analysis of the reweighting vector w and its impact on document semantics.
- Ambiguity about test-time use of w and the meaning of “semantic difference.”

Overall assessment
A well-executed and clearly presented application of supervised metric learning to WMD with convincing empirical results. I am not opposed to acceptance, provided the authors clarify the motivation and constraints for w, the inference procedure for new documents, and the intended meaning of “semantic difference,” and better articulate the novelty relative to prior work.
