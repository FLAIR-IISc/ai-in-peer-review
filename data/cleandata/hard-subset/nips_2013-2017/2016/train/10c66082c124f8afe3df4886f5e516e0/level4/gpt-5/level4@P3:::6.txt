Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised metric for text that builds on the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD computes document distances by solving an optimal transport problem between word embeddings, and has shown strong performance for kNN classification, but is entirely unsupervised. S-WMD incorporates supervision by learning (i) a linear transformation A of the word embedding space and (ii) a word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The learning objective is a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss à la Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), optimized with gradients of the transport cost.

A key technical challenge is differentiating through the WMD, which is a nested LP with cubic complexity in the document vocabulary size. The authors avoid nested optimization by using the entropy-regularized transport (Sinkhorn distance) to obtain an efficient O(q^2) approximation and to recover approximate dual variables, enabling practical gradients with respect to both A and w. They further accelerate training via an initialization based on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) with NCA (“S-WCD”), mini-batching, and a WCD-based neighbor filter.

Empirically, S-WMD is evaluated on 8 datasets against 26 baselines, including unsupervised WCD/WMD; classical representations (BOW, TF-IDF, BM25, LSI, LDA, mSDA) with and without supervised metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML). S-WMD achieves the lowest kNN error on 5 of 8 datasets and competitive results on the rest, while being efficient to train. This work relates closely to metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and to learning ground distances for EMD, and adapts Sinkhorn regularization for fast supervised learning of transport-based document distances.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is well motivated. The use of Sinkhorn relaxation to approximate gradients of WMD is appropriate and addresses the computational bottleneck. The gradient with respect to A via the relaxed transport plan and with respect to w via approximate dual variables is clearly derived. Nonconvexity and uniqueness issues are acknowledged, and the relaxation ensures uniqueness of the transport plan.
- Empirical evaluation: The experimental setup is broad, with strong baselines and hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian optimization. The kNN-based evaluation is consistent with the method’s goal of learning a distance. The efficiency claims are supported with training-time reports.
- Missing analyses: It would strengthen the paper to include ablations quantifying the separate contributions of A and w (A-only, w-only), sensitivity to λ (Sinkhorn regularization), and the effect of using p=1 vs p=2 in the ground cost. These would clarify where the gains come from and how robust the method is.

Clarity
- Overall, the paper is clearly written and well organized. The background on WMD, Sinkhorn, and NCA provides sufficient context.
- Please add intuition for Eq. (5): specifically, how the reweighting d̃ = (w ◦ d)/(w>d) changes mass conservation and effectively emphasizes/de-emphasizes word transport. A brief example would help.
- Clarify the use of the regularized problem (Eq. 2): Is λ in Eq. (2) the same λ referenced later? Please unify notation and briefly discuss the chosen value (λ=10) and sensitivity.
- Table/figure details:
  - The text says “on the right we also show the average error across datasets,” but Table 2 appears to show average rank. Please correct the description or the table heading.
  - Define “n” versus “ne” in Table 2.
  - Specify how k for kNN is selected (fixed across datasets, tuned per dataset, or per method).
- Minor: The claim that the distance is “particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words” follows from moving mass between nearby word embeddings; it would be helpful to support this with a small controlled example (e.g., synonym replacements) in the supplement.

Originality
- The idea of supervising an optimal-transport-based document distance by learning a linear ground metric and word weights is novel in the NLP setting. Prior supervised EMD/ground-metric learning has largely been in vision; learning histogram weights alongside the ground metric provides additional flexibility not commonly explored there.
- The integration of Sinkhorn-based approximations for efficient end-to-end learning under an NCA-style objective is an elegant and practically useful contribution.

Significance
- The method advances the state of the art on multiple datasets and is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners who rely on distance-based retrieval/classification. It retains the interpretability and advantages of WMD while making it task-aware.
- Code availability further increases impact. The approach should be applicable beyond kNN to other tasks that depend on document distances.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean extension of WMD to supervised settings by learning both ground metric and word importance.
- Sound use of Sinkhorn relaxation to obtain efficient, differentiable approximations.
- Strong empirical results against many competitive baselines with careful tuning.
- Practical training pipeline (initialization via S-WCD, batching, neighbor filtering) and code release.

Weaknesses
- Lack of ablation studies to disentangle contributions of A vs w and to assess sensitivity to λ and other training hyperparameters.
- Some presentation details need clarification (notation for λ, table annotations, definition of k).
- Discussion of distance choices (e.g., p=1 vs p=2 or cosine-based ground costs) is brief; the squared Euclidean choice may not be optimal for some datasets.

Questions for the authors
- Please provide ablations for A-only and w-only to quantify their relative impact on performance.
- Is λ in Eq. (2) the same as elsewhere in the text? How sensitive are results to λ and to the number of Sinkhorn iterations?
- How is k for kNN chosen? Is it fixed across datasets or tuned on validation data?
- In Table 2, what do “n” and “ne” denote?
- The text mentions “average error on the right”; do you mean average rank?
- Could you include a brief intuitive explanation and example for the effect of the reweighting in Eq. (5)?

Recommendation rationale (pros/cons)
Pros:
- Meaningful and well-executed supervised extension of a popular and useful document distance.
- Solid theoretical grounding with practical acceleration and strong empirical gains.
- Broad and fair comparative evaluation and available code.

Cons:
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses limit interpretability of where improvements come from.
- Minor clarity issues in notation and reporting.

Overall, this is a high-quality and impactful contribution that, with a few clarifications and additional analyses, would be a strong addition to the conference.
