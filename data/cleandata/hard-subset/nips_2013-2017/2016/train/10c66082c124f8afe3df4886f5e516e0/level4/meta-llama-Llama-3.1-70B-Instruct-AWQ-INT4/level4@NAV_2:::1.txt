This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach built on top of the recent "word-movers distance" (a transport distance applied to word2vec word embeddings). The proposed approach can be applied to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances after linear projection. On top of learning the linear projection to maximize performance, the proposed approach also learns a weighting of words. The latter is specific to the document application, it consists in re-weighting the frequency of each word by a learned weight, and re-normalizing the word frequencies. The paper presents an efficient optimization method to learn the proposed parameterized metric. A thorough experimental evaluation shows the effectiveness of the proposed approach by comparing it to a large number of reasonable baselines on a number of document classification datasets using a kNN classifier based on the proposed learned metric.

The main idea of the paper is to extend the recent word-movers distance, which is an interesting and technically useful extension (shown to be effective on a number of kNN text classification tasks). However, the potential impact is perhaps limited by the fact that earth-movers distance with a metric learning via a "word centroid distance" initialization performs similar to the full learning of the proposed distance. In other words, the main technical contributions of this paper do not seem to contribute very much over this initialization method based on existing techniques.

One thing that I missed in the evaluation is an assessment of the relative importance of the word-specific weights, and the projection matrix A. It would be useful to see performance with each one of these components being active in isolation. Additionally, an evaluation of the "word centroid embedding" (weighted mean of word embeddings for the document) with all tested existing metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA) would be beneficial.

The originality of the paper is mostly related to the way to optimize the metric, which is an interesting and useful contribution. However, the idea of a linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance is a limited contribution in itself.

The paper is very well written and a pleasure to read. There are a few minor issues that could be clarified: the authors should specify which paper they are referring to in Line 152, and Equation 11 should be revised to include stars for alpha and beta. It would also be useful to explicitly recall around Line 170 that this is the Sinkhorn scaling that is referred to in the introduction in Line 56.
