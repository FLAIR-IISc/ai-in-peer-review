The authors tackle the problem of text classification, building upon prior work that leveraged Word Mover’s Distance (Wasserstein distance) by introducing a supervised learning approach to determine word-specific weights. Their key contribution lies in utilizing the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient approximation of the Wasserstein distance, and demonstrating its effectiveness through comprehensive evaluation across eight datasets against 26 baseline methods – achieving state-of-the-art average performance. 

This is a clearly written and well-presented paper, offering a thorough explanation of the topic and a detailed, yet accessible, mathematical analysis. The primary limitations are a degree of incremental novelty relative to the original Word Mover’s Distance work, and the inclusion of comparisons to World Centroid Distance, which I found unhelpful and somewhat distracting. 

Furthermore, the discussion surrounding the regularization parameter, lambda, feels incomplete. Based on my experience, lambda’s value significantly influences performance, potentially dominating equation (2). While theoretically, a large lambda should approximate standard Wasserstein distance with slower convergence, practical applications of Sinkhorn often employ small lambda values to accelerate convergence, resulting in behavior substantially different from Wasserstein. Consequently, I am interested in understanding whether the current implementation with lambda tends toward a pure Wasserstein-like behavior or a more typical Sinkhorn (entropic) behavior.
