

This paper proposes a supervised extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) for computing document distances. WMD, originally motivated by the Earth Mover's Distance, computes the minimal transport cost between two word distributions in the embedding space, using word2vec embeddings for word-level distances. The authors introduce two key learning components: (1) a linear transformation of word embeddings and (2) a reweighting of the word distribution via a vector $w$, aiming to maximize separation between documents of different labels. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and demonstrates a strong grasp of related literature through its comprehensive review. Experimental results on multiple novel datasets are provided.

However, several critical concerns require clarification before acceptance:  
1. **Motivation for reweighting**: The rationale for reweighting the word distribution using $w$ is insufficiently justified. As currently formulated, this reweighting risks altering the fundamental semantic content of the document (e.g., by amplifying or suppressing key words), potentially transforming it into a different document. The authors must rigorously demonstrate how $w$ preserves document integrity while enhancing label separation.  
2. **Contribution novelty**: The adoption of a linear transformation $A$ and reweighting $w$ within the Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA) objective for supervised WMD constitutes a straightforward extension rather than a significant advancement. This formulation lacks novelty or substantial technical innovation compared to existing supervised metric learning approaches.  
3. **Generalization to unseen documents**: The paper does not address how $w$ is determined for a completely new, unseen document. Since $w$ appears to be learned per-document during training, the mechanism for applying the model to novel documents (where $w$ is undefined) remains unclear.  
4. **Ambiguity in "semantic difference"**: The abstract references "semantic difference" as a driver for distance learning. However, this phrasing conflates label differences (e.g., authorship or topic) with semantic meaning. The authors must clarify whether "semantic difference" refers to label-specific distinctions or a distinct semantic interpretation, as the current wording is misleading.  

While the paper is technically sound and well-presented, these unresolved issues—particularly the lack of justification for reweighting, minimal contribution novelty, and unclear generalization procedure—significantly undermine its impact. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance pending thorough clarification of the above concerns.
