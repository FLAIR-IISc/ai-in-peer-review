### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a supervised document distance metric. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which leverages word embeddings to calculate an unsupervised distance between documents as an optimal transport problem. The key contribution of this paper is to extend WMD to a supervised setting by learning two components: 1) a linear transformation of the underlying word embedding space, and 2) a vector of word-importance weights.

A major technical challenge is that the gradient of the WMD objective is computationally prohibitive due to a nested optimization problem. The authors cleverly circumvent this by approximating the WMD with its entropy-regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance), which permits an efficient and differentiable solution. The model is trained by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out k-NN classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). The proposed S-WMD is evaluated extensively on eight text classification datasets, where it demonstrates state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance against a large suite of 26 unsupervised and supervised baselines.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Extension:** The paper addresses a clear limitation of the original WMD—its inability to incorporate supervision. Extending this powerful distance metric to a supervised setting is a logical and significant contribution. The dual approach of learning both a geometric transformation and word-specific weights is compelling.

2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The primary technical insight—using the Sinkhorn distance to create a differentiable and efficient approximation for the WMD gradient—is elegant and effective. This makes an otherwise intractable optimization problem practical, which is crucial for the method's feasibility.

3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is exceptionally thorough. The authors compare S-WMD against 26 distinct baselines, including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with multiple metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN). The evaluation spans eight diverse datasets, lending strong credibility to the results.

4.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The results are impressive, with S-WMD achieving the lowest k-NN test error on five of the eight datasets and demonstrating the best average rank across all experiments. This clearly shows the method advances the state of the art in supervised document distance learning.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Ablation Study on Learned Components:** The method learns both a linear transformation `A` and word importance weights `w`. It would be highly beneficial to include an ablation study that disentangles the contributions of these two components. For example, what is the performance when learning only `A` (with `w` fixed to ones) or only `w` (with `A` fixed to identity)? This would provide valuable insight into whether the geometric transformation or the feature re-weighting is the primary driver of the performance gains.

2.  **Contextualization with Non-Metric-Learning Classifiers:** The evaluation is strictly based on k-NN classification, which is standard for metric learning papers. However, to better position the work within the broader field of text classification, it would be valuable to include comparisons against strong, non-metric-learning baselines like a logistic regression or SVM classifier on TF-IDF features, or even a simple CNN/LSTM trained on the word embeddings. This would help readers understand how S-WMD + k-NN compares to other standard classification paradigms.

**Evaluation according to NIPS Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by a rigorous and extensive set of experiments. The approach for approximating the gradient is well-founded, and the overall work is complete.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation and background are clearly explained. However, some minor details in the experimental setup could be clarified (see specific questions below).
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on existing concepts like WMD, NCA, and Sinkhorn distances, their combination to create a supervised document metric is novel. It clearly distinguishes itself from prior work in metric learning, which typically operates on fixed, high-dimensional document vectors rather than transforming the underlying word embedding space.
*   **Significance:** The results are significant. Given that WMD has become a strong baseline for document similarity, a supervised extension that demonstrably improves its performance is likely to be widely used and built upon by researchers in NLP and machine learning. The state-of-the-art results on several benchmarks underscore its importance.

**Specific Questions and Suggestions for the Authors:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Clarification (λ):** In Section 3.1, the optimal dual variables (α∗λ, β∗λ) are derived using `1/λ`. Is this the same λ from the entropy regularizer `(-1/λ)h(T)` in Equation (2)? If so, the relationship appears inverted, as a larger λ in Eq. (2) implies a closer approximation to the true WMD (less regularization), whereas in the dual variable update it would lead to a smaller magnitude. Please clarify this relationship.

2.  **Justification for WMD's Forgiveness:** The conclusion states that the method is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words." This is a key strength inherited from WMD. For clarity, it would be helpful to briefly reiterate in the conclusion *why* this is the case—namely, because the distance is measured between pre-trained word embeddings that place conceptually similar words (e.g., "king" and "queen") close together.

3.  **Value of `k` in k-NN:** The value of `k` used for the k-NN classification experiments is not mentioned. Was `k` treated as a hyperparameter and tuned for each dataset/method, or was a fixed value used throughout? Please add this detail to the experimental setup section.

4.  **Minor Clarifications in Tables:**
    *   In Table 1, the distinction between `n` and `ne` for vocabulary size is not defined.
    *   In the text describing Table 2 (line 257), it is mentioned that the table shows "average error," but the column header is "Avg. Rank." Please correct this minor discrepancy.
