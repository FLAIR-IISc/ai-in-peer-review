This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised document metric based on the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) [19]. WMD frames the distance between two documents as an optimal transport problem between their respective word embeddings. The key limitation of WMD is its unsupervised nature. This work addresses that limitation by learning two components from labeled data: (1) a linear transformation of the word embedding space, and (2) a vector of word-importance weights that re-scales the document's word frequencies. To overcome the computational challenge of differentiating through the WMD objective, the authors propose an efficient optimization procedure based on an entropy-regularized version of the transport problem (the Sinkhorn distance). The effectiveness of S-WMD is demonstrated through a comprehensive experimental evaluation on eight text classification datasets, where it is compared against 26 baselines using a k-NN classifier.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of the original WMD: its inability to incorporate supervision. The proposed extension is technically sound, well-motivated, and builds naturally upon the WMD framework.
*   The derivation of an efficient gradient-based optimization algorithm is a significant contribution. By relaxing the WMD objective using the Sinkhorn distance, the authors develop a practical method for a problem that would otherwise be computationally prohibitive.
*   The experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors compare their method against a large and diverse set of strong baselines, including various document representations combined with standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML), on multiple datasets. The results generally demonstrate the superiority of the proposed S-WMD.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the background, their proposed method, and the experimental setup, making a complex topic highly accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The significance of the full optimization procedure is somewhat diminished by the strong performance of the initialization method ("S-WMD init."). The results in Table 2 show that S-WMD init., which applies NCA to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD), achieves a large portion of the final performance gain. This suggests that much of the benefit comes from the supervised WCD initialization, raising questions about the marginal utility of the more complex S-WMD optimization that follows.
*   The evaluation would be strengthened by a more direct comparison with standard metric learning on word centroid representations. The paper applies NCA for initialization but does not report results for applying other metric learning baselines, like LMNN and ITML, directly to the word centroid document representations. Including these baselines would help to better isolate the benefits of the full transport-based metric learning over simpler, yet powerful, alternatives.
*   The paper lacks an ablation study to disentangle the contributions of the two learned components: the linear transformation matrix A and the word importance vector w. It would be valuable to see the performance of S-WMD when learning only A or only w. This would provide deeper insight into which component is more critical for the method's success.
*   While the combination of ideas is novel, the core concept of applying linear metric learning to the ground distance of an Earth Mover's Distance has been explored in other domains (e.g., Wang et al. [35]). The primary originality here lies in its application to WMD for text and the specific, efficient optimization strategy developed.

**Minor Points for Clarification:**

*   In Section 3.1 (around line 170), it would be helpful to explicitly state that the matrix-scaling algorithm for the relaxed transport is the Sinkhorn scaling method mentioned in the background section.
*   In the gradient w.r.t. w (Equation 10), should the dual variables α and β be the optimal ones, denoted with a star (α* and β*)? This seems implied but is not explicitly written.
*   Line 152: The sentence begins "It can be shown that," followed by citations [1, 7, 8]. It is slightly unclear if this result is from one specific paper or is a general result found across all three. A more precise attribution would be helpful.
