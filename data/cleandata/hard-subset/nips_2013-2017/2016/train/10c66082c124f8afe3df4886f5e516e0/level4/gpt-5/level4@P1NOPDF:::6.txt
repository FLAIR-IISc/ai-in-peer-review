The paper proposes a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for metric learning on textual documents. The original WMD is unsupervised and measures distances between word embeddings; this work incorporates supervision in the form of “these documents belong to the same class.” MATLAB code is promised. I appreciated the authors’ careful responses, which clarified several of my questions.

The method learns two components of the distance: (i) a linear transformation A (as in traditional metric learning), and (ii) word-importance weights w. I would like a clearer intuition for Eq. (5), which defines how w is used. In particular, how should we interpret these weights—are they akin to term importance, class-conditional reweighting, or transport costs—and why is this specific parametrization appropriate? In addition, it would help to disentangle the contributions of A versus w to the performance gains. An ablation study (A only, w only, both) would make the source of improvement more transparent.

In the experiments, the learned distance is evaluated via a classification task. I understand that directly evaluating a distance metric can be challenging, but for context it would be useful to include a simple classifier baseline on the same datasets (e.g., linear or kernel SVM on averaged embeddings or TF–IDF). That said, the authors’ response addressed this concern, and it is no longer an issue for me.

Questions for clarification:
- I did not understand when Eq. (2) is used. How does it fit into the training and evaluation pipeline?
- The λ in Eq. (2) appears to differ from the λ mentioned around line 170. Are these different hyperparameters, or is this a notational inconsistency/typo?
- Could you provide stronger support for the claim that the method is “particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words” (line 316)? For instance, a controlled analysis or qualitative examples showing improved alignment under paraphrase/synonymy would help substantiate this.

Other comments and minor issues:
- Line 257: “on the right we also show the average error across datasets.” I could not locate this; is it supposed to be in Table 3? Please clarify where the average is reported.
- Table 2: What is the difference between “n” and “ne”? I did not find a definition in the text.
- I could not find how k (for k-NN classification) is chosen. Is k fixed across datasets and methods, tuned per dataset, or optimized per method? Please specify the selection protocol (e.g., cross-validation, validation set) to ensure a fair comparison.

Overall, the supervised extension of WMD via learning A and w is a promising direction. Clarifying the role and intuition of Eq. (5), providing ablations to isolate contributions, tightening the notation around λ and Eq. (2), and addressing the reporting details (average error, “n” vs. “ne,” and k selection) would significantly improve the paper’s clarity and evidential support.
