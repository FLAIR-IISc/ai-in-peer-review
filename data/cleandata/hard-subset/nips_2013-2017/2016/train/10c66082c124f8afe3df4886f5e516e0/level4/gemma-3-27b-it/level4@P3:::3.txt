This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a document distance metric by incorporating supervision into the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) framework. The authors address the challenge of efficiently learning a metric that combines a linear transformation of word embeddings with word importance weighting, minimizing leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. They achieve this by leveraging a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and a probabilistic filtering technique to enable practical gradient computation. The method is evaluated on eight real-world text classification tasks, demonstrating consistent and significant performance improvements over a comprehensive set of 26 baselines.

The core idea of learning a supervised metric on top of WMD is compelling and well-motivated. Recent successes of WMD highlight the power of leveraging word embeddings for document similarity, but its unsupervised nature limits its adaptability to specific tasks. This work directly addresses that limitation. The proposed approach is a sensible combination of existing techniques – metric learning, optimal transport, and word embeddings – and the authors successfully navigate the computational challenges inherent in incorporating supervision into the WMD framework. The extensive experimental evaluation is a major strength, demonstrating consistent gains across diverse datasets and against a large number of competitive baselines. The clarity of the presentation is also commendable, making a complex topic reasonably accessible.

However, there are areas where the paper could be strengthened. While the authors acknowledge the use of entropy regularization (Cuturi [6]), they don’t fully articulate the trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational efficiency. A more detailed discussion of the impact of the regularization parameter λ on performance would be valuable.  The mathematical notation, while generally clear, occasionally lacks precision (e.g., the transition between Euclidean distance in Eq. 1 and squared Euclidean distance in Eq. 6). An algorithmic box outlining the key steps of the S-WMD training procedure would significantly enhance readability and understanding.

Several related works could be discussed more thoroughly. The authors correctly point out Lebanon's work on reweighting histograms for text classification, and Le/Cuturi’s work on Aitchison transformations, however the connection to broader metric learning literature, especially concerning non-Euclidean metrics and manifold learning, could be more explicit. While Wang et al. [35] is mentioned, a more critical assessment of its limitations (as acknowledged by the authors) and how S-WMD overcomes them would be beneficial. Finally, exploring the sensitivity of the learned word importance weights (w) would provide further insight into the method's behavior; quantifying the impact of these weights or providing qualitative examples of important words for different tasks would be insightful. 

**Strengths:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to supervised document distance learning.
*   Strong experimental results across a diverse set of datasets.
*   Clear and generally well-written presentation.
*   Addresses a computational bottleneck by leveraging efficient approximations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited discussion of the trade-offs associated with entropy regularization.
*   Occasional imprecision in mathematical notation.
*   Lack of an algorithmic box outlining the training procedure.
*   Could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work and the impact of the learned word weights.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in text processing – learning task-specific document similarity metrics.
*   The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging benchmark.
*   The technical contributions – efficient gradient computation and integration of metric learning with WMD – are significant.
*   The extensive experimental evaluation provides strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The mathematical presentation could be more rigorous.
*   The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of related work and the impact of key design choices.
*   While the results are strong, the improvements over the baselines, while consistent, are not always dramatic.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of text processing and metric learning. The strengths of the work – the novel approach, strong experimental results, and clear presentation – outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address the points raised above regarding mathematical rigor, related work discussion, and algorithmic clarity.
