This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach built on top of the recent "word-movers distance" (WMD) [19], a transport distance applied to word2vec word embeddings. The proposed approach, called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), can be applied to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances after linear projection. On top of learning the linear projection to maximize performance, S-WMD also learns a weighting of words, which is specific to the document application. This weighting consists of re-weighting the frequency of each word by a learned weight and re-normalizing the word frequencies.

The paper presents an efficient optimization method to learn the proposed parameterized metric. A thorough experimental evaluation shows the effectiveness of S-WMD by comparing it to a large number of reasonable baselines on a number of document classification datasets using a kNN classifier based on the proposed learned metric.

The main strengths of this paper are its technical contributions, which extend the recent WMD in a useful and effective way, and its thorough experimental evaluation. The paper is very well written and a pleasure to read.

However, the potential impact of this paper may be limited by the fact that the earth-movers distance with a metric learning via a "word centroid distance" initialization performs similarly to the full learning of the proposed distance. Additionally, it would be useful to include an evaluation of the "word centroid embedding" (weighted mean of word embeddings for the document) with all tested existing metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA) in Table 3. Furthermore, an assessment of the relative importance of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A would be beneficial. It would be interesting to see performance with each one of these components being active in isolation.

Overall, the originality of this paper is mostly related to the way to optimize the metric, and the idea of a linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance is very interesting but a limited contribution in itself.
