The authors propose a novel method to learn the parameters of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which is essentially the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) computed on text documents using word embeddings to calculate bin-to-bin dissimilarities. They parameterize the EMD cost (i.e., ground metric) matrix using a Mahalanobis distance between vector representations. Additionally, they propose learning histogram coefficients reweighting (as seen in Lebanon's work, and discussed below). The authors provide the gradients of NCA-type costs for this problem and present compelling experimental evidence. Overall, the paper reads like a nice combination of existing techniques, yielding very convincing experimental results. I would support acceptance.

The strengths of the paper lie in its simplicity and relatively straightforward idea, although the implementation and testing are not trivial. Consequently, the experimental section is a strong aspect of this paper. To improve the paper, the authors should better handle the interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations, be more rigorous with their mathematical derivations (computations and notations are somewhat sloppy), and ideally provide an algorithmic box to clearly illustrate their proposal.

A few minor comments:

* In Eq. 1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used as a cost, whereas in Eq. 6, for the purpose of Mahalanobis metric learning, this cost becomes the squared Euclidean metric (and thus what is usually referred to as 2-Wasserstein). To avoid such inconsistencies, it is probably easier to simply state that WMD is p-Wasserstein between clouds of word embeddings, regardless of the value of p.
* The "reweighting" approach proposed in Eq. 5 is not new, as it was already proposed by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification ("metric learning for text documents"). See also "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations" by Le/Cuturi.
* Are there any experimental insights on the importance of the weight vector w in the learned metric? Does this innovation matter, and is it relevant in any way?
* In the related work section, it would be interesting to more clearly underline the differences with previous work. For instance, this paper clearly builds upon the work of Cuturi/Avis, but uses entropy regularization, which clearly speeds up and scales up applications. The cost is also different: while discrete in that work, it is now continuous but parameterized with linear maps. These two arguments are missing. This paper also draws from Wang et al, but that paper is not publishable as it is. The OT optimization is also run only once in their paper, and clearly does not work as proposed.

The paper's clarity and flow are good, and the experimental results are well-presented. However, addressing the above points will further strengthen the paper.
