

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method to learn a document distance metric that builds upon the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) framework proposed by Kusner et al. (2015). While WMD leverages word embeddings to compute document distances through optimal transport between word distributions, S-WMD extends this by incorporating supervision to better align document distances with semantic class labels. The authors propose learning both a linear transformation of word embeddings and a word importance weight vector to minimize leave-one-out classification error in a kNN setting. The approach efficiently approximates the computationally intensive WMD gradient using the Sinkhorn distance, making the method practical for real-world applications.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality, with a clear theoretical foundation connecting to existing metric learning frameworks like Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). The authors provide a thorough literature review that properly contextualizes their work within NIPS proceedings and related literature, showing a solid understanding of the field. The method is well-motivated as an extension of unsupervised WMD to supervised settings, addressing a recognized limitation in the original WMD approach.

The paper excels in clarity. The mathematical formulation is well-organized, with careful explanations of the optimal transport problem, regularization, and gradient computation. The experimental section is detailed and well-structured, presenting results across eight diverse text classification datasets with comprehensive comparisons against 26 baselines. The visualization of document embeddings using t-SNE effectively demonstrates the improved class separation achieved by S-WMD compared to unsupervised baselines.

Regarding originality, S-WMD represents a meaningful extension of WMD to supervised settings by incorporating two key components: a linear transformation of the embedding space and word-specific importance weights. While the underlying metric learning framework is inspired by NCA, the application to the WMD framework with the Sinkhorn approximation is novel and well-executed. The authors properly distinguish their work from related approaches, particularly noting how their method differs from Wang et al.'s (2016) work on EMD metric learning by incorporating histogram reweighting.

The significance of this work is somewhat limited, however. While S-WMD consistently outperforms 26 baselines across eight datasets, the improvements are generally modest (typically 1-5% error reduction), and the paper does not clearly articulate why these improvements matter for practical applications. The authors claim that S-WMD "may well be the best performing distance metric for documents to date," but this is not convincingly demonstrated against the most recent state-of-the-art approaches. The significance would be stronger if the authors could show how S-WMD enables new applications or provides fundamental insights beyond incremental performance gains.

The paper has several specific weaknesses that need addressing. First, the motivation for the word importance weight vector w is underdeveloped. The current explanation suggests w reweights word frequencies, but it's unclear why this wouldn't fundamentally alter the document representation (e.g., by removing or emphasizing words that don't belong to the document). The authors should clarify how w preserves the document's semantic content while adjusting word importance.

Second, the paper doesn't adequately address how the method handles new, unseen documents. The training process learns w for the training set, but how should one determine w for a new document? The authors should specify whether w is learned per-document or if a global w is used, and how this affects generalization.

Third, the abstract states that the method "captures semantic differences encoded in labels," but this phrasing is misleading. The method captures label-based dissimilarity, which may not correspond to semantic differences (e.g., documents about "dogs" vs. "cats" might have very different labels but semantic similarities). The authors should clarify whether "semantic difference" here refers specifically to label-defined differences.

Strengths: Well-structured paper with clear mathematical formulation, thorough experimental evaluation across diverse datasets, effective use of Sinkhorn approximation to make the method practical, strong connection to existing metric learning frameworks.

Weaknesses: Limited significance justification (incremental improvements rather than transformative insights), underdeveloped motivation for word importance weights, insufficient explanation for handling new documents, ambiguous use of "semantic difference."

Pro acceptance: The paper makes a meaningful contribution to extending WMD to supervised settings with a technically sound approach that demonstrates consistent improvements over strong baselines. The method is well-motivated, clearly presented, and addresses a genuine limitation of the original WMD.

Con against acceptance: The significance of the contribution is marginal, with only modest performance gains over existing methods. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why these improvements matter for real-world applications or advance the field beyond incremental metric learning.

Overall, while the paper is technically sound and well-written, the significance of the contribution is limited. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the questions about word importance weights, handling new documents, and clarifying the "semantic difference" terminology in the revision. The method is a solid extension of WMD that would be valuable to the NIPS community, particularly for those working on document representation and metric learning.
