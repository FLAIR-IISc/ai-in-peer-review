The authors analyze the problem of classifying text into categories. They extend a previous work that suggested monitoring word-specific weights using the Word Movers Distance (Wassermann). This contribution suggests using the Synchhorn Distance as a proxy to improve the speed of Wassermann distances, and shows the best average performance against 26 baselines on 8 datasets. It is a very well-written paper that presents the subject well and analyzes the mathematics behind the problem in depth but with great clarity. The only weaknesses, perhaps, are limited novelty compared to the previous Word Movers Distance paper, and the reference to the World centroid distance seems not relevant but rather confusing. I feel that the lambda (regularizing factor) is poorly discussed. In my experience, the value of lambda has a very large impact, sometimes it is the dominant factor in equation (2). Theoretically, given a sufficiently large lambda at the expense of slower convergence, the results should be similar to the pure Wassermann distance, but most people using the Synchhorn use a very small lambda to improve the convergence rate, which achieves a behavior clearly different from Wassermann. Therefore, I am curious whether, in this contribution, lambda dictates a pure Wassermann behavior or a Synchhorn (entropic) behavior.
