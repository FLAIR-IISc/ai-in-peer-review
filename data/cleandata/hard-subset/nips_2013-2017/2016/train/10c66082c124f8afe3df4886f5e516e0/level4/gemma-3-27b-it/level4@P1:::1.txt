This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach built on top of the recent "word-movers distance" [19]—a transport distance applied to word2vec word embeddings. The proposed approach can be applied to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances after linear projection. Beyond learning the linear projection to maximize performance, the method also learns a weighting of words, specific to the document application. This involves re-weighting the frequency of each word by a learned weight and then re-normalizing the word frequencies. The paper presents an efficient optimization method to learn this parameterized metric. A thorough experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach by comparing it to a large number of reasonable baselines on several document classification datasets using a kNN classifier based on the learned metric. 

**Strengths:** This work extends the recent word-movers distance, representing a technically interesting and useful extension, as evidenced by its effectiveness on multiple kNN text classification tasks.

**Weaknesses:** The potential impact may be limited by the fact that the earth-movers distance with a metric learning initialization using a "word centroid distance" performs similarly to the full learning process of the proposed distance. Specifically, the difference between the last two rows in Table 3 is small. In other words, the core technical contributions of this paper may not substantially outperform this initialization method, which is based on existing techniques. 

Furthermore, the evaluation lacks a comparison of the "word centroid embedding" (weighted mean of word embeddings for the document) with all tested existing metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA). It would also be insightful to assess the relative importance of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A, perhaps by showing performance with each component activated in isolation.

**Originality:** The originality primarily lies in the optimization method (Lines 156–175). While the idea of linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance is interesting, it represents a relatively limited contribution in itself.

**Significance:** The results are strong and demonstrate consistent improvements over a broad range of baselines. The proposed method addresses a key limitation of the original WMD—its lack of task-specific supervision—and offers a practical approach to incorporating this information.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well written and a pleasure to read. However, a few minor clarifications would improve it further:

*   Line 152: "The authors": of which paper? [1,7,8]?
*   Equation 11: shouldn’t α and β have asterisks here?
*   It would be helpful to explicitly recall around line 170 that this is the Sinkhorn scaling referred to in the introduction (Line 56).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results consistently outperform numerous baselines.
*   Addresses a clear limitation of the original Word Mover's Distance.
*   The proposed optimization method is efficient and practical.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The performance gain over a strong initialization (S-WCD) is sometimes modest.
*   A more complete ablation study (evaluating the contribution of each component) would be beneficial.
*   The lack of evaluation of existing metric learning approaches directly on the word centroid embedding is a minor weakness.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of document similarity and metric learning. The minor weaknesses identified do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.
