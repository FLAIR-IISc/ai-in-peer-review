This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised document metric. The work builds directly upon the recently introduced Word Mover's Distance (WMD) [19], which leverages word embeddings (e.g., word2vec) to compute the distance between two documents as an optimal transport problem. While WMD is entirely unsupervised, S-WMD incorporates label information to learn a task-specific distance. The proposed method learns two key components: (1) an affine transformation of the word embedding space to adjust word representations, and (2) a word-importance vector to re-weigh word frequencies based on their relevance to the classification task.

A central technical challenge is that differentiating through the WMD objective is computationally prohibitive and can involve non-unique subgradients. The authors overcome this by replacing the exact transport problem with a differentiable, entropy-regularized approximation (the Sinkhorn distance), which leads to a practical and efficient update rule. The model is trained to minimize a stochastic version of the leave-one-out kNN classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation on eight text classification datasets, comparing S-WMD against 26 baselines and demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important problem: how to incorporate supervision into the powerful WMD framework. Extending WMD, a highly successful unsupervised metric, to the supervised setting is a logical and valuable contribution. The proposed approach of jointly learning a linear transformation of the embedding space and a word-weighting scheme is novel and tailored specifically to the document context.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The method for making the learning problem tractable is a key strength. Approximating the WMD gradient using the Sinkhorn distance is a clever solution that turns a computationally prohibitive nested optimization into an efficient learning algorithm. The initialization strategy, which uses a faster "Word Centroid Distance" (WCD) with NCA, is also sensible and effective.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and a pleasure to read. The motivation is clear, the background is explained thoroughly, and the proposed method is presented in a logical and understandable manner.

4.  **Experimental Rigor:** The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation. Comparing against 26 competitive baselines across eight diverse datasets provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD. The consistent top performance across most datasets robustly supports the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Marginal Gain Over Initialization:** A primary concern is that the performance gain of the full S-WMD method over its simpler initialization scheme ("S-WMD init.", which is S-WCD) appears to be marginal. As shown in Table 2, the initialization method already achieves most of the performance improvement. This raises questions about the practical significance of the paper's main technical contribution—the complex optimization of the full S-WMD objective—if a much simpler method performs almost as well.

2.  **Lack of Ablation Study:** The model learns two distinct components: the projection matrix `A` and the word-weighting vector `w`. The paper does not include an ablation study to analyze the relative importance of each component. It would be highly beneficial to see results from models trained with only `A` (i.e., `w` fixed to ones) and only `w` (i.e., `A` fixed to identity). This analysis is crucial for understanding the sources of performance gain and whether both components are necessary.

3.  **Missing Baselines:** The authors apply NCA to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) to initialize their method. However, the evaluation is missing a comparison of the WCD representation with other standard metric learning algorithms like ITML and LMNN. Including these baselines would provide a more complete assessment of whether the proposed S-WMD formulation is superior to simply applying off-the-shelf metric learning techniques to the WCD document representation.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is of high quality, but the following additions would strengthen it significantly:

*   **Ablation Study:** I strongly recommend adding an ablation study to evaluate the independent contributions of the learned matrix `A` and the word weights `w`.
*   **Expanded Baselines:** Including ITML and LMNN applied to the WCD vectors would make the experimental comparison more comprehensive.
*   The writing is excellent, but a few minor clarifications could be made:
    *   In Section 3.1 (around line 152), the reference for the subgradient formulation in Eq. (8) is not specified. Please clarify which paper this is from.
    *   In Eq. (10), it would improve clarity to consistently use `α*` and `β*` to denote the optimal dual variables, as is done later in the text.
    *   When describing the fast gradient computation, it would be helpful to explicitly state that the alternating fixed-point algorithm is Sinkhorn scaling, better connecting this section to the background provided earlier.

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound method for supervised document metric learning. The writing is clear and the experimental results are compelling. The main weaknesses are the seemingly small improvement over a strong initialization baseline and the absence of an ablation study. Despite these points, the work is a solid contribution to the field and is likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.
