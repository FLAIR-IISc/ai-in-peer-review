This paper presents a supervised learning approach to refine the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), a document distance metric based on optimal transport of word embeddings. WMD, building on the Earth Mover’s Distance, elegantly leverages pre-trained word embeddings to quantify document similarity. The authors address a key limitation of WMD – its unsupervised nature – by introducing a method to learn both a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a word importance weighting vector. The goal is to minimize k-nearest neighbor classification error, effectively making documents with similar labels closer and those with differing labels further apart. 

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, providing a comprehensive overview of related work in document distance metrics, metric learning, and optimal transport. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the existing literature, appropriately citing relevant publications (e.g., [19, 2, 11, 15, 36]). The experimental evaluation is extensive, encompassing eight datasets and comparisons against a substantial 26 baseline methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a relevant problem:** Extending WMD to a supervised setting is a logical and valuable step, potentially improving performance in downstream tasks like document classification.
* **Solid technical foundation:** The proposed approach leverages established techniques – optimal transport, metric learning with NCA – in a novel combination. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to approximate the WMD gradient is a clever optimization.
* **Comprehensive evaluation:** The extensive experimental results, including comparisons to numerous baselines, provide strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness.  The reported improvements across multiple datasets are significant.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is easy to follow, with a logical flow of ideas and well-defined sections. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Motivation for word reweighting:** The rationale behind learning a word importance vector `w` could be more thoroughly explained. While the authors state it aims to emphasize useful words, the potential for altering document meaning through reweighting warrants further discussion.  It’s unclear *why* modifying the document distribution in this way is beneficial, and under what conditions it might be detrimental.
* **Incremental contribution:** While technically sound, the core idea – applying supervised metric learning (specifically NCA) to WMD – feels somewhat incremental. The combination is natural, but doesn't represent a paradigm shift.  The authors could better highlight the unique challenges addressed in applying NCA to the WMD framework, and how their approximations overcome these challenges.
* **Handling of new documents:** The paper doesn't fully address how the learned word weighting vector `w` would be applied to entirely new documents not seen during training.  The process for determining appropriate weights for novel vocabulary or document distributions needs clarification.
* **Semantic difference clarification:** The use of the term "semantic difference" in the abstract is vague.  Expanding on what constitutes a semantic difference – beyond simply the document label – would strengthen the paper’s framing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and well-executed improvement to a recent and impactful document distance metric (WMD).
* The extensive experimental validation demonstrates consistently strong performance against a wide range of baselines.
* The paper is clearly written and provides a valuable contribution to the field of document similarity and metric learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The core contribution feels somewhat incremental, and the motivation for certain design choices (e.g., word reweighting) is not fully convincing.
* The paper lacks a detailed discussion on applying the learned model to unseen documents.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution that warrants acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, clarifying the motivation for word reweighting, elaborating on the handling of new documents, and articulating the novelty of their approach beyond the straightforward application of NCA would significantly strengthen the paper.  While the contribution may not be groundbreaking, the practical improvements demonstrated through rigorous experimentation are noteworthy.
