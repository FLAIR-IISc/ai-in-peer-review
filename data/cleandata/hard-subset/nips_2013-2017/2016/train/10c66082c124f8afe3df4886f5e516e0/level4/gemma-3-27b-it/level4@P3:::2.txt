This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), an extension to the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervised learning to refine the document distance metric. The authors address a key limitation of WMD – its unsupervised nature – by learning both a transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights, optimized to minimize leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. A crucial contribution is an efficient approximation to the gradient calculation, enabling practical training despite the computational complexity of the underlying optimal transport problem. The paper includes a comprehensive experimental evaluation on eight datasets, consistently demonstrating significant performance improvements over a large number (26) of competitive baselines.

The paper builds upon the growing body of work leveraging word embeddings for document analysis, particularly Kusner et al.’s [19] WMD, which frames document distance as an optimal transport problem. It also connects to the broader field of metric learning [10, 15, 36, 37], acknowledging the scalability challenges of many existing methods and positioning S-WMD as a solution that operates in the lower-dimensional word embedding space. The authors appropriately reference relevant work on efficient optimal transport computation [6, 7, 8] and related approaches to learning ground distances [34, 35].

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically well-motivated and clearly explained. The use of the Sinkhorn distance [6] to approximate the WMD gradient is a clever and crucial optimization, making the approach practical. The derivation of the gradient with respect to both the transformation matrix and word weights appears correct and is thoroughly detailed.
*   **Extensive Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength. Testing against 26 baselines across 8 diverse datasets provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD. The ablation study with S-WMD initialization further clarifies the contribution of the full training procedure.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors effectively explain complex concepts and provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The inclusion of pseudocode for the algorithm is helpful.
*   **Originality:** While building on existing work, the combination of supervised metric learning with the WMD framework, along with the efficient gradient approximation, represents a novel contribution.  The introduction of word importance weighting further distinguishes this work.
*   **Significance:** The consistent and substantial performance improvements over strong baselines suggest that S-WMD is a significant advance in document distance metrics. The potential impact is high, as improved document distance metrics benefit many downstream applications in information retrieval and text mining.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** Although addressed with approximations, the computational complexity remains a concern, particularly for very large datasets. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the scalability limitations and potential avenues for further optimization.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The extensive tuning with Bayesian optimization hints at potential sensitivity to hyperparameters. A discussion of the robustness of the method to hyperparameter choices would be valuable.
*  **Limited Error Analysis:** While results are reported, the error analysis is limited. Providing insights into *where* S-WMD succeeds and fails (e.g., specific document types, label ambiguities) would further strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong technical foundation and novelty.
*   Compelling experimental results demonstrating significant performance improvements.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Addresses a meaningful problem in document analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity may limit applicability to very large-scale datasets.
*   Potential hyperparameter sensitivity requires careful tuning.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper with a strong technical contribution and a comprehensive experimental evaluation. The authors have successfully addressed a key limitation of the WMD and demonstrated that supervised learning can significantly improve document distance metrics. The strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS 2016.  The work is likely to be influential in the field of text analysis and information retrieval.
