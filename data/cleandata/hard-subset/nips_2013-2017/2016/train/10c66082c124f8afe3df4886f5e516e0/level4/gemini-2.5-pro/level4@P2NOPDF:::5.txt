This paper addresses the problem of text classification by proposing a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn word-specific weights within the WMD framework, allowing the model to prioritize important words for classification. To mitigate the high computational cost of the exact Wasserstein distance used in WMD, the authors employ the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient proxy. The proposed method is empirically evaluated on eight benchmark datasets against a comprehensive set of 26 baselines, demonstrating state-of-the-art average performance.

**Strengths:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written. The authors do an excellent job of introducing the topic, motivating their approach, and situating it within the relevant literature. The mathematical derivations are presented with a commendable balance of depth and clarity, making the technical contributions easy to follow and appreciate. The extensive empirical evaluation is another significant strength, providing strong evidence for the method's effectiveness across a variety of text classification tasks.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

Despite the paper's strengths, there are a few areas that could be improved.

1.  **Novelty:** The primary contribution is a supervised adaptation of the existing Word Mover's Distance. While this extension is valuable and empirically successful, the conceptual leap from the original WMD paper could be viewed as incremental.

2.  **Clarity of Baselines:** The comparison to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) felt somewhat distracting. It wasn't immediately clear why this specific baseline was chosen for detailed comparison or what unique insights this particular comparison was intended to provide. A clearer justification for its inclusion would be helpful.

3.  **Discussion of the Regularization Parameter (lambda):** My main concern is the limited discussion of the entropic regularization parameter, lambda, used in the Sinkhorn distance calculation (Equation 2). The choice of lambda is critical, as it governs the trade-off between computational speed and fidelity to the true Wasserstein distance. In practice, the value of lambda can have a profound impact on the resulting distance metric, sometimes becoming the dominant factor in the optimization.

    Theoretically, as lambda increases, the Sinkhorn distance converges to the Wasserstein distance, but this comes at the cost of slower convergence. Conversely, small values of lambda, which are often used to accelerate computation, result in a heavily regularized (entropic) transport plan that can behave quite differently from the sparse, unregularized Wasserstein solution.

    The paper would be substantially strengthened by a more detailed analysis of this parameter. The authors should clarify what value(s) of lambda were used in the experiments and provide a justification for this choice. Does this value place the method closer to the "true" Wasserstein regime (emulating the original WMD), or does it operate in a highly entropic regime where the behavior is fundamentally different? An ablation study or sensitivity analysis showing the impact of lambda on both classification performance and convergence speed would provide crucial insight into the practical behavior and characteristics of the proposed method. This would help readers understand whether the model's success stems from a more efficient approximation of WMD or from the distinct properties introduced by the entropic regularization itself.
