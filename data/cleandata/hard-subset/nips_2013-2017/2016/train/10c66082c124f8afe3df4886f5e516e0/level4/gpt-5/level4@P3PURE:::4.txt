This paper proposes a supervised approach to learning document distances on top of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD, motivated by Earth Mover’s Distance, computes optimal transport from one distribution of words to another to obtain a document–document distance, with word2vec-based distances as the ground cost between words. The authors extend this by learning (1) a linear transformation of word embeddings and (2) a reweighting of the word distribution, aiming to better separate documents with different labels. The paper is clearly written and easy to follow, includes a thorough and accurate review of related work, and demonstrates results on many novel datasets.

That said, the motivation for the specific formulation needs more explanation, and the overall significance appears limited.

1) Motivation for reweighting w: The paper reweights the word distribution using w, but the rationale for doing so is not well motivated. This reweighting could potentially turn a document into a substantially different one by altering its empirical word distribution. Please justify why this reweighting is appropriate in a supervised WMD setting and explain how it avoids changing a document into a different document. Clear constraints or interpretability of w (e.g., nonnegativity, normalization, sparsity, global vs. document-specific) would help substantiate this choice.

2) Significance: The contribution seems incremental. Combining a linear transformation A and a reweighting w with an NCA-style objective to supervise WMD is a reasonable extension, but the formulation does not appear groundbreaking or conceptually novel.

3) Generalization to new documents: How is the distance computed for a previously unseen document? In particular, how is the reweighting w obtained for a new document at test time? If w is document-specific, what inference procedure is used? If w is global, how is it learned and applied without overfitting? Please clarify the training–testing protocol.

4) Clarification of “semantic difference”: In the abstract, “semantic difference” appears to correspond to label differences. However, authorship differences or topic differences are not necessarily semantic differences per se. What exactly is meant by “semantic difference” in this context?

Overall, the paper is well written and shows solid understanding of the literature, with comprehensive experiments. I do not object to acceptance provided the above questions are addressed satisfactorily, but as it stands the motivation requires stronger justification and the significance seems modest.
