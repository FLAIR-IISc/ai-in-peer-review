Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised metric learning framework built on the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) of Kusner et al. WMD measures document similarity as an optimal transport cost between normalized bags of words using pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec). S-WMD augments WMD by learning (i) a linear transformation A applied to the embedding space (a generalized Euclidean ground metric) and (ii) a word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms prior to normalization. The metric is trained to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification objective, following the NCA formulation. Direct differentiation through the WMD LP is impractical, so the authors use an entropy-regularized approximation (Sinkhorn distance) to obtain efficient gradients, and they further accelerate training via batch sampling and neighborhood pruning guided by the Word Centroid Distance (WCD). An S-WCD initialization (NCA learned on word centroids) provides a strong starting point.

This work sits at the intersection of metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and optimal transport (EMD/Wasserstein; Cuturi’s Sinkhorn distances). It extends prior WMD-based kNN classification by incorporating supervision and efficient optimization, while remaining in the low-dimensional word-embedding space.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation: learn a task-specific transport ground metric and word weights to align document distances with labels.
- Sound and efficient optimization: entropy-regularized transport (Sinkhorn) yields tractable gradients; neighborhood filtering and batching make training practical.
- Strong empirical study: eight datasets and 26 competitive baselines, with consistent improvements in many cases; code availability enhances reproducibility.
- Good writing and presentation: the method is explained clearly, with helpful connections to NCA and Sinkhorn.

Weaknesses and missing analyses
- Gains over initialization: On several datasets, the learned S-WMD only modestly improves over the S-WMD initialization (S-WCD with NCA). This raises questions about how much the full transport-based learning, including w and the A-updates under the transport objective, contributes beyond a high-quality centroid-based metric.
- Baseline coverage: The evaluation includes S-WCD (NCA on centroids), but omits ITML- and LMNN-trained metrics on centroid features. Given those methods are included for other representations, including them on the centroid representation would complete the comparison.
- Ablation of components: There is no ablation isolating the effect of learning A alone (no w) versus w alone (fixed A). Such analysis would clarify where the improvements come from and when each component matters (e.g., long vs. short documents).
- Sensitivity/robustness: Little discussion of sensitivity to λ in the Sinkhorn approximation, choice of p in the ground distance, or the neighborhood truncation M. Brief robustness checks would strengthen the claims.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: The approach is technically sound, and the approximations are standard and well motivated. The derivations rely on the relaxed (strongly convex) transport problem to provide unique solutions for gradient computation. More ablations and additional centroid-based baselines would better substantiate the specific contributions of w and A beyond initialization.
- Clarity: The paper is well organized and generally easy to follow. Minor notation and referencing issues are noted below.
- Originality: Learning a linear ground metric for Wasserstein/EMD is not entirely new, but this paper’s combination—learning A and w for WMD on text, trained with an NCA-style objective and accelerated via Sinkhorn and neighborhood filtering—is a useful and novel synthesis in this domain.
- Significance: Practical significance is promising: a supervised extension of WMD that often improves kNN classification while remaining efficient. The impact would be higher if improvements over the S-WCD initialization were consistently substantial and if component-wise contributions were clearly established.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and practically effective supervised extension of WMD.
- Efficient training via Sinkhorn and batching; scalable to realistic datasets.
- Strong empirical results across diverse text classification tasks, with public code.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited improvement over a strong centroid-based initialization on several datasets.
- Missing baselines (ITML/LMNN on centroids) and ablations (A vs. w).
- Minor clarity/notation issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add centroid-based baselines with ITML and LMNN (in addition to NCA) to fairly assess supervised learning on word centroids.
- Provide ablations: (i) learn A only (w fixed to 1), (ii) learn w only (A fixed), (iii) full model. Report across datasets with different average document lengths.
- Report sensitivity to λ (and possibly p = 1 vs. p = 2) and the neighbor truncation M to demonstrate robustness and justify chosen defaults.
- Analyze cases (e.g., BBCSPORT, OHSUMED) where TF-IDF+NCA outperforms S-WMD; discuss whether document length or IDF effects drive this gap.
- Minor clarifications:
  - Specify the antecedent of “the authors” around the discussion of uniqueness/subgradients (currently ambiguous).
  - In the gradient section, ensure α and β are marked as α*, β* (or α*λ, β*λ) when referring to the relaxed dual variables used in Eq. (10) and implementation.
  - Explicitly name and cite Sinkhorn scaling at the point where u and v are introduced, to connect to the earlier introduction.

Overall recommendation
A solid, well-executed supervised extension of WMD with strong empirical results. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on adding the missing centroid-based baselines and ablations to clarify the incremental value of the full transport-based learning over the strong S-WCD initialization.
