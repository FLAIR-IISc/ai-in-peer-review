The authors address the problem of text classification, building upon prior work that leveraged Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) – a proxy for the Wasserstein distance – by introducing a supervised learning approach to refine word-specific weights. Their key contribution is the use of the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient approximation of the Wasserstein distance, and they demonstrate strong performance on eight datasets, achieving state-of-the-art results when compared to 26 baselines. This is a very well-written paper that clearly presents the topic and provides an in-depth, yet accessible, analysis of the underlying mathematics. 

The main weaknesses lie in the incremental novelty relative to the original Word Mover’s Distance paper, and the inclusion of comparisons to World Centroid Distance, which I found unhelpful and somewhat distracting. Furthermore, the discussion of lambda (the regularization parameter) feels insufficient. Based on my experience, the value of lambda can significantly impact performance, often becoming the dominant factor in equation (2). While theoretically, results should converge to those of a pure Wasserstein distance with a sufficiently large lambda – albeit at the cost of slower convergence – practitioners typically employ small lambda values to accelerate convergence. This, however, leads to behavior distinctly different from that of the Wasserstein distance. Therefore, I am curious whether, in this work, lambda encourages behavior consistent with a pure Wasserstein distance or with the entropic regularization inherent in the Sinkhorn distance.
