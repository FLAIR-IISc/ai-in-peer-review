This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised document distance metric. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which has shown state-of-the-art results in unsupervised document classification. WMD leverages pre-trained word embeddings (like word2vec) and formulates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost required to "transport" the words of one document to become the words of the other, where cost is measured by Euclidean distance in the embedding space.

The key contribution of this paper is to extend the unsupervised WMD framework to a supervised setting, where document labels are available. The authors propose learning two components to tailor the distance metric to a specific classification task: (1) an affine transformation `A` of the word embedding space, and (2) a word-importance vector `w` that re-weighs the normalized bag-of-words histograms. The learning objective is to minimize a stochastic version of the leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor (kNN) classification error, drawing inspiration from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A significant technical challenge is that computing the gradient of the WMD objective is computationally prohibitive and involves a nested optimization problem. The authors cleverly circumvent this by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized approximation (the Sinkhorn distance), which is differentiable and can be computed much more efficiently. This approximation makes the end-to-end training of S-WMD practical.

### Strengths:

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of the original WMD—its inability to incorporate supervision. The proposed method of learning both a transformation of the embedding space and word-specific importance weights is a novel and powerful way to adapt the metric to a specific task. Given the strong performance of WMD, a supervised extension that improves upon it is a significant contribution to the field of text classification and metric learning.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The approach is technically sound. The main technical contribution—using the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance to create a differentiable and efficient approximation for the WMD gradient—is an elegant solution to a difficult problem. The proposed optimization procedure, including a sensible initialization strategy using a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD), is well-thought-out and practical.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive. S-WMD is benchmarked on eight diverse real-world datasets against a very large set of 26 competitive baselines, including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with several metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN). The results are compelling, showing that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance on the majority of the datasets. The inclusion of visualizations and training time analysis further strengthens the experimental section.

4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors state that the code will be made publicly available, which is commendable and will be a great benefit to the research community.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:

1.  **Ablation Study:** The method introduces two learnable components: the linear transformation `A` and the word importance weights `w`. A key missing piece in the analysis is an ablation study to understand the relative contribution of each component. It would be highly informative to see results for a version that learns only `A` (with `w` fixed to ones) and a version that learns only `w` (with `A` fixed to the identity matrix). This would provide valuable insight into which component is more critical for the performance gains and on which types of datasets.

2.  **Intuition behind Word Weighting:** While the use of word importance weights is intuitive, the specific formulation in Equation (4), where the weights are applied via a Hadamard product to the normalized bag-of-words vector followed by re-normalization, could be better motivated. A brief discussion on why this particular formulation was chosen over other alternatives (e.g., incorporating `w` directly into the ground cost of the transport problem) would enhance the paper's clarity.

3.  **Comparison to Standard Classifiers:** While the paper's focus is on learning a distance metric for kNN classification, the ultimate goal is classification. To better contextualize the performance, it would be interesting to include a comparison against standard, non-metric-learning-based classifiers, such as a Linear SVM on TF-IDF features, which often serves as a very strong baseline in text classification. This would help gauge how S-WMD + kNN compares to other dominant classification paradigms.

### Minor Comments and Questions:

*   **Clarity on `lambda`:** In Section 2, `lambda` is introduced as a regularization parameter for the Sinkhorn distance (Equation 2). It appears again on line 170 in the context of deriving the dual variables. It would be helpful to explicitly state that this is the same parameter and briefly comment on its role in the dual formulation for readers less familiar with this area.
*   **Justification of WMD Properties:** The conclusion states that WMD is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words." This property is inherited from the base word embeddings. It would strengthen the claim to briefly show or argue how the learned transformation `A` preserves or enhances this desirable property.
*   **Table and Text Inconsistencies:**
    *   Line 257: The text states, "on the right we also show the average error across datasets." However, the rightmost column of Table 2 is labeled "Avg Rank." Please correct this for consistency.
    *   Table 2: The column header "ne" for the 20NEWS dataset appears to be a typo for "n=".
*   **Experimental Details:** The value of `k` used for the kNN classification experiments is not specified. Was `k` tuned as a hyperparameter for each method/dataset, or was a fixed value used? This is an important detail for reproducibility.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and effective method for supervised document classification. The technical approach is clever, and the experimental results are strong and convincing. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to providing deeper analysis and clarifying some details. I recommend acceptance.
