### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach extends the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which frames the distance between two documents as an optimal transport problem between their respective word embeddings. While WMD is entirely unsupervised, S-WMD incorporates supervision by learning two key components: 1) an affine transformation of the word embedding space, which corresponds to learning a Mahalanobis distance, and 2) a word-importance vector that re-weights word frequencies in the document's bag-of-words representation.

The authors formulate the learning problem as the minimization of a stochastic leave-one-out k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) classification error, adopting a loss function similar to that of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A key technical contribution is an efficient method for computing the gradient of this loss function. A direct gradient computation would be intractable due to a nested optimization problem within the WMD calculation. To overcome this, the authors leverage an entropy-regularized version of the optimal transport problem (the Sinkhorn distance), which provides a differentiable and computationally efficient approximation. The paper presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation on eight text classification datasets, demonstrating that S-WMD consistently outperforms a large suite of 26 competitive baselines, including unsupervised WMD and various document representation methods combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

### **Strengths of the Paper**

1.  **Significance and Strong Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this work lies in its impressive empirical performance. S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art kNN classification error on a majority of the eight benchmark datasets, significantly outperforming strong baselines like WMD, LSI, and TF-IDF combined with metric learning. The thoroughness of the experimental section, comparing against 26 methods, provides convincing evidence of the proposed method's effectiveness and its potential to become a leading document distance metric.

2.  **Novel and Effective Synthesis of Ideas:** The paper presents a clever and well-motivated combination of several powerful concepts: the geometric intuition of WMD, the supervised framework of metric learning (specifically NCA), and the computational efficiency of regularized optimal transport. While the individual components are not new, their synthesis into a coherent and high-performing model for supervised document classification is a notable contribution.

3.  **Technical Contribution for Tractability:** The authors successfully address the significant computational challenge of differentiating through the WMD calculation. The use of the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable proxy for the true Wasserstein distance is a key technical insight that makes the entire framework practical. This, combined with batching and nearest-neighbor filtering, results in an efficient training algorithm.

4.  **Clarity of Motivation:** The motivation for the work is clear and compelling. Moving beyond unsupervised distances to learn task-specific metrics that can adapt the underlying word embedding space and emphasize important words is an intuitive and powerful idea.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is strong overall, there are several areas where clarity and rigor could be improved.

1.  **Mathematical and Notational Precision:**
    *   There is a slight inconsistency in the definition of the ground metric. Equation (1) for WMD uses the standard Euclidean distance, ||xi − xj||, implying a 2-Wasserstein distance. However, the supervised loss in Equation (6) is based on the squared Euclidean distance in the transformed space, ||A(xi − xj)||^2. For clarity, it would be better to frame WMD generally as the p-Wasserstein distance and explicitly state that p=2 is used for the ground metric, which then becomes squared for the supervised formulation.
    *   The authors could better articulate the relationship between the original WMD formulation and the entropy-regularized approximation used for gradient calculation. A brief discussion of the trade-offs (speed vs. approximation accuracy) and the role of the hyperparameter λ would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Positioning with Respect to Prior Work:**
    *   The idea of re-weighting histogram bins (Equation 4) has been explored before in text classification, notably by Lebanon ("Metric Learning for Text Documents"). Acknowledging this and other related works (e.g., Le & Cuturi's work on Riemannian metric learning for histograms) would more accurately position the paper's contribution as the effective integration of this technique within the S-WMD framework, rather than a completely novel component.
    *   The related work section could more clearly differentiate S-WMD from previous attempts to learn the EMD ground metric. For example, the paper builds on ideas from Cuturi & Avis, but a key difference is the move from a discrete, fully parameterized ground metric to one parameterized by a continuous linear map. Similarly, compared to Wang et al., the proposed method's use of iterative gradient descent with an efficient regularized solver is a significant improvement over their approach. Explicitly highlighting these distinctions would underscore the novelty of the proposed method.

3.  **Lack of Ablation Study:** The model learns both a matrix `A` and a weight vector `w`. The experimental results do not disentangle the contributions of these two components. An ablation study—for instance, evaluating a version of S-WMD that only learns `A` (with `w` fixed to ones) and one that only learns `w` (with `A` fixed to identity)—would provide valuable insight into which component drives the performance gains. This would help readers understand the importance of the word re-weighting mechanism.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The overall presentation is good, but the addition of a formal algorithm box summarizing the complete S-WMD training procedure (including initialization, batch selection, gradient computation, and updates) would improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility for other researchers.

### **Final Recommendation**

This paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound method for supervised document distance learning. The combination of ideas is elegant, and the proposed solution to the difficult gradient computation problem is practical and effective. Most importantly, the method is validated by extensive experiments that demonstrate state-of-the-art results. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to improving clarity, mathematical precision, and positioning relative to prior work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Achieves state-of-the-art performance on a wide range of text classification tasks.
*   Provides a novel and effective synthesis of WMD, metric learning, and regularized optimal transport.
*   Solves a non-trivial technical challenge (differentiating through WMD) with an efficient approximation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Some components (e.g., histogram re-weighting) are not entirely novel and could be better contextualized.
*   The paper would benefit from increased mathematical precision and an ablation study.

Overall, the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance. The suggested improvements would further strengthen what is already a high-quality contribution to the field.
