Summary:
The paper proposes a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which itself computes document distances via optimal transport over word embeddings. Building on word2vec as the base metric between words, the authors learn (1) a linear transformation of the word embeddings and (2) a reweighting of the word distribution to better separate documents with different labels, optimizing a Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA)-style objective. The manuscript is clearly written, provides a thorough review of related work, and includes experiments on several datasets.

Strengths:
- Clear exposition and comprehensive positioning within the literature on WMD, optimal transport, and supervised metric learning.
- Practical, straightforward formulation that adapts a popular unsupervised distance to supervised settings.
- Empirical evaluation on multiple datasets, demonstrating the feasibility of the approach.

Concerns and questions:
1) Motivation and implications of reweighting the word distribution:
- The rationale for introducing the reweighting vector w is not well justified. As described, reweighting can substantially alter a document’s effective representation and potentially change its semantics.
- Please clarify whether w is global (shared across the vocabulary) or document-specific. If global, explain how it relates to established term-weighting schemes (e.g., IDF, supervised term weighting); if document-specific, explain how its estimation avoids overfitting and preserves the original content.
- What constraints or regularization are imposed on w (e.g., nonnegativity, normalization, sparsity, proximity to IDF) to prevent pathological solutions and maintain semantic fidelity?
- Provide ablations isolating the effect of w versus the linear transformation A to demonstrate that reweighting genuinely helps and does not simply memorize label information.

2) Incremental contribution and significance:
- The combination of a linear embedding transform A and word reweighting w optimized with an NCA objective is a sensible supervised extension, but it appears incremental. The paper would be strengthened by a clearer articulation of novelty relative to:
  - Prior supervised adaptations of WMD or OT-based distances;
  - Classic metric learning methods (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and supervised embedding learning approaches applied to bag-of-words or embeddings;
  - Heuristic improvements to WMD (e.g., IDF-weighted WMD, supervised term weighting).
- Any theoretical insight (e.g., properties of the learned transport metric under A and w, generalization considerations) or stronger empirical evidence (e.g., statistically significant improvements over strong supervised baselines) would help support the significance.

3) Generalization to new documents:
- The paper should clearly explain how distances are computed at test time for unseen documents. If w is global, this is straightforward; if it is document- or pair-specific, how is w inferred for new documents without labels, and at what computational cost?
- Provide a precise inference procedure, including whether additional optimization is needed per test document, and discuss scalability.
- If w depends on labels, how is the method applied in unsupervised retrieval or k-NN classification for new documents?

4) Meaning of “semantic difference”:
- The abstract’s phrase “semantic difference” seems to stand in for label differences (e.g., topic, sentiment, authorship). Please define what is meant by “semantic” in this context and avoid conflating labels that reflect metadata (e.g., authorship) with semantics. If the goal is a task-specific distance, consider using that terminology or clarifying the scope.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include ablations: WMD; WMD+IDF; WMD+A only; WMD+w only; and WMD+A+w. This will clarify where gains come from.
- Report constraints/regularization on A and w, and analyze sensitivity to these choices. For example, bounding deviations of w from IDF or uniform can preserve document identity while allowing supervision to guide emphasis.
- Provide interpretability analyses: examples of learned high-weight and low-weight terms; changes in nearest neighbors before/after learning; and qualitative transport plans to show that documents are not semantically distorted.
- Discuss computational aspects: how gradients are computed through the transport solver; whether entropy-regularized OT (e.g., Sinkhorn) is used; per-iteration complexity; and how training scales with dataset size.
- Strengthen baselines by including supervised metric learning and embedding approaches applied to document representations (e.g., averaged embeddings with learned metric) and known WMD enhancements.
- Clarify evaluation protocols, ensure statistical significance testing, and detail hyperparameter selection to support reproducibility.

Overall assessment:
This is a clearly written and practically motivated supervised extension of WMD that appears to work across several datasets. However, the motivation and safeguards for reweighting w require deeper justification, the generalization procedure for new documents needs to be clearly specified, and the claimed novelty over existing metric learning and WMD variants should be better substantiated. I am open to acceptance if the authors address these concerns with stronger motivation, ablations, clearer test-time procedures, and more compelling comparisons; otherwise, the contribution feels incremental.
