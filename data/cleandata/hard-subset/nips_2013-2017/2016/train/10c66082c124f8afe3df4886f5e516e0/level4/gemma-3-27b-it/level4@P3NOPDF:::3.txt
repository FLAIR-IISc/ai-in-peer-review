## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title - Please Fill In]

This paper presents a novel and effective approach to learning the parameters of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), a metric commonly used for comparing text documents based on word embeddings. The authors parameterize the underlying ground metric of the Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) – upon which WMD is based – using a Mahalanobis distance defined by learned linear transformations of the word embedding vectors. Further, they introduce a learned reweighting scheme for histogram coefficients, building on prior work in metric learning for text. The paper delivers compelling experimental results demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed method, and overall represents a well-executed combination of existing techniques with a significant practical impact. I recommend acceptance.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its simplicity and clarity of idea. While the concept of learning the metric within WMD is not entirely new, the proposed parameterization with a Mahalanobis distance and the associated gradient derivation are significant contributions. Implementing and rigorously evaluating this approach is non-trivial, and the strong experimental validation provided is a major positive aspect of this submission. The paper clearly demonstrates the practical benefit of learning an appropriate metric for WMD.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

While the paper is strong overall, several areas could benefit from further refinement. The interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations of the problem would benefit from clearer explanation. Currently, the discussion feels somewhat fragmented, and a more unified presentation would improve understanding. 

The mathematical presentation also requires attention. While the core ideas are accessible, several notations and computations are presented somewhat sloppily, potentially obscuring the underlying logic. A more rigorous and consistent mathematical treatment would strengthen the paper’s credibility. 

Finally, providing an algorithmic box outlining the key steps of the proposed method would significantly improve clarity. This would allow readers to more easily grasp the practical implementation details and understand the flow of the optimization process.

**Specific Comments:**

* **Wasserstein Distance Consistency:** The use of Euclidean distance in Eq. 1 as a cost function, while transitioning to a squared Euclidean metric (equivalent to the 2-Wasserstein distance) in Eq. 6 for Mahalanobis metric learning, introduces a slight inconsistency. To address this, the authors could explicitly frame WMD as a p-Wasserstein distance between clouds of word embeddings, recognizing that the choice of ‘p’ dictates the specific metric used.

* **Prior Work on Reweighting:** The reweighting approach presented in Eq. 5 is not novel and has been previously proposed by Lebanon in the context of text document classification [cite Lebanon’s work]. Additionally, Le and Cuturi’s work on unsupervised Riemannian metric learning for histograms using Aitchison transformations [cite Le/Cuturi] is relevant and should be acknowledged.

* **Importance of Learned Weights:** The authors should provide more experimental insights into the learned weight vector *w* within the metric.  What is the impact of this learned component? Does it consistently improve performance, and if so, in what scenarios? A deeper analysis of *w* could demonstrate the value of this specific innovation.

* **Related Work Discussion:** The related work section could be strengthened by more clearly delineating the differences between this work and prior approaches. For example, the paper builds significantly upon the work of Cuturi and Avis [cite Cuturi/Avis], but leverages entropy regularization – a key distinction that enables scalability and improved performance.  The transition from discrete costs (Cuturi/Avis) to continuous, parameterized costs using linear maps is also a significant contribution not currently highlighted.  Furthermore, the mention of Wang et al. [cite Wang et al.] is somewhat dismissive. While their work may not be publishable in its current form, acknowledging its attempt and explaining why this work overcomes the limitations (specifically, the single OT optimization run and resulting instability) would demonstrate a more thorough understanding of the field.



In conclusion, this is a well-written and impactful paper that addresses an important problem in natural language processing. The proposed method is simple yet effective, and the experimental results are convincing. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field. I strongly support its acceptance to NIPS.
