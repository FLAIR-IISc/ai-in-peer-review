This paper proposes a supervised metric-learning approach built on the recent word mover’s distance (WMD) [19], a transport distance applied to word2vec embeddings. The method applies to any transport distance whose ground cost is a squared Euclidean distance after a linear projection. In addition to learning the linear projection to maximize performance, the approach also learns word-specific weights: it reweights each word’s frequency by a learned parameter and then renormalizes the document’s word frequencies. The paper introduces an efficient optimization procedure to learn this parameterized metric. A thorough experimental evaluation demonstrates effectiveness by comparing against numerous reasonable baselines on several document classification datasets, using a kNN classifier with the learned metric.

Strengths
- The work extends WMD in a technically sound and practically useful way, showing strong performance on multiple kNN text classification tasks.
- The idea of integrating linear metric learning and word reweighting into the transport framework is appealing and well motivated.
- The paper is very well written and a pleasure to read.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- The potential impact appears limited because an earth mover’s distance combined with metric learning via a “word centroid distance” initialization performs similarly to the full learning of the proposed distance; see the small gap between the last two rows of Table 3. In other words, the main technical contributions seem to add relatively little over this initialization based on existing techniques.
- Table 3 is missing an evaluation of the “word centroid embedding” (the weighted mean of word embeddings for a document) when paired with all tested existing metric-learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA). Including these comparisons would clarify how much of the gain comes from the transport formulation versus simpler centroid-based representations under standard metric learning.
- An ablation study is needed to assess the relative importance of the word-specific weights versus the projection matrix A. Please report performance with only word weights learned (A fixed/identity) and with only A learned (uniform word weights), to show each component’s contribution in isolation.

Originality
- The primary novelty lies in the optimization strategy (Lines 156–175). While the concept of linear metric learning within the earth mover’s distance is interesting, it represents a fairly limited incremental contribution by itself without stronger empirical separation from simpler baselines.

Minor clarity issues
- Line 152: “The authors” — which paper(s) does this refer to? [1, 7, 8]?
- Equation (11): Should α and β be starred here?
- Around Line 170, it would help to explicitly remind the reader that this is the Sinkhorn scaling referred to in the introduction (Line 56).
