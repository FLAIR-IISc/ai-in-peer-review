

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel approach that extends the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by incorporating supervision to improve document classification performance. The authors propose learning both a linear transformation of word embeddings and word-specific importance weights within the WMD framework. This is achieved by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error using an efficient approximation of the optimal transport problem, leveraging the Sinkhorn distance to avoid the cubic computational complexity of exact WMD computation.

The work builds upon Kusner et al.'s WMD [19], which established a powerful document metric by treating document distances as optimal transport problems between word embeddings. Unlike previous metric learning approaches that operate on document representations, S-WMD transforms the word embedding space directly, maintaining low dimensionality throughout. This is particularly significant as it avoids the need for dimensionality reduction when applying metric learning to high-dimensional text data.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, outperforming 26 competitive baselines across 8 document classification tasks. The evaluation includes thorough comparisons with various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) and supervised metric learning approaches (NCA, ITML, LMNN). The authors provide a well-structured experimental setup with five-fold cross-validation where appropriate, and the results consistently show S-WMD's superiority in most settings.

Strengths:
1. Quality: The paper presents a technically sound approach with rigorous theoretical justification for the approximation methods. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets and baselines. The authors carefully address both the strengths and limitations of their approach, acknowledging that S-WMD initialization (S-WCD) already performs well.

2. Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the background, methodology, and results. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the connection between the theoretical framework and the practical implementation is well-maintained. The supplementary materials further enhance understanding.

3. Originality: While the core concept of learning a metric within the WMD framework has some precedent (e.g., Wang & Guibas [34]), the specific combination of linear transformation learning and word importance weighting within the optimal transport framework represents a meaningful contribution. The efficient optimization method using Sinkhorn scaling is particularly novel.

4. Significance: The approach directly addresses a meaningful limitation of unsupervised WMD (lack of supervision), which is crucial for real-world document classification tasks. The consistent improvements across diverse datasets demonstrate the method's practical value, especially for datasets with shorter documents where the supervised approach shows significant gains over unsupervised baselines.

Weaknesses:
1. The most significant concern is the marginal improvement of the full S-WMD approach over its initialization (S-WCD), as shown in Table 3. The paper states that "S-WMD achieves the lowest kNN test error on 5/8 datasets" but fails to emphasize that S-WCD (the initialization) already performs remarkably well, often ranking near the top. This suggests that the core technical contribution (the learning procedure) may not provide substantial additional benefit over simpler initialization methods.

2. The evaluation lacks a critical comparison of the "word centroid embedding" (weighted mean of word embeddings) with standard metric learning approaches (NCA, ITML, LMNN). This would better establish the relative contribution of the S-WMD framework versus applying existing metric learning to the WCD baseline.

3. The paper does not isolate the contributions of the word importance weights versus the linear projection matrix A. It would be valuable to see performance when each component is used in isolation, which would clarify which aspect of the approach provides the most significant improvement.

4. The paper could better articulate why the specific approximation (Sinkhorn distance with λ=10) is optimal for this application, rather than simply stating it provides a "nice trade-off."

Pro Acceptance Arguments:
- The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the application of optimal transport to document classification
- The technical approach is well-motivated and rigorously justified
- The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing
- The method is efficient and practical, with training times comparable to baseline metric learning approaches
- The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear

Con Acceptance Arguments:
- The marginal improvement over S-WCD (the initialization) is concerning and needs stronger justification
- The contribution of the learning procedure over simpler initialization methods is not sufficiently demonstrated
- The evaluation lacks critical comparisons to isolate the contributions of key components
- The significance could be better articulated by more clearly distinguishing this work from prior metric learning approaches

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the application of optimal transport in document classification. While the marginal improvement over the initialization method is a concern, the method is well-justified, well-executed, and demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple datasets. The paper should be accepted with the understanding that the primary contribution is the efficient optimization framework within the WMD context, rather than the metric learning concept itself. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better address the relative contribution of the learning procedure versus the initialization.
