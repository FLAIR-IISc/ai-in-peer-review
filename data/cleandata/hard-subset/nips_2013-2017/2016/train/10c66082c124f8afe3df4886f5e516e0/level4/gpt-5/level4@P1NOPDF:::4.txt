This paper presents a supervised approach to learning document distances on top of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD, motivated by Earth Mover’s Distance, computes optimal transport between word distributions to measure document distance, using word2vec as the ground metric between words. The authors propose to learn (1) a linear transformation A of the word embeddings and (2) a reweighted word distribution w, so that documents with different labels are better separated. The paper is clearly written, with a thorough and accurate review of related work, and includes experimental results on numerous datasets, including some novel ones.

However, the motivation for the formulation needs further explanation, and the overall significance appears limited.

1) Motivation for reweighting w: The rationale for reweighting the word distribution is not well justified. As presented, this reweighting could alter a document’s word distribution to the point that it effectively becomes a different document. Please explain more clearly why this reweighting helps, and under what constraints it avoids changing the document’s identity. For example, do you enforce normalization, sparsity, or proximity to the original distribution? Empirical or theoretical justification would strengthen this component.

2) Significance of the contribution: Introducing a linear transformation A and a reweighting vector w within an NCA objective for a supervised variant of WMD is a reasonable extension, but it does not feel particularly novel or technically deep. The formulation appears straightforward rather than groundbreaking. Please better position the contribution relative to closely related supervised metric-learning approaches on transport-based distances and clarify what is truly new.

3) Generalization to new documents: How is the distance computed for a completely new, unseen document? Specifically, how is its reweighting w obtained? If w is document-specific and learned during training, what is the inference procedure at test time? If w is global or class-specific, please clarify how it is applied.

4) Meaning of “semantic difference”: In the abstract, “semantic difference” seems to refer to the document label. Differences in authorship or topic do not straightforwardly correspond to semantic differences. Please define precisely what you mean by “semantic difference” in this context.

Overall, the paper is well written and easy to follow. I would not object to acceptance if the above concerns—especially the motivation and impact of reweighting, the novelty claims, and the test-time procedure for w—are addressed satisfactorily.
