This paper presents a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns task-specific weights for the underlying distance, effectively turning a powerful unsupervised similarity measure into a discriminatively trained, domain-adapted metric. The approach is well motivated, technically sound, and clearly described. The authors provide a comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple text classification datasets, comparing against a wide range of baselines and feature representations. The proposed method consistently delivers substantial performance gains, often by a large margin.

Strengths
- Clear contribution: Extending WMD with supervision to learn a discriminative, domain-specific metric is a natural and impactful idea. It bridges the gap between a principled distance measure and task-driven optimization.
- Sound methodology: The formulation and learning procedure are coherent and well explained. The paper includes the right level of technical detail to understand and reproduce the method.
- Strong empirical results: The evaluation is thorough, spanning several datasets and many competitive baselines. The superiority of the proposed approach appears consistent and robust.
- Practical relevance: A learned metric that preserves the interpretability and flexibility of WMD while adapting to specific tasks is likely to be useful in a range of applications.
- Clarity and organization: The paper reads well, with a logical flow from motivation to method to experiments.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Computational efficiency and scalability: WMD can be computationally expensive. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of training and inference costs, including:
  - Asymptotic and empirical runtime comparisons with vanilla WMD and other baselines.
  - Scalability with respect to document length, vocabulary size, and dataset size.
  - Any approximations, pruning strategies, or acceleration techniques used, and their impact on accuracy.
- Ablation and interpretability: To better isolate what drives the gains:
  - Provide an ablation that quantifies the improvement due solely to learning the weights, relative to standard WMD and to simpler parameterizations (e.g., uniform or class-specific weights).
  - Analyze the learned weights to assess interpretability and whether they align with intuitive semantic distinctions. This could further support the claim of domain-specific adaptation.
- Robustness and generalization:
  - Examine sensitivity to the choice of word embeddings, initialization, and hyperparameters.
  - Report performance as a function of labeled data size to show how well the method leverages limited supervision.
  - Discuss cross-domain generalization: do weights learned on one dataset transfer to related tasks or domains?
- Baseline breadth and fairness:
  - Clarify whether strong neural text classifiers (e.g., contemporary CNN/RNN or fastText-like baselines) were included and tuned comparably; if not, adding them would strengthen the empirical case.
  - Ensure all baselines receive equal hyperparameter tuning and similar resource budgets to avoid confounding factors.
- Reproducibility: Include or commit to releasing code, pre-processing scripts, hyperparameters, and detailed instructions to facilitate replication.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely paper that advances a widely used distance measure by integrating supervision in a principled way. The method is clearly presented and empirically validated with broad and convincing experiments. Addressing the points above—especially runtime/scalability analysis and ablations clarifying where the gains come from—would further strengthen the work. Despite these suggestions, the contribution is significant and well supported by results. I recommend acceptance and would be pleased to see this paper presented at NIPS.
