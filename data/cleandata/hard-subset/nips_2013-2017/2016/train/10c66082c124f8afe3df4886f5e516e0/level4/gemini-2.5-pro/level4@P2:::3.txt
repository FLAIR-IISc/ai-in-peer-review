### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which formulates the distance between two documents as an optimal transport (or Earth Mover's Distance) problem between their respective word embeddings. While WMD is entirely unsupervised, S-WMD introduces supervision by learning two key components: 1) a linear transformation (a Mahalanobis metric) of the underlying word embedding space, and 2) a vector of importance weights for each word in the vocabulary, which re-scales the document's bag-of-words representation.

The authors formulate the learning problem as the minimization of a stochastic k-Nearest Neighbor leave-one-out classification error, adopting the differentiable loss function from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A direct gradient calculation for this loss is computationally prohibitive, as it would require solving a nested optimization problem for each distance calculation within the gradient step. The key technical contribution of this work is an efficient approximation of this gradient. The authors achieve this by replacing the exact WMD computation with its entropy-regularized counterpart, the Sinkhorn distance, which is differentiable and can be computed much more efficiently. The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation across eight datasets, demonstrating that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results compared to a large suite of 26 unsupervised and supervised baselines.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

This is a well-executed paper that combines several existing techniques in a novel and effective way. The idea is simple and elegant, yet its implementation and evaluation are non-trivial. The experimental section is a particularly strong component of this work.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Performance:** The paper addresses an important problem: adapting powerful unsupervised document distance metrics for supervised tasks. The experimental results are extensive and highly convincing, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on 5 out of 8 benchmark datasets against a very strong set of baselines. This clearly demonstrates the method's effectiveness and advances the state of the art in text classification.

2.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The proposed method is a clever synthesis of WMD, metric learning via NCA, and efficient optimal transport using Sinkhorn distances. While the individual components are not new, their combination to create a trainable, high-performance document metric is novel and impactful.

3.  **Practicality:** The authors provide a practical solution to the computationally challenging problem of differentiating through the WMD. By leveraging the entropy-regularized transport problem, they derive an efficient gradient approximation that makes the end-to-end training of their model feasible.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is strong overall, but could be improved with greater mathematical precision and a more thorough discussion of related work and model components.

1.  **Mathematical Rigor and Clarity:** The manuscript is somewhat imprecise in its mathematical formulations. For instance, the original WMD in Equation (1) is defined with a general p-norm on the word vectors (typically the standard Euclidean distance), whereas the supervised formulation in Equation (6) implicitly uses the *squared* Euclidean distance as the ground metric for the NCA loss. This makes the learned metric a 2-Wasserstein distance. While this is a valid choice, the inconsistency should be clarified. The authors could state explicitly that WMD is a p-Wasserstein distance and that their supervised method focuses on learning the ground metric for the squared 2-Wasserstein distance. This would improve the paper's technical clarity.

2.  **Positioning Relative to Prior Work:**
    *   The concept of re-weighting histogram bins, as done with the vector `w` in Equation (4), is not new. This idea was explored by Guy Lebanon in "Metric Learning for Text Documents" in the same context. More recent work by Le and Cuturi ("Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations") also deals with related concepts. Citing and briefly discussing this prior work would better contextualize the paper's contribution.
    *   The paper builds on the work of Cuturi & Avis, who first considered learning the ground distance for EMD. It would be beneficial to highlight the key differences more explicitly, such as the use of entropy regularization for scalability and the fact that the ground metric here is continuous and parameterized by a linear map, rather than being discrete.
    *   The relationship to Wang et al. [35] could also be clarified. While similar in spirit, that work has notable differences in its optimization scheme that are worth mentioning to distinguish the current, more robust approach.

3.  **Analysis of Model Components:** The model learns both a linear transformation `A` and a word weight vector `w`. The current experiments do not disentangle the contribution of each component. An ablation study would significantly strengthen the paper by showing the performance of S-WMD when learning only `A` or only `w`. This would provide valuable insight into whether the primary performance gain comes from transforming the embedding space, re-weighting the words, or if both are essential. This would also help answer the question of how important the novel word-weighting component is to the final results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Proposes a novel, elegant, and highly effective method for supervised document distance learning.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art classification performance across a wide range of datasets, backed by a thorough and convincing experimental evaluation.
*   Provides a practical and scalable solution to the difficult problem of differentiating through the optimal transport problem in this context.
*   The work addresses a significant problem and is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers in the community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Minor weaknesses in mathematical precision and clarity could be improved.
*   The paper could better position itself with respect to some key prior works on histogram re-weighting and learning EMD metrics.
*   The lack of an ablation study makes it difficult to fully assess the individual contributions of the two learned components of the model.

**Overall Recommendation:**

The strengths of this paper, particularly its strong empirical results and the elegance of the proposed method, significantly outweigh its minor weaknesses. The suggestions for improvement can be addressed in a final version. I recommend acceptance.
