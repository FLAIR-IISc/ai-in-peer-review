## Review of NIPS 2016 Paper: [Paper Title – Please fill in]

This paper presents a supervised learning approach to refine document distance estimation based on Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD, inspired by Earth Mover’s Distance, leverages optimal transport to quantify the dissimilarity between document word distributions. The original WMD utilizes word embeddings, typically word2vec, to calculate the cost of moving “mass” between words. This work proposes learning both a linear transformation of these word embeddings and a reweighting of the word distributions themselves, with the goal of enhancing document separation based on their labels.

The paper is well-written and easy to understand, presenting a clear explanation of the method and its connection to prior work. The authors demonstrate a strong grasp of the relevant literature with a thorough and well-structured related work section. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, encompassing several datasets not previously explored in this context. 

However, while technically sound, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling motivation and a limited overall contribution. Several key areas require further clarification.

First, the motivation behind reweighting the word distribution using weights *w* is unclear. The paper does not adequately justify *why* this reweighting is beneficial. Indeed, significant reweighting carries the risk of substantially altering the document’s representation, potentially diminishing its original meaning. A stronger justification is needed, demonstrating how the reweighting improves document separation *without* fundamentally changing the document's semantic content. The authors should address whether constraints or regularization techniques are employed to mitigate overly aggressive reweighting.

Second, the contribution of this work, while a valid extension to WMD, feels incremental. Applying a Normalized Cut-like (NCA) objective function to learn a linear transformation *A* and reweighting *w* within a supervised WMD framework is a reasonable approach, but it doesn't represent a significant breakthrough in the field. The formulation, while correct, lacks a novel conceptual element that would elevate its impact.

Third, the practical application of the learned reweighting scheme to new, unseen documents requires clarification.  How is the reweight *w* determined for a document not encountered during training?  The paper needs to detail a strategy for generalizing this component of the model, or acknowledge the limitation of requiring re-training for each new document or set of documents.

Finally, the use of the phrase “semantic difference” in the abstract is ambiguous. While the context suggests this refers to document labels, the term itself is broad.  Distinctions based on authorship or topic, as presented in the experimental results, don’t necessarily fall under a strict definition of “semantic difference.”  The authors should either clarify their intended meaning of this term or replace it with a more precise description.

Despite these concerns, the paper presents a functional and well-executed extension of WMD. The experimental results are encouraging and demonstrate the method's effectiveness. I do not fundamentally object to the acceptance of this paper, *provided* the authors adequately address the concerns raised regarding the motivation for reweighting, the significance of the contribution, the generalization to new documents, and the clarity of the terminology used. A revised version addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper’s overall impact.
