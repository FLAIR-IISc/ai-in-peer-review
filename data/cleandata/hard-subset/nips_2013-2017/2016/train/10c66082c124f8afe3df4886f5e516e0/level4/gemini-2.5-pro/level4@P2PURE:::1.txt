This paper introduces a supervised metric learning method that extends the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD) [19], an optimal transport distance between documents based on word2vec embeddings. The method learns a linear projection of the word embeddings and is applicable to any transport distance that relies on squared Euclidean distances. In addition to learning the projection matrix, the approach also learns a task-specific weight for each word in the vocabulary. These learned weights are used to re-scale word frequencies within a document before normalization, effectively emphasizing more important words. The authors present an efficient optimization algorithm for learning the parameters of this new metric. A thorough experimental evaluation on several document classification datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed metric within a k-NN classifier, outperforming a wide range of baselines.

+ This work presents a technically interesting and useful extension to the Word Mover's Distance. The method's effectiveness is well-demonstrated on several k-NN text classification benchmarks.

- The paper's impact may be limited by the finding that the full proposed method performs similarly to a simpler baseline: initializing with a "word centroid distance" and then applying the Earth Mover's Distance without further learning. This is evidenced by the small performance gap between the last two rows of Table 3. Consequently, the novel optimization and learning components—the main technical contributions—appear to offer little improvement over an intelligent initialization strategy that uses existing techniques.

- The evaluation would be strengthened by including a baseline where standard metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA) are applied directly to "word centroid embeddings" (the weighted average of a document's word embeddings).

- The paper lacks an ablation study to assess the relative contributions of the two learned components: the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A. An evaluation showing the performance of the model with each component used in isolation would be highly valuable.

o The primary novelty appears to be the optimization method (Lines 156–175). While the core idea of learning a linear transformation for the Earth Mover's Distance is interesting, it is a more incremental contribution on its own.

+ The paper is very well written and a pleasure to read.
