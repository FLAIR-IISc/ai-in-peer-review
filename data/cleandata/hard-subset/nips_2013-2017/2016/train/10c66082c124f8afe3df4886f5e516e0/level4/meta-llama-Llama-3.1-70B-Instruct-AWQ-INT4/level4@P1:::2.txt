This paper proposes an extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a document metric that leverages word embeddings to compute distances between documents. The key contribution of this work is the incorporation of supervision to learn a domain-specific metric, which the authors term the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD). The method is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the underlying concepts and techniques.

The paper's strongest aspect is its extensive experimental evaluation, which demonstrates the effectiveness of S-WMD on several classification datasets and compares it to a wide variety of baseline methods and features. The results show that S-WMD outperforms all other methods by a significant margin, which is a testament to the power of discriminative training in learning a domain-specific metric.

The technical aspects of the paper are sound, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of the S-WMD algorithm, including the optimization procedure and the approximation techniques used to speed up computation. The paper also provides a good discussion of related work and highlights the key differences between S-WMD and other metric learning approaches.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it does not provide a detailed analysis of the learned weights and their relationship to the underlying word embeddings. While the authors do provide some visualizations of the learned weights, a more in-depth analysis could provide additional insights into the strengths and limitations of the S-WMD approach.

Overall, this is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing. The extensive experimental evaluation and the clear explanation of the technical aspects make it a compelling read. I would very much like to see this paper presented at NIPS.
