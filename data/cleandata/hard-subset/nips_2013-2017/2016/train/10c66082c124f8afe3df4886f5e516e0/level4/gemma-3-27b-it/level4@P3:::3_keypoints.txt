- Proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD) to learn a task-specific document distance by adding supervision to WMD via a linear transform of embeddings and word importance weights.
- Uses a relaxed optimal transport (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering to enable efficient gradient computation for training.
- Optimizes leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error to learn the metric and word weights.
- Evaluated on eight real-world text classification tasks against 26 baselines, showing consistent and significant improvements.
- Core idea is well-motivated: adds adaptability to WMD’s strong embedding-based similarity foundation.
- Method sensibly combines metric learning, optimal transport, and word embeddings while addressing computational challenges.
- Experimental evaluation is extensive and a major strength, with clear, accessible presentation.
- Does not fully discuss the trade-off between entropy regularization (λ) and approximation vs. efficiency; sensitivity analysis is missing.
- Mathematical notation has occasional imprecision (e.g., Euclidean vs. squared Euclidean transitions).
- Lacks an algorithmic box summarizing the S-WMD training procedure to aid readability.
- Related work coverage is incomplete—needs stronger connections to broader metric learning, non-Euclidean metrics, and manifold learning.
- Should provide a more critical assessment of closely related methods (e.g., Wang et al.) and explicitly state how S-WMD overcomes their limitations.
- Needs analysis of learned word importance weights (sensitivity, quantitative impact, or qualitative examples) to better interpret behavior.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, achieves state-of-the-art results, and contributes significant technical innovations for efficient supervised WMD learning.
- Arguments against acceptance: mathematical presentation could be more rigorous, related-work and design-choice discussions are shallow, and empirical gains are consistent but not always large.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve mathematical rigor, related work discussion, and algorithmic clarity.
