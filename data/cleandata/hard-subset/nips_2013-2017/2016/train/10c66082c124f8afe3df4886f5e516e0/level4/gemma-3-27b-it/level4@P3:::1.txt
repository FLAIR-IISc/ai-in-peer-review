This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach extending the recent “word-movers distance” (WMD) [19], a transport distance applied to word2vec word embeddings. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space alongside word-specific importance weights to improve document distance estimation for classification tasks. These weights re-normalize word frequencies, emphasizing words most relevant to the classification problem. The paper details an efficient optimization technique for learning this parameterized metric and demonstrates its effectiveness through a comprehensive experimental evaluation, comparing it to a large number of baselines across several document classification datasets using a kNN classifier.

This work builds upon the interesting and technically sound WMD, offering a valuable extension that demonstrably improves performance on kNN-based text classification. The approach is well-motivated, addressing the limitation of the original WMD’s unsupervised nature by incorporating task-specific supervision. However, the potential impact may be somewhat limited. The results suggest that initializing the metric with a supervised "word centroid distance" (S-WCD) achieves comparable performance to the full learned metric (see the last two rows of Table 3). This raises the question of how much the technical contributions of this paper genuinely add beyond this effective initialization based on existing techniques. 

Several aspects of the evaluation could be strengthened. The paper lacks a comparison of the word centroid embedding (weighted mean of word embeddings) *combined with* the existing metric learning algorithms (ITML, LMNN, NCA). This would provide a clearer baseline for assessing the benefit of the more complex WMD-based approach.  Furthermore, an ablation study evaluating the relative importance of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A would be insightful. Reporting performance with each component active in isolation would illuminate their individual contributions. 

The originality primarily lies in the optimization strategy (Lines 156-175). While the concept of linear metric learning within the Earth Mover’s Distance is interesting, it is a relatively incremental contribution. The clarity of the writing is a major strength; the paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. There are a few minor points requiring clarification: 

*   Line 152: “The authors” – of which paper(s) are being referenced ([1,7,8])?
*   Equation 11: Shouldn’t α and β have asterisks (α∗ and β∗) to denote the optimal dual variables?
*   Around line 170, explicitly referencing the Sinkhorn scaling mentioned in the introduction (Line 56) would improve readability.

**Strengths:**

*   Strong technical foundation, building on the impactful WMD.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation with numerous baselines.
*   Efficient optimization technique for the proposed metric.
*   Demonstrated improvement over existing methods on several tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The performance gain over a simpler initialization (S-WCD) is not substantial, potentially limiting the overall impact.
*   Missing ablation study to assess the individual contributions of the learned components.
*   Lack of comparison with existing metric learning algorithms applied to the word centroid embedding.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound extension to a recent and influential method (WMD).
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate consistent improvement over a large number of competitive baselines.
*   The clarity of the writing and thoroughness of the evaluation are commendable.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The incremental nature of the contribution, particularly considering the strong performance of the S-WCD initialization.
*   The lack of a complete ablation study and comparison with relevant baselines (metric learning with word centroid embedding) leaves some questions unanswered.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of document distance learning. While the improvement over a simpler initialization is modest, the proposed approach is technically sound, well-motivated, and thoroughly evaluated.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above (clarifications and additional experiments). Specifically, performing the suggested additional experiments—evaluating the word centroid embedding with existing metric learning algorithms and conducting an ablation study—would significantly strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution.
