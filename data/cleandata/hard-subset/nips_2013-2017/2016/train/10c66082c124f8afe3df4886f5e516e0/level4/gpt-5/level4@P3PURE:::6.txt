This paper proposes a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for textual documents. While the original WMD is unsupervised and measures distances between word embeddings, this work leverages supervision of the form “these documents belong to the same class.” The authors promise to release MATLAB code. I appreciate the authors’ careful responses, which clarified several of my earlier questions.

The method learns two components: (1) a linear transformation A (in line with traditional metric learning), and (2) word-importance weights w. I would like a clearer intuition for Eq. (5), which defines how w is used, and an ablation or analysis disentangling the contributions of A versus w to the reported gains.

In the experiments, the approach is evaluated via a classification task. I recognize that evaluating a distance metric directly is not straightforward, but for context it would still be useful to compare against a simple classifier on the same datasets. These concerns are now largely irrelevant, as clarified by the authors, but documenting this comparison (or the rationale) would help readers.

Questions and requests for clarification:
- I did not understand when Eq. (2) is used. The lambda there seems different from the lambda referenced around line 170—are these the same hyperparameter, or do they serve different roles?
- What is the evidence supporting the claim at line 316 that the method is “particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words”? A brief empirical or theoretical justification would strengthen this statement.
- Line 257 states “on the right we also show the average error across datasets.” I could not find this—should it appear in Table 3, or elsewhere?
- In Table 2, what is the difference between “n” and “ne”?
- How is k selected for kNN classification? Is k dataset-dependent, method-dependent, or fixed across experiments? Please specify the selection procedure and whether a validation set is used.

Overall, the supervised extension of WMD via learning A and w is promising, but the paper would benefit from clearer intuition for Eq. (5), explicit attribution of improvements to each learned component, and the requested clarifications above.
