This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document metric by extending the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The key idea is to incorporate supervision to adapt the distance metric for a given classification task. The authors achieve this by simultaneously learning two components: an affine transformation of the underlying word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector.

A significant technical challenge is that computing the gradient of the original WMD is computationally prohibitive and involves a nested, non-differentiable optimization problem. The authors propose an elegant and practical solution by relaxing the WMD optimization problem using an entropy-regularized objective, known as the Sinkhorn distance. This makes the objective strongly convex and allows for an efficient and differentiable approximation of the transport plan, leading to a practical gradient-based training algorithm. The method is initialized by first learning a metric for the simpler Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and then fine-tuned using a batch-based optimization scheme.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Performance:** The paper's primary strength is its convincing and extensive experimental evaluation. The proposed S-WMD is benchmarked on eight diverse document classification tasks against 26 competitive baselines, including various document representations (TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with leading metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results on five of the eight datasets and demonstrates the best average rank across all methods, often by a significant margin. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

2.  **Technically Sound and Well-Motivated Method:** The paper presents a logical and well-motivated extension to the unsupervised WMD. The idea of learning a domain-specific metric via discriminative training is a natural next step for this line of research. The technical approach to overcoming the intractability of the gradient calculation by using the Sinkhorn distance approximation is both clever and effective, making the method practical for real-world use.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The authors provide the necessary background on word embeddings, WMD, and metric learning, clearly articulate the problem, and precisely detail their proposed solution and its derivations. The inclusion of results on training time and visualizations further strengthens the paper's completeness.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Novelty in Combination:** The novelty of the work lies primarily in the novel and effective combination of existing ideas (WMD, NCA-style loss, Sinkhorn distance for gradient approximation) rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new algorithm. However, this combination is non-trivial and solves an important problem, representing a strong engineering and research contribution.

2.  **Performance on Specific Datasets:** While S-WMD is the top performer on average, it is outperformed by other methods on three datasets (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON). The authors acknowledge this and offer plausible hypotheses, such as the potential sub-optimality of the squared Euclidean distance on CLASSIC or the strength of TF-IDF-based methods on datasets with long documents. This is a minor point but indicates that the method, while excellent, is not a universal solution for all text classification tasks.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   The paper addresses the important problem of incorporating supervision into the powerful WMD framework.
    *   The proposed method is technically sound, and the use of the Sinkhorn distance to enable efficient training is an elegant solution to a difficult problem.
    *   The experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough and demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on a majority of the tested benchmarks, significantly advancing the field of document classification.
    *   The paper is well-written, clear, and provides all the necessary details for reproducibility.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The core contribution is a novel combination of existing techniques rather than a completely new theoretical framework.

This is a solid and high-impact paper. The proposed method is sound, well-explained, and backed by an extensive set of experiments that show a clear and significant improvement over a wide variety of strong baselines. The strengths far outweigh any minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance and believe it will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
