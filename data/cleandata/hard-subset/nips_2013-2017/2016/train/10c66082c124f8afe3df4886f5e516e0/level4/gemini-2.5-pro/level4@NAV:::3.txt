This work introduces a method for learning the parameters of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which adapts the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) to text by using word embeddings for the ground metric. The authors propose to parameterize this ground cost using a learnable Mahalanobis distance between word vectors. Additionally, they learn histogram reweighting coefficients, a technique with precedent in the literature. The paper successfully derives gradients for an NCA-style loss function and presents strong empirical validation for the proposed approach.

Overall, the paper represents an effective synthesis of established techniques, leading to highly convincing experimental outcomes. I am in favor of its acceptance. A key strength is the proposal of a simple and conceptually straightforward idea, the implementation and validation of which are non-trivial. Consequently, the experimental validation is a major contribution of this work.

For improvement, the authors should more clearly articulate the connection between the regularized and non-regularized formulations. Furthermore, the mathematical presentation could be more rigorous, as some notations and computations lack precision. The inclusion of an algorithm summary or pseudocode block would significantly enhance clarity.

Minor Comments:
*   A minor inconsistency exists between the cost functions used: Equation 1 employs the Euclidean distance, while Equation 6, for the Mahalanobis learning, uses the squared Euclidean distance. This corresponds to a shift from a 1-Wasserstein to a 2-Wasserstein distance. To unify the presentation, the authors could frame the WMD as a p-Wasserstein distance, which would generalize across these cases.
*   The histogram reweighting technique presented in Equation 5 has precedent. This approach was previously introduced by Lebanon in the context of text classification and is also explored by Le and Cuturi in "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations." The paper should acknowledge this prior work.
*   Could the authors provide experimental insights into the contribution of the learned weight vector `w`? An ablation study demonstrating the impact of this component would clarify its importance and relevance to the model's performance.
*   The related work section could be improved by better articulating the distinctions from prior art. For instance, while the method builds upon the work of Cuturi and Avis, the current paper's use of entropy regularization (enabling greater scalability) and its parameterization of a continuous cost function with linear maps (as opposed to a discrete one) are significant, unstated advantages. Furthermore, when comparing to Wang et al., the authors could highlight that their proposed optimization is more robust, as the single OT optimization step in the prior work is known to be insufficient.
