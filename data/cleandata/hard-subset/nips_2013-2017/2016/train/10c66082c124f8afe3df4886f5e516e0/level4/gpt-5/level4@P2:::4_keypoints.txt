- Paper proposes S-WMD: a supervised extension of WMD that learns a linear embedding transform A and a global word-importance vector w, trained with an NCA-style leave-one-out kNN loss using entropy-regularized (Sinkhorn) OT for gradients.  
- Empirical evaluation on eight text classification datasets against 26 baselines shows consistent improvements on most tasks.  
- Builds on prior work: WMD (Kusner), Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi), and supervised metric learning (NCA/LMNN/ITML); contribution is applying learned ground metric and word weights specifically for WMD with Sinkhorn training.  
- Strength: Clear, well-organized writing with thorough related work discussion.  
- Strength: Practical, scalable training using Sinkhorn relaxation and batching.  
- Strength: Broad empirical evaluation across diverse datasets and many competitive baselines.  
- Strength: Conceptually simple approach that is easy to implement and reproducible (code provided).  
- Weakness: Motivation for the learned word reweighting vector w is not well justified; w applied to normalized histograms could distort document content.  
- Weakness: Need clarification on constraints/regularization for w to avoid degenerate or semantically misleading solutions, and empirical ablations/qualitative analyses showing w’s benefit.  
- Weakness: Incremental novelty — combining A, w, NCA-style objective, and Sinkhorn is sensible but not clearly more novel than prior learned-ground-metric/EMD work.  
- Weakness: Ambiguity in test-time inference — unclear whether w is a single global vector or document-specific, and how to apply/estimate w for unseen documents.  
- Weakness: Terminology “semantic difference” is vague given supervision by heterogeneous labels (topic/sentiment/authorship); authors should clarify intended meaning.  
- Evaluation: Method appears technically sound and practical, but w’s role needs clearer justification and analysis; clarity is good but would benefit from more intuition and ablations; originality is modest; significance is supported by strong empirical results.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong empirical performance, efficient training, public code, and clear exposition.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited conceptual novelty, insufficient analysis/motivation of w, and ambiguity about test-time use of w and “semantic difference.”  
- Overall: Well-executed and clearly presented supervised metric learning for WMD with convincing results, but acceptance contingent on clarifying/justifying w, inference procedure for new documents, and the claimed novelty/semantics.
