Summary
The paper proposes a supervised metric learning framework built on top of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), a transport distance defined over word2vec embeddings. The method jointly learns (i) a linear projection of word embeddings, so that transport costs become squared Euclidean distances in the projected space, and (ii) per-word weights that reweight normalized word frequencies in documents. An efficient optimization scheme is presented, enabling scalable learning of the parameterized transport metric. The approach is evaluated extensively on multiple document classification datasets using kNN with the learned metric, and compared against a broad set of baselines.

Strengths
- Extends WMD with supervised learning of both a projection matrix and word-specific weights, providing a principled way to adapt the transport cost to labeled data.
- The optimization is efficient and well-motivated, making the approach practical for real datasets.
- Experiments are thorough and demonstrate strong performance across several kNN text classification benchmarks.
- The paper is clearly written and generally easy to follow.

Main Concerns
- Limited incremental gain over a strong initialization: The empirical improvements of the fully learned transport distance over an Earth Mover’s Distance initialized with a “word centroid distance” metric learning step appear modest (see the last two rows of Table 3). This raises concerns about how much additional benefit the proposed joint learning (projection + word weights within the OT framework) provides beyond this simpler baseline. If a simple centroid-based metric learning delivers nearly the same performance, the practical impact of the more complex approach may be limited.
- Scope of originality: While the idea of linear metric learning inside a transport distance is appealing, the most original aspect appears to be the specific optimization strategy (roughly Lines 156–175). Conceptually, learning a linear projection for squared Euclidean transport costs and reweighting words are natural extensions; the paper’s novelty thus rests more on the formulation and training efficiency than on fundamentally new modeling ideas.

Missing Evaluations and Ablations
- Centroid embedding with standard metric learning: It would strengthen the empirical claims to include results for the simple “word centroid embedding” representation (i.e., the weighted mean of word embeddings per document) combined with popular supervised metric learning methods such as ITML, LMNN, and NCA. This would directly test whether the transport machinery provides significant gains over a strong and much cheaper alternative across all datasets.
- Component-wise ablations: Please report results isolating the contributions of (a) the projection matrix A only (without learned word weights), and (b) the learned word-specific weights only (without A). Such ablations would clarify the relative importance of each component and whether their joint learning provides additive benefits.

Clarity and Minor Issues
- Line 152 (“The authors”): Please specify which prior work this refers to (e.g., [1], [7], [8], or a subset).
- Equation (11): Should α and β be α* and β*, consistent with their optimal values elsewhere in the text?
- Around Line 170: It would help to explicitly note that the scaling procedure is the Sinkhorn algorithm referenced earlier (e.g., Intro, Line 56), to reinforce the connection between the optimization and the regularized OT framework.

Overall Assessment
This is a well-written and carefully executed paper that advances supervised metric learning for transport-based document distances. The combination of a learned linear projection with learned word weights is elegant, and the optimization is efficient. The experimental section is broad and generally compelling.

However, the work’s impact is tempered by two issues: (1) the relatively small gains over a strong centroid-based metric learning initialization, and (2) the lack of ablations that would isolate where the improvements come from. Including the suggested evaluations—particularly centroid embeddings with ITML/LMNN/NCA and the component-wise ablations—would make the empirical case more convincing and clarify the unique value of learning within the OT framework beyond simpler baselines.

If these additions show consistent, meaningful improvements attributable to the transport structure and the joint learning of A and word weights, the paper would make a solid contribution to supervised metric learning for text. As it stands, the technical development is solid and the results are promising, but the incremental benefit over simpler alternatives is not yet fully established.
