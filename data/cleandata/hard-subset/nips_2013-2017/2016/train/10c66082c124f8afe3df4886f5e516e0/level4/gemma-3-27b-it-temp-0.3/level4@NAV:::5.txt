The authors tackle the problem of text classification, building upon prior work leveraging Word Mover's Distance (WMD) – specifically, by introducing a supervised learning approach to refine word-specific weighting. Their key contribution lies in employing the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient approximation of the Wasserstein distance, and demonstrating its effectiveness through comprehensive evaluation across eight datasets and comparison against 26 baselines, achieving state-of-the-art average performance. 

This is a clearly presented and well-written manuscript that provides a thorough and accessible explanation of the underlying mathematical principles. However, the novelty relative to the original WMD work appears incremental. Furthermore, the inclusion of comparisons to World Centroid Distance feels somewhat tangential and detracts from the core narrative. 

My primary concern relates to the discussion of the regularization parameter, lambda. Based on my experience, lambda’s value significantly influences performance, potentially dominating equation (2). While theoretically, a sufficiently large lambda should approximate standard Wasserstein distance (albeit with slower convergence), practical implementations often utilize small lambda values to accelerate convergence, resulting in behavior distinct from true Wasserstein distance. Therefore, I am interested in understanding whether the observed results are driven by a lambda value that promotes Wasserstein-like behavior or a more typical Sinkhorn (entropic) behavior.
