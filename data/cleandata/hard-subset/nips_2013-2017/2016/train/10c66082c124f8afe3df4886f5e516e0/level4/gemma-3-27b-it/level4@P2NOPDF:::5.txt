This paper addresses the problem of text classification, building upon prior work that leveraged Word Mover's Distance (WMD), also known as the Wasserstein distance, for this task. The authors extend this approach by introducing learned, word-specific weights to enable supervised learning. A key contribution is the proposal to utilize the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient proxy for the Wasserstein distance. The method is rigorously evaluated across eight datasets, demonstrating superior average performance compared to 26 baseline methods.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains both the motivation and the mathematical foundations of the proposed approach. The authors present a comprehensive and accessible discussion of the underlying theory. 

However, the novelty of this work is somewhat incremental to the original WMD paper. While the supervised learning aspect and the use of Sinkhorn distance are valuable improvements, they represent extensions rather than a fundamentally new direction. Furthermore, the comparison to and discussion of World Centroid Distance feels tangential and, at times, obscures the core contributions. The relevance of these comparisons isn’t immediately apparent and could be clarified or potentially removed.

My primary concern relates to the treatment of the regularization parameter, λ. The value of λ significantly impacts performance, often dominating the behavior of equation (2) in the paper. While theoretically, a sufficiently large λ should approximate the behavior of the pure Wasserstein distance (albeit with potentially slower convergence), practical implementations typically employ small λ values to accelerate convergence. This leads to behavior distinctly different from the original Wasserstein distance due to the entropic regularization inherent in the Sinkhorn approximation. The paper does not sufficiently address *where* on this spectrum of behaviors the chosen λ values fall. Specifically, it is unclear whether the reported results are closer to a true Wasserstein approximation or a more strongly entropic, Sinkhorn-driven behavior. Understanding this distinction is crucial for interpreting the results and assessing the practical implications of the proposed method. Further discussion and analysis of the sensitivity to λ, potentially including an ablation study with varying λ values, would significantly strengthen the paper.
