This paper introduces a novel supervised metric learning approach for textual documents, extending the previously established Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). While WMD operates in an unsupervised manner by directly comparing word embeddings, this work leverages supervision in the form of document-level class labels to refine the distance metric. The authors indicate that an implementation in MATLAB will be released.

The core contribution lies in learning both a linear transformation matrix, *A*, and word importance weights, *w*, to modulate the distance calculation. *A* represents a standard linear transformation of the embeddings, while *w* dynamically adjusts the contribution of each word to the overall distance. I found the derivation and intuition behind Equation (5), which governs the application of the word importance weights, somewhat unclear. Disentangling the individual contributions of *A* and *w* to the observed performance gains would strengthen the paper. Specifically, it would be valuable to understand how much each component contributes to the overall improvement over the baseline WMD.

The method is evaluated on a document classification task. Recognizing the inherent difficulty in evaluating distance metrics, it would be helpful to establish a performance baseline using a simple classifier (e.g., logistic regression) trained directly on document embeddings. This comparison would provide context for the reported improvements and demonstrate the value of the learned metric.

However, several points require further clarification. The usage of Equation (2) remains unclear; could the authors explain its purpose within the broader framework? Furthermore, the value of λ used in Equation (2) appears inconsistent with its definition in line 170. The authors claim that the method is “particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words” (line 316). A more detailed explanation of *why* this is the case, and how the learned metric achieves this forgiveness, would be beneficial.

Regarding specific details, line 257 states, “on the right we also show the average error across datasets.” I was unable to locate this information; is it intended to be presented in Table 3? Additionally, Table 2 includes entries labeled “n” and “ne”. The distinction between these two values is not defined in the text. Finally, the definition of *k* used in the k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) classification experiments is absent. Is *k* a dataset-specific parameter, or is it determined by the proposed method? Clarifying whether *k* is tuned based on the dataset or learned as part of the metric learning process would provide a more complete understanding of the experimental setup.
