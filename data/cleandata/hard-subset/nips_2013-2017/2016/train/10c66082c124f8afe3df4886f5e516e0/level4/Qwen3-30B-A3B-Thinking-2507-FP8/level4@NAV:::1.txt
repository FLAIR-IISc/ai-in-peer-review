

This paper introduces a supervised metric learning framework built upon the recent "word-movers distance" [19], a transport distance applied to word2vec embeddings. The approach is generalizable to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances following linear projection. It simultaneously learns a linear projection to maximize performance and a word-specific weighting mechanism, where each word's frequency is reweighted by a learned scalar, normalized, and applied to document representations. The paper provides an efficient optimization method for the parameterized metric. Comprehensive experiments demonstrate the approach's effectiveness through comparisons against numerous baselines on multiple document classification datasets using a kNN classifier based on the learned metric.  

+ This work extends the word-movers distance with a technically interesting and effective extension for kNN text classification tasks.  
- The potential impact is limited, as the earth-movers distance with metric learning initialized via "word centroid distance" achieves performance comparable to the full proposed method (see last two rows of Table 3). Thus, the core technical contributions appear to offer only marginal gains over this initialization based on existing techniques.  
- Table 3 omits evaluation of the "word centroid embedding" (weighted document mean of word embeddings) combined with standard metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA).  
- The evaluation lacks an assessment of the relative importance of the word-specific weights versus the projection matrix A; performance with each component activated in isolation would be valuable.  
- The originality primarily lies in the optimization procedure (Lines 156–175). While the concept of linear metric learning within earth-movers distance is interesting, the contribution is constrained.  
+ The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. Minor clarifications are needed:  
  - Line 152: "The authors" refers to which paper? [1,7,8]?  
  - Equation 11: Should α and β carry stars?  
  - Explicitly recall around Line 170 that the Sinkhorn scaling referenced in Line 56 is used.
