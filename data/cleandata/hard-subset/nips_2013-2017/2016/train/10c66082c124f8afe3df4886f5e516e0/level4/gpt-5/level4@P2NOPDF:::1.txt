This paper introduces a supervised metric learning method built on Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) [19], a transport distance computed over word2vec embeddings. The approach is applicable to transport distances that use squared Euclidean costs after a linear projection. In addition to learning a projection that optimizes downstream performance, the method learns word-specific weights: document word frequencies are reweighted by learned parameters and then renormalized. The authors present an efficient optimization procedure to learn these parameters. Extensive experiments compare the proposed metric, used with a kNN classifier, against many reasonable baselines across multiple document classification datasets and show strong empirical performance.

Strengths:
- A timely and technically interesting extension of WMD to a supervised setting. The combination of learning a linear projection and word-specific weights within a transport distance is well-motivated and practically useful.
- Solid empirical study with a broad set of baselines and datasets; kNN performance is consistently competitive.
- Clear presentation and generally high-quality writing.

Limitations and suggestions:
- The overall impact seems tempered by the observation that Earth Mover’s Distance initialized via a “word centroid distance” metric learning performs comparably to the fully learned distance. The small gap between the last two rows in Table 3 suggests that much of the gain may come from this initialization rather than from the proposed joint learning. Please analyze and discuss this more thoroughly.
- Table 3 appears to omit a crucial baseline: using the “word centroid embedding” (weighted mean of word embeddings for a document) combined with standard metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA). Including these would clarify how much of the improvement is due to the transport formulation versus simpler representations plus off-the-shelf metric learning.
- An ablation study is needed to isolate the contributions of the two learned components: the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A. Reporting performance with (i) only A learned, (ii) only word weights learned, and (iii) both together would clarify their relative importance.
- While the idea of linear metric learning inside EMD/WMD is appealing, the novelty beyond the optimization strategy (Lines 156–175) feels limited. Strengthening the conceptual contribution or providing deeper theoretical or empirical insights into why the joint learning helps over strong initializations would increase the paper’s significance.

Originality and significance:
- The main originality lies in the optimization approach and in integrating linear projection and word reweighting within WMD. The concept itself is interesting but, given the competitive performance of the centroid-initialized baseline, the incremental contribution over existing techniques may be modest unless the above ablations show clear added value.

Clarity and minor comments:
- Line 152: “The authors” — please specify which prior work is referenced (e.g., [1, 7, 8]?).
- Equation (11): Should α and β be starred to match the notation of the optimized variables elsewhere?
- Around Line 170, explicitly remind the reader that the scaling used is the Sinkhorn algorithm referenced in the introduction (Line 56).
- Consider adding brief complexity/runtime comparisons for the proposed optimization versus baselines, to contextualize the practicality of the method.

Overall assessment:
A well-written and thorough empirical paper that extends WMD with supervised metric learning via joint projection and word reweighting. The approach is promising and shows strong results, but its added value over strong, simpler baselines—especially centroid-based metric learning—needs clearer empirical separation. Including the missing baselines and ablations would significantly strengthen the paper’s case for novelty and impact.
