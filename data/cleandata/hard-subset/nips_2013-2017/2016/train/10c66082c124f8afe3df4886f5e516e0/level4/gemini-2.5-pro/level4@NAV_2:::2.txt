This paper presents Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel approach for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The method extends the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a powerful unsupervised metric that calculates the minimum cost to transport words from one document to another within a word embedding space. The authors introduce supervision by framing the problem as one of metric learning, where they jointly learn an affine transformation for the word embedding space and a set of importance weights for words in the vocabulary. To make this learning process computationally feasible, the authors propose a key technical contribution: an efficient gradient computation based on an entropy-regularized version of the underlying optimal transport problem, also known as the Sinkhorn distance. The proposed metric is optimized to minimize the stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification error.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a clear limitation of the original WMD: its inability to leverage supervisory signals. The idea of learning a domain-specific metric directly within the WMD framework is both novel and impactful. This work successfully bridges the gap between the powerful non-parametric matching of WMD and the task-specific adaptability of metric learning.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-executed. The main challenge—differentiating through the WMD linear program—is elegantly solved by using an arbitrarily close, entropy-smoothed approximation. This relaxation makes the gradient computation tractable and the overall algorithm practical. The authors also propose a sensible initialization strategy (S-WCD) and an efficient batch optimization scheme, demonstrating careful consideration of the practical aspects of their algorithm.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It provides the necessary background on WMD, metric learning, and regularized optimal transport before clearly motivating and detailing its own contributions. The derivations are presented with sufficient detail, and the overall structure logically guides the reader through the problem, the proposed solution, and its empirical validation.

4.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are extensive and convincing. The authors evaluate S-WMD on eight real-world datasets, comparing it against a very large set of 26 competitive baselines that span classical representations (TF-IDF, LSI, LDA) and state-of-the-art methods (mSDA), both with and without supervision from leading metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The results are impressive, showing that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance on a majority of the tasks, often by a significant margin.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The proposed algorithm introduces several new hyperparameters (e.g., the regularization parameter λ, batch size, neighbor set size). While the experimental results are strong, the paper would benefit from a brief discussion or analysis of the sensitivity of the model's performance to these choices. For instance, a comment on the trade-off between approximation accuracy and convergence speed as a function of λ would be insightful for practitioners.

2.  **Analysis of the Learned Metric:** The visualization of learned word weights (Figure 2) is a nice addition for interpretability. The analysis could be slightly deepened by discussing the learned linear transformation A. Does it primarily scale dimensions, rotate the space, or perform a more complex transformation? Understanding what the model learns about the embedding space itself could provide valuable insights.

3.  **Choice of Ground Metric:** The authors briefly note that using a squared Euclidean distance might be suboptimal for the CLASSIC dataset. This is an interesting point. While out of scope for this paper, it might be worth mentioning as a direction for future work whether the proposed framework could be generalized to other ground metrics beyond the L2-norm.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a significant and well-motivated contribution to the field of text analysis and metric learning. The method is technically sound, the experimental evaluation is thorough and compelling, and the paper is written with excellent clarity. It builds upon an important recent line of work (WMD) and substantially improves upon it by incorporating supervision in a principled and efficient manner. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the work. This paper is a strong candidate for acceptance and will likely be of high interest to the NIPS community.
