

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) metric for document classification. WMD, introduced by Kusner et al., computes document distances by solving an optimal transport problem between word embeddings, achieving state-of-the-art results in kNN classification. The authors extend this framework by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to improve document classification performance. This builds upon prior work in metric learning, particularly Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), while maintaining the core WMD approach that leverages pre-trained word embeddings rather than learning document representations.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough review of related work that demonstrates a strong understanding of the field. The authors effectively position their work within the context of both document distance metrics and metric learning literature, citing relevant NIPS proceedings and other key publications. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, testing S-WMD on eight real-world text classification tasks against 26 competitive baselines, with consistent improvements over unsupervised WMD and other document representations.

Regarding quality, the technical approach is sound, with well-justified approximations (using Sinkhorn distance) to make the optimization tractable. The paper provides a clear derivation of the gradient updates, though the motivation for the word importance reweighting (w) could be strengthened. The authors should more clearly explain why this reweighting doesn't fundamentally alter the document's semantic content, as the current justification is insufficient to show how the method preserves document meaning while improving classification.

In terms of originality, while the approach is technically sound, the contribution appears incremental rather than groundbreaking. The authors apply standard metric learning techniques (NCA) to the WMD framework, which is a reasonable extension but not a novel conceptual leap. The paper would benefit from more explicit comparison to related work on supervised metric learning for text, such as Wang et al.'s work on learning ground distances for EMD.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited, as the improvements over unsupervised WMD are consistent but not dramatic across all datasets. The paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how this approach advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way beyond the specific classification tasks tested. The claim that the method captures "semantic differences" in the abstract is ambiguous without clarification about whether this refers to topic, authorship, or other semantic distinctions.

Specific concerns include: (1) The rationale for word importance reweighting needs stronger justification regarding how it preserves document meaning; (2) The paper doesn't address how to determine word weights for completely new documents; (3) The connection between "semantic difference" and document labels requires clearer explanation.

Overall, the paper presents a solid technical contribution that extends WMD to supervised learning with practical computational benefits. While the contribution is incremental rather than transformative, the method is well-executed, thoroughly evaluated, and could be useful for practitioners. The authors should address the specific concerns raised above to strengthen the paper's significance and motivation.
