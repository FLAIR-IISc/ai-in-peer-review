The authors have analyzed the problem of classifying text into different categories. They have expanded on a previous work by Word Mover's Distance (Wasserstein), which proposed to supervise it by learning word-specific weights. This contribution suggests using the Sinkhorn distance as an alternative to the Wasserstein distance to increase its speed. It shows the best average performance on 8 datasets against 26 baselines. It is a very well-written paper that presents the subject well and deeply analyzes the mathematics behind the problem, but with great clarity. The weaknesses are probably limited novelty compared to the previous Word Mover's Distance paper, and the references do not seem relevant, but rather misleading, in contrast to the World Centroid Distance. I think the lambda (regularization factor) is discussed weakly. From my experience, the value of lambda has a huge impact, sometimes becoming the main reason for eq(2). Theoretically, using a sufficiently large lambda should produce results similar to the pure Wasserstein distance, but at the cost of slower convergence. However, most people use a very small lambda to improve the rate of convergence using the Sinkhorn, which creates a completely different behavior than the Wasserstein. So I am curious whether lambda determines a pure Wasserstein behavior or a Sinkhorn (entropic) behavior in the case of this contribution.
