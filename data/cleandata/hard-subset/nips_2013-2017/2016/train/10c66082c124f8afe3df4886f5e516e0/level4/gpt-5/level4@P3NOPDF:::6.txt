Summary:
This paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for measuring distances between textual documents. While WMD is an unsupervised optimal-transport-based distance over word embeddings, the authors introduce supervision of the form “these documents belong to the same class” to learn a better task-specific metric. The method jointly learns (i) a linear transformation A of the word embeddings (as in classic metric learning) and (ii) a set of word-importance weights w. The approach is evaluated on document classification via kNN using the learned distance. MATLAB code is promised.

Strengths:
- Timely and relevant extension of WMD to the supervised setting.
- Clear motivation for leveraging label information to adapt distances to downstream classification tasks.
- The joint learning of both a transformation A and word weights w is appealing, as it can potentially capture both global geometry and per-word relevance.
- Empirical results indicate consistent gains over unsupervised WMD and other baselines on several datasets.
- Author response was thoughtful and clarified several points.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Intuition and formulation of the word-importance weights: The paper would benefit from more intuition behind Eq. (5), specifically how w interacts with the transport plan and whether it effectively reweights source/target histograms or modifies ground costs. A small illustrative example would help.
- Contribution disentanglement: It is unclear how much of the improvement comes from A versus w. An ablation (only A, only w, both) is needed to attribute gains and to justify the additional complexity.
- Usage of Eq. (2) and regularization parameters: I did not understand when Eq. (2) is invoked in the training pipeline and how it relates to the main objective. The λ in Eq. (2) appears to differ from the λ around line 170. Please clarify whether these are distinct hyperparameters, what each regularizes, and provide sensitivity analyses.
- Justification of the “forgiving” claim (line 316): The claim that the method is “particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words” needs either a theoretical argument (e.g., properties of the learned ground metric) or targeted empirical evidence. For instance, a controlled experiment where synonyms/paraphrases are swapped would make this concrete.
- Evaluation scope: While evaluating a distance metric via kNN is reasonable, additional baselines would contextualize the gains. Simple classifiers such as linear SVM or logistic regression on bag-of-words, tf-idf, averaged word embeddings, and possibly supervised sentence/document encoders would help readers gauge absolute performance. If these are out of scope, a brief rationale would be useful.
- Experimental details: 
  - k in kNN: Please specify how k is chosen (fixed across datasets or selected via validation), and whether results are sensitive to k.
  - Table 2: Clarify the difference between “n” and “ne.”
  - Line 257: The text claims that the “average error across datasets” is shown on the right; I could not find this (is it Table 3?). Please correct or point to the exact location.
- Computational aspects: Since WMD is computationally intensive, a discussion of runtime and memory overhead for learning and inference relative to vanilla WMD would be valuable. If approximations (e.g., pruning, low-rank, Sinkhorn/entropic regularization) are used, please describe them and their effect on accuracy.
- Reproducibility: Code availability is appreciated. Please include all hyperparameter settings (e.g., λs, learning rates, regularizers, constraints on A and w), data preprocessing, and exact validation protocol for selecting k and λ.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a compact ablation: WMD, WMD+only A, WMD+only w, WMD+A+w. This will directly answer which component is responsible for the gains.
- Expand the explanation around Eq. (5): give the modeling intuition, constraints on w (e.g., nonnegativity, normalization), how w is learned (objective and gradients), and its effect on the ground metric or histograms.
- Clarify Eq. (2): where it appears in the learning algorithm, the role of λ, and how it differs from the λ near line 170. Include a brief hyperparameter sensitivity plot or table.
- Substantiate the “forgiving” claim with a small analysis: 
  - Qualitative: nearest neighbors before vs. after learning when synonyms/paraphrases are present.
  - Quantitative: performance on datasets or subsets enriched with paraphrase variation.
- Report runtimes and, if applicable, any acceleration strategies. If training is significantly more expensive than vanilla WMD but inference remains similar, state this explicitly.
- Detail the kNN setup: value(s) of k, selection method, and sensitivity. If k varies by dataset, explain the policy.
- Clean up minor presentation issues: fix the pointer to the “average error” summary; define “n” vs. “ne” in Table 2.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-motivated and potentially impactful supervised extension of WMD. The joint learning of A and w is a sensible way to align the embedding space with task labels while modulating token importance. The empirical results are promising. However, the paper would be strengthened by clearer exposition of the weighting mechanism, a careful disentanglement of where the gains arise (A vs. w), and resolution of several clarity issues (usage of Eq. (2), λ definitions, evaluation details). With these revisions—particularly the ablation and clarifications—I would be inclined to recommend acceptance.
