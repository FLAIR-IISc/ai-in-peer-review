This paper presents Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised document metric for text classification. The work extends the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which is an unsupervised metric based on the optimal transport (or Wasserstein) distance between word embeddings. The key contributions are twofold: 1) incorporating supervision by learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space and a vector of word-importance weights, and 2) proposing an efficient training algorithm. To make the optimization tractable, the authors use the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable and computationally efficient proxy for the WMD. The proposed method is evaluated on eight text classification datasets, where it consistently outperforms a large suite of 26 competitive baselines on average.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper is exceptionally well-written. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, positioning their work relative to prior art (WMD, metric learning), and explaining the underlying mathematics.
*   The technical presentation is both in-depth and remarkably clear. The derivation of the gradient for the S-WMD loss, which cleverly leverages the Sinkhorn distance and its dual, makes a complex topic accessible and easy to follow.
*   The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against 26 baselines across 8 diverse datasets. The results demonstrate the strong performance of the proposed S-WMD method, establishing a new state-of-the-art for kNN-based text classification on these benchmarks.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   While this is a strong piece of engineering, the conceptual novelty could be viewed as somewhat incremental. The core idea is to apply standard metric learning principles (specifically, an NCA-style loss) to the WMD framework. The main technical innovations lie in making this combination computationally feasible, which is a valuable but perhaps more of an engineering contribution.
*   The repeated comparisons to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) were slightly confusing. While WCD is effectively used for an efficient initialization (S-WCD), its role as a primary baseline is less clear, and its inclusion could potentially detract from the main story, which is the successful introduction of supervision to WMD.
*   A significant point that seems under-discussed is the role of the entropy regularization parameter, `lambda`. In Eq. (2), `lambda` controls the trade-off between the original transport cost and the entropic penalty. The authors state that "the larger lambda is, the closer this relaxation is to the original Wasserstein distance" and that they use `lambda = 10` as a trade-off. However, the impact of this choice is critical. In practice, the behavior of the Sinkhorn distance can vary dramatically with `lambda`. A very large `lambda` approximates the non-differentiable Wasserstein distance at the cost of slow convergence, while a small `lambda` leads to a very fast but heavily biased (more entropic) solution that behaves quite differently. It would be very insightful if the authors could provide some analysis or discussion on the sensitivity of their results to the choice of `lambda`. For instance, does the chosen value of 10 place the algorithm in a regime that closely mimics the true WMD, or does it operate in a more 'Sinkhorn-like' regime where the entropic regularization plays a major role in the learned metric?

Overall, this is a very well-executed paper with clear exposition and strong empirical results. The proposed method is a solid contribution to the field of text classification.
