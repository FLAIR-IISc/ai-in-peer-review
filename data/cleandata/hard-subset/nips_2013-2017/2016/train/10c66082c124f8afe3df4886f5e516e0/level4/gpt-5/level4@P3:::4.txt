Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparison. WMD models the distance between two documents as the optimal transport cost of moving one document’s word histogram to another in a pre-trained word embedding space (e.g., word2vec). The authors introduce supervision in two ways: (1) a learned linear transformation A applied to the word embeddings (ground metric), and (2) a learned nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms (with renormalization). Parameters are learned by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN error (NCA-style objective), with gradients approximated efficiently via entropically regularized optimal transport (Sinkhorn) to avoid nested, cubic-time transport problems. The method is initialized by learning an NCA metric on word centroid representations (S-WCD) and trained with mini-batches and nearest-neighbor filtering.

This builds on and connects several lines of work: WMD for unsupervised document distances (Kusner et al., ICML 2015), entropic OT/Sinkhorn distances for speed (Cuturi, NIPS 2013), and supervised metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML). It is also related to supervised EMD/ground-metric learning in vision (Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.), but applies these ideas to the word-embedding ground space and adds histogram reweighting.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation: how to incorporate supervision into WMD without reverting to high-dimensional document feature spaces.
- Sensible learning objective: NCA’s stochastic LOO loss is appropriate for kNN distances and directly ties to the authors’ evaluation protocol.
- Efficient approximation: the use of Sinkhorn to obtain unique plans and dual potentials enables practical gradient computation and training.
- Empirical breadth: eight datasets across varied tasks with 26 baselines. S-WMD often achieves the best or competitive kNN accuracy, with interpretable learned word weights.
- Reproducibility: code availability and reasonable complexity analysis; training appears fast given the approximation and batching.

Weaknesses and questions
- Motivation and constraints for w: Please better motivate histogram reweighting and specify constraints/regularization. While renormalization preserves the simplex, large disparities in w could overly distort document content. Are there nonnegativity, norm, sparsity, or smoothing constraints on w? What prevents degenerate solutions (e.g., putting all mass on a few words)? An ablation comparing A-only vs w-only vs both would clarify the contribution of w.
- Generalization to new documents: Clarify explicitly that A and w are global parameters. At test time, a new document uses the learned w for reweighting and the learned A for the ground metric; no per-document estimation is needed. Please state test-time complexity and memory.
- Novelty: The components (linear ground-metric learning, NCA loss, Sinkhorn) are known, and the formulation is a natural extension of WMD. The paper’s novelty is in combining them for text, deriving practical gradients, and demonstrating gains. Please position the contribution more precisely relative to supervised EMD/ground-metric learning and discuss why reweighting histograms is particularly important in the word-embedding setting.
- “Semantic difference” wording: In the abstract and introduction, “semantic difference” seems to refer to label-induced similarity (e.g., topic or authorship). Consider rephrasing to avoid conflating labels with inherent lexical semantics.
- Evaluation scope: Results are only for kNN. Since S-WMD learns a distance, this is reasonable, but additional evaluations (e.g., retrieval metrics, clustering quality, or using the learned distances as kernels) would strengthen claims of general utility.
- Missing ablations/sensitivity: Report sensitivity to λ (entropy regularization), batch size, neighbor filtering size N, and k in kNN. Include a runtime vs. accuracy trade-off plot and a comparison of the Sinkhorn-based approximation to the exact WMD (on small instances) to quantify approximation error.
- Regularization and optimization details: Provide details on regularization of A (e.g., Frobenius norm, low-rank r, constraints), optimization hyperparameters (learning rates, early stopping), and initialization sensitivity. The choice of squared Euclidean cost vs. Euclidean in the ground space could be discussed.
- Clarity and notation: There are a few typos/inconsistencies (e.g., “.2 Optimization” likely 3.2; “5, 21?” in references; mixing p and q for vocabulary sizes; the expression for α*λ, β*λ includes “1/p 1” which needs clearer dimensionality). References are incomplete and should be fixed.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is sound, and the Sinkhorn-based approximation is standard and appropriate. The gradient derivations are plausible; please tighten the dual-variable derivation for w and specify conditions under which the subgradient is used. The empirical results support the claims, though additional ablations would improve confidence.
- Clarity: Generally well written and easy to follow; suggest correcting typos, clarifying notation, and expanding the motivation for w.
- Originality: Moderate. The contribution is a careful and practical integration of known components adapted to WMD for text, plus the addition of w.
- Significance: Empirically useful and likely to be adopted by practitioners who already use WMD. Gains are meaningful on several datasets, and the approach is efficient. The conceptual advance is incremental rather than groundbreaking.

Specific answers to raised points
1) Why reweight w? Intuitively, w encodes supervised term importance akin to IDF/feature weighting, but learned jointly with the ground metric and renormalized to stay on the simplex. Please justify with theory or ablations, and describe constraints/regularization to prevent distortions.
2) Contribution significance: While not radical, the combination is nontrivial and practically valuable; emphasizing the efficient gradient design and strong empirical results will help.
3) New documents: w is global. For a new document, compute d̃ = (w ◦ d)/(wᵀd) and use A-transformed embeddings in the transport cost.
4) “Semantic difference”: Please clarify that the learned metric aligns distances with task-specific label similarity (e.g., topic/author), not a universal semantic notion.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical and efficient supervised extension of a popular document distance.
- Strong empirical performance across diverse datasets and many baselines.
- Clear formulation with available code.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty; missing ablations and some clarity/notation issues.
- Limited evaluation scope (kNN only) and incomplete discussion of regularization and sensitivity.

Overall
A solid, well-executed contribution with practical impact. I lean toward acceptance if the authors clarify the role and constraints of w, improve notation and references, and add key ablations/sensitivity analyses.
