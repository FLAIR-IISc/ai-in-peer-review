The paper proposes a new method for metric learning for textual documents, building upon previous work known as "Word Mover's Distance" (WMD). The original WMD is unsupervised, measuring the distance between the embeddings of words. This paper extends this to the supervised setting, where the supervision is of the type 'these documents belong to the same class'. The proposed method, called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), learns a linear transformation of the word embeddings and word importance weights to improve the distance metric.

The paper provides a clear and concise introduction to the background and related work, including a discussion of the limitations of previous methods. The authors also provide a detailed explanation of their proposed method, including the formulation of the optimization problem and the derivation of the gradient updates.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, with comparisons to 26 competitive baselines on 8 different document corpora. The results show that S-WMD achieves the lowest kNN test error on 5 out of 8 datasets, and outperforms all other methods on average across all datasets. The authors also provide visualizations of the learned distances using t-SNE, which demonstrate the effectiveness of S-WMD in capturing semantic similarities between documents.

One potential limitation of the paper is that the evaluation is focused solely on kNN classification, and it would be interesting to see how well S-WMD performs on other tasks, such as clustering or information retrieval. Additionally, the authors could provide more insight into the intuition behind the usage of word importance weights, and which of the two learned components (the linear transformation or the word importance weights) is responsible for the reported improvements.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed method is a significant contribution to the field of metric learning for textual documents. The authors have made their code available, which will facilitate the reproduction of their results and the adoption of their method by other researchers.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel method for supervised metric learning for textual documents.
* The method is thoroughly evaluated on a range of datasets and compared to multiple baselines.
* The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the background and related work.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Weaknesses:

* The evaluation is focused solely on kNN classification, and it would be interesting to see how well S-WMD performs on other tasks.
* The authors could provide more insight into the intuition behind the usage of word importance weights.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the learned distances and the impact of the supervision on the performance of the method.
