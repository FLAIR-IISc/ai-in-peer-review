This paper introduces a supervised metric learning technique that enhances the Word Mover's Distance [19], a transport-based metric applied to word2vec embeddings. The methodology is generalizable to any transport distance that relies on squared Euclidean distances following a linear projection. Beyond learning this projection to optimize performance, the model also learns a set of word-specific weights. In the context of document analysis, this involves re-weighting and re-normalizing word frequencies based on these learned weights. The authors present an efficient optimization algorithm for learning the parameters of their proposed metric. A comprehensive experimental evaluation on several document classification datasets demonstrates the approach's effectiveness, comparing it against a wide range of competitive baselines within a kNN classification framework.

+ This work offers a technically interesting and useful extension to the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance, and its utility is convincingly demonstrated on multiple kNN text classification tasks.

- The potential impact of the work may be limited, as the performance of the fully learned metric is only marginally better than a simpler baseline using the Earth Mover's Distance initialized with a "word centroid distance" (see the final two rows of Table 3). This suggests the paper's primary technical contributions do not yield significant improvements over an initialization scheme built on existing methods.

- The evaluation in Table 3 would be more complete if it included a comparison of the "word centroid embedding" (the weighted mean of a document's word embeddings) against all the tested metric learning algorithms (ITML, LMNN, NCA).

- An ablation study assessing the relative importance of the two learned components—the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A—is notably absent. It would be beneficial to see the performance when each of these components is used in isolation.

o The paper's primary originality lies in the optimization strategy (Lines 156–175). While the idea of incorporating linear metric learning into the Earth Mover's Distance is compelling, it represents a limited conceptual contribution on its own.

+ The paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read.

There are a few minor points that could be clarified:
- Line 152: The reference to "The authors" is ambiguous; it is unclear which of the cited works [1,7,8] is being discussed.
- Equation 11: For consistency, it seems that alpha and beta should be denoted with stars.
- It would be helpful to explicitly mention around line 170 that the described procedure is the Sinkhorn scaling method that was alluded to in the introduction on line 56.
