This paper proposes a supervised extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a document metric that leverages word embeddings to compute distances between documents. The authors introduce a technique to learn a supervised metric, called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), which incorporates supervision into the WMD by learning an affine transformation of the underlying word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector. The method is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the approach.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a thorough background on the WMD and related work on metric learning. The experimental evaluation is extensive, covering eight real-world text classification tasks and comparing the proposed method to 26 competitive baselines. The results show that S-WMD consistently outperforms the baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The strengths of the paper include its sound methodology, clear explanation, and thorough experimental evaluation. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the results, highlighting the benefits of supervision in learning a domain-specific metric.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it may be seen as an incremental extension to the WMD, rather than a fundamentally new approach. However, the authors address this concern by demonstrating the significant improvements in performance achieved by incorporating supervision into the WMD.

Overall, I believe that this is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. I would very much like to see this paper presented at NIPS.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a sound and well-motivated extension to the Word Mover's Distance.
* The experimental evaluation is extensive and thorough, covering a wide range of classification tasks and baseline methods.
* The results demonstrate significant improvements in performance achieved by incorporating supervision into the WMD.
* The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow and understand.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper may be seen as an incremental extension to the WMD, rather than a fundamentally new approach.
* Some readers may find the experimental evaluation to be overly extensive, with too many baseline methods and features considered.
