- Proposes Supervised-WMD (S-WMD): learn a linear embedding transform and word importance weights to create a supervised Word Mover’s Distance for document classification.  
- Frames metric learning by directly minimizing a stochastic approximation of leave-one-out classification error under WMD.  
- Uses entropy regularization (Sinkhorn distance) to approximate optimal transport, reducing computational complexity from cubic to quadratic.  
- Evaluated on eight text classification tasks and compared to 26 baselines, outperforming all on average and achieving lowest error on five datasets.  
- Builds on Kusner et al. (WMD), Cuturi (entropy-regularized OT), and NCA metric learning; leverages low-dimensional word embeddings rather than high-dimensional document representations.  
- Implementation and optimization details are clear, with efficient training, clever initialization, and batch gradient descent.  
- Notational inconsistencies: confusion between Euclidean and squared-Euclidean in equations — suggest consistently using p-Wasserstein (p=2) notation.  
- Reweighting approach (Equation 5) is not novel; similar histogram reweighting appears in Lebanon (2006) and Cuturi & Le (2014); authors should better cite and distinguish prior work.  
- Lacks ablation analysis on the contribution of the learned weight vector w; should show impact versus using only the linear transform A.  
- Originality is moderate: combining WMD with metric learning is novel, but technical components reuse established methods; clarify differences from Wang et al. (2015) which learns the ground metric.  
- Entropy-regularization use is a valuable practical contribution but should be explicitly contrasted with previous literature.  
- Significance is strong: supervised WMD yields consistent practical improvements and is applicable to real-world text classification.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and reproducible, though some mathematical details need tightening.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to fix notation and more thoroughly discuss and distinguish histogram reweighting from prior work.
