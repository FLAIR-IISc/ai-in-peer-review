

The paper introduces a supervised extension of Word Mover's Distance (WMD) for textual document metric learning, leveraging pairwise class labels ("documents belong to the same class") to learn a distance metric. The authors propose two key innovations: a linear transformation matrix **A** (a standard approach) and document-specific word importance weights **w**. The method shows promise in classification tasks, and the authors commit to releasing MATLAB code. While the core contribution is well-motivated, several aspects require clarification for reproducibility and deeper understanding.

The primary technical contribution lies in Eq. (5), which formalizes the use of **w** to weight word embeddings. However, the paper lacks a clear explanation of the intuition behind this formulation. Specifically:  
- How do the word weights **w** interact with the linear transformation **A**?  
- Which component (the weights **w** or the transformation **A**) drives the reported performance gains?  
A comparative ablation study isolating the contributions of **w** and **A** would strengthen the claims. The current presentation does not sufficiently distinguish their individual roles.

Regarding experimental evaluation, the paper assesses the metric via kNN classification accuracy. While this is standard, the evaluation would benefit from:  
- A baseline comparison against a simple classifier (e.g., SVM or logistic regression) trained on raw embeddings, to contextualize whether the metric learning itself is the key improvement or if the gains stem from the classification step.  
- Explicit specification of the k-value used in kNN classification. Is k fixed (e.g., k=5) or dataset-dependent? The paper does not clarify this, making reproducibility challenging.

Several technical points require clarification:  
1. **Equation 2 vs. Line 170**: The parameter λ in Equation 2 appears inconsistent with λ in Line 170. Please explicitly define λ’s role and confirm whether these are identical or distinct parameters.  
2. **"Forgiving" claim (Line 316)**: The assertion that the method "is particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words" lacks supporting evidence. Provide a concrete example or analysis demonstrating this property, especially relative to the original WMD.  
3. **Line 257**: The statement "on the right we also show the average error across datasets" cannot be located in Table 3. Please verify the table’s content or relocate this description.  
4. **Table 2**: The columns labeled "n" and "ne" are ambiguous. Define these abbreviations or clarify their meaning.  
5. **Equation 2 usage**: The context for applying Equation 2 is unclear. Specify when this equation is invoked during training or inference.

The paper’s strengths include a logical extension of WMD to the supervised setting and a clear empirical evaluation across multiple text datasets. The proposed word weights **w** offer an intuitive mechanism for adapting the metric to document-level semantics, which is promising. However, the current presentation obscures the relative impact of **w** versus **A**, and missing methodological details (k, λ, table labels) hinder critical assessment.

To improve the manuscript, the authors should:  
- Add a dedicated ablation study comparing the full model (with **w** and **A**) against variants using only **w** or only **A**.  
- Clarify all hyperparameters (k, λ) and notation (n/ne, λ consistency).  
- Provide a concrete example illustrating the "forgiving" property (Line 316).  
- Correct Table 2 labels and ensure all claims (e.g., Line 257) align with the presented results.

Overall, the work addresses a meaningful gap in metric learning for text, but the current version requires substantial refinement to fully justify its contributions. Addressing these points would significantly enhance the paper’s rigor and impact.
