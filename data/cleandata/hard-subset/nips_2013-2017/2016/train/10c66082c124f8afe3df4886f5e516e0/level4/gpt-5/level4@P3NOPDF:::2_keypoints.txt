- Presents a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance that learns task-specific weights, turning an unsupervised similarity into a discriminatively trained metric.
- Clear contribution: bridges a principled distance measure (WMD) with task-driven optimization.
- Sound methodology: coherent, well-explained formulation and learning procedure amenable to reproduction.
- Strong empirical results: thorough evaluation across multiple datasets and baselines with consistent, often large, performance gains.
- Practical relevance: preserves WMD’s interpretability and flexibility while adapting to specific tasks.
- Clarity and organization: well-written with logical flow from motivation to experiments.
- Need more detailed analysis of computational efficiency and scalability.
- Provide asymptotic and empirical runtime comparisons with vanilla WMD and other baselines.
- Evaluate scalability with respect to document length, vocabulary size, and dataset size.
- Describe any approximations, pruning strategies, or acceleration techniques and their impact on accuracy.
- Include ablations to quantify gains due solely to learning weights versus standard WMD and simpler parameterizations.
- Analyze learned weights for interpretability and alignment with intuitive semantic distinctions.
- Examine sensitivity to choice of word embeddings, initialization, and hyperparameters.
- Report performance as a function of labeled data size to show effectiveness with limited supervision.
- Investigate cross-domain generalization: do learned weights transfer to related tasks or domains?
- Clarify inclusion and tuning of strong neural baselines (e.g., CNN/RNN/fastText-like) to ensure fair comparisons.
- Ensure all baselines receive comparable hyperparameter tuning and resource budgets to avoid confounding factors.
- Improve reproducibility by releasing code, preprocessing scripts, hyperparameters, and detailed instructions.
- Overall assessment: solid, timely, and well-supported contribution recommended for acceptance, with suggested improvements on runtime/scalability analysis and ablations.
