This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific distance metric for text documents. It builds upon the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which measures the optimal transport cost between the word embeddings of two documents. The key contribution is an efficient algorithm that adapts this framework to a supervised setting by learning a linear transformation of the embedding space and a set of word-importance weights, guided by document class labels. To make the problem tractable, the authors minimize a stochastic, differentiable approximation of the leave-one-out k-NN classification error and use a relaxed version of the WMD problem to enable efficient gradient-based optimization. The authors promise to make their Matlab code available.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The extension of the powerful WMD framework to a supervised setting is a significant and well-motivated contribution. Learning a metric that is tailored to a specific classification task is a natural next step for WMD, and this paper presents a compelling approach to do so.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed optimization method is well-conceived. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to create a differentiable and efficient approximation of the WMD gradient is a clever solution that makes the approach computationally feasible on a large scale.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is extensive and convincing. S-WMD is compared against 26 competitive baselines across 8 diverse datasets. The results are consistently strong, demonstrating a clear and often substantial improvement over both the unsupervised WMD and other combinations of document representations and supervised metric learning techniques.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The proposed S-WMD metric is learned by optimizing two components: a linear transformation `A` of the word embeddings and a word-importance vector `w`. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an ablation study that isolates the contribution of each component. This analysis would provide valuable insight into whether the performance gains come primarily from transforming the geometric space (`A`), re-weighting the importance of individual words (`w`), or if the combination of both is essential.

While k-NN classification is the correct evaluation paradigm for a learned distance metric, the significance of the results could be better contextualized. For comparison, it would be interesting to see how a standard supervised classifier (e.g., an SVM with TF-IDF features) performs on the same datasets. This would help to establish how S-WMD with k-NN compares to alternative supervised learning approaches for these text classification tasks, not just to other distance metrics.

**Questions and Minor Comments:**

1.  **Role of λ:** The role of the regularization parameter `λ` could be clarified. Is the `λ` from the Sinkhorn distance formulation in Eq. (2) the same `λ` used to recover the dual variables on page 4? Details on how this hyperparameter was set would be helpful.
2.  **Justification of Claim:** The conclusion claims that S-WMD is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words" (line 316). While this is an intuitive benefit inherited from the original WMD, the paper could strengthen this claim by providing a concrete example or analysis demonstrating how the *supervised* components (the learned matrix `A` and weights `w`) specifically enhance this property.
3.  **Clarity in Results Section:**
    *   Line 257 refers to "the average error across datasets," but Table 2 appears to show the "average rank." Please clarify or correct this.
    *   The column headers "n" and "ne" in Table 2 are not defined in the text.
    *   The value of `k` used for k-NN classification is not specified. Please clarify whether `k` was tuned as a hyperparameter for each method and dataset or if a fixed value was used, as this detail is important for reproducibility.
