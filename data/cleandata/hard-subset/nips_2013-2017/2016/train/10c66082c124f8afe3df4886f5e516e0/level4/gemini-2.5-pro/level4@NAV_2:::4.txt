This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds upon the recently introduced Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which frames the distance between two documents as an optimal transport problem between their respective word embeddings. While WMD is entirely unsupervised, S-WMD incorporates label information to learn a metric that is better suited for a given classification task. The authors achieve this by learning two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation of the underlying word embedding space and (2) a vector of word-importance weights that re-scales the documents' word frequency histograms. A key technical contribution is an efficient optimization scheme that approximates the gradient of the WMD, which would otherwise be computationally prohibitive, by leveraging the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed method for approximating the gradient of the WMD to make the metric learning tractable is clever and well-executed. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing the proposed method against an extensive set of 26 competitive baselines across eight different datasets. The consistently strong performance demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The authors provide a comprehensive background on WMD and related metric learning techniques, positioning their work effectively within the existing literature. The motivation for the problem and the description of the proposed algorithm are presented logically.
*   **Significance:** The results are compelling and show that S-WMD can achieve state-of-the-art performance in kNN-based document classification. Given the strong performance of the original WMD, this supervised extension is an important contribution that is likely to be used by other researchers and practitioners working on text classification and retrieval tasks.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Originality:** While the results are significant, the conceptual contribution could be viewed as an incremental extension of prior work. The approach combines the existing WMD framework with a well-established metric learning objective (NCA) and learns a linear transformation (`A`) and a re-weighting vector (`w`). The primary novelty lies in the elegant solution to the complex optimization problem rather than a fundamentally new framework for document distance.
*   **Motivation of Word Re-weighting:** The motivation for learning the word-importance vector `w` could be further elaborated. This operation directly modifies the document's bag-of-words representation before the optimal transport calculation. It would be beneficial to discuss the implications of this, as one could argue it risks altering the document's core content. A more detailed justification for why this is a desirable and principled way to incorporate word importance, perhaps with an analysis of the learned weights (e.g., showing they down-weight stop words and up-weight discriminative terms), would strengthen the paper.
*   **Applicability to New Documents:** For clarity, it would be helpful to explicitly state how the learned metric is applied to new, unseen documents at test time. It is understood that the learned matrix `A` and vector `w` are global parameters, but a sentence confirming this would remove any potential ambiguity for the reader.
*   **Terminology:** A minor point on clarity: the abstract mentions capturing "semantic difference," which in the context of the experiments refers to class label distinctions (e.g., topic or sentiment). The authors might consider using more specific language, as "semantic difference" can have multiple interpretations (e.g., topic vs. authorship style), to further improve the paper's precision.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and effective method for supervised document metric learning. The strong empirical results and clear presentation make a compelling case for its utility. My main reservations concern the incremental nature of the conceptual framework and the need for a more thorough motivation for the word re-weighting component. Nevertheless, the technical contribution and the state-of-the-art results make this a valuable addition to the field. I recommend acceptance, provided the authors can address the points above in the final version.
