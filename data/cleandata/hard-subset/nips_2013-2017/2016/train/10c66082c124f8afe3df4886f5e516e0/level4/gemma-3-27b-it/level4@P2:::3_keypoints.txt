- Introduces S-WMD: learns an affine transformation of embeddings and a word-importance weight vector to supervise WMD for document similarity.
- Uses a relaxed optimal transport (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering to make supervised WMD computationally tractable.
- Demonstrates strong empirical gains on eight text classification tasks, outperforming 26 baselines.
- Builds on WMD by retaining its word-embedding based document distance while adding task-specific supervision.
- Main contribution: efficient incorporation of supervision into WMD via gradient approximation and metric learning.
- Strength: clear motivation and elegant combination of optimal transport, metric learning, and embeddings.
- Strength: practical efficiency of the proposed gradient approximation is a notable advantage.
- Strength: comprehensive experimental validation and comparisons against many baselines.
- Suggestion: include ablation studies, especially to quantify the importance of the word-importance vector.
- Weakness: mathematical notation and explanations are sometimes imprecise, hindering reproducibility.
- Weakness: lacks a clear algorithmic box or step-by-step description of S-WMD.
- Weakness: related work discussion should be expanded, specifically reweighting histograms (Lebanon, Le/Cuturi) and clarifying relation to Wang et al. (2016).
- Weakness: limited analysis of learned word weights—need insight into which words are upweighted and why.
- Weakness: should discuss limitations and justification for using squared Euclidean (2-Wasserstein) distance in optimization.
- Quality: technically sound overall, but would benefit from greater mathematical rigor and more detailed algorithmic description.
- Clarity: generally well-written with logical flow, but inconsistent notation and missing explicit algorithmic summary reduce readability.
- Originality: novel combination of techniques and an efficient gradient approximation; components individually are not entirely new.
- Significance: clear empirical improvement with practical applicability to IR, classification, and recommendation using pre-trained embeddings.
- Acceptance arguments: strong empirical results, novel and efficient supervised WMD approach, clear motivation, and comprehensive baselines.
- Rejection arguments: need for more rigorous math, clearer algorithmic description, deeper analysis of learned weights, and a more nuanced related work section.
- Overall assessment: recommend acceptance; strengths outweigh weaknesses, but addressing suggested improvements would strengthen the paper.
