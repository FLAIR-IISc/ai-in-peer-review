This paper presents a supervised approach to learning document distance, building upon the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD, originally motivated by the Earth Mover’s Distance, calculates optimal transport between word distributions to quantify document similarity. The initial formulation utilizes word2vec embeddings for word-level transport costs. This work proposes learning (1) a linear transformation of these word embeddings and (2) a reweighted word distribution, with the goal of increasing separation between documents of differing labels. The paper is clearly written and easy to follow, demonstrating a thorough understanding of related work through a comprehensive literature review. The authors provide experimental results on several datasets. However, the motivation behind the formulation requires further clarification, and the overall significance of the contribution appears limited. 

Specifically, several questions remain. First, the motivation for reweighting the word distribution using weights *w* is unclear. This reweighting has the potential to substantially alter the document’s representation, and the authors do not adequately explain why this manipulation is beneficial without fundamentally changing the document itself. Justification for this reweighting requires demonstrating its positive impact while preserving document identity. Second, the contribution of this work, namely adopting a linear transformation *A* and reweighting *w* within a Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) objective for supervised WMD, feels incremental. While a plausible extension, this formulation does not represent a groundbreaking or novel advance. Third, the practical application of this algorithm to new, unseen documents is unclear. Specifically, how is the reweighting *w* determined for documents lacking labeled training data? Finally, the use of “semantic difference” in the abstract is ambiguous. If this refers to document labels, it is a potentially misleading use of the term; authorship or topic differences do not readily equate to semantic differences. Clarification of what constitutes “semantic difference” is needed. 

Despite these concerns, the paper is well-written, and I do not oppose acceptance pending satisfactory responses to these questions and a more compelling articulation of the work’s significance.
