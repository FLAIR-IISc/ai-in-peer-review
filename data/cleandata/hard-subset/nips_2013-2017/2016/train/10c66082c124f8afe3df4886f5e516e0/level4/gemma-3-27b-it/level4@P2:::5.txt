This paper addresses the problem of text classification by extending the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), a recent approach leveraging optimal transport between word embeddings, to a supervised setting. The authors propose Supervised-WMD (S-WMD), which learns word-specific weights to improve classification performance. A key contribution is the use of the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient approximation to the Wasserstein distance underlying WMD, and the development of an efficient update rule. The method is evaluated on eight datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to 26 competitive baselines. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presents both the problem and the proposed solution. The mathematical foundations are thoroughly explained with commendable clarity. The authors effectively build upon prior work on WMD and metric learning, situating their contribution within the existing literature.

However, the novelty relative to the original WMD paper is somewhat limited. While extending WMD to a supervised setting is valuable, the core idea builds directly on existing work. The comparison to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) feels somewhat misplaced and even confusing; the relevance of this comparison isn’t fully established. Furthermore, the discussion of the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn distance approximation is surprisingly brief. The value of λ can significantly impact performance, potentially shifting the behavior of the method between a true Wasserstein distance (large λ) and a more entropic Sinkhorn distance (small λ). It would be beneficial to explore this trade-off and clarify whether the chosen λ promotes behavior closer to the Wasserstein distance or exploits the faster convergence properties of the Sinkhorn approximation. Understanding this choice is crucial as it dictates the characteristics of the learned distance metric.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong empirical results:** S-WMD consistently outperforms a large number of strong baselines across multiple datasets.
*   **Clear and thorough presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand, even for readers not intimately familiar with optimal transport.
*   **Practical contribution:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance provides a practical and efficient solution for supervised metric learning with word embeddings.
*   **Solid theoretical foundation:** The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the underlying mathematical principles.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Incremental novelty:** The contribution builds directly on the existing WMD work and the novelty is not groundbreaking.
*   **Unclear discussion of λ:** The limited discussion of the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn approximation hinders a full understanding of the method's behavior.
*   **Questionable comparison to WCD:** The relevance of comparing against WCD isn’t well-motivated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with valuable contributions to the field of text classification. The authors present a well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated method for learning a supervised word mover’s distance. While the novelty is incremental, the practical improvements and clear presentation make it a worthwhile contribution. Addressing the concerns regarding the discussion of λ and the WCD comparison would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
