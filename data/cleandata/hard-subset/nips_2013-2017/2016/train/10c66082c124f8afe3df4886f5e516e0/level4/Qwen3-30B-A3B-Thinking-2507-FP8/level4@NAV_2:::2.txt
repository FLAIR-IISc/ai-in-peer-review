

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), extending the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) [Kusner et al., 2015] by incorporating label information to learn a discriminative document metric. The authors address a critical limitation of WMD—its lack of task-specific supervision—by jointly learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out classification error. The key innovation lies in their efficient approximation of the WMD gradient using the Sinkhorn distance relaxation [Cuturi, 2013], avoiding the cubic complexity of exact WMD computation while enabling scalable training. The method is rigorously evaluated across 8 text classification datasets with 26 competitive baselines, including document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) and metric learning approaches (NCA, LMNN, ITML). S-WMD achieves the lowest test error on 5/8 datasets and outperforms all baselines on average, with particularly strong gains on smaller documents (e.g., 42% relative improvement on CLASSIC over NCA+TF-IDF).

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound and well-justified. The authors provide a clear derivation of the relaxed gradient (Eq. 8–10), demonstrate computational efficiency (O(p²) complexity), and validate results with rigorous ablation (S-WMD init. vs. trained) and visualization (t-SNE, word weights). The experimental design thoroughly addresses scalability (Table 3) and robustness (5-fold splits), with results consistently supporting the claims. The only minor gap is limited analysis of failure cases (e.g., why S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT).

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written. The background (Section 2) concisely contextualizes WMD within metric learning and optimal transport, while Section 3 clearly articulates the S-WMD formulation, gradient derivation, and optimization. The pseudo-code (Algorithm 1) and results (Tables 2–3, Figure 1) are highly accessible. The authors effectively address potential concerns about computational cost via the Sinkhorn approximation.

**Originality**: This work significantly advances the WMD framework by introducing *supervised metric learning directly within the word embedding space*, a departure from prior unsupervised document metrics (LSI, LDA) and standard metric learning (NCA, LMNN), which operate on document representations. The dual learning of affine transformations *and* word importance weights (Eq. 4–5) is novel, as existing metric learning methods do not reweight word histograms. Compared to recent NIPS work (e.g., Wang et al., 2015 on EMD metric learning), S-WMD uniquely integrates histogram reweighting and operates in the low-dimensional word space.

**Significance**: The results demonstrate a clear advancement in document distance metrics, with S-WMD becoming the new state-of-the-art for kNN classification. The method’s efficiency (training time comparable to baselines) and consistent outperformance across diverse tasks (news, sentiment, product) make it broadly applicable. The interpretability of learned word weights (Figure 2) further enhances its practical utility for domain-specific text analysis.

**Pro**: Strong theoretical grounding, exceptional experimental validation across 8 datasets with 26 baselines, efficient implementation, clear significance for text classification tasks.  
**Con**: Limited discussion of S-WMD’s limitations (e.g., performance on long documents like BBCSPORT) and minimal sensitivity analysis of the Sinkhorn parameter λ.  

This work is a high-quality, significant contribution to NIPS. It addresses a well-motivated gap in document metrics with a technically sound, efficient solution that delivers state-of-the-art results. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. I recommend acceptance.
