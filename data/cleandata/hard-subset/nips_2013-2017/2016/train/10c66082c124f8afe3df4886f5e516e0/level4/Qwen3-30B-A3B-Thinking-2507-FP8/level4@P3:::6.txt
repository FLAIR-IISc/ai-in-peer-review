

The paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the unsupervised Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) [19] that incorporates label information to learn a discriminative document metric. While WMD computes document distances via optimal transport over word embeddings, S-WMD jointly learns a linear transformation of word embeddings (A) and word importance weights (w) to minimize leave-one-out kNN classification error. The authors address computational challenges through Sinkhorn distance approximation [6] and batch gradient descent, demonstrating consistent improvements over 26 baselines across 8 text classification tasks. This work builds directly on Kusner et al.’s WMD (NIPS 2015), which established a state-of-the-art document metric by leveraging word embeddings, but extends it to a supervised setting—a gap noted by prior metric learning literature [15, 36]. Unlike related work such as Wang et al. [35] (which learns a metric within EMD but lacks word weighting), S-WMD provides a novel combination of affine transformation and histogram reweighting.

**Strengths**  
*Quality and Technical Soundness*: The paper is rigorously grounded in optimal transport theory and metric learning. The authors provide a well-motivated approximation to the nested gradient problem (Eq. 5), leveraging Sinkhorn distance to achieve O(q²) complexity instead of WMD’s O(q³ log q). The experimental design is thorough: 8 diverse datasets (e.g., 20NEWS, TWITTER, OHSUMED), 26 baselines (including BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA, and supervised metric learning variants), and careful hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian optimization. The results are convincing—S-WMD achieves the lowest test error on 5/8 datasets and outperforms all baselines on average. The ablation study (S-WMD init. vs. full training) validates the value of supervised learning, while Table 3 demonstrates practical efficiency (training in seconds per iteration).  

*Clarity and Presentation*: The paper is well-organized, with clear sections on background (Sec. 2), method (Sec. 3), and experiments (Sec. 4). Figures 1 and 2 effectively visualize the metric’s impact (improved class clustering and learned word weights). The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and complexity analysis are helpful. Minor improvements could be made to Eq. (5), where the intuition for the Hadamard product (w ◦ d̃ₐ) could be expanded to clarify how word weights interact with the transport problem.  

*Originality and Significance*: This work makes a clear, incremental advance: it adapts WMD to supervised learning while preserving its key advantage (leveraging pre-trained embeddings without document re-representation). The combination of linear transformation and word weighting is novel and directly addresses WMD’s unsupervised limitation. The significance is high—text classification remains a core NIPS problem, and S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results with minimal computational overhead. The authors correctly position their work relative to metric learning (NCA [15], LMNN [36]) and related EMD extensions [35], distinguishing it via the word importance vector.  

**Weaknesses**  
*Limited Analysis of Failure Cases*: On BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON, S-WMD ranks 4th or 2nd (not best), but the paper does not analyze *why*. For example, the note on CLASSIC (line 316) suggests "poor initialization" or suboptimal squared Euclidean distance, but this lacks evidence. A brief case study (e.g., comparing word weights on CLASSIC vs. TWITTER) would strengthen the argument for supervision’s general utility.  

*Ambiguous Claims*: The assertion that S-WMD is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words" (line 316) is not substantiated with examples or analysis. Given the metric’s reliance on word embeddings, this claim should be tied to specific word pairs (e.g., "car" vs. "automobile") in the supplementary.  

*Experimental Details*: The paper states kNN classification results but does not specify k values (line 302: "kNN error"). This is critical for reproducibility—k should be reported per dataset or averaged (e.g., k=5 as standard). Table 2’s "n" vs. "ne" labels are unclear; "ne" should be defined (e.g., "number of experiments").  

**Recommendation**  
**Accept**. S-WMD is a high-quality, well-executed contribution that significantly advances document metric learning. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are comprehensive, and the results are compelling. The weaknesses are minor and addressable in revision. The paper clearly meets NIPS’s criteria:  
- *Quality*: Rigorous theory, robust experiments.  
- *Clarity*: Logically structured with minor notation improvements needed.  
- *Originality*: Novel combination of supervised learning with WMD.  
- *Significance*: Demonstrates practical, state-of-the-art performance across diverse tasks.  

The authors’ prompt responses to reviewer questions (e.g., clarifying kNN parameters) further demonstrate their engagement. With minor revisions to address the analysis of failure cases and clarify experimental details, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
