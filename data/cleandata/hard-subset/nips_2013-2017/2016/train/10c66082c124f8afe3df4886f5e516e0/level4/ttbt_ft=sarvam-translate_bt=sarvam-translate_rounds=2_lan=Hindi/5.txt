The authors analyze the problem of classifying text into categories. They build upon a previous work that suggests monitoring word-specific weights using the Word Movers Distance (Wasserman). This contribution suggests using the Synchrone Distance as a proxy for improving the speed of Wasserman distances, and shows the best average performance against 26 baselines on 8 datasets. It is a very well-written paper that presents the subject well and analyzes the mathematics behind the problem in depth but with great clarity. Perhaps the only weaknesses are limited novelty compared to the previous Word Movers Distance paper, and the reference to the world center distance does not seem relevant, but rather confusing. I think the lambda (regularization factor) is poorly discussed. In my experience, the value of lambda has a very large impact, sometimes it is the dominant factor in equation (2). Theoretically, given a sufficiently large lambda at the cost of slower convergence, the results should be similar to the pure Wasserman distance, but most people using Synchrone use a very small lambda to improve the convergence rate, which achieves a behavior clearly different from Wasserman. Therefore, I am curious whether, in this contribution, lambda dictates a pure Wasserman behavior or a Synchrone (entropic) behavior.
