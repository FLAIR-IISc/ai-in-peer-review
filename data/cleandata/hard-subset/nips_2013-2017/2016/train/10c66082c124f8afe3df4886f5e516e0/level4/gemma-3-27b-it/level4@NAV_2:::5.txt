The authors address the problem of text classification by extending the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), a previously proposed metric based on optimal transport (Wasserstein distance), to incorporate supervision through learned word-specific weights. Their key contribution is an efficient technique utilizing the Sinkhorn distance as a tractable approximation to the WMD, enabling supervised training. The method is evaluated on eight datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to 26 competitive baselines. 

This is a well-written and clearly presented paper that effectively contextualizes the work within the existing literature on document distances and metric learning. The mathematical foundations are thoroughly explained, and the proposed approach is motivated with strong theoretical reasoning. The extensive experimental evaluation provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the Supervised-WMD (S-WMD) metric.

However, the incremental novelty relative to the original WMD paper could be more explicitly discussed. While the addition of supervision is significant, the extent to which it represents a substantial advancement beyond the initial unsupervised approach warrants further clarification. The comparison to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) feels somewhat tangential and occasionally introduces confusion rather than clear insight. 

A potential area for deeper investigation is the discussion of the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn distance approximation. The choice of λ can dramatically influence the behavior of the approximation, potentially shifting it from resembling a true Wasserstein distance (large λ) to a more strongly regularized, entropic approximation (small λ). It would be beneficial to analyze the impact of λ on performance and convergence, and to clarify whether the selected value promotes behavior closer to a Wasserstein distance or leverages the properties of the Sinkhorn approximation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results on a diverse set of datasets, consistently outperforming numerous baselines.
*   Clear and thorough presentation of the method, including mathematical derivations and algorithmic details.
*   Well-motivated approach that builds upon a recent and impactful technique (WMD).
*   Practical efficiency achieved through the use of the Sinkhorn distance approximation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty compared to the original WMD paper.
*   The connection to and relevance of the WCD comparisons is unclear.
*   Insufficient discussion of the regularization parameter λ and its impact on the approximation.
