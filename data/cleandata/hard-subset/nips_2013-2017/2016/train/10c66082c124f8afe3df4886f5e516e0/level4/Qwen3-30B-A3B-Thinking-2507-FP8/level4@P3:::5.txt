

The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of Kusner et al.'s Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision for document classification. WMD, introduced in NIPS 2015, computes document distances as optimal transport costs between word embeddings, achieving state-of-the-art results in kNN classification. The authors extend this by learning both an affine transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights to better align document distances with semantic class labels. Crucially, they employ the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient approximation to the exact WMD, enabling practical supervised learning. The paper evaluates S-WMD on eight document classification datasets against 26 baselines, demonstrating consistent improvements over prior work.

The paper's strengths are substantial. Methodologically, the authors provide a rigorous theoretical foundation for their approach, carefully deriving the gradient updates for both the linear transformation and word importance weights. The use of Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient approximation to the WMD is well-justified and effectively addresses the cubic time complexity of exact WMD computation. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering diverse document classification tasks (news categorization, sentiment analysis, product identification) with thorough comparison against multiple strong baselines. The results demonstrate that S-WMD achieves the lowest average error across all eight datasets, outperforming both unsupervised WMD and numerous document representation approaches.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear exposition of both the mathematical foundations and practical implementation details. The authors effectively relate their work to prior contributions in metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and document representation (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA), providing context for their methodological choices. The pseudocode and visualization of learned word weights (Figure 2) particularly enhance clarity. The paper's clarity is further evidenced by the fact that it provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a meaningful contribution by adapting the WMD framework to a supervised setting. While metric learning techniques have been applied to document classification before, the authors' approach is novel in its application to the WMD framework, leveraging the low-dimensional word embedding space rather than transforming the high-dimensional document representations. The incorporation of word importance weights provides additional flexibility compared to previous metric learning approaches. The paper clearly differentiates itself from the original WMD paper and related work through its explicit focus on supervised learning within the WMD framework.

The significance of the work is demonstrated by the consistent empirical improvements across multiple datasets. The S-WMD approach shows particular promise for applications where document similarity is critical but labeled data is limited, as it leverages the rich semantic information in pre-trained word embeddings while adapting to the specific classification task. The efficiency of the Sinkhorn approximation makes the method practical for real-world applications, as evidenced by the training times reported in Table 3.

The primary weakness lies in the discussion of the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn approximation. The paper states λ=10 but does not thoroughly explore its impact on the trade-off between approximation accuracy and computational efficiency. The authors acknowledge that larger λ values provide closer approximations to the true Wasserstein distance but note that this comes at the cost of slower convergence. However, they do not address the common practice of using small λ values (e.g., 0.1-1) to achieve faster convergence, which results in a behavior distinctly different from the Wasserstein distance. A more detailed analysis of how λ affects performance would strengthen the paper's theoretical foundation. The authors could clarify whether their method achieves behavior closer to pure Wasserstein distance or the entropic Sinkhorn approximation, and how this impacts the classification results.

The paper also could better explain why S-WMD underperforms compared to unsupervised WMD on certain datasets (e.g., CLASSIC), which would provide deeper insight into the method's limitations. While the paper mentions this as a possible issue with initialization, a more thorough analysis would be valuable.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document classification. The method is theoretically sound, well-executed, and empirically validated across multiple datasets. While the discussion of the regularization parameter could be strengthened, this is a minor concern given the paper's otherwise strong presentation and results. The work demonstrates a thoughtful extension of the WMD framework that should be of interest to the NIPS community working on document representation and metric learning. I recommend acceptance.
