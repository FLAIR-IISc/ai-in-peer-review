Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparison. WMD measures the optimal transport cost to move the bag-of-words of one document to another in a pre-trained word embedding space, achieving strong kNN classification performance but lacking a way to incorporate labels. S-WMD introduces two learned components: (i) a linear transformation A of the word embeddings (a generalized Euclidean/Mahalanobis metric in the embedding space), and (ii) a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights and renormalizes the word histograms. The parameters are trained to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss (NCA-style), thereby aligning the transport ground cost with the class labels.

Directly differentiating WMD is expensive and may be non-smooth. The paper adopts the entropically-regularized transport (Sinkhorn) relaxation to obtain a smooth, efficient O(q^2) approximation, using the scaling vectors u,v both to compute the relaxed transport T* and to recover approximate dual variables α,β for the gradient with respect to w. Training is further accelerated with (a) an initialization based on supervised metric learning on document centroids (S-WCD via NCA), (b) minibatching, and (c) restricting the soft neighborhood to the nearest neighbors under the cheap WCD lower bound. Experiments on 8 datasets with 26 baselines show S-WMD achieves the best average rank and the lowest error on 5/8 tasks, with reasonable training times.

This work connects several lines of research: metric learning for kNN (e.g., NCA, LMNN, ITML), optimal transport with entropy regularization (Sinkhorn), learning ground metrics for EMD/OT (e.g., Cuturi–Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.), and word-embedding-based document distances (WMD). The addition of histogram reweighting relates to prior work on metric learning for text histograms (e.g., Lebanon’s metric learning for text documents) and to Aitchison-geometry-based approaches (e.g., Le & Cuturi) that emphasize the importance of compositional structure and reweighting.

Strengths
- Clear, practical idea: learn A and w to adapt the WMD ground cost to labels; leverages strong unsupervised embeddings while injecting task supervision.
- Solid engineering: Sinkhorn-based approximation for gradients, dual-variable reuse, and efficient neighbor pruning yield a scalable training procedure.
- Thorough experimental study: 8 datasets, 26 baselines, fair protocol (kNN across methods, PCA + metric learning for representational baselines, BO for hyperparameters), and visualization.
- Strong empirical performance: best average rank and consistent gains on most datasets; training times are reasonable.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Regularized vs. unregularized WMD: The paper trains with entropic OT but is not fully explicit about whether evaluation also uses the regularized distance or exact WMD. Please clarify training/evaluation choices, the role of λ, and provide an ablation on λ (including a sensitivity study and the trade-off between accuracy and speed).
- Distance definition inconsistencies: Eq. 1 uses ||xi−xj||p, while the learned ground metric is squared Euclidean under A. Consider presenting WMD as p-Wasserstein more generally, and then specializing to the A-induced squared Euclidean ground cost to avoid confusion.
- Word-weight vector w: This is an important design element but is under-analyzed. Please include ablations isolating the contribution of w (A only, w only, both), its learned sparsity/density, and performance on long vs. short-document datasets. Clarify constraints/regularization on w (nonnegativity? normalization? any penalty to avoid degenerate concentration on rare words?).
- Mathematical clarity: Some derivations/notations are terse. It would help to (i) clearly state uniqueness conditions and subgradient use, (ii) separate the exact and relaxed objectives, (iii) define α,β recovery from u,v more explicitly and justify the approximation, and (iv) indicate when gradients use T*λ versus exact T*. A compact algorithm box summarizing training (inputs, initialization, Sinkhorn steps, gradient formulas, neighbor pruning, updates) would improve readability.
- Related work positioning: More clearly articulate differences from Cuturi–Avis (discrete ground metrics vs. continuous metric parameterization; use of Sinkhorn for scalable supervision) and from Wang et al. (single-pass OT vs. iterative supervised training; histogram reweighting absent there). Please also discuss connections to histogram metric learning in text (e.g., Lebanon) and Aitchison/Compositional approaches (Le & Cuturi).
- Fairness and reporting: Training time excludes initialization. Please report end-to-end time (initialization + S-WMD) and memory. Also clarify neighbor list size sensitivity (N) and its effect on accuracy/time.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with a reasonable and principled use of entropic OT for scalable gradients. The NCA objective and minibatch/neighbor pruning are standard and appropriate.
- Some theoretical claims (e.g., “arbitrarily close” approximation) would benefit from a brief discussion of how λ and iteration count affect approximation quality and convergence in practice.
- Empirical evaluation is strong and supports the claims; an ablation on w and λ would further strengthen it.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but notation and the toggling between exact and relaxed formulations can be tightened. Unify the p-Wasserstein presentation, define all variables at first use, and add the requested algorithm box.
- Minor editorial issues (typos, inconsistent equation formatting) should be cleaned up.

Originality
- The components are known (WMD, Sinkhorn OT, NCA, Mahalanobis learning), but their combination for supervised document distance with joint learning of A and w is novel and well-motivated. The use of Sinkhorn dual variables to obtain gradients for w is a neat, practical twist.
- Relation to prior OT metric-learning works should be positioned more explicitly.

Significance
- Addresses an impactful problem—supervising WMD—yielding consistent gains over strong baselines across diverse text tasks. Likely to be adopted by practitioners using WMD and kNN or retrieval-based systems.
- The approach is modular and should transfer to other embeddings and tasks.

Questions for the authors
- How much of the gain comes from A vs. w? Please include ablations and qualitative analysis of learned word weights.
- Do you evaluate with regularized or unregularized transport at test time? How sensitive are results to λ?
- What constraints/regularization are imposed on w? Any mechanisms to prevent overfitting to rare words?
- How sensitive is performance to the neighbor set size N and batch size B?

Pros for acceptance
- Practical and effective supervised extension of WMD with strong empirical gains.
- Scalable training via Sinkhorn and thoughtful engineering.
- Broad relevance and likely impact.

Cons for acceptance
- Some mathematical and presentation inconsistencies (p vs. squared ground cost; exact vs. relaxed objective) reduce clarity.
- Missing ablations on key design choices (w, λ, N) and end-to-end runtime reporting.

Recommendation
Accept. The method is simple, well-motivated, and empirically strong. With improved clarity around the regularized formulation, an algorithm summary, and ablations on w and λ, this would be a solid contribution to supervised metric learning and OT-based document distances.
