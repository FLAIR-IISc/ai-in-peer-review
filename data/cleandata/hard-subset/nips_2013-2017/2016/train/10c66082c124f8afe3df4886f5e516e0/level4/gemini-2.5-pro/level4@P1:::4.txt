This paper proposes a method for learning a supervised document distance based on the Word Mover's Distance (WMD). The original WMD, inspired by the Earth Mover's Distance, calculates the optimal transport cost between the word distributions of two documents, using pre-trained word embeddings (like word2vec) to define the ground distance between individual words. This paper extends the unsupervised WMD to a supervised setting by learning two components: (1) a linear transformation of the word embedding space, and (2) a re-weighting vector for the word distributions. The goal is to learn a metric that brings documents with the same label closer together and pushes those with different labels further apart, optimized by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out k-NN classification error, similar to Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA).

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow. The authors provide a thorough review of related work, demonstrating a strong command of the relevant literature in document representation, metric learning, and optimal transport. The empirical evaluation is extensive, comparing the proposed Supervised WMD (S-WMD) against a large suite of competitive baselines across eight diverse datasets, which strengthens the paper's conclusions.

Despite these strengths, there are several points that could be clarified to improve the paper's contribution and impact:

1.  **Motivation for Word Re-weighting:** The motivation for re-weighting the word distribution with the vector `w` needs more explanation. This mechanism seems powerful, but it also raises the concern that it could fundamentally alter a document's content by, for example, down-weighting key terms to zero. It would be beneficial for the authors to provide more intuition or justification for this component. For instance, does this re-weighting primarily learn to suppress common stop-words, or does it learn more complex, task-specific word importances? An analysis showing that the re-weighted document remains faithful to the original would help justify this design choice.

2.  **Significance of the Contribution:** The core technical contribution involves applying a linear transformation `A` and a re-weighting vector `w` to the WMD formulation and optimizing them with an NCA-style objective function. While empirically effective, this approach could be perceived as a straightforward combination of existing techniques (WMD and NCA). The novelty of the work would be more apparent if the authors emphasized the specific technical challenges that were overcome. For example, the efficient gradient approximation for the WMD distance is a key part of making this model practical, and highlighting this aspect more could better frame the work's originality.

3.  **Generalization to New Documents:** The paper should explicitly state how the learned metric is applied to new, unseen documents at test time. Specifically, how is the word importance vector `w` used? Is `w` a global vector learned from the entire training corpus that is then applied to re-weight the word counts of any new document? Clarifying this process is crucial for understanding the practical application of the method.

4.  **Terminology:** This is a minor point, but the abstract mentions learning to capture "semantic differences," which the experiments operationalize as differences in class labels (e.g., topic, author, sentiment). While class labels are a form of semantic distinction, the term "semantic" in NLP often implies a broader, more universal meaning. Using more precise terms like "task-specific similarity" or "class-discriminative distance" might more accurately describe what the model is learning.

In summary, the paper presents a well-executed and empirically strong piece of work. The proposed method is a logical and effective extension of WMD to the supervised domain. I am leaning towards acceptance but would strongly encourage the authors to address the points above, particularly regarding the motivation for the word re-weighting and the perceived incrementality of the contribution, in their final version.
