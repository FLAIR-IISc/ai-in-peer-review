This paper introduces a supervised metric learning approach leveraging the word-movers distance [19], a transport distance applied to word2vec embeddings. The method generalizes to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances following a linear projection. Critically, the approach learns both this projection matrix and a weighting for each word, adapting word frequencies via learned weights and subsequent re-normalization – a component specifically designed for document-level tasks. The authors present an efficient optimization procedure for learning these parameters and demonstrate the effectiveness of the learned metric through extensive experiments. These experiments compare the proposed approach against numerous strong baselines on several document classification datasets, employing a k-nearest neighbors (kNN) classifier. 

This work represents a technically sound extension of the recent word-movers distance, and the empirical results confirm its utility in kNN-based text classification. However, the potential impact appears somewhat limited. The results in Table 3 suggest that an initialization strategy based on the “word centroid distance” – essentially using the earth-movers distance with a metric learning approach initialized with word centroids – achieves comparable performance to the full, learned distance. Specifically, the performance difference between these methods (last two rows of Table 3) raises questions regarding the incremental benefit of the proposed full learning procedure over this simpler initialization utilizing existing techniques. 

Furthermore, the evaluation would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison. I would expect to see an assessment of a simple “word centroid embedding” (the weighted mean of word embeddings for a document) used in conjunction with established metric learning algorithms like ITML, LMNN, and NCA. Including this baseline would provide a clearer understanding of the contribution of the proposed method beyond simply utilizing informative document representations. 

An additional missing element is an analysis of the relative importance of the learned word-specific weights and the projection matrix A. Evaluating the performance of each component in isolation – with only the weights active, or only the projection matrix – would provide valuable insights into their individual contributions to the overall effectiveness of the approach. While the optimization method (lines 156-175) is a notable contribution, the core idea of applying linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance framework, though interesting, represents a relatively incremental advancement.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. There are a few minor points that could be clarified:

*   Line 152: The reference to “the authors” is ambiguous. Please specify which paper is being referenced (e.g., [1, 7, 8]).
*   Equation 11: Consider adding stars to α and β to indicate they are intermediate variables derived from the optimization process.
*   Around line 170, explicitly mention that the scaling referred to is the Sinkhorn scaling, as introduced in line 56. This will improve readability and ensure clarity for the reader.
