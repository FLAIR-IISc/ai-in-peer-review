This paper addresses the problem of supervised text classification by extending the Word Mover's Distance (WMD). The primary contributions are twofold: first, the authors introduce a supervised framework for WMD by learning word-specific weights, and second, they propose using the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient proxy for the Wasserstein distance. The method is evaluated on 8 datasets against 26 baselines, where it achieves the best average performance.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, presenting the topic and the underlying mathematical analysis with great depth and clarity.

Despite these strengths, I have a few concerns. The novelty of the contribution appears somewhat incremental when compared to the original Word Mover's Distance paper. Additionally, the comparison against the Word Centroid Distance seems misplaced and adds confusion rather than clarifying the method's benefits.

More importantly, the role of the regularization parameter, lambda (λ), is insufficiently discussed. In practice, the value of λ can have a significant impact on the Sinkhorn distance, sometimes becoming the dominant factor in eq(2). Theoretically, a large λ should yield results similar to the pure Wasserstein distance, albeit at the cost of slower convergence. However, many applications of Sinkhorn use a small λ to accelerate convergence, which results in a behavior qualitatively different from the Wasserstein distance due to the entropic regularization. It is therefore unclear whether the proposed method operates in a regime that emulates the pure Wasserstein distance or one that is dominated by the entropic behavior of the Sinkhorn approximation. Further discussion on the choice and sensitivity of λ in the experiments would significantly strengthen the paper.
