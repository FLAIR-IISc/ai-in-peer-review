- Introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD) that learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and word-specific importance weights for document classification.  
- Uses a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss and Sinkhorn distance to approximate WMD efficiently, avoiding cubic complexity.  
- Builds on Kusner et al.'s WMD but performs metric learning in the word-embedding space, keeping low dimensionality.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance, outperforming 26 baselines across 8 classification tasks with thorough evaluation and cross-validation.  
- Experimental comparisons include diverse document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) and metric learning baselines (NCA, ITML, LMNN).  
- Paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical justification for approximations and clear connections between theory and implementation.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written with precise mathematical formulations and helpful supplementary material.  
- The combination of linear projection and word importance weighting within optimal transport is an original and meaningful contribution.  
- Efficient optimization using Sinkhorn scaling is a notable methodological novelty.  
- Approach addresses a key limitation of unsupervised WMD by incorporating supervision, yielding consistent gains, especially on short documents.  
- Major weakness: S-WMD shows only marginal improvement over its initialization S-WCD, questioning the added value of the learning procedure.  
- Missing critical comparison of weighted word centroid embeddings (WCD) with standard metric learning (NCA, ITML, LMNN) to contextualize gains.  
- Does not isolate effects of word importance weights versus the linear projection A, leaving unclear which component drives performance.  
- Unclear justification for the choice of Sinkhorn regularization parameter (λ=10) and why it is optimal.  
- Pro arguments: meaningful application of optimal transport, well-motivated and justified method, comprehensive experiments, practical efficiency, and clear presentation.  
- Con arguments: marginal gains over initialization, insufficient demonstration of learning procedure's unique contribution, missing component-isolation experiments, and unclear distinction from prior metric learning work.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper worth acceptance with minor revisions to better quantify the learning procedure's contribution and isolate component effects.
