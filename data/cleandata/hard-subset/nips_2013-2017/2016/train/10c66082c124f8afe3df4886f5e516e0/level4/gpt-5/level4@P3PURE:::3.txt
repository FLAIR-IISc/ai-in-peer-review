Summary
The paper proposes learning the parameters of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD)—i.e., Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) on text documents where bin-to-bin dissimilarities are computed from word embeddings—by parameterizing the EMD ground metric with a Mahalanobis distance over vector representations. In addition, the authors learn a reweighting of histogram coefficients. They derive gradients for NCA-type objectives and present compelling experimental evidence. The paper reads as a well-executed synthesis of known ideas, and the empirical section is particularly strong. I support acceptance.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and coherent idea that ties together optimal transport with metric learning.
- Practical contribution: gradients for NCA-type costs in this setting and an implementation that is nontrivial in practice.
- Strong, convincing experimental results.

Areas for improvement
- Clarify the relationship and interplay between regularized and unregularized OT formulations. It is not always clear which formulation is used where, and how this choice affects the learned parameters and gradients.
- Improve mathematical rigor: some computations and notations are sloppy; tightening the derivations would help reproducibility and clarity.
- Add an algorithmic box (pseudo-code) to clearly summarize the full training pipeline (objective, regularization, parameterization of the ground metric, reweighting, and gradient computation).

Minor comments
- In Eq. 1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used as the cost; in Eq. 6, for Mahalanobis metric learning, the cost effectively becomes the squared Euclidean metric (i.e., the 2-Wasserstein setting). To avoid inconsistency, it would be clearer to present WMD as the p-Wasserstein distance between point clouds of word embeddings, with p specified consistently.
- The “reweighting” approach in Eq. 5 is not new; see Guy Lebanon’s work on metric learning for text documents (“metric learning for text documents”) and also “Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations” by Le and Cuturi.
- Please provide experimental insights into the importance of the weight vector w in the learned metric. Does this innovation matter in practice? How sensitive are the results to learning w, and in what scenarios is it most relevant?

Related work and positioning
- The paper clearly builds on work by Cuturi/Avis, but here entropy regularization is used, which both accelerates computations and improves scalability. This point should be emphasized, as it is central to the practicality of the approach.
- The cost structure also differs: whereas prior work used a discrete cost, the present paper uses a continuous cost parameterized by linear maps. This is an important distinction that is currently missing from the discussion.
- The paper also draws from Wang et al. However, that work, as it stands, is not publishable: the OT optimization is run only once and, as proposed, does not work. This comparison should be clarified so readers understand the advantages and correctness of the present approach.

Recommendation
Strong accept. The method is a compelling combination of existing techniques with solid empirical validation. With improved clarity on regularization choices, sharper mathematical presentation, and an algorithmic summary, the paper would be even stronger.
