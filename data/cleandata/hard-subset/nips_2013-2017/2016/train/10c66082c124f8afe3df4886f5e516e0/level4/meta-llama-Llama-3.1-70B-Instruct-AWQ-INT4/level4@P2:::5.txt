The authors present a novel approach to supervised text classification by learning a supervised metric, called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), which builds upon the previously proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD). The key contribution of this work is the incorporation of supervision into the WMD, enabling the learning of word-specific weights that improve the accuracy of text classification tasks. The authors achieve this by utilizing the Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for the Wasserstein distance, which significantly improves the computational efficiency of the approach.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the mathematical formulation of the problem. The evaluation of S-WMD on eight datasets against 26 baselines demonstrates its superior performance, making it a strong contender for state-of-the-art text classification methods.

One potential weakness of the paper is the limited novelty compared to the original WMD paper. While the incorporation of supervision is a significant contribution, it is not entirely clear how this approach differs from other metric learning methods. Additionally, the references to the World Centroid Distance (WCD) may be confusing, as it is not entirely clear how WCD relates to the proposed S-WMD method.

A more in-depth discussion of the regularizing factor, lambda, would be beneficial. In the authors' experience, the value of lambda can have a significant impact on the results, and it is unclear whether the chosen value of lambda (10) dictates a pure Wasserstein behavior or a Sinkhorn (entropic) behavior. Theoretically, results should be similar to the pure Wasserstein distance given a large enough lambda at the cost of slower convergence. However, most people using Sinkhorn use a very small lambda to improve convergence rate, achieving a behavior plainly different from Wasserstein.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel approach to supervised text classification.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the mathematical formulation of the problem.
* The evaluation of S-WMD on eight datasets against 26 baselines demonstrates its superior performance.

Weaknesses:

* Limited novelty compared to the original WMD paper.
* Unclear discussion of the regularizing factor, lambda.
* References to the World Centroid Distance may be confusing.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to supervised text classification.
* The evaluation of S-WMD on eight datasets against 26 baselines demonstrates its superior performance.
* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the mathematical formulation of the problem.

Arguments for rejection:

* Limited novelty compared to the original WMD paper.
* Unclear discussion of the regularizing factor, lambda.
* References to the World Centroid Distance may be confusing.

Overall, the paper presents a strong contribution to the field of text classification, and with some revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, it has the potential to be a top-tier publication.
