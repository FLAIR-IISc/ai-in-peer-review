This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach that extends the recently introduced "Word Mover's Distance" (WMD), an optimal transport distance applied to word2vec embeddings. The proposed method is generalizable to any transport distance based on a squared Euclidean metric under a linear projection. In addition to learning the linear projection matrix to maximize performance, the approach also learns a set of word-specific weights. This latter component, designed for document applications, involves re-weighting each word's frequency by a learned parameter and subsequently re-normalizing the document's word distribution. The authors present an efficient optimization method for learning the parameterized metric. A thorough experimental evaluation on several document classification datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach, comparing it against a large number of strong baselines using a k-NN classifier.

**Strengths:**

*   This work provides a technically interesting and useful extension to the Word Mover's Distance. Its effectiveness is well-demonstrated on several k-NN text classification tasks.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and a pleasure to read.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The potential impact of this work may be limited by the finding that the full proposed method performs very similarly to a simpler baseline. Specifically, initializing the Earth Mover's Distance with a metric learned via a "word centroid distance" performs almost as well as the end-to-end learned metric proposed in the paper (as seen by comparing the last two rows of Table 3). This suggests that the paper's main technical contributions—the joint optimization of the projection matrix and word weights—offer only a marginal improvement over an initialization method based on existing techniques.
*   The evaluation in Table 3 would be more comprehensive if it included a comparison of the "word centroid embedding" (the weighted mean of a document's word embeddings) combined with the other metric learning baselines tested (ITML, LMNN, NCA). This would help to isolate the benefits of the WMD framework from the benefits of standard metric learning on a simple document representation.
*   Additionally, the evaluation lacks an ablation study to assess the relative contributions of the two learned components: the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A. It would be very informative to see the performance when only one of these components is learned at a time, to understand the source of the performance gains.

**Originality:**

*   The primary novelty appears to lie in the optimization procedure for the metric (Lines 156–175). While the core idea of applying linear metric learning within the Earth Mover's Distance framework is interesting, it is a relatively incremental contribution on its own.

**Minor Issues and Clarifications:**

*   **Line 152:** The phrase "The authors" is ambiguous; it is unclear which of the cited papers ([1, 7, 8]) is being referred to.
*   **Equation 11:** It seems that alpha and beta should carry the star notation (α*, β*) to be consistent with the surrounding text and their status as optimal dual variables.
*   **Line 170:** It would be helpful to explicitly state that the method being described is the Sinkhorn scaling algorithm mentioned in the introduction (Line 56) for better readability.
