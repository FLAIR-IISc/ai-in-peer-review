

This paper proposes an extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) metric for document classification by introducing a supervised learning approach called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD). The authors address the limitation of the original WMD, which is entirely unsupervised, by learning a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a word importance weight vector to improve document classification performance. The method minimizes the stochastic leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error using a relaxed optimal transport formulation (Sinkhorn distance) to make the training computationally feasible. The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over 26 baselines across eight text classification datasets.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature by building upon Kusner et al.'s WMD (NIPS 2015), which was a significant advancement in document representation by leveraging word embeddings to compute document distances through optimal transport. The authors effectively situate their work within the broader context of metric learning (e.g., NCA, LMNN, ITML) and document representation techniques (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA), providing a thorough review of related work that demonstrates strong familiarity with the field.

**Strengths:**

*Quality*: The paper is technically sound with a well-justified approach to the challenging problem of incorporating supervision into WMD. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their relaxation of the optimal transport problem using the Sinkhorn distance, which significantly improves computational efficiency while maintaining accuracy. The experimental design is comprehensive, evaluating the method across eight diverse text classification tasks with rigorous comparison to 26 baselines, including multiple document representations combined with standard metric learning approaches. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach through ablation studies and analysis of the learned word weights.

*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the background, motivation, and technical details, making the complex optimal transport concepts accessible to readers without prior expertise. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the algorithmic description is thorough. The visualization of document embeddings using t-SNE effectively demonstrates the improvement of S-WMD over WMD.

*Originality*: While the core idea of extending WMD to a supervised setting is not entirely novel (as the authors acknowledge), the specific formulation using a linear transformation of word embeddings combined with word importance weights represents a meaningful and practical extension. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from prior work, particularly from Wang et al.'s EMD-based metric learning (ICML 2015), by introducing histogram reweighting that adds extra flexibility.

*Significance*: The results demonstrate significant improvements in classification accuracy across multiple datasets (achieving the best average performance among all 27 methods), making the work highly relevant for practical text classification tasks. The method builds upon the successful WMD framework while addressing a key limitation (lack of supervision), which is particularly valuable for applications where labeled data is available. The efficiency of the training process (as shown in Table 3) makes the approach practical for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*Originality*: While the paper makes a solid contribution, the novelty is somewhat incremental. The core idea of learning a linear transformation and reweighting features for metric learning is well-established in the literature (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The authors could better articulate why their specific combination with the WMD framework is novel and why it outperforms direct application of standard metric learning to document representations.

*Significance*: The paper would benefit from a more compelling discussion of the broader impact of their work. While the empirical results are strong, the paper does not sufficiently address why this approach would be preferred over simply applying standard metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) to a pre-learned document representation (e.g., LDA, mSDA). The authors should clarify the unique advantages of their approach that make it superior to these alternatives.

*Technical details*: The motivation for the word importance weights (w) requires further clarification. The authors note that "different classification tasks and data sets may value words differently," but they do not provide specific examples of how this reweighting improves the document representation without fundamentally altering the document's content. It would be helpful to see examples of how the learned weights align with document semantics.

*Generalization*: The paper does not sufficiently address how the method handles out-of-vocabulary words or new documents during inference. The authors should clarify whether the word importance weights (w) are learned per-document or globally, and how the method would handle unseen words in a test document.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of document representation and metric learning. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are comprehensive and well-executed, and the results demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods. The paper is well-written and accessible, and the authors have demonstrated a deep understanding of the relevant literature.

I recommend acceptance. The minor weaknesses in articulating the novelty and broader significance are outweighed by the strong empirical results, clear writing, and practical contribution to the field. The authors should address the questions about word importance weights and out-of-vocabulary words in the camera-ready version to further strengthen the paper. The work is well-suited for NIPS as it advances the state-of-the-art in a practical and well-motivated direction.
