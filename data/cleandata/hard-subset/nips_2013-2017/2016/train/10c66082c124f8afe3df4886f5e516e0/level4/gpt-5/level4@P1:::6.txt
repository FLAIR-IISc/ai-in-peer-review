Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper extends the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) of Kusner et al. to a supervised setting. The proposed Supervised WMD (S-WMD) learns (i) a linear transformation A of the word embedding space and (ii) a vector of word-importance weights w that reweights document histograms. Supervision is incorporated by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification loss in the spirit of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). To make optimization tractable, the authors replace the exact optimal transport in WMD with the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance (Cuturi), enabling efficient approximate gradients via the dual and the scaling vectors u, v. They also propose an initialization based on NCA over Word Centroid Distance (WCD), followed by batch training with a nearest-neighbor filtering heuristic. Experiments on 8 datasets against 26 baselines (including TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA, WCD, WMD, and metric learning methods like NCA/LMNN/ITML after PCA) show S-WMD generally improves kNN accuracy, with code promised in Matlab.

Strengths:
- Clear and timely problem: incorporating label supervision into WMD, a strong unsupervised document distance.
- Technical approach: well-motivated use of entropy-regularized optimal transport for efficiency; NCA-style objective aligns the metric with kNN classification; joint learning of A and w is a natural way to adapt both geometry and vocabulary salience.
- Empirical scope: thorough comparison across many datasets and baselines; visualization of embeddings and learned word weights; training time measurements.
- Practicality: efficient training via Sinkhorn relaxation and batching; reasonable hyperparameter choices and tuning.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Intuition for w in Eq. (5): I would appreciate a clearer explanation of how the histogram reweighting d̃ = (w ◦ d)/(w>d) interacts with transport and how it differs in effect from changing A. In particular, which component (A vs w) drives most of the observed gains? An ablation (only A, only w) would help isolate their contributions.
- Use of Eq. (2) and λ: It is not fully clear when Eq. (2) is used operationally in training. Also, the λ in Eq. (2) appears to differ from the λ mentioned around line 170—please clarify the notation and which λ controls the Sinkhorn regularization in practice.
- Claim about “forgiving” distances (line 316): What empirical or theoretical evidence supports the statement that the learned S-WMD is “particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words”? WMD has this property due to embeddings and transport; please clarify how supervision further enhances this effect or provide examples/analysis.
- Reporting details and minor clarity issues:
  - Line 257: “on the right we also show the average error across datasets.” I could not find this; is it meant to be in Table 3?
  - Table 2: What is the difference between “n” and “ne”?
  - k in kNN: How is k chosen (per dataset? per method?) and how is it tuned?
- Evaluation baselines: While evaluating a metric via kNN is natural, it would be helpful—if only for context—to mention how a simple classifier (e.g., linear SVM/LogReg on TF-IDF or averaged embeddings) performs on the same splits. After the authors’ response, this concern is less pressing, but a brief comparison or discussion would still aid readers. [I appreciated the careful responses of the authors, which clarified my questions.]
- Sensitivity and robustness: Performance drops on some datasets (e.g., BBCSPORT, CLASSIC) are attributed to initialization and the squared Euclidean ground metric; this suggests sensitivity. A brief robustness analysis (e.g., alternative ground metrics p=1 vs p=2, different initializations) would strengthen the paper.
- Approximation accuracy: The training relies on the relaxed transport plan T*λ and dual variables α*, β* from u, v. Some discussion of approximation error vs. λ, and whether the approximation materially changes nearest-neighbor rankings, would be valuable.

Quality:
The method is technically sound and builds on solid foundations (Wasserstein/OT, Sinkhorn, NCA). The gradient derivations and use of the dual variables are reasonable, and complexity claims are credible. However, some notational ambiguities (λ) and missing experimental details (k, ablations) detract from clarity.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized, but the roles of A versus w, the precise use of Eq. (2) in optimization, and certain reporting details need clarification. Figures and visualizations are helpful.

Originality:
The combination of supervised metric learning with WMD, learning both a ground metric A and histogram weights w, is a meaningful extension beyond prior supervised EMD work in vision and earlier document-distance baselines.

Significance:
Given the strong performance and generality of the approach, S-WMD is likely to be useful to practitioners working on document similarity and classification, especially when embeddings are available and labels are limited.

Pros for acceptance:
- Practical, well-motivated supervised extension to a widely used document distance.
- Efficient training via Sinkhorn; scalable and implementable.
- Strong empirical results across many datasets and baselines.
- Insightful visualizations and promised code availability.

Cons for acceptance:
- Insufficient ablation to disentangle contributions of A vs w.
- Notational/clarity issues (use of λ, reporting details).
- Sensitivity to initialization/ground metric not fully explored.
- Limited discussion of approximation error due to Sinkhorn relaxation.

Questions to authors:
- Please clarify when Eq. (2) is used in training and reconcile the λ notation (including the value used, e.g., λ=10).
- Provide intuition and, if possible, an ablation for the impact of w vs A in Eq. (5).
- Substantiate the “forgiving” claim with analysis or examples.
- Specify where the “average error across datasets” appears, define “n” vs “ne” in Table 2, and state how k in kNN is chosen.
