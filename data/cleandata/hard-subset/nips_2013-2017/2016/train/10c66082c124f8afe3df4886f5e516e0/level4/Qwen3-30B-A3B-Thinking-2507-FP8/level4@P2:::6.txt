

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), extending Kusner et al.’s unsupervised Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) to incorporate label supervision for text classification. The authors propose learning a linear transformation matrix **A** applied to word embeddings and a word importance weight vector **w** to refine document distances. Crucially, they avoid the cubic complexity of exact WMD computation by leveraging Cuturi’s Sinkhorn distance approximation, enabling efficient gradient-based training that minimizes stochastic leave-one-out kNN error. Evaluated across 8 text classification tasks against 26 baselines (including BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, and metric learning variants), S-WMD achieves the lowest error on 5 datasets and outperforms all competitors on average, with significant gains over unsupervised WMD (e.g., 51% relative reduction on BBCSPORT vs. LSI). The code is publicly available.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Quality & Significance:** The method is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical grounding in optimal transport and metric learning. The empirical results are compelling—S-WMD consistently outperforms diverse baselines across multiple datasets, demonstrating clear practical significance for text classification. The efficiency gains from Sinkhorn approximation (O(q²) vs. O(q³ log q)) make the approach feasible for real-world use.  
2. **Originality:** While building on WMD (NIPS 2015) and metric learning (e.g., NCA, LMNN), S-WMD uniquely combines *word-level* supervision (via **w**) with *embedding-space* transformation (**A**), a novel integration not present in prior work. The paper adequately contrasts with related methods (e.g., Wang et al. 2016, which learns ground distances but not word weights).  
3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions of S-WMD (Eq. 5), the Sinkhorn relaxation (Section 3.1), and the training algorithm (Algorithm 1). The experimental setup (Section 4) thoroughly validates the method, including t-SNE visualizations showing improved class separation (Fig. 1) and word weight analysis (Fig. 2).  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Intuition for Weight Vector **(Eq. 5): The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of how **w** modifies document distances (e.g., *why* reweighting word frequencies improves performance beyond **A** alone). While the authors clarify in response that **w** captures task-specific word salience, this distinction could be emphasized earlier.  
2. **Baseline Context:** The claim that S-WMD "is particularly forgiving when documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words" (p. 10) lacks explicit justification. While Fig. 2 shows meaningful word weights, a concrete example comparing S-WMD and WMD on a specific document pair would strengthen this argument.  
3. **Minor Technical Omissions:** The role of λ in Eq. 2 (used for Sinkhorn) is conflated with its use in the loss function (Eq. 6); a brief clarification would aid reproducibility. Additionally, k for kNN classification is not specified—this is minor but should be noted (e.g., "k=5" as in prior WMD work).  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**  
*Pro:*  
- S-WMD is a significant, well-executed advance over unsupervised WMD, with state-of-the-art results on 5/8 datasets and consistent outperformance of 26 baselines.  
- The efficient Sinkhorn-based optimization makes the method scalable, addressing a key limitation of prior supervised WMD approaches.  
- The work directly addresses a gap in metric learning for text: leveraging word embeddings *without* discarding them for document-level representations.  

*Con:*  
- The contribution is incremental in the sense that **A** and **w** build on standard metric learning (NCA, LMNN), though their integration with WMD is novel.  
- The paper’s strongest claim ("particularly forgiving for syntactically different but semantically similar words") is not empirically substantiated with examples, though the weight visualization (Fig. 2) supports it indirectly.  

**Recommendation:**  
This paper makes a strong contribution to NIPS. It successfully bridges unsupervised word embeddings (word2vec) with supervised metric learning for text, delivering both theoretical novelty and empirical impact. The results are robust, the methodology is sound, and the code availability enhances reproducibility. While the intuition for **w** could be sharpened, the paper’s strengths far outweigh these minor gaps. The work aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on high-impact, rigorous machine learning research. **I recommend acceptance.**
