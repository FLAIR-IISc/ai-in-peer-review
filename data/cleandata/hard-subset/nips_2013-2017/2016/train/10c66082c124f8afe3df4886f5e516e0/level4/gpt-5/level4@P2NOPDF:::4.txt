This paper presents a supervised approach to learning document distances built on top of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD itself adapts Earth Mover’s Distance to text by computing an optimal transport plan between word distributions of two documents, using word2vec-based distances as the ground cost. The authors propose to learn (1) a linear transformation of the word embeddings and (2) a reweighting of the word distribution, with the aim of increasing separation between documents with different labels. The paper is clearly written, the review of prior work is thorough and accurate, and the experiments cover several novel datasets. However, the motivation for the formulation requires deeper justification, and the overall significance appears limited.

Strengths:
- Clear exposition and easy to follow.
- Comprehensive related work that demonstrates solid understanding of the literature.
- Empirical evaluation on multiple datasets.

Main concerns and questions:
1) Motivation for reweighting the word distribution (w). The rationale for introducing a reweighting vector over words is underdeveloped. As presented, reweighting can substantially alter a document’s empirical word distribution and potentially change its effective content. The paper should justify why such reweighting is appropriate, specify what constraints prevent it from distorting document identity (e.g., normalization or regularization), and provide empirical evidence that it helps in a way that aligns with the original text rather than creating a different document representation.

2) Significance of the contribution. Combining a learned linear transformation A and distribution reweighting w with an NCA-style objective is a plausible supervised extension of WMD, but it is not particularly novel. The paper would benefit from a clearer argument about what is fundamentally new relative to prior supervised metric learning, supervised optimal transport, or past work that adapts embedding spaces for classification. As it stands, the formulation seems incremental rather than conceptually groundbreaking.

3) Generalization to new documents. It is unclear how the method computes distances for unseen documents. If w is document-specific, how is w inferred for a new document at test time? If w is global (shared across documents or classes), this should be stated explicitly, along with how it is learned and then applied without access to labels at test time. Please clarify the training/inference procedure and how the learned parameters (A and any w) are used to obtain distances for new documents.

4) Meaning of “semantic difference.” In the abstract, “semantic difference” seems to refer to label differences between documents (e.g., topic or authorship). However, differences in authorship or topic labels are not necessarily synonymous with semantic distance in embedding space. Please clarify what you mean by semantic difference in this context and ensure the terminology aligns with the labels and evaluation tasks.

Overall assessment:
The paper is well written and the empirical section is solid, but the motivation for the reweighting component and the novelty claims need to be strengthened. Clarifying how the approach generalizes to unseen documents is also important for practical applicability. I would not oppose acceptance if the authors address these concerns convincingly—particularly by (a) justifying and constraining the reweighting mechanism to preserve document identity, (b) articulating a clearer and more substantial novelty claim beyond an NCA-based supervised extension, and (c) explaining the inference procedure for new documents.
