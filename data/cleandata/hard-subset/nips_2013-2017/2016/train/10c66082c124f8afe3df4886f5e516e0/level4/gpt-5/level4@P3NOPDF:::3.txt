Summary
The paper proposes a supervised approach to learn the parameters of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), i.e., an Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) on documents where ground costs are computed from word embeddings. The ground metric is parameterized by a Mahalanobis distance in the embedding space, and the method additionally learns per-dimension or per-bin reweighting of histogram features. The learning objective is of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) type, with gradients derived through the optimal transport layer (using entropic regularization). The experimental results are strong and convincingly demonstrate performance gains over standard WMD and relevant baselines.

Overall assessment
This is a clear, compelling extension of WMD that combines known building blocks—learnable ground metrics, entropic OT, and NCA-style supervision—into a practical and effective method. The idea is simple and principled but not trivial to implement or tune; the experimental section is correspondingly robust and persuasive. I recommend acceptance.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: WMD benefits significantly from task-adapted ground metrics; the proposed parameterization and objective make this adaptation feasible at scale.
- Practicality: Entropy-regularized OT enables efficient training with backpropagated gradients.
- Strong empirical results: The experiments are comprehensive and compelling.
- Methodological soundness: Using a Mahalanobis metric offers interpretability and a well-understood inductive bias.

Areas for improvement
- Mathematical rigor and notation: Some derivations and notational choices are loose. Please tighten the presentation (clear variable definitions, consistent symbols, and explicit assumptions), and separate carefully what is computed under unregularized EMD versus entropic OT.
- Regularized versus unregularized formulations: Clarify the conceptual and empirical relationship between the two. Do you train and evaluate with the same regularization? How sensitive are results to the regularization strength? What is the effect on the learned ground metric?
- Algorithmic clarity: An algorithmic box/pseudocode for the full training loop (cost construction, Sinkhorn iterations, NCA loss, gradient computation, parameter updates) would greatly improve readability and reproducibility.
- Consistency of distances: In Eq. 1 you use the Euclidean distance between embeddings as the ground cost; in Eq. 6, the Mahalanobis parameterization effectively introduces a squared Euclidean form when M = I. To avoid confusion, it would be cleaner to frame WMD as a p-Wasserstein distance between point clouds in embedding space and specify which p is used throughout (most of your derivations appear consistent with p = 2).
- Novelty of reweighting: The reweighting in Eq. 5 is not new; it has clear antecedents in Lebanon’s “Metric learning for text documents” and related work on histogram metric learning, including “Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations” (Le & Cuturi). Please acknowledge these connections and delineate your specific contributions relative to them.
- Ablations on the weight vector w: Provide empirical insights into the importance of the learned reweighting. How much does w contribute beyond the learned Mahalanobis metric? Are gains consistent across datasets? Does w improve interpretability (e.g., highlighting discriminative terms/dimensions)?
- Related work positioning:
  - Ground metric learning and entropy-regularized OT: The paper builds on lines of work stemming from Cuturi and collaborators, including Sinkhorn distances and ground metric learning. Your approach differs by parameterizing a continuous, learnable cost via linear maps in the embedding space and tightly integrating entropic OT in the training loop; these distinctions should be highlighted.
  - Prior attempts to learn WMD-like metrics (e.g., Wang et al.): Clarify how your method overcomes limitations such as performing only a single OT solve or not iterating the transport optimization within learning. Emphasize the advantages of your end-to-end, repeatedly optimized, entropy-regularized approach.
- Practical details: Please ensure the learned Mahalanobis matrix M is PSD (e.g., parameterize M = L L^T) and discuss regularization on M and w (e.g., Frobenius, sparsity) and their impact on stability and generalization.

Specific comments
- Gradients and stability: Since gradients pass through Sinkhorn iterations, comment on the choice of entropic regularization strength, number of iterations, and any stabilization tricks (e.g., log-domain Sinkhorn, epsilon scheduling). A brief complexity analysis would be useful.
- Evaluation protocol: Include ablations for (i) fixed ground metric vs learned M; (ii) with/without w; and (iii) entropic vs unregularized evaluation. Reporting runtime and memory overhead relative to vanilla WMD will help practitioners.
- Interpretation: If feasible, visualize or summarize the learned metric (e.g., spectrum of M, top-weighted features in w) to provide insight into what the model learns from text.
- Notation and consistency: Unify the use of p-Wasserstein, avoid mixing Euclidean and squared Euclidean without explicit statements, and standardize symbols across equations.

Minor notes
- Tighten the exposition around Eqs. 1, 5, and 6 to avoid the appearance of inconsistencies in the choice of p and ground costs.
- Update the related work section to more clearly acknowledge prior art on histogram reweighting/metric learning and on entropy-regularized OT.

Conclusion
A well-motivated and effective contribution that advances supervised metric learning for WMD by learning both a Mahalanobis ground metric and optional histogram reweighting, optimized through an NCA-style objective with entropic OT. The method is practical and empirically strong. With improved mathematical clarity, clearer positioning relative to prior work, explicit ablations (particularly on w), and an algorithmic summary, the paper would be even more compelling. I recommend acceptance.
