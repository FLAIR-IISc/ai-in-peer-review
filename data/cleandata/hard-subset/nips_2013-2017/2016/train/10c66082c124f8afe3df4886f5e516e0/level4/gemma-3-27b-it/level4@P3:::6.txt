This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel approach to learning a document distance metric by extending the recent Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) framework to a supervised setting. WMD cleverly leverages word embeddings and optimal transport to calculate document similarity without explicit document representation learning. The authors address the limitation of WMD being entirely unsupervised by proposing to learn both a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word-specific importance weights, minimizing leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. This builds upon a rich history of work in document distance metrics, starting with bag-of-words and TF-IDF representations [30], and progressing through dimensionality reduction techniques like LSI [11] and LDA [2], and more recently, embedding-based approaches like WMD [19]. The contribution sits within the broader field of metric learning [10, 15, 36, 37], but distinguishes itself by operating directly on word embeddings, avoiding the quadratic scaling issues associated with traditional methods applied to high-dimensional document representations.

The core idea of learning both a transformation *A* and word weights *w* is promising, allowing the model to adapt the word embedding space and emphasize relevant terms for specific classification tasks. The authors demonstrate a practical and efficient update rule utilizing a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance [6]) to overcome the computational challenges inherent in the original WMD formulation. The extensive experimental evaluation across eight datasets and comparison with 26 baselines provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD, consistently achieving superior performance. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of supervised metric learning with the WMD framework is a significant contribution. Learning both a transformation of the embedding space and word weights offers a flexible and powerful approach.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears technically sound, with a clear mathematical formulation and a practical approximation for efficient gradient computation. The use of the Sinkhorn distance is a clever way to address the computational complexity of the original WMD.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental results across diverse datasets and compared to a large number of baselines strongly support the effectiveness of S-WMD.
*   **Efficiency:**  The authors demonstrate that S-WMD is computationally efficient, making it practical for real-world applications.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and its underlying principles.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Intuition for *w*:** While the authors explain the purpose of the word importance weights *w*, the intuition behind Eq. (5) and the impact of *w* specifically could be further elucidated. A more detailed explanation of how *w* modulates the document distance would be beneficial.
*   **Ablation Study:** A more focused ablation study examining the individual contributions of the linear transformation *A* and the word weights *w* could help to better understand their relative importance and interplay.
*   **Baseline Selection:** While the comparison to 26 baselines is thorough, including a simpler classifier trained directly on the raw features (e.g., bag-of-words with logistic regression) would provide a valuable point of reference to assess the value added by the more complex S-WMD model.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** Discussion of the sensitivity of performance to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., λ in the Sinkhorn distance approximation, learning rates) and their tuning process would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for supervised document distance learning.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over a wide range of baselines.
*   The paper addresses a practical problem with a computationally efficient solution.
*   The contribution is significant and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper could benefit from further clarification regarding the intuition behind the word importance weights and a more focused ablation study.
*   The lack of comparison to a very simple baseline makes it slightly harder to assess the true value added by S-WMD.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of document distance learning. The proposed S-WMD method is novel, technically sound, and empirically well-validated. While there are a few minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
