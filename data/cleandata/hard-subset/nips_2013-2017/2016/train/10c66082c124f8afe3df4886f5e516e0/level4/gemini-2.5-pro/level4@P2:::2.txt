This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the recently introduced Word Mover's Distance (WMD). While WMD provides a powerful unsupervised metric for document similarity by leveraging word embeddings, it cannot be adapted to specific classification tasks. The authors address this limitation by introducing a supervised learning framework. The core idea is to learn two components simultaneously: (1) an affine transformation of the underlying word embedding space, and (2) a vector of word-importance weights. These components are optimized to minimize a stochastic version of the leave-one-out kNN classification error, effectively tailoring the document distance metric to the task-specific labels.

A key technical challenge is that computing the gradient of the WMD objective is computationally prohibitive, as it involves a nested optimization problem. The authors present a clever and practical solution by approximating the WMD with its entropically regularized version, the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation makes the objective strongly convex and allows for an efficient gradient computation, making the entire learning process feasible. The proposed method builds directly upon the work of Kusner et al. (2015) on WMD and draws inspiration from the metric learning literature, particularly from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) for its loss function.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles an important and natural problem: how to incorporate supervision into the powerful WMD framework. Given the impressive performance of unsupervised WMD, a supervised variant that demonstrably improves upon it is a significant contribution. The method provides a new state-of-the-art approach for kNN-based document classification, which is likely to be widely used and built upon by researchers in text analysis and information retrieval.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Originality:** The approach is technically sound and well-motivated. The main contribution is not just the idea of supervising WMD, but the elegant solution to the difficult optimization problem that arises. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to enable efficient gradient computation is a crucial insight that makes the method practical. Furthermore, the decision to learn both a transformation of the word space (matrix A) and word-specific importance weights (vector w) provides the model with valuable flexibility, which is well justified.

3.  **Extensive and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is exceptionally thorough and convincing. The authors compare S-WMD against an extensive set of 26 competitive baselines across 8 diverse datasets. The baselines include classic methods (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), modern deep learning approaches (mSDA), and the original WMD, combined with several leading metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN). S-WMD consistently outperforms these strong baselines on the majority of datasets, often by a significant margin. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of first introducing the necessary background on word2vec, WMD, and NCA, and then clearly motivating their proposed method. The derivation of the gradient approximation is explained with sufficient detail, and the overall algorithm is presented concisely. The experimental setup and results are presented transparently and effectively.

**Weaknesses / Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Comparison to End-to-End Deep Learning Models:** The baselines are comprehensive for methods that rely on pre-defined document representations followed by a kNN classifier. However, it would be interesting to understand how S-WMD compares against end-to-end deep learning models for text classification (e.g., CNNs or LSTMs), which also learn task-specific representations. While this is not strictly a metric learning approach, it is a competing paradigm for the same downstream task. A brief discussion of the relative positioning of S-WMD in this broader context would be beneficial.

2.  **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The method introduces several hyperparameters, including the regularization parameter λ, the batch size B, and the nearest neighbor set size N. The paper states these are tuned via Bayesian optimization, but a short analysis or discussion on the sensitivity of the model's performance to these parameters would strengthen the paper and be useful for future practitioners. For instance, how does the trade-off between approximation accuracy and speed change with λ?

3.  **Initialization:** The authors propose an intelligent initialization scheme by first running NCA on the Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD). Table 2 shows that this initialization step ("S-WMD init.") is responsible for a substantial portion of the performance gain. While the final S-WMD model still improves upon this, it would be valuable to clarify how critical this specific initialization is. Did the authors experiment with other initializations (e.g., random or identity for A), and how did they perform? This would help delineate the contribution of the full S-WMD optimization versus the clever initialization.

**Summary:**

Overall, this is a high-quality and impactful paper that I would strongly recommend for acceptance. It presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound method for learning a supervised document distance metric. The core idea of adapting the powerful Word Mover's Distance using a practical gradient-based optimization is a significant contribution. The claims are convincingly supported by an exceptionally thorough experimental evaluation that demonstrates state-of-the-art performance. The paper is well-written and clear. The minor weaknesses identified are primarily suggestions for additional discussion that could further strengthen an already excellent paper.
