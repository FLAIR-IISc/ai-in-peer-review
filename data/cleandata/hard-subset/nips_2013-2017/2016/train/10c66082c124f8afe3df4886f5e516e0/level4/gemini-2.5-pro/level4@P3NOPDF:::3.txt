This paper proposes a method for learning the parameters of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a metric for measuring the dissimilarity between text documents based on the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD). The core idea is to parameterize the EMD ground metric using a Mahalanobis distance between word embeddings. In addition to learning the Mahalanobis matrix, the authors also propose learning a reweighting vector for the document histograms. The paper derives the gradients for a Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA)-style loss function to train these parameters and presents compelling experimental results that demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.

Overall, this is a solid paper that presents an elegant and effective synthesis of established techniques. The central idea is straightforward, yet its implementation and empirical validation are non-trivial. The strong experimental section is a key strength of this work, and I support its acceptance. Below, I outline several suggestions for improvement that would further strengthen the manuscript.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed method is intuitive and well-motivated: learning a task-specific metric for WMD is a natural and valuable extension of the original framework.
*   The combination of Mahalanobis metric learning with histogram reweighting within an end-to-end learning framework for WMD is a solid contribution.
*   The experimental results are thorough and convincing, showcasing significant improvements over baseline methods across several tasks. This section provides strong evidence for the paper's claims.

**Areas for Improvement:**

The main areas for improvement concern the clarity of the mathematical presentation, the contextualization of the contributions with respect to prior work, and the addition of an algorithmic overview.

1.  **Mathematical Rigor and Notation:** The mathematical presentation could be improved for clarity and rigor. Some notations and derivations feel slightly imprecise. A careful review of all equations and their descriptions would benefit the paper.
2.  **Algorithmic Clarity:** The paper would be significantly easier to follow with the inclusion of an algorithm box or pseudo-code. This would provide readers with a clear, step-by-step overview of the proposed training procedure, from data input to parameter updates.
3.  **Regularized vs. Unregularized OT:** The discussion on the role of entropy regularization in the Optimal Transport (OT) problem could be more explicit. While it is used for significant computational benefits (i.e., the Sinkhorn algorithm), its connection to the unregularized WMD should be clarified throughout the paper. Making this distinction and its implications more salient would improve the paper's theoretical grounding.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

*   **Consistency of the Distance Metric:** There appears to be an inconsistency in the definition of the cost function. Equation 1 uses the Euclidean distance between word embeddings as the cost, whereas the Mahalanobis learning framework in Equation 6 implies a squared Euclidean distance. The latter corresponds to the 2-Wasserstein distance. To unify the framework and avoid confusion, I suggest the authors frame WMD generally as a p-Wasserstein distance and remain consistent with their choice of 'p' throughout the paper.
*   **Novelty of the Reweighting Scheme:** The histogram reweighting approach proposed in Equation 5, while effective, should be properly contextualized with respect to prior art. This technique was previously introduced by Lebanon in "Metric learning for text documents" for the same application. A related approach is also discussed in "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations" by Le and Cuturi. The manuscript should acknowledge this prior work to accurately position its contribution.
*   **Analysis of the Weight Vector:** Could the authors provide an ablation study or further analysis on the impact of the learned weight vector `w`? It would be valuable to understand the contribution of this component to the overall performance and in which scenarios it is most beneficial.
*   **Positioning with Respect to Related Work:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison to prior work on learning for OT.
    *   The work of Cuturi & Avis on learning discrete OT costs is a key predecessor. The current work differentiates itself in two significant ways that should be highlighted: (1) the use of entropy regularization, which is crucial for scalability, and (2) the parameterization of a continuous ground metric via linear maps, as opposed to learning a fully discrete cost matrix. Explicitly stating these distinctions would better situate the paper's contributions.
    *   Similarly, when discussing Wang et al., the authors could clarify the key differences in the optimization procedure. For example, a potential distinction is how the OT optimization is integrated into the learning loop, which appears to be a critical factor for successful training.
