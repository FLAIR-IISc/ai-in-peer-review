This paper introduces a supervised metric learning approach leveraging the word-movers distance [19], a transport distance applied to word2vec embeddings. The method generalizes to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances following a linear projection. Beyond learning this projection to maximize performance, the approach also learns a weighting for each word. This latter component, specific to document applications, involves re-weighting word frequencies with learned weights followed by re-normalization. The authors present an efficient optimization method for learning the proposed parameterized metric and demonstrate its effectiveness through a comprehensive experimental evaluation. They compare the approach to numerous baselines on document classification datasets, utilizing a kNN classifier based on the learned metric. 

This work represents a technically interesting and useful extension of the recent word-movers distance, effectively applied to several kNN text classification tasks. However, the potential impact may be limited. The results indicate that an earth-movers distance initialized with a metric learning approach based on “word centroid distance” achieves comparable performance to the full learning of the proposed distance (see the last two rows of Table 3). Consequently, the core technical contributions of this paper do not appear to offer substantial improvements over this initialization method, which builds upon existing techniques.

The evaluation would benefit from including a comparison of the “word centroid embedding” (the weighted mean of word embeddings for a document) against all tested metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA). Additionally, an assessment of the relative importance of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A is lacking. Investigating the performance with each component active in isolation would provide valuable insight. The originality primarily resides in the optimization method (Lines 156–175); while the idea of linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance framework is interesting, it is a relatively limited contribution on its own.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read. There are a few minor points that could be clarified: On Line 152, the reference to “the authors” is ambiguous – which paper are they referring to ([1,7,8])? In Equation 11, α and β should likely include asterisks. Finally, explicitly referencing the Sinkhorn scaling (introduced in Line 56) around Line 170 would improve clarity.
