This paper proposes a supervised method for learning a document distance metric built upon the Word Mover's Distance (WMD). WMD computes the optimal transport cost between the word distributions of two documents, where the ground distance between words is typically derived from pre-trained word embeddings like word2vec. The authors extend this framework by learning two components to better separate documents with different labels: (1) a linear transformation of the word embeddings, and (2) a reweighting of each document's word distribution.

The paper is clearly written, easy to follow, and contains a thorough review of related work, demonstrating the authors' strong understanding of the literature. Furthermore, the authors validate their algorithm with experimental results across several novel datasets.

However, the paper has several weaknesses, primarily concerning the motivation behind the proposed formulation and its overall significance. My main concerns are as follows:

1.  The motivation for reweighting the word distribution via the weights `w` is unclear. This reweighting scheme could potentially alter the meaning of a document so significantly that it no longer represents the original text. The paper would be strengthened by a justification for this approach, perhaps by showing how these weights improve performance without fundamentally changing a document's core topic or meaning.

2.  The technical contribution appears incremental. The proposed method of learning a linear transformation `A` and word weights `w` using a Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA) objective function is a straightforward extension of WMD to a supervised setting. While effective, the formulation does not seem to represent a groundbreaking advance in the field.

3.  The paper does not clearly explain how the learned metric would be applied to new, unseen documents. Specifically, it is unclear how the reweighting vector `w` would be determined for a document that was not part of the training set. This is a critical point for the practical application of the method.

4.  The use of the term "semantic difference" in the abstract is potentially confusing. It appears to be used as a synonym for class labels, such as authorship or topic. This is not the conventional meaning of "semantic difference," and clarification is needed. If the authors intend a different meaning, it should be explicitly defined.

In summary, while the paper is well-written and presents interesting experimental results, its contributions are limited by a lack of clear motivation for certain design choices and concerns about the method's novelty and practical application. I do not object to the acceptance of this paper, but my support is contingent on the authors providing satisfactory explanations for the issues raised above.
