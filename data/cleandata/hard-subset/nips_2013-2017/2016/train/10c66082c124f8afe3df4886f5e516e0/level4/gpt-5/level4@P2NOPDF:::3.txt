This paper proposes learning the parameters of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD)—i.e., the Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) on text documents—by parameterizing the ground cost between word embeddings with a Mahalanobis metric. In addition, the method learns a reweighting of histogram coefficients. The authors derive gradients for a Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA)-type objective and present compelling experimental results. The overall impression is that of a well-executed combination of established ideas, yielding strong empirical performance. I would support acceptance.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple idea: learn a Mahalanobis metric over word embeddings and optionally reweight word histograms within a WMD/OT framework.
- Practical impact: while conceptually straightforward, the approach is nontrivial to implement and evaluate; the experimental section is therefore an important and strong component.
- The use of entropy-regularized OT makes the approach scalable and efficient in practice.

Areas for improvement:
- Clarify the relationship and trade-offs between regularized and unregularized OT formulations. The current presentation obscures when each is used and why.
- Improve mathematical rigor. Some derivations and notations are imprecise; tightening these would aid reproducibility and understanding.
- Provide a concise algorithmic summary (e.g., a pseudocode box) that makes the full training loop, loss, and gradient computation explicit.

Minor comments and clarifications:
- Consistency of costs: In Eq. 1 the cost is the Euclidean distance between word embeddings; in Eq. 6, for Mahalanobis learning, it becomes the squared Euclidean distance (which aligns with the 2-Wasserstein setting). To avoid confusion, consider simply stating that WMD here is the p-Wasserstein distance between point clouds of word embeddings, with the choice of p made explicit and consistent.
- Reweighting novelty: The reweighting scheme of Eq. 5 is not new; similar ideas have appeared in the context of text classification (e.g., Guy Lebanon’s work on metric learning for text documents). Also see “Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations” (Le & Cuturi) for related histogram reparameterizations. Please position your contribution accordingly.
- Importance of the weight vector w: Provide ablations or sensitivity analyses quantifying the contribution of the learned weights relative to metric learning alone. Does learning w materially improve performance, and in which regimes (dataset size, vocabulary size, regularization strength)?
- Related work positioning: 
  - The paper clearly builds on Cuturi/Avis and related OT-based document distances. Emphasize two key differences: (i) the use of entropy regularization, which substantially accelerates and scales computation; and (ii) a continuous, parametric ground metric (via linear/Mahalanobis maps) instead of a fixed or discrete cost. These distinctions deserve explicit discussion.
  - The approach also relates to Wang et al. Please clarify the methodological differences and address limitations in that prior work (e.g., their single-pass OT computation), explaining why the present formulation addresses those shortcomings.

Overall assessment:
The paper presents a solid, practical extension of WMD by learning a parametric ground cost and optional histogram reweighting, optimized via an NCA-type objective with efficient, regularized OT. The idea is simple yet effective, and the experiments are convincing. With improved clarity on the regularized vs. unregularized formulations, tighter mathematical exposition, and a concise algorithm summary, the paper would be even stronger. I recommend acceptance.
