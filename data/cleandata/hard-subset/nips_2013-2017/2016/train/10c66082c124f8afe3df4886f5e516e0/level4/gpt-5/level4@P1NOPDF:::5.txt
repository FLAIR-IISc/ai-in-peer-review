This paper addresses text classification by extending prior work on Word Mover’s Distance (Wasserstein) to a supervised setting via learned, word-specific weights. It further accelerates the approach by using the Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for the Wasserstein distance, and evaluates the method on eight datasets against 26 baselines, reporting the best average performance. The paper is very well written, presents the topic clearly, and provides a thorough and accessible mathematical treatment.

The main weaknesses are limited novelty relative to the original Word Mover’s Distance work, and a confusing, arguably irrelevant discussion of comparisons to Word Centroid Distance. More importantly, the treatment of λ (the entropic regularization parameter) is insufficient. In my experience, the choice of λ has a substantial impact and can even dominate Eq. (2). Theoretically, with sufficiently large λ one should recover behavior close to the unregularized Wasserstein distance (at the cost of slower convergence), whereas in practice many users choose very small λ to speed up convergence, leading to behavior that differs markedly from Wasserstein.

Given this, it is crucial to clarify the role of λ in your method: does the approach operate in a near-Wasserstein regime or in a distinctly entropic Sinkhorn regime? Please detail how λ is selected (per dataset or globally), the search range and tuning protocol, and provide a sensitivity analysis showing performance and convergence as a function of λ. It would also help to report whether the gains over baselines persist across reasonable λ values, and to discuss any trade-offs observed between accuracy and computational efficiency as λ varies.

Despite these concerns, the paper’s empirical results are strong, and the presentation is excellent. Addressing the λ-related questions and tightening the discussion around Word Centroid Distance would significantly strengthen the contribution.
