

The paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of Kusner et al.'s Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to improve document classification. While WMD leverages word embeddings to compute document distances via optimal transport, S-WMD learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to minimize classification error. The authors address the computational challenge of optimizing WMD by using Cuturi's Sinkhorn distance approximation and propose a stochastic neighborhood approach inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). The method is evaluated across eight text classification tasks, consistently outperforming 26 baselines including traditional document representations (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), deep learning approaches (mSDA), and unsupervised variants (WCD, WMD).

The paper makes a significant contribution by bridging the gap between unsupervised document distance metrics and supervised learning. While prior work on metric learning (e.g., NCA, LMNN) typically operates on high-dimensional document representations, S-WMD operates directly on the low-dimensional word embedding space, avoiding the need for dimensionality reduction. This approach is particularly relevant to NIPS as it demonstrates how to effectively incorporate supervision into optimal transport-based document metrics, a novel combination that advances the state of the art in text classification.

Strengths of the paper are substantial. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering eight diverse text classification tasks with thorough comparisons against 26 baselines. The results are compelling, with S-WMD achieving the lowest error rate on five out of eight datasets and outperforming all competitors on average. The authors provide a clear rationale for their methodological choices, particularly the use of Sinkhorn distance for computational efficiency and the stochastic neighborhood approach for differentiable optimization. The writing is generally clear, and the technical contributions are well-motivated. The paper's focus on practical implementation details, including initialization strategies and batch gradient descent optimizations, adds significant value for reproducibility.

The paper also demonstrates strong theoretical grounding by properly relating to foundational work on optimal transport (EMD, Wasserstein distance), metric learning (NCA), and word embeddings (word2vec). The authors effectively contextualize their work within the NIPS literature, particularly building on Kusner et al.'s WMD and Cuturi's Sinkhorn distance, while addressing limitations of prior unsupervised approaches.

A few weaknesses warrant attention. While the reweighting approach (Equation 5) is inspired by Lebanon's work on metric learning for text documents, the paper could more clearly articulate how their method differs from and improves upon this prior work. The mathematical presentation would benefit from greater rigor, particularly in the gradient derivations (Equations 8-10), where some notation is inconsistent (e.g., switching between squared Euclidean distance and general p-norms). The paper could also provide more insight into the practical impact of the weight vector w—how much do the learned weights contribute to performance improvements, and which words are most frequently upweighted?

The paper's significance is high, as it demonstrates how to effectively incorporate supervision into a state-of-the-art document distance metric with minimal computational overhead. The method is not only theoretically sound but also practically efficient, with training times comparable to standard metric learning approaches. The authors' use of the Sinkhorn approximation (instead of exact WMD computation) enables application to larger datasets while maintaining competitive performance.

The paper addresses all four NIPS evaluation criteria effectively. Quality is demonstrated through rigorous experiments with appropriate baselines and statistical validation. Clarity is maintained throughout, with well-organized sections and sufficient technical detail for reproducibility. Originality is clear in the combination of optimal transport, metric learning, and text classification. Significance is evident in the consistently superior performance across diverse text classification tasks.

Pro/Con for Acceptance:
- PRO: Strong empirical results across eight datasets with comprehensive baseline comparison; novel integration of supervision into WMD; practical computational improvements via Sinkhorn approximation
- PRO: Well-motivated approach that builds on established NIPS work while addressing clear limitations of unsupervised methods
- PRO: Clear, well-organized writing with sufficient detail for reproducibility
- CON: Some mathematical notation could be more precise; could better distinguish contributions from Lebanon's prior work on reweighting
- CON: Limited discussion of the practical impact of the word importance weights

Overall, this paper presents a well-executed and significant advancement in document distance metrics. The method is both theoretically sound and practically valuable, with strong empirical evidence supporting its superiority over existing approaches. The minor weaknesses in mathematical presentation and related work contextualization do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution to the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
