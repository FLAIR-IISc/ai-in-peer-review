## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title - *Please replace with actual title*]

This paper presents a novel supervised metric learning approach for textual documents, extending the previously established Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). While the original WMD operates in an unsupervised manner by measuring the distance between word embeddings, this work introduces supervision by leveraging document-level class labels. The authors propose learning both a linear transformation matrix (A) and, crucially, word importance weights (w) to refine the distance calculation. The promise of publicly released code (MATLAB) is a positive aspect. I found the authors’ responses to my initial questions to be thorough and helpful, clarifying several points.

The core contribution lies in adapting WMD to a supervised learning framework. The method learns to adjust both the underlying embedding space (via A) and the contribution of individual words within a document (via w).  While the concept is compelling, the intuition behind the application of the word importance weights, as defined in Equation (5), requires further clarification. Specifically, it would be beneficial to understand *why* this particular weighting scheme is chosen and what effect it’s intended to have on the distance calculation.  Furthermore, dissecting the relative contribution of the learned transformation (A) versus the word weights (w) to the reported performance gains would strengthen the analysis. Are the improvements primarily driven by adjusting the embedding space, re-weighting words, or a synergistic effect of both?

The experimental evaluation focuses on document classification. Recognizing the challenges inherent in evaluating distance metrics directly, the authors appropriately employ a classification task. However, to provide a more comprehensive baseline, it would be insightful to see the performance of a simpler classifier (e.g., logistic regression or a support vector machine) using basic document representations (e.g., TF-IDF) on the same datasets. This would help to isolate the benefit specifically attributable to the proposed learned metric. (Note: This point is partially addressed by the authors' clarifications.)

I have several specific questions regarding the paper’s technical details. Firstly, the usage of λ in Equation (2) is unclear. The symbol appears to have a different meaning or scope than the λ referenced in line 170. Clarification on this discrepancy is needed. Secondly, the authors claim that WMD “is particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words” (line 316).  I would appreciate a more detailed argument supporting this claim, perhaps with a concrete example illustrating how the method handles such cases.

Finally, several minor points require attention. In line 257, the authors state they present “the average error across datasets” on the right-hand side. However, I could not locate this information in Table 3.  Regarding Table 2, the distinction between "n" and "ne" is not defined within the paper. Additionally, the value of *k* used in the k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) classification is not specified. Is this parameter determined based on the dataset, the method itself, or through cross-validation? Explicitly stating this detail would improve reproducibility.



**Overall:**

This paper presents a promising extension to Word Mover's Distance, addressing a key limitation by incorporating supervised learning. The experimental results demonstrate encouraging performance. However, clarifying the intuition behind the word weighting scheme, quantifying the contribution of each learned component, and addressing the specific questions outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper. I believe addressing these points would raise the quality of this work and make it a more valuable contribution to the field.
