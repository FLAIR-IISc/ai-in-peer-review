

This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach built upon the Word Movers' Distance [19], a transport-based distance for word2vec embeddings. The method generalizes to any transport distance derived from squared Euclidean distances after linear projection. It jointly learns a linear projection matrix and document-specific word weights—reweighting word frequencies via learned coefficients and renormalizing the document representation. The authors present an efficient optimization scheme for the parameterized metric and demonstrate its effectiveness through thorough experiments on document classification datasets using a k-NN classifier.  

**Strengths:**  
The work extends Word Movers' Distance with a technically interesting and useful modification, validated by strong performance across multiple text classification tasks. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.  

**Major Concerns:**  
The primary technical contribution appears limited. As shown in Table 3, Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) initialized with a "word centroid distance" (i.e., weighted mean of word embeddings) performs nearly identically to the full proposed metric learning approach (last two rows). This suggests the learned projection and word weights provide minimal incremental gain over a simple initialization. The marginal performance gap raises questions about the necessity of the proposed optimization framework.  

Further, the evaluation omits two critical comparisons:  
1. Performance of the "word centroid embedding" (weighted document mean) with standard metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA), which would clarify whether the centroid itself is a strong baseline.  
2. A component-wise ablation study isolating the contribution of the word weights versus the projection matrix *A*. Without this, it remains unclear which aspect drives the observed gains.  

**Originality:**  
The core idea of linear metric learning within EMD is not novel. The primary novelty lies in the optimization procedure (Lines 156–175), which is technically sound but insufficiently differentiated from existing approaches given the marginal empirical gains.  

**Minor Suggestions:**  
- Line 152: Clarify "The authors" (which paper do they reference: [1,7,8]?).  
- Equation 11: Should α and β include stars (e.g., α*, β*)?  
- Around Line 170: Explicitly state that the Sinkhorn scaling referenced in Line 56 is the algorithm used.  

In summary, while the paper is well-executed and the metric learning framework is technically coherent, the empirical evidence does not strongly justify the complexity of the proposed method over simpler centroid-based initialization. The impact is therefore constrained by the limited marginal improvement observed. The missing ablation studies and baseline comparisons weaken the argument for the approach’s necessity.
