

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), extending Kusner et al.'s unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) to incorporate supervision for text classification. The authors propose learning an affine transformation of the underlying word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector to minimize stochastic leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. To address the computational inefficiency of optimizing the original WMD, they employ the Sinkhorn distance as a practical approximation, enabling efficient gradient computation and training.

The work builds upon the significant contribution of WMD (Kusner et al., ICML 2015), which introduced a document distance metric leveraging word embeddings through optimal transport. While WMD was shown to achieve state-of-the-art results in kNN document classification, it operates in an entirely unsupervised manner. The authors correctly identify that incorporating supervision would be valuable, as different classification tasks may require different document dissimilarity measures. Their approach to learning a supervised metric within the WMD framework is well-motivated and technically sound.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results across 8 text classification datasets, outperforming 26 competitive baselines on average. The evaluation is comprehensive, with detailed analysis of results across different datasets and clear visualization of the improvements using t-SNE. The authors also provide a thorough comparison with related work, including metric learning approaches and document representation methods.

Strengths:
1. Quality: The paper is technically rigorous with clear mathematical derivations, particularly for the gradient computations. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, with appropriate baselines and statistical validation. The method is well-justified theoretically and empirically.

2. Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a logical flow from problem statement to method to evaluation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with machine learning concepts. The supplementary materials further enhance the clarity.

3. Originality: While the core idea extends the WMD framework, the specific approach of learning both a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights within the optimal transport framework is novel. The use of Sinkhorn distance for computational efficiency is well-justified and practical.

4. Significance: The consistent performance gains across multiple datasets demonstrate that S-WMD is a valuable contribution to text classification. The method is efficient (training times comparable to baseline metric learning approaches) and directly addresses a limitation of the original WMD.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty of the work is somewhat limited as it directly builds upon Kusner et al.'s WMD framework. While the supervised extension is valuable, the paper could better articulate how this differs from other metric learning approaches applied to text, particularly those that learn document representations rather than modifying the underlying word embedding space.

2. The discussion of the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn approximation (Equation 2) is insufficient. As noted in the draft, λ critically impacts the behavior of the approximation. The paper should clarify whether their implementation achieves pure Wasserstein behavior (with large λ) or Sinkhorn behavior (with small λ), as these represent fundamentally different approaches. The choice of λ=10 should be justified in terms of the trade-off between approximation accuracy and computational efficiency.

3. The comparison with Word Centroid Distance (WCD) appears confusingly placed. While WCD is a baseline method, the paper's initial description of the method (Section 3.2) suggests S-WMD is built upon WCD, which could mislead readers about the relationship between these methods.

4. The paper could benefit from more detailed discussion of when S-WMD might not be appropriate, such as for extremely short documents where the optimal transport formulation may not be meaningful.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation and text classification. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. The limited novelty is offset by the thorough evaluation and clear presentation. The weaknesses identified are minor and could be addressed with minor revisions.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Extends a highly influential method (WMD) to a supervised setting with practical benefits
- Demonstrates consistent performance improvements across multiple datasets
- Provides a clear, well-justified computational approach using Sinkhorn distance
- Exceptionally well-written with thorough experimental evaluation

Arguments against acceptance:
- The novelty is somewhat incremental, as it builds directly on the WMD framework
- Insufficient discussion of the critical λ parameter choice
- Some confusion in the relationship between S-WMD and WCD

Given the strong empirical results, clear presentation, and practical significance, I recommend acceptance. The minor weaknesses identified are easily addressable through minor revisions and do not detract from the paper's overall contribution. The method represents a significant improvement to the state-of-the-art in document distance metrics and should be of interest to the NIPS community.
