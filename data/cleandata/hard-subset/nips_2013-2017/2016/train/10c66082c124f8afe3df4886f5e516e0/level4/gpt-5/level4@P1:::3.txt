Summary
This paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparison. The key idea is to learn (i) a Mahalanobis ground metric over word embeddings via a linear map A, and (ii) a word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The objective directly minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out kNN error using an NCA-style loss, where pairwise document distances are computed via optimal transport. Because differentiating through exact WMD would entail an impractical nested optimization, the paper adopts an entropy-regularized OT relaxation (Sinkhorn distance) to obtain efficient and differentiable approximations to both transport plans and dual variables. Initialization is done by applying NCA to word centroid embeddings (S-WCD), followed by batch gradient descent with neighbor pruning. Across 8 datasets and 26 baselines, the method (S-WMD) achieves strong kNN performance and trains efficiently.

Relation to prior work
- WMD (Kusner et al.) leverages word embeddings to define document distances via OT; this paper adds supervision by learning a ground metric and histogram weights.
- The approach builds on entropy-regularized OT (Cuturi), enabling scalable computation and stable gradients via Sinkhorn iterations.
- The learning objective follows NCA (Goldberger et al.), optimizing a stochastic kNN criterion.
- Learning ground metrics for EMD relates to Cuturi & Avis and to supervised EMD work (e.g., Wang et al.). In contrast, this paper parameterizes a continuous ground cost via a linear map on embeddings and jointly learns reweighting.
- Histogram reweighting for text has precedents, e.g., Lebanon’s work on metric learning for text documents and Le & Cuturi’s work on Aitchison transforms for histograms.

Quality
- The method is conceptually simple and technically sound as a combination of established components: Sinkhorn-regularized OT for tractable transport, an NCA-style loss for supervision, and a linear ground metric with histogram reweighting.
- The experimental section is a strong point: many datasets, extensive baselines, and consistent gains.
- That said, the mathematical exposition contains notational inconsistencies and could be more rigorous. In particular, the switch between Euclidean and squared Euclidean costs (Eq. 1 vs Eq. 6) creates confusion about p-Wasserstein vs 2-Wasserstein throughout.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but several aspects would benefit from clarification:
  - Better treatment of the regularized versus unregularized formulations, including how λ affects approximation fidelity, convergence, and final performance.
  - Clearer, more consistent notation for the ground cost and the Wasserstein order p to avoid Eq. 1/Eq. 6 inconsistencies. A simple remedy is to frame WMD as p-Wasserstein over word embeddings and fix p throughout.
  - An algorithm box (pseudo-code) summarizing the training loop, including initialization (S-WCD), Sinkhorn gradient approximations, batching, and neighbor pruning, would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The contribution is primarily a well-executed synthesis:
  - Learning a linear ground metric for OT is known; here it is applied to word embeddings and combined with NCA.
  - Entropy regularization for OT is known; here it is used to obtain practical gradients for an NCA objective on documents.
  - Histogram reweighting is not new in text; please cite Lebanon (“metric learning for text documents”) and Le/Cuturi (“Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations”).
- The joint learning of A and w in this specific WMD/NCA framework for text is a useful and practical instantiation, but novelty is incremental.

Significance
- The empirical improvements over a large suite of baselines suggest the method is practically relevant for supervised document similarity.
- Efficiency via Sinkhorn and neighbor filtering makes the approach usable at scale.
- The paper’s main impact is likely to be as a strong supervised metric for kNN-type tasks, and as a blueprint for combining Sinkhorn OT with NCA.

Strengths
- Simple and effective idea; strong empirical results across diverse datasets.
- Solid engineering: thoughtful initialization (S-WCD), efficient training with batch updates and neighbor pruning, and practical use of Sinkhorn approximations.
- Clear motivation for learning both the ground metric and word weights.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Regularization vs exact OT: please provide a more thorough discussion of λ, its effect on the objective and gradients, and empirical sensitivity.
- Notation and rigor: the mathematical development would benefit from tighter notation and consistency (Euclidean vs squared Euclidean; p-Wasserstein).
- Prior work: explicitly acknowledge that reweighting is not new (Lebanon; Le/Cuturi) and sharpen the distinction from Cuturi & Avis (your cost is continuous and parameterized by a linear map) and from Wang et al. (their OT optimization is run once and is not effective as proposed).
- Algorithm presentation: include a concise pseudo-code box summarizing the full training pipeline.

Questions for the authors
- How important is the learned weight vector w in practice? Please provide an ablation (A-only vs A+w) to quantify its contribution.
- Do results depend strongly on the choice of p and on λ in the Sinkhorn relaxation?
- Can you report runtime comparisons with exact WMD-based learning (where feasible) to substantiate the efficiency gains?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong and consistent empirical performance across many competitive baselines.
- Practical and well-motivated use of Sinkhorn OT to enable supervision.
- Method is simple enough to be adopted, yet nontrivial to implement.

Cons for acceptance
- Novelty is primarily a combination of known techniques.
- Some mathematical and expository rough edges; missing citations for reweighting and clearer positioning relative to closely related OT metric-learning work.
- Lack of ablation on the contribution of w.

Overall recommendation
I support acceptance. The paper offers a clear, effective supervised variant of WMD with compelling results. Addressing the clarity issues, prior-work positioning, and the ablation on w would further strengthen the contribution.
