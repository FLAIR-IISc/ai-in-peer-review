Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper extends Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) to a supervised setting by learning (i) a linear transformation A of the word embedding space and (ii) a word-importance vector w that re-weights document histograms. The resulting Supervised WMD (S-WMD) aims to align the optimal-transport-based document distance with label semantics. To make training practical, the authors replace the exact WMD with its entropically regularized Sinkhorn relaxation, enabling O(q^2) computation per pair (q = unique words per document). They optimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective à la NCA over document pairs, initialize A via NCA on word centroids (S-WCD), and use minibatching with neighbor filtering to reduce cost. Experiments on eight datasets compare against 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised) and report consistent improvements in kNN error, visualizations via t-SNE, interpretable learned word weights, and competitive training times. This builds on WMD (Kusner et al.), metric learning (NCA/LMNN/ITML), and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi), and relates to prior work that learns ground metrics for EMD, while adding histogram re-weighting and applying it to text.

Strengths:
- Clear and well-motivated problem: incorporate supervision into a strong unsupervised distance (WMD) without leaving the low-dimensional embedding space.
- Sound training objective: NCA-style stochastic LOO is standard in metric learning; the derivations for ∂/∂A and ∂/∂w are sensible with the Sinkhorn approximation.
- Practical efficiency: use of entropic regularization, scalable fixed-point updates (u, v), minibatching, and WCD-based neighbor pruning.
- Extensive empirical comparison: eight datasets, 26 baselines, consistent gains for kNN; informative visualizations and interpretability of w.
- Code availability increases reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Limited ablations. It is hard to disentangle the contributions of A vs w. Please report A-only, w-only, and A+w, and the effect of the Sinkhorn parameter λ on accuracy/run time.
- Intuition for w in Eq. (5). Provide more explanation for the normalized reweighting d̃ = (w ◦ d)/(w^T d): how does normalization affect transport behavior, class-specific stop-word down-weighting, and stability? A small toy example would help.
- Evaluation tied to kNN. While evaluating a distance is nontrivial, a simple discriminative baseline (e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression on TF-IDF or mSDA) on the same splits would contextualize gains.
- Notation/clarity. Make explicit where Eq. (2) (the entropic relaxation) is used within the training loop and state whether the λ there is identical to the λ referred to later. Ensure consistent symbols and cross-references.
- Ground metric choice. The squared Euclidean ground cost is fixed; in some datasets (e.g., CLASSIC) WMD outperforms S-WMD. Discuss whether p=1 or cosine-based ground distances were tried, and whether they could be learned.
- Hyperparameter reporting. Clarify how k for kNN is chosen (per dataset? via validation?), and specify whether “n” vs “ne” in Table 2 refer to sample size vs number of evaluation examples (or something else).
- Initialization sensitivity. Results show S-WMD-init already strong; please comment on sensitivity to initialization and whether training ever degrades S-WCD performance.

Quality:
The method is technically sound, with a reasonable approximation for gradients via Sinkhorn. The optimization is nonconvex, but the initialization and batching strategy are plausible. Claims are mostly supported by experiments, although ablations are needed to isolate design choices. The “forgiving to syntactic variation” claim is intuitive for embedding-based ground costs; citing concrete examples or a robustness test to paraphrases would strengthen it.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. Please improve the intuition for Eq. (5), clarify the use and meaning of λ throughout, define k-selection, and correct table/figure references. A short algorithm box integrating Eq. (2), (5)–(11) with clear variable definitions would help.

Originality:
Combining supervised learning of a low-dimensional ground metric with histogram reweighting within an OT-based document distance is a novel and natural extension of WMD. Prior EMD metric-learning work in vision did not reweight histograms nor target NLP tasks. The NCA objective and Sinkhorn are known, but the integration is original and well executed.

Significance:
Results show consistent improvements over strong baselines across diverse datasets, suggesting S-WMD is a practical drop-in distance for retrieval and kNN classification. The approach is likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., domain adaptation, semi-supervised settings, richer ground costs). Broader impact includes interpretability via w and compatibility with pretrained embeddings.

Specific questions for the authors:
- Where exactly is Eq. (2) invoked during training, and is its λ the same as the λ mentioned later? Please unify notation and state chosen λ values and stopping criteria.
- Please provide ablations: A-only vs w-only vs both; effect of λ; effect of neighbor set size N and batch size B.
- What empirical evidence supports the “forgiving to syntactic variation” claim (e.g., paraphrase robustness, synonym substitutions)?
- How is k for kNN selected for each dataset? Is it consistent across methods?
- In Table 2, what do “n” and “ne” denote?
- The text says “on the right we also show the average error across datasets,” but I do not see it; did you mean “average rank” or is this Table 3?

Minor comments:
- Define all symbols on first use; ensure consistent use of d, p, q, n, and r.
- Report memory footprint of storing K and T during Sinkhorn for typical q.
- Consider discussing whether w becomes sparse and whether any regularization on w was used.

Pros for acceptance:
- Well-motivated, novel supervised extension of WMD with principled optimization.
- Efficient training via Sinkhorn and batching; code provided.
- Strong, broad empirical gains and interpretability.

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing ablations; limited analysis of when each component helps or hurts.
- Dependence on kNN evaluation without a discriminative baseline comparison.
- Some notational and reporting ambiguities.
