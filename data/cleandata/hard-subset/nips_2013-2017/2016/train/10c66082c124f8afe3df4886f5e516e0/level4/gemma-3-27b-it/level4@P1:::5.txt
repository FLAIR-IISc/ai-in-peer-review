The authors address the problem of text classification by extending prior work on Word Mover’s Distance (WMD, a Wasserstein distance) to a supervised setting through learning word-specific weights. Their contribution proposes utilizing the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient proxy for the Wasserstein distance and demonstrates strong performance on eight datasets, outperforming 26 baseline methods on average. This is a well-written paper that clearly presents the topic and analyzes the underlying mathematics with both depth and clarity. 

The primary weakness lies in the incremental novelty compared to the original WMD paper. Additionally, the comparison to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) feels somewhat tangential and, at times, confusing. The discussion of lambda, the regularization parameter in the Sinkhorn distance, is also limited. In practice, the value of lambda significantly impacts performance; a sufficiently large lambda should theoretically approach pure Wasserstein distance, albeit with slower convergence. However, many implementations utilize a small lambda to accelerate convergence, resulting in behavior substantially different from that of the Wasserstein distance. It would be valuable to understand whether, in this work, lambda promotes behavior closer to pure Wasserstein distance or retains the characteristics of the Sinkhorn (entropic) approximation.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear mathematical foundation and robust experimental validation. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential issues, such as the sensitivity to lambda. The work appears complete, offering a practical and efficient algorithm.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The explanations of WMD, the Sinkhorn distance, and the proposed S-WMD are clear and accessible. The notation is consistent, and the presentation of the gradient calculation is well-structured.

**Originality:** While building on the strong foundation of WMD, the introduction of supervised learning with word-specific weights and the efficient gradient approximation represent a novel contribution. The application of the Sinkhorn distance in this context and the thorough evaluation against a large number of baselines further enhance the originality.

**Significance:** The consistently strong performance across diverse datasets, exceeding 26 competitive baselines, indicates the potential for significant impact. The improved efficiency afforded by the Sinkhorn approximation makes the method practical for real-world applications. This work addresses a challenging problem in text classification and provides a valuable advancement in the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results on a diverse set of datasets.
*   Clear and well-motivated technical contribution.
*   Efficient implementation leveraging the Sinkhorn distance.
*   Thorough comparison to a comprehensive set of baselines.
*   Well-written and easily understandable presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty compared to the original WMD paper.
*   The relevance of the comparison to the WCD is not fully clear.
*   Limited discussion of the impact of the regularization parameter lambda.
