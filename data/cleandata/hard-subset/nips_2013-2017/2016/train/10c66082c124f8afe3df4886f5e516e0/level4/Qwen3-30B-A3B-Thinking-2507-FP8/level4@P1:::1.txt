

This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach built upon the recently introduced Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which computes document distances by treating documents as distributions over word embeddings. The authors extend WMD by learning a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word-specific importance weights to improve document classification performance. The key innovation lies in efficiently optimizing these parameters by approximating the nested optimization problem inherent in the WMD framework, leveraging the Sinkhorn distance for computational efficiency. The method is thoroughly evaluated across eight text classification tasks against 26 baselines, demonstrating consistent improvements over unsupervised WMD and other document representation methods.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution that builds upon prior work in both metric learning and document representation. It extends the WMD framework (Kusner et al., 2015) by incorporating supervision, which is particularly valuable for document classification tasks where semantic similarity is label-dependent. The authors correctly identify that direct application of supervision to WMD would be computationally prohibitive, and their solution using the Sinkhorn approximation (Cuturi, 2013) provides a practical and efficient optimization method. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering a diverse range of text classification tasks and comparing against a large number of relevant baselines, including both unsupervised document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) and supervised metric learning approaches applied to these representations.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The technical background is well-motivated, with clear explanations of the WMD framework, regularized transport problems, and linear metric learning. The proposed method is logically presented, with careful attention to the computational challenges and their solutions. The writing is precise and accessible to readers familiar with machine learning concepts, making it a pleasure to read. The authors also provide a clear implementation summary and thorough experimental details, including training times and visualization of learned word weights.

Regarding originality, the core idea of extending WMD to a supervised setting is novel, particularly in its specific formulation as a joint optimization of word embedding transformation and word importance weights. While the general concept of metric learning within a transport framework has been explored (Wang & Guibas, 2013; Cuturi & Avis, 2014), the authors' specific application to text documents with word-level reweighting represents a meaningful contribution. However, the novelty of the optimization technique (Section 3.1) is somewhat limited, as the Sinkhorn approximation has been applied to similar problems before.

The significance of this work is strong, particularly for text classification tasks where semantic similarity is crucial. The consistent performance gains across eight datasets (with S-WMD outperforming all 26 baselines on average) demonstrate the practical value of the approach. The visualization of learned word weights (Figure 2) provides compelling evidence that the method successfully captures document-class-relevant features.

I have two primary concerns regarding the evaluation that should be addressed. First, the paper claims S-WMD outperforms all baselines, but Table 3 shows that the initialization method (S-WCD, which is simply NCA applied to the Word Centroid Distance) performs remarkably close to the final S-WMD results. This raises questions about the incremental value of the full optimization procedure. A more thorough comparison between S-WCD and S-WMD would strengthen the paper's claims about the necessity of the proposed learning framework.

Second, the paper does not adequately assess the relative contribution of the two key components: the linear transformation matrix A and the word importance weights w. It would be valuable to see performance when each component is used in isolation (i.e., with only A, with only w, and with both). Without this analysis, it's difficult to determine whether the word reweighting component provides significant additional value beyond the linear transformation.

Minor technical points to clarify: In Equation 11, the dual variables α and β should carry star notation to indicate they are optimal solutions. The reference to "Sinkhorn scaling" in line 170 should explicitly connect to the earlier mention of the Sinkhorn distance in line 56. The phrase "the authors" in line 152 should be clarified to reference specific prior work.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to document representation learning. While the incremental value of the full optimization procedure could be better justified, the proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing approaches. The paper is well-written and the experimental evaluation is thorough. With minor clarifications regarding the initialization method and component contributions, this work would make a solid contribution to NIPS.
