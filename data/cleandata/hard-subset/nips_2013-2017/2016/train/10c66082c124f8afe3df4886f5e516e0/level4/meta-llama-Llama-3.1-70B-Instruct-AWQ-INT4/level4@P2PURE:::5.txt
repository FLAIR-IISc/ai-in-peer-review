The authors analyze the problem of text classification into categories, building upon a previous work that proposed the use of Word Mover's Distance (Wasserstein) in a supervised setting by learning word-specific weights. This contribution introduces the Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for the Wasserstein distance, aiming to improve its computational efficiency. The authors evaluate their method on 8 datasets against 26 baselines, demonstrating the best average performance. 

The paper is well-written, presenting the topic clearly and providing an in-depth analysis of the underlying mathematics. However, there are a couple of weaknesses worth noting. Firstly, the novelty of the approach may be limited compared to the previous Word Mover's Distance paper. Secondly, the comparison to the World Centroid Distance is not entirely relevant and may be confusing. 

Furthermore, the discussion of lambda, the regularizing factor, is somewhat lacking. In my experience, the value of lambda can significantly impact the results, often becoming the dominant factor in equation (2). Theoretically, with a sufficiently large lambda, the results should be similar to those obtained using the pure Wasserstein distance, albeit at the cost of slower convergence. However, in practice, most users of Sinkhorn distance employ a small lambda to improve convergence rates, resulting in a behavior that deviates from the Wasserstein distance. Therefore, I am curious to know whether, in this contribution, the chosen lambda value dictates a behavior closer to the pure Wasserstein distance or the Sinkhorn (entropic) distance.
