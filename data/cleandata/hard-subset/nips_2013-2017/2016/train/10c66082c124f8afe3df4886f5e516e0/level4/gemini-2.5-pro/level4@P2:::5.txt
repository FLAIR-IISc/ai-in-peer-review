This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised document metric for text classification. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which formulates the distance between two documents as an optimal transport problem between their respective word embeddings. The primary limitation of WMD is that it is entirely unsupervised. This paper addresses that limitation by learning two components: an affine transformation of the underlying word embedding space and a vector of word-importance weights. These components are learned jointly to minimize a stochastic approximation of the leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor classification error.

A key technical challenge is that direct gradient-based optimization of the WMD metric is computationally prohibitive. To overcome this, the authors make a critical contribution by approximating the WMD with the regularized Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation not only makes the optimization problem computationally tractable (reducing complexity from O(q³) to O(q²)) but also ensures the objective is strongly convex and differentiable, leading to a practical and efficient update rule. The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation on eight real-world text classification datasets, comparing S-WMD against 26 competitive baselines and demonstrating state-of-the-art average performance.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of the original WMD. By successfully incorporating supervision, the authors develop a powerful document distance metric that significantly improves classification performance. Given the strong results of the original WMD, this supervised extension is a valuable contribution that researchers and practitioners are likely to use and build upon.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The method is technically sound and well-motivated. The use of the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable and efficient proxy for the true WMD is an elegant and effective solution to a major computational hurdle. The combination of learning an affine transformation and word-specific weights provides a flexible model for adapting the distance metric to a specific task.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and clear. The authors provide the necessary background on word embeddings, WMD, and metric learning before carefully detailing their own contributions. The mathematical derivations are presented with great clarity, making the proposed algorithm easy to follow and understand.

4.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The method is benchmarked on eight diverse datasets against a wide array of strong baselines, including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with multiple metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN). The consistently superior performance of S-WMD across these tasks provides compelling evidence of its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / Suggestions for improvement):**

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** While the work is a strong contribution, its novelty could be seen as incremental. The core idea of learning a linear transformation for a ground metric has been explored in other contexts (e.g., Wang et al., 2010 for computer vision). The main innovation here is the specific and effective application of this idea to the WMD framework for text, combined with the crucial Sinkhorn distance approximation for efficient optimization.

2.  **Insufficient Discussion of Hyperparameters:** The role and sensitivity of the Sinkhorn regularization parameter, `lambda`, is insufficiently discussed. The authors state that `lambda=10` is used as a "nice trade-off between speed and approximation accuracy" but provide no further justification, ablation study, or sensitivity analysis. The behavior of the Sinkhorn distance can vary significantly with `lambda`; a small value can lead to a distance that is quite different from the true Wasserstein distance. An analysis showing how performance varies with `lambda` would strengthen the paper and provide valuable insight into whether the method operates in a regime that closely approximates the original WMD or one that is dominated by the entropic regularization.

3.  **Clarity of Baseline Comparisons:** The frequent inclusion of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and its supervised version (S-WCD) in the main results tables can be slightly confusing. While S-WCD is effectively used as an initialization strategy for the full S-WMD model, the most critical comparisons are between S-WMD, its unsupervised counterpart WMD, and the other state-of-the-art baselines. Highlighting the WCD-based methods less prominently could improve the focus of the results section.

Overall, this is a very well-executed paper that presents a significant and practical improvement to a powerful document distance metric. The strengths, particularly the strong empirical results and clear presentation, outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
