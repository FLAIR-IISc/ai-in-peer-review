This paper introduces a method for learning the parameters of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a metric for text documents based on the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) between word embedding distributions. Specifically, the authors parameterize the ground metric of the EMD using a Mahalanobis distance. This allows them to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space to better suit a given task. In addition, the method learns a set of weights for the document histograms. The parameters of the Mahalanobis metric and the histogram weights are learned by optimizing a Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA) style objective function, for which the authors derive the necessary gradients. The proposed approach is validated with compelling experimental results.

Overall, this is a strong paper that combines several existing concepts in a novel and effective way. The core idea is simple and elegant, yet its successful implementation and validation are non-trivial. The experimental section is particularly convincing and demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed method. I recommend acceptance.

However, the paper could be further improved by addressing several points regarding clarity, mathematical rigor, and its connection to related work.

**Major Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Mathematical Rigor and Presentation:** The manuscript would benefit from a more rigorous mathematical presentation. Some notations and computations appear somewhat imprecise. A clearer distinction and motivation for the use of both the regularized (entropic) and unregularized EMD formulations would also strengthen the paper. To significantly improve clarity for the reader, I strongly suggest including a pseudo-code box that outlines the complete training algorithm.

*   **Positioning with Respect to Prior Work:** The related work section could be enhanced by more clearly delineating the contributions of this paper from previous methods.
    *   The paper builds upon the framework of learning with EMD, notably the work of Cuturi and Avis. However, this paper introduces two key distinctions that should be emphasized: (1) the use of entropy regularization for the EMD, which is critical for the method's scalability, and (2) the parameterization of a continuous ground metric via a linear map, as opposed to learning a discrete cost matrix.
    *   Similarly, when discussing Wang et al., it would be beneficial to highlight the technical differences in the optimization procedure. For example, a key advantage of the proposed approach seems to be its more robust optimization scheme compared to the single optimal transport computation used in that work.
    *   Furthermore, the histogram reweighting scheme presented in Equation 5 has strong parallels to previously published work, such as Lebanon's "Metric Learning for Text Documents" and Le and Cuturi's "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations." Acknowledging and discussing these connections would provide better context for this component of the model.

**Minor Comments & Questions**

*   There appears to be an inconsistency between the definition of the ground cost in Equation 1 (Euclidean distance) and its use in Equation 6 for the Mahalanobis metric (squared Euclidean distance). The latter corresponds to the 2-Wasserstein distance. To resolve this and improve generality, the authors might consider framing the WMD as a p-Wasserstein distance, clarifying which value of `p` is used in their formulation and experiments.

*   Could the authors provide some experimental insights into the contribution of the learned histogram weight vector `w`? An ablation study showing performance with and without learning `w` would be valuable to understand the importance of this component of the model. Does this reweighting provide significant benefits across the different tasks?
