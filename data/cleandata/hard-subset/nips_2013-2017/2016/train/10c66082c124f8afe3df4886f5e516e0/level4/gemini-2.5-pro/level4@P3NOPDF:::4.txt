This paper proposes a supervised method for learning a document distance metric, building upon the Word Mover's Distance (WMD). WMD, inspired by the Earth Mover's Distance, calculates the minimum "cost" required to transform the word distribution of one document into another. This cost is determined by an optimal transport plan, where the ground distance between individual words is typically given by their cosine distance in a pre-trained word embedding space like word2vec.

The key contribution of this work is to make this distance metric supervised. The authors introduce two learnable components: (1) a linear transformation matrix `A` that projects the word embeddings into a new space where distances are more discriminative, and (2) a vector `w` of word-specific weights that re-scales the word distributions (the document's bag-of-words representation). These parameters are optimized using a large-margin objective, akin to Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA), to pull documents with the same label closer together while pushing those with different labels further apart.

**Strengths**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the background of WMD before introducing their own contributions.
*   **Literature Review:** The paper includes a thorough review of related work, demonstrating the authors' strong command of the relevant literature on document distance metrics and metric learning.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive set of experiments on several novel datasets, showcasing the effectiveness of their method across different tasks.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

Despite the clear presentation and solid empirical results, I have several concerns regarding the motivation, significance, and practical application of the proposed method.

1.  **Motivation for Word Reweighting (`w`):** The paper's motivation for reweighting the word distribution with the vector `w` is not sufficiently justified. This reweighting step has the potential to fundamentally alter the semantic content of a document. For instance, by heavily down-weighting important keywords, one could effectively change the document's topic or meaning. For this component to be compelling, the authors should provide a stronger theoretical or intuitive justification. Could the authors provide qualitative examples showing how `w` helps? For example, does it learn to up-weight discriminative terms (like "touchdown" for a sports category) and down-weight common stop words, and does it do so without distorting the document's core message?

2.  **Significance of Contribution:** The technical novelty of the paper appears to be incremental. The approach of learning a linear transformation (`A`) and feature weights (`w`) under a large-margin or NCA-style objective is a well-established pattern in supervised metric learning. Applying this pattern to WMD is a logical extension, but the formulation itself does not seem particularly groundbreaking or to introduce a novel conceptual framework. The contribution lies more in the successful application of existing ideas to a specific problem rather than in the development of a new methodology.

3.  **Inference on New Documents:** A crucial practical detail is missing from the paper: how the learned model calculates distances for new, unseen documents at test time. Specifically, it is unclear how the word reweighting `w` is determined for a document that was not part of the training set. Is `w` a global set of weights learned for the entire vocabulary? If so, this should be stated clearly. If the weights are document-specific, the mechanism for inferring them for a new document must be explained, as this is critical for the method's applicability.

4.  **Clarification of Terminology ("Semantic Difference"):** The abstract uses the phrase "semantic difference," but the experiments focus on tasks like authorship attribution and topic classification. In these contexts, the model is learning to distinguish class labels, which is not always synonymous with semantic difference in its traditional linguistic sense. For instance, two documents from different authors could be semantically identical. The authors should clarify if "semantic difference" is intended to have a broader meaning here or if a more precise term, such as "class-discriminative distance," would be more appropriate.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents a well-executed and clearly described extension of Word Mover's Distance for supervised tasks. The empirical results are promising. However, the paper's overall impact is currently limited by a lack of clear motivation for the word-reweighting component, concerns about the significance of the technical novelty, and a critical omission regarding the method's application to new documents. I am currently on the borderline but would be willing to raise my score if the authors can satisfactorily address these major concerns in their rebuttal.
