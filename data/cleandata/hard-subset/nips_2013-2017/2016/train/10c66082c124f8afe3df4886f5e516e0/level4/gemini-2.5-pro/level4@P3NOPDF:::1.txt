### Summary

This paper introduces a supervised metric learning approach for document classification, building upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD). WMD is an effective distance measure between documents that leverages word embeddings and optimal transport theory. The proposed method, Supervised WMD (S-WMD), extends this by learning a task-specific distance metric. Specifically, it learns two components: (1) a linear projection of the word embeddings to a more discriminative space, and (2) a set of word-specific importance weights that re-scale word frequencies within a document. The paper also presents an efficient alternating optimization algorithm to learn these parameters. A thorough experimental evaluation on several document classification datasets demonstrates that S-WMD, when used with a k-NN classifier, outperforms WMD and a variety of other baseline methods.

### Strengths

*   **Timely and Useful Extension:** The paper extends the influential Word Mover's Distance to a supervised setting. Given the success of WMD, developing a method to tailor it to specific tasks is a logical and valuable contribution to the field of text analysis and metric learning.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing S-WMD against a large and appropriate set of baselines. The results clearly show that the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on several k-NN text classification benchmarks.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and a pleasure to read. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining their methodology, and presenting their results. The technical details are conveyed with precision and clarity.

### Weaknesses

The main weaknesses of the paper relate to the practical significance of the core technical contribution and some missing experiments that would help contextualize the results.

1.  **Limited Impact of the Full Optimization:** My primary concern is that the main technical contribution—the end-to-end learning algorithm—appears to provide only a marginal benefit over a much simpler approach. In Table 3, the performance of the full S-WMD is very similar to the "WMD-MD" baseline, which simply uses a projection matrix learned via a "word centroid distance" as initialization. This suggests that most of the performance gain comes from a good initialization based on existing techniques, and the complex alternating optimization procedure adds very little on top. This observation somewhat diminishes the practical impact of the proposed learning framework.

2.  **Missing Ablation Study:** The proposed method learns two distinct components: the projection matrix `A` and the word-specific importance weights. The paper does not include an ablation study to disentangle the contributions of these two components. To better understand the model, it would be highly beneficial to see the performance under the following conditions:
    *   Learning only the projection matrix `A` (i.e., fixing all word weights to be uniform).
    *   Learning only the word weights (i.e., fixing `A` as the identity matrix).
    Such an analysis is crucial for understanding the source of the improvements and the relative importance of each learned component.

3.  **Missing Baselines:** The evaluation could be strengthened by including another important set of baselines. The paper introduces the "word centroid embedding" (a weighted mean of word embeddings) as a simple way to represent a document for learning the projection matrix. However, this simple document vector representation is not evaluated in combination with standard, off-the-shelf metric learning algorithms like ITML, LMNN, or NCA. Comparing S-WMD to a baseline like "Word Centroid Embedding + LMNN" would help clarify whether the performance gains stem from the underlying transport-based distance or from the specific learning algorithm proposed in this paper.

### Minor Comments and Questions

The paper is very polished, but a few minor points could be clarified:

*   **Line 152:** In the phrase "The authors show...", it is ambiguous which authors from the citations [1, 7, 8] are being referred to. Please clarify this.
*   **Equation 11:** For notational consistency, should the dual variables `alpha` and `beta` here be denoted with a star (e.g., `alpha*`, `beta*`) to indicate that they are the optimal solutions to the dual problem?
*   **Line ~170:** It would improve readability to explicitly state that the iterative optimization procedure described here is an instance of the Sinkhorn scaling algorithm, which was mentioned in the introduction (Line 56). This would help the reader connect the two parts of the paper.
