

This paper proposes a supervised extension of Word Mover's Distance (WMD) for document similarity learning. Building on the unsupervised WMD framework—which computes document distance via optimal transport between word distributions using word2vec embeddings—the authors introduce two novel components: (1) a linear transformation applied to word embeddings, and (2) a reweighted word distribution via a learnable vector *w*. The goal is to enhance label separation in a supervised setting. The paper is well-structured, with a thorough literature review demonstrating strong familiarity with prior work. Experimental results on multiple novel datasets are presented, supporting the method's efficacy. However, the core contributions require stronger justification, and the technical significance appears limited. Below, I elaborate on key concerns.

First, the motivation for reweighting the word distribution using *w* is inadequately explained. The current formulation (Eq. 3) reweights word frequencies per document, potentially altering the document's semantic content. For instance, if *w* heavily weights a rare word, the transformed distribution may no longer represent the original document. The authors must clarify *why* this reweighting preserves document semantics while improving label separation. A concrete example showing how *w* adjusts without distorting meaning—e.g., by emphasizing topic-relevant words while suppressing noise—would be essential. Without this, the approach risks conflating document representation with artificial feature engineering.

Second, the method’s contribution is incremental. Combining linear transformation *A* and reweighting *w* with the Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA) objective is a natural extension of WMD to supervised learning. While technically sound, this formulation lacks novelty: similar adaptations exist in other distance-learning frameworks (e.g., metric learning with word embeddings). The paper does not convincingly argue why this specific combination is superior to alternatives (e.g., direct NCA on word embeddings or kernel methods). The claim that this "overcomes limitations of WMD" is unsubstantiated without comparative analysis against stronger baselines.

Third, the generalization to unseen documents is unclear. The paper trains *w* per document (Eq. 3), but for a new, unlabeled document, how is *w* determined? The authors must specify: (a) whether *w* is learned globally (e.g., as a shared parameter) or per-document (which would require additional inference), and (b) how *w* is computed for documents outside the training set. If *w* is global, it must be justified as universally applicable across diverse topics—a significant assumption unsupported by the current analysis. If per-document, the computational overhead and feasibility for large-scale deployment become critical concerns.

Fourth, the abstract’s reference to "semantic difference" is ambiguous. The authors state the method captures "semantic differences between documents," yet the experiments focus on label separation (e.g., topic or author classification). Semantic differences typically imply meaning-level contrasts (e.g., "climate change" vs. "economic policy"), not label assignments. If "semantic difference" here refers solely to label distinctions, this misrepresents the method’s capability. Clarification is needed: does the model capture *inherent* semantic dissimilarity, or merely *label-driven* dissimilarity? The latter would limit applicability beyond supervised tasks.

Despite these concerns, the paper’s clarity, comprehensive literature review, and empirical results are commendable. The authors demonstrate a solid grasp of WMD and its limitations. However, the above gaps undermine the paper’s impact. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should: (1) rigorously justify *w*’s role in preserving document semantics; (2) contrast their approach with non-trivial baselines (e.g., supervised variants of WMD or metric learning); (3) clarify the generalization mechanism for new documents; and (4) redefine "semantic difference" to align with empirical results.

In summary, the work presents a plausible extension to WMD but lacks sufficient novelty and theoretical grounding. The proposed method is a valid technical step but does not advance the field meaningfully. With the above revisions—particularly addressing the motivation for reweighting and generalization—the paper could be a solid contribution. As it stands, the significance is marginal, and the method’s utility hinges on resolving these ambiguities. I recommend **major revision** before acceptance. The authors should explicitly address all points above in the revision.
