### Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other within a word embedding space (e.g., word2vec). While WMD has shown state-of-the-art performance in unsupervised settings, it cannot leverage labeled data.

The authors propose to learn a supervised metric by incorporating two key components: 1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, which adapts the word representations to the specific task, and 2) a word importance vector `w`, which re-weights the normalized bag-of-words representation of each document to emphasize task-relevant words. To learn `A` and `w`, the paper formulates an objective function based on minimizing the stochastic leave-one-out k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) classification error, following the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) framework.

A key technical challenge is that the gradient of the WMD is computationally prohibitive and involves a nested optimization problem. To overcome this, the authors propose an efficient approximation by relaxing the underlying optimal transport problem with an entropic regularization term (leading to the Sinkhorn distance), which yields a differentiable and much faster objective. The final algorithm uses a clever initialization scheme based on a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and employs batch gradient descent to learn the parameters efficiently. The method is evaluated on eight text classification datasets, where it consistently outperforms a large suite of 26 competitive baselines.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  **High Quality and Significance:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of a strong, recently proposed baseline (WMD). Making WMD supervised is a natural and valuable research direction. The results are significant, as S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance on the majority of the eight datasets, outperforming an extensive set of 26 unsupervised and supervised baselines. This demonstrates a clear advance in the field of document classification.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound. The formulation of the learning problem using an NCA-style loss is well-founded. The main technical contribution—the use of the regularized Sinkhorn distance to create a differentiable and efficient approximation for the WMD gradient—is elegant and crucial for making the approach practical. The authors also demonstrate careful engineering through their initialization strategy and batching approach, which contribute to the method's effectiveness.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The background section provides a concise yet thorough overview of WMD, word2vec, and NCA, making the paper accessible. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the proposed method is described in sufficient detail.

4.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare S-WMD against a wide range of strong baselines, including classic methods (TF-IDF, LSI, LDA), modern deep learning approaches (mSDA), and several combinations with leading metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The consistent outperformance across multiple datasets with varying characteristics strongly supports the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Motivation for Word Re-weighting (`w`):** The motivation for learning the word importance vector `w` could be further clarified. This re-weighting fundamentally alters the document's normalized bag-of-words representation (`d̃a`), potentially creating a new "document" that is quite different from the original. While this is empirically effective, a deeper discussion on the interpretation of this transformation would be beneficial. For instance, does this re-weighting primarily serve to down-weight non-informative "stop words" in a task-specific way, or does it risk distorting the document's representation in a less interpretable manner?

2.  **Originality in Formulation:** While the application to WMD is novel and the results are significant, the core components of the learning framework—learning a linear transformation `A` and employing an NCA-style loss—are standard techniques in the metric learning literature. The primary novelty lies in the successful adaptation of these ideas to the complex WMD framework and, critically, in the technical contribution of making this combination computationally tractable. A clearer framing of the contribution in this context would be helpful.

3.  **Generalization to New Documents:** The paper does not explicitly discuss how the learned word importance vector `w` is applied to unseen test documents. Is the vector `w` learned on the training vocabulary and then used to re-weight the bag-of-words vectors of test documents? A clarification on this point is necessary to fully understand the model's generalization mechanism.

4.  **Minor Point on Terminology:** In the abstract, the phrase "semantic meaning of similarity encoded in the labels" is used. This could be interpreted in different ways. For example, document labels can encode authorship, topic, or sentiment, which are distinct types of similarity. It might be clearer to state that the metric is learned to align with the *task-specific similarity* defined by the labels, as "semantic" often implies a more general, task-independent meaning of text.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality, well-executed paper that presents a valuable and effective extension to an important document distance metric. The approach is technically sound, the empirical results are state-of-the-art, and the paper is written with exceptional clarity. While there are minor points that could be clarified regarding the motivation and generalization of the word-reweighting component, the contributions are solid and significant. This work represents a demonstrable step forward for document classification. I recommend acceptance.
