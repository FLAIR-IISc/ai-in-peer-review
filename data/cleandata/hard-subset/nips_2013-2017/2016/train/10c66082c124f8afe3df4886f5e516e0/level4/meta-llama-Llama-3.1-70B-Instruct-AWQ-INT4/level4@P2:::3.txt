This paper proposes a novel approach to learning a supervised document distance metric, building upon the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) introduced by Kusner et al. The authors' method, dubbed Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), learns a generalized Euclidean metric within the word embedding space and a word importance vector. This approach enables the incorporation of supervision into the WMD, which was previously lacking.

The paper relates to previous work on document distances, including the classical bag-of-words (BOW) and term frequency-inverse document frequency (TF-IDF) representations, as well as more recent methods such as Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) and Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The authors also draw inspiration from metric learning algorithms, including Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) and Information-Theoretic Metric Learning (ITML).

The S-WMD method is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their approach. The use of a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem, combined with a probabilistic filtering of the training set, reduces the computation time significantly. The authors also provide a clever initialization and batch gradient descent to speed up the training process.

The experimental results are compelling, with S-WMD achieving the lowest kNN test error on 5/8 datasets and outperforming all other 26 methods on average across all datasets. The visualization of the learned distance metric using t-SNE also demonstrates its effectiveness in capturing semantic relationships between documents.

Strengths of the paper include its simplicity and straightforward idea, as well as the convincing experimental results. However, there are some areas for improvement. The authors could provide more rigorous mathematical derivations and notations, and ideally include an algorithmic box to clarify their proposed method.

Minor comments include the inconsistency in using the Euclidean distance between word embeddings as a cost in Eq. 1, and the squared Euclidean metric in Eq. 6. The authors could also provide more insight into the importance of the weight vector w in the learned metric and its relevance to the results.

In terms of related work, the authors could more clearly underline the differences between their approach and previous work, such as Cuturi/Avis and Wang et al. Specifically, they could highlight the use of entropy regularization, which speeds up and scales up applications, and the difference in cost functions.

Overall, I would support acceptance of this paper, given its simplicity, technical soundness, and convincing experimental results.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* Novel approach to learning a supervised document distance metric
* Compelling experimental results, with S-WMD achieving state-of-the-art performance on 5/8 datasets
* Clear and well-organized presentation of the approach
* Effective use of relaxed optimal transport problem and probabilistic filtering to reduce computation time

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* Minor inconsistencies in mathematical derivations and notations
* Lack of insight into the importance of the weight vector w in the learned metric
* Limited discussion of related work and differences with previous approaches
