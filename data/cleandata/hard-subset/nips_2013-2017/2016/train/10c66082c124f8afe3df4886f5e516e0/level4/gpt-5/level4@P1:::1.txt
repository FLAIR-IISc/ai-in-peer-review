Summary
This paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns both (i) a linear projection of the word-embedding space and (ii) a word-importance vector that reweights document histograms prior to normalization. The learned metric, S-WMD, is trained to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification loss (in the style of NCA), with distances computed via optimal transport over the transformed embeddings. To make training efficient, the authors replace the exact WMD with an entropy-regularized Sinkhorn approximation, enabling fast, differentiable updates and batch training with nearest-neighbor filtering. Experiments on eight text classification datasets compare S-WMD against 26 baselines (including WMD, WCD, LSI, LDA, TF-IDF, BM25, mSDA, and NCA/LMNN/ITML on PCA-reduced features), showing consistent improvements and often state-of-the-art kNN accuracy.

Relation to prior work
The method builds directly on WMD [19] by introducing supervision, and on the Sinkhorn-regularized transport of Cuturi [6] for tractability. The optimization objective follows the NCA framework [15]. Prior work has learned ground metrics for EMD [7, 35], but the proposed method differs by learning both a linear embedding transformation and word-specific histogram reweighting tailored to document labels.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated supervised extension of WMD that leverages both embedding adaptation and word reweighting.
- Practical and efficient training via the Sinkhorn relaxation and neighbor filtering; the complexity discussion is helpful.
- Thorough empirical evaluation across diverse datasets and a strong set of baselines, demonstrating consistent gains.
- Well written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses and missing evaluations
- The incremental benefit of full S-WMD over the supervised word-centroid initialization (S-WMD init.) appears modest on several datasets. The difference between the last two rows in Table 3 suggests much of the performance is already achieved by the S-WCD initialization, potentially limiting the impact of the main technical contributions.
- The baselines lack an evaluation of the “word centroid embedding” (i.e., document-level weighted mean of word embeddings) paired with standard metric learners (ITML, LMNN, NCA). This would clarify how much the proposed objective contributes beyond applying existing metric learning to centroid representations.
- An ablation study is missing to disentangle the contributions of the projection matrix A versus the word-importance weights. Reporting performance with only A learned (no reweighting) and with only w learned (no projection) would quantify the value of each component.
- The conceptual novelty lies mainly in the optimization strategy (Lines 156–175). While learning a linear metric within an EMD-type ground distance is interesting, it is a relatively incremental contribution; related ideas exist in supervised EMD learning and metric learning literature [7, 35]. The reweighting is a practical extension, but its isolated contribution is not evaluated.

Quality
- The method is technically sound, and the use of Sinkhorn distances yields an efficient and differentiable surrogate for training. The gradients and complexity are carefully derived and discussed.
- The stochastic kNN loss is standard and appropriate; the neighbor filtering is sensible given sparsity of large-document corpora.
- However, the evaluation would be stronger with the additional baselines and ablations noted above to support claims about where the gains come from.

Clarity
- The paper is well organized and generally very clear. Minor clarifications:
  - Line 152: “The authors”—please specify which paper ([1, 7, 8]?) to avoid ambiguity.
  - Equation 11: α and β appear to be the optimal dual variables; should they be written with stars (α*, β*) for consistency with earlier notation?
  - Around line 170, explicitly mention that the scaling procedure is the Sinkhorn scaling referred to in the introduction (line 56).

Originality
- The integration of supervised metric learning into WMD with both embedding transformation and histogram reweighting is a useful combination. Nonetheless, the core idea of learning a linear ground metric in EMD has precedents; the main novelty is the specific stochastic-NCA objective with a Sinkhorn-based approximation and the joint learning of w. The originality is thus moderate.

Significance
- The approach is practical and often improves over strong baselines. However, the limited gap over the S-WCD initialization tempers the perceived impact. Demonstrating clear gains over centroid-based metric learning and quantifying the contribution of w versus A would strengthen the case for broader adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid supervised extension to a popular and effective distance (WMD).
- Efficient, well-engineered training procedure.
- Broad and competitive empirical study with generally strong results.
- Clear exposition.

Cons for acceptance
- Modest improvement over the supervised centroid initialization suggests the main technical contributions may add limited value in practice.
- Missing baselines (centroid embeddings with ITML/LMNN/NCA) and missing ablations (A-only, w-only) leave open questions about where the gains arise.
- Conceptual novelty is incremental relative to prior supervised EMD/metric learning work.

Minor issues
- Line 152: clarify the referenced “authors.”
- Equation 11: consider α*, β*.
- Remind readers around line 170 that the fixed-point scaling is the Sinkhorn algorithm.
