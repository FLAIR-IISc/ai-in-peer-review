This paper presents a supervised approach to learning document distance based on Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD, building on the Earth Mover’s Distance, calculates the optimal transport cost between word distributions in a document embedding space. The authors leverage word embeddings, initialized with word2vec, and propose learning both a linear transformation of the embedding space and a re-weighting of word frequencies to improve document separation based on labels. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, providing a thorough review of related work and demonstrating a good understanding of the existing literature. Extensive experimental results are presented on a variety of datasets. However, the motivation for certain design choices could be strengthened, and the overall significance of the contribution is somewhat limited.

Specifically, the motivation behind re-weighting the word distribution with vector *w* requires further explanation. While the idea is intuitively appealing, it's unclear why modifying the document distribution in this way doesn’t fundamentally alter the document's representation. Demonstrating that this re-weighting enhances separation *without* drastically changing the document’s inherent meaning would strengthen the justification. Furthermore, the contribution of this work, while technically sound, feels incremental. Applying a linear transformation and re-weighting within an NCA framework represents a plausible extension to WMD for supervised learning, but it doesn't constitute a particularly novel or groundbreaking approach. 

Additional questions arise regarding practical application. How does the algorithm handle entirely new documents without pre-existing weights? Determining appropriate re-weightings for unseen documents is a crucial consideration. Finally, the term “semantic difference” in the abstract is ambiguous. While label differences represent a semantic distinction in the context of classification, concepts like authorship or topic differ from this notion. Clarifying the intended meaning of “semantic difference” would improve precision.

Overall, this is a well-written and competent paper. While I have reservations regarding the significance of the contribution and the clarity of the re-weighting strategy, I do not object to its acceptance, provided the authors address the raised concerns and offer more detailed explanations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Clear writing and organization.
*   Thorough literature review.
*   Extensive experimental validation on multiple datasets.
*   Solid technical foundation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Motivation for word re-weighting is unclear.
*   Contribution feels incremental and lacks significant novelty.
*   Handling of new documents with unknown weights is not addressed.
*   Ambiguous use of the term "semantic difference."
