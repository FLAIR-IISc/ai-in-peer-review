

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension to the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) introduced by Kusner et al. (NIPS 2015). The authors recognize that while WMD leverages high-quality word embeddings to compute document distances through optimal transport, it lacks a mechanism to incorporate task-specific supervision. S-WMD addresses this limitation by learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights to minimize the stochastic leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. The authors provide a practical approximation using the Sinkhorn distance to overcome the computational challenges of the original WMD's nested optimization problem.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on document distances by bridging the gap between unsupervised word embeddings and task-specific metric learning. It relates well to previous work, particularly the NCA approach for metric learning (Goldberger et al., NIPS 2005) and the WMD framework (Kusner et al., ICML 2015), while introducing novel elements: the word importance weights and the adaptation of metric learning to the optimal transport setting.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with a clear theoretical foundation and practical implementation. The authors provide a rigorous derivation of the gradient approximation using the Sinkhorn distance, which is essential for making the method computationally feasible. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering 8 diverse text classification datasets with 26 competitive baselines. The results consistently demonstrate S-WMD's superiority over unsupervised baselines (WMD, WCD) and supervised metric learning approaches applied to standard document representations. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the method's training time and efficiency, showing it's comparable to or faster than standard metric learning approaches. The paper is technically complete, with appropriate acknowledgment of limitations (e.g., the method's performance on CLASSIC dataset).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section provides a clear explanation of the necessary concepts (word embeddings, WMD, metric learning), and the proposed method builds logically on this foundation. The mathematical derivations are well-presented, and the pseudo-code for the algorithm is helpful. The experimental section is thorough, with appropriate dataset descriptions, baseline comparisons, and visualization of results. The only minor clarity issue is the occasional use of the term "WCD" without first defining it as "Word Centroid Distance" in the main text (it's defined in Section 2 but not immediately after first mention).

**Originality:** The paper demonstrates strong originality by extending the WMD framework to incorporate supervision. While metric learning has been extensively studied, applying it to the optimal transport setting of WMD represents a novel approach. The introduction of word importance weights adds an additional dimension of flexibility not present in prior work. The authors adequately distinguish their approach from related work, particularly noting how their method differs from Wang et al. (ECCV 2016) who learn a metric within EMD but don't consider histogram re-weighting. The paper's contribution is clearly distinct from previous NIPS proceedings.

**Significance:** S-WMD represents a significant advance in document distance metrics, with the potential to impact numerous text classification tasks. The consistent outperformance across 8 diverse datasets (5/8 achieving the best results) and the substantial improvement over state-of-the-art unsupervised methods (e.g., 51% relative error reduction on OHSUMED compared to LSI) demonstrate clear practical value. The method leverages existing high-quality word embeddings without requiring additional training, making it easily adoptable. The approach also provides theoretical insights into how supervision can refine document distances, advancing our understanding of optimal transport in NLP tasks.

**Strengths:**
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across diverse datasets and baselines
- Clear theoretical foundation with practical computational approximation
- Strong empirical results demonstrating consistent superiority
- Well-organized, well-written, and technically sound
- Practical implementation with reasonable training times

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could provide more insight into why S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT and CLASSIC (beyond the brief mention of initialization issues)
- The theoretical analysis of the approximation error introduced by the Sinkhorn distance could be more thorough
- The paper doesn't explore how S-WMD might integrate with deep learning approaches to word embeddings

**Recommendation:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document distances. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated across a wide range of tasks. The paper's clarity and completeness make it suitable for NIPS. I recommend acceptance as a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in document representation and classification.

The authors have made a compelling case for the value of incorporating supervision into the WMD framework, and their method provides a practical solution to a meaningful problem in text classification. The paper's extensive evaluation and clear presentation make it a valuable contribution to the field.
