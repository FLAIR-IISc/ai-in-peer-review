This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach for document classification, building upon the recently introduced Word Mover's Distance (WMD). The method learns a linear projection of word embeddings to be used within a transport distance (based on squared Euclidean distances) and also learns word-specific weights to modulate word frequencies. The authors present an efficient optimization procedure for learning the parameters of this metric. A thorough experimental evaluation on several document classification datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach, comparing it against a large number of strong baselines using a k-NN classifier.

**Strengths**

*   This work presents an interesting and technically sound extension to the Word Mover's Distance. The empirical results show that this extension is effective for k-NN text classification tasks.
*   The paper is very well-written, clearly structured, and a pleasure to read.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

*   The primary concern is that the impact of the main technical contribution—the full optimization of the proposed distance—appears limited. As shown in the last two rows of Table 3, the performance of the fully learned metric is very close to that achieved by simply initializing with a metric learned via a "word centroid distance." This suggests that the complex optimization procedure may not provide substantial benefits beyond this simpler initialization scheme based on existing techniques.
*   The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including a more direct baseline. Specifically, it would be valuable to see an evaluation of applying standard metric learning algorithms (e.g., ITML, LMNN, NCA) directly to a "word centroid embedding" (the weighted mean of word embeddings for a document).
*   The paper would also benefit from an ablation study to disentangle the contributions of its two learned components: the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A. Evaluating the performance with each component learned in isolation would clarify their relative importance.

**Originality**

*   The core idea of applying linear metric learning within the earth-mover's distance framework is interesting, though it represents a limited contribution in itself. The main novelty lies in the specific optimization method developed to learn the metric (Lines 156–175).
