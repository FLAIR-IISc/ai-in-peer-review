This paper presents a supervised approach to learning document distances building upon the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD, motivated by Earth Mover’s Distance, formulates document distance as an optimal transport problem between word embeddings. Specifically, the authors propose learning (1) a linear transformation of the word embedding space and (2) a word-importance weight vector to improve document separation based on labels. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, demonstrating a strong understanding of related work in metric learning and document representation. Extensive experimental results are provided across eight datasets, showcasing the performance of the proposed Supervised-WMD (S-WMD) metric.

However, certain aspects of the formulation warrant further clarification and raise concerns about the significance of the contribution. First, the motivation for reweighting the word distribution with vector *w* is not fully explained. While intuitively appealing, the potential for substantial alteration of the original document representation through this reweighting requires more justification. Demonstrating that the reweighting preserves document semantic content while enhancing discriminative power would strengthen the approach. Second, the contribution feels incremental. Applying a linear transformation *A* and reweighting *w* within a Nearest Component Analysis (NCA) framework represents a natural extension of WMD to a supervised setting, but lacks a novel algorithmic leap. Third, the method’s applicability to entirely new documents is unclear. Determining an appropriate reweighting *w* for documents outside the training set poses a challenge. Finally, the use of “semantic difference” in the abstract is vague; clarifying whether this refers specifically to label-based distinctions, or encompasses other forms of dissimilarity (e.g., authorship, topic) is crucial. 

Overall, this is a well-executed paper with a solid technical foundation. Addressing the concerns outlined above would significantly enhance its impact. While I don’t strongly object to acceptance, these points should be addressed to fully realize the paper’s potential.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong experimental results consistently outperform numerous baselines.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the method and related work.
*   Addresses a practical problem in document distance learning.
*   Efficient implementation leveraging recent advances in optimal transport approximation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Motivation for the word reweighting is not fully justified.
*   The contribution is incremental and lacks a particularly novel algorithmic component.
*   Challenges remain in applying the method to unseen documents.
*   Vague terminology ("semantic difference") requires clarification.
