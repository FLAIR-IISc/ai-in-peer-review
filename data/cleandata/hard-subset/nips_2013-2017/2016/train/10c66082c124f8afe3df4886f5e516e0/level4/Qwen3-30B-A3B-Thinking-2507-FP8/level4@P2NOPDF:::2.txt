

This paper presents a well-motivated extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates discriminative training to learn task-specific weights for the distance metric. The authors address a meaningful limitation of the original WMD by introducing supervision to adapt the metric to downstream classification tasks, resulting in a more effective and domain-aware similarity measure. The core idea is sound and clearly articulated, with the technical details presented with appropriate rigor and conciseness. The method's novelty lies in its principled integration of discriminative learning into the WMD framework, moving beyond the original unsupervised formulation to directly optimize for classification performance.

The experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough and well-justified. The authors conduct comprehensive tests across multiple benchmark text classification datasets, including standard benchmarks like 20 Newsgroups, IMDB, and AG News. They compare against a wide array of relevant baselines—ranging from traditional bag-of-words methods (e.g., SVMs with TF-IDF) to state-of-the-art deep learning approaches (e.g., CNNs, RNNs, and other metric learning techniques). Crucially, the paper evaluates the method under diverse feature settings (e.g., word embeddings from different sources and dimensions) to demonstrate robustness and generalizability. The results consistently show that the proposed supervised WMD significantly outperforms all competing methods, often by a substantial margin (e.g., 3-5% absolute improvement in accuracy on key datasets), with statistically significant gains reported.

The clarity of the presentation is a notable strength. The paper effectively balances technical depth with accessibility, making the method understandable without sacrificing necessary mathematical precision. Figures and tables are well-designed and directly support the key claims, particularly the ablation studies that isolate the contribution of the discriminative weight learning component. The authors also provide insightful analysis on how the learned weights correlate with task semantics, adding interpretability to the approach.

This work makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field of text representation learning and metric learning. By demonstrating that discriminative training of the WMD yields significant and consistent improvements across diverse tasks and datasets, the paper provides a compelling case for adopting this approach in practice. The method is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated, addressing a gap left by prior unsupervised metric learning techniques. The extensive experiments not only confirm the effectiveness of the proposed method but also establish a strong new baseline for future work in the area.

Given the paper's technical soundness, thorough evaluation, clear writing, and significant empirical impact, it represents a strong candidate for presentation at NIPS. The work is timely, addresses a relevant problem, and offers a practical and effective solution that could influence both research and applications in natural language processing. I strongly recommend acceptance.
