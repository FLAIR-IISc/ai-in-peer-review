### **Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD) [19], which leverages pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec) to calculate the distance between two documents as an optimal transport problem. While WMD has shown impressive performance, it is entirely unsupervised. This paper addresses this limitation by incorporating supervision.

The proposed S-WMD learns two key components to tailor the distance metric for a specific classification task: 1) an affine transformation (`A`) of the word embedding space, which adapts the geometry of word representations, and 2) a word-importance vector (`w`), which re-weights word frequencies to emphasize task-relevant words. The authors formulate an objective function based on minimizing the stochastic k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) leave-one-out classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA).

A direct optimization of this objective is computationally prohibitive due to the nested optimal transport problem within the gradient calculation. The main technical contribution of the paper is an efficient optimization scheme. This is achieved by approximating the WMD with the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance, which permits a much faster and differentiable computation of the transport plan and its gradient. The authors also propose an effective initialization strategy using a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and employ a batch gradient descent with nearest-neighbor filtering to further improve training efficiency. A comprehensive experimental evaluation on eight document classification datasets demonstrates that S-WMD consistently outperforms a large suite of 26 competitive baselines, establishing a new state-of-the-art for kNN-based text classification.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Timely Extension:** The paper provides a logical and valuable extension to the Word Mover's Distance, which has garnered significant interest. Moving from an unsupervised metric to a supervised one is a natural and important next step, allowing the powerful WMD framework to be adapted to specific downstream tasks.

2.  **Technically Sound and Novel Optimization:** The core challenge of embedding a WMD calculation within a gradient-based optimization loop is non-trivial. The proposed solution, which leverages the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance to create an efficient and differentiable approximation, is elegant and effective. This makes an otherwise intractable problem practical.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is a major strength. The authors compare S-WMD against an extensive set of 26 baselines, including classic methods (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), neural methods (mSDA), and combinations of these with standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN). The method's strong performance across eight diverse datasets convincingly demonstrates its effectiveness.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and a pleasure to read. The background is explained succinctly, the proposed method is motivated and derived logically, and the results are presented clearly.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Marginal Gain Over a Simpler Initialization Method:** The primary weakness concerns the practical significance of the full S-WMD optimization. As shown in Table 2, the performance of "S-WMD init" (which is essentially applying NCA to the Word Centroid Distance, a much simpler method) is remarkably close to the final S-WMD performance. For example, on several datasets like OHSUMED, RECIPE, and AMAZON, the difference is negligible. While the full S-WMD method does improve the average rank, the small margin of improvement raises questions about the value-add of the complex optimization machinery (the main technical contribution) over the simpler and computationally cheaper initialization technique.

2.  **Incomplete Baselines for Word Centroid Representation:** The "S-WMD init" method, also referred to as Supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD), applies NCA to the word centroid document representation. However, the evaluation does not include comparisons of this word centroid representation with other powerful metric learning algorithms like LMNN and ITML. It is possible that applying ITML or LMNN to the word centroid vectors could perform as well as or better than S-WCD or even the full S-WMD. Including these baselines is crucial for a fair assessment of whether the performance stems from the supervised WMD framework itself or just from applying any standard metric learning algorithm to a simple word centroid embedding.

3.  **Lack of Ablation Study:** The method learns two components simultaneously: the projection matrix `A` and the word-importance weights `w`. The paper does not provide an ablation study to disentangle the relative contributions of these two components. It would be highly informative to see the performance of S-WMD when only learning `A` (with `w` fixed to ones) and when only learning `w` (with `A` fixed to the identity matrix). This would provide deeper insight into what aspects of the learned metric are most crucial for its success.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally very clear. A few minor points could further improve it:
    *   Line 152: When discussing the uniqueness of the transport plan optimizer, the text says "The authors...". It would be helpful to clarify which reference this refers to (e.g., "[1] shows that...").
    *   Around line 170, it is mentioned that the relaxed transport can be solved using an alternating matrix-scaling algorithm. It would be beneficial to explicitly remind the reader here that this refers to the Sinkhorn distance algorithm introduced in Section 2, strengthening the connection between the background and the method.
    *   In the derivation of the gradient w.r.t. `w` (Eq. 10), the notation for the dual variables could be slightly more precise. Are `α*` and `β*` the exact optimal dual variables, or should they be explicitly written as their relaxed counterparts, `α*λ` and `β*λ`, which are computed from `u` and `v`? Ensuring this notation is consistent would enhance clarity.

### **Summary of Arguments for and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and technically sound method for supervised metric learning within the powerful Word Mover's Distance framework.
*   Addresses the computational challenge of the nested optimization problem with an elegant and efficient approximation based on the Sinkhorn distance.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on kNN document classification through a very thorough and convincing experimental evaluation against a wide array of strong baselines.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The performance gain of the full, complex S-WMD optimization over its much simpler initialization method (S-WCD) is often marginal, questioning the practical significance of the main technical contribution.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks key baselines (e.g., applying ITML/LMNN to the word centroid representation) and a proper ablation study to isolate the effects of the learned projection matrix `A` and word weights `w`.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the noted weaknesses, the strengths of this paper—its novelty, technical soundness, excellent empirical results, and clarity—are substantial. The weaknesses primarily relate to the analysis and completeness of the evaluation rather than a fundamental flaw in the method itself. These points can be addressed in a final version. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of metric learning and its application to text data. I recommend acceptance.
