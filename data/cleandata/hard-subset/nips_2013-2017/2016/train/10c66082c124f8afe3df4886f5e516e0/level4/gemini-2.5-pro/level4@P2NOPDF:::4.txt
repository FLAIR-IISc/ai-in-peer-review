This paper introduces a supervised extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a metric for measuring document dissimilarity. The original WMD method, inspired by the Earth Mover's Distance, calculates the minimum cost required to "transport" the word distribution of one document to another. The cost of transporting individual words is typically based on the Euclidean distance between their word2vec embeddings.

The authors propose to learn a more effective distance metric by optimizing two components within the WMD framework: (1) a linear transformation matrix `A` that projects the word embeddings into a new space, and (2) a global word-reweighting vector `w` that modifies the importance of words in each document's distribution. These parameters are learned using an objective function that aims to separate documents with different labels while pulling together documents with the same label.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and easy to follow.
*   It provides a thorough review of related work, demonstrating the authors' strong understanding of the relevant literature on document distance metrics and optimal transport.
*   The proposed algorithm is evaluated on a variety of datasets, and the experimental results are presented comprehensively.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
The main weaknesses of the paper relate to the motivation behind certain design choices and the overall significance of the contribution.

1.  **Motivation for Word Reweighting:** The motivation for reweighting the word distribution using the vector `w` is not sufficiently explained. This operation has the potential to fundamentally alter a document's representation by significantly up-weighting or down-weighting certain words across the entire corpus. A major concern is that this could distort a document's original meaning to the point where it no longer reflects its content. The paper would be much stronger if the authors provided a clear justification for this component, perhaps with an analysis showing that it refines the distance metric without corrupting the documents' core semantics.

2.  **Significance of Contribution:** The technical contribution appears to be incremental. Applying a learned linear transformation (`A`) and a reweighting scheme (`w`) to adapt an unsupervised method for a supervised task, using a standard objective like Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA), is a logical but somewhat straightforward extension of the original WMD. The formulation does not seem to introduce a particularly novel or groundbreaking technique.

3.  **Generalization to New Documents:** The paper should clarify how the algorithm computes distances for new, unseen documents at test time. Specifically, how is the reweighting vector `w` applied? The description suggests `w` is a global set of weights learned during training. Is this vector then applied universally to any new document? Clarifying the inference process is crucial for understanding the method's practical applicability and generalization capabilities.

4.  **Clarification of "Semantic Difference":** The abstract uses the phrase "semantic difference" to describe what the learned metric captures. However, the experiments focus on tasks such as topic classification and authorship attribution. These tasks are not always considered purely "semantic" in the traditional sense. The authors should clarify whether "semantic difference" is used as a general term for any difference in document labels or if it has a more specific, intended meaning in this context.

**Conclusion:**
In summary, the paper presents a well-written and clearly explained approach for a supervised WMD. The main concerns are the limited novelty of the formulation and the need for better justification for the word reweighting component, along with clarification on the method's application to new documents.

Despite these points, I believe the paper is a solid contribution. I do not object to its acceptance, provided the authors can satisfactorily address the questions raised above in their rebuttal or a revised version of the manuscript.
