This paper presents a supervised method for learning a document distance metric, building upon the Word Mover's Distance (WMD). The WMD framework, inspired by the Earth Mover's Distance, calculates the optimal transport cost between the word distributions of two documents, where the transport cost between individual words is typically derived from their word2vec embeddings. In this work, the authors propose a method to learn (1) a linear transformation of the word embeddings and (2) a reweighting of the word distributions, with the goal of maximizing the margin between documents from different classes.

The paper is well-written, its presentation is clear, and it is easy to comprehend. The authors provide a comprehensive literature survey, demonstrating a strong command of related work. Furthermore, the algorithm's performance is evaluated on several novel datasets.

However, the rationale behind the proposed formulation could be better justified, and the overall contribution appears limited.

1) The motivation for reweighting the word distribution with the parameter `w` is not well-established. This technique risks fundamentally altering the content of a document, potentially transforming it into something different entirely. The submission would be stronger if the authors could provide a more robust justification for this reweighting and show how it improves performance without distorting a document's original meaning.

2) The significance of the contribution is not substantial. Applying a linear transformation `A` and a reweighting vector `w` within an NCA objective function is a logical, yet straightforward, extension of WMD to a supervised setting. This formulation, while functional, does not represent a groundbreaking or technically innovative approach.

3) It is unclear how the proposed method generalizes to unseen documents. How is the reweighting vector `w` determined for a completely new document that was not part of the training data?

4) The term "semantic difference" used in the abstract seems to be a synonym for the document's class label. This is a slightly unconventional usage, as differences in authorship or topic are not typically described this way. The authors should clarify what they specifically mean by this term.

Overall, the paper is well-presented, and I would not object to its acceptance, provided the authors can satisfactorily address these questions.
