This paper proposes a supervised approach to learning the distance between documents based on the word mover's distance (WMD), which was previously introduced in the literature as a variant of the earth mover's distance. Specifically, WMD calculates the optimal transport from one distribution of words to another to obtain the document distance. Initially, word2vec embeddings are used to determine the transport distance between words. The authors extend this framework by learning (1) a linear transformation of the embedded words and (2) a reweighted word distribution, aiming to increase the separation between documents with different labels. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a thorough review of related works that demonstrates the authors' good understanding of the existing literature. The experimental results on various novel datasets are also provided.

However, several aspects of the paper require further clarification and motivation. Firstly, the formulation could benefit from a more detailed explanation. Specifically, the use of reweighting the word distribution using w is not well-motivated and seems to potentially alter the document's meaning entirely. The authors should provide a clear justification for this reweighting, explaining how it improves the results without changing the document's semantic meaning.

Secondly, the contribution of this work is not significant. While the adoption of a linear transformation A and reweighting w with the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) objective function is a possible extension of WMD to the supervised setting, this formulation does not bring groundbreaking or innovative ideas to the table.

Thirdly, it is unclear how the proposed algorithm would determine the distance for a completely new document. Specifically, how would one obtain the reweighting w for a novel document?

Lastly, the term "semantic difference" in the abstract appears to refer to the label of the document. However, this interpretation is not explicitly stated, and it is unclear whether the authors intend for it to encompass other aspects, such as authorship or topic differences.

In general, the paper is well-written, and I do not object to its acceptance, provided that the authors address these questions and concerns adequately.
