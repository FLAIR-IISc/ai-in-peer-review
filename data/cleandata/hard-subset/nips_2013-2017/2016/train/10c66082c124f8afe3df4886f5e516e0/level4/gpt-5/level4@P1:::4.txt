Summary
The paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparison. WMD measures the distance between two documents as the optimal transport cost of moving the normalized bag-of-words distribution of one document to another in a pre-trained word embedding space (e.g., word2vec), and has shown strong performance for kNN classification. This paper introduces Supervised WMD (S-WMD), which learns (1) a linear transformation A applied to the word embeddings and (2) a global word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The parameters are trained to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN error using an NCA-style objective. Because differentiating through the exact WMD creates a nested and computationally heavy optimization, the authors use the entropic OT (Sinkhorn) relaxation to obtain a fast, stable approximation of both the transport plan and dual variables, enabling efficient gradient computation. They initialize A via an NCA-learned metric on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) representation (S-WCD) and then fine-tune with S-WMD using minibatches and neighbor filtering. Across eight datasets and 26 baselines (including WMD, WCD, BOW/TF-IDF/LSI/LDA/mSDA, and supervised metric learning on those representations), S-WMD achieves the best average rank and is the top performer on five datasets.

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on: (i) WMD (Kusner et al.) and entropically regularized optimal transport (Cuturi), (ii) supervised metric learning for generalized Euclidean distances (NCA, LMNN, ITML), and (iii) supervised/learned ground metrics for EMD in computer vision. S-WMD’s novelty lies in combining an embedding-space linear transform and global histogram reweighting with OT, optimized end-to-end via an NCA loss and Sinkhorn-based gradients.

Strengths
- Clarity and organization: The paper is clearly written, with a thorough review of relevant literature and a solid technical exposition of the Sinkhorn-based approximation and gradients.
- Technical soundness: The use of entropic regularization to obtain efficient and differentiable approximations is appropriate and well executed.
- Empirical evaluation: Strong and broad experiments on eight datasets against 26 competitive baselines, with consistent gains on average. Code availability is a plus.
- Practicality and efficiency: The initialization via S-WCD and minibatch/neighborhood filtering make the method computationally feasible.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
1) Motivation and implications of reweighting w: The introduction of a global word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms needs stronger justification. Reweighting can, in principle, substantially alter a document’s effective distribution and potentially its semantics. Please clarify:
- Is w constrained (e.g., nonnegative, bounded, regularized) to prevent pathological reweighting?
- How does w relate to classical weighting schemes (e.g., IDF)? An empirical analysis or correlation study would help.
- Can you demonstrate that reweighting improves class separation without “changing the document into a different document,” e.g., via ablations (A only vs w only vs both) and qualitative analysis of learned weights?

2) Significance/novelty: While the method works well, the formulation—learning a linear A and a global w under an NCA objective with Sinkhorn OT—feels like a natural, incremental extension of WMD plus standard metric learning. The contribution is practical rather than conceptual. Providing deeper insight (e.g., generalization bounds, theoretical properties of the learned ground metric, or tighter links to supervised EMD literature) or more extensive ablations could strengthen the perceived novelty.

3) Generalization to new documents: Please clarify how the method is applied at test time. Is w a single global vector learned from training data and then applied uniformly to any new document (i.e., d̃ = (w ◦ d)/(w>d))? If so, state this explicitly. If w is per-document (which the current text does not suggest), how is it inferred for unseen documents? This point is crucial for understanding deployment.

4) “Semantic difference” terminology: The abstract and introduction refer to making distances match the “semantic meaning of similarity encoded in the labels.” However, the labels can reflect different notions (topic, author, sentiment), not all of which are strictly semantic. Please clarify what you intend by “semantic,” and specify that the supervision defines the task-specific notion of similarity.

Additional observations
- The authors note that on some datasets (e.g., CLASSIC) S-WMD underperforms WMD, possibly due to squared Euclidean ground cost and/or initialization. It would be useful to report sensitivity to the ground metric (e.g., cosine or p=1) and to the initialization (S-WCD).
- Given the reliance on λ in the Sinkhorn relaxation, a brief sensitivity analysis of λ would be helpful.
- The visualizations of learned word weights are useful; further tying them to class labels (e.g., per-class interpretation) would strengthen the motivation for w.

Evaluation against review criteria
- Quality: Technically sound and well supported by experiments. The training objective and gradient approximations are appropriate. Some aspects (motivation and constraints for w, test-time behavior) need clearer exposition and ablations.
- Clarity: Generally very clear and well organized. A few terms (“semantic difference”) and the role of w at test time should be defined more precisely.
- Originality: Moderate. The paper combines known ingredients (WMD/OT, NCA, linear metric learning) in a sensible way for supervised document distances.
- Significance: Empirically strong with broad applicability. While conceptually incremental, the method appears practically useful and improves over strong baselines.

Pros
- Clear, thorough paper with strong empirical results and open-source code.
- Efficient training via Sinkhorn approximation, initialization, and batching.
- Consistent improvements over many baselines across diverse datasets.

Cons
- Motivation and constraints for histogram reweighting w are underexplained.
- Conceptual novelty is limited; more ablation/theory would help.
- Test-time application of w and the meaning of “semantic” need clarification.

Overall
A well-executed, practical extension of WMD that performs strongly across datasets. I would not object to acceptance, especially if the authors address the questions above (particularly the motivation and constraints for w, ablations isolating the effects of A vs w, and clear test-time usage).
