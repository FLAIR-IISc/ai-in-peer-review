This paper proposes a novel method for learning a supervised document distance metric, building upon the previous work on Word Mover's Distance (WMD). The original WMD is unsupervised, measuring the distance between word embeddings. The authors extend this to the supervised setting, where the supervision is of the type 'these documents belong to the same class'.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method, which includes learning a linear transformation A and word importance weights w. The intuition behind the usage of w in Eq. (5) is not entirely clear, and it would be helpful to understand which of these two components is responsible for the reported improvements.

The experimental evaluation of the method is thorough, with a comparison to 26 competitive baselines on eight different document corpora. However, evaluating a distance metric is not easy, and it would be interesting to see how well a simple classifier does on the same datasets for comparison.

The authors provide a clear explanation of the related work in the field of metric learning, including both supervised and unsupervised techniques. The paper also includes a discussion on the advantages of the proposed method, including its ability to capture the semantic meaning of similarity encoded in the labels.

There are a few minor issues with the paper. For example, the lambda in Eq. (2) seems to be different from the lambda in ln170, and it would be helpful to clarify this. Additionally, the authors claim that the method is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words," but the argument for this claim is not entirely clear.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method. The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the authors provide a good discussion of the related work and the advantages of the proposed method.
