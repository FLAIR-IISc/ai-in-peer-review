The authors tackle the problem of categorizing text by expanding on a previous study that introduced Word Mover's Distance (Wasserstein) and making it supervised through the learning of word-specific weights. A key contribution of this paper is the use of Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for Wasserstein distance to enhance computational efficiency. The authors evaluate their method on eight datasets, comparing it to 26 baselines and achieving the best average performance. The paper is well-written, clearly presenting the topic and providing an in-depth analysis of the underlying mathematics.

However, there are a couple of potential drawbacks. Firstly, the novelty of this work may be limited compared to the original Word Mover's Distance paper. Additionally, the references to World Centroid Distance are somewhat confusing and do not seem particularly relevant. Furthermore, the discussion of lambda (the regularizing factor) appears to be lacking. In my experience, the value of lambda can significantly impact the results, sometimes dominating the equation. Theoretically, a large enough lambda should yield results similar to the pure Wasserstein distance, albeit at the cost of slower convergence. However, in practice, a small lambda is often used to improve convergence rates, resulting in behavior distinct from Wasserstein. I would be interested to know whether the chosen value of lambda in this study results in behavior akin to pure Wasserstein or Sinkhorn (entropic) behavior.
