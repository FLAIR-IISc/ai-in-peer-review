The paper proposes a method to learn the parameters of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), i.e., the Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) applied to text documents where bin-to-bin dissimilarities are computed from word embeddings. The ground cost matrix is parameterized via a Mahalanobis distance over the embedding vectors, and the authors also learn a reweighting of histogram coefficients (see Lebanon’s work; comment below). They derive gradients for NCA-type objectives tailored to this setting and present compelling experimental results.

Overall, the paper reads as a well-executed combination of existing techniques, supported by strong empirical evidence. I would support acceptance. Strengths include the simplicity and clarity of the core idea—while still nontrivial to implement and evaluate—and a thorough experimental section.

Areas for improvement:
- Clarify the relationship and trade-offs between regularized and unregularized formulations.
- Tighten mathematical rigor: some derivations and notation feel sloppy.
- Include a clear algorithmic summary (e.g., a boxed procedure or pseudocode) to make the overall method easier to follow.

Minor comments:
- In Eq. 1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is used as the ground cost, whereas in Eq. 6, for Mahalanobis metric learning, the cost effectively becomes the squared Euclidean distance (i.e., the 2-Wasserstein case). To avoid this inconsistency, it may be clearer to state that WMD is the p-Wasserstein distance between point clouds of word embeddings, for an appropriate p.
- The “reweighting” in Eq. 5 is not new; it was previously proposed by Guy Lebanon in the specific context of text document classification (“Metric Learning for Text Documents”). See also Le and Cuturi, “Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations.”
- Please provide experimental insight into the role of the learned weight vector w: how important is it in practice? Does this component materially improve performance, and in what scenarios?
- Related work should more clearly delineate differences from prior art. This work builds on Cuturi/Avis but introduces entropy regularization, which improves speed and scalability; additionally, the cost is now continuous and parameterized via linear maps rather than discrete as in that work. These points are currently missing. The paper also draws from Wang et al., but that work, as written, is not publishable: it runs the OT optimization only once and does not function as proposed.
