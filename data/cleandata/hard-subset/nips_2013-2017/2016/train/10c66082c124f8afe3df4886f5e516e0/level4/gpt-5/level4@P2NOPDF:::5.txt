This paper studies text classification and extends prior work on Word Mover’s Distance by making it supervised through learned, word-specific weights. Its main contribution is to use the Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for the Wasserstein distance to improve computational speed, and to evaluate the method on eight datasets against twenty-six baselines, where it achieves the best average performance. The manuscript is very well written, presents the problem clearly, and provides a deep yet accessible mathematical treatment.

The main weaknesses are the limited novelty relative to the original WMD paper and the discussion around Word Centroid Distance (WCD), which I find tangential and somewhat confusing. More importantly, the role of the regularization parameter lambda appears under-discussed. In my experience, lambda has a substantial effect and can even dominate the objective in equation (2). Theoretically, as lambda grows, the Sinkhorn formulation should approach the true Wasserstein distance, albeit with slower convergence; in practice, many choose small lambda for speed, which leads to behavior that can differ markedly from Wasserstein.

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:
- In your experiments, does the chosen lambda place the method closer to the pure Wasserstein regime or to the entropically regularized (Sinkhorn) regime?
- How was lambda selected (e.g., cross-validation, heuristic), and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- Do larger lambda values empirically recover behavior close to Wasserstein, and what is the associated runtime/accuracy trade-off?

Overall, this is a strong and clearly presented paper with convincing empirical results. However, the incremental novelty over supervised WMD is modest, and the treatment of lambda, as well as the motivation and relevance of the WCD comparison, would benefit from deeper clarification.
