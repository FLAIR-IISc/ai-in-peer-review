Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns a task-specific document metric for kNN classification. The authors parameterize the ground cost in the optimal transport (OT) formulation by a linear map A (i.e., a Mahalanobis distance on word embeddings) and additionally learn a word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. Training minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss (à la NCA) using an efficient approximation: the entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) to avoid nested LPs and to obtain tractable gradients. They also propose a practical initialization via supervised word centroid distance (S-WCD) by applying NCA to document centroids (WCD). With batching and neighbor filtering, the method scales well. Across eight datasets and 26 baselines, S-WMD achieves the best average rank and is state of the art on most tasks.

The work builds on several strands: WMD for document distance via OT over word embeddings (Kusner et al.); entropy-regularized OT for efficiency (Cuturi); supervised metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML); learning ground metrics for EMD/OT (Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.); and histogram reweighting/geometry for text (e.g., Lebanon’s metric learning for text, and Aitchison-transform–based approaches such as Le & Cuturi). The paper’s contribution is to combine these elements into a supervised, scalable, and effective OT-based document metric.

Strengths
- Clear, useful problem: learn supervision-aware document distances while retaining WMD’s semantic robustness.
- Practical and efficient: Sinkhorn relaxation, dual-variable reuse for gradients, minibatching, and neighbor filtering yield fast training.
- Sensible initialization (S-WCD) that improves stability and reduces training time.
- Strong empirical evaluation: eight datasets, extensive baselines (unsupervised and supervised with PCA + NCA/ITML/LMNN), and consistent gains; helpful visualizations and timing results.
- Interpretable: learned w highlights class-discriminative words; linear A is easy to inspect.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity and consistency in math:
  - Align the choice of p across the paper. Eq. 1 uses Euclidean distance, while Eq. 6 and the optimization use squared Euclidean distances (2-Wasserstein). A simple unifying statement that WMD is the p-Wasserstein distance over word embeddings would avoid confusion.
  - Tighten notation and assumptions (e.g., uniqueness of transport plan, use of subgradients when needed).
  - More explicitly explain the relationship between the unregularized WMD objective and the entropy-regularized version used for gradients, and discuss the approximation bias as a function of λ.
- Novelty positioning:
  - The histogram reweighting in Eq. 5 is not new; please cite and contrast with Guy Lebanon’s metric learning for text and with Aitchison-based histogram metrics (e.g., Le & Cuturi). Clarify what is novel in combining reweighting with learned ground costs for OT in the word-embedding setting.
  - Contrast more directly with Cuturi & Avis (learning ground costs for EMD) and Wang et al. (supervised EMD for vision). Here the ground cost is continuous and parameterized by a linear map, and entropy regularization is critical for scalability—these distinctions deserve emphasis.
- Ablations and insights:
  - Quantify the contribution of the weight vector w (e.g., S-WMD with w fixed to 1 vs. learned w). Does w materially improve accuracy or interpretability across datasets?
  - Sensitivity studies: λ in Sinkhorn, number of neighbors M/N for probability truncation, batch size, and the choice of squared vs. unsquared Euclidean distance.
  - Given that S-WMD initialization already performs strongly, clarify typical additional gains and when full S-WMD is most beneficial.
- Presentation:
  - Provide a concise algorithm box with all hyperparameters, default settings, and stopping criteria; ensure symbols match those in the text (Algorithm 1 partially covers this but could be clearer).
  - Minor typos and notation cleanup would further improve readability.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance
- Quality: The method is technically sound and the optimization is well-motivated. While theoretical guarantees are limited, the approximations are standard in modern OT and validated empirically. Strong experiments and fair comparisons.
- Clarity: Generally readable but could benefit from more consistent notation, explicit statements about p and λ, and a crisper presentation of Algorithm 1 and assumptions.
- Originality: The components (Sinkhorn OT, NCA-style loss, learned Mahalanobis ground cost, histogram reweighting) are known, but their integration for supervised WMD over word embeddings is novel and practically impactful.
- Significance: High. A strong, general-purpose document metric that leverages supervision and scales is valuable; results suggest S-WMD is competitive or superior to widely used baselines.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical improvements over a large set of baselines.
- Efficient and practical training procedure.
- Interpretable learned components (A and w).
- Clear relevance to NIPS topics in OT, NLP, and metric learning.

Cons against acceptance
- Moderate conceptual novelty; relies on known building blocks.
- Some mathematical and expository rough edges.
- Missing ablations on w and sensitivity to λ/neighbor filtering.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-executed paper that assembles the right ingredients to produce a practical, high-performing supervised document distance. With improved positioning relative to prior work, clearer math/algorithm presentation, and a few ablations (especially on w), this would be a strong contribution. I recommend acceptance.
