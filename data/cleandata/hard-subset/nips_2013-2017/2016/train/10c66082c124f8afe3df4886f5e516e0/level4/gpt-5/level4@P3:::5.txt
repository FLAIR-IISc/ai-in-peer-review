Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper extends the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) of Kusner et al. (ICML 2015) by learning a supervised ground metric and word-importance weights for document comparison. The authors propose Supervised WMD (S-WMD), which learns (i) a linear transformation A of the word embedding space and (ii) a word importance vector w that reweights normalized bag-of-words histograms. The training objective is a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss à la Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA; Goldberger et al., NIPS 2004). As direct differentiation through the exact WMD is expensive and may require subgradients, they adopt the entropy-regularized transport (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) to obtain fast, smooth approximations to both the transport plan and the dual potentials, enabling efficient gradients. They also propose practical speedups: initializing A via supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD; NCA on document centroids), minibatch training, and focusing gradient computations on a small WCD-based neighbor set.

Empirically, S-WMD is evaluated on eight datasets against 26 baselines spanning classic representations (BOW, TF-IDF, BM25, LSI, LDA, mSDA), unsupervised WCD/WMD, and three metric learning methods (NCA, ITML, LMNN) applied to those representations. S-WMD achieves the best average rank and the lowest error on 5/8 datasets, with competitive results on the remainder. Visualizations via t-SNE suggest improved class separation relative to WMD. Training is reported to be efficient thanks to Sinkhorn-based gradients and neighbor filtering. Code is released.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The formulation is sensible and builds on solid foundations in optimal transport and metric learning. The use of entropic regularization to obtain differentiable approximations to the transport plan and dual variables is well-motivated. The learning objective mirrors established kNN-LOO relaxations.
- Empirical support: The breadth of baselines and datasets is strong. Hyperparameters are tuned via Bayesian optimization; training-time comparisons are reported. The method shows consistent gains in average rank.
- Areas needing clarification:
  - The treatment of the entropic regularization parameter λ is brief. Since λ governs the interpolation between Wasserstein and Sinkhorn (entropic) behavior, and affects both bias and optimization stability, a sensitivity analysis would be valuable. The paper fixes λ=10; please report performance vs λ, convergence behavior, and any annealing tried.
  - Ablations: How much do A and w each contribute? Results for “only A,” “only w,” and “both” would clarify their relative importance. Also show the effect of the neighbor set size N and batch size B.
  - Ground metric choice: The paper fixes p=2 (squared Euclidean) for word distances. Some datasets might favor p=1 or cosine-based costs. A brief rationale and small sensitivity check would strengthen the claims.
  - Gradient correctness/approximation: The mapping from Sinkhorn scaling vectors (u,v) to approximate dual potentials (α,β) is crucial. A short derivation or citation with precise constants would help reproducibility. Discuss approximation error vs λ.
  - Fairness and stability: Since some baselines undergo PCA to 200 dimensions before metric learning, confirm that this preprocessing does not disadvantage methods sensitive to dimensionality reduction.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The exposition of WMD, entropic regularization, and the NCA-based loss is accessible. A few suggestions:
- Unify the notation for vocabulary size and histogram dimensionality; ensure “p” vs “n” is consistent (e.g., in defining 1 as an all-ones vector).
- Clarify the role of WCD and S-WCD: WCD is a lower bound to WMD; S-WCD is used solely for initialization and a neighbor filter. Currently, the interplay among WCD, S-WCD, and S-WMD can be confusing to readers unfamiliar with these distinctions.
- Fix minor typos (e.g., “imporance,” “Sinhorn”) and ensure references are complete and consistently formatted.

Originality:
The core novelty lies in integrating metric learning into WMD by learning both a linear transform of the embedding space and word-level importance, trained with a kNN surrogate, and making this feasible with Sinkhorn-based gradients and neighbor filtering. While prior work has learned ground distances for EMD in vision (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.), applying and scaling these ideas to text with modern embeddings, plus the histogram reweighting and practical training pipeline, is a meaningful and timely contribution. Relative to WMD alone, the idea of supervised adaptation is natural but nontrivial to implement efficiently.

Significance:
The proposed approach achieves strong empirical gains and offers a practical recipe for bringing supervision into transport-based document metrics. Given the popularity of WMD and word embeddings, this can impact practitioners who use nearest-neighbor retrieval/classification. The code release further enhances potential uptake. Limitations include reliance on pre-trained embeddings, a computational cost that can still be high for very long documents, and focus on kNN rather than end-to-end classifiers.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated method that brings supervision to WMD via efficient Sinkhorn-based learning.
- Strong empirical evaluation across many datasets and baselines; best average performance.
- Practical training strategy (initialization + neighbor filtering) and code release.
- Conceptually bridges metric learning and optimal transport for text in a scalable way.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited discussion of λ and the entropic bias; no sensitivity/ablation on this critical parameter.
- Modest algorithmic novelty relative to existing building blocks; more thorough ablations would clarify where the gains come from.
- Some notational and explanatory confusion around WCD/S-WCD vs S-WMD, and minor editorial issues.

Specific suggestions:
- Add a λ sweep (e.g., 1, 5, 10, 20, 50) showing accuracy, training time, and qualitative behavior (e.g., sparsity of transport plans). Discuss whether S-WMD is behaving closer to true Wasserstein (large λ) or entropic Sinkhorn (small λ) in your experiments.
- Provide ablations: (i) learn A only; (ii) learn w only; (iii) both; (iv) random vs S-WCD initialization. Report performance and convergence speed.
- Consider a small comparison to document-level supervised representation methods available circa 2016 (e.g., Paragraph Vector) to complement the current baselines.
- Clarify computational/memory scaling for very long documents; indicate any filtering of rare words or maximum vocabulary per document at training time.
- Improve notation consistency and reference formatting.

Overall recommendation:
This is a well-executed and practically important extension of WMD with clear empirical benefits. Addressing the λ sensitivity and providing ablations would strengthen the paper further. I lean positive.
