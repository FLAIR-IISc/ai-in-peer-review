

The authors address text classification by extending the Word Mover's Distance (Wasserstein) framework to a supervised setting through learned word-specific weights. Their key contribution introduces the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient proxy for the Wasserstein distance, evaluated on eight datasets against 26 baselines, achieving the best average performance. The paper is well-written, providing a clear and thorough mathematical analysis of the problem. However, two primary weaknesses warrant attention. First, the novelty is limited compared to the foundational Word Mover's Distance work, offering incremental progress rather than a substantial conceptual advance. Second, the comparison to World Centroid Distance appears tangential and confusing, detracting from the core contribution. Most critically, the regularization parameter λ (in Equation 2) is inadequately discussed. In practice, λ profoundly influences performance, often dominating the behavior of the model. Theoretically, sufficiently large λ should yield results equivalent to the pure Wasserstein distance (at the cost of slower convergence), yet most Sinkhorn implementations use small λ values to accelerate computation, producing behavior distinct from Wasserstein. It remains unclear whether the authors set λ to achieve a Wasserstein-like solution or adopted the typical entropic regularization regime. This ambiguity significantly impacts the interpretation of their results and the claimed advantages of their approach.
