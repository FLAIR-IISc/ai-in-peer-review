

The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method to learn a supervised metric that extends the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) introduced by Kusner et al. (ICML 2015). The authors address a key limitation of WMD by incorporating supervision through learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights, which directly minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out classification error. The method leverages Cuturi's Sinkhorn distance for computational efficiency, enabling practical application to text classification tasks. This work builds on prior advancements in optimal transport (EMD, Wasserstein distance) and metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML), while addressing a significant gap in the WMD framework.

The paper demonstrates compelling empirical results across eight text classification datasets, consistently outperforming 26 competitive baselines including both unsupervised methods (WMD, WCD) and supervised metric learning approaches applied to various document representations. The experimental validation is comprehensive, covering diverse tasks (news categorization, sentiment analysis, product identification) with appropriate statistical analysis and visualization.

Quality: The technical approach is sound with a solid theoretical foundation. The authors provide well-justified approximations (Sinkhorn distance) to make the method computationally feasible, and the experimental validation is thorough. However, the mathematical presentation could be more rigorous, particularly regarding the relationship between the regularized (Sinkhorn) and non-regularized formulations. The paper inconsistently uses Euclidean distance (Eq. 1) versus squared Euclidean distance (Eq. 6), which should be clarified as the distinction matters for Wasserstein metrics. The authors should specify whether their method is based on 1-Wasserstein or 2-Wasserstein distance.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written overall, with a logical narrative flow. The core idea is accessible to the NIPS audience. The experimental results are presented effectively with appropriate visualizations (Figures 1-2). To further improve clarity, the authors should provide a formal algorithmic box summarizing the full S-WMD procedure, which would enhance reproducibility and make the method more transparent. Additionally, mathematical notation should be standardized throughout.

Originality: The core contribution of extending WMD to a supervised setting with learned linear transformations is novel and addresses a significant limitation of the original WMD. However, the reweighting approach (Eq. 5) is not entirely new, having been previously proposed by Lebanon in "Metric Learning for Text Documents" (2010). The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with this prior work to clarify the relationship between the approaches. The connection to Cuturi's Sinkhorn distance for computational efficiency is well-motivated but could be more explicitly framed as a key innovation for practical application.

Significance: The results demonstrate substantial improvements over state-of-the-art document distance metrics across multiple datasets, with S-WMD achieving the lowest error rate on 5 of 8 tasks. The method provides a practical way to incorporate supervision into WMD without requiring extensive retraining of word embeddings. The training time (Table 3) is competitive with baseline metric learning methods, making it suitable for real-world applications. The visualization in Figure 1 further supports the claim that S-WMD produces more discriminative document representations.

Strengths: The paper makes a clear, well-executed contribution to the field. The experimental validation is thorough and convincing, the method is efficiently implemented, and the approach builds logically on prior work in optimal transport and metric learning.

Weaknesses: The mathematical treatment could be more rigorous, particularly regarding the relationship between regularized and non-regularized formulations. The reweighting approach, while effective, is not entirely novel. The related work section could better distinguish the paper's contributions from prior metric learning approaches for text documents.

The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by providing an efficient supervised extension of WMD. While not entirely novel in every aspect, the combination of ideas is well-executed and yields significant improvements in document classification performance. The experimental results are strong, the method is efficient, and the paper is well-written overall. I recommend acceptance with the authors addressing the noted mathematical and related work concerns in the final version.

Pro: Strong experimental validation across multiple datasets, efficient implementation, clear connection to prior work in optimal transport and metric learning.
Con: Mathematical treatment could be more rigorous, reweighting approach is not entirely novel, related work section could better distinguish contributions from prior work.
