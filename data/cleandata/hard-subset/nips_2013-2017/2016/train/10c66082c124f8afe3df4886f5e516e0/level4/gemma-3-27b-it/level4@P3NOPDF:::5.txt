## Review of NIPS 2016 Submission: [Paper Title – Replace with actual title]

This paper presents an interesting and well-executed extension to the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for text classification. The authors address a key limitation of WMD – its unsupervised nature – by introducing learned word-specific weights, effectively making the distance function supervised. To mitigate the computational expense of WMD, they propose utilizing the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient proxy. A comprehensive evaluation across eight datasets, comparing against 26 baselines, demonstrates state-of-the-art average performance.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains a complex topic. The mathematical foundations are presented with both depth and clarity, making the core ideas accessible. The motivation for the work is well-articulated, and the authors effectively build upon their previous contributions regarding WMD. The empirical evaluation is particularly strong, offering a thorough comparison to a diverse set of existing methods. 

However, the paper possesses a few weaknesses. While the addition of learned weights represents a valuable improvement, the novelty relative to the original WMD paper feels incremental. The connection to and comparison with World Centroid Distance (WCD) appears somewhat tangential and, rather than illuminating the contribution, introduces unnecessary complexity and potential confusion. The discussion surrounding the regularization parameter, λ, is also insufficient.

My primary concern lies with the role of λ and its impact on the behavior of the Sinkhorn distance. In my experience, λ significantly influences the outcome, and can often become the dominant factor in equation (2). Theoretically, a sufficiently large λ should approximate the behavior of the true Wasserstein distance, albeit with slower convergence. Conversely, the typical approach with Sinkhorn is to employ a small λ to accelerate convergence, resulting in a markedly different, entropic behavior. The paper lacks a detailed discussion of where this implementation falls on that spectrum. It remains unclear whether the chosen λ values promote behavior closer to the original Wasserstein distance or exploit the entropic regularization inherent in Sinkhorn. An ablation study varying λ and analyzing its effect on performance and computational cost would be highly valuable. Further clarification on the choices made for λ, and the reasons behind them, is crucial for understanding the method’s behavior and limitations. 

Despite this concern, the overall quality of the paper is high. The proposed method represents a practical and effective advancement in text classification. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the concerns regarding the discussion of λ and potentially clarifying the relevance of the comparison to WCD.



**Strengths:**

*   Clear and comprehensive writing.
*   Strong mathematical foundation and explanation.
*   Extensive and well-executed empirical evaluation.
*   State-of-the-art average performance.
*   Effective extension of existing WMD work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Incremental novelty compared to previous WMD work.
*   Questionable relevance of comparison to World Centroid Distance.
*   Insufficient discussion of the regularization parameter λ and its impact.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the chosen values for λ and how they were determined?
*   Have you performed any ablation studies varying λ to understand its impact on performance and computational cost?
*   Could you clarify the motivation for including the comparison to World Centroid Distance, and how it relates to the core contribution of this work?
