This paper introduces a novel method for learning the parameters of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), effectively a computationally efficient approximation of the Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) tailored for text documents utilizing word embeddings to define bin-to-bin dissimilarities. The core contribution lies in parameterizing the EMD cost matrix – the ground metric – using a Mahalanobis distance computed from vector representations. Furthermore, the authors incorporate a learned reweighting of histogram coefficients, building upon prior work by Lebanon and others. They provide gradients for Non-convex Cost Aggregation (NCA)-type costs within this framework and demonstrate its effectiveness through compelling empirical results.

Overall, the paper presents a well-reasoned combination of established techniques, resulting in a significant practical improvement. The strong experimental validation strongly supports acceptance. The work's strengths lie in the simplicity and intuitiveness of the core idea, coupled with the non-trivial effort required for its implementation and thorough testing. The experimental section, therefore, represents a particularly strong aspect of this submission.

However, several areas could benefit from further refinement. First, the interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations deserves clearer articulation. Second, while the concepts are presented understandably, the mathematical treatment could be more rigorous; the notations and computations are occasionally imprecise. Finally, including a high-level algorithmic box would significantly enhance the reader's understanding of the proposed method.

Specifically, I have the following more detailed comments:

*   **Mathematical Consistency:** In Equation 1, the Euclidean distance between word embeddings is employed as the initial cost function. However, in Equation 6, when learning a Mahalanobis metric, this cost transforms into the *squared* Euclidean metric, which is equivalent to the 2-Wasserstein distance. To avoid this inconsistency and provide a more generalized perspective, it would be beneficial to explicitly state that the WMD can be viewed as a *p*-Wasserstein distance between clouds of word embeddings, regardless of the specific value of *p*.

*   **Novelty & Related Work:** The histogram reweighting approach presented in Equation 5 is not entirely novel. It was previously proposed by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification. Furthermore, the work of Le and Cuturi ("Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations") is also relevant. A more comprehensive discussion of related work, and a clear delineation of the contributions of this paper, is necessary. 

*   **Impact of Learned Weights:** The paper would benefit from experimental analysis investigating the influence of the learned weight vector *w* in the metric learning process. Does this component contribute meaningfully to performance? Is its inclusion essential, or does it provide only marginal improvements? Understanding its relevance is crucial.

*   **Comparison to Existing Optimal Transport Methods:** The paper builds directly upon the work of Cuturi and Avis, but leverages entropy regularization to achieve improved scalability. The shift from a discrete cost (in their work) to a continuous, parameterized cost using linear maps is also a significant difference – yet these crucial distinctions are not explicitly highlighted. Furthermore, mentioning Wang et al. is helpful, but acknowledging the limitations and unpublished status of that work is important. The authors should also emphasize that Wang et al.’s single-iteration OT optimization proved insufficient for practical application.



In conclusion, this paper offers a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the points raised above will further strengthen the work and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance.
