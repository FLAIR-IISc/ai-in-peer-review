Thank you to the authors for their detailed responses, which have clarified several of my initial questions.

This paper proposes a novel supervised metric learning method for textual documents, extending the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD). While the original WMD measures the distance between documents based on their word embeddings, this work adapts the framework for a supervised setting, leveraging class labels to learn a more effective distance metric. The authors have committed to releasing their Matlab code, which is a welcome contribution.

The proposed method learns the distance metric by optimizing two components: a linear transformation `A`, which is a standard approach in metric learning, and a set of word importance weights `w`. This dual approach is interesting; however, the paper could be strengthened by clarifying the individual contributions of these components. Specifically, the intuition behind the formulation in Equation (5), which incorporates the word weights `w`, is not sufficiently explained. A more detailed justification would help the reader understand the underlying mechanism. Furthermore, it is unclear which of the two components—the transformation `A` or the weights `w`—is the primary driver of the reported performance gains. An ablation study that evaluates the performance of the model with only `A` and only `w` would provide valuable insight into the contributions of each component.

The method is evaluated through its performance on a k-Nearest Neighbors classification task, demonstrating its effectiveness.

**Questions and Comments:**

1.  Could you clarify the role of Equation (2) in the final method? The parameter `lambda` introduced there also appears to be distinct from the `lambda` defined on line 170. Please clarify the relationship between these two.

2.  The paper claims the method is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words" (line 316). While this is an appealing property inherited from WMD, could you provide more specific arguments or examples from your learned metric that substantiate this claim in the context of your supervised extension?

3.  Line 257 states: "on the right we also show the average error across datasets." I was unable to locate this information. Could you clarify if this is referring to a specific part of Table 3, or if it is missing from the paper?

4.  In Table 2, what is the distinction between the "n" and "ne" columns? This does not appear to be defined in the text.

5.  The experimental setup uses k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) classification. Could you please specify how the value of `k` was selected? For reproducibility, it would be helpful to know if it was chosen via cross-validation, fixed for all datasets, or set independently for each method.
