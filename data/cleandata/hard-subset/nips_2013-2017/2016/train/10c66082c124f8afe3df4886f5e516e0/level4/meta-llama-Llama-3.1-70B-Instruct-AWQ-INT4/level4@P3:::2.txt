The paper "Supervised Word Mover's Distance" proposes an extension to the word mover's distance, a document metric that leverages word embeddings to compute distances between documents. The key innovation of this work is the use of supervision to learn weights on the distance, which allows the method to adapt to specific tasks and datasets. The authors provide a thorough explanation of their approach, including a clear description of the optimization problem and the algorithm used to solve it.

The paper's main strength lies in its extensive experimental evaluation, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method on a wide range of classification datasets and baseline methods. The authors evaluate their approach on eight real-world text classification tasks and compare it to 26 competitive baselines, including unsupervised and supervised methods. The results show that the proposed method, Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), consistently outperforms almost all baselines, achieving state-of-the-art performance on five out of eight datasets.

The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the results, including visualizations of the learned weights and the effect of supervision on the performance of the method. The paper's experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed, providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The method itself is sound and well-explained, with a clear description of the optimization problem and the algorithm used to solve it. The authors also provide a detailed discussion of the related work, including a comprehensive survey of metric learning methods and their applications.

Overall, this is a solid paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing. The proposed method is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and achieves state-of-the-art performance on a wide range of classification datasets. I would very much like to see this paper presented at NIPS.
