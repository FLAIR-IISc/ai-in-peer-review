The paper proposes a novel method for learning a supervised metric for textual documents, extending the recently introduced Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The original WMD is an unsupervised distance measure based on the optimal transport cost between word embeddings. This work introduces Supervised WMD (S-WMD), which incorporates supervision to improve document distance estimation. The authors learn both a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word-specific importance weights, aiming to minimize k-nearest neighbor classification error. Code is provided in Matlab.

The key idea is to learn a metric that reflects semantic similarity based on document labels. The authors introduce an affine transformation (A) of the word embeddings and a word importance vector (w). I would appreciate a more detailed explanation of the intuition behind Equation (5), particularly how the word importance weights (w) contribute to the overall performance improvement – disentangling the effects of A and w would be insightful.

The experimental evaluation demonstrates strong performance on eight text classification datasets, consistently outperforming a comprehensive set of 26 competitive baselines. This includes comparisons to unsupervised WMD, various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA), and combinations with standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN). The reported results are compelling.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of learning a supervised metric directly within the WMD framework is a significant contribution.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The S-WMD consistently outperforms numerous strong baselines across diverse datasets.
*   **Efficient Implementation:** The authors address the computational complexity of incorporating supervision into WMD through a relaxed optimization technique (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering, resulting in a practical algorithm.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.
*   **Code Availability:** Providing Matlab code promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Intuition for Word Weights:** A more detailed explanation of the role and impact of the word importance weights (w) would be beneficial. While the concept is introduced, a deeper understanding of its contribution to performance is lacking.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The influence of the regularization parameter λ (used in the Sinkhorn distance approximation) on performance isn’t fully explored.
*   **kNN Parameter:** The paper doesn't specify how the value of 'k' for kNN classification was determined. Is it dataset-dependent or method-dependent? Clarifying this would enhance reproducibility. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to supervised metric learning for document distances.
*   The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating a clear improvement over state-of-the-art methods.
*   The authors effectively address the computational challenges associated with incorporating supervision into WMD.
*   The paper is well-written and contributes significantly to the field of document similarity and text classification.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of detailed insight into the contribution of the word importance weights limits the understanding of the proposed method.
*   The absence of a discussion on the impact of the regularization parameter (λ) and the choice of 'k' for kNN classification slightly reduces the completeness of the evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed S-WMD is a novel and effective method for learning supervised document distances, and the empirical results are convincing. While some areas could benefit from further clarification and analysis, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
