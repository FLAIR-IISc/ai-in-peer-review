

This paper extends the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) [19] by introducing a supervised metric learning approach (S-WMD) that jointly optimizes an affine transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights. The method efficiently approximates the gradient of the leave-one-out kNN classification error using the Sinkhorn distance relaxation, enabling practical training. The authors evaluate S-WMD on eight text classification tasks against 26 baselines, demonstrating consistent improvements over unsupervised WMD and other document representations.

Strengths: The paper makes a technically sound contribution by adapting metric learning to the optimal transport framework. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with comprehensive comparisons across diverse datasets and baselines. The proposed Sinkhorn-based approximation significantly improves computational efficiency (Table 3), and the writing is clear and well-structured. The method builds coherently on prior work (WMD [19], NCA [15]) while addressing the lack of supervision in existing document metrics.

Weaknesses: The most significant limitation is the relatively small performance gap between S-WMD and its initialization (S-WCD, described in Section 3.2). As shown in Table 3, S-WMD achieves only marginal gains over S-WCD on most datasets, suggesting that the core technical contributions (learning A and w) provide limited incremental value beyond the strong baseline established by the initial metric. The evaluation lacks critical ablation studies: (1) performance when using only the word weights (w) or only the linear transformation (A), and (2) the full S-WCD baseline (word centroid embedding + ITML/LMNN/NCA) is missing from the comparison. This omission prevents assessing the relative importance of the paper's key innovations. While the optimization method is efficient, the marginal impact on classification error weakens the significance claim.

Originality: The core idea of applying linear metric learning within the WMD framework is novel, but the primary technical contribution lies in the efficient gradient approximation via Sinkhorn scaling (Section 3.1). The paper properly contextualizes this within prior work on metric learning (NCA [15], ITML [10]) and optimal transport (Cuturi [6]).

Significance: The method offers a practical extension to WMD for supervised tasks, but the limited improvement over S-WCD (a straightforward application of existing techniques) reduces its perceived impact. To strengthen significance, the authors should clarify why the joint optimization of A and w is necessary beyond the initialization method, and provide ablation results demonstrating the individual contributions of A and w.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written. Minor suggestions: (1) Clarify the reference to [1,7,8] in Line 152; (2) Equation 11 should include stars on α* and β* for consistency with Section 3.1; (3) Explicitly state that the Sinkhorn scaling (Line 56) is the method used for approximation.
