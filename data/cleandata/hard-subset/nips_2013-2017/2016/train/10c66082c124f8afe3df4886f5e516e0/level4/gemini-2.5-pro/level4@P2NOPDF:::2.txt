This paper presents a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) for document classification. The core contribution is a method that learns a linear transformation of the word embedding space to create a task-specific distance metric. By training the transformation's parameters discriminatively, the model learns to re-weight embedding dimensions, thereby emphasizing features most relevant to the classification task at hand.

The approach is well-motivated and technically sound. It builds logically upon the established WMD framework, representing a valuable step towards adapting general-purpose similarity measures for specific domains. The methodology is presented with exceptional clarity, striking an excellent balance between theoretical detail and intuitive explanation. The authors provide just the right amount of information, making the paper easy to follow and understand.

A key strength of this work is its comprehensive and rigorous experimental evaluation. The authors test their method on multiple classification benchmarks against an impressively wide array of strong baselines and feature representations. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating that the proposed method consistently outperforms all competing approaches by a large margin. This thorough validation provides robust evidence for the model's effectiveness.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that is well-written, methodologically sound, and supported by convincing results. The work makes a clear and significant contribution to the fields of text classification and metric learning. I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance and believe it will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
