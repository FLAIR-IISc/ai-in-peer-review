The authors introduce a new approach to learn the parameters of WMD, essentially EMD applied to text documents where word embeddings define bin-to-bin dissimilarities. They parameterize the EMD ground cost matrix with a Mahalanobis distance over vector representations. In addition, they propose learning a reweighting of histogram coefficients (see Lebanon’s work; comment below). They derive gradients for NCA-style objectives in this setting and back them with compelling empirical evidence. Overall, the paper reads as a well-executed combination of known techniques and showcases very convincing experiments. I would support acceptance. The strengths lie in the method’s simplicity and a conceptually straightforward idea that is nonetheless nontrivial to implement and evaluate; the experimental section is therefore a strong asset. Areas for improvement include a clearer treatment of the interplay between regularized and unregularized formulations, more rigorous mathematics (some computations/notation are a bit sloppy), and ideally an algorithmic box to make the proposal more explicit.

A few minor comments:
- In Eq. 1, the transport cost is the Euclidean distance between word embeddings; in Eq. 6, for Mahalanobis metric learning, that cost becomes the squared Euclidean metric (i.e., the 2-Wasserstein case). To avoid such inconsistencies, it may be simpler to state that WMD is the p-Wasserstein distance between clouds of word embeddings, for general p.
- The “reweighting” in Eq. 5 is not new; it was already proposed by Guy Lebanon specifically for text document classification (“metric learning for text documents”). See also “Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations” by Le/Cuturi.
- Any experimental insight into the importance of the weight vector w in the learned metric? Does this innovation make a difference? How relevant is it?
- Related work: it would help to more clearly delineate differences from prior art. This paper builds on Cuturi/Avis but uses entropy regularization, which accelerates and scales applications. The cost is also different: discrete in that work, here continuous and parameterized by linear maps. These two points are currently missing. The paper also draws from Wang et al., but that work is not publishable in its current form; they run OT only once, and the method does not function as proposed.
