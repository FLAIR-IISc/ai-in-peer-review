This paper proposes a method for learning a supervised version of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which the authors call Supervised WMD (S-WMD). WMD computes the distance between two text documents as an Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) problem, using word embeddings to define the ground distance between individual words. The core idea of this work is to make this distance task-specific by learning its parameters from labeled data. Specifically, the authors propose learning two components: (1) a linear transformation of the word embedding space, which is equivalent to learning a Mahalanobis distance for the ground metric, and (2) a vector of word-importance weights that re-scales the document's bag-of-words representation.

To learn these parameters, the authors minimize a loss function based on Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), which approximates the leave-one-out k-NN classification error. A key challenge is that computing the gradient of the WMD is computationally prohibitive. The authors overcome this by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized counterpart (the Sinkhorn distance), which allows for a fast and differentiable approximation of the optimal transport plan and its gradient. The method is evaluated on eight text classification tasks, where it demonstrates strong performance against a comprehensive set of baselines.

**Strengths**

*   The paper's central idea is elegant and well-motivated. It provides a principled way to incorporate supervision into the powerful WMD framework, which has been shown to be a very effective unsupervised document distance.
*   The primary strength of the paper is its thorough and convincing experimental evaluation. The proposed S-WMD is benchmarked against 26 competitive methods on eight diverse datasets, and it consistently achieves state-of-the-art or near-state-of-the-art results. This provides strong evidence for the practical utility of the proposed method.
*   The approach of using an entropy-regularized approximation to make the gradient computation tractable is a crucial and clever contribution. This makes an otherwise computationally prohibitive optimization problem practical to solve.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a well-executed idea. The following points are suggestions for further improvement, primarily concerning clarity, positioning with respect to prior art, and experimental analysis.

1.  **Clarity and Mathematical Formulation:**
    *   The interplay between the exact WMD (an LP) and its entropy-regularized approximation could be clarified. The gradient is derived for the relaxed problem, and it would be beneficial for the authors to explicitly discuss how this relates to optimizing the original, non-regularized distance.
    *   There is a minor inconsistency in the definition of the ground distance. Equation 1 uses the standard Euclidean distance between word embeddings, whereas the metric learning formulation (e.g., Equation 6) uses the squared Euclidean distance. This corresponds to a shift from a 1-Wasserstein or 2-Wasserstein distance to a 2-Wasserstein distance *squared*. It would be simpler and more consistent to frame the WMD as a p-Wasserstein distance from the beginning and stick to a single formulation.
    *   While Algorithm 1 provides a helpful high-level overview, its clarity could be improved by adding more detail, for instance, by referencing the specific equations used for the gradient updates inside the loop.

2.  **Originality and Discussion of Prior Work:**
    *   The idea of learning histogram re-weighting coefficients (the vector `w` in Equation 4) is not entirely new. This technique was previously proposed by Guy Lebanon in "Metric Learning for Text Documents" for a similar purpose. A related concept also appears in "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations" by Le and Cuturi. Acknowledging and citing this prior work would better position the paper's contribution.
    *   The related work section could be strengthened by more clearly delineating the differences from previous work on learning EMD ground metrics. For instance, this paper builds on the direction explored by Cuturi & Avis (2011) but introduces entropy regularization, which enables significant scaling and speed improvements. Furthermore, this work parameterizes a continuous ground metric via a linear map, whereas that work learned a discrete cost matrix. The paper also relates to Wang et al. (2012), but key differences in the optimization approach should be highlighted.

3.  **Experimental Analysis:**
    *   The paper introduces two learned components: the matrix `A` and the word-importance vector `w`. It would significantly strengthen the paper to include an ablation study that evaluates the contribution of each component individually. For example, how much does learning `w` contribute to the final performance compared to learning `A` alone? This would provide valuable insight into the source of the impressive performance gains.

**Conclusion**

This is a very good paper that presents an effective, well-motivated method for supervised document metric learning. The combination of existing techniques is sound, the technical solution for efficient optimization is valuable, and the experimental validation is extensive and compelling. While there are minor opportunities to improve the clarity of the presentation and the discussion of related work, the overall contribution is significant. I support its acceptance.
