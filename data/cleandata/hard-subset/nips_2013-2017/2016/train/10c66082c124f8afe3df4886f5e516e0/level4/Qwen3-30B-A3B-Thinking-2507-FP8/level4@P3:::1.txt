

This paper proposes a supervised extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) [19], a recently introduced document metric that leverages word embeddings to compute distances between documents as optimal transport problems. The authors introduce Supervised-WMD (S-WMD), which learns a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word-specific importance weights to improve document classification performance. The approach efficiently optimizes these parameters by approximating the WMD using the Sinkhorn distance [6] and applying stochastic neighborhood learning inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [15]. The method is evaluated on eight document classification tasks, where it consistently outperforms 26 baselines, including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with multiple metric learning algorithms.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution that builds directly on prior work. The WMD [19] itself represents a significant advancement over traditional document metrics by leveraging high-quality word embeddings to capture semantic relationships. The authors correctly identify that WMD is unsupervised and thus lacks the ability to incorporate task-specific supervision. Their extension to supervised learning is well-motivated, as document similarity should be task-dependent (e.g., topic classification vs. author identification). The paper demonstrates how to efficiently incorporate supervision within the WMD framework, addressing a clear limitation of the original approach.

The strengths of this paper are substantial. First, the technical contribution is well-executed: the authors provide a rigorous approximation to the otherwise computationally prohibitive gradient computation, enabling efficient optimization. The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering eight diverse document classification tasks with detailed comparison against a comprehensive set of baselines (26 in total). The results consistently show S-WMD outperforming all baselines in terms of kNN classification error, with the method achieving the lowest error on five of eight datasets and the best average performance across all datasets. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the mathematical formulations and a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses that limit its impact. The most significant issue is that the proposed S-WMD does not substantially outperform a simple baseline: the Supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD), which is initialized with NCA on the WCD metric (a lower bound of WMD). As shown in Table 2 and Table 3, S-WMD only marginally improves over S-WCD, with the average rank difference being small (S-WMD: 1.0, S-WCD: 1.1). This suggests that the main technical innovation (learning the linear transformation and word weights within the WMD framework) provides limited additional value over a straightforward application of metric learning to the simpler WCD representation. 

The evaluation would be significantly strengthened by including a comparison of the word centroid embedding (with weights) combined with standard metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA), as this would better isolate the contribution of the proposed S-WMD framework. Additionally, the paper lacks an ablation study that evaluates the relative importance of the two key components: the linear transformation matrix A and the word importance weights w. Understanding which component contributes more to performance would clarify the paper's technical contribution.

The originality of the work is somewhat limited. While the efficient optimization approach is technically interesting, the core idea of learning a linear metric within the WMD framework is not highly novel—it builds directly on NCA and metric learning literature. The paper's main technical contribution appears to be the efficient approximation method (Section 3.1), rather than a fundamentally new approach to metric learning for text. This is further evidenced by the minimal performance gain over the S-WCD baseline, which uses established metric learning techniques on the WCD representation.

In terms of significance, the paper demonstrates that supervised learning can improve document metrics, but the marginal gains over simpler approaches may limit its practical impact. The method is efficient (Table 3 shows training times comparable to baseline metric learning), but the small performance improvements relative to the simpler S-WCD approach question whether the additional complexity is justified for most applications.

To summarize the pros and cons for acceptance:

Pros:
- Well-executed technical contribution with efficient optimization
- Thorough experimental evaluation across diverse document classification tasks
- Clear, well-written paper that advances the state of the art in document metrics
- Provides a useful extension to the influential WMD framework

Cons:
- Marginal performance gains over a simpler baseline (S-WCD)
- Limited evaluation of component importance (A vs. w)
- Insufficient comparison of the word centroid embedding with standard metric learning
- Originality is somewhat constrained by reliance on established metric learning techniques

Overall, this paper makes a technically sound contribution that advances document metrics, but the marginal improvements over simpler baselines and limited novelty of the core approach weaken its significance for NIPS. The paper would be significantly stronger with additional ablation studies and comparisons to the word centroid embedding with standard metric learning. I recommend a borderline decision: accept if the authors can address the evaluation gaps in the camera-ready version, but with reservations about the paper's contribution relative to simpler alternatives. The paper is well-written and well-executed, but the scientific contribution may not be sufficiently novel or impactful for top-tier NIPS acceptance.
