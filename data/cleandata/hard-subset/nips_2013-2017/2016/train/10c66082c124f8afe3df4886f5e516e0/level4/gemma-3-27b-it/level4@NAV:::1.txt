This paper introduces a supervised metric learning method leveraging the word-movers distance [19] – a transport distance utilizing word2vec embeddings – and generalizes it to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances following linear projection. The approach learns both this projection and word-specific weights, re-weighting word frequencies and renormalizing them for improved document representation. An efficient optimization strategy is presented for learning this parameterized metric. Comprehensive experiments demonstrate the method’s efficacy, comparing it against numerous baselines on document classification datasets using a kNN classifier built on the learned metric. 

This work represents a valuable technical extension of the word-movers distance, demonstrably effective in kNN-based text classification. However, the practical impact may be constrained; the performance is comparable to earth-movers distance initialized with a “word centroid distance”, as evidenced by the results in Table 3. This suggests the core technical contributions offer a limited advantage over this existing initialization technique. 

The evaluation would be strengthened by including a comparison of the “word centroid embedding” (the weighted mean of word embeddings) with existing metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA). Additionally, an analysis of the individual contributions of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A would be insightful, showing performance gains from each component in isolation. The originality primarily resides in the optimization strategy (Lines 156–175); while integrating linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance is interesting, its contribution is relatively modest.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. Several minor points could be addressed for further clarity: 
- Line 152: Clarify which authors are being referenced (“The authors” – referring to which of [1,7,8]?).
- Equation 11: Consider adding asterisks to α and β for consistency.
- Around line 170, explicitly state the connection to the Sinkhorn scaling introduced earlier in line 56.
