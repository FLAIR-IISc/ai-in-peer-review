

The paper extends Kusner et al.'s Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a NIPS 2015 contribution that established a state-of-the-art document metric via optimal transport over word embeddings, to a supervised setting (S-WMD). It proposes learning an affine transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to minimize leave-one-out kNN classification error, using Cuturi's Sinkhorn distance approximation to avoid the cubic complexity of exact WMD computation. The authors evaluate S-WMD on eight text classification tasks against 26 baselines, demonstrating consistent outperformance.  

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with rigorous derivation of the Sinkhorn-based gradient approximation and thorough empirical validation across diverse datasets. The paper carefully addresses limitations (e.g., sensitivity to λ) and provides reproducible implementation details. However, the theoretical connection to the pure Wasserstein distance requires deeper justification: while the paper uses λ=10, it does not sufficiently explain whether this yields a Wasserstein-like metric (as λ→∞) or an entropically regularized approximation (common in practice with small λ), which critically impacts the interpretation of results.  

**Clarity**: The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured mathematical exposition, precise definitions (e.g., Eq. 5, 10), and effective visualizations (Fig. 1, 2). The motivation for learning word weights (Eq. 4) and the efficient gradient computation (Section 3.1) are particularly well-explained. The discussion of WCD comparisons (Section 4) could be strengthened by clarifying why WCD—being a lower-bound approximation of WMD, not a metric learning method—is included as a baseline.  

**Originality**: The core contribution (supervised metric learning via WMD) is incremental over Kusner et al.'s WMD, as the Sinkhorn approximation is directly adapted from Cuturi's NIPS 2013 work. While the application to document classification is novel, the methodological novelty is limited; the paper would benefit from more explicit differentiation from related work like Wang et al. (NIPS 2015) on EMD metric learning, which also uses ITML but lacks word reweighting.  

**Significance**: The results are significant: S-WMD achieves the best average performance across eight datasets against 26 baselines, with notable gains on smaller-document tasks (e.g., +42% over NCA-TFIDF on CLASSIC). The efficiency (Table 3) and practicality of the approach for real-world text classification make it a valuable addition to the field.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:  
*Pro*: Strong empirical validation, clear technical writing, efficient implementation, and meaningful improvements over unsupervised baselines.  
*Con*: Limited methodological novelty (relying on established Sinkhorn approximation), insufficient discussion of λ's theoretical and practical implications, and ambiguous WCD baseline justification.  

Overall, this is a high-quality, well-executed paper that advances document metric learning with practical impact, though the novelty is somewhat constrained by its reliance on prior optimal transport techniques. The concerns regarding λ and WCD are minor and could be addressed with minor revisions.
