This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach built on top of the recent "word-movers distance" (a transport distance applied to word2vec word embeddings). The proposed approach can be applied to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances after linear projection. In addition to learning the linear projection to maximize performance, the proposed approach also learns a weighting of words. The latter is specific to the document application and involves re-weighting the frequency of each word by a learned weight, followed by re-normalizing the word frequencies.

The paper presents an efficient optimization method to learn the proposed parameterized metric. A thorough experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach by comparing it to a large number of reasonable baselines on several document classification datasets using a kNN classifier based on the proposed learned metric.

One of the strengths of this work is its extension of the recent word-movers distance, which is an interesting and useful extension. The experimental results show that this extension is effective on several kNN text classification tasks. However, the potential impact of this work may be limited by the fact that earth-movers distance with metric learning via a "word centroid distance" initialization performs similarly to the full learning of the proposed distance. This is evident from the difference in the last two rows of Table 3. In other words, the main technical contributions of this paper do not seem to contribute significantly over this initialization method based on existing techniques.

There are a few aspects of the evaluation that could be improved. Firstly, it would be useful to include an evaluation of the "word centroid embedding" (weighted mean of word embeddings for the document) with all tested existing metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA). Secondly, an assessment of the relative importance of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A would provide valuable insights. It would be interesting to see the performance when each of these components is active in isolation.

The originality of the paper is mostly related to the optimization of the metric (lines 156-175). The idea of linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance is very interesting, but it is a limited contribution in itself.

The paper is well-written and a pleasure to read. However, there are a few minor issues that could be clarified. On line 152, it would be helpful to specify which paper the authors refer to ([1,7,8]?). In Equation 11, it seems that alpha and beta should carry stars. Additionally, it would be useful to explicitly recall around line 170 that this is the Sinkhorn scaling referred to in the introduction (line 56).

Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of metric learning, with a well-written and clear presentation. While the potential impact may be limited by the performance of existing techniques, the experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
