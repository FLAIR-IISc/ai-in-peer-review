This paper extends Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by learning a supervised metric tailored to a target task. The proposed Supervised WMD (S-WMD) jointly learns (i) a linear transformation of the word-embedding space and (ii) a global vector of word-importance weights, so that optimal transport distances between documents better reflect label-based similarity. Training minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective (à la NCA), but replaces exact WMD with an entropically regularized transport (Sinkhorn) to obtain efficient, differentiable approximations and practical gradients. The method uses a strong initialization via supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD, NCA on document centroids), mini-batch training, and neighbor filtering for scalability. Across eight datasets and 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised), S-WMD achieves the best or near-best kNN performance, with code provided.

Relation to prior work: WMD (Kusner et al.) demonstrated the value of optimal transport over pre-trained embeddings for document distance. This paper builds directly on that idea, integrating supervision through metric learning. The optimization and probabilistic neighborhood formulation are inspired by NCA, while the computational strategy relies on entropic regularization and Sinkhorn scaling (Cuturi). Compared to classic metric learning (e.g., LMNN, ITML), the contribution is to learn in embedding space and within the transport ground cost, with additional histogram reweighting.

Quality: The approach is technically sound and well motivated. The use of the Sinkhorn relaxation is appropriate and yields a practical O(p^2) per-pair computation (p = unique words per document). The gradient derivations (for both A and w) follow from primal/dual optimal transport properties, with clear acknowledgement of approximation (via λ) and uniqueness considerations. The training scheme—initialization with S-WCD, batching, and neighbor pruning—is sensible and effective in practice. Experiments are thorough: eight datasets spanning multiple tasks, broad baselines (including strong supervised baselines like NCA/LMNN/ITML after PCA), and runtime discussion. Code availability further supports reproducibility.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The problem setup, loss function, and gradient approximations are explained at the right level of detail. The empirical section is comprehensive, and the visualizations (t-SNE embeddings; learned word weights) help interpret the gains.

Originality: While the components (OT with Sinkhorn, NCA-style objective, linear metric learning) are known, their integration to learn a supervised ground metric and word-importance weights for WMD is a meaningful and novel combination. The supervised reweighting of histograms alongside learning the embedding transform is a useful extension beyond prior supervised EMD/OT work.

Significance: Results are strong and consistent, with S-WMD outperforming a wide range of baselines on most datasets and ranking best on average. Given the popularity of WMD and the practical need to inject supervision, S-WMD is likely to be useful to both researchers and practitioners. The approach remains data-efficient by leveraging pre-trained embeddings and operates directly in a low-dimensional embedding space.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Dependence on kNN/NCA: The objective optimizes an NCA-style leave-one-out proxy for kNN. It would help to report performance with other classifiers or show that improvements transfer beyond kNN.
- Ablations: Provide ablations isolating the impact of the linear transform A vs. the word weights w, and the effect of the S-WCD initialization. Sensitivity to λ (Sinkhorn), batch size, and neighbor set size N would strengthen the empirical narrative.
- Ground metric choice: The squared Euclidean ground cost may be suboptimal on some datasets (as noted). Exploring p = 1, cosine, or learned non-linear transforms (e.g., via a low-capacity network) could improve robustness.
- Computational limits: Although O(p^2) is much better than exact WMD, very long documents can still be expensive. Include memory/runtime scaling curves vs. document length and vocabulary pruning strategies.
- Baseline fairness: The use of transductive LDA should be clearly marked, and significance testing across methods would be useful. Consider including recent neural baselines (where feasible) for context.
- Model details: Clarify constraints and regularization on w (non-negativity, normalization), how OOV/rare words and stopwords are handled, and whether any regularization is applied to A to prevent overfitting. Reporting stability over random seeds would also help.

Pros:
- Clear, principled extension of WMD with supervision via metric learning.
- Efficient and practical training using Sinkhorn-based approximations.
- Strong, consistent empirical gains across diverse datasets and many baselines.
- Good clarity, visualization, and released code.

Cons:
- Limited analysis of component contributions and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Ground metric and linearity constraints may limit performance on some datasets.
- Evaluation centered on kNN; broader applicability could be demonstrated.

Overall assessment: This is a solid, well-executed paper that advances supervised document distance learning by marrying optimal transport with metric learning in embedding space. The methodological choices are justified, and the empirical results are compelling. I recommend acceptance.
