The paper proposes a novel method for metric learning on textual documents, building upon the existing "Word Mover's Distance" framework, which is originally unsupervised and measures the distance between word embeddings. This work extends the framework to a supervised setting, where the supervision is provided in the form of document class labels. The authors have promised to make the MATLAB code available.

I appreciate the authors' detailed responses to my queries, which have clarified several aspects of the paper. The proposed method involves learning two components to determine the distance: a linear transformation A (adopting the traditional approach) and word importance weights w. However, I would have liked a clearer explanation of the intuition behind Equation (5), which defines the usage of w, and a better understanding of which component is primarily responsible for the reported improvements.

In the experimental section, the method is evaluated on a classification task. While I understand that assessing a distance metric can be challenging, it would be interesting to see a comparison with the performance of a simple classifier on the same datasets, if only for baseline purposes.

I have several questions and comments. Firstly, I did not understand when Equation (2) is used, as the lambda in this equation appears to be different from the lambda in line 170. Secondly, I would appreciate clarification on the argument behind the claim that the proposed method is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words" (line 316).

Additionally, I have a few minor comments. In line 257, the text mentions "on the right we also show the average error across datasets," but I could not find this information, and it is unclear whether it is supposed to be presented in Table 3. In Table 2, the difference between "n" and "ne" is not explicitly stated. Furthermore, the definition of k for kNN classification is not provided, and it is unclear whether this value is dataset-dependent or method-dependent.
