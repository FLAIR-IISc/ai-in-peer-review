This paper proposes a novel method for metric learning in textual documents, extending the existing unsupervised "Word Mover's Distance" approach to a supervised setting. The original Word Mover's Distance measures the distance between word embeddings without supervision. In contrast, this work incorporates supervision in the form of document class labels. The authors have also promised to make the code (in Matlab) available, which is a welcome addition.

The paper introduces two key components for learning the distance metric: a linear transformation A, representing the traditional approach, and word importance weights w. While the idea is intriguing, I would appreciate a clearer explanation of the intuition behind Equation (5), which defines the usage of w. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to understand the relative contributions of both components to the reported improvements.

The method is evaluated in a classification task, which is a reasonable choice given the challenge of assessing a distance metric. However, for the sake of comparison, it would be interesting to see the performance of a simple classifier on the same datasets. This would provide a baseline for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

I had a few questions and comments while reading the paper. Firstly, I was unclear about the usage of Equation (2) and the role of lambda, which seems to differ from the lambda mentioned in line 170. The authors' argument that the proposed method is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words" (line 316) could be further clarified. 

Additionally, I noticed a few minor issues. In line 257, the text mentions "on the right we also show the average error across datasets," but I couldn't find this information in the paper. Is this supposed to be included in Table 3? In Table 2, the difference between "n" and "ne" is not explicitly stated. Moreover, the definition of k for kNN classification is not provided; is this dependent on the dataset or the method?

Overall, the paper presents an interesting extension to the Word Mover's Distance approach. With some clarifications and minor revisions, it has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field of metric learning for textual documents.
