

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel approach to metric learning for document classification that extends the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) framework. WMD, introduced by Kusner et al. (2015), computes document distances by treating documents as distributions over word embeddings and solving an optimal transport problem. The key contribution of this paper is to extend WMD to a supervised setting where the goal is to learn a metric that makes documents from the same class closer and documents from different classes farther apart. The authors propose learning two components: a linear transformation matrix A that modifies the word embeddings, and a word importance weight vector w that emphasizes certain words for classification.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature. It builds upon the growing body of work on metric learning (e.g., NCA, LMNN, ITML) while also leveraging recent advances in word embeddings and optimal transport (e.g., Cuturi's Sinkhorn distance). The authors clearly situate their work in relation to the original WMD paper and other document representation methods.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a technically sound contribution that is well-motivated. The supervised extension of WMD is a natural and valuable direction for the field.
2. The method is carefully developed with attention to computational efficiency. The authors provide a clever approximation to the gradient computation that avoids the cubic complexity of the original WMD.
3. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with results on eight datasets and comparison to 26 baselines. The results consistently show S-WMD outperforming the baselines, with the method achieving the lowest error on five out of eight datasets.
4. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good technical details in the methodology section.

Weaknesses:
1. While the authors introduce a word importance weight vector w, it's not entirely clear how much of the improvement comes from the linear transformation A versus the word weights w. The paper would benefit from an ablation study showing the relative contributions of these two components.
2. The paper claims that S-WMD is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words" (line 316), but the justification for this claim is not sufficiently supported. A concrete example or more detailed explanation would strengthen this point.
3. The paper mentions using "kNN classification" but does not specify the value of k used in experiments. This is an important hyperparameter that could affect the results, and its value should be clearly stated.
4. The paper references "average error across datasets" on line 257, but this metric is not clearly presented in Table 2 (which instead shows average rank). It would be helpful to clarify this point.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of document representation and metric learning. The method is novel, well-justified, and demonstrates significant improvements over existing approaches. While there are some minor weaknesses in the presentation and explanation, these do not undermine the technical contribution.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel approach to supervised metric learning for document distances
- Strong experimental results across multiple datasets
- Careful consideration of computational efficiency
- Clear theoretical foundation built on existing work

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of ablation study to isolate the contributions of A and w
- Insufficient justification for the "forgiving" claim about syntactically different but conceptually similar words
- Missing specification of k for kNN classification

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a significant problem in text classification with a method that advances the state of the art. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and has clear practical value. The authors have provided a clear and detailed explanation of their method, and the results demonstrate a meaningful improvement over existing approaches. While the paper would benefit from addressing the minor weaknesses noted above, these do not detract from the overall contribution of the work.
