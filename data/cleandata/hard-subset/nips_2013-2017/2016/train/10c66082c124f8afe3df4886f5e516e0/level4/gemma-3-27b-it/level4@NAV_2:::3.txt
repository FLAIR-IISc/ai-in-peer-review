The authors propose a novel method, Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), to learn a supervised metric for the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights to minimize the leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. Recognizing the computational challenges of directly optimizing the WMD, the authors leverage a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem – the Sinkhorn distance – to enable efficient gradient computation. The paper presents a theoretically sound approach with compelling experimental results on eight real-world text classification tasks, consistently outperforming a comprehensive set of 26 baselines.

The strengths of this work lie in its elegant combination of existing techniques to address a practical problem. The approach is conceptually straightforward yet non-trivial to implement and optimize, as demonstrated by the strong experimental validation. The paper thoroughly evaluates S-WMD against a diverse set of competitive methods, providing convincing evidence of its effectiveness. The inclusion of algorithmic details, while not a full “box,” is helpful, and the discussion of computational complexity is appreciated.

Several areas could benefit from further clarification and refinement. While the paper mentions both regularized and non-regularized formulations, a more explicit discussion of the trade-offs between these approaches would be valuable. A more rigorous mathematical treatment, with precise definitions and notations, would enhance the clarity and reproducibility of the work. Specifically, the transition between the Euclidean distance in Eq. 1 and the squared Euclidean distance used for the Mahalanobis metric in Eq. 6 could be made clearer by consistently framing WMD as a p-Wasserstein distance. 

Minor points for consideration: The reweighting scheme proposed in Eq. 5 is not entirely novel; similar approaches have been explored previously in the context of text document classification (e.g., Lebanon’s work) and histogram metric learning (e.g., Le/Cuturi). Providing a more detailed discussion of these related works would strengthen the paper's positioning within the existing literature.  Investigating and reporting experimental insights into the learned word importance weights (vector *w*) would be beneficial. Does this component significantly contribute to the performance gains? What patterns emerge in the learned weights?  The related work section could more explicitly highlight the differences between this approach and prior work leveraging the Cuturi & Avis formulation, particularly regarding the continuous parameterization via linear maps versus the discrete optimization in their work, and the role of entropy regularization for scalability. Mentioning the limitations of Wang et al.’s related work would also be useful, as their approach suffered from practical implementation issues.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results consistently outperform a large number of competitive baselines.
*   Novel and effective combination of existing techniques to address a practical problem.
*   Sound theoretical foundation and efficient gradient approximation.
*   Clear presentation and thorough evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Some mathematical notations could be more precise and consistent.
*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work, particularly regarding reweighting schemes and the Cuturi & Avis formulation.
*   Further analysis of the learned word importance weights would provide valuable insights.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant potential impact. Despite the minor areas for improvement, the strong experimental results and the innovative combination of techniques justify acceptance to a top-tier conference like NIPS.
