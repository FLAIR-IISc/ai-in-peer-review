- Proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD) that learns an affine embedding transformation A and word-importance weights w for task-specific document distance.
- Objective optimizes stochastic kNN leave-one-out classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA).
- Uses entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance to approximate WMD for efficient, differentiable transport and gradient computation.
- Introduces supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) as an initialization and uses batch gradient descent with nearest-neighbor filtering for efficiency.
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance, outperforming 26 baselines on eight document classification datasets.
- Well-motivated extension from unsupervised WMD to a supervised metric learning setting.
- Technically sound and novel optimization approach making nested WMD gradients practical via Sinkhorn approximation.
- Thorough and convincing experimental evaluation across many baselines and datasets.
- Paper is clearly written and well organized.
- Main weakness: full S-WMD yields only marginal gains over the much simpler S-WCD initialization on several datasets.
- Missing baselines: no comparisons of word centroid representations with metric learning methods like LMNN or ITML.
- Lacks an ablation study disentangling contributions of A versus w (learning A only or w only).
- Minor clarity issues: clarify reference for uniqueness of transport plan (line 152).
- Minor clarity issue: explicitly connect alternating matrix-scaling remark to the Sinkhorn algorithm (around line 170).
- Notational clarity: specify whether dual variables α*/β* should be the relaxed α*λ/β*λ from u and v in Eq. 10.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically solid method, elegant Sinkhorn-based solution, strong empirical results, and clear writing.
- Arguments against acceptance: marginal improvement over simpler initialization and incomplete evaluation (missing baselines and ablation).
- Recommendation: accept, with revisions to add baselines and ablation analyses.
