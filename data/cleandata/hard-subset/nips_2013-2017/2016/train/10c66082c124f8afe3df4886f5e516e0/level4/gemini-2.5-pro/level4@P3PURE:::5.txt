This paper addresses the problem of text classification. The authors extend the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which is based on the Wasserstein distance, by introducing a supervised framework for learning word-specific weights. To improve computational speed, the paper proposes using the Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for the exact Wasserstein distance. The proposed method is evaluated on eight datasets against 26 baselines, where it achieves the best average performance.

The paper is exceptionally well-written. The authors provide a clear presentation of the topic and a thorough, yet highly accessible, analysis of the underlying mathematics.

However, there are a few weaknesses that could be addressed:

1.  The primary contribution is an extension of the original Word Mover's Distance paper, which may limit its novelty.

2.  The comparisons to the Word Centroid Distance seem confusing and potentially irrelevant. A clarification on the motivation for including this baseline would be helpful.

3.  The role and impact of the regularization parameter, lambda, is not sufficiently discussed. Based on prior experience with Sinkhorn approximations, the value of lambda can have a significant impact, at times becoming the dominant term in the objective function (Eq. 2). Theoretically, a large lambda should ensure that the Sinkhorn distance closely approximates the true Wasserstein distance, albeit at the cost of slower convergence. Conversely, practitioners often use a small lambda to accelerate convergence, which results in a distance metric whose behavior is distinctly different from the Wasserstein distance due to the strong entropic regularization. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion on this trade-off. Specifically, could the authors clarify whether the value of lambda used in their experiments leads the model to approximate a "pure" Wasserstein behavior or a more "entropic" Sinkhorn-like behavior?
