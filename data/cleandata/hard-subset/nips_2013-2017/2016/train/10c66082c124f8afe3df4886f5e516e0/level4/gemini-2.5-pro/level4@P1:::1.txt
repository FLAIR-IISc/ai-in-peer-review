This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a metric learning approach that extends the recently introduced Word Mover's Distance (WMD). WMD computes the distance between two documents as an optimal transport problem between their respective word embeddings. The key idea of S-WMD is to learn a task-specific distance by incorporating supervision. This is achieved in two ways: by learning a linear transformation of the underlying word embedding space, and by learning an importance weight for each word in the vocabulary, which re-weights the document's word frequencies. To overcome the computational challenge of differentiating through the WMD optimization, the authors propose an efficient gradient-based optimization method based on an entropy-regularized version of the transport problem (the Sinkhorn distance). The effectiveness of S-WMD is demonstrated through a thorough experimental evaluation on eight document classification datasets, where it is compared against 26 baselines using a k-NN classifier.

**Strengths**

1.  This work is a technically interesting and useful extension of the Word Mover's Distance. By incorporating supervision, the authors have developed a more powerful document distance metric that is well-suited for k-NN text classification, as convincingly demonstrated by the strong empirical results.
2.  The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and a pleasure to read. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the proposed method against a wide range of strong and relevant baselines.
3.  The proposed optimization scheme, which uses a clever approximation based on the Sinkhorn distance to make the problem tractable, is a significant contribution. It enables the learning of a complex, supervised metric that would otherwise be computationally prohibitive.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The primary concern is that the practical impact of the full S-WMD optimization appears limited when compared to its initialization. The results in Table 2 show that the performance of the full S-WMD method is often only marginally better than its initialization scheme ("S-WMD init."), which is based on applying Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) to the simpler Word Centroid Distance. This suggests that the main technical contribution—the efficient gradient computation for the full WMD—provides limited additional benefit over a simpler approach that uses existing techniques.
2.  The evaluation would benefit from an ablation study to assess the relative importance of the two learned components: the projection matrix A and the word-specific importance weights w. It is currently unclear how much each component contributes to the final performance.
3.  The experimental comparison could be strengthened by including additional baselines. Specifically, it would be valuable to see how the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) document representation performs when combined with other standard metric learning algorithms like ITML and LMNN, in addition to the reported NCA baseline (S-WCD).

**Originality**

The main novelty lies in the efficient optimization method for learning the supervised metric. While the core idea of learning a linear metric within an Earth Mover's Distance framework has been explored before in other domains, the application to WMD and the specific formulation using Sinkhorn-based gradients for both the linear map and the word weights is novel.
