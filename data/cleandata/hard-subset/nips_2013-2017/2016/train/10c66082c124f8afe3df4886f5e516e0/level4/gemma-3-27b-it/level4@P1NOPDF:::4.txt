This paper presents a supervised approach to learning document distance, building upon word mover’s distance (WMD). WMD, originally motivated by the earth mover’s distance, calculates an optimal transport cost between word distributions to quantify document dissimilarity. The initial formulation typically employs word2vec embeddings to represent words. In this work, the authors propose learning (1) a linear transformation of these word embeddings and (2) a reweighted word distribution, with the goal of increasing separation between documents of different classes.

The paper is clearly written and easy to follow, and includes a thorough review of related work demonstrating a strong understanding of the existing literature. The authors provide experimental results on several datasets, including novel ones. However, the motivation behind the formulation could be strengthened, and the overall significance of the contribution feels limited.

Specifically, I have several concerns:

1) The use of reweighting the word distribution with weights *w* is not sufficiently motivated. It’s unclear why this reweighting is beneficial, and it raises the concern that it could fundamentally alter the document’s representation. If the authors could demonstrate *why* this reweighting improves performance *without* drastically changing the document's meaning, the approach would be more justifiable.

2) The contribution of this work appears incremental. Applying a linear transformation *A* and reweighting *w* within a Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) objective function represents a plausible extension to the supervised setting of WMD. However, the formulation doesn’t present a groundbreaking or particularly novel advance.

3) The process for calculating a distance to a new, unseen document is unclear. For a document not encountered during training, how is the reweighting *w* determined?

4) The use of the phrase “semantic difference” in the abstract is ambiguous. It appears to refer to document labels, but concepts like authorship or topic don’t intuitively qualify as semantic differences. Clarification of what the authors intend by “semantic difference” is needed.

Overall, this is a well-written paper and I do not oppose its acceptance, contingent on the authors providing satisfactory explanations to the questions raised above.
