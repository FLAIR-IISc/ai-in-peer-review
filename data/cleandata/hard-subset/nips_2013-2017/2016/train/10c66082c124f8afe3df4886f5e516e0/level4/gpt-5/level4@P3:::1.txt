Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that incorporates label information to improve kNN-based document classification. WMD measures document distances via optimal transport between word embeddings (e.g., word2vec), achieving strong performance in unsupervised settings. S-WMD learns (i) a linear transformation A on the word embeddings (a supervised ground metric) and (ii) a word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The learning objective minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss in the spirit of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). To make optimization practical, the paper replaces the exact optimal transport with the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn approximation, enabling O(q^2) computations per document pair and yielding tractable gradients for both A and w. The method is initialized by training NCA on word centroid representations (S-WCD) and then refined with batch training and neighbor pruning.

The work builds on WMD (Kusner et al., ICML 2015) and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013), and relates to metric learning methods (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and prior attempts to learn ground metrics in EMD/Wasserstein settings (e.g., Wang & Guibas; Cuturi & Avis). The combination of NCA-style supervision with Sinkhorn-based transport and joint learning of both A and w is a novel and practical contribution.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of supervised metric learning directly in the word embedding space, with a principled NCA-style loss and efficient Sinkhorn-based optimization.
- Joint learning of a ground metric (A) and word-importance weights (w) that are task-specific, offering flexibility beyond prior EMD metric learning.
- Strong empirical evaluation: 8 datasets, 26 baselines (including WMD, classic document representations, and supervised metric learners after PCA). S-WMD achieves the best average rank and is best on 5/8 datasets.
- Sensible engineering choices: S-WCD initialization, batch training with neighbor pruning, and practical runtime that is comparable to training linear metrics on baselines.
- Clear writing and useful visualizations (t-SNE plots; top-weighted words), with public code.

Weaknesses and questions
- Incremental gains over S-WCD initialization: The final S-WMD often improves only modestly over S-WCD (and sometimes less than WMD on specific datasets), raising questions about the added value of the full supervised optimal transport refinement relative to simpler centroid-based metric learning.
- Missing ablations: The paper does not isolate the contribution of A vs. w. Results with “learn A only” (w=1) and “learn w only” (A=I), as well as “no Sinkhorn” (exact OT for small q), would clarify where the gains come from.
- Baselines on centroids: Since S-WCD is a strong baseline, it would be informative to report centroid representations combined with LMNN/ITML/NCA (beyond NCA) to understand whether S-WMD’s advantage is due to the transport layer or simply better metric learning in a low-dimensional space.
- Sensitivity and stability: The method depends on λ (Sinkhorn), the neighbor set size M, batch size, and the choice of k in kNN. A brief sensitivity analysis would strengthen claims of robustness. Also, discuss convergence behavior and stability across random seeds.
- Theory: The approximation replaces the exact transport optimizer Tab with T*λ and uses relaxed dual variables for gradient computation. More discussion on approximation bias, conditions for convergence, and the trade-off in λ would be valuable.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: Technically sound and carefully executed. The use of Sinkhorn to obtain differentiable, efficient gradients is appropriate. The main gap is limited analysis of when and why the supervised refinement improves over the S-WCD initialization, and the absence of ablations for A vs. w.
- Clarity: The paper is well written and easy to follow. Minor notational issues are listed below. The algorithmic pipeline (initialization, batch optimization, neighbor pruning) is clearly presented.
- Originality: Moderate. Prior work has learned ground metrics within EMD and leveraged NCA/LMNN-like objectives. The novelty lies in combining NCA with Sinkhorn-based OT in the word embedding space and jointly learning histogram weights, which is a practical and coherent package.
- Significance: Empirically significant: strong results across diverse datasets and baselines with a reusable toolbox. Conceptually, it reinforces supervised optimal transport as a competitive approach for text distances. The modest improvements over S-WCD on some datasets slightly temper the impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: (i) A only vs. w only vs. both; (ii) S-WMD with different λ; (iii) effect of neighbor pruning M and batch size; (iv) sensitivity to k in kNN.
- Strengthen centroid baselines: Report word centroid representations with LMNN and ITML (after PCA), not only NCA, to fairly assess the added value of the transport layer.
- Report deltas: For each dataset, include the absolute and relative improvement from S-WCD init. to S-WMD to quantify the gain from the supervised OT refinement.
- Robustness: Show results with alternative embeddings (e.g., GloVe/fastText) to demonstrate generality.
- Long vs. short documents: Since S-WMD underperforms on some long-document corpora, analyze performance by document length and discuss whether ground distance choice (L2 vs. L1) or weighting helps.
- Theoretical note: Briefly discuss the bias introduced by T*λ and conditions under which the gradient wrt A and w is a good proxy for the true WMD gradient.

Minor comments and clarifications
- Clarify “The authors” (around Line ~152): specify which citation you refer to (e.g., Cuturi 2013; Cuturi & Peyré 2016; Cuturi & Avis).
- Eq. (10): The dual variables should be denoted consistently as α* and β*, and, when using the Sinkhorn relaxation, α*λ and β*λ to make the approximation explicit.
- Notation overload: The symbol p is used both for the norm exponent and for a dimension/size later (e.g., “p-dimensional all-ones vector”). Please disambiguate (e.g., use d for embedding dimension, n for vocabulary size, q for document support size, and avoid reusing p).
- Remind the reader that the alternating scaling u = da./(Kv), v = db./(Kᵀu) is the Sinkhorn scaling described earlier.
- State whether ground costs use squared Euclidean consistently and, if so, whether you tried L1 or unsquared L2.
- Report the chosen k in kNN and how it was tuned within the Bayesian optimization framework. If possible, include confidence intervals or significance tests for top results.
- Consider releasing trained A and w for the datasets to facilitate reuse.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, efficient method that consistently improves over strong unsupervised distances and many supervised baselines.
- Clean integration of OT with supervised metric learning, with code available and competitive runtimes.
- Broad evaluation and useful qualitative analyses.

Cons for acceptance
- Improvements over S-WCD initialization are sometimes small; limited ablation to pinpoint where gains come from.
- Missing centroid+LMNN/ITML baselines; limited sensitivity analysis and theoretical discussion of the approximation.

Overall recommendation
A solid and well-executed contribution with practical impact. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on adding the requested ablations and clarifications.
