This paper proposes a supervised extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a metric for measuring dissimilarity between text documents. The original WMD is an unsupervised method that calculates the minimum "cost" required to transform the word embeddings of one document into those of another. This work adapts the framework for a supervised setting, where the goal is to learn a distance metric from pairs of documents labeled as belonging to the same or different classes. The authors propose to learn two key components simultaneously: a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a set of word-specific importance weights. The authors have also committed to making their Matlab implementation publicly available.

**Strengths**

The primary contribution of this work is the extension of the powerful WMD framework to a supervised learning context. This is a well-motivated and valuable direction, as it allows the metric to be tailored to specific tasks. The proposed method, which learns both a global semantic transformation (the matrix `A`) and word-level importance (the weights `w`), is particularly compelling. This dual approach provides the flexibility to capture task-relevant semantic relationships while also emphasizing words that are discriminative for the classification problem at hand. The empirical results presented suggest that this approach is effective.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

While the proposed method is promising, the paper could be strengthened by providing deeper insights and clarifications in a few key areas:

1.  **Intuition behind Word Importance:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed intuitive explanation of Equation (5), which defines how the word importance weights (`w`) are incorporated into the distance calculation. A clearer explanation of how these weights modify the transport cost would help readers better understand the underlying mechanism of the model.

2.  **Ablation Study:** The method introduces two learnable components: the linear transformation `A` and the word importance weights `w`. The paper demonstrates that their combined use leads to strong performance, but it is unclear which component is the primary driver of the improvements. An ablation study that evaluates the performance of the model with only `A` (i.e., `w` fixed to uniform) and only `w` (i.e., `A` fixed to identity) would provide valuable insight into their relative contributions. This analysis would significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions about the effectiveness of its dual-component design.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the clarity of the manuscript:

*   **Role of Equation (2):** The purpose of Equation (2) and its relation to the main objective function is unclear. The regularization parameter `lambda` in this equation appears to be distinct from the `lambda` introduced on line 170. Please clarify when Equation (2) is used and explain the roles of the two different `lambda` parameters.

*   **Justification for Claim on Line 316:** The paper claims the method is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words." While this is an expected benefit of using word embeddings, the paper would be stronger if it provided a more explicit justification or a concrete example to support this claim within the specific context of the proposed supervised method.

*   **Missing Information in Results:**
    *   **Line 257:** The text states, "on the right we also show the average error across datasets." I could not locate this information in the accompanying figures or tables. Could you please clarify where this result is presented, or correct the text if it is an error?
    *   **Table 2:** The table includes columns labeled "n" and "ne," but the distinction between these two is not defined in the text. Please add a description of what these headers represent.
    *   **kNN Hyperparameter:** The paper uses a k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) classifier for evaluation but does not specify how the value of `k` was selected. Please clarify whether `k` was a fixed value, chosen via cross-validation, or determined by some other method, and whether this process was consistent across all datasets and compared methods.
