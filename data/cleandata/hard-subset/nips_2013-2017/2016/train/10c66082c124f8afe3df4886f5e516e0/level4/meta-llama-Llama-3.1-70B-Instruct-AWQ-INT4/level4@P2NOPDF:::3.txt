The authors propose a novel method for learning the parameters of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which is essentially the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) computed on text documents using word embeddings to determine bin-to-bin dissimilarities. They parameterize the EMD cost matrix, also known as the ground metric, using a Mahalanobis distance between vector representations. Additionally, the authors suggest learning histogram coefficients reweighting, a concept that has been explored in previous work, such as Lebanon's research.

The authors provide the gradients of Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA) type costs for this problem and present compelling experimental evidence. Overall, the paper effectively combines existing techniques and yields convincing experimental results. I would support acceptance. The paper's strengths lie in its simplicity and relatively straightforward idea, although its implementation and testing are not trivial. The experimental section is a strong aspect of this paper.

However, there are areas that require improvement. The authors should better handle the interplay between regularized and non-regularized formulations and be more rigorous with their mathematical computations and notations. Ideally, they should provide an algorithmic box to clearly illustrate their proposed method.

Several minor comments and suggestions are also worth noting. In Equation 1, the authors use the Euclidean distance between word embeddings as a cost, whereas in Equation 6, the cost becomes the squared Euclidean metric for the purpose of Mahalanobis metric learning, which is typically referred to as 2-Wasserstein. To avoid inconsistencies, it would be more accurate to state that WMD is p-Wasserstein between clouds of word embeddings, regardless of the value of p.

The "reweighting" approach proposed in Equation 5 is not new, as it was previously suggested by Guy Lebanon in the context of text document classification ("metric learning for text documents"). A similar concept can also be found in "Unsupervised Riemannian Metric Learning for Histograms Using Aitchison Transformations" by Le and Cuturi.

It would be beneficial to provide experimental insights into the importance of the weight vector w in the learned metric. Specifically, it would be interesting to know whether this innovation has a significant impact and if it is relevant in any way.

In the related work section, the authors should more clearly highlight the differences between their work and previous studies. For instance, this paper builds upon the work of Cuturi and Avis, but uses entropy regularization, which significantly improves scalability and speed. The cost function is also different, as it is continuous and parameterized with linear maps, unlike the discrete cost in the previous work. These distinctions are essential to emphasize. Additionally, the authors should acknowledge the work of Wang et al, although that paper is not publishable in its current form. The OT optimization in their paper is only run once and does not work as proposed.

By addressing these points, the authors can strengthen their paper and provide a clearer understanding of their proposed method and its contributions to the field.
