

This paper introduces a supervised metric learning framework built upon the Word Movers Distance (WMD) [19], a transport-based distance measure for word2vec embeddings. The approach generalizes to any transport distance derived from squared Euclidean distances following linear projection. It simultaneously learns a linear projection matrix to maximize classification performance and a word-specific weighting scheme that re-scales word frequencies within documents (followed by re-normalization). The authors propose an efficient optimization method for the parameterized metric and demonstrate its effectiveness through comprehensive experiments on document classification tasks using a kNN classifier. The results are compared against a broad set of baselines across multiple datasets.

**Strengths:**
+ The work extends WMD in a technically interesting and practically useful manner, as evidenced by its strong performance on kNN-based text classification tasks.

**Major Concerns:**
- The potential impact is limited by the observation that Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) with metric learning initialized via the "word centroid distance" achieves comparable results to the full proposed method (see the last two rows of Table 3). This suggests the core technical contributions—specifically the learned metric and word weights—provide only marginal incremental gains over a simple initialization based on existing techniques.
- Table 3 omits a critical baseline: the evaluation of the "word centroid embedding" (weighted mean of word embeddings) combined with standard metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA).
- The relative contribution of the two key components (word-specific weights and projection matrix **A**) is not assessed. Ablation studies showing performance when each component is used in isolation would be essential to determine their individual importance.

**Originality & Presentation:**
+ The optimization strategy (Lines 156–175) is novel. While the core idea of linear metric learning within EMD is interesting, it constitutes a relatively modest contribution in isolation.
+ The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, with only minor technical clarifications needed:
  - Line 152: Clarify which paper(s) "the authors" refer to (e.g., [1,7,8]).
  - Equation 11: Confirm whether α and β should carry asterisks (e.g., α*, β*).
  - Around Line 170: Explicitly connect the Sinkhorn scaling method referenced in Line 56 to the optimization procedure.
