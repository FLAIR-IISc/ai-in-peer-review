This paper introduces a supervised metric learning technique extending the recent word-movers distance (WMD) [19], a transport distance leveraging word2vec embeddings. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word-specific importance weights, effectively adapting the WMD for supervised classification tasks. The approach can be generalized to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances after linear projection. A key contribution is an efficient optimization method for learning this parameterized metric. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their Supervised WMD (S-WMD) through extensive experiments, comparing it against a comprehensive set of 26 baselines across eight document classification datasets using k-nearest neighbors (kNN) classification.

This work builds upon the promising WMD metric, offering a technically sound and practically useful extension demonstrated by strong performance on kNN-based text classification. The method’s ability to incorporate supervision into the WMD framework is a valuable addition. However, the potential impact might be somewhat limited. The experimental results show that the unsupervised word centroid distance (WCD), used for initialization, achieves performance comparable to the fully learned S-WMD (see the last two rows of Table 3). This suggests the core benefit might reside in the initialization strategy rather than the full supervised learning process. 

Several avenues for further evaluation could strengthen the paper. The authors do not present results for applying the standard metric learning algorithms (ITML, LMNN, NCA) directly to the word centroid embedding. Evaluating these combinations would provide a clearer understanding of the contribution of the S-WMD's specific formulation. Additionally, assessing the individual contributions of the learned word weights and the projection matrix *A* would be insightful. A ablation study, showing performance with each component active in isolation, would demonstrate their relative importance.

The originality primarily lies in the optimization strategy (Lines 156–175). While integrating linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance is an interesting concept, it represents a relatively incremental contribution. The paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read. A few minor clarifications would enhance it:

*   Line 152: Specify the authors referenced as "The authors" (likely referring to [1, 7, 8]).
*   Equation 11: Ensure α and β are denoted with stars to indicate their relationship to the dual problem.
*   Around line 170, explicitly mention that the scaling process described refers to the Sinkhorn scaling introduced earlier in the paper (Line 56).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Extends a recent and promising document distance metric (WMD).
*   Demonstrates strong empirical results across multiple datasets and a large number of baselines.
*   Presents a technically sound and efficient optimization method.
*   The paper is very well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The performance gain over the unsupervised initialization (WCD) is modest, potentially limiting the overall impact.
*   The evaluation could be more comprehensive, including comparisons with metric learning applied directly to the word centroid embedding and an ablation study of the learned components.
*   The originality of the approach is somewhat incremental, as the core idea of combining metric learning with the WMD is relatively straightforward.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of document distance metrics. While the performance gains might not be dramatic, the method is well-motivated, technically sound, and clearly presented. The thorough experimental evaluation provides strong evidence for its effectiveness. Addressing the minor suggestions regarding evaluation would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors consider incorporating the suggested evaluations in a future version.
