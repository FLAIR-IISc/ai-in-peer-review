The authors present a thorough analysis of the problem of text classification into categories, expanding upon previous work that introduced Word Mover's Distance (Wasserstein) and making it supervised by learning word-specific weights. This contribution proposes using the Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for the Wasserstein distance to improve its computational efficiency. The authors evaluate their method on eight datasets against 26 baselines, demonstrating the best average performance.

The paper is well-written, presenting the topic clearly and providing an in-depth analysis of the underlying mathematics. The authors' explanations are concise and easy to follow, making the paper accessible to a broad audience. However, there are a few weaknesses that detract from the overall quality of the work. Firstly, the novelty of the contribution is somewhat limited compared to the original Word Mover's Distance paper. Additionally, the discussion of World Centroid Distance as a baseline is not entirely clear and seems somewhat out of place.

A more significant concern is the discussion of lambda (the regularizing factor), which seems somewhat lacking. In the reviewer's experience, the value of lambda can have a substantial impact on the results, often being the dominant factor in equation (2). Theoretically, results should be similar to the pure Wasserstein distance given a large enough lambda, albeit at the cost of slower convergence. However, in practice, many users of Sinkhorn distance opt for a very small lambda to improve convergence rates, resulting in behavior that deviates significantly from the Wasserstein distance. It would be beneficial to know whether, in this contribution, lambda dictates a pure Wasserstein behavior or an entropic Sinkhorn behavior.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of text classification, and the results are promising. However, addressing the concerns mentioned above would strengthen the work and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method.
