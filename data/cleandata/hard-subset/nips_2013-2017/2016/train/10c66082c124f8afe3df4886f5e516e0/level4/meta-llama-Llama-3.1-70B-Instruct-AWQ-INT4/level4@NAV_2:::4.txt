This paper proposes a supervised method for learning the distance between documents on top of word mover's distance (WMD). WMD is a previously proposed document metric that leverages word embeddings to compute distances between documents as an optimal transport problem. The authors extend this approach by learning a linear transformation of the word embeddings and a re-weighted word distribution to better capture the semantic meaning of similarity encoded in the labeled data.

The paper is well-written and provides a thorough review of related works in the field. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the literature and provide experimental results on several novel datasets. However, the motivation behind the proposed formulation could be better explained, and the significance of the contribution is not entirely clear.

The authors use a re-weighting of the word distribution using a vector w, but the motivation behind this choice is not well-explained. It is unclear why re-weighting the word distribution is necessary or how it helps to improve the performance of the WMD. Additionally, the re-weighting process could potentially change the document into a completely different one, which may not be desirable.

The contribution of this work is not groundbreaking, as adopting a linear transformation A and re-weighting w with a Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) objective function is a straightforward extension of the WMD to the supervised setting. The authors could have explored more innovative approaches to improve the performance of the WMD.

Furthermore, it is unclear how the algorithm can find a distance for a new document. For a completely new document, it is unclear how to find a suitable re-weighting vector w. The authors should provide more details on how to handle this scenario.

The term "semantic difference" in the abstract seems to refer to the label of the document. However, it is unclear if this term means something else, such as authorship difference or topic difference. The authors should clarify the meaning of this term to avoid confusion.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. The writing is clear, and the paper is well-organized. The authors provide enough information for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

However, the originality of the paper is limited, as the authors build upon existing work on WMD and NCA. The paper does not provide a novel combination of familiar techniques or a new perspective on the problem.

The significance of the paper is also limited, as the results are not significantly better than the state-of-the-art methods. While the authors demonstrate improved performance on some datasets, the improvement is not substantial enough to justify the significance of the contribution.

In conclusion, while the paper is well-written and provides a good understanding of the literature, the motivation behind the proposed formulation could be better explained, and the significance of the contribution is not entirely clear. The authors should address these concerns to strengthen the paper.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper is well-written and provides a thorough review of related works.
* The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the literature.
* The experimental results are well-presented and provide a clear comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The motivation behind the proposed formulation is not well-explained.
* The contribution is not groundbreaking, and the significance is limited.
* The paper does not provide a novel combination of familiar techniques or a new perspective on the problem.
* The results are not substantially better than the state-of-the-art methods.
