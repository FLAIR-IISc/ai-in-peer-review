This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), an extension to the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to learn a weighted metric. Building on the recent success of WMD [19] in kNN-based document classification, the authors address the limitation of its unsupervised nature by jointly learning a transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights. The core idea – minimizing leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error – is well-motivated and grounded in prior work on metric learning [10, 15, 36, 37]. A key contribution is the efficient approximation to the gradient calculation, circumventing the computationally prohibitive nested optimization problem inherent in the original WMD formulation, leveraging the Sinkhorn distance [6]. The paper presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation across eight real-world text classification tasks, demonstrating consistent and significant improvements over a wide array of 26 competitive baselines.

The strengths of this work are numerous. First, the technical approach is sound and well-explained. The authors clearly articulate the problem, motivate their solution, and provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to enable efficient gradient computation is particularly clever and crucial for practical application. Second, the experimental evaluation is remarkably thorough. The inclusion of a diverse set of datasets and a broad spectrum of baselines – including both traditional document representations (e.g., BOW, TF-IDF [30]) and more recent techniques like mSDA [4] and LSI [11] – lends strong credibility to the reported results. The consistent outperformance of S-WMD across these benchmarks is compelling. Finally, the authors acknowledge limitations and provide insightful analysis, such as the potential impact of document length on performance.

However, there are minor areas for consideration. While the explanation of the theoretical background is clear, the connection to broader metric learning literature could be slightly strengthened, particularly discussing limitations compared to more recent advances. The choice of λ=10 in the Sinkhorn approximation is empirically justified, but a more detailed ablation study exploring the sensitivity to this parameter could be beneficial. Although the paper demonstrates substantial gains, it would be valuable to discuss the computational trade-offs more explicitly, including the memory requirements associated with the learned transformations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and novel approach to incorporating supervision into WMD.
*   Significant and consistent performance improvements over a large number of baselines.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation on diverse datasets.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in text representation and classification.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor – connection to the broader metric learning field could be slightly strengthened.
*   Minor – further exploration of the Sinkhorn approximation parameter (λ) could be valuable.
*   Minor – more detailed discussion of computational trade-offs would be helpful.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document distance learning. The technical innovation, combined with the extensive experimental validation, makes it a highly suitable candidate for presentation at NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
