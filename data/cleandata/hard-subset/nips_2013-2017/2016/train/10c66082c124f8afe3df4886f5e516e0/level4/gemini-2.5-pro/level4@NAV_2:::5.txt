This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document metric. This work builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which formulates the distance between two documents as an optimal transport problem between their word embeddings. While WMD is entirely unsupervised, S-WMD incorporates supervision by learning an affine transformation of the underlying word embedding space and a vector of word-importance weights. The model is trained to minimize a stochastic version of the leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor classification error, drawing inspiration from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A key technical contribution is an efficient method for computing the gradient of the WMD-based objective. Since a direct gradient computation would require solving a prohibitive nested optimization problem, the authors leverage an approximation based on the entropically regularized Sinkhorn distance, which makes the learning algorithm practical and efficient.

**Strengths of the paper:**
*   **Significance and Performance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of adapting the powerful, unsupervised WMD metric for supervised classification tasks. The proposed S-WMD method achieves state-of-the-art results for k-NN based text classification, as demonstrated by a comprehensive empirical evaluation across eight real-world datasets and against 26 competitive baselines. The performance improvement over strong unsupervised methods like WMD and supervised baselines is significant.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The authors correctly identify the primary computational bottleneck in supervising WMD and propose a clever and well-justified approximation using the Sinkhorn distance to make the approach feasible. The claims are well-supported by extensive and rigorous experiments.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of providing the necessary background on word embeddings, WMD, and metric learning, which helps to clearly motivate the proposed method. The mathematical derivations are presented with notable clarity.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Originality:** While the results are impressive, the core conceptual contribution is an effective combination of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new framework. The approach synergizes the WMD, an NCA-style loss function, and the Sinkhorn distance approximation. Although this specific combination and its application to text classification is novel and valuable, the work is more of an incremental advance than a completely new direction.
*   **Insufficient Discussion of Hyperparameters:** The role of the entropy regularization parameter λ is underdeveloped. The paper states that λ=10 offers a good trade-off, but the sensitivity of the model to this crucial hyperparameter is not explored. The behavior of the Sinkhorn distance can vary dramatically with λ; a large λ approximates the original Wasserstein distance, whereas a small λ results in a fundamentally different, entropically smoothed objective. The paper would be substantially strengthened by an analysis of how performance changes with λ, which would clarify whether the algorithm's success stems from a close approximation to WMD or from the distinct properties of the regularized transport.
*   **Clarity on Auxiliary Components:** The use of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) for both initialization (training S-WCD) and for selecting nearest neighbors during batch updates could be motivated more explicitly. While WCD is a reasonable and fast approximation, a clearer justification for its role in the algorithm would improve the paper's self-containedness and prevent potential reader confusion.

In summary, this paper presents a high-quality, technically solid, and significant contribution to the field. The proposed S-WMD method is a powerful tool for document classification that clearly advances the state of the art. The primary weaknesses relate to the incremental nature of the approach and the need for a more detailed analysis of its key components and hyperparameters, but these do not detract from the overall value of the work.
