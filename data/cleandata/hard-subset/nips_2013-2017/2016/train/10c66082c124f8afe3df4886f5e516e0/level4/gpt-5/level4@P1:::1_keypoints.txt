- Proposes supervised WMD (S-WMD) that learns a linear embedding projection A and a word-importance vector w, trained with a stochastic leave-one-out kNN (NCA-style) loss over Sinkhorn-regularized OT distances.  
- Uses entropy-regularized Sinkhorn approximation and nearest-neighbor filtering for efficient, differentiable batch training.  
- Empirical evaluation on eight datasets against 26 baselines shows consistent improvements and often state-of-the-art kNN accuracy.  
- Strength: clear, well-motivated supervised extension leveraging embedding adaptation and word reweighting.  
- Strength: practical and efficient training; complexity and gradients are carefully derived and discussed.  
- Strength: thorough empirical study with diverse datasets and strong baselines; well written and easy to follow.  
- Weakness: full S-WMD yields modest gains over the supervised word-centroid initialization (S-WCD init), suggesting limited added value of the main technical contribution.  
- Missing baseline: no evaluation of centroid document embeddings (weighted mean of word embeddings) paired with standard metric learners (ITML, LMNN, NCA).  
- Missing ablation: no experiments isolating the contributions of A-only and w-only (projection vs reweighting).  
- Novelty: moderate — learning a linear ground metric in EMD has precedents; novelty mainly in the stochastic-NCA objective with Sinkhorn and joint learning of w.  
- Quality: method is technically sound; stochastic kNN loss and neighbor filtering are appropriate for sparse corpora.  
- Significance: practical and often improves performance, but limited impact until centroid-based metric learning and component contributions are quantified.  
- Pros for acceptance: solid supervised extension, efficient implementation, broad empirical gains, clear exposition.  
- Cons for acceptance: modest improvement over centroid init, missing baselines/ablations, and incremental conceptual novelty.  
- Minor clarity fixes: specify referenced paper at line 152, denote Eq. 11 dual variables as α*,β*, and explicitly state the fixed-point scaling is the Sinkhorn algorithm around line 170.
