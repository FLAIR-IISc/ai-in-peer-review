

The authors address text classification into categories by extending prior work on Word Mover's Distance (Wasserstein) to a supervised setting through learned word-specific weights. Their key contribution replaces the Wasserstein distance with the Sinkhorn distance as a computational proxy, significantly improving speed. Evaluated across 8 datasets against 26 baselines, their method achieves the best average performance. The paper is exceptionally well-written, providing clear exposition of the topic and thorough, accessible mathematical analysis. However, two weaknesses are noted: the novelty is limited compared to the original Word Mover's Distance paper, and the comparisons with World Centroid Distance appear irrelevant and confusing. Additionally, the discussion of the regularization parameter λ is inadequate. In practice, λ has a substantial impact, often dominating the behavior in Equation (2). Theoretically, sufficiently large λ should yield results comparable to the pure Wasserstein distance (at the cost of slower convergence), but common practice employs small λ values to accelerate convergence, resulting in behavior qualitatively distinct from Wasserstein. It remains unclear whether this work achieves pure Wasserstein behavior or entropic Sinkhorn behavior through its choice of λ.
