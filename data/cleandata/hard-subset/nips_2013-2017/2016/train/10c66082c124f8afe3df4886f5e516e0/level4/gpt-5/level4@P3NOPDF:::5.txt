Summary
This paper tackles supervised text classification using optimal transport between documents. It extends Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by learning word-specific weights and accelerates computation by using the Sinkhorn distance as a proxy for the Wasserstein distance. The authors report results on eight datasets against 26 baselines and achieve the best average performance. The paper is clearly written, with a solid and accessible mathematical treatment.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and thorough, well-explained mathematical analysis.
- Practical contribution: leveraging Sinkhorn distance to obtain substantial computational speedups.
- Supervised extension via learned word-specific weights is intuitive and well-motivated for classification.
- Broad and competitive empirical evaluation across eight datasets and 26 baselines, with strong average performance.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty may be limited relative to the original WMD work; the main advances appear to be the supervised weighting scheme and the use of the Sinkhorn approximation for speed. Both are valuable but incremental.
- The discussion comparing to Word Centroid Distance (WCD) is not fully convincing and, at times, confusing. As presented, it is not clear why WCD is an appropriate or informative point of comparison for the proposed approach.
- The role of the regularization parameter λ (the entropic regularization strength in the Sinkhorn formulation) is under-discussed. In practice, λ can strongly influence both the computed transport plan and the resulting classifier. Depending on its definition (and the scaling of the cost matrix), different λ regimes can yield behavior close to unregularized Wasserstein transport or, alternatively, a distinctly entropic (more diffuse) transport. The paper does not sufficiently clarify which regime it targets, how λ is selected, or how sensitive results are to this choice.
- It is not clear whether the observed performance gains are primarily due to the supervised word weighting, the use of Sinkhorn regularization, or their combination. An ablation would strengthen the claims.

Requests for clarification
- Precisely define λ and its relationship to the entropy term (e.g., whether λ multiplies the negative entropy or corresponds to its inverse). Because conventions vary, an explicit definition would avoid confusion and help interpret the limiting behaviors.
- Discuss the limiting regimes: as λ varies, do you empirically approach the unregularized Wasserstein distance or a more entropic behavior? Please include a sensitivity analysis or curves showing performance and runtime as a function of λ on representative datasets.
- How is λ chosen in practice (per dataset, per fold)? Is it tuned via validation, fixed globally, or adapted based on cost scaling? Please report the selected values and any normalization applied to the cost matrix or embeddings to make λ comparable across datasets.
- Provide runtime comparisons across different λ values and against exact OT (when feasible) to quantify the speed–accuracy trade-off.
- Clarify the comparison to WCD: why is it an appropriate baseline for this method, and what specific aspect of your approach does it test? If the intention is to isolate the contribution of learned weights versus transport structure, please make that explicit.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper
- Add an ablation study:
  - Uniform vs learned word-specific weights (with all else fixed).
  - Sinkhorn-regularized OT vs unregularized OT (where tractable) to isolate the effect of the approximation.
  - Impact of λ on classification accuracy and on the sparsity/diffuseness of the transport plan.
- Expand the discussion of how cost scaling (e.g., embedding normalization, metric choice) interacts with λ, since scaling can dramatically change the effective regularization.
- Ensure the terminology is consistent (e.g., “Word Centroid Distance” rather than “World Centroid Distance”) and streamline references to avoid confusion.
- If space permits, provide qualitative examples illustrating how learned word weights and the Sinkhorn regularization influence nearest-neighbor relationships or decision boundaries.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed and clearly presented paper that applies entropically regularized optimal transport to supervised text classification, delivering strong empirical results and practical speedups. While the conceptual distance from prior WMD work is not large, the combination of supervised weighting and Sinkhorn-based efficiency is compelling for real-world use. The main gap is the limited discussion and analysis of the regularization parameter λ, which is central to both the theoretical interpretation and practical performance of Sinkhorn-based methods. Addressing the λ-related questions and providing targeted ablations would substantially strengthen the paper’s clarity and claims. I lean toward acceptance contingent on clarifying the λ regime and augmenting the empirical analysis accordingly.
