- Proposal: Introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD) to learn a task-specific document distance by incorporating label information into WMD.  
- Main contributions: Learns an affine transformation of word embeddings and a word-importance vector to improve discrimination for classification.  
- Technical innovation: Replaces WMD with entropically regularized Sinkhorn distance to obtain a differentiable, efficiently computable approximation for gradient-based learning.  
- Optimization: Trains via batch gradient descent with a Word Centroid Distance (WCD) based initialization.  
- Experimental scope: Thorough evaluation against 26 baselines on 8 diverse datasets, using Bayesian optimization for hyperparameters and reporting standard errors.  
- Quality: Technically sound, elegant, and practical approach that makes supervised learning of WMD feasible.  
- Clarity: Paper is very well-written and organized, with clear derivations, algorithms, and helpful visualizations (learned embeddings, important-word cloud).  
- Originality: Novel extension of WMD to supervised metric learning for documents; first successful formulation of learning a ground metric in this context.  
- Significance: Achieves state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance across datasets and releases code, suggesting high practical impact.  
- Strength of empirical claims: Robust experimental design and careful evaluation lend strong support to the results.  
- Weakness — training time context: Recommend reporting training times for main supervised baselines (e.g., NCA, LMNN, ITML) for direct comparison.  
- Weakness — hyperparameter sensitivity: Suggest reporting sensitivity analysis for the Sinkhorn regularization parameter (λ), since λ=10 was chosen as a speed/accuracy tradeoff.  
- Weakness — dataset-specific performance: Authors should expand discussion on cases where S-WMD is not top (e.g., BBCSPORT, CLASSIC) to clarify limitations (document length, distance choice).  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, well-motivated method with a key technical contribution (Sinkhorn), extensive rigorous experiments, clear presentation, and likely high impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: No significant flaws; only minor suggestions for improvement, not fundamental issues.
