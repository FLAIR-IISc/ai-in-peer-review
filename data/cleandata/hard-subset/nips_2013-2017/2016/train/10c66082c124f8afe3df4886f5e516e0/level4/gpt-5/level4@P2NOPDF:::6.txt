This paper introduces a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for learning document metrics. Whereas the original WMD is unsupervised and measures distances between documents via optimal transport over word embeddings, this work incorporates supervision of the form “these documents belong to the same class.” The authors commit to releasing MATLAB code. I appreciate the authors’ careful responses during the rebuttal, which clarified several points.

The method learns two components: (1) a linear transformation A of the embedding space (a standard metric-learning approach), and (2) word-importance weights w. I would like clearer intuition for Eq. (5), which defines how w is used, and a more explicit explanation of which component—A, w, or their combination—drives the reported performance gains. An ablation study isolating the contribution of each component would be valuable.

Evaluation is conducted via kNN classification using the learned distance. While I recognize that evaluating a metric directly is challenging, it would be informative to compare against simple classifier baselines (e.g., logistic regression or SVM on bag-of-words or averaged embeddings) on the same datasets to contextualize improvements. This concern was addressed in the authors’ rebuttal, but including such baselines in the final version would still strengthen the paper.

Questions and requests for clarification:
- Usage of Eq. (2): It is not clear when or where Eq. (2) is applied in the training or inference pipeline. Please specify the exact role of Eq. (2) and at which stage it is used.
- λ notation: The λ in Eq. (2) appears to differ from the λ referenced around line 170. Are these distinct hyperparameters or the same one with different roles? If distinct, please disambiguate with different symbols or subscripts and state how each is tuned.
- Justification of the “forgiving” property (line 316): You claim the approach is “particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words.” Please provide empirical evidence (e.g., controlled examples or quantitative analysis) or a theoretical argument showing how the learned A and/or w specifically enhances robustness to syntactic variation while preserving semantic similarity, beyond what standard WMD already provides.

Minor comments and presentation issues:
- Line 257: The text says “on the right we also show the average error across datasets,” but I could not locate this. Is this referring to Table 3 or a figure panel? Please ensure that the average error is clearly presented and labeled.
- Table 2: Please clarify the difference between columns “n” and “ne.” If “ne” denotes a subset (e.g., non-empty, named entities, or example count after filtering), define this explicitly in the caption or main text.
- k in kNN: How is k chosen? Is it fixed across datasets, selected via validation, or optimized per method? Please report the selection procedure and the chosen values to ensure fair comparisons.

Overall, the paper tackles an interesting and practical problem: bringing supervision to WMD-style document metrics by learning both a feature-space transformation and per-word importance. The idea is intuitive, and the approach appears promising. The paper would benefit from:
- Clearer intuition and motivation for Eq. (5) and the role of w, ideally with an ablation study.
- Disambiguation of λ and a precise description of when Eq. (2) is used.
- Stronger justification of the “forgiving” claim, with supporting evidence.
- Minor clarifications on tables, line references, and the kNN setup, plus (if feasible) inclusion of simple classifier baselines to contextualize performance.
