This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach that builds upon the recent "word-movers distance" [19], a transport distance applied to word2vec word embeddings. The proposed approach can be applied to any transport distance based on squared Euclidean distances after linear projection. In addition to learning the linear projection to maximise performance, the proposed approach also learns a weighting of words, which is specific to the document application. This weighting involves re-weighting the frequency of each word by a learned weight and re-normalising the word frequencies. The paper presents an efficient optimisation method to learn the proposed parameterised metric. A thorough experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach by comparing it to a large number of reasonable baselines on a number of document classification datasets using a kNN classifier based on the proposed learned metric.

The work extends the recent word-movers distance in an interesting and technically useful way, as shown by its effectiveness on several kNN text classification tasks. However, the potential impact of this work may be limited by the fact that the earth-movers distance with a metric learning via a "word centroid distance" initialisation performs similarly to the full learning of the proposed distance, as seen in the last two rows of Table 3. This suggests that the main technical contributions of this paper may not provide a significant improvement over this initialisation method based on existing techniques.

There are a few aspects that could be improved in the evaluation. Firstly, it would be beneficial to include an evaluation of the "word centroid embedding" (weighted mean of word embeddings for the document) with all tested existing metric learning approaches (ITML, LMNN, NCA) in Table 3. Secondly, an assessment of the relative importance of the word-specific weights and the projection matrix A would be useful. It would be interesting to see the performance with each of these components being active in isolation.

The originality of the paper is mostly related to the optimisation of the metric (Lines 156-175). While the idea of linear metric learning within the earth-movers distance is very interesting, it is a limited contribution in itself.

The paper is very well written and a pleasure to read. However, there are a few minor issues that could be clarified. For instance, in Line 152, it would be helpful to specify which paper the authors refer to ([1,7,8]?). Additionally, in Equation 11, alpha and beta should likely carry stars. Finally, it would be useful to explicitly recall around Line 170 that this is the Sinkhorn scaling referred to in the introduction (Line 56).
