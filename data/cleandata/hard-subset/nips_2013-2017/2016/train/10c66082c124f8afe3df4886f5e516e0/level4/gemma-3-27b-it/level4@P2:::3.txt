This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document similarity. The core idea is to learn an affine transformation of the word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector to minimize nearest neighbor classification error. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering. The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on eight text classification tasks, consistently outperforming a comprehensive set of 26 baselines.

The paper builds upon the recent success of WMD [Kusner et al., 2016] by extending it to a supervised setting. WMD cleverly utilizes word embeddings to define document distance via optimal transport, bypassing the need to learn document representations directly. This work retains that advantage while adding the ability to incorporate task-specific supervision. The contribution lies in efficiently adapting the WMD framework for supervised learning, addressing a key limitation of the original unsupervised approach. The method’s success across diverse datasets suggests a robust and generalizable improvement over existing techniques.

**Strengths:** The paper's strengths lie in its clear motivation, the elegant combination of existing techniques (optimal transport, metric learning, word embeddings), and the strong experimental validation. The practical efficiency of the proposed gradient approximation is a significant advantage. The comprehensive comparison against a large number of baselines provides strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The inclusion of ablation studies, particularly regarding the importance of the word-importance vector, would be valuable (see weaknesses).

**Weaknesses:** While the paper presents convincing results, some areas could be improved. The mathematical notation and explanations are occasionally imprecise, potentially hindering reproducibility. An algorithmic box outlining the key steps of S-WMD would enhance clarity. The discussion of related work could be more nuanced. The connection to prior work on reweighting histograms (e.g., Lebanon’s work, Le/Cuturi) is acknowledged but could be expanded. Finally, while the experimental results are strong, further analysis of the learned word weights (the 'w' vector) would be insightful – understanding *which* words are being upweighted and why could provide valuable insights into the learned metric. The authors should also more clearly differentiate their approach from Wang et al. [2016], acknowledging the issues with that prior work more explicitly. The paper could benefit from discussing the limitations of using the squared Euclidean distance (2-Wasserstein) within the optimization framework, and why this choice was made.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The gradient derivation and the approximation technique seem reasonable, and the experimental results support the claims. However, greater mathematical rigor and a more detailed algorithmic description would strengthen the overall quality.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the mathematical notation could be more consistent and precise. The flow of ideas is logical, but a more explicit algorithmic summary would improve readability.

**Originality:** The combination of optimal transport, metric learning, and word embeddings is novel, and the efficient gradient approximation is a significant contribution. While the individual components are not entirely new, the specific application and combination are original.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods for document similarity, suggesting that S-WMD could be valuable for a variety of applications, including information retrieval, text classification, and document recommendation. The potential to leverage readily available word embeddings makes this approach particularly attractive.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results across diverse datasets.
*   Novel and efficient approach to incorporating supervision into WMD.
*   Clear motivation and well-defined problem.
*   Comprehensive comparison against a large number of baselines.
*   Potential for significant impact in various NLP applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Mathematical notation and explanations could be more rigorous.
*   Lack of a clear algorithmic description.
*   Limited analysis of the learned word weights.
*   Related work section could be more nuanced.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of document similarity and metric learning. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the strong experimental results provide compelling evidence of the method’s effectiveness. Addressing the suggested improvements would further enhance the paper's quality and impact.
