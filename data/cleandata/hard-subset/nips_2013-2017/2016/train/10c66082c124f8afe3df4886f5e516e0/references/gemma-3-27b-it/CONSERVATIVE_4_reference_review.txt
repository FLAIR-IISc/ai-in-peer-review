This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance by learning a linear transformation of word representations and re-weighted word frequencies. The core idea is to minimize leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric, leveraging a smoothed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) for efficient computation. The authors claim state-of-the-art results across 8 datasets and 26 baselines.

The motivation—improving WMD with supervision—is reasonable. WMD, while effective, is unsupervised and doesn’t explicitly tailor distances to specific tasks. The authors correctly identify the computational challenges and propose a seemingly effective workaround using the Sinkhorn distance. The inclusion of word importance weighting is also a sensible addition.

However, despite the promising premise and reported results, I find this paper deeply unconvincing. My primary concern is the sheer number of baselines claimed to be outperformed. While comprehensive benchmarking is admirable, achieving SOTA results against *26* methods raises significant red flags. The presentation lacks sufficient detail to assess the fairness of these comparisons. Specifically, the tuning of hyperparameters for all baselines using Bayesian optimization, while mentioned, isn’t elaborated. It’s crucial to know the computational budget allocated to each baseline’s hyperparameter search—optimizing 26 methods thoroughly is a substantial undertaking, and any imbalance could skew the results. 

Furthermore, the paper feels somewhat hand-wavy in several aspects. The justification for the choice of λ=10 in the Sinkhorn distance regularization is weak ("a nice trade-off"). A more thorough ablation study exploring different λ values would strengthen the argument. The initialization strategy, while drawing on NCA and WCD, isn’t fully justified beyond being a “reasonable approximation.” The authors state the WCD is a lower bound on WMD but do not address how this impacts the subsequent metric learning process.

The complexity analysis, while provided, doesn’t address practical limitations. The O(q^2) complexity of the Sinkhorn distance computation is still potentially expensive for large vocabularies. The reliance on nearest neighbor search (N=200) within the batch gradient descent also introduces a parameter sensitive to dataset size and embedding quality.

The visualization (Figure 1) is standard t-SNE output and doesn't provide particularly compelling evidence for S-WMD’s superiority. The learned word weights (Figure 2) are intuitive, but their analysis lacks depth – are these weights consistent across datasets? Do they align with linguistic intuition beyond simply highlighting important words?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem: improving document distance metrics.
*   Proposes a technically sound approach leveraging the Sinkhorn distance.
*   Reports strong empirical results on a variety of datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The extremely high number of baselines outperformed is suspicious and lacks sufficient justification.
*   Insufficient detail regarding hyperparameter tuning and computational budget for baselines.
*   Lack of rigorous analysis of key parameters (λ, N).
*   The paper presents a large experimental study but lacks significant insights beyond "supervision helps."
*   The claimed improvement over WMD, a strong baseline, feels overstated given the limited theoretical justification for the chosen method.

**Overall:**

I am skeptical of the claims made in this paper. While the idea of supervised WMD is interesting, the lack of detailed analysis, the sheer scale of the benchmarking without sufficient transparency, and the reliance on empirical results without deeper theoretical justification lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper needs significantly more scrutiny and a more convincing presentation of its results to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors need to provide a much clearer picture of the experimental setup and address the concerns about the fairness and robustness of their comparisons.
