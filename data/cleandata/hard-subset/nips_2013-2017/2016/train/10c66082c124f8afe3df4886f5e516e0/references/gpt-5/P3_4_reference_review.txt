Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), which learns a task-specific document distance by adapting the ground metric and word importance within the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) framework. Concretely, it learns: (i) a linear transformation A applied to word embeddings, and (ii) a nonnegative word-importance vector w applied to normalized BoW histograms. The distance between two documents is the optimal transport cost under the transformed ground cost ‖A(xi−xj)‖^2 with histograms reweighted by w. To enable efficient and differentiable training, the authors use entropic regularization (Sinkhorn distances) to approximate the transport plan T* and dual potentials (α, β), and optimize a Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA)-style loss that approximates leave-one-out kNN accuracy. Initialization is done by running NCA on word centroids (S-WCD), followed by stochastic batch training with a neighbor-pruning heuristic based on WCD. Experiments on 8 datasets against 26 baselines show S-WMD achieves the best average rank and best accuracy on 5/8 datasets; code is provided.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: WMD is strong but unsupervised; S-WMD injects supervision without learning document representations, keeping computation in low-dimensional embedding space.
- Methodologically coherent: combines metric learning (NCA) with entropic OT (Sinkhorn) and histogram reweighting in a principled way. The use of envelope-theorem-style gradients for the ground metric is sensible when using the regularized OT.
- Practical contributions: careful initialization (S-WCD), batch training with neighbor pruning, and complexity analysis. The approach is implementable; code availability is a plus.
- Empirical performance: broad comparison across many classical baselines and their supervised metric-learning variants; solid average improvements and competitive per-dataset results.
- Interpretability: learned word weights (w) are meaningful; qualitative visualizations (t-SNE, word clouds) support claims.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Objective/gradient mismatch and clarity: The loss uses D_A,w but gradients are computed using the entropic-regularized solution T*λ and approximate duals (αλ, βλ). This should be clarified as optimizing a surrogate D^λ_A,w consistently in both the loss and gradients. As written, it appears to optimize an unregularized objective with regularized gradients.
- Derivation and constraints on w: The method hinges on reweighting histograms d̃ = (w◦d)/(w^T d). The paper does not clearly state constraints on w (nonnegativity) or regularization (e.g., ℓ2/ℓ1) to avoid degenerate/unstable solutions. Since scaling w by a constant is unidentifiable, discuss parameterization (e.g., w = exp(u)) and how negativity is prevented. This is important for correctness and reproducibility.
- Dual potentials for w-gradient: The formula for αλ, βλ from the Sinkhorn scaling (u, v) is stated without derivation and uses an ad-hoc centering term. Please provide a precise statement with references or an appendix derivation for the entropic-OT dual and how the normalization in d̃ affects the gradient. As written, it’s difficult to verify.
- Notational slippage and typos: p is overloaded (norm exponent vs. number of unique words); “.2 Optimization” section header; missing or malformed references [5, 21?]; undefined constants in αλ, βλ; “1/p” vector length; “th Conference…” line. These reduce clarity.
- Limited ablations: It would help to quantify the contributions of A vs. w (only-A, only-w), and the impact of initialization, λ, neighbor-pruning N, and batch size B. Sensitivity to λ is only briefly mentioned.
- Baselines: While classical baselines are extensive, comparisons to contemporary supervised text representations (e.g., fastText, simple CNN/LSTM text classifiers circa 2016) are missing. Even if kNN is the evaluation protocol, a discussion or additional experiment would strengthen significance claims.
- Scope of evaluation: All results are kNN classification. Since WMD is popular for retrieval/ranking, reporting retrieval metrics (e.g., MAP, nDCG) or robustness to domain shift would broaden impact.

Quality
Overall technically sound, with a reasonable optimization strategy and solid empirical evaluation. The main theoretical caveat is the lack of precise alignment between the surrogate (entropic OT) used for gradients and the loss as written, and the insufficiently specified constraints/derivation for w. These are fixable with clarifications/appendix.

Clarity
Readable and generally well organized, but marred by notational inconsistencies and several typos. The optimization details for w and the exact objective (regularized vs. unregularized) need clearer exposition. Including a short derivation for gradients (or a pointer to a precise lemma) would help experts reproduce results.

Originality
The combination is novel in NLP: learning a supervised ground metric and global histogram weights for WMD using entropic OT and NCA. Related vision work on supervised EMD is acknowledged; the histogram reweighting and NLP application are new. The use of Sinkhorn to enable end-to-end learning of WMD-like metrics is timely.

Significance
WMD is widely used; a supervised, efficient variant with demonstrated gains is valuable. The approach is likely to influence subsequent supervised OT for text and related tasks. The practical recipe (initialization + Sinkhorn + NCA loss) is likely to be adopted.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please explicitly define the optimized objective as D^λ_A,w and use it consistently in the loss; discuss the limit λ→∞ and practical trade-offs. Provide sensitivity plots for λ.
- Specify constraints/parameterization and regularization for w. If w must be nonnegative, how is this enforced? Consider adding ℓ1/ℓ2 penalties to stabilize learning.
- Provide a precise statement/derivation of αλ, βλ from u, v for entropic OT, and justify the centering term.
- Report ablations: only-A, only-w; effect of initialization; effect of neighbor-pruning N; and runtime vs. accuracy trade-offs.
- Clarify r (output dimension of A) and whether dimensionality reduction is learned or fixed; add regularization on A (e.g., Frobenius norm) to prevent overfitting.
- Add at least one modern supervised text baseline (e.g., fastText) and/or retrieval metrics.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a principled, efficient approach.
- Strong empirical results and public code.
- Method is broadly useful and extensible.

Cons for acceptance
- Some theoretical/derivational details and constraints are underspecified.
- Minor clarity/notation issues and limited ablations.
- Baselines could better reflect contemporary supervised text models.

Recommendation
Lean accept, contingent on clarifying the objective/gradients and detailing constraints/derivations for w; otherwise a strong borderline accept. Confidence: high.
