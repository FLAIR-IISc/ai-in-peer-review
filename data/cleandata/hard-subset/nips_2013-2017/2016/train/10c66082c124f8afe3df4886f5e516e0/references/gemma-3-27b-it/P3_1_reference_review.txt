## Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a document distance metric by combining the strengths of the unsupervised Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) with supervised metric learning. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of word embeddings *and* re-weight word frequencies to better reflect semantic similarity encoded in document labels. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering. Extensive experiments across 8 datasets demonstrate the superiority of S-WMD compared to 26 baselines, showcasing significant improvements in k-NN classification performance.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges the limitations of traditional document representations (BOW, TF-IDF) and the emergence of embedding-based methods like WMD. It correctly identifies the growing field of metric learning, and highlights how existing approaches often struggle with high-dimensional data and require dimensionality reduction. Importantly, the authors emphasize the unique contribution of S-WMD: learning a metric *within* the word embedding space *without* altering document representations, and simultaneously learning word importance weights.  The connection to Cuturi’s work on Sinkhorn distances is appropriately emphasized as a key enabler for scalability. The comparison to Wang et al. [35] is also relevant and highlights the novelty of their word re-weighting approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The combination of learning a linear transformation of word embeddings *and* learning word importance weights is a novel and insightful approach.  It's a smart way to inject supervision into WMD without losing the benefits of leveraging pre-trained word embeddings.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling results across a diverse range of datasets, consistently outperforming a large number of competitive baselines. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Scalability:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance significantly addresses the computational bottleneck of incorporating supervision into WMD.  The described optimizations, like probabilistic filtering and the batch gradient descent, further improve efficiency. The reported training times are reasonable.
* **Clear Explanation & Good Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology, including the mathematical formulations.  The connection to existing work is well-established.
* **Thorough Evaluation:**  The evaluation is comprehensive, including comparisons to a wide range of methods and a detailed ablation study (through the S-WMD init. results). The visualization via t-SNE provides further insight into the learned representations.
* **Availability of Code:** Providing the code is excellent and will aid reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** While the authors mention Bayesian optimization, a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter sensitivity, specifically regarding λ (the entropy regularization parameter) and the learning rates, would be beneficial. How do these parameters impact performance, and are there guidelines for choosing appropriate values?
* **Squared Euclidean Distance:** The choice of the squared Euclidean distance within the WMD formulation feels somewhat arbitrary. The authors could briefly explore other distance metrics (e.g., cosine similarity) and justify the selection of the squared Euclidean distance.  The observation about CLASSIC dataset performance might suggest this choice isn't universally optimal.
* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:**  While the paper provides a clear description of the algorithm, a deeper theoretical analysis of the convergence properties or the conditions under which the learned metric is guaranteed to improve performance would strengthen the work. 
* **Batch Size/Neighbor Selection:** The choice of batch size (32) and the number of nearest neighbors (200) feels a bit arbitrary. While pragmatic, a justification or sensitivity analysis regarding these values would be useful.
* **Potential for Overfitting:** Learning word importance weights adds another layer of parameters. While the results are strong, the risk of overfitting, particularly on smaller datasets, is present.  A discussion of regularization strategies, or an analysis of performance on held-out datasets, could alleviate this concern.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective method for learning a supervised document distance metric.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods.
* The scalability issues associated with supervised WMD are addressed through clever algorithmic optimizations.
* The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and thoroughly evaluated.
* The code is available, which promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is limited.
* The choice of certain hyperparameters and components (squared Euclidean distance, batch size, neighbor selection) lacks detailed justification.
* The potential for overfitting is not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of document similarity and metric learning.  The proposed S-WMD method is novel, effective, and scalable. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper far outweigh them. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to NIPS and **strongly recommend acceptance.**



**Rating:** 8.5/10 (Strong Accept)
