This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance by learning a linear transformation of word representations and re-weighted word frequencies. The core idea leverages the efficiency of the Sinkhorn distance to make supervised learning feasible within the WMD framework. The authors demonstrate strong performance across eight datasets and numerous baselines.

While the idea of incorporating supervision into WMD is promising and the results are impressive, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet distracting, flaws that collectively detract from its overall quality. My assessment is heavily skewed towards identifying these issues, acknowledging a potential disconnect between my perfectionist approach and a broader evaluation of significance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of learning a metric directly *within* the WMD framework, without modifying the document representation itself, is a novel contribution.
*   **Performance:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate superior performance compared to a large number of baselines, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Efficiency:** The utilization of the Sinkhorn distance is crucial for enabling supervised learning with WMD, addressing a significant computational bottleneck.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The extensive experimentation across a diverse range of datasets and comparison to a large number of baselines is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Formatting and Typos:** The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies and minor typos. For example, the asterisk denoting equal contribution appears at the very end and feels tacked on. There are inconsistent spaces around mathematical operators (e.g., “n∑” instead of “n∑”). The citation format is occasionally inconsistent. The phrase “In NIPS, pp,” appears where a complete page range should be given. These detract from the paper’s professionalism.
*   **Mathematical Notation:** The notation is sometimes sloppy. For instance, the use of “◦” for the Hadamard product is unusual and could be more clearly defined initially. The use of both *d* and *D* to represent distances is confusing. There’s a lack of clarity on whether variables are vectors or scalars in several equations. Specifically, equation (4) needs clarification as to the dimensionality of *w* and its interaction with *d<sub>a</sub>*.
*   **Ambiguous Terminology:** The term “importance” applied to word weights is vague. The paper doesn’t sufficiently articulate *why* certain words might be more important, and this is left to be learned, rather than motivated.
*   **Gradient Calculation Detail:** While the authors describe approximating the gradient, the explanation remains high-level. More details regarding the implementation of the gradient approximation, especially related to the matrix scaling algorithm, would be beneficial.
*   **Experimental Details:** The Bayesian optimization process for hyperparameter tuning is mentioned but lacks specifics. What search space was used? What acquisition function? This information is crucial for reproducibility. The initialization with S-WCD is mentioned as beneficial, yet the ablation study in the provided results doesn’t explicitly highlight the improvement gained from this initialization, making the claim less convincing.
*   **Related Work Coverage:** The related work section, while extensive, feels slightly disorganized. The connection between ITML and the proposed method isn’t fully explored; a more detailed comparison of their loss functions and optimization strategies would be helpful. Specifically, a comparison of the metric learning objectives would be very beneficial to the readers.
*   **Figure Quality:** The resolution of Figure 1 is low, making it difficult to discern the clustering structure. Similarly, the visualization in Figure 2, while insightful, would benefit from clearer labeling and a more informative color scheme.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The authors briefly mention the potential limitations related to document length. A more thorough discussion of the method's limitations and potential failure cases is warranted.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel approach to supervised WMD.
*   Strong empirical results.
*   Addresses a significant computational challenge.
*   Extensive experimental evaluation.

**Con:**

*   Numerous minor flaws in formatting, notation, and clarity.
*   Lack of detail in certain aspects of the implementation (gradient calculation, Bayesian optimization).
*   Vague terminology and limited discussion of limitations.
*   Visualization quality could be improved.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its merits, the paper's numerous imperfections hinder its overall impact. The constant need to mentally correct minor issues while reading detracts from appreciating the core contribution. While the technical soundness is not in question, the lack of polish and detail raises concerns about the rigor of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject**. While the idea is strong and the results are positive, the cumulative effect of the numerous minor flaws prevents me from recommending acceptance. I encourage the authors to address these issues thoroughly and resubmit the paper. A meticulous revision addressing the formatting, notation, clarity, and experimental details would significantly strengthen the manuscript. I believe the core contribution deserves to be published, but not in its current state.
