This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds upon the recently introduced Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which defines the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" words from one to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. The authors introduce supervision by learning two components: (1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, and (2) a vector `w` of word importance weights. These parameters are learned by minimizing a stochastic approximation of the k-NN leave-one-out classification error, a loss function inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A significant technical challenge is the high computational cost and non-differentiability of the WMD optimization. To address this, the authors leverage the entropy-regularized version of the optimal transport problem, known as the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation allows for faster, differentiable computation of the document distance and its gradients. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation across eight datasets, comparing S-WMD to a wide range of baselines, including classic document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA), modern unsupervised methods (WMD), and these representations combined with established metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Principled Approach:** The paper presents a well-reasoned extension of an established method (WMD). The decision to incorporate supervision by learning a transformation of the embedding space and re-weighting word histograms is intuitive. The primary technical contribution—the use of the Sinkhorn distance to make the optimization tractable—is a well-motivated and technically sound choice. It directly addresses the main bottleneck of supervising the WMD, transforming a computationally prohibitive problem into a feasible one.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is a major strength of this work. The authors compare their method against a very comprehensive suite of 26 competitive baselines on eight diverse datasets. This includes not just standard document representations but, critically, those same representations paired with powerful metric learning algorithms. This extensive comparison provides strong evidence for the proposed method's efficacy. The fact that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance across most of these datasets demonstrates its robustness and general utility.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The background section provides the necessary context on word embeddings, WMD, and NCA. The proposed method is detailed logically, from the definition of the S-WMD distance and loss function to the crucial gradient computation steps and optimization heuristics. The results are presented clearly and support the paper's main claims.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Conceptual Novelty:** While the engineering and empirical contributions are strong, the conceptual novelty is somewhat limited. The work is a combination of several existing, well-established ideas: WMD, NCA-style metric learning, and the Sinkhorn relaxation for optimal transport. Supervised learning for Earth Mover's Distance ground metrics has also been explored previously in other domains (e.g., Wang et al., 2012, for computer vision). The novelty here lies in the specific application to text documents via WMD and the combination with word-importance weighting, rather than a fundamental breakthrough in metric learning or optimal transport.

2.  **Dependence on Initialization:** The optimization problem is non-convex, and performance is highly dependent on a good initialization. The authors propose a clever initialization scheme based on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD), which appears effective. However, the performance of the final S-WMD model is still tethered to the quality of this simpler, surrogate objective. The paper reports that S-WMD improves upon its initialization, but it is not entirely clear how much of the final performance is attributable to the initialization versus the subsequent fine-tuning.

3.  **Analysis of Failure Cases:** The method does not outperform all baselines on every dataset (e.g., BBCSPORT, CLASSIC). The authors suggest this might be due to document length or suboptimal distance choices. While this is plausible, a more in-depth analysis of these cases would strengthen the paper. For instance, on what kinds of document pairs does S-WMD fail where a simpler method like TF-IDF+NCA succeeds? Understanding the limitations of the model is as important as demonstrating its strengths.

4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method introduces several hyperparameters, notably the Sinkhorn regularization parameter λ, the batch size `B`, and the neighbor set size `N`. The paper states λ=10 is used as a trade-off, but provides little analysis of how sensitive the final results are to this choice. A more rigorous ablation study or sensitivity analysis would provide greater confidence in the method's stability and ease of use.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-Acceptance:**
    *   The paper addresses a clear and important problem: learning a supervised distance metric for text documents.
    *   The proposed solution is methodologically sound and effectively combines powerful existing techniques.
    *   The use of the Sinkhorn relaxation to enable efficient training is a solid technical contribution.
    *   The empirical evaluation is exceptionally thorough and provides compelling evidence that S-WMD is a new state-of-the-art method for kNN-based document classification.

*   **Con-Acceptance:**
    *   The work is more of an effective combination of existing ideas than a fundamentally new contribution.
    *   The analysis of the method's limitations and sensitivity to hyperparameters could be more comprehensive.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the concerns about conceptual novelty, the paper presents a methodologically sound, well-executed, and empirically powerful contribution. The problem it solves is relevant, and the solution is elegant and effective. The strength of the experimental validation, which pits the proposed method against a formidable set of established baselines, is significant and meets the high standards expected for NIPS. The results convincingly demonstrate a clear advance in the state of the art for this task. Therefore, I lean towards acceptance.
