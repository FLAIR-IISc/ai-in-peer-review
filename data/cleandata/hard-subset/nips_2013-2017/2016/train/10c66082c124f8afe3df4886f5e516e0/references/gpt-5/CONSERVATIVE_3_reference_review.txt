Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of WMD that learns (i) a linear transformation of word embeddings A and (ii) a word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The training objective is an NCA-style surrogate for leave-one-out kNN error, where pairwise document distances are computed via optimal transport under the learned ground metric and reweighted histograms. To make training tractable, the authors replace the exact WMD with the entropically regularized Sinkhorn distance for computing both transport plans and dual potentials, enabling O(q^2) per-pair complexity (q = unique words per document). They further accelerate training via an initialization based on supervised word centroids (S-WCD), mini-batching, and neighbor truncation. Experiments on 8 datasets with many baselines suggest S-WMD often achieves the lowest kNN error.

Relation to prior work
This builds directly on WMD (Kusner et al. 2015) and entropic OT/Sinkhorn (Cuturi 2013). The learning objective follows NCA (Goldberger et al. 2005), and the idea of learning a ground metric for EMD is related to Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; and Wang et al. for vision tasks. The combination—learning both a ground metric over word embeddings and histogram weights for documents—appears new in the text domain. The approach remains close to established methods.

Strengths
- Uses well-established components (WMD/OT, Sinkhorn, NCA) in a coherent way; the contribution is incremental but practical.
- Clear, intuitive modeling choice: adapting the ground metric and word weights to labels should help kNN tasks.
- Sensible computational strategy (Sinkhorn relaxation, S-WCD initialization, neighbor pruning) enabling scalable training.
- Broad empirical evaluation across multiple datasets with many baselines; reported improvements are often substantial.
- Claims are mostly aligned with the methodology; code availability is stated.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness:
- Dual potentials and gradients: The gradient w.r.t. w uses dual variables (α, β) from the unregularized problem but substitutes them with quantities derived from Sinkhorn scaling (u, v). For entropic OT, the dual potentials are typically f = (1/λ) log u, g = (1/λ) log v (up to constants). The provided expressions (including mean-correction terms) are not derived or referenced rigorously. Please include a formal derivation or a citation showing the exact relationship used and any invariances handled by offsets.
- Training/test mismatch: The loss uses the entropic relaxation for gradients; it is unclear whether test-time distances for kNN use the exact WMD or the same Sinkhorn-regularized distance with learned A, w. If different, this mismatch should be discussed and empirically assessed.
- Constraints/regularization on w: Eq. (4) implies w ≥ 0 to keep d̃ in the simplex. Are nonnegativity constraints enforced during optimization? Is any regularization (e.g., L1/L2) applied to prevent degenerate solutions (zeroing many words) or extreme scaling? This is not specified.
- Choice of λ: λ is fixed to 10 without sensitivity analysis. Given the centrality of entropic regularization to both accuracy and runtime, a study of λ’s effect on performance and convergence is necessary.
- Neighbor truncation bias: The gradient uses only M neighbors based on WCD prefiltering. This introduces a biased gradient estimator. Evidence that this does not materially change the learned metric (ablation vs. no truncation / larger M) is missing.
- Ablations: It is unclear how much each component contributes. Ablations (A-only, w-only, full; with/without initialization; with/without Sinkhorn; effect of N) would strengthen the conclusions.

Clarity:
- Several notation issues and typos: p is used for both exponent and dimensionality; Section 3.2 header formatting; placeholders like [5, 21?]; inconsistent use of n/p; missing derivation details for gradients; some equations need clearer definitions of symbols. The writing is generally understandable but could be tightened.
- Complexity analysis mixes symbols (p, d, r) in ways that are hard to track; a consolidated notation table would help.

Originality:
- The work is an incremental but useful combination: supervised learning of a ground metric in OT for textual histograms using NCA and Sinkhorn. The histogram reweighting w in addition to A adds flexibility relative to prior EMD metric learning in vision. Overall, modest novelty.

Significance:
- Empirical results indicate consistent improvements over strong baselines across multiple datasets, which is valuable for kNN-style text classification and potentially retrieval. However, conclusions would be stronger with ablations, λ sensitivity, and fairness checks (e.g., using exact same pretrained embeddings where applicable, and clarity on PCA reductions for baselines vs. S-WMD).
- Evaluation is limited to kNN; demonstrating impact on a retrieval or ranking task, or comparing to strong supervised neural baselines (e.g., contemporaneous CNN/RNN text classifiers) would contextualize the gains.

Requested clarifications/experiments
- Provide a precise derivation (or authoritative reference) for using Sinkhorn scalings to approximate α, β in the gradient w.r.t. w, including handling of additive constants.
- State clearly the test-time distance (exact WMD vs. Sinkhorn). Report performance with both to show robustness.
- Specify constraints and regularization on w and A; if w is constrained to be nonnegative, describe how this is enforced.
- Add sensitivity analyses for λ, M (neighbor set size), and batch size; include training stability plots.
- Include ablations: A-only, w-only, initialization vs. random, and effect of removing neighbor truncation.
- Report runtime including initialization, and memory usage; discuss scalability to longer documents/larger vocabularies.

Minor comments
- Fix citation placeholders and typos; unify notation; ensure figures/tables are clearly referenced. Clarify that “p” in Eq. (1) is the ground metric exponent, and use a different symbol for vocabulary/document sizes.

Pros
- Solid use of established methods; practical, well-motivated extension of WMD.
- Significant empirical improvements on multiple datasets; code made available.
- Efficient training via Sinkhorn/NCA with reasonable complexity.

Cons
- Insufficient theoretical detail on gradient through dual potentials; unclear constraints on w.
- Limited ablation/sensitivity; potential bias from neighbor truncation.
- Some clarity/notation issues; limited evaluation scope (kNN only).

Recommendation: Weak accept
Justification: While the contribution is incremental, it is well-grounded in established methodology, addresses a relevant problem, and shows convincing empirical gains with a practical, efficient algorithm. Clarifications and additional analyses would strengthen the work, but the core idea and results merit acceptance.

Confidence: 4/5
