Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised metric-learning approach that improves the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparison. The key ideas are: (i) learn a linear transformation A of the pretrained word embedding space (e.g., word2vec) so that the ground cost in the optimal transport (OT) better reflects label-driven similarity; and (ii) learn a global word-importance vector w to reweight document histograms before computing transport. The objective is a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss (NCA-style), where pairwise distances are WMD computed with the transformed embedding and reweighted histograms. To make optimization feasible, the authors replace exact OT with the entropically regularized Sinkhorn distance, enabling O(q^2) computation per pair, and derive gradients with respect to A via Danskin’s theorem (using the optimal transport plan) and with respect to w via dual potentials approximated from Sinkhorn scaling vectors (u, v). They further speed training with a WCD-based neighbor shortlist and mini-batches. Experiments across 8 datasets and 26 baselines show that S-WMD achieves the best or near-best kNN accuracy on most datasets, while training efficiently; code is released.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on WMD (Kusner et al., ICML’15) and entropic OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS’13). It follows the NCA paradigm (Goldberger et al., NIPS’04) to optimize expected LOO kNN accuracy, but applies it in an OT-based document metric that does not require learning a document representation. It is related to metric learning in EMD ground space (e.g., Wang & Guibas, ECCV’09) and supervised OT ground metric learning in vision, but differs by (a) targeting the word embedding space leveraging pretrained vectors and (b) jointly learning global vocabulary weights. Compared to unsupervised topic models (LDA), LSI, and denoising autoencoders (mSDA), the method is a supervised distance learner rather than a representation learner.

Strengths
- Well-motivated problem: adding supervision to WMD is timely and relevant for document classification and retrieval.
- Sound technical formulation: learning both a ground metric (A) and histogram weights (w) within OT is principled. Gradients for A via optimal plan and for w via dual potentials are standard and appropriate with entropic regularization.
- Practical efficiency: using Sinkhorn, neighbor shortlisting by WCD, and mini-batching makes training feasible; reported per-iteration runtimes are reasonable.
- Empirical performance: across 8 datasets, S-WMD is best on 5/8 and competitive on the rest, with consistent average rank improvement over 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised).
- Reproducibility: code is released; hyperparameters tuned via Bayesian optimization.

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity/notation issues:
  - The paper alternates between q and p to denote the number of unique words; p is also used for the norm order. This is confusing. Please standardize symbols.
  - In Sec. 3.1, Kij = exp(−λ||xi − xj||^2) is written independent of A, but when learning A the kernel should be K_A,ij = exp(−λ||A(xi − xj)||^2). Please clarify and ensure consistency with gradients.
  - The expressions for α*λ and β*λ subtract log(u)>1/p 1; p here appears to be the histogram dimension. Please define clearly and explain the centering.
  - There are several typos and formatting glitches (e.g., “.2 Optimization”, random stray text, “th Conference on...”).
- Assumptions and regularization:
  - The constraints on w are not specified. Is w constrained to be nonnegative? Is there any regularization (L1/L2) on w and/or A to avoid degenerate solutions that overemphasize rare class-specific words? An explicit discussion of constraints and regularizers, and their effect, would strengthen the work.
  - Learning a single global w may be limiting for multi-domain datasets; per-class or group-sparse variants could be considered.
- Optimization details:
  - The derivative with respect to A uses the optimal plan but ignores dT*/dA. With entropic regularization and using Danskin’s theorem, this is valid; stating this explicitly would help.
  - The dual potentials for the regularized problem differ from unregularized OT; the approximation quality as a function of λ is only anecdotally discussed (λ=10). A sensitivity analysis (λ vs. accuracy/runtime) would be helpful.
- Evaluation scope:
  - Results are limited to kNN. It would be informative to show that the learned distance also benefits other tasks (e.g., retrieval mAP, clustering) or classifiers (e.g., kernel SVM with distance kernel).
  - Ablations are limited. Reporting performance of “learn A only” vs “learn w only” vs full S-WMD, and the benefit of Sinkhorn vs exact OT on small problems, would help isolate contributions.
  - Fairness: for metric learning baselines (NCA/ITML/LMNN), you reduce to 200D by PCA; for S-WMD there is no analogous dimensionality constraint. Please discuss whether this benefits S-WMD and, if possible, include versions where A is restricted to rank r to match the 200D setting, or allow baselines to use their native dimensionality when feasible.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The use of entropic OT, NCA-style loss, and gradient derivations are appropriate. Some theoretical details (assumptions on w, regularization, and the exact link between Sinkhorn (u, v) and dual potentials used in Eq. (10)) would benefit from a more rigorous treatment or an appendix proof. Empirical evidence is solid across multiple datasets, with sensible speedups.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, but impacted by notation overload and several typos/formatting issues. The optimization section would benefit from clearer definitions, a short statement of Danskin’s theorem applicability, and a more explicit algorithmic description of how K depends on A during training.

Originality
Moderately novel. The idea of learning the ground metric for EMD/OT is known, but applying it to WMD with pretrained embeddings, jointly with histogram reweighting, and optimizing an NCA objective is a thoughtful and useful combination. The dual-gradient trick for w via Sinkhorn scalings is a nice practical contribution.

Significance
High practical significance. WMD is widely used; a supervised, efficient variant that consistently improves kNN accuracy is valuable to both researchers and practitioners. The approach is generic and could extend to other pretrained embeddings. The empirical gains and efficiency suggest likely adoption.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify constraints/regularization for w and A; consider adding L2 on A and L1/L2 on w, and report ablations.
- Standardize notation (use q for unique words, r for projection rank, avoid reusing p).
- Explicitly define K_A and state use of Danskin’s theorem for gradients.
- Add sensitivity analyses for λ, N (neighbor shortlist size), and batch size.
- Provide ablations for learning only A vs only w.
- Broaden evaluation beyond kNN (e.g., retrieval metrics) and include statistical tests.
- Fix typos/formatting.

Recommendation
Accept (weak accept). The paper offers a practical, well-engineered supervised extension to WMD with strong empirical results and reasonable theory. Addressing clarity and ablations would further strengthen it.

Confidence
High in assessment of methodology and empirical claims; medium in theoretical details of the dual approximation for w pending a fuller derivation.
