This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The core idea is to extend the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which is an unsupervised metric based on optimal transport between word embeddings. The authors propose to make WMD supervised by jointly learning two components: 1) a linear transformation of the word embedding space to tailor word-level similarities to the specific task, and 2) a set of word-specific importance weights to re-scale the document's bag-of-words representation. The learning objective, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), is to minimize a differentiable proxy for the leave-one-out k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) classification error.

A major challenge is that computing the gradient of the WMD is computationally prohibitive. The authors present a key technical contribution to overcome this: they relax the underlying optimal transport problem using the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation not only makes the computation significantly faster (quadratic instead of cubic in the number of unique words) but also ensures the objective is strongly convex, yielding a unique and well-defined gradient. The authors further propose several practical optimizations, including a clever initialization scheme based on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batch gradient descent strategy that prunes the neighborhood search, making the overall algorithm efficient.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a clear and important problem: adapting powerful unsupervised semantic document distances for supervised tasks. While metric learning is a well-established field, its application to the WMD framework is novel and non-trivial. The proposed method of simultaneously learning a transformation in the embedding space and re-weighting the document histograms is elegant and powerful. The resulting S-WMD method demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art in kNN-based document classification, which is a significant contribution.

2.  **Strong Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The main technical hurdle—the intractability of differentiating through the WMD optimization—is solved elegantly by leveraging the Sinkhorn distance relaxation. The derivations for the gradients with respect to the transformation matrix `A` and the importance weights `w` are well-founded. The proposed optimization strategy, combining a principled initialization with an efficient batch gradient computation, shows a thoughtful approach to making the method practical.

3.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength of this work. The authors benchmark their method on eight diverse datasets against an extensive set of 26 competitive baselines. These baselines include classic document representations (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), deep learning models (mSDA), and the original WMD, many of which are paired with established metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The results are very strong, with S-WMD achieving the lowest error on five of the eight datasets and the best average rank overall. The inclusion of t-SNE visualizations and word-weight analysis provides further qualitative support for the method's effectiveness.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It provides the necessary background on word embeddings, WMD, Sinkhorn distance, and NCA in a clear and concise manner. The motivation for the proposed approach is well-articulated, and the methodology is presented logically. The paper provides enough detail to allow for the reproduction of its results, and the authors' promise to release the code is commendable.

### Weaknesses

The paper is very strong, and the following points are minor suggestions for improvement rather than significant flaws.

1.  **Discussion on Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method introduces several hyperparameters, including the Sinkhorn regularization parameter `λ`, learning rates, batch size `B`, and the size of the nearest neighbor set `N`. The paper states that hyperparameters were tuned via Bayesian Optimization, which is a sound approach. However, a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the model to these parameters, particularly the crucial `λ`, would be beneficial. The parameter `λ` controls the trade-off between the speed/stability of the Sinkhorn approximation and its faithfulness to the original WMD. Some insight into how this choice affects performance and convergence would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Choice of Ground Metric:** The paper formulates the distance within the WMD objective using the squared Euclidean norm (`p=2` in Equation 1). The original WMD paper also explored the L1 norm. In the analysis of the CLASSIC dataset results, the authors speculate that the choice of the squared Euclidean distance might be suboptimal. It would be interesting to briefly discuss whether the proposed framework could be extended to other ground metrics and what the technical challenges might be.

3.  **Dependence on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The method's performance relies heavily on the quality of the initial pre-trained word embeddings. While this is an inherent property of all such methods, it would be valuable to briefly mention this limitation and perhaps comment on how performance might vary with different embedding models (e.g., GloVe, FastText) beyond the word2vec embeddings used here.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty:** Proposes the first, to my knowledge, supervised learning framework for the Word Mover's Distance.
*   **Significance:** Achieves new state-of-the-art results on multiple document classification benchmarks, outperforming a very large number of strong baselines. The ideas are likely to be influential.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The use of the Sinkhorn relaxation to enable efficient and stable gradient-based optimization is a clever and crucial technical contribution.
*   **Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is thorough, convincing, and supports the claims of the paper unequivocally.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, making a complex topic accessible.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper has no major weaknesses. The minor points raised above (hyperparameter sensitivity, choice of ground metric) could be addressed with small additions to the text and do not undermine the core contributions.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, significant, and technically sound contribution to the field of document analysis and metric learning. It is well-motivated, clearly explained, and backed by a comprehensive set of experiments. I strongly recommend acceptance.
