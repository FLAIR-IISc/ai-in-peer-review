Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparisons. WMD measures the optimal transport cost of moving one document’s word distribution to another’s in a pretrained word-embedding space. S-WMD learns (i) a linear transformation of the embedding space (A) and (ii) per-word importance weights (w), so that the induced ground cost and document histograms better reflect task-specific notions of similarity. The learning objective follows the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) stochastic leave-one-out kNN criterion. To make training tractable, the paper replaces exact WMD with entropy-regularized optimal transport (Sinkhorn distances), enabling O(q^2) computation per document pair and providing dual variables used to differentiate w. The method uses a strong initialization via supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) (NCA on WCD centroids) and employs mini-batch optimization with a WCD-based neighbor filter.

Experiments across eight datasets compare against 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised variants of BoW, TF-IDF, BM25, LSI, LDA, mSDA, WCD, WMD; and NCA/ITML/LMNN after PCA). S-WMD attains the best kNN error on 5/8 datasets and the best average rank. Visualizations with t-SNE illustrate tighter class clusters under S-WMD, and training times are reported as practical.

The work builds on: word embeddings (word2vec), WMD/EMD/Wasserstein distances, Sinkhorn regularization for fast transport, and metric learning methods (NCA, LMNN, ITML). Related EMD ground-metric learning in vision (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.) is acknowledged; the paper’s novelty is to adapt this idea to text with word embeddings and to jointly learn per-word weights.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling problem: adapting WMD to supervised tasks without leaving the low-dimensional embedding space is well motivated.
- Solid technical approach: combining NCA with entropy-regularized OT yields a differentiable and efficient surrogate; the use of Sinkhorn dual variables to backpropagate into w is neat.
- Practical engineering: strong initialization (S-WCD), batch training, and neighbor filtering provide a sensible and effective training recipe.
- Extensive empirical evaluation: many baselines (including supervised metric learners) across diverse datasets; S-WMD generally improves over WMD and other methods, with good training times.
- Interpretability: learned word weights are meaningful; qualitative plots support the claims.

Weaknesses
- Scope of evaluation: results are limited to kNN classification. It would strengthen the case to show robustness across other tasks (e.g., retrieval/IR, clustering) or to derive a kernel from S-WMD for linear classifiers.
- Ablations and sensitivity: limited analysis of the contributions of A vs. w, the effect of λ (entropy regularization), neighborhood size N, batch size B, and choice of ground cost (L2 vs. cosine). While S-WMD init. is shown, deeper ablations would clarify where gains come from.
- Theoretical justification: gradients for w rely on approximate dual variables from Sinkhorn; a discussion of approximation bias and conditions under which this is accurate would be helpful.
- Notational clarity and minor presentation issues: p is used both as the norm exponent and as the number of unique words; Section “.2 Optimization” is misnumbered; some references and footnotes are awkwardly placed; dataset details and hyperparameters are scattered.
- Baselines: modern supervised text baselines (e.g., fastText, linear SVM/logistic regression on TF-IDF, doc2vec, or simple CNN/RNN) are not reported; at least fastText/linear SVM would be relevant and computationally fair.
- Generalization and scalability: inference still requires OT per query; a discussion of test-time speed versus WMD (and possible pruning/approximations) would be valuable, especially for long documents.

Quality
The method is technically sound and well aligned with prior literature. The combination of NCA with Sinkhorn OT and the derivation of gradients for A and w are reasonable; however, the use of approximate dual variables for w deserves more discussion. Empirical results are convincing overall, though additional ablations and broader baselines would strengthen the conclusions.

Clarity
Generally clear, but notation and sectioning need polishing. The algorithm and gradients are explained at a high level; more detail (e.g., constraints/regularization on A and w, positivity of w, normalization) would improve reproducibility. Figures and qualitative analyses help.

Originality
Moderate. Learning the OT ground metric is not new; the novelty is in applying it to WMD for text with joint learning of word weights and leveraging Sinkhorn for training. The initialization and training heuristics are practical contributions.

Significance
Good. WMD is widely used; a supervised, efficient variant that consistently improves kNN performance is useful for text classification/retrieval, especially in low-data settings leveraging pretrained embeddings.

Constructive feedback and questions
- Please provide ablations isolating the effect of A vs. w (e.g., learn only A, only w, and both), and sensitivity to λ, N, and batch size. Include a study comparing L2 vs. cosine ground costs.
- Clarify constraints and regularization: Is w constrained to be nonnegative? Is there sparsity/entropy regularization on w to prevent overfitting? Is A constrained to be low-rank or PSD? Report typical ranks used.
- Expand baselines to include fastText and linear SVM/logistic regression on TF-IDF; optionally include a simple CNN/LSTM if feasible. Also compare against SIF-weighted centroids and supervised doc2vec.
- Report test-time complexity and wall-clock costs for kNN with S-WMD versus WMD (with and without pruning). Discuss approximate nearest neighbor search with OT lower bounds (e.g., RWMD) as a deployment path.
- Improve clarity: fix notational overload of p; correct section numbering; provide a succinct table of hyperparameters and chosen values; describe datasets, preprocessing, and split protocols in more detail.
- Provide more details on the dual-variable derivation for w (α*, β* from u, v). Include a brief justification or cite theory on the accuracy of these gradients under entropic smoothing, and a sensitivity study for λ.
- Consider reporting additional metrics (macro/micro-F1) for imbalanced datasets and adding variance/confidence intervals across all datasets.
- Discuss limitations: e.g., bag-of-words OT ignores order; extremely long documents may be costly; domain shift across corpora.

Pros
- Well-motivated problem and method; principled use of Sinkhorn OT for efficiency.
- Strong empirical gains over many baselines; code available.
- Interpretable learned weights; practical training recipe with good runtimes.

Cons
- Evaluation mostly limited to kNN; missing some strong supervised baselines.
- Limited ablations/sensitivity; theoretical aspects of gradient approximation underexplored.
- Minor clarity/notation issues.

Recommendation: Accept (borderline strong). The paper offers a practical and effective supervised extension of WMD with sound methodology and solid empirical evidence. Addressing the suggested clarifications and ablations would further strengthen the contribution. Confidence: high.
