

# Review: Supervised Word Mover's Distance

## Summary
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an improvement to the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) for document classification. WMD computes document distances by finding the optimal transport cost of moving words from one document to another in word embedding space. The authors extend this by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings (A) and word-specific importance weights (w) to make WMD more suitable for classification tasks. They address the computational challenge of WMD by using the Sinkhorn distance approximation, enabling efficient training. The method is evaluated across 8 datasets with 26 baselines, demonstrating consistent improvements over unsupervised methods and other document representations.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of S-WMD, including the loss function, gradient derivations, and optimization algorithm. They correctly identify the cubic complexity of WMD and justify their use of the Sinkhorn approximation (Cuturi, 2013), which reduces complexity to quadratic. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with 8 diverse datasets, 26 baselines, and detailed analysis of performance across different document types. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as S-WMD's underperformance on BBCSPORT and CLASSIC datasets, and provide reasonable explanations (long document lengths and poor initialization, respectively). The implementation details are sufficient for reproduction, and the training times (Table 3) demonstrate practical efficiency.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section effectively introduces WMD and related metric learning techniques. The mathematical formulations are clear, with appropriate references to prior work (e.g., Cuturi's Sinkhorn distance). The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and implementable. The results section uses clear tables (Table 2) and visualizations (Figures 1-2) to communicate key findings. The authors make a good effort to contextualize their work within existing literature, particularly in the related work section. The writing is concise yet comprehensive, making the paper accessible to NIPS readers familiar with machine learning and NLP.

## Originality
The core contribution—supervised learning of the WMD metric—is novel. While metric learning has been extensively studied, applying it to WMD in this specific way (learning both linear transformations and word weights) represents a meaningful extension. The authors clearly distinguish their work from prior art, particularly noting that Wang et al. (2015) learned metrics within EMD but didn't consider histogram re-weighting. The combination of Sinkhorn approximation for computational efficiency and supervised learning for performance gains is a novel application of existing techniques. The paper's novelty is sufficiently distinct from prior NIPS papers, as confirmed by reviewing recent proceedings.

## Significance
The work addresses a significant problem in text classification: improving document distance metrics for better classification performance. S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results across 5/8 datasets and outperforms all 26 baselines on average. The computational efficiency (Table 3) makes it practical for real-world applications, with training times comparable to or better than baseline metric learning methods. The approach is broadly applicable to text classification tasks, from sentiment analysis to news categorization. The visualization of learned word weights (Figure 2) provides intuitive insights into how the model distinguishes classes, adding to the method's practical value.

## Strengths
1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper correctly identifies the computational bottleneck of WMD and provides a well-justified solution using Sinkhorn approximation.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The 8-dataset, 26-baseline evaluation is thorough and convincing, with appropriate statistical analysis.
3. **Practical efficiency**: The training times (Table 3) demonstrate that the method is not just theoretically sound but also practically feasible.
4. **Clear presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making complex concepts accessible.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited analysis of failures**: The paper doesn't deeply analyze why S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT and CLASSIC. While the authors mention "long document lengths" and "poor initialization," a more detailed analysis would strengthen the work.
2. **Insufficient word weight analysis**: Figure 2 shows top words with high weights, but the paper lacks deeper analysis of how these weights correlate with class labels across datasets.
3. **Comparison with metric learning methods**: The comparison with NCA, ITML, and LMNN is somewhat indirect (applied to different document representations rather than directly to S-WMD), limiting the ability to isolate the contribution of the S-WMD framework.

## Overall Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of document representation and metric learning. The technical quality is excellent, the evaluation is comprehensive, and the method is both novel and practical. While the paper could benefit from deeper analysis of the failure cases and more detailed examination of the word importance weights, these are minor weaknesses in an otherwise outstanding work. The paper meets all NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper advances the state of the art in document distance metrics with a well-motivated, technically sound approach. The results demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods, and the computational efficiency makes the method broadly applicable. This work will likely be of significant interest to the NIPS community working on text classification, metric learning, and natural language processing.
