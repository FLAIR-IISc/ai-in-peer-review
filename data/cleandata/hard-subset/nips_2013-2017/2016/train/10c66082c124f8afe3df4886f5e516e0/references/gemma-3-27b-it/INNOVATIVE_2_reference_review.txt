This paper presents a novel approach to improving the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by learning a metric within the word embedding space, coupled with word-specific importance weights. The core idea – learning a transformation of word embeddings *and* re-weighting word frequencies to optimize for a downstream classification task – is a compelling and potentially significant contribution. This departs from the typical paradigm of learning document representations and directly tackles the distance metric itself, leveraging the strengths of WMD while addressing its limitations. The authors skillfully combine metric learning techniques (NCA) with the efficient Sinkhorn distance approximation to make the optimization tractable, a clever engineering feat. 

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, though the sheer number of baselines makes it a bit dense. The motivation is sound – existing document distance methods often struggle with semantic similarity – and the authors effectively position their work within the broader context of metric learning and optimal transport. The experimental results are particularly strong, showing consistent improvements across a diverse set of datasets and comparisons to a very comprehensive benchmark. The visualization of learned word weights is insightful, demonstrating that the model is indeed learning meaningful representations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of learning a word embedding transformation *and* re-weighting word frequencies within the WMD framework is a genuinely novel idea. This isn't simply incremental improvement; it’s a different approach to the problem.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms a large number of baselines across multiple datasets. The dominance in the results is convincing.
* **Efficient Implementation:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance approximation is crucial for making the supervised learning of WMD feasible.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the technical details, including the gradient computations.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The inclusion of 26 baselines is commendable and provides a thorough assessment of the method's performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, the theoretical underpinnings could be further strengthened. A deeper analysis of the properties of the learned metric and its relationship to the underlying word embeddings would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors mention Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning, but a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings would be beneficial.
* **Scalability:** Although the authors address computational complexity, a more detailed analysis of the scalability to very large datasets (e.g., those with millions of documents) would be useful. 
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While results are presented, the paper doesn't delve into *why* S-WMD fails on certain datasets (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON).  Understanding these cases could provide further insights.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The novelty and potential impact of the proposed approach are high. This paper introduces a genuinely new way to think about document distance learning.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods.
* The technical details are well-explained and the implementation appears sound.
* The authors address a critical limitation of WMD – its lack of task-specific adaptation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical justification is relatively limited.
* While the experiments are thorough, deeper error analysis could provide further insights.
* The scalability to truly massive datasets remains an open question.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach. While the theoretical analysis could be more detailed and some further analysis is warranted, the significant empirical improvements and clever engineering make this a valuable contribution to the field. The paper's strengths far outweigh its weaknesses. I believe this paper is a good fit for NIPS and recommend acceptance.  I am willing to overlook the minor theoretical gaps given the significant practical impact and innovative nature of the work. This is precisely the type of bold, novel approach that NIPS should be promoting.
