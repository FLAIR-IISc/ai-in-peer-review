Summary
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparison. The method learns (i) a linear transformation A in the pre-trained word embedding space and (ii) a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms, such that the induced WMD better aligns with class labels. The objective is an NCA-style stochastic neighbor loss optimizing leave-one-out kNN accuracy. To make training tractable, the authors replace exact OT with the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance to approximate both the transport plan and dual variables, initialize A with a supervised variant of the word centroid distance (S-WCD), and compute gradients only against a WCD-selected neighbor subset. Experiments on 8 datasets against 26 baselines report improved kNN classification accuracy and reasonable training times; code is provided.

Relation to prior work
The paper is grounded in: WMD (Kusner et al.), metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML), and entropy-regularized OT (Cuturi). It is close to supervised EMD ground metric learning in vision (e.g., Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.), but differs by operating in a low-dimensional word embedding space and by jointly learning word-importance weights on text histograms. Using Sinkhorn to enable end-to-end optimization through an OT-based objective is consistent with recent OT literature.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: learning task-specific ground distances in WMD is sensible and practically relevant.
- Methodologically coherent: combining A and w provides flexibility; initialization via S-WCD is thoughtful; Sinkhorn-based gradients enable scalability.
- Empirical breadth: 8 datasets and 26 baselines is substantial for a metric learning paper; public code is a plus.
- Practicality: batching and neighbor pruning yield modest training times.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Theoretical justification is thin for key approximations:
  - Gradients are taken through a relaxed Sinkhorn problem (λ fixed to 10) and computed on a WCD-pruned neighbor set. There is no analysis of bias introduced by (i) relaxation (distance and dual approximation) and (ii) truncation of pab to a small neighbor set. Unbiasedness or consistency of the stochastic gradients is not established.
  - The derivation of the dual variables α*,β* from u,v is sketched but not fully justified; the formula includes notational ambiguities (division by “p” that elsewhere denotes different quantities).
- Constraints/regularization:
  - The histogram reweighting w affects normalized counts. If w can be negative, d̃ is ill-defined; if w is constrained to be nonnegative, this should be stated along with the parameterization (e.g., softplus) and any regularization. Without constraints or regularization, w (size n) risks overfitting.
  - Similarly, A can grow without bound unless the objective implicitly normalizes scale; regularization on A (e.g., Frobenius penalty) is not discussed.
- Experimental methodology:
  - The evaluation is limited to kNN classification with Euclidean or WMD-like distances. Claims of state-of-the-art classification would be stronger with comparisons to standard supervised classifiers (e.g., linear SVM/LogReg on TF-IDF, fastText, CNN/RNN baselines, doc2vec).
  - Baseline fairness: many supervised baselines are PCA-reduced to 200 dimensions, while S-WMD operates in the original embedding dimension; dimensionality reduction may disadvantage some baselines. LDA is trained transductively.
  - Missing ablations: no study disentangles the effects of A vs w; no sensitivity analysis for λ (Sinkhorn), neighbor set size N, batch size B, or choice of p=1 vs p=2 in the ground metric. The good performance of “S-WMD init.” suggests much of the gain might come from the initialization; quantifying the incremental benefit is important.
  - Test-time cost is unclear: with kNN, computing S-WMD to all training documents can be expensive. Do the authors use Sinkhorn at test time? Is there a fast filtering scheme? Reporting inference time would help.
- Notational clarity issues: p, q, n are overloaded; a section heading “.2 Optimization” appears misnumbered; the α,β expressions contain ambiguous constants.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-organized, but the above notational inconsistencies, missing constraints on w, and abbreviated derivations of the dual gradients reduce reproducibility. More algorithmic detail (regularization, constraints, stopping criteria, choice of r, initialization of w positivity) would improve clarity.

Originality
The combination is incremental: supervised learning of an EMD/Wasserstein ground metric has precedent in vision, and NCA/Sinkhorn are established. Novelty here is the specific instantiation for text with word embeddings and learned histogram reweighting, plus practical training tricks. This is a reasonable, if not revolutionary, contribution.

Significance
If the reported gains over strong unsupervised distances and metric-learning baselines hold broadly, S-WMD could be useful in retrieval and kNN-style settings. However, without comparisons to widely used supervised text classifiers and without ablations/sensitivity, it is hard to assess broader impact. Practical deployment also depends on test-time efficiency, which is not fully addressed.

Questions for the authors
- Are w’s entries constrained to be nonnegative? How is this enforced in optimization? Is there regularization on w and A?
- What is r (the learned dimensionality)? How is it chosen?
- Do you use Sinkhorn at test time? What are test-time costs vs WMD? Any filtering/indexing?
- Sensitivity to λ, N, B, and to the choice of p (L1 vs L2 ground metric)?
- Ablation: performance with only A, only w, both?
- How robust is S-WMD to different pretrained embeddings or to domain mismatch?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant problem with a principled approach leveraging modern OT.
- Strong empirical results across multiple datasets and many baselines.
- Public code; practical training procedure.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited theoretical support for key approximations and sampling heuristics.
- Missing constraints/regularization details raise overfitting and well-posedness concerns.
- Evaluation scoped narrowly to kNN; fairness of baselines and lack of ablations weaken the empirical case.
- Clarity/notation issues and some transductive choices.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is promising and potentially useful, but I would like to see stronger theoretical grounding of the optimization, clearer problem formulation (constraints/regularization), ablations and sensitivity analyses, fairer and broader baselines (including standard supervised classifiers), and test-time efficiency results.

Confidence
High (familiar with metric learning, OT/Sinkhorn, and text classification).
