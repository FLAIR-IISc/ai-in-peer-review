

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

## Summary
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method that extends the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word-specific importance weights. Building on Kusner et al.'s WMD (2015), which computes document distances as optimal transport between word embeddings, the authors introduce supervision to make the distance metric task-specific. The approach leverages the Sinkhorn distance approximation to handle computational complexity, and learns the metric by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out classification error. The method is evaluated across 8 datasets and compared against 26 baselines, including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) and metric learning techniques.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with solid theoretical grounding. The authors provide clear derivations of the gradient with respect to both the linear transformation matrix A and the word importance vector w. The experimental design is comprehensive, evaluating performance across 8 diverse document datasets and comparing against 26 baselines. The results demonstrate clear improvements over unsupervised WMD (with 5/8 datasets achieving the lowest error rates) and show that supervision consistently benefits the metric learning approach. The computational efficiency claims are supported with empirical evidence in Table 3.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with WMD or metric learning. The mathematical formulations are clear and well-explained, with a helpful algorithm description (Algorithm 1) and supporting visualizations (Figures 1-2). The authors effectively distinguish their approach from related work in Section 5.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by extending WMD to a supervised setting. While WMD itself was a significant contribution (Kusner et al., 2015), the authors successfully address the gap of making it task-specific. The combination of metric learning with the optimal transport framework for word embeddings is novel, and the paper properly contextualizes this work relative to related approaches (e.g., Wang et al., 2015).

**Significance:** The method demonstrates clear practical value, improving state-of-the-art results on multiple document classification tasks. The computational efficiency (making it competitive with standard metric learning approaches) and the ability to leverage pre-trained word embeddings without requiring task-specific training make the approach broadly applicable. The paper provides evidence that supervision significantly improves document distances across diverse text classification tasks.

## Weaknesses

**Limited theoretical analysis:** While the paper provides a solid computational framework, it lacks deeper theoretical analysis of the generalization properties of the proposed metric. It would be valuable to include a discussion of how the learned metric might generalize to unseen documents or how the choice of λ affects the generalization performance.

**Dataset limitations:** The paper acknowledges that S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON, but provides only superficial explanations. A more thorough analysis of why the method struggles with these particular datasets (e.g., document length, class distribution, vocabulary characteristics) would strengthen the contribution.

**Comparison to deep learning approaches:** While the paper compares against 26 baselines, it does not include recent deep learning-based text classification methods (e.g., BERT, ELMo) that have become the new state-of-the-art in many NLP tasks. Given the rapid evolution of text representation learning, this omission is notable.

**Parameter sensitivity:** The paper uses λ=10 as a fixed parameter without thorough analysis of its sensitivity. A more comprehensive parameter study would help readers understand the robustness of the approach.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contribution is significant, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the method is well-motivated and clearly presented. While the paper has some limitations (particularly regarding comparisons to state-of-the-art deep learning methods), these do not undermine the core contribution, which is a novel and effective approach to supervised document distance learning.

**Suggestions for improvement:**
1. Include a brief comparison with recent deep learning text classification approaches (even if only as a limited experiment) to better position the work in the current landscape.
2. Expand the discussion of the failure cases (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON) to provide more insight into the method's limitations and potential improvements.
3. Conduct a more thorough analysis of parameter sensitivity, particularly for λ and the batch size.
4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the method might integrate with modern contextual word embeddings (e.g., BERT) to leverage more advanced word representations.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of document representation learning. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing approaches.
