### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by Kusner et al. (2015), an unsupervised metric that calculates the minimum cost to "transport" words from one document to another in a pre-trained word embedding space. While WMD has shown state-of-the-art performance in unsupervised kNN classification, its distance is task-agnostic.

The key idea of this paper is to incorporate label information to learn a more discriminative distance. The authors propose two supervised modifications to the WMD framework: 1) learning a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space to warp the geometry of word vectors for the specific task, and 2) learning a word importance vector `w` that re-weights the contribution of each word in a document.

The learning objective is formulated to minimize a differentiable proxy of the k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) leave-one-out (LOO) error, adopting the stochastic neighborhood-based loss function from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) by Goldberger et al. (2005). A significant technical challenge is the high computational cost and non-differentiability of the WMD calculation, which is based on an exact optimal transport problem. To make the optimization feasible, the authors replace the exact WMD with its entropically regularized version, solvable efficiently via the Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm (Cuturi, 2013). This relaxation provides a smooth, differentiable approximation of the distance, for which the authors derive the gradients with respect to both `A` and `w`.

To further improve efficiency, the paper proposes a practical optimization strategy involving a clever initialization of the matrix `A` using NCA on the much faster Word Centroid Distance (WCD), followed by batch gradient descent. The batch-wise gradient computation is also accelerated by restricting the NCA probability calculations to a small set of nearest neighbors for each document. The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation on 8 text classification datasets, comparing S-WMD against 26 baselines, and demonstrate that their method achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive kNN classification performance.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by extensive and rigorous experiments.

*   The approach of using the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable proxy for WMD is theoretically sound and well-motivated, building on recent advances in computational optimal transport. The derivations for the gradients with respect to the learned transformation `A` and weights `w` appear correct and are a key technical contribution.
*   The empirical evaluation is comprehensive. The method is tested on 8 diverse datasets against a very large number of strong baselines, including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with established metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN). This thorough comparison provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The authors are careful to address the computational challenges of their approach, proposing several practical solutions (initialization, batching, neighbor filtering) that make the method viable. The inclusion of training time analysis in Table 3 adds to the paper's completeness.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

*   The motivation for adding supervision to WMD is clearly articulated. The background section provides the necessary context on word2vec, WMD, Sinkhorn distance, and NCA, making the paper self-contained.
*   The proposed method, S-WMD, is developed logically, with each technical choice justified. The problem formulation, loss function, and gradient computations are presented clearly.
*   The experimental setup is detailed, and the results in tables and figures (e.g., the t-SNE visualizations and word cloud) are well-presented and effectively support the paper's conclusions.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel and significant extension of the WMD framework.

*   While individual components have been explored before (NCA loss, Sinkhorn distances, learning a ground metric for EMD in computer vision), their synthesis to create a supervised document distance metric for text is novel.
*   The idea of simultaneously learning a transformation of the word embedding space and a set of word importance weights is an original contribution that provides the model with significant flexibility to adapt to different classification tasks.
*   The paper clearly situates its contribution with respect to prior work, including both unsupervised document distances like WMD and traditional metric learning methods that operate on fixed document feature vectors.

**Significance:** The results presented are important and likely to have a notable impact.

*   The paper demonstrates a new state-of-the-art for kNN-based document classification, a fundamental task in NLP and information retrieval. The superior performance across multiple datasets suggests that S-WMD is a powerful and robust method.
*   The work provides a new paradigm for supervised document similarity learning, moving beyond learning metrics on fixed, high-dimensional document representations to learning them directly within the low-dimensional word embedding space. This approach is elegant and computationally advantageous.
*   Researchers and practitioners working on text classification, semantic similarity, and information retrieval are likely to use and build upon these ideas. The provided code further enhances the potential for impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The method's performance is only evaluated in the context of kNN classification. While the goal is to learn a distance metric optimized for kNN, it would be interesting to see if the learned metric `DA,w` could be beneficial for other distance-based algorithms, such as clustering or retrieval ranking.
*   The paper fixes the Sinkhorn regularization parameter `λ=10`, stating it provides a good trade-off between speed and accuracy. While this is reasonable, a brief analysis of the method's sensitivity to this key hyperparameter would have strengthened the paper by providing a better understanding of its robustness.
*   While much faster than a naive approach, the computational complexity still scales quadratically with the number of unique words per document. This might limit its applicability to tasks involving extremely long documents.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel, well-motivated, and powerful method for supervised document distance learning that significantly advances the state-of-the-art.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The technical approach is solid, leveraging recent advances in optimal transport to create a tractable and effective learning algorithm.
3.  **Empirical Strength:** The claims are backed by extensive and convincing experiments against a wide array of strong baselines on multiple datasets.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and easy to understand, making the contributions accessible.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
1.  **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The evaluation is confined to kNN classification, leaving the utility of the learned metric for other tasks unexplored.
2.  **Incremental Aspects:** The core ideas of NCA loss and Sinkhorn relaxation are drawn from prior work, making the contribution a novel synthesis rather than a completely new theoretical concept.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a complete, high-quality piece of research with significant contributions to the field of machine learning for text analysis.
