

# NIPS Review: "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

## Summary

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that learns a task-specific metric for document classification. While WMD is an unsupervised distance metric that leverages word embeddings to compute the optimal transport cost between documents, S-WMD introduces two key innovations: (1) a linear transformation of the word embeddings to better align with semantic similarity, and (2) word-specific importance weights to emphasize words that are most discriminative for the classification task. The authors address the computational challenge of learning this metric by using the Sinkhorn distance approximation, which allows them to efficiently compute gradients and optimize the model. The paper evaluates S-WMD across 8 datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on 5 of them compared to 26 baselines.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a clear methodology and thorough experimental evaluation. The authors provide a well-justified approach to the problem by leveraging the Sinkhorn distance approximation, which makes the computational complexity tractable. The gradient computation, while complex, is carefully derived and justified. The experimental section is comprehensive, with results on 8 diverse datasets and 26 baselines, including both unsupervised and supervised methods.

The paper acknowledges the limitations of their approach, particularly noting that the squared Euclidean distance between word vectors might not be optimal for all datasets (e.g., the CLASSIC dataset). The authors also discuss the computational efficiency of their method, with training times comparable to or better than baseline metric learning approaches.

However, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of the method. While the computational aspects are well-handled, there's no theoretical justification for why the learned metric would improve classification performance or analysis of the approximation error introduced by the Sinkhorn distance. The authors could have strengthened their theoretical foundation by discussing the relationship between the approximate solution and the exact WMD.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, the background section provides sufficient context for WMD and metric learning, and the proposed method is described in detail with clear mathematical formulations. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is particularly helpful for understanding the optimization process.

The paper is well-structured and flows logically from problem statement to method to experiments. The figures (particularly Figure 1 showing t-SNE embeddings) effectively illustrate the improvements of S-WMD over WMD. The supplementary material is mentioned as providing additional details, which is a good practice.

The authors provide sufficient implementation details for reproducibility, including the use of λ=10 for the Sinkhorn distance, batch size B=32, and neighbor set size N=200. The paper is clearly written and accessible to experts in the field.

## Originality

The paper makes a clear and meaningful contribution to the field. While WMD [19] was a significant contribution, it was unsupervised, and the authors correctly identify that document similarity should be task-specific. The combination of metric learning with WMD is novel, particularly the addition of the word importance weights (w) which provides extra flexibility beyond standard metric learning approaches.

The related work section adequately covers previous metric learning approaches and the WMD framework, and the paper clearly positions S-WMD in relation to these works. The authors properly cite relevant work, including the closely related work by Wang et al. [35] who learn a metric within EMD ground distance but do not consider histogram re-weighting.

This work is sufficiently different from previous NIPS papers. The WMD paper itself was presented at ICML 2015, and while there have been some metric learning papers at NIPS, the specific combination of supervised metric learning with WMD is novel. The authors properly distinguish their work from prior contributions.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. Document distance metrics are fundamental to many text retrieval tasks, and improving these metrics can have broad impact. The paper demonstrates that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results on 5 out of 8 datasets, with significant improvements over unsupervised baselines (e.g., 51% relative error reduction on OHSUMED compared to LSI).

The computational efficiency of S-WMD is particularly noteworthy, as the training time is comparable or better than baseline metric learning approaches. This makes the method practical for real-world applications. The authors also provide a compelling visualization (Figure 1) showing that S-WMD produces better class clustering than WMD.

The approach could influence how document distance metrics are developed in the future, particularly for task-specific applications. The method's ability to leverage large unsupervised word embeddings while still incorporating task-specific supervision is a valuable contribution.

## Strengths

1. Clear and well-motivated extension to the WMD framework
2. Novel combination of linear metric learning with histogram re-weighting for document distances
3. Practical computational approach using Sinkhorn distance approximation
4. Comprehensive experimental evaluation across 8 datasets with 26 baselines
5. Strong empirical results with state-of-the-art performance on 5/8 datasets
6. Excellent clarity and organization of the paper

## Weaknesses

1. Lack of theoretical analysis of the method's convergence or approximation guarantees
2. Limited discussion of sensitivity to the choice of λ in Sinkhorn distance (only mentions λ=10 without analysis)
3. Insufficient explanation for why S-WMD underperforms on CLASSIC compared to WMD
4. Limited exploration of how the method might perform with different word embedding techniques (only uses word2vec)
5. The initialization method (using NCA on WCD) is described but its criticality to performance isn't discussed

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of document distance metrics. The empirical results are strong and demonstrate clear advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches. While there are some weaknesses (particularly the lack of theoretical analysis), the paper's strengths far outweigh these limitations.

The paper addresses an important problem in text classification and provides a practical, efficient solution that could be widely adopted. The combination of supervised learning with the WMD framework is novel and well-executed, and the experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.

## Final Comments

This paper presents a valuable extension to the Word Mover's Distance that incorporates task-specific supervision. The method is computationally efficient, well-motivated, and achieves state-of-the-art results across multiple document classification tasks. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of text representation and document distance metrics.

The paper meets all the NIPS review criteria at a high level. The quality is excellent, the clarity is exceptional, the originality is strong, and the significance is substantial. The weaknesses noted are minor in the context of the overall contribution.

I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and would be of interest to researchers working on text representation, metric learning, and document classification.
