### Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost of "transporting" words from one document to the other within a pre-trained word embedding space. While WMD is a powerful unsupervised metric, its utility is limited by the fact that the underlying word embedding and distance function are not optimized for any specific classification task.

The authors propose to learn a supervised metric by incorporating two learnable components into the WMD framework: 1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, which adapts the word representations to the task, and 2) a word importance vector `w`, which re-weighs words based on their relevance for the classification problem. The objective is to learn `A` and `w` such that the resulting S-WMD distance is small for documents of the same class and large for documents of different classes.

A key challenge is the prohibitive computational cost and non-differentiability of the standard WMD, which is based on an exact Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) calculation. To overcome this, the authors make several crucial technical contributions. They replace the EMD with its entropy-regularized version, the Sinkhorn distance, which is differentiable and can be computed much more efficiently (O(q²) vs. O(q³)). They then formulate a learning objective based on Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), which provides a differentiable proxy for the k-NN classification error. Finally, they devise an efficient training scheme using mini-batch stochastic gradient descent, a smart initialization strategy based on a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD), and a neighbor-filtering heuristic to further reduce computation. The authors conduct a comprehensive experimental evaluation on eight text classification datasets, comparing S-WMD against 26 strong baselines, and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a clear and important problem: how to incorporate supervision into the powerful WMD framework. WMD was a significant advance in unsupervised document similarity, and this paper presents a well-motivated and logical next step. The ability to tailor the document distance to a specific task is highly valuable, and the proposed method is likely to be widely used and built upon by researchers and practitioners.

2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The authors provide an elegant and practical solution to a non-trivial technical problem. Directly optimizing WMD is computationally intractable. The combination of the Sinkhorn relaxation for differentiability and speed, the NCA loss function for a soft k-NN objective, and the efficient batch training strategy constitutes a solid and complete contribution. This makes the method computationally feasible, which is crucial for its practical adoption.

3.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength of this paper. The authors compare their method against an extensive set of 26 baselines across 8 diverse datasets. The baselines include classic methods (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), modern neural methods (mSDA), and the original WMD, combined with several strong metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). S-WMD achieves the best performance on 5 of the 8 datasets and the best average rank overall, clearly demonstrating its superiority and advancing the state-of-the-art in k-NN based document classification.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It provides the necessary background on word embeddings, WMD, Sinkhorn distance, and NCA before clearly laying out the proposed method. The derivations for the gradients are presented, and the overall algorithm is summarized effectively. The experimental setup is detailed, and the results are presented clearly in tables and figures.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Originality:** While the synthesis of the components is novel and effective, the core ideas (WMD, Sinkhorn distance, NCA, linear metric learning) are pre-existing. The idea of learning a ground metric for EMD has also been explored in other domains, such as in computer vision (e.g., Wang et al., 2012). The paper acknowledges this related work but could be more explicit about how its joint learning of a space transformation (`A`) and histogram re-weighting (`w`) for text specifically distinguishes it from these prior efforts.

2.  **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** S-WMD does not achieve the top rank on three datasets (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON). The discussion provides plausible but somewhat speculative reasons, such as long document lengths favoring TF-IDF or a "poor initialization". A deeper analysis of these cases would strengthen the paper. For instance, does the Sinkhorn approximation become less accurate for certain data distributions? Or is the linear transformation (`A`) insufficient for some tasks? Investigating the properties of the data or the learned parameters on these datasets could provide more valuable insights.

3.  **Superficial Visualization of Learned Metric:** The word cloud in Figure 2 provides some intuition that the learned weights `w` are reasonable. However, it offers little insight into what the more complex linear transformation `A` is actually doing to the semantic geometry of the word embedding space. Visualizing how the relationships between key word vectors change after applying `A` could provide a more profound understanding of the learned metric.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and timely problem by making the state-of-the-art WMD framework supervised.
*   Presents a strong, well-executed technical solution to the significant computational challenges involved.
*   Features an exceptionally thorough and convincing experimental validation that demonstrates a clear advance in the state-of-the-art.
*   The paper is clear, well-structured, and provides enough detail for reproducibility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The work is primarily a novel combination of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new idea.
*   The analysis of when and why the method might not be optimal is somewhat limited.

**Conclusion:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of text analysis and metric learning. The proposed S-WMD method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically shown to be highly effective. Despite the component ideas being known, their synthesis into a practical and powerful algorithm for supervised document distance learning is a valuable and non-trivial achievement. The strengths, particularly the impressive experimental results and the practical solution to the computational hurdles, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. This paper clearly advances the state-of-the-art and is an excellent fit for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
