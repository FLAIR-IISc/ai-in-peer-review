### Summary

This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds upon the recently introduced Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which leverages word embeddings to compute the optimal transport cost between documents. While WMD is powerful, it is entirely unsupervised. The authors address this limitation by introducing supervision in two ways: 1) learning a linear transformation of the underlying word embedding space to better align with the supervised task, and 2) learning a vector of word-specific importance weights to emphasize or de-emphasize words based on their discriminative power.

The core technical challenge is that optimizing the WMD is computationally expensive and non-differentiable, making it difficult to embed within a gradient-based learning framework. The authors' key contribution is to overcome this by replacing the exact optimal transport problem with its entropy-regularized version, known as the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation is not only faster to compute (quadratic vs. cubic complexity) but also yields a smooth, differentiable objective. The authors derive the gradients for both the linear transformation and the word weights and propose an efficient optimization scheme using a clever initialization based on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD), batch gradient descent, and nearest-neighbor filtering. The method is evaluated on 8 text classification datasets, where it is shown to outperform an extensive set of 26 baselines, including unsupervised WMD and various document representations combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

### Review

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical motivation and the extensive experimental results. The main technical contribution—using the differentiable Sinkhorn distance as a surrogate for WMD within a metric learning objective—is elegant and effective. This makes the challenging problem of learning a supervised transport-based metric tractable. The derivation of the gradients for both the embedding transformation `A` and the word weights `w` appears sound. The proposed optimization strategy, including the WCD-based initialization and batch-wise nearest-neighbor approximation, is practical and well-justified for improving efficiency. The work is a complete and self-contained contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors provide a clear background on word embeddings, WMD, and Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), which makes the paper accessible. The motivation for S-WMD is compellingly presented, and the technical sections logically build upon one another, from the definition of the S-WMD distance to the loss function and the efficient gradient computation. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail, and the results in Table 2 are comprehensive and easy to interpret. The inclusion of visualizations (t-SNE plots and word clouds) provides excellent qualitative support for the method's effectiveness.

**Originality:** This work is highly original. While metric learning and optimal transport for documents are established fields, this paper is the first to successfully combine them in this way. Extending the unsupervised WMD to a supervised setting is a novel and significant contribution. The idea of simultaneously learning a transformation in the word embedding space and re-weighting the document histograms is also a novel combination that provides additional flexibility. The work is clearly differentiated from prior art, such as Wang et al. [35], by its focus on text, its use of the NCA loss, and the inclusion of word importance weights. The paper adequately cites and builds upon relevant work in metric learning, word embeddings, and optimal transport.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is significant. The WMD has been shown to be a very strong baseline for document similarity, and this paper presents a principled and effective way to improve it for specific supervised tasks. The strong performance across a wide range of datasets suggests that S-WMD could become a new state-of-the-art standard for kNN-based document classification. Researchers and practitioners working on text classification, retrieval, and other tasks requiring nuanced document similarity will likely find this method highly useful. The technique of using a regularized, differentiable optimal transport distance within a larger learning system may also inspire similar approaches in other domains, such as computer vision.

### Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):

*   **Novelty and Impact:** The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated method for supervised document metric learning, effectively extending the powerful but unsupervised WMD framework.
*   **Strong Technical Contribution:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance relaxation to enable efficient, gradient-based optimization is a clever and crucial contribution that makes the approach feasible.
*   **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The method is validated through a comprehensive set of experiments against 26 strong baselines on 8 diverse datasets, demonstrating superior performance on average and setting a new state of the art on several of them.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making the problem, solution, and results easy to understand.

### Arguments for Rejection (Cons) / Suggestions for Improvement:

While this is a very strong paper, the following points could further improve it:

*   **Ablation Study:** The proposed S-WMD model learns two components: a linear transformation `A` and word importance weights `w`. An ablation study analyzing the individual contribution of each component would be highly valuable. For instance, what is the performance when only `A` is learned (i.e., `w` is fixed to all ones) or when only `w` is learned (i.e., `A` is fixed to identity)? This would provide deeper insight into which component drives the performance gains.
*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The method relies on the regularization parameter `λ` for the Sinkhorn approximation. The authors state that `λ=10` offers a good trade-off, but a brief analysis or discussion of how performance and training speed are affected by different values of `λ` would strengthen the paper's claims and provide practical guidance for users.
*   **Discussion on Distance Choice:** The paper formulates the word-level distance as the squared Euclidean distance `||A(xi - xj)||^2_2`, which is common in metric learning but differs from the standard Euclidean distance `||xi - xj||_2` used in the original WMD paper. The authors correctly note this might be a reason for underperformance on one dataset. A short discussion justifying this choice (e.g., for compatibility with the NCA loss) would be beneficial.
*   **Initialization Importance:** The initialization via S-WCD is presented as a key part of the optimization. It would be interesting to quantify its importance by comparing it to a simpler initialization (e.g., `A=I`) to show how much it contributes to the final performance and convergence speed.

Overall, these are minor points for improvement on an already excellent paper. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
