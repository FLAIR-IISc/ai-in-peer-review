This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document similarity. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weight word frequencies, guided by supervised labels, to better capture semantic similarity. The authors address the computational complexity of incorporating supervision by leveraging a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering. The paper presents extensive experiments on 8 datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to 26 baseline methods.

The paper builds upon prior work in document distance metrics (e.g., BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA) and recent advances using word embeddings (WMD). It also connects to the broader field of metric learning, referencing methods like NCA, LMNN, and ITML. The authors appropriately position their work as bridging the gap between unsupervised document representations and supervised metric learning specifically tailored for the WMD framework. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to approximate the WMD and make it computationally tractable is a significant contribution, aligning with recent efforts to accelerate Wasserstein distance computations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of supervised learning with the WMD, and the specific approach of learning both a linear transformation of word embeddings *and* word importance weights, is a novel contribution. Applying metric learning principles directly within the WMD framework is a valuable insight.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance is a well-established technique to address the computational cost of optimal transport. The gradient computation appears to be carefully derived and optimized, and the described batch gradient descent and initialization strategy seem sensible.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The extensive experimental evaluation across 8 datasets and comparison against a large number of baselines provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD. The ablation study with the initialization strategy (S-WMD init.) is also informative.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanation of the method is accessible. The inclusion of pseudocode is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention tuning hyperparameters with Bayesian optimization, there's a lack of discussion on the sensitivity of the method to different hyperparameter settings. A brief analysis of how performance changes with varying λ (in the Sinkhorn distance) or learning rates would be beneficial.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Word Reweighting:** While empirically effective, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical justification for re-weighting word frequencies. Is there a principled way to interpret this reweighting, perhaps in the context of feature selection or attention mechanisms?
*   **Dataset Choice & Limitations:** While comprehensive, the dataset choices could be further justified, and the paper could acknowledge potential limitations related to the characteristics of these specific datasets. For example, the authors acknowledge that S-WMD may struggle with very long documents.
*   **Comparison with More Recent Metric Learning Methods:** While the baselines are numerous, the comparison doesn't include some of the more recent advances in deep metric learning, such as methods leveraging neural networks to learn non-linear embeddings.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for improving document similarity using supervised learning.
*   The extensive empirical evaluation demonstrates strong performance compared to a large number of baselines.
*   The paper addresses a computationally challenging problem with a practical and efficient solution.
*   The work has the potential to impact a variety of applications involving document similarity.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of theoretical justification for the word re-weighting component.
*   Limited discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   The absence of comparison with more recent deep metric learning approaches.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of document similarity and metric learning. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty, technical soundness, and comprehensive empirical evaluation outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance.
