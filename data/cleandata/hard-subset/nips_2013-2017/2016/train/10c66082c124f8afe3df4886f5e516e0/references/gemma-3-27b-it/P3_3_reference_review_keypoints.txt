- Introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD): learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weights word frequencies to improve WMD for document similarity.
- Objective: directly minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA).
- Computational contribution: uses Sinkhorn distance as a relaxation to compute gradients efficiently, making supervised WMD training scalable.
- Empirical result: strong state-of-the-art performance across eight datasets, outperforming 26 baselines.
- Novelty: combines metric learning in embedding space with learned word-frequency re-weighting for task-specific importance of words.
- Thorough experiments: extensive evaluation, hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian optimization, and reporting of standard errors.
- Initialization: uses S-WCD for effective initialization, aiding optimization.
- Practicality: reasonable training times suggest practical applicability.
- Good writing and presentation: clear explanations and appropriate mathematical notation.
- Relation to prior work: builds on WMD, optimal transport (Sinkhorn), and metric learning literature; situates method well among prior approaches.
- Sensitivity concern: performance depends on Sinkhorn regularization parameter λ, requiring more systematic study.
- Choice of transport cost: using squared Euclidean distance appears arbitrary; other metrics (e.g., p=1, cosine) should be explored.
- Limited analysis of learned word weights: visualization provided but lacks in-depth cross-dataset analysis of patterns and consistently high-weighted words.
- Overfitting risk: supervised approach may overfit on smaller datasets; more discussion of regularization or cross-validation is needed.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: impact of batch size (B) and neighbor set size (N) on performance and training time needs further exploration.
- Recommendation and verdict: reviewer rates the paper a Strong Accept, citing technical soundness, significant contribution, and high empirical impact.
