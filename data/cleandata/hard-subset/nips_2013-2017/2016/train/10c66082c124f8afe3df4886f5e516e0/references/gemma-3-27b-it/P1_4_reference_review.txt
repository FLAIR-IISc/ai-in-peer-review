## Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document similarity. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weighted word frequencies to better align WMD distances with semantic similarity as defined by document labels. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering of the training set. They demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on 8 datasets and 26 baseline methods.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the recent work on WMD [Kusner et al., 2016], which established a powerful baseline for document similarity leveraging word embeddings. It also connects to the broader field of metric learning [Yang & Jin, 2017], particularly linear metric learning approaches like NCA [Goldberger et al., 2004]. However, S-WMD distinguishes itself by operating directly on word embeddings rather than learning document representations, and by jointly learning both a transformation of the embeddings *and* word-specific importance weights.  Related work on learning ground distances for EMD [Cuturi & Avis, 2014; Wang & Guibas, 2016] is also acknowledged, though S-WMD adds the novel component of word re-weighting.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates compelling results across a diverse set of datasets, consistently outperforming a large number of established baselines. The extensive comparison (26 methods!) provides strong evidence of the method's effectiveness.
* **Novel Approach:** Combining linear metric learning with re-weighted word frequencies within the WMD framework is a novel and well-motivated idea. The ability to learn both a transformation of the embedding space *and* adjust word importance offers increased flexibility.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors address a significant challenge – the computational complexity of supervised WMD – by effectively using the Sinkhorn distance approximation.  The resulting efficiency allows scaling to reasonably sized datasets.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured.  The mathematical derivations are relatively clear, and the explanation of the optimization process is detailed.
* **Thorough Experimentation & Analysis:** The paper includes visualizations (t-SNE) and analysis of learned word weights, providing further insight into the behavior of the model.  The ablation study of the initialization (S-WMD init.) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the use of Bayesian optimization is commendable, the paper doesn't delve deeply into the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameter choices. A more detailed discussion on this would be beneficial.
* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors provide a gradient derivation, the theoretical properties of the learned metric and the convergence guarantees of the optimization process are not extensively explored.  A deeper theoretical understanding would strengthen the paper.
* **Quadratic Complexity:** Though mitigated by the Sinkhorn approximation, the core complexity still scales quadratically with vocabulary size.  This might limit the application of the method to extremely large vocabularies.  Discussing this limitation more explicitly would be helpful.
* **Dependency on Word Embeddings:** The performance is inherently tied to the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. While this is acknowledged, it’s a potential bottleneck and a more thorough analysis of the impact of different embedding types (e.g., GloVe, fastText) could be included.
* **S-WMD init. Performance:** The strong performance of S-WMD init. is somewhat surprising and warrants further investigation. It might suggest that the full S-WMD training process doesn't always lead to substantial improvements, depending on the dataset.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to supervised document similarity.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance.
* The authors address a significant computational challenge and provide an efficient implementation.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is limited.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity is not thoroughly explored.
* The quadratic complexity may pose a limitation for very large vocabularies.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of document similarity. The proposed S-WMD method achieves impressive results and offers a novel approach to incorporating supervision into the WMD framework. While some limitations exist, the strengths of the paper far outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Confidence:** 8/10
