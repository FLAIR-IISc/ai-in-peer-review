

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method to learn a metric for document distances by incorporating supervision into the Word Mover's Distance framework. The authors aim to improve the WMD by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word-specific importance weights, using a Sinkhorn approximation for computational efficiency.

## Quality

The paper contains several technical inaccuracies that undermine its quality. The claim that "the time complexity of solving the WMD optimization problem is O(q^3 log q)" is incorrect; the standard complexity for the simplex method for linear programming is O(q^3), not O(q^3 log q). The paper also incorrectly states that "the WCD always lower bounds the WMD" without specifying that this only holds for Euclidean distance (p=2) and not for general p-norms used in WMD.

The mathematical notation is inconsistent throughout the paper. For instance, the distance function is written as "DA,w(d a,db)" with a space in the subscript in Equation (5) but as "DA,w(da,db)" without space later in the paper. Similarly, "d̃ a i" appears multiple times where it should be "d̃_a,i" or "d̃_{a,i}" for proper notation.

The paper incorrectly states that "the WCD scales asymptotically by O(dq)" when it should be O(d) for the Euclidean distance computation. The WCD is simply the Euclidean distance between document vectors, which is O(d) per distance computation, not O(dq) as claimed.

The reference to Gardner et al. [13] for Bayesian optimization is incomplete in the reference list (it's listed as "In ICML, pp, , , " with missing page numbers), which is unprofessional for a NIPS submission.

## Clarity

The paper suffers from inconsistent notation and formatting issues that significantly reduce clarity. The mathematical expressions contain numerous spacing errors (e.g., "d̃ a i" instead of "d̃_a,i"), inconsistent use of subscripts, and inconsistent capitalization of terms (e.g., "WMD" vs "WMD" with spaces). The algorithm description in Section 3.2 is particularly confusing due to inconsistent notation.

The paper claims "S-WMD right after initialization (S-WMD init.) performs quite well" but provides no meaningful comparison between the initialization and the final model, making it impossible to assess whether the training process provides significant improvement.

The explanation of the gradient calculation for w (Equation 10) is particularly messy and lacks proper mathematical notation, making it difficult to follow the derivation. The paper should have used clearer notation to distinguish between vectors, matrices, and operations.

## Originality

While the paper builds on the WMD framework, the novelty is limited. The authors cite Wang et al. [35] as "most similar to our work" but fail to properly contrast their approach with this prior work. Wang et al. [35] also learn a metric within a generalized Euclidean EMD ground distance, but the current paper does not explain how their approach to histogram re-weighting provides a meaningful difference.

The paper's claim that "there has been relatively little work towards learning supervised word embeddings" is inaccurate; Tang et al. [32] (cited in the paper) already proposed learning sentiment-specific word embeddings using label information.

## Significance

The empirical results show promising performance on 5/8 datasets, but the paper fails to provide standard errors for the reported results, making it impossible to assess statistical significance. The paper states that standard errors are reported for the datasets without predefined splits, but Table 2 does not include these errors.

The paper claims that "S-WMD is surprisingly fast," but the training times in Table 3 are not compared to the time required to train other methods (e.g., NCA, ITML, LMNN) on the same datasets, making it difficult to evaluate the claimed efficiency.

The paper's conclusion that S-WMD "may well be the best performing distance metric for documents to date" is overstated given the limited number of datasets where it outperforms the competition (only 5/8), and the fact that it doesn't significantly outperform unsupervised WMD on most datasets.

## Overall Assessment

This paper contains multiple technical inaccuracies, inconsistent notation, and unclear explanations that significantly undermine its quality. While the core idea of incorporating supervision into WMD is reasonable, the paper fails to properly establish the novelty of the approach or to present the technical details with the precision expected at NIPS.

The minor methodological flaws, formatting inconsistencies, and technical inaccuracies collectively outweigh the paper's modest empirical improvements over baselines. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to correct these issues before being suitable for publication at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Reject**

*Pro:*
- Builds on the influential WMD framework
- Provides empirical results across 8 datasets
- Introduces an efficient Sinkhorn-based approximation

*Con:*
- Contains multiple technical inaccuracies (time complexity, lower bound claim)
- Inconsistent mathematical notation throughout
- Incomplete references and missing details in results
- Overstates significance of results without statistical validation
- Fails to properly contrast with related work (Wang et al. [35])
