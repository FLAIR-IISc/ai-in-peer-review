**Summary:**
This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach extends the unsupervised Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by simultaneously learning a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a set of word-specific importance weights. The objective is to minimize a stochastic version of the k-nearest neighbor leave-one-out classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). To make the optimization computationally tractable, the authors leverage the Sinkhorn distance, an entropy-regularized version of the optimal transport problem, and employ a batch-based gradient descent with a nearest-neighbor filtering strategy. The method is evaluated on eight document classification tasks, where it is shown to outperform an extensive list of 26 unsupervised and supervised baselines on average.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The core idea of learning a supervised metric for the WMD is both novel and significant. It addresses a key limitation of the original WMD, which is its entirely unsupervised nature. The proposed two-pronged approach—learning a transformation `A` for the embedding space and importance weights `w` for the words—is a powerful and intuitive combination that allows the model to adapt to the specific semantics of a given classification task.
2.  **Technical Contribution:** The paper makes a solid technical contribution by devising an efficient optimization scheme for a seemingly intractable problem. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to obtain differentiable and fast approximations of the WMD and its gradients is well-motivated and crucial for the method's feasibility. The proposed initialization strategy using a supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) is also a clever heuristic to navigate the non-convex optimization landscape.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing S-WMD against a wide array of strong baselines across eight datasets. The method demonstrates state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance in most cases, which strongly supports the authors' claims. The qualitative visualizations using t-SNE and the word cloud provide compelling evidence that the learned metric effectively captures task-relevant similarities.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising core idea and results, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws ranging from critical presentation issues to unaddressed methodological details.

1.  **Major Presentation and Formatting Issues:**
    *   The reference list is in a completely unacceptable state for a formal publication. Entries are malformed, missing essential information (e.g., venue, year, page numbers), and contain stray commas (e.g., "Athena Scientific,, , ,"). This reflects a significant lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.
    *   Several tables and figures referenced in the text are missing. Table 1 (dataset statistics) and Table 3 (training times) are mentioned but not provided. The link for Figure 2 is a local file path (`file:///C:/Users/...`), which is unusable.
    *   There are numerous minor formatting errors. The conference information below the abstract ("th Conference on...") is incomplete and oddly placed. The footnote symbol for the second author is missing in the author list.

2.  **Lack of Clarity and Inconsistencies:**
    *   The notation for norms is inconsistent and ambiguous. Equation (1) uses `‖xi − xj‖p2`, which is unclear. Does this mean the squared p-norm, or is `p` intended to be 2? Later, Equation (5) uses the standard `‖A(xi − xj)‖22` for the squared L2-norm. This inconsistency is confusing.
    *   The gradient in Equation (7) appears to contain a typo: `pab pa`. Based on the description below Equation (11), this should likely be `pab / pa`.
    *   In the derivation of the dual variables `α*` and `β*`, the term `log(u)>1 p 1` is opaque. What is `p` in this context? It was previously the norm order. Is `1` the all-ones vector? This part of the derivation needs clearer explanation.
    *   The authors state they tune hyperparameters for all baseline methods using Bayesian optimization but do not specify how the hyperparameters for S-WMD itself (e.g., `λ`, learning rates `ηA`, `ηw`, batch size `B`, neighbor set size `N`) were chosen. The justification for setting the crucial regularization parameter `λ=10` as a "nice trade-off" is weak and unscientific. This suggests a less rigorous tuning process for the proposed method compared to the baselines.

3.  **Methodological Gaps:**
    *   A critical potential flaw is the lack of a positivity constraint on the word importance weights `w`. The weights are initialized to 1 and updated via standard gradient descent. There is no mechanism described to prevent weights from becoming negative. If weights become negative, the re-weighted histogram in Equation (4), `d̃a = (w ◦ da)/(w>da)`, could become unstable or undefined if the denominator `w>da` approaches zero or becomes negative. This is a serious oversight that could undermine the stability of the entire method.
    *   The paper admits that S-WMD's performance on the CLASSIC dataset is worse than the unsupervised WMD, speculating this may be due to the choice of the squared Euclidean distance. This choice feels arbitrary, and its potential sub-optimality is a significant weakness that is acknowledged but not addressed. Were other ground metrics considered? Why was this specific choice made?
    *   The justification for performance drops on certain datasets (e.g., long documents for BBCSPORT) is post-hoc speculation without supporting analysis. This weakens the overall argument.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper presents a promising and novel direction for metric learning in text analysis, its current state is far below the quality standards for NIPS. The core contribution is unfortunately overshadowed by severe presentational deficiencies, methodological ambiguities, and unaddressed technical concerns. The unformatted reference list and missing figures/tables alone are grounds for rejection. Furthermore, the lack of discussion on a positivity constraint for the weight vector `w` is a critical methodological gap.

I believe this work has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field. However, it requires a major revision to address the numerous flaws. I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the manuscript, fixing all formatting and presentation issues, clarifying the mathematical notation and derivations, providing a rigorous justification for hyperparameter choices, and addressing the potential instability of the word weights `w`. After such a revision, the paper would be much more suitable for publication.
