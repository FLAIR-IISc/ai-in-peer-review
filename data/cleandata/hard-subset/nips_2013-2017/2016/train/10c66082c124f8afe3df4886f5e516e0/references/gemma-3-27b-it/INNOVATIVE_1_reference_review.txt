This paper introduces a supervised learning approach to the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), aiming to improve its performance by learning a transformation of the word embeddings and re-weighting word frequencies based on task-specific labels. The core idea – leveraging the power of WMD's embedding space while incorporating supervision – is compelling and represents a novel direction. It smartly addresses the computational challenge of supervised WMD by utilizing the Sinkhorn distance for efficient approximation. The extensive experimental validation across eight datasets and comparison with 26 baselines strengthens the claim of superiority.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of metric learning *within* the word embedding space, alongside re-weighted word frequencies, is a genuinely new approach. It moves beyond simply learning document representations and directly tackles the distance metric itself, which is a significant conceptual shift.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** Addressing the cubic complexity of WMD through the Sinkhorn distance is a crucial contribution, making supervised learning feasible. The probabilistic filtering further enhances practicality.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation demonstrating state-of-the-art or competitive performance across diverse datasets. The ablation studies (S-WMD init.) also provide insights into the contribution of each component.
*   **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details, building logically from background material to the proposed method and experiments.
*   **Thorough Comparison:** Comparing against a large number of baselines including various metric learning algorithms provides a robust assessment of the method’s performance.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While Bayesian optimization is used for tuning, the paper doesn't delve into the sensitivity of the method to different hyperparameters. A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter impact would strengthen the findings.
*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical justification for the proposed method, particularly the interaction between the linear transformation and the word re-weighting, could be more rigorous.
*   **Potential for Overfitting:** With a relatively large number of parameters (the transformation matrix A and the word weighting vector w), there's a possibility of overfitting, particularly on smaller datasets. The regularization techniques used are not explicitly detailed, making it difficult to assess this risk.
*   **Focus on kNN:** The evaluation is almost exclusively focused on kNN classification. Exploring the performance of S-WMD in other downstream tasks (e.g., information retrieval, clustering) would broaden the impact assessment.
*   **Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper doesn't deeply analyze why S-WMD doesn’t perform as well on certain datasets (e.g., BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON). Understanding these limitations would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is highly novel and has the potential to significantly impact document distance learning.
*   The proposed method demonstrates strong empirical performance, surpassing numerous strong baselines.
*   The authors successfully address a major computational bottleneck, making supervised WMD practical.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient details for reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical grounding could be strengthened.
*   The analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and potential overfitting is limited.
*   The evaluation is somewhat narrow, focusing primarily on kNN.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a genuinely novel and promising approach to supervised document distance learning. While minor weaknesses exist regarding theoretical analysis and detailed parameter sensitivity, the significant novelty, strong empirical results, and computational efficiency outweigh these concerns. The paper introduces a valuable contribution to the field, opening new avenues for research in metric learning and document understanding. I recommend **acceptance**. The paper prioritizes a creative idea and demonstrates strong experimental validation over absolute methodological perfection, which aligns with my reviewing preferences. A few minor revisions addressing the weaknesses discussed above would further strengthen the paper.
