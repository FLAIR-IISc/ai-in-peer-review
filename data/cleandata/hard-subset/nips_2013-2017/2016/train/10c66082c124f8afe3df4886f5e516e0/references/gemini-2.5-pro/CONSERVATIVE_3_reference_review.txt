**Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach extends the recently proposed unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which leverages word embeddings to compute the optimal transport cost between documents. The authors incorporate supervision by learning two components simultaneously: a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a set of importance weights for each word in the vocabulary. The learning objective is framed as minimizing a stochastic version of the k-NN leave-one-out classification error, following the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) framework. To make this computationally demanding optimization tractable, the authors employ a regularized version of the optimal transport problem (the Sinkhorn distance) and use a batch-based gradient descent with an efficient neighbor-selection scheme. The proposed S-WMD is evaluated extensively on eight document classification datasets, where it is shown to outperform a large number of established baselines, including WMD and various document representations combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The most compelling aspect of this work is its thorough and rigorous experimental evaluation. The authors compare S-WMD against an impressive set of 26 baselines across eight diverse datasets. These baselines include established methods like TF-IDF, LSI, and LDA, as well as more recent techniques like mSDA and the original WMD. Crucially, they also include strong supervised baselines by applying metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML) to these representations. The fact that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance in this demanding setting provides strong evidence for its effectiveness.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The paper builds upon a well-understood and effective unsupervised method (WMD). The proposed extension to a supervised setting is methodologically sound. The authors identify the key computational and theoretical challenges in optimizing WMD (cubic complexity, non-differentiability) and address them with principled approximations. The use of the entropically regularized Sinkhorn distance is a well-established technique to make the optimal transport problem faster and differentiable, and its application here is appropriate and well-executed.

3.  **Well-Considered Optimization Strategy:** The non-convex nature of the objective function is handled pragmatically. The authors propose a sensible initialization strategy by first learning a metric for the much faster Word Centroid Distance (WCD), which provides a good starting point for the full S-WMD optimization. Furthermore, the use of batch gradient descent combined with a nearest-neighbor approximation (using WCD as a proxy) to compute the gradients is a clever and necessary engineering step to make the method scalable and practical.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** While the engineering and empirical results are strong, the conceptual novelty is somewhat limited. The work is primarily a skillful combination of several existing, powerful ideas: WMD [19], Sinkhorn distances [6], and NCA [15]. The main contribution lies in demonstrating that this specific combination is highly effective for document classification, and in developing the necessary machinery to make it work efficiently. The introduction of word-importance weights (`w`) adds a degree of novelty, but re-weighting features is a common theme in supervised learning.

2.  **Reliance on Approximations and Unexplored Sensitivity:** The method's tractability hinges on several approximations. First, the Sinkhorn distance is used in place of the true Wasserstein distance. Second, the NCA gradient is approximated using only a subset of nearest neighbors. The paper does not provide an analysis of how sensitive the final performance is to the key hyperparameters governing these approximations, namely the regularization strength `λ` for the Sinkhorn distance and the number of neighbors `N` used in the gradient computation. A more rigorous treatment would include a sensitivity analysis to build confidence that the reported results are robust to these choices.

3.  **Superficial Analysis of Failure Cases:** S-WMD does not achieve the best performance on three of the eight datasets (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON). The discussion of these results is brief, attributing the underperformance to potential issues like poor initialization or the choice of a squared Euclidean ground metric. A deeper investigation would be beneficial. For example, on BBCSPORT, a simpler approach (NCA on TF-IDF) performs best. Is there a fundamental reason why a transport-based distance might be less effective than a representation-based one for certain types of datasets (e.g., those with longer documents, as the authors suggest)? A more thorough analysis of these cases would strengthen the paper's conclusions about the method's applicability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses the important problem of learning a supervised document distance.
*   The empirical results are extensive and demonstrate a significant improvement over a wide range of strong and established baselines, advancing the state of the art in kNN-based document classification.
*   The proposed method is technically sound, combining existing ideas in a novel and effective way to solve a challenging optimization problem.
*   The work is well-presented, clear, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The primary contribution is an effective combination of existing methods rather than a fundamentally new approach.
*   The paper lacks a sensitivity analysis for key hyperparameters related to the approximations used, which are critical to the method's practicality.
*   The analysis of cases where the method is outperformed by baselines could be more insightful.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly the impressive and thorough empirical validation, outweigh its weaknesses. The authors have developed a method that demonstrably advances the state of the art on a standard text processing task. While the conceptual novelty is more incremental than groundbreaking, the work represents a solid and valuable contribution to the field. The combination of ideas is non-trivial, and the engineering required to make it efficient and effective is commendable. I recommend acceptance, but I would urge the authors to add a discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity (particularly for `λ`) in the final version to further solidify the work's rigor.
