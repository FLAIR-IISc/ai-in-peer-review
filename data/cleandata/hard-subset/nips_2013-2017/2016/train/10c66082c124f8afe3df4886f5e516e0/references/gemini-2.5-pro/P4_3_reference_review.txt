### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific distance metric for text documents. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by Kusner et al. (2015), which defines the distance between two documents as the minimum cost of transporting words from one to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. While WMD is entirely unsupervised, S-WMD incorporates label information to learn a more discriminative distance.

The core idea is to learn two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation matrix `A` that projects the word embeddings into a new space where distances are more meaningful for the specific classification task, and (2) a vector `w` of word-specific importance weights that re-scales the document's bag-of-words representation to emphasize task-relevant words.

The authors formulate the learning problem as minimizing the leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor (kNN) classification error. To make this non-differentiable objective tractable, they adopt the soft-nearest neighbor loss function from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) by Goldberger et al. (2005). A key technical challenge is the computational cost and non-differentiability of the WMD, which is an instance of the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) solved via linear programming. The authors overcome this by replacing the exact EMD with its entropy-regularized approximation, the Sinkhorn distance (Cuturi, 2013). This relaxation makes the distance computation significantly faster (quadratic instead of cubic in vocabulary size) and yields a smooth, differentiable objective. The paper provides gradient derivations for both the transformation matrix `A` and the importance weights `w`.

To further improve efficiency, the authors propose several practical optimization strategies: initializing the matrix `A` by first running NCA on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD), using mini-batch stochastic gradient descent, and restricting the neighborhood probability computations to a smaller set of nearest neighbors identified via the computationally cheaper WCD.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a well-motivated and novel approach to supervised document distance learning. It effectively combines ideas from optimal transport (WMD), metric learning (NCA), and fast computational methods (Sinkhorn distance). The introduction of learnable word importance weights (`w`) in conjunction with a learned transformation (`A`) of the embedding space is a significant and powerful extension to prior work on learning ground metrics for EMD. The strong empirical results suggest this work could have a substantial impact on document classification tasks.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The technical approach is sound. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to create a differentiable and efficient surrogate for WMD is a key contribution that makes the entire framework computationally feasible. The derivations for the gradients with respect to `A` and `w` appear correct and are well-explained. The proposed optimization strategy, including the initialization with Supervised WCD and the use of mini-batches with neighbor filtering, is practical and effective.

3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare S-WMD against an extensive set of 26 baselines across 8 diverse datasets. These baselines include classical document representations (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), modern neural methods (mSDA), and the original WMD/WCD, each combined with multiple standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The fact that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance on nearly all datasets strongly supports the authors' claims. The use of Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning across all methods further strengthens the fairness of the comparison.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. It provides sufficient background on word embeddings, WMD, and NCA, making the work accessible. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the proposed algorithm is described in adequate detail.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Analysis of Learned Parameters:** While Figure 2 provides a word cloud visualization of the learned importance weights `w`, a more in-depth analysis would be beneficial. For instance, it would be insightful to see a comparison between the learned weights and unsupervised importance measures like IDF, or to analyze how the weights for the same word change across different classification tasks (e.g., topic vs. sentiment). This could provide a deeper understanding of what the model is capturing.

2.  **Dependence on Squared Euclidean Ground Metric:** The method is formulated using a squared Euclidean distance in the transformed word embedding space (`||A(xi - xj)||^2`). The authors note that this might be suboptimal for the CLASSIC dataset where the original WMD (with L2 distance) performs better. A brief discussion on the possibility of using other ground metrics and the potential challenges this would introduce to the optimization framework would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The complexity of a single gradient update is `O(TBN[p^2 + d^2(p + r)])`. While the authors demonstrate this is practical for the datasets tested (training times are reported in seconds or minutes), the quadratic dependence on the number of neighbors `N` and batch size `B` could be a bottleneck for very large training sets where a larger neighborhood might be necessary to get a good gradient estimate. A discussion of the method's scalability to datasets with millions of documents would be a useful addition.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   The paper addresses the important problem of learning task-specific document distances.
*   It proposes a novel and principled method, S-WMD, that effectively integrates supervision into the powerful WMD framework.
*   The technical solution to the computational challenge of differentiating through the WMD problem is elegant and sound.
*   The contribution is supported by extensive and rigorous experiments, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance against a wide array of strong baselines.
*   The paper is clearly written and presents a complete piece of work.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   The analysis of the learned model components (specifically the word weights `w`) could be more thorough.
*   The paper does not fully explore the limitations imposed by the choice of a squared Euclidean ground metric.
*   Potential scalability to extremely large datasets is not fully addressed.

In summary, the paper's strengths in novelty, technical quality, and empirical validation significantly outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a solid and impactful contribution to the field of metric learning and text analysis.
