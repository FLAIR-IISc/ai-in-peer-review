## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), an algorithm for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by incorporating supervision. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word representations alongside re-weighted word frequencies, minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric. The authors leverage the Sinkhorn distance to achieve computational efficiency. The paper presents extensive experimental results on 8 datasets, demonstrating superiority over 26 baseline methods.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of incorporating supervised learning into the WMD framework is a valuable contribution. Learning both a word embedding transformation *and* word importance weights is a sensible extension.
* **Extensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors demonstrate S-WMD’s performance across a diverse set of datasets and against a very comprehensive baseline comparison, which is commendable.  The reported improvements are often substantial.
* **Computational Efficiency:** Addressing the computational complexity of supervised WMD using the Sinkhorn distance is a significant practical contribution.
* **Clear Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of existing document distance methods and positions S-WMD as a solution.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Minor Technical Inconsistencies & Lack of Rigor:** The presentation contains several minor but pervasive technical slips. For example, the gradient calculation in Section 3.1 refers to "the optimal solution to the dual transport problem," yet the subsequent equations define approximations of the dual (α* and β*) *derived from* the primal solution. The distinction is crucial and not clearly conveyed. Further, the claim that T∗λ is “always unique” is not fully justified – while the smoothed problem *is* strictly convex, the authors do not demonstrate uniqueness in the context of their gradient computation.
* **Overstated Claims of Superiority:** While the experimental results are strong, the presentation tends toward hyperbole ("state-of-the-art," "superiority").  Several datasets show marginal improvements, and the relative gains aren’t always significant, especially considering the complexity added. The error tables (Table 2) lack confidence intervals, making it difficult to assess statistical significance.
* **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices:** Several critical design choices are made without sufficient justification. For instance, the choice of λ=10 in the Sinkhorn distance regularization is presented as a good trade-off without an ablation study or sensitivity analysis. Similarly, the use of the squared Euclidean distance in Eq. (5) isn't justified beyond a brief statement, and alternative distance metrics may yield better performance.
* **Formatting and Writing Issues:**  The paper suffers from numerous minor formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent spacing, references not perfectly formatted). More importantly, some explanations are convoluted and could be significantly improved for clarity. The sentence structure in several paragraphs is awkward.
* **Missing Ablation Studies:** The impact of learning the word embedding transformation (A) *vs.* learning the word importance weights (w) is not isolated. An ablation study would be critical to understand the contribution of each component.
* **Limited Analysis of Learned Weights:** The visualization of learned word weights (Figure 2) is superficial. A more thorough analysis of *why* certain words are upweighted for specific classes would be insightful.

**Detailed Comments:**

* **Section 1:** The opening paragraph uses "nearly orthogonal" without quantification. This should be clarified. The statement about dimensionality reduction being necessary before applying metric learning to high-dimensional text is a simplification; many metric learning algorithms can handle high dimensionality directly.
* **Section 2:** The description of word2vec is somewhat verbose. A more concise explanation focused on its relevance to WMD would be preferable.  The reference “[5, 21?]” is unconventional and should be corrected.
* **Section 3:** Equation (4) is not clearly explained. Why is the re-weighted vector normalized?  The claim that the gradient computation is faster is significant but lacks a detailed complexity analysis beyond a brief statement.  The use of stochastic gradient descent with a nearest-neighbor subset is a good optimization strategy, but the choice of N=200 needs justification.
* **Section 4:** The description of the datasets is adequate, but the motivation for choosing these datasets could be strengthened.  The lack of confidence intervals in Table 2 is a major concern.  The observation that S-WMD may be outperformed by NCA with TF-IDF on datasets with long documents highlights a limitation of the approach that should be discussed more thoroughly.
* **Section 5:** This section is reasonable, but could be more comprehensive.
* **References:** The reference formatting is inconsistent.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel approach to supervised document distance learning.
* Strong empirical results on a diverse set of datasets.
* Computational efficiency through the use of the Sinkhorn distance.

**Con:**

* Numerous minor technical inconsistencies and lack of rigor.
* Overstated claims of superiority and lack of statistical significance.
* Insufficient justification for design choices and lack of ablation studies.
* Formatting and writing issues.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach to supervised document distance learning. However, the numerous minor flaws—ranging from technical inconsistencies to writing quality—detract from the overall impact. While the experimental results are strong, the lack of rigorous analysis and justification raises concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the method.  

**Recommendation:**

**Reject.** While the ideas are novel and the results are encouraging, the paper requires significant revision to address the identified weaknesses before it would be suitable for NIPS. Specifically, the authors need to: (1) clarify and rigorously justify the technical details, (2) provide statistical significance testing, (3) conduct ablation studies, and (4) improve the clarity and conciseness of the writing. A more humble and nuanced presentation of the results would also be beneficial.
