This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embeddings and re-weight word frequencies to align WMD distances with semantic similarity encoded in labels. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by utilizing a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering. Extensive experiments across eight datasets demonstrate the superiority of S-WMD compared to 26 baseline methods.

The paper builds upon the recent success of WMD [Kusner et al., 2015] which leverages word embeddings for document distance calculation, avoiding the limitations of traditional sparse representations like bag-of-words.  It also connects to the broader field of metric learning [Weinberger & Saul, 2009; Davis et al., 2007], specifically drawing inspiration from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [Goldberger et al., 2005].  The application of the Sinkhorn distance [Cuturi, 2013] to accelerate WMD computation is a relevant and well-motivated technique.  Recent work exploring learned distance metrics for EMD-related tasks [Wang & Guibas, 2017; Wang et al., 2018] positions this work within a growing body of research.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling results across a diverse set of datasets, consistently outperforming a large number of strong baselines. The ablation study (S-WMD init.) is useful and provides insight into the contribution of the learning process.
* **Novel Approach:** Learning a metric specifically tailored to the word embedding space within the WMD framework is a novel and promising approach. The combination of learning a linear transformation *and* re-weighting word frequencies is a good design choice.
* **Computational Efficiency:** Addressing the computational bottleneck of supervised WMD is crucial, and the use of the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering provides an effective solution.  The reported training times are reasonable.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed method. The background section provides adequate context. The visualization with t-SNE helps illustrate the effectiveness of S-WMD.
* **Thorough Experimentation:** The comprehensive comparison against a large number of baselines, including various document representations and metric learning algorithms, is a significant strength.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors motivate their approach and describe the optimization process, there is a lack of deeper theoretical analysis of the convergence properties or the statistical guarantees of the learned metric.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The reliance on Bayesian Optimization for hyperparameter tuning is mentioned, but the sensitivity of the results to these parameters isn’t thoroughly discussed.  A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter settings would strengthen the findings.
* **Squared Euclidean Distance:** The choice of squared Euclidean distance in the optimization objective is not explicitly justified. Exploring other distance metrics might be beneficial. While noted as a potential limitation in the discussion of the CLASSIC dataset, this is not explored further.
* **Dependence on Word Embeddings:** The performance of S-WMD is inherently tied to the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. While the paper acknowledges this, a sensitivity analysis to different embedding models (beyond word2vec) would be valuable.
* **Scaling to very large vocabularies:** Although the Sinkhorn approximation helps, the O(q^2) complexity remains a potential bottleneck for extremely large vocabularies. This limitation should be explicitly acknowledged.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to improving document distance calculation.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* The authors address a significant computational challenge.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of deeper theoretical analysis is a weakness.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameters and the dependence on pre-trained embeddings warrant further investigation.
* The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of limitations and potential extensions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document distance learning. The proposed S-WMD method is novel, effective, and computationally feasible. While there are some minor weaknesses related to theoretical analysis and hyperparameter sensitivity, the strong empirical results and the practical significance of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors consider addressing the aforementioned weaknesses in future work.  Specifically, the authors should consider including a sensitivity analysis around hyperparameters in the supplementary material and exploring alternative distance metrics in the optimization objective.
