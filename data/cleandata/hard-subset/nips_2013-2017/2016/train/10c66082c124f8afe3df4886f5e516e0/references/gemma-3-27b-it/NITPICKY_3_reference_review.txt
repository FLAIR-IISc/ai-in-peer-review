## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by learning a linear transformation of word representations and re-weighted word frequencies. The core idea leverages the efficiency of the Sinkhorn distance approximation to make supervised learning feasible within the WMD framework. The authors present extensive experiments on 8 datasets demonstrating state-of-the-art performance against 26 baselines.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of metric learning *within* the word embedding space of WMD, alongside word frequency re-weighting, is a novel approach. While metric learning and WMD individually are well-established, their integrated, supervised application is a valuable contribution.
* **Efficiency:** The utilization of the Sinkhorn distance offers a substantial computational improvement over exact WMD calculation, enabling supervised learning on reasonably sized datasets. The proposed batch gradient descent and initialization further contribute to efficiency.
* **Empirical Performance:** The extensive experimental evaluation demonstrates consistently strong performance across a diverse set of datasets and baselines, showcasing the practical effectiveness of S-WMD. The comparative results are well-presented and convincingly support the claims.
* **Clear Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of unsupervised document distance metrics and establishes a strong rationale for the proposed supervised approach.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper presents the loss function and gradient derivation, the theoretical justification for the proposed method, particularly the choice of a linear transformation and re-weighting scheme, is lacking. A deeper investigation into the properties of the learned metric space would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experimental section mentions Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning.  However, the sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters isn't discussed. It's unclear how robust the performance is to suboptimal hyperparameter settings. The authors should provide some insights into this aspect.
* **Overly Complex Presentation:** The paper contains a large number of equations, and the presentation of the gradient calculation, while technically correct, feels dense and difficult to follow.  Simplification of the notation and clearer explanations would significantly improve readability.
* **Minor Inconsistencies & Formatting Issues:**
    * In equation (4), the normalization `d̃a = (w ◦ da)/(w>da)` appears to be incorrect. This should be `d̃a = (w ◦ da) / sum(w ◦ da)`, to ensure the sum of elements in `d̃a` remains 1.
    * Several references are formatted inconsistently (e.g., [21?]).
    * Equation (10) feels disconnected from the text and could benefit from further elaboration.
* **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper doesn’t provide an ablation study to analyze the contribution of each component (linear transformation vs. word re-weighting). It would be insightful to see the performance of S-WMD with only the linear transformation or only the re-weighting.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper glosses over potential limitations, such as the sensitivity to word embedding quality and the computational cost for very large vocabularies.  A more candid discussion of these limitations would strengthen the paper.

**Detailed Comments & Concerns:**

* **Section 3.1 - Gradient Computation:** The jump from "relaxed optimal dual variables" to the actual gradient calculation in Equation (10) is abrupt and requires more explanation. The derivation should be explicitly shown.
* **Section 4 – Results:** While the tables present a comprehensive comparison, the statistical significance of the improvements is not always clear. Providing p-values or confidence intervals would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Related Work:** The related work section is quite extensive but could benefit from a more focused discussion of recent advancements in supervised metric learning *specifically within the context of optimal transport or embedding spaces*.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The method presents a novel and promising approach to supervised document distance learning.
* The empirical results are highly encouraging and demonstrate significant performance improvements over a wide range of baselines.
* The efficient implementation makes the approach practical for real-world applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of theoretical justification and the complex presentation detract from the overall quality of the work.
* The minor inconsistencies and the absence of an ablation study raise concerns about the thoroughness of the evaluation.
*  The paper lacks a critical discussion of its limitations.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the strong empirical results and novel contributions, I am leaning towards **rejection**. While the paper presents a valuable and potentially impactful method, the lack of theoretical depth, the overly complex presentation, the minor errors, and the absence of a comprehensive ablation study prevent it from meeting the high standards expected of a NIPS publication. A major revision addressing these concerns—particularly the theoretical justification, presentation clarity, and the inclusion of an ablation study—would be required for reconsideration. The minor formatting inconsistencies, while not critical, should also be addressed. The potential for a significant impact on the field is acknowledged, but the current presentation falls short of NIPS quality.
