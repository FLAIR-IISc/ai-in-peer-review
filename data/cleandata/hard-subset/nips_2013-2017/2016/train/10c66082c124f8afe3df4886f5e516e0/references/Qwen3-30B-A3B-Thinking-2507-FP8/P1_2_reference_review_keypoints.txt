- Proposes S-WMD: a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance that learns a linear embedding transform and word-specific importance weights for improved document distances in classification.

- Key contributions: supervised metric learned via stochastic leave-one-out classification loss, Sinkhorn approximation for computational efficiency, and a probabilistic filtering technique to reduce runtime.

- Empirical claim: outperforms 26 baselines on 8 datasets, achieving lowest kNN error on 5/8 datasets and best average rank.

- Builds on WMD and metric learning (notably NCA), moving from unsupervised to supervised document distance learning.

- Strength: solid theoretical formulation with well-derived loss and gradients and careful handling of computational challenges.

- Strength: strong experimental evaluation (8 datasets, 26 baselines), with t-SNE visualizations and analysis of learned word weights.

- Strength: computational efficiency demonstrated via complexity analysis and training-time comparisons; competitive with linear metric learning baselines.

- Strength: clear, well-organized presentation with sufficient background and readable derivations.

- Strength: topic is relevant to the NIPS community, combining optimal transport, metric learning, and word embeddings.

- Weakness: limited and superficial analysis of failure cases (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON), lacking deeper investigation into dataset characteristics affecting performance.

- Weakness: experiments use only word2vec; no evaluation with other embeddings (GloVe, ELMo, BERT) to show generality.

- Weakness: insufficient analysis of the impact of initialization (S-WMD init.) versus full supervised training and marginal gains from training.

- Weakness: scalability to extremely large datasets or vocabularies is not fully addressed despite complexity analysis.

- Overall assessment: significant, technically sound contribution with convincing empirical results; strengths outweigh weaknesses.

- Recommendation: accept.

- Suggested improvements: analyze underperforming datasets in depth; test with alternative embeddings; quantify gains from initialization vs full training; discuss scaling to massive datasets; consider integration with modern deep contextual representations (e.g., BERT).
