The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance by learning a linear transformation of word representations and re-weighted word frequencies. The core idea is compelling: leveraging the strengths of WMD – its ability to operate in a low-dimensional space via word embeddings – while incorporating supervision to refine the distance metric for specific tasks. The authors address the computational challenges of supervised WMD by utilizing the Sinkhorn distance as a relaxation of the optimal transport problem and employing probabilistic filtering. The empirical evaluation across 8 datasets and 26 baselines demonstrates the superiority of S-WMD.

However, despite the promising premise and strong empirical results, several methodological and presentational flaws detract from the overall quality of the work. My assessment leans towards rejection due to the cumulative effect of these issues.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of learning a linear transformation of word embeddings *and* re-weighting word frequencies within the WMD framework is a novel contribution.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The extensive experimental evaluation, demonstrating superior performance against a large number of baselines, provides strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** Addressing the computational complexity of supervised WMD through the Sinkhorn distance relaxation is a significant practical contribution.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of unsupervised approaches and motivates the need for a supervised metric learning approach tailored to WMD.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Gradient Approximation Concerns:** The relaxation using the Sinkhorn distance, while addressing computational issues, is presented as a nearly exact approximation. The authors state, “This is because computing the gradient just for a single pair of documents will require time complexity O(q3 log q)…”. However, the theoretical justification for this relaxation’s accuracy—particularly concerning the gradient—is superficial. The statement that the relaxation is “strongly convex” doesn’t automatically guarantee accurate gradients. A more thorough analysis of the approximation error introduced by the entropy regularization is crucial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Tuning Details:** While the authors mention Bayesian optimization, the specifics of the hyperparameter search (search spaces, acquisition functions, number of iterations) are absent. A lack of such detail makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the results and reproduce them. The choice of λ=10 is somewhat arbitrary and lacks a clear justification beyond a “nice trade-off.”
*   **PCA Preprocessing:** The use of PCA dimensionality reduction *before* metric learning for the baselines seems inconsistent. The paper explicitly states a key advantage of WMD is operating in a low-dimensional space. Applying PCA to other methods feels like a workaround to make them comparable rather than a fair assessment. Why not apply PCA to the word embeddings used by S-WMD as well?
*   **WCD Initialization:** The initialization with S-WCD seems potentially problematic. If the WCD is a lower bound on WMD, initializing with a lower bound might constrain the learning process and prevent the algorithm from exploring potentially better solutions. This needs to be investigated and justified.
*   **Loss Function Analysis:** The choice of the KL-divergence loss, borrowed from NCA, is not fully justified in the context of WMD. Is it the most appropriate loss function for this problem? A comparison with alternative loss functions might be valuable.
*   **Minor Technical Inconsistencies:** Equation (4) defines `d̃a` as a re-weighted document vector, but it's not clear how to handle potential negative values resulting from the re-weighting, especially given its usage in the subsequent optimization. Is a clipping or normalization step applied? This is a critical detail.
*   **Presentation and Formatting:** Several minor formatting inconsistencies exist (e.g. inconsistent use of equation references), and the writing could be more concise. The phrase "surprisingly accurate word relationships" in describing word2vec feels hyperbolic and doesn't add substantive information.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Strong empirical performance across multiple datasets.
*   Novel combination of techniques.
*   Practical addressing of computational challenges.

**Con:**

*   Insufficient theoretical justification for the Sinkhorn relaxation and its impact on gradient accuracy.
*   Lack of detail regarding hyperparameter tuning.
*   Questionable PCA preprocessing.
*   Potential issues with the WCD initialization.
*   Minor technical details and writing issues.



**Overall Assessment:**

While S-WMD presents a promising approach to supervised document distance learning, the methodological concerns, lack of detail in critical aspects of the implementation, and minor technical flaws raise significant doubts about the robustness and generalizability of the results. The paper, as currently written, feels incomplete. Although the empirical results are compelling, they are not sufficient to outweigh the aforementioned issues. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. Addressing the theoretical concerns regarding the gradient approximation, providing a detailed explanation of the hyperparameter tuning process, and clarifying the technical details would significantly strengthen the paper and potentially warrant reconsideration.
