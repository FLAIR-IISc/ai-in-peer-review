## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and well-executed approach to incorporating supervision into the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), a powerful technique for document similarity. The authors address a significant challenge – the computational cost of supervised WMD – with a clever application of the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering. The results are strong, consistently outperforming a large number of baselines across diverse datasets. While the presentation could benefit from some clarification, and the reliance on Euclidean distance within the Sinkhorn relaxation is a potential limitation, I believe this work represents a significant contribution and recommend **acceptance**.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of learning a document distance metric tailored to specific classification tasks, moving beyond the purely unsupervised nature of traditional document representation learning (e.g., LSI, LDA, autoencoders).  It leverages the recent success of WMD, which utilizes word embeddings to define a distance between documents based on the ‘cost’ of moving words between them. The key innovation is learning both a linear transformation of the word embeddings *and* word-specific importance weights, optimizing directly for classification performance. This builds upon the existing literature on metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) by adapting it to the WMD framework and tackling the computational challenges inherent in optimal transport. The authors correctly identify the quadratic scaling of typical metric learning methods and present a solution that operates in a lower-dimensional word embedding space.  They also appropriately situate their work within the broader context of Wasserstein distance computation, leveraging Cuturi’s work on the Sinkhorn distance to enable efficient supervised learning.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The core strength of the paper lies in its consistent and substantial performance gains across a diverse set of datasets and a *very* comprehensive comparison with a large number of baselines (26 in total!).  The results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed S-WMD.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors’ use of the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering is a significant contribution. Addressing the cubic complexity of the original WMD is crucial for making supervised learning feasible. The reported training times are reasonable, and the analysis of the complexity is insightful.
* **Novelty:** Learning both a linear transformation of the word embeddings *and* word weights is a novel combination.  This allows the model to adapt the word representations to the specific classification task, and to emphasize the relevance of different words for different classes.
* **Theoretical Soundness:**  The grounding in optimal transport theory, metric learning, and the application of the Sinkhorn distance provides a solid theoretical foundation. The gradient derivation is well-explained.
* **Clear Code Availability:**  Providing publicly available code is a significant benefit for reproducibility and further research.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Presentation:** While generally well-written, certain sections (particularly the gradient derivation) are dense and could benefit from more detailed explanation.  The connection between the dual transport problem and the gradient computation isn’t entirely clear without a strong background in optimal transport.  More intuitive explanations would broaden accessibility.
* **Sinkhorn Relaxation and Euclidean Distance:** The reliance on the squared Euclidean distance within the Sinkhorn relaxation is a potential limitation. While computationally efficient, it may not be the most appropriate distance metric for all applications. Exploring alternative distances within the Sinkhorn framework could yield further improvements. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  While the authors use Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning, it would be valuable to discuss the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings and provide more insight into the best configurations found for each dataset.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses heavily on successes. A more detailed analysis of the datasets where S-WMD *doesn’t* outperform baselines, and the potential reasons for this (e.g., dataset characteristics, limitations of the word embeddings), would strengthen the analysis.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  Exceptional empirical performance across a broad range of datasets.
*  Addresses a crucial challenge in applying WMD to supervised learning.
*  Novel combination of techniques with a sound theoretical basis.
*  Demonstrates computational efficiency through a clever application of Sinkhorn distance and filtering.
*  Publicly available code promotes reproducibility.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Presentation could be more accessible, particularly in the technical details.
*  Reliance on Euclidean distance within the Sinkhorn relaxation might be suboptimal.
*  Limited discussion of failure cases and hyperparameter sensitivity.

**5. Specific Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Section 3.1:**  The explanation of the gradient computation could be expanded. Perhaps adding a step-by-step breakdown with more intuitive explanations.
* **Section 4:**  Provide a table summarizing the best hyperparameters found for each dataset.
* **Figure 2:**  The visualization of learned word weights is effective.  Perhaps add an example of the negative weights (words that are downweighted) as well.
* **Related Work:**  Briefly discuss recent advances in faster Wasserstein distance computation beyond Cuturi’s work (e.g., algorithms based on stochastic gradient descent).

**6. Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the minor weaknesses in clarity and the potential limitations of the Sinkhorn relaxation, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the drawbacks. The demonstrated performance gains, the computational efficiency, and the novelty of the approach make this a valuable contribution to the field. I strongly recommend **acceptance** to NIPS 2016.
