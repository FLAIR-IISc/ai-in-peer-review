This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel approach to learning a metric for the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea—combining a linear transformation of word embeddings with re-weighted word frequencies to optimize WMD for a specific task—is genuinely interesting and potentially impactful. It builds on the recent success of WMD by Kusner et al. and addresses a key limitation: the unsupervised nature of the original method. The authors cleverly leverage the Sinkhorn distance to address computational challenges, and present a comprehensive empirical evaluation across eight datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to a large number of baselines.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Learning a metric *within* the word embedding space, rather than attempting to represent documents directly, is a fresh perspective. The combination of linear transformation and word re-weighting is a strong contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical grounding, particularly the use of the Sinkhorn distance for efficient computation, appears solid. The gradient derivation, while complex, is well-explained.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive comparison against 26 baselines across 8 datasets is commendable. The consistent performance gains are convincing.
*   **Efficiency:** The authors address the computational complexity of WMD and propose a practical solution through the relaxed optimization.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details. The motivation is well articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention Bayesian optimization, the specific hyperparameter settings and their sensitivity analysis are not detailed enough. Understanding the robustness of the method to parameter changes would be valuable.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** While the initialization (S-WMD init) is evaluated, a more detailed ablation study investigating the contribution of *each* component (linear transformation vs. word re-weighting) would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Theoretical Justification of Squared Euclidean Distance:** The choice of squared Euclidean distance in the objective is not thoroughly justified. Exploring other distance metrics might be beneficial.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** Although the authors demonstrate improvements in efficiency, the computational cost still scales with document size. The applicability of S-WMD to extremely large documents might be limited. While mentioned, a more in-depth discussion of scalability bottlenecks is needed.
*   **Visualization Interpretation:** The visualization with t-SNE is helpful, but could be enriched with further analysis. For example, discussing *which* words are being upweighted in different classes to offer better intuition.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Highly novel and promising approach to supervised document distance learning.
*   Strong empirical results across a diverse range of datasets.
*   Addresses a significant limitation of the original WMD.
*   Technically sound and well-motivated.
*   Efficient implementation using the Sinkhorn distance.

**Con:**

*   Hyperparameter sensitivity requires further investigation.
*   Limited ablation study hampers a deeper understanding of the method’s components.
*   Scalability to very large documents remains a concern.
*   The justification for the squared Euclidean distance is weak.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a highly original idea. While there are minor weaknesses, the novelty and empirical success of S-WMD are compelling. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of document distance learning. I am particularly impressed by the creative use of the Sinkhorn distance and the comprehensive empirical evaluation. I believe this work has the potential to be highly influential, and that future research will build upon it.

**Recommendation:** Accept. While not perfect, the innovation and potential impact are high enough to outweigh the minor flaws. I encourage the authors to address the points raised in the weaknesses section in future work.
