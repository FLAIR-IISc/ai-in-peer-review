This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance by learning a linear transformation of word representations and re-weighted word frequencies. The motivation stems from the observation that unsupervised methods like WMD may not be optimal for task-specific document distance calculations. The authors address the computational challenges of supervised WMD by leveraging the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering. They demonstrate strong performance across 8 datasets and 26 baselines.

While the problem addressed is relevant – adapting document distance metrics to specific tasks – and the experimental results are comprehensive, I have significant reservations about the novelty and overall contribution. The paper feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. The core idea of learning a transformation on word embeddings to improve document distance is not entirely new; prior work exists in metric learning applied to embeddings (though not specifically within the WMD framework, a point the authors acknowledge). The proposed method feels like a skillful application of existing techniques (metric learning, Sinkhorn distance approximation) to a specific problem, rather than a substantial methodological innovation.

The strength of the paper lies in the thoroughness of the experimental evaluation. The authors compare against a large number of baselines, including variations of standard document representations and metric learning techniques. The datasets are diverse, and the reported results consistently show S-WMD performing competitively, often achieving state-of-the-art results. The inclusion of training time measurements is also helpful. However, even with extensive comparisons, the gains over strong baselines often appear modest.

Several weaknesses raise concerns. First, the reliance on word2vec embeddings introduces a dependency on a pre-trained model. The paper doesn't sufficiently explore the sensitivity of the results to the choice of embedding. Are the observed improvements robust across different embedding models? Second, the linear transformation of word embeddings, while computationally efficient, feels potentially restrictive. More complex, non-linear transformations might yield further improvements, but are dismissed without justification. Third, while the Sinkhorn approximation is cleverly employed for computational efficiency, the authors don’t thoroughly analyze the impact of the regularization parameter (λ) on the quality of the distance metric. A more detailed ablation study investigating the sensitivity to λ is needed. Finally, the discussion of related work, while extensive, misses opportunities to more deeply contextualize the contribution within the broader landscape of metric learning and optimal transport.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical performance across a diverse set of datasets.
*   Comprehensive comparison against a large number of baselines.
*   Addresses a relevant and practical problem in document analysis.
*   Effective use of the Sinkhorn approximation to address computational challenges.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty: The core idea is an application of known techniques, not a groundbreaking innovation.
*   Modest gains over strong baselines.
*   Potential limitations of the linear transformation and the sensitivity to the word embedding choice.
*   Insufficient analysis of the regularization parameter in the Sinkhorn approximation.
*   The paper lacks a deeper theoretical justification for the proposed method.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the paper is technically sound and presents compelling experimental results, its lack of substantial novelty and theoretical depth limit its overall contribution. The extensive empirical evaluation is commendable, but the modest gains and potential limitations of the approach raise concerns. I believe the paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of the design choices (embedding model, transformation complexity, regularization parameter) and a more nuanced discussion of its relationship to existing work. It currently feels more like an engineering effort to optimize an existing technique than a fundamental scientific advance. I suggest acceptance with the expectation that the authors will address the reviewers' concerns in a revised version.
