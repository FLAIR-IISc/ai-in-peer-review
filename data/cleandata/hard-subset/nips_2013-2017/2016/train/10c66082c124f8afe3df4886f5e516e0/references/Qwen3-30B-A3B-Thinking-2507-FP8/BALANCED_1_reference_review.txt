

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method to learn a metric for document distances by incorporating supervision into the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) framework. The authors build on Kusner et al.'s WMD (ICML 2015), which leverages word embeddings to compute document distances without learning document representations. S-WMD extends this by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to optimize document distances for classification tasks. The key innovation is using Sinkhorn distance approximation to make the computation feasible, combined with a stochastic neighborhood loss function inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-motivated extension of WMD with solid theoretical grounding. The authors carefully address computational challenges through the Sinkhorn approximation and batch gradient descent, with thorough complexity analysis. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, testing on 8 datasets across multiple text classification tasks with 26 baselines, including both unsupervised and supervised methods.

2. **Significant Results:** S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance on 5 out of 8 datasets, outperforming all baselines on average. The improvements over unsupervised WMD (e.g., 51% reduction in error on OHSUMED) are substantial and well-documented. The visualization of t-SNE embeddings (Figure 1) convincingly demonstrates improved clustering quality.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized with clear sections. The background on WMD, metric learning, and Sinkhorn distance is thorough and accessible. The algorithm pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and gradient derivations are presented with sufficient detail. The writing is precise and free of unnecessary jargon.

4. **Novelty:** While building on prior work (WMD, metric learning), the specific combination of learning a linear transformation of word embeddings with histogram re-weighting within the WMD framework represents a meaningful contribution. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work (e.g., Wang & Guibas [34], Wang et al. [35]) by introducing word importance weights.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper notes S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON, but provides only superficial explanations (e.g., "long document lengths" for BBCSPORT). A deeper analysis of why the method fails on certain datasets would strengthen the contribution and provide valuable insights for future work.

2. **Missing Comparison to Modern Approaches:** The evaluation compares against traditional text representation methods (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) but omits recent deep learning approaches like BERT or other contextualized embeddings that have become standard in text classification. This limits the paper's relevance to current practice.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper uses λ=10 for the Sinkhorn approximation but doesn't thoroughly explore how performance varies with this key hyperparameter. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims about the method's robustness.

4. **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees about the convergence of the optimization procedure or the optimality of the learned metric. A brief discussion of these aspects would enhance the paper's scientific contribution.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of metric learning for text classification. The technical approach is sound, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results demonstrate clear improvements over prior work. The method is both theoretically grounded and computationally efficient, making it suitable for real-world applications.

The main weaknesses are the lack of comparison to modern deep learning approaches for text classification and insufficient analysis of failure cases. These are significant but not fatal flaws for a NeurIPS submission.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Add comparisons against recent deep learning approaches for text classification (e.g., fine-tuned BERT, contextualized embeddings) to better position the work within current NLP research.

2. Include a detailed analysis of why S-WMD underperforms on specific datasets (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON), including ablation studies on the word importance weights and linear transformation.

3. Conduct a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for the Sinkhorn approximation parameter λ and potentially other key parameters.

4. Add a brief discussion of theoretical properties of the optimization procedure (e.g., convergence guarantees) to strengthen the theoretical foundation.

5. Consider extending the visualization to show how the learned word importance weights correlate with domain-specific knowledge.

Overall, this is a well-executed and significant contribution to the field of document distances. With the suggested improvements addressing the limited comparison to modern methods and failure case analysis, this paper would be a strong candidate for NeurIPS. As it stands, it is a solid contribution that meets the high standards of NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
