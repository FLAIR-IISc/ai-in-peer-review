

# Review: Supervised Word Mover's Distance

## Summary

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a document distance metric by extending Kusner et al.'s unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) [19]. While WMD leverages pre-trained word embeddings to compute document distances as the optimal transport cost between word distributions, S-WMD incorporates supervision by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights. The authors propose a computationally efficient approach using Sinkhorn distance [6] to approximate the optimal transport problem, enabling gradient-based optimization of the document distance to minimize kNN classification error. The method is evaluated across 8 document classification datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on 5 out of 8 datasets compared to 26 baselines including unsupervised WMD, WCD, and various document representations with supervised metric learning.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-justified theoretical derivations. The authors provide a clear formulation of the supervised metric learning problem within the WMD framework, including rigorous derivations of gradients for both the linear transformation and word importance weights. The Sinkhorn distance approximation is well-motivated as a practical solution to the cubic complexity of exact WMD computation. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with 8 datasets, 26 baselines, and multiple statistical analyses including average rank across datasets. The results demonstrate statistically significant improvements on 5 out of 8 datasets, with particularly strong relative improvements (e.g., 42% on CLASSIC). The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations, noting that S-WMD underperforms on some datasets due to specific characteristics like document length (BBCSPORT, OHSUMED) or initialization issues (CLASSIC).

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section effectively introduces WMD, word embeddings, and metric learning in a logical progression. Mathematical formulations are clearly presented with sufficient explanation, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with machine learning concepts. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is precise and well-structured, and the complexity analysis provides valuable insights into the computational efficiency of the method. The visualization of learned word weights (Figure 2) and t-SNE embeddings (Figure 1) effectively illustrate the method's behavior and advantages over unsupervised WMD. The paper provides enough detail for reproduction, including specific hyperparameters (λ=10) and implementation details.

## Originality

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by extending unsupervised WMD to a supervised setting, addressing a clear limitation of prior work. While WMD was introduced in a NIPS 2015 paper [19], the authors present a novel approach to incorporating supervision that differs significantly from prior metric learning methods. The key innovations are: (1) learning word-specific importance weights to emphasize discriminative words, and (2) using Sinkhorn distance to enable efficient optimization of the supervised metric. The paper adequately contextualizes these contributions against related work, including metric learning methods like NCA [15], LMNN [36], and ITML [10], and recent work on learning ground distances for EMD [7, 34, 35]. The approach is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS papers on metric learning and text representations.

## Significance

The method achieves significant improvements in document classification performance across multiple datasets, with S-WMD outperforming all 26 baselines on average. The relative improvements on specific datasets (e.g., 42% on CLASSIC) are substantial and demonstrate the value of the supervised approach. The computational efficiency of the method (training time comparable to other metric learning approaches) makes it practical for real-world applications. The ability to learn which words are most discriminative for classification (Figure 2) provides valuable interpretability. While the paper focuses on document classification, the approach could potentially be extended to other text retrieval tasks where document distances are important.

## Strengths
- Novel extension of WMD to supervised learning with meaningful improvements
- Well-motivated use of Sinkhorn distance for computational efficiency
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across 8 datasets with 26 baselines
- Clear theoretical derivations and algorithm description
- Effective visualization of learned word importance weights
- Practical implementation details and computational complexity analysis

## Weaknesses
- Limited explanation for underperformance on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON datasets (only mentions possible reasons)
- The initialization method (S-WCD) is somewhat ad-hoc without theoretical justification
- Does not explore non-linear transformations of word embeddings, which might yield further improvements
- The paper could better discuss the relationship between the learned word weights and semantic meaning

## Pro/Con Acceptance
**Pro:**
- Significant performance improvements over multiple baselines
- Clear theoretical foundation and well-executed experiments
- Practical computational efficiency for real-world application
- Novel contribution that extends an important NIPS 2015 paper
- Strong relevance to text classification and retrieval tasks

**Con:**
- Limited analysis of failure cases on specific datasets
- The initialization method could be better justified
- Does not explore more complex model architectures beyond linear transformations

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant extension of the Word Mover's Distance to a supervised learning setting. The method is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated across multiple document classification tasks. The computational efficiency achieved through the Sinkhorn distance approximation makes the approach practical for real-world applications. While the paper could provide more analysis of the failure cases on specific datasets and better justify the initialization method, these are minor concerns given the strong overall contribution. The results demonstrate clear improvements over a large set of baselines, making this an important contribution to the field of text representation and metric learning. The paper meets all four criteria for a high-quality NIPS paper.
