This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by learning a linear transformation of word representations and re-weighted word frequencies. The core idea is to minimize leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric, leveraging a smoothed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) for computational efficiency. The authors present results on 8 datasets, claiming superiority over 26 baseline methods.

While the motivation—incorporating supervision into WMD—is reasonable, the paper suffers from a lack of convincing justification for the complexity introduced. WMD is already a relatively sophisticated approach, and the gains demonstrated, while statistically significant in some cases, do not appear substantial enough to warrant the added algorithmic overhead and hyperparameter tuning. The central contribution feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. 

The authors correctly identify the computational challenge of incorporating supervision directly into WMD and present a sensible approach using the Sinkhorn distance. However, this relaxation introduces approximation errors which are not thoroughly analyzed or discussed in terms of their impact on the final results. The probabilistic filtering of the training set also feels ad-hoc and its justification is weak. More rigorous analysis of the error introduced by these approximations would strengthen the paper considerably.

The experimental evaluation is extensive, with comparisons to a large number of baselines. However, the presentation of results is somewhat overwhelming. A more focused comparison to the most relevant baselines (e.g., WMD with different word embeddings, metric learning applied to WCD) would have been more insightful. Furthermore, the ablation studies are lacking; it's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from the linear transformation *versus* the word re-weighting. Presenting results with only one component active would clarify the individual contributions. While the authors acknowledge the potential influence of document length, their attempt to mitigate this through initialization seems insufficient and doesn't fully address the issue.

The paper’s clarity is mixed. The background material is well-presented, providing necessary context for readers unfamiliar with WMD and metric learning. However, the derivation of the gradients and the details of the optimization procedure are dense and could benefit from more thorough explanation. The notation is generally consistent but could be improved for readability.

The originality of the approach is moderate. Applying metric learning to WMD is a natural extension, and the use of the Sinkhorn distance is a well-established technique. The combination is novel, but not profoundly so. Related work is reasonably covered, although the connections to other recent work on learning ground distances for EMD/WMD (e.g., Wang & Guibas, 2017) could be more explicitly discussed.

The significance of the work is questionable. While the reported improvements are statistically significant, the practical impact is limited. The computational cost remains significant, and the benefits do not clearly outweigh the added complexity. It’s unclear if the proposed method will be widely adopted by the research community or used in practical applications.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem – improving document distance measures.
*   Presents a technically sound solution leveraging existing techniques (Sinkhorn distance, metric learning).
*   Extensive experimental evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution – the performance gains are modest and don't justify the added complexity.
*   Insufficient analysis of approximation errors introduced by the Sinkhorn distance.
*   Lack of ablation studies to disentangle the contributions of different components.
*   Questionable practical significance.
*   Overly complex presentation of results.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. The paper presents a technically sound, but ultimately incremental, contribution. The benefits do not clearly outweigh the costs, and the paper lacks the originality and significance expected of a NIPS publication. While the experimental evaluation is thorough, it doesn't convincingly demonstrate a substantial advance over existing methods. I recommend the authors focus on a more thorough analysis of the approximations and a more targeted evaluation against the most relevant baselines before resubmitting.
