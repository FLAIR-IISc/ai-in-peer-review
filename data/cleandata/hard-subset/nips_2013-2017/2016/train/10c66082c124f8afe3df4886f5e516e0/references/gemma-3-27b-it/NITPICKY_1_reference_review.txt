This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance by learning a linear transformation of word representations and re-weighted word frequencies. The core idea is to minimize leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric, leveraging a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) for computational efficiency. The authors demonstrate strong performance across 8 datasets and 26 baselines.

While the paper presents a conceptually sound and potentially impactful approach, its execution suffers from a remarkable number of minor, yet cumulatively significant, flaws. The writing style, though generally clear, is occasionally imprecise, and the presentation of mathematical derivations lacks rigor. More critically, the experimental validation, while extensive, is marred by inconsistencies in hyperparameter tuning and a somewhat superficial analysis of results.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of directly learning a metric *within* the WMD framework, rather than solely focusing on document representations, is a valuable contribution. The addition of word importance weights is also a sensible extension.
* **Performance:** The empirical results, despite the concerns noted below, demonstrate competitive performance against a large number of baselines. The consistent outperformance across many datasets suggests the method has genuine merit.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance is a clever way to address the computational bottleneck of WMD, making supervised learning feasible.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of unsupervised approaches and provides a strong rationale for incorporating supervision into the WMD framework.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Mathematical Rigor:** The derivation of the gradient with respect to *w* relies on the relaxed optimal dual variables, claiming they are obtained via the vectors *u, v*. However, the connection between *u,v* and the true dual variables is not fully justified, particularly with the relaxation in place.  The claim that the approximation becomes more accurate as λ grows needs more justification; larger λ can also lead to instability.  The text glosses over crucial details regarding the uniqueness of the optimal transport solution.
* **Implementation Details & Reproducibility:** The description of the Bayesian Optimization (BO) process for hyperparameter tuning is insufficient. What search space was used? What acquisition function? The lack of these details hinders reproducibility.  The statement that results are averaged over five 70/30 splits *only* for certain datasets is strange and unexplained.
* **Inconsistent Evaluation:** The use of PCA for dimensionality reduction *before* metric learning for baselines, while standard, isn't consistently applied across all baselines. The rationale for the 200-dimensional reduction isn’t given. The choice to use squared Euclidean distance in eq. (5) feels arbitrary and isn't thoroughly justified.
* **Overstated Claims:** The claim that S-WMD is "the best performing distance metric for documents to date" is a significant overstatement, given that performance variations exist across datasets and the margin of improvement isn't overwhelming in many cases.
* **Minor Errors & Inconsistencies:** Several minor errors and inconsistencies exist: The equation numbering is irregular (e.g., eq. (11) appears after the conclusion). The citation style is inconsistent.  In Section 3.1, "eq. (2)" is referenced, but is actually eq. (5) in the paper. Figure 1 caption is overly brief and lacks crucial information about the datasets shown.
* **Related Work Coverage:** While extensive, the related work section could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of existing metric learning approaches *specifically applied to word embeddings* beyond simply mentioning them.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is novel and addresses an important problem.
* The experimental results are promising, even with the reservations noted above.
* The proposed computational efficiency improvements are significant.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of mathematical rigor and detail weakens the technical contribution.
* The inconsistencies in the experimental setup and analysis raise concerns about the reliability of the results.
* The overblown claims detract from the overall credibility of the work.
* The cumulative effect of numerous minor flaws suggests a lack of careful attention to detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty and potential impact of the proposed method, the numerous flaws outlined above lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the paper demonstrates promising results, the lack of mathematical rigor, inconsistencies in experimental evaluation, and overstated claims undermine its credibility. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a worthwhile contribution to the field. The authors need to demonstrate a higher level of technical precision and experimental thoroughness before this work is ready for publication in NIPS. I believe that a major revision addressing these points would be necessary for reconsideration.
