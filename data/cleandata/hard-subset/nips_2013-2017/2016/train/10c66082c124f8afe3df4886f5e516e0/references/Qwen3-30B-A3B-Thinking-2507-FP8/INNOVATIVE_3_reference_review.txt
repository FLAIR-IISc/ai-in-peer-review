

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes a novel extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) framework by incorporating supervision to learn a metric that better captures semantic similarity for document classification. The core contribution is the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), which learns both a linear transformation of word embeddings and word-specific importance weights to improve document distances. The authors address the computational challenges of applying supervision to WMD through an efficient Sinkhorn distance approximation and probabilistic filtering of training data.

The paper makes a compelling case for the novelty of its approach. While metric learning has been extensively studied for document representations, this work is the first to apply supervision directly to the WMD framework, which operates on word embeddings rather than document representations. This is a significant conceptual leap that allows the model to leverage the semantic structure of word embeddings while adapting to specific classification tasks. The authors build elegantly on prior work (NCA, Sinkhorn distance) while introducing a new direction in text distance learning.

The technical execution is strong. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the supervised WMD, derive efficient gradient computations using the Sinkhorn relaxation, and implement a practical optimization strategy with batch gradient descent and a clever initialization via supervised word centroid distance. The computational complexity analysis shows that their approach is practical, with training times comparable to standard metric learning methods.

The experimental results are impressive. The paper evaluates S-WMD across 8 diverse document classification tasks, comparing against 26 baselines including BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA, WCD, and WMD. S-WMD achieves the lowest kNN error on 5 out of 8 datasets and outperforms all baselines on average. The visualization results (t-SNE embeddings) provide intuitive evidence that S-WMD produces better-separated classes than WMD. The authors also provide a thoughtful analysis of why S-WMD underperforms on certain datasets (e.g., CLASSIC), showing they've carefully considered limitations.

What makes this paper stand out is the conceptual novelty. The authors identify a fundamental gap in the WMD framework (unsupervised nature) and propose a principled solution that directly addresses this limitation. This isn't just an incremental improvement on existing methods—it's a new direction for how we think about document distances. The idea of learning word-specific importance weights to emphasize discriminative words for classification tasks is particularly insightful.

The paper is well-written and clear, with excellent organization. The background section effectively contextualizes the work within metric learning and optimal transport theory. The experimental setup is thorough, with multiple datasets, careful hyperparameter tuning, and appropriate baselines. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., performance on CLASSIC dataset) without overstating their results.

While the improvement over WMD is not dramatic on all datasets (only 5 out of 8), this is a common occurrence in machine learning and doesn't diminish the significance of the core contribution. The paper's novelty and the practical nature of the extension make it a strong contribution to the field.

The only minor weaknesses are that the paper doesn't fully explore why S-WMD underperforms on certain datasets (beyond the CLASSIC example), and the initialization method could be more theoretically grounded. However, these are minor compared to the paper's substantial contributions.

In summary, this paper presents a bold, novel approach to document distance learning that significantly extends the WMD framework. The conceptual contribution is substantial, the technical execution is sound, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate its superiority over existing methods. The paper's novelty and potential impact on text classification and retrieval make it a strong candidate for NeurIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Strengths:**
- Genuine novelty: First supervised extension of WMD framework
- Conceptually bold approach to adapting word embeddings for specific tasks
- Strong experimental results across diverse datasets
- Practical computational solution to a non-trivial problem
- Well-written with clear theoretical foundation

**Weaknesses:**
- Improvement over WMD not uniform across all datasets
- Initialization method could be more theoretically motivated
- Limited discussion of why S-WMD underperforms on specific datasets

This paper represents a significant step forward in document distance learning, with the potential to influence future work in text representation and similarity. The novelty of the approach justifies overlooking minor methodological limitations, and the results demonstrate clear practical value.
