### Summary

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific distance metric for documents. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), an unsupervised metric that calculates the minimum cost to "transport" words from one document to another in a pre-trained word embedding space. The key insight of this paper is that for specific classification tasks, this distance can be significantly improved by incorporating supervision. The authors propose learning two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space to better align word-level distances with the task's notion of similarity, and (2) a vector `w` of word-importance weights to up-weight or down-weight words in the document's bag-of-words representation.

To make this learning tractable, the authors make several crucial technical contributions. They formulate the learning problem by minimizing a differentiable surrogate of the k-NN classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A major challenge is that computing gradients through the WMD optimization problem is computationally prohibitive and non-trivial. The authors cleverly overcome this by replacing the exact WMD (a linear program) with its entropy-regularized version, the Sinkhorn distance, which has a fast, differentiable, and strongly convex formulation. This allows for efficient gradient computation with respect to both `A` and `w`. The final algorithm is made practical through a smart initialization scheme (using a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance) and a batched, nearest-neighbor-focused stochastic gradient descent procedure. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of S-WMD through extensive experiments on 8 datasets, showing state-of-the-art performance against 26 strong baselines.

### Strengths

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a very natural and important problem: how to inject supervision into one of the most powerful recent unsupervised document distances (WMD). The proposed solution is both elegant and effective. Learning a transformation of the embedding space and re-weighting the words are two complementary and powerful ideas. The resulting S-WMD method demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art in document classification, as shown by the comprehensive experimental results. This work is likely to be highly influential and widely used by practitioners and researchers.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Quality:** The technical execution is excellent. The core difficulty of the problem lies in the computational complexity and non-differentiability of the standard WMD. The use of the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance is a key insight that makes the entire framework feasible. The derivation of the gradients for both the matrix `A` and the weight vector `w` (leveraging the dual variables of the relaxed transport problem) is sound. Furthermore, the authors address the practical challenges of a non-convex objective function and slow per-iteration complexity with well-motivated engineering solutions: a principled initialization via S-WCD and an efficient batching/neighbor-filtering strategy. This demonstrates a deep understanding of both the theory and the practicalities of the problem.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It begins with a clear introduction to the necessary background concepts (word2vec, WMD, Sinkhorn distance, NCA), making the work accessible. The methodology is presented logically, starting from the high-level objective function and progressively detailing the gradient computation and optimization strategies. The experimental section is thorough, the baselines are well-chosen, and the results are presented clearly in tables and compelling visualizations (t-SNE plots and word clouds). The work is a complete and polished contribution.

4.  **Extensive and Convincing Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength. The authors compare their method against a very large and diverse set of 26 baselines on 8 different datasets, spanning multiple tasks. These baselines include classical methods (TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), deep learning approaches (mSDA), and applying standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML) to these representations. S-WMD's superior performance across this gauntlet of tests provides very strong evidence for its effectiveness.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

1.  **Ablation Study:** The model learns two distinct components: the linear transformation `A` and the word weights `w`. It would be very insightful to include an ablation study to understand the relative contribution of each component. For instance, how does a model with a fixed identity matrix `A` (only learning `w`) perform? And how does a model with fixed uniform weights `w` (only learning `A`) perform? This would provide a clearer picture of which component is more critical and how they interact.

2.  **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The Sinkhorn relaxation introduces a key hyperparameter, λ, which controls the trade-off between approximation quality and computational speed. The authors state they use λ=10, but a brief analysis of how sensitive the final classification accuracy is to the choice of λ would strengthen the paper's claims of robustness.

3.  **Discussion of Limitations:** The complexity of the gradient computation is O(p^2) where p is the number of unique words in a document. While a huge improvement over the original WMD, this could still be a bottleneck for very long documents with large vocabularies. A brief discussion of this limitation and potential avenues for future work (e.g., further approximations) would be beneficial.

4.  **Initialization:** The paper correctly points out the importance of the S-WCD initialization. To further highlight its importance, it would be interesting to see how S-WMD performs with a simpler initialization, such as `A` as an identity matrix and `w` as the ones vector. This would help quantify the benefit of the proposed initialization procedure.

### Justification for Recommendation

Overall, this is an outstanding paper that presents a novel, technically sound, and highly effective method for a significant problem in machine learning and natural language processing. The combination of ideas from optimal transport, metric learning, and word embeddings is creative and well-executed. The empirical results are comprehensive and convincing, establishing a new state-of-the-art. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to suggestions for additional analysis that would further strengthen an already excellent paper. The work is of high quality, originality, and significance, making it a perfect fit for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
