### **Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which leverages word embeddings to calculate the optimal transport cost between two documents. The key insight of this work is that the unsupervised WMD can be significantly improved by incorporating supervision from labeled data.

The authors propose learning two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, which adapts the geometric relationships between words for the specific task, and (2) a word importance vector `w`, which re-weights the contribution of each word in a document. The learning objective is to minimize a stochastic version of the k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) leave-one-out classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA).

A major technical challenge is the high computational cost and non-differentiability of the WMD optimization problem. The authors overcome this by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized version, the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation makes the problem differentiable and computationally efficient (quadratic instead of cubic time), enabling gradient-based optimization. The paper further proposes practical speed-ups, including a clever initialization scheme (using NCA on the Word Centroid Distance) and a batch-based training procedure that considers only a local neighborhood of documents. The method is evaluated on 8 diverse document classification datasets, where it is shown to outperform an extensive set of 26 baselines, including WMD and various document representation methods combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important problem: adapting powerful unsupervised document distance metrics for supervised tasks. While metric learning is a mature field, its application to the WMD framework is novel and impactful. The idea of learning the metric within the low-dimensional word embedding space, rather than the high-dimensional document space, is elegant and circumvents the scalability issues that plague many metric learning methods on text data. The simultaneous learning of a spatial transformation (`A`) and feature re-weighting (`w`) is a powerful combination.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper's core technical contribution—the use of the Sinkhorn distance to create a differentiable and efficient objective function for learning—is excellent. This is a critical step that makes the entire framework computationally feasible. The derivation of the gradients for both the matrix `A` and the weight vector `w` appears sound and leverages properties of the relaxed optimal transport dual solution. The proposed optimization strategy, including the NCA-based initialization and nearest-neighbor filtering for batch updates, demonstrates careful thought towards making the method practical.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is exceptionally thorough and convincing.
    *   **Breadth:** The method is tested on 8 datasets covering different domains (news, sentiment, etc.), demonstrating its general applicability.
    *   **Baselines:** The comparison against 26 baselines is comprehensive and includes classic methods (TF-IDF, LSI), modern representation learning (mSDA), WMD, and these representations combined with strong metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). This rigorous comparison firmly establishes the state-of-the-art performance of S-WMD.
    *   **Results:** S-WMD achieves the best performance on 5 out of 8 datasets and the best average rank overall. The gains over the unsupervised WMD and other supervised methods are substantial. The qualitative visualizations (t-SNE plots and word importance clouds) provide good intuition and support the quantitative results.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the background section effectively sets the stage, and the proposed method is described in a logical and understandable manner. The inclusion of an algorithm pseudo-code and a complexity analysis is helpful for reproducibility.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Limited Ablation Study:** The paper convincingly shows that S-WMD outperforms its initialization (S-WCD). However, S-WMD learns two components: the transformation `A` and the weights `w`. A more detailed ablation study that isolates the contribution of each component would provide valuable insights. For example, how does a model with only `A` (i.e., `w` fixed to ones) or only `w` (i.e., `A` fixed to identity) perform? This would help clarify whether the geometric transformation or the word re-weighting is the dominant factor for improvement across different tasks.

2.  **Discussion on the Squared Euclidean Ground Metric:** The method is formulated using the squared Euclidean distance (`||A(xi - xj)||_2^2`) as the ground metric between word vectors. The authors correctly note that this might be suboptimal for the CLASSIC dataset, where the original WMD (which often uses L2, not squared L2) performs better. A brief discussion on the implications of this choice would be beneficial. The squared L2 norm is convenient for differentiability, but it would be useful to mention whether the framework could be extended to other norms and what the associated challenges might be.

3.  **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The paper states that the Sinkhorn regularization parameter `λ` is set to 10. While this is a reasonable choice, the performance of the Sinkhorn approximation and the convergence speed of the optimization are sensitive to this parameter. A short discussion or experiment on the sensitivity of S-WMD's performance to the choice of `λ` would strengthen the paper's claim of robustness.

4.  **Comparison to End-to-End Supervised Models:** The comparison is focused on kNN-based classification methods. While this is the natural setting for a metric learning paper, the document classification field is dominated by deep learning models like CNNs, LSTMs, and more recently, Transformers. It would be valuable to add context by briefly discussing how S-WMD fits into this broader landscape. While a full experimental comparison may be out of scope, acknowledging these methods and positioning S-WMD as a strong, data-efficient alternative for kNN-based retrieval and classification would be helpful.

### **Summary of Pros and Cons**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and practical problem in a novel way.
*   Elegant formulation that learns a metric in the low-dimensional word embedding space.
*   Clever use of the Sinkhorn distance makes the approach computationally feasible and efficient.
*   Extensive, state-of-the-art results on multiple benchmarks against a very strong set of baselines.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Lack of an ablation study to disentangle the effects of the learned matrix `A` and the weights `w`.
*   Limited discussion of key design choices, such as the ground metric norm and the sensitivity to hyperparameter `λ`.

### **Final Recommendation**

This is a very strong paper with a novel idea, solid technical execution, and an outstanding empirical evaluation. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to suggestions for further analysis that would strengthen the paper rather than invalidate its core contributions. The work represents a significant advance in metric learning for text data and is highly likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
