Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of WMD that learns (i) a linear transformation A of pre-trained word embeddings and (ii) per-word importance weights w to better align the document distance with labels. The distance between two documents is still computed as an optimal transport (OT) cost in the embedding space, but supervision is injected by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective (à la NCA). To make training tractable, the authors approximate the OT problem using entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distances and derive approximate gradients via the primal (transport plan) and dual (potentials) of the smoothed OT. They further accelerate training using (a) an NCA-initialization learned on the Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) and (b) neighbor pruning using the cheaper WCD. Experiments on 8 datasets against 26 baselines show S-WMD often achieves the best kNN accuracy.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds directly on WMD (Kusner et al., ICML’15), Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi, NIPS’13), and classical metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML). There is also prior work on learning ground metrics for EMD/OT (Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.) and supervised use of Wasserstein losses (Frogner et al.). The novelty here is combining NCA-style supervision with an OT-based document distance in embedding space, with joint learning of A and w and a practical Sinkhorn-based training loop.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: WMD is strong but unsupervised; learning to adapt the ground metric and word weights to labels is natural and potentially impactful.
- Sensible use of established tools: NCA objective, Sinkhorn OT, and centroid-based pruning are well-understood and appropriate.
- Practical algorithmic contributions: Approximate gradients via smoothed OT and dual potentials; batching and neighbor filtering; a workable initialization (S-WCD).
- Broad empirical comparison: 8 datasets, many baselines (unsupervised and supervised with metric learning), and publicly available code.
- Gains in several settings: Best results on 5/8 datasets; strong t-SNE visualizations suggest better class separation.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Methodological clarity and rigor:
  - The loss is defined in terms of DA,w (unregularized WMD), but gradients are computed with T*λ and (αλ, βλ) from the smoothed problem. It is unclear whether the algorithm is optimizing a coherent surrogate objective (e.g., loss composed with regularized distance) or simply using a biased gradient estimate. A formal statement/justification (e.g., optimizing L(A,w) with Dλ instead of D) is needed.
  - Constraints and regularization are under-specified. Are there constraints w ≥ 0, w ≠ 0 enforced during optimization? Is A regularized (e.g., Frobenius norm, low-rank r, orthogonality)? Without regularization, overfitting is a real risk given the flexibility of A and w.
  - Notation has inconsistencies (p used both for norm exponent and word count; Section 3.2 header formatting; expressions for dual variables include unexplained normalizations). This hinders reproducibility.

- Experimental protocol:
  - Hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian optimization is mentioned but the validation protocol is unclear. Using test data in any way for tuning would inflate results; please clarify whether a held-out validation set or nested CV was used for all methods.
  - LDA is trained transductively (train+test); while common in some prior work, this makes comparisons less clean. Either justify this consistently across methods or report inductive LDA as well.
  - kNN classification is the sole quantitative task. Document distances are also used in retrieval/ranking; adding retrieval experiments (MAP/NDCG) would strengthen claims.
  - Ablations are missing: effect of λ; effect of neighbor set size N and batch size B; contribution of w vs A (e.g., A-only, w-only); sensitivity to the squared vs unsquared Euclidean ground metric; impact of the WCD-based neighbor pruning on final accuracy.
  - Fairness of baselines: For representation methods (LDA, mSDA, TF-IDF), Euclidean kNN may not be the most suitable similarity measure or classifier. It would help to include standard classifiers per representation (e.g., linear SVM/logistic on TF-IDF/mSDA) to contextualize S-WMD’s absolute performance.

- Significance/novelty:
  - The technical novelty is incremental: NCA-style supervision with Sinkhorn OT and word weighting is a reasonable amalgam of known components. Prior work has learned ground metrics for EMD/OT; highlighting what is fundamentally new beyond the combination (e.g., the w reweighting within OT vs TF-IDF, and empirical benefits) would help.
  - Improvements are not universal (5/8 best), and in some long-document datasets simpler supervised baselines perform on par or better. Stronger evidence (statistical tests, effect sizes) would be useful.

- Complexity/runtime:
  - Theoretical complexity is discussed, but actual wall-clock comparisons vs strong baselines (including supervised TF-IDF+NCA/LMNN) are not fully shown in the excerpt. Given the O(p^2) dependence, analysis of scalability to longer documents/vocabularies is important.

Questions for the authors
1. Are you actually minimizing the NCA loss composed with the regularized distance Dλ (with λ fixed), or with the original DA,w and using approximate gradients? Please formalize the objective being optimized.
2. What constraints/regularizers are imposed on A and w (e.g., w ≥ 0, ||A||F penalties)? How are r and λ selected?
3. How is hyperparameter tuning performed across all methods—do you use a validation split distinct from the test set (or nested CV)?
4. How sensitive is performance to λ and the neighbor pruning size N? What is the trade-off between accuracy and speed?
5. What is the effect of using only A, only w, and both? Do you enforce nonnegativity on w and how is it parameterized?
6. Does WCD-based pruning ever miss “true” hard negatives under S-WMD, and does increasing N fix this?

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and, if possible, align the loss and gradients with a well-defined smoothed objective; include a brief convergence discussion.
- Add regularization details and ablations; report sensitivity plots (λ, N, r).
- Ensure a strictly inductive evaluation (no transductive training) or clearly separate transductive results.
- Include retrieval tasks and additional classifiers for baseline representations.
- Clean up notation, fix typos, and provide full derivations in the supplement.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The idea is sensible and potentially useful, and the empirical results are promising. However, the current draft lacks methodological clarity about the optimized objective, has incomplete experimental controls/ablations, and shows mixed wins relative to strong, established baselines. With clarified objective, stronger ablations, and a more rigorous evaluation protocol, I could be persuaded to accept.

Confidence: High (familiar with OT/Sinkhorn, metric learning, and text classification).
