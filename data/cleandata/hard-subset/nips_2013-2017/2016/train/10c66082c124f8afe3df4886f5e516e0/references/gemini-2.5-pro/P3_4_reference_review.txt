**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. While WMD is powerful, it is entirely unsupervised.

The authors propose to incorporate supervision by learning two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, which adapts the geometry of word representations to the task, and (2) a word importance vector `w`, which re-weights word frequencies in the document's bag-of-words representation. The learning objective is to minimize a differentiable approximation of the k-NN leave-one-out classification error, following the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) framework.

A key challenge is that differentiating through the WMD objective, which involves solving an optimal transport linear program, is computationally prohibitive and the gradient may not be unique. The authors overcome this by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized version, the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation is strictly convex, differentiable, and can be solved much more efficiently (quadratically instead of cubically in the number of unique words). The authors derive gradients for both `A` and `w` using the primal and dual solutions of this relaxed problem. To make training practical, they propose a clever initialization scheme based on a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and employ a batch gradient descent strategy that considers only a small subset of nearest neighbors for each document. The method is evaluated on 8 text classification datasets, where it is shown to outperform a large suite of 26 strong baselines, including WMD and various document representations combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of WMD. Making this powerful distance metric supervised is a natural and highly valuable extension. The proposed method of jointly learning a transformation of the embedding space (`A`) and a re-weighting of the document histograms (`w`) is a novel and well-motivated formulation. While learning ground metrics for Earth Mover's Distance has been explored in other domains (e.g., computer vision), its application to WMD for text documents, combined with the word importance learning, constitutes a significant and original contribution.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The technical approach is elegant and well-executed. The main contribution—using the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable proxy for WMD to enable gradient-based optimization—is an excellent insight that makes the entire framework feasible. The derivations for the gradients with respect to `A` and `w` are non-trivial and appear correct. The proposed practical optimizations, especially the NCA-on-WCD initialization and the nearest-neighbor-based batch updates, are crucial for making the method computationally tractable and demonstrate a thoughtful engineering effort.

3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is exceptionally thorough and convincing.
    *   The use of 8 diverse datasets strengthens the generality of the claims.
    *   The comparison against 26 baselines is impressive. Crucially, the authors don't just compare against unsupervised methods; they compare against strong supervised baselines created by applying leading metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML) to various state-of-the-art document representations. This provides very strong evidence for the superiority of the proposed S-WMD framework.
    *   The results clearly demonstrate that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance on the majority of the datasets, often by a significant margin.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. It provides clear background on word embeddings, WMD, Sinkhorn distances, and NCA. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the proposed method is described with sufficient detail for an expert to understand the key ideas.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of an Ablation Study:** The model learns two distinct components: the transformation `A` and the weights `w`. It would be very insightful to understand the relative importance of each. An ablation study that evaluates the performance of learning only `A` (with `w=1`) and only `w` (with `A=I`) would significantly strengthen the paper by providing a deeper understanding of where the performance gains come from. Is the synergy between `A` and `w` crucial, or does one component dominate?

2.  **Limited Discussion on Scalability:** While the authors have made great strides in efficiency, the `O(p^2)` complexity with respect to the number of unique words per document (`p`) remains a potential bottleneck for very long documents. The paper notes that TF-IDF+NCA performs well on datasets with longer documents (BBCSPORT) and suggests this might be a reason. A more explicit discussion of the scalability limits of S-WMD with respect to document length would be beneficial.

3.  **Justification for Squared Euclidean Norm:** The objective in Eq. (5) uses the squared Euclidean distance `||A(xi - xj)||_2^2` as the ground metric. The original WMD paper also considered the L1 or L2 norm. The choice of the squared L2 norm is likely for mathematical convenience in the gradient derivation, but this is not explicitly stated. A brief justification for this choice and a comment on whether it might be suboptimal (as hinted in the discussion of the CLASSIC dataset result) would be helpful.

**Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant advance in the area of document similarity. The problem is important, the proposed solution is novel and technically sound, and the experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to opportunities for deeper analysis rather than fundamental flaws. The work is a natural and powerful successor to WMD and is highly likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
