## Peer Review: Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

**Paper Summary:**

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document similarity.  WMD leverages word embeddings to calculate distances between documents based on the optimal transport cost of moving words between them. The authors address the challenge of incorporating supervision into WMD without incurring prohibitive computational costs. They achieve this by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weighting word frequencies, while utilizing an efficient approximation to the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering. The core contribution lies in minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric.  Extensive experiments across 8 datasets and comparisons with 26 baselines demonstrate the superiority of S-WMD.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly on the foundation of WMD [Kusner et al., 2016] and recent advances in efficient optimal transport computation [Cuturi, 2013]. It draws inspiration from metric learning techniques like Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [Goldberger et al., 2005] but crucially adapts these methods to operate within the word embedding space, avoiding dimensionality reduction and preserving the benefits of low-dimensional representations. The authors appropriately situate their work within the broader context of document distance measures (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA) and related metric learning approaches. The comparison to other metric learning techniques like LMNN and ITML is a strength.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Applying supervised learning directly to the WMD framework is a novel and promising approach. The combination of learning a word embedding transformation *and* re-weighting word frequencies is a particularly strong contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance to approximate the optimal transport problem is well-motivated and significantly reduces computational complexity. The gradient derivation appears correct, and the initialization strategy based on S-WCD is sensible.
*   **Empirical Results:** The extensive experimental evaluation across a diverse set of datasets and a large number of baselines strongly supports the claim that S-WMD outperforms existing methods. The ablation study highlighting the benefits of the full S-WMD model (versus the initialization) is valuable.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and technical details. The use of equations is appropriate, and the notation is consistent. The algorithm is also described clearly, and the explanations are easy to follow.
*   **Significance:** Improving document distance measures has broad implications for tasks like information retrieval, text classification, and document recommendation. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on multiple datasets suggests that S-WMD could have a significant impact on these applications.
*   **Efficient Implementation:** The authors clearly put thought into efficient implementation using batch gradient descent and careful approximations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning, but doesn't provide specific details on the range of values explored or the computational cost. It would be helpful to understand the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors address the cubic complexity of the original WMD, the complexity remains substantial, particularly with the O(q^2) Sinkhorn approximation and the additional computational burden of learning A and w. A more detailed analysis of the actual runtime performance for different document sizes and vocabulary sizes would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors demonstrate improvements in kNN accuracy, a deeper analysis of *where* S-WMD excels and fails would be valuable. Are there specific types of documents or classification tasks where it performs particularly well or poorly?
*   **Word Weight Interpretation:** The learned word weights (w) are mentioned as being representative of class-specific importance, but the visualization only showcases the 20NEWS dataset. Providing similar visualizations or analyses for other datasets would strengthen this claim.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art Embeddings:** The experiments primarily use word2vec embeddings. It would be worthwhile to assess the performance of S-WMD with more recent and powerful embeddings like BERT or RoBERTa.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to improving document distance measures.
*   The extensive experimental results demonstrate the superiority of S-WMD over a large number of baselines.
*   The authors address a challenging computational problem with a clever and efficient solution.
*   The potential impact of this work on various NLP applications is significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity remains a concern, especially for large-scale applications.
*   The lack of detailed hyperparameter analysis and error analysis weakens the overall conclusions.
*   The paper could benefit from experimentation with more modern word embeddings.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document similarity and metric learning. The authors successfully address the challenge of incorporating supervision into the WMD framework and demonstrate impressive empirical results. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper far outweigh them. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the following:

1.  Provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the range of values explored and the computational cost.
2.  Include a more detailed analysis of the computational runtime for different document sizes and vocabulary sizes.
3.  Discuss limitations and failure cases, and consider performing error analysis.
4.  Expand the visualization of learned word weights to include more datasets.
5.  Consider experimenting with more recent word embeddings.
