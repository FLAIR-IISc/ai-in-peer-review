Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns a distance for document comparison tailored to a labeled task. The key idea is to learn (i) a linear transformation A on pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec) and (ii) a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms, so that the optimal transport cost between documents aligns with label semantics. The learning objective is a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss (à la NCA), where distances are the (regularized) optimal transport costs under A and w. To make training tractable, the paper uses entropically regularized OT (Sinkhorn) to approximate the WMD and to obtain dual variables for efficient gradients; it further accelerates training via batch sampling and neighbor pruning based on a cheap lower bound (WCD). Initialization is obtained by running NCA on word centroids (S-WCD).

This builds on: WMD for unsupervised document comparison (Kusner et al., ICML’15); entropic OT and Sinkhorn scaling (Cuturi, NIPS’13; Cuturi & Doucet); classic metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML); and prior work on learning EMD ground metrics in vision (e.g., Wang & Guibas; Cuturi & Avis). The novelty here is applying supervised metric learning to the OT ground cost in the word-embedding space for text, together with supervised histogram reweighting, and making it practical with Sinkhorn-based differentiable approximations.

Experiments on 8 datasets against 26 baselines show S-WMD achieves the best kNN error on 5/8 datasets and the best average rank, with qualitative t-SNE visualizations and reasonable training times.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Clear, well-motivated formulation of supervised OT for documents that leverages pre-trained embeddings without redefining document representations.
- Practicality: Smart use of entropic regularization and dual/scale variables (u, v) to get efficient, stable gradients; batching and WCD-based neighbor pruning further reduce cost.
- Empirical coverage: 8 datasets spanning multiple tasks, extensive baselines (unsupervised and supervised metric learning on classic representations), and code release.
- Initialization via S-WCD is simple and effective; S-WMD improves further.
- Interpretability: Learned word weights w are human-interpretable and qualitatively sensible.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical grounding: The training objective uses gradients that mix a regularized OT approximation (λ finite) and the unregularized WMD intent. There is no analysis of the bias introduced by using T*λ and approximate dual variables (α*, β*) for gradients w.r.t. A and w, nor of how λ affects consistency or the learned metric.
- Limited scope of evaluation: Results are exclusively with kNN; it would help to test whether S-WMD distances also benefit other classifiers (e.g., kernel SVM, 1-NN vs. larger k, or retrieval metrics).
- Baseline comparability: Many baselines are first PCA-reduced to 200 dims before metric learning; while pragmatic, this may favor methods that benefit from PCA. It would be useful to report sensitivity to the PCA dimension and to include cosine-distance baselines on TF-IDF and embeddings.
- Ablations: The paper lacks ablations isolating contributions of A vs. w, the impact of the initialization, neighbor pruning (N), and λ. One dataset (CLASSIC) shows WMD > S-WMD; understanding when supervision hurts would be valuable.
- Notation and presentation: Some ambiguous uses of symbols (p used for norm exponent and for vocabulary/ones-vector size), minor typos and incomplete references (e.g., “[5, 21?]”), and missing hardware specs for timing.
- Potential overfitting/regularization: There is little discussion of regularizing A (e.g., Frobenius, trace) or w (e.g., nonnegativity, sparsity/entropy) to avoid degenerate solutions that overemphasize rare words.

Comments on the review criteria
- Quality: Method is technically sound and carefully engineered; gradient derivations rely on standard OT duality and entropic regularization. However, theoretical guarantees (e.g., convergence to unregularized WMD or bounds on gradient bias vs. λ) are missing. Empirical results are solid and reproducible code is provided.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized; algorithmic choices and complexity are explained. A few notation collisions and reference formatting issues should be fixed. More details on hyperparameters (k, λ, learning rates) and hardware would improve clarity.
- Originality: Moderate to good. Learning OT ground metrics has precedents in vision, but the application to text with word embeddings, supervised histogram reweighting, and an NCA-style objective with Sinkhorn-based gradients is novel and timely for NLP.
- Significance: Likely to be useful to practitioners who rely on WMD or document distances; shows consistent improvements and offers interpretable weights. Could spur further work on supervised OT in NLP.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an ablation study: train with only A, only w, and report sensitivity to λ, N (neighbor set size), batch size, and initialization. Include at least 1 dataset where S-WMD underperforms and diagnose why.
- Analyze the effect of entropic regularization: show performance vs. λ, gradient stability, and runtime. If possible, compare to unregularized OT on small instances to quantify approximation bias.
- Regularization: Add constraints/penalties on A (e.g., orthonormal rows or Frobenius norm) and on w (nonnegativity, sparsity or entropy regularization), and report stability/generalization effects.
- Baselines: Add cosine distance on TF-IDF and average word embeddings; include a learned cosine metric (e.g., via logistic regression on centroids) and compare beyond kNN, e.g., k-NN k>1, or linear SVM using distances as features.
- Clarity fixes: Resolve notation collisions (use distinct symbols), complete and standardize references, and report hardware and exact hyperparameter settings.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Practical and well-motivated supervised extension of WMD with clear gains on diverse datasets.
- Efficient and scalable training via Sinkhorn and neighbor pruning; code available.
- Interpretable word weights; strong empirical coverage with many baselines.

Cons:
- Limited theoretical analysis of the approximation and training dynamics.
- Evaluation confined to kNN; missing ablations and some baselines.
- Minor clarity/notation/reference issues.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a useful and well-executed contribution that advances supervised document distances by marrying WMD with metric learning and efficient OT approximations. Addressing the ablations, baseline breadth, and clarity issues would strengthen the final version.
