

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

## Summary
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to improve document classification. Building on Kusner et al.'s WMD (ICML 2015), the authors incorporate supervision by minimizing a stochastic version of the leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric. The key innovation lies in learning word-specific importance weights and transforming word embeddings to better align with semantic similarity as defined by class labels. The authors use the Sinkhorn distance approximation to make the computation feasible, and demonstrate S-WMD's superiority across 8 datasets and 26 baselines.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation, with well-formulated mathematical derivations for the gradient computation. The authors properly address the computational challenges by leveraging the Sinkhorn distance approximation, which significantly reduces the complexity from O(q³log q) to O(q²). The experimental design is thorough, with evaluation across 8 diverse datasets and comparison to 26 baselines.

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The results are impressive, with S-WMD achieving the lowest kNN test error on 5/8 datasets and outperforming all 26 baselines on average. The ablation study (comparing S-WMD to its initialization) shows that the supervised training provides significant improvement. The visualization in Figure 1 compellingly demonstrates the improved clustering of documents after supervised training.

3. **Practical Impact**: The paper effectively addresses a meaningful problem in text classification by making WMD more adaptable to specific classification tasks. The training time (Table 3) is reasonable and comparable to baseline metric learning methods, making the approach practical for real-world applications.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized with a strong background section that contextualizes the work within existing literature on metric learning and WMD. The mathematical notation is consistent and clearly defined throughout.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Limitations**: While the paper provides a good computational solution, it lacks theoretical guarantees about the convergence of the optimization process. The non-convexity of the loss function (equation 6) is acknowledged but not thoroughly addressed. The paper doesn't provide any convergence analysis or bounds on the quality of the solution.

2. **Comparison with Related Work**: The paper mentions Wang et al. [35] as similar work but doesn't sufficiently distinguish their approach. The authors should clarify how S-WMD differs from their method, particularly regarding the histogram re-weighting component. A more comprehensive comparison with recent metric learning methods for text would strengthen the contribution claim.

3. **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases**: The paper notes that S-WMD performs poorly on BBCSPORT and CLASSIC datasets but doesn't thoroughly analyze why. For instance, the authors mention "poor initialization" for CLASSIC but don't explore whether this could be addressed through better initialization strategies or hyperparameter tuning.

4. **Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient detail about the hyperparameter tuning process. For example, the choice of λ=10 is made without justification, and the paper doesn't discuss how sensitive the method is to this choice. The authors also don't specify how many iterations were used for training, making it difficult to fully reproduce the results.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by extending the WMD framework to incorporate supervision in a computationally efficient manner. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate consistent improvements over a wide range of baselines. The technical approach is sound, and the paper is well-written and organized.

However, the lack of theoretical guarantees and the limited discussion of failure cases prevent the paper from reaching the highest level of impact expected at NIPS. The authors would strengthen their contribution by providing more thorough theoretical analysis, better distinguishing their method from related work, and offering a more detailed discussion of failure cases.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance, but with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. Add a short theoretical section discussing convergence properties or providing bounds on the solution quality.

2. Expand the related work section to more clearly distinguish S-WMD from Wang et al. [35] and other recent metric learning methods for text.

3. Include a more detailed analysis of the failure cases (BBCSPORT and CLASSIC), possibly with a case study showing how the learned weights differ from successful cases.

4. Provide more details about hyperparameter selection and sensitivity analysis.

5. Consider adding a discussion of the method's scalability to larger vocabularies or document collections, which would be important for practical deployment.

These improvements would strengthen an already strong paper and better position it for acceptance at NIPS. The core idea is novel, well-executed, and represents a meaningful advancement in the field of text classification metrics.
