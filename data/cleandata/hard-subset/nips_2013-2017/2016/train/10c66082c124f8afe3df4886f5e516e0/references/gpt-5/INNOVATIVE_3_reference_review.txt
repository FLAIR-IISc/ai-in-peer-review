Summary
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised metric learning framework that learns (i) a linear transformation A of the word-embedding space (ground metric) and (ii) a word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms, with the goal of aligning WMD distances with class labels. The learning objective is an NCA-style stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss over document pairs, where gradients through the optimal transport (OT) problem are enabled by an entropically-regularized OT relaxation (Sinkhorn). Gradients w.r.t. A use the relaxed transport plan T*λ; gradients w.r.t. w use dual potentials (α, β) recovered from the Sinkhorn scaling vectors (u, v). Efficiency is further improved via an NCA/WCD-based initialization (S-WCD) and neighbor pruning using fast WCD. Across 8 datasets and 26 baselines (including WMD, TF-IDF/LSI/LDA/mSDA with and without metric learning), S-WMD attains the best average rank and wins on 5/8 datasets.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on WMD (Kusner et al.), entropic OT/Sinkhorn (Cuturi), and metric learning (NCA/LMNN/ITML). Prior work has considered learning ground metrics for EMD/OT in vision (e.g., Cuturi&Avis; Wang&Guibas), but not for document distances with word embeddings, nor with a joint word-weighting scheme and an NCA objective. Using Sinkhorn both to accelerate computation and to expose differentiable surrogates for transport plans/dual potentials is a natural but nontrivial integration.

Strengths
- Novel and impactful idea: learning the OT ground metric and word weights for WMD in a supervised manner for text. This extends WMD from an unsupervised, fixed metric to a task-adaptive distance.
- Clever use of Sinkhorn: the relaxed transport plan and recovered duals provide stable, efficient gradients for both A and w; this addresses the non-differentiability of the exact LP.
- Practical training recipe: S-WCD initialization + WCD-based neighbor pruning are simple and effective; the complexity analysis and timings suggest feasibility.
- Extensive evaluation: 8 datasets, 26 strong baselines with Bayesian hyperparameter tuning; consistent gains and strong average rank. Nice interpretability via learned word weights.
- Conceptual generality: the approach should transfer to other embeddings (e.g., GloVe, fastText) and tasks where distances matter.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Objective/approximation mismatch: The loss is written in terms of DA,w (the exact WMD), but gradients use the Sinkhorn relaxation. Please clarify whether training uses Sinkhorn distance consistently in the loss, and whether test-time distances for kNN use exact WMD or Sinkhorn. An ablation (train/eval with Sinkhorn vs. exact) would help.
- Constraints on w: The reweighted histogram d̃ requires w ≥ 0 to remain in the simplex; the paper does not state constraints or regularization on w. If unconstrained, negative entries could arise. Please specify constraints, parameterization (e.g., softplus), and any regularization.
- Choice of ground cost: You use squared Euclidean in the ground cost (yielding 2-Wasserstein-like geometry) while standard WMD often uses Euclidean. On CLASSIC, squared cost seems suboptimal. A simple ablation (L2 vs. L2^2) would be informative.
- Sensitivity/ablations: No study of λ (Sinkhorn regularization), neighbor set size N, batch size B, r (rank of A), or the WCD pruning threshold. Reporting sensitivity and robustness would strengthen the empirical story.
- Missing comparisons: It would be useful to include supervised neural baselines that learn document embeddings (e.g., simple CNN/RNN or Siamese/triplet losses) and cosine-distance baselines, given their prevalence by now. Also, a comparison to learning only A (no w) and only w (no A) would isolate contributions.
- Theoretical guarantees: Beyond the practical justification, there is little discussion of the bias introduced by optimizing the relaxed problem and then evaluating with the exact one (if that is the case). Even qualitative guidance on λ’s effect and convergence/stability of gradients would help.
- Minor clarity issues: The formula for α*λ, β*λ recovered from u, v could use a precise statement with constants and dimensions; a pointer to a derivation or appendix would aid reproducibility. Notation M vs. N for neighbor counts appears inconsistent.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the use of Sinkhorn for differentiability is appropriate and the gradient expressions are reasonable. The evaluation is broad and the gains are meaningful. The main caveats are clarity around the exact-vs-relaxed objective, constraints on w, and lack of ablations.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. A few missing details (constraints/parameterization of w, exact-vs-relaxed loss, dual-potential formulas) should be clarified. The algorithmic description is easy to follow, and the visualization adds interpretability.

Originality
Moderate-to-high. While metric learning and OT relaxations are known, their combination to learn a task-specific WMD for text, with both ground-metric and word-importance learning under an NCA objective, appears novel and well-motivated.

Significance
High. WMD is widely used; providing a supervised, efficient, and interpretable extension that improves kNN classification across diverse datasets is likely to influence subsequent work in OT-based NLP and metric learning. The approach is practical and could be adopted in retrieval/ranking pipelines.

Pros
- Bold, clear idea with substantial practical impact.
- Strong empirical results across many competitive baselines.
- Efficient, well-engineered training method (Sinkhorn + pruning + good init).
- Interpretable word weights.

Cons
- Incomplete clarity on training/evaluation distances and constraints on w.
- Few ablations; sensitivity to λ and neighbor pruning not explored.
- Limited comparison to supervised neural embedding baselines.

Questions for the authors
1) Do you enforce w ≥ 0 (and how)? Any regularization on A or w?
2) Is the loss computed with Sinkhorn distance during training? At test time, do you use exact WMD or Sinkhorn?
3) Sensitivity to λ, N, B, and r? Can you report runtime vs. average document length p?
4) How do results change with Euclidean vs. squared Euclidean ground costs?
5) Can you provide ablations for “only A” vs. “only w” and comparisons to a simple Siamese/triplet network?

Recommendation
Accept. The paper pushes WMD in a novel, compelling direction with solid empirical gains and practical training tricks. The minor gaps in clarity/ablations are addressable and should not overshadow the contribution.
