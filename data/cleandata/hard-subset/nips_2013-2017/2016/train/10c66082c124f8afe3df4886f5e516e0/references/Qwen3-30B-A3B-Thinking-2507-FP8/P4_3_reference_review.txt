

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

## Summary
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to improve document distances for classification tasks. The authors build upon Kusner et al.'s (2015) WMD, which computes document distances by finding the optimal transport cost of moving words between documents in word embedding space. S-WMD introduces two key innovations: a linear transformation of word embeddings to learn a more discriminative metric, and a word importance vector to re-weight words based on their relevance to classification. The authors address the computational complexity of incorporating supervision by using the Sinkhorn distance approximation and propose an efficient optimization algorithm. The paper demonstrates S-WMD's effectiveness on 8 document classification datasets, showing consistent improvements over 26 baselines including unsupervised WMD, various document representations, and supervised metric learning approaches.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear theoretical foundation. The authors provide a well-motivated loss function that directly minimizes the leave-one-out classification error, and they carefully derive the gradients for optimization. The computational efficiency is addressed through the use of the Sinkhorn distance approximation, which reduces the time complexity from cubic to quadratic. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with 8 datasets across different text classification tasks, 26 baselines, and detailed ablation studies (including the initialization method S-WMD init.). The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the potential suboptimality of using squared Euclidean distance for certain datasets, and provide empirical evidence for their claims. The paper is well-structured with a clear progression from problem statement to method to evaluation.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section provides sufficient context for the proposed method, clearly explaining WMD, the Sinkhorn distance approximation, and related metric learning approaches. The mathematical formulations are presented logically, with clear explanations of each component. The experimental results are presented in well-organized tables and figures, with the t-SNE visualizations effectively demonstrating the improvement in document clustering. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including implementation specifics and hyperparameter choices. The writing is concise and accessible to readers familiar with machine learning concepts, with no unnecessary jargon.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution by extending the unsupervised WMD to a supervised setting, which represents a natural progression from the previous work. While metric learning has been extensively studied, incorporating supervision directly into the WMD framework is novel. The authors clearly differentiate their work from related approaches in the related work section, particularly noting that Wang et al. (2016) learned a metric within EMD but did not consider histogram re-weighting, which provides additional flexibility in S-WMD. The paper builds upon the NIPS 2015 WMD paper while making a distinct contribution that advances the state of the art. The method is sufficiently different from previous NIPS papers, as evidenced by the thorough related work discussion.

## Significance
S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results on 5 out of 8 datasets, with the method outperforming all 26 baselines on average. This represents a meaningful advance in document distance metrics, as the authors demonstrate consistent improvements across diverse text classification tasks. The computational efficiency of the method (with training times comparable to or better than other metric learning approaches) makes it practical for real-world applications. The paper provides valuable insights through visualizations of learned word weights, showing which words are most important for classification. The approach has clear potential for broader application in text classification and retrieval tasks, and the authors demonstrate how supervision can enhance the expressive power of the WMD framework.

## Strengths
- Novel extension of WMD to a supervised setting with clear theoretical foundation
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across 8 datasets with 26 baselines
- Efficient optimization using Sinkhorn distance approximation
- Clear and well-organized presentation of technical details
- Practical computational efficiency demonstrated through training time comparisons
- Useful visualizations of learned word weights and document embeddings

## Weaknesses
- Limited analysis of why S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON datasets
- Does not explore alternative distance metrics for word vectors beyond squared Euclidean
- The connection to the specific characteristics of the underperforming datasets could be strengthened

## Arguments for Acceptance
- The paper makes a clear, significant contribution by extending WMD to a supervised framework
- The method achieves state-of-the-art results on multiple document classification tasks
- The technical approach is sound and well-executed
- The paper is well-written with comprehensive experiments and analysis
- The computational efficiency is a practical advantage for real-world applications

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- The method does not consistently outperform baselines across all datasets
- The underperformance on three datasets could raise concerns about generalizability
- The paper could provide deeper analysis of the performance differences across datasets

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant contribution to the field of document distance metrics. The method effectively extends the WMD framework with supervision, achieves state-of-the-art results, and is computationally efficient. The minor weaknesses in the analysis of underperforming datasets do not undermine the overall quality and contribution of the work. The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS.
