Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The authors learn (i) a linear transformation A of the pre-trained word embedding space (a Mahalanobis metric in the ground space) and (ii) a non-uniform word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. They optimize these parameters to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective (NCA-style), where the pairwise document distances are given by optimal transport over the transformed embedding space and reweighted histograms. To make training tractable and differentiable, they use an entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) relaxation to compute both the transport plan and approximate dual potentials, enabling efficient gradients. They further accelerate training with a good initialization from supervised word-centroid distances (S-WCD), mini-batches, and WCD-based neighbor pruning. On 8 datasets with 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised), S-WMD achieves the best average rank and the lowest error on 5/8 datasets. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
This builds directly on WMD (Kusner et al., ICML’15) and the OT/Sinkhorn literature (Cuturi 2013). The learning objective is inspired by NCA and connects to supervised EMD/OT metric learning in vision (e.g., Wang & Guibas 2009), but differs by: (a) operating in word-embedding ground space rather than histogram feature space, (b) jointly learning per-word importance weights, and (c) targeting text with pre-trained embeddings. The paper also relates to classic text representations (BOW/TF-IDF/LSI/LDA), and compares against metric learning methods (LMNN, ITML, NCA) applied to document vectors. This combination for text distances via OT appears novel and well-situated in the literature.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation: learning A and w directly in the WMD ground cost is a natural way to inject supervision while leveraging high-quality pre-trained embeddings.
- Sound computational strategy: entropy-regularized OT and using Sinkhorn scaling to obtain both plans and dual potentials is standard and enables efficient gradients; neighbor pruning and S-WCD initialization are practical and effective.
- Thorough empirical study: 8 datasets, many competitive baselines (including supervised metric learning on strong representations), Bayesian hyperparameter optimization, and reasonable results (best on 5/8, best average rank).
- Interpretability: learned word weights highlight class-discriminative vocabulary; t-SNE visualizations support the claim that the learned distance clusters classes more tightly.
- Reproducibility: code release and methodology details (batches, neighbor sizes, λ, etc.) support replication.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Objective vs. distance at test time: Training uses the Sinkhorn-regularized problem; it is unclear whether test-time kNN uses the regularized distance or exact WMD with learned A,w. If the latter, an explicit statement and a small ablation (regularized vs. exact at test) would help, as optimizing a surrogate may introduce mismatch.
- Constraints/regularization on w and A: The paper does not state explicit constraints on w (e.g., nonnegativity) even though Eq. (4) requires w ◦ d to define a valid histogram. Similarly, no regularization is mentioned for A or w. Without these, overfitting or degenerate solutions are possible. Provide constraints (e.g., w ≥ 0, ||A||_F penalty) and sensitivity analysis.
- Theoretical justification of gradients: The gradient w.r.t. A using T* is standard under uniqueness, but the derivation for ∂/∂w via dual potentials is only sketched, and the specific centered forms α*λ, β*λ from Sinkhorn u,v deserve justification and a reference. A brief appendix proof or citation to entropic OT duality would strengthen the work.
- Notation and clarity issues: p is overloaded (norm exponent vs. number of unique words), section numbering has a typo (.2 Optimization), and some quantities (r, the output dimension of A) are not clearly specified in experiments. Minor but distracting.
- Scope of evaluation: Results are only for kNN. For retrieval this is appropriate, but comparisons to strong supervised text classifiers (e.g., CNN-based sentence/doc models or fastText contemporaries) are missing. Even a brief discussion of where distance-based methods are preferable (e.g., low-resource, retrieval, domain shift) would contextualize the contribution.
- Limited ablations: No sensitivity analysis for λ, batch/neighbor sizes, the effect of learning w vs. only A, or using different ground metrics (L2 vs. L1). An ablation would help attribute gains and understand robustness.
- Efficiency at scale: While training is efficient, test-time nearest-neighbor search with OT distances remains expensive. The paper uses WCD for pruning in training; a brief note on test-time acceleration (e.g., WCD lower bounds, approximate kNN) would improve the practicality story.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with reasonable approximations grounded in entropic OT. The optimization is well-motivated, and empirical evidence supports claims. The main technical gaps are the missing constraints/regularization on w, and limited theoretical detail for the dual-based gradient. Both are addressable.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, with intuitive explanations and helpful figures. A few notation/format issues (p overloading, section numbering) and missing details (constraints, test-time metric, r) should be fixed.

Originality
The idea of learning a supervised OT distance for text by shaping the ground metric and histogram weights, trained with an NCA objective and enabled by Sinkhorn, is a novel and meaningful combination for NLP. Prior supervised EMD learning exists in vision, but this instantiation for word embeddings and text distances appears new.

Significance
Likely to be useful: it advances state of the art among distance-based document methods, is interpretable, and leverages pre-trained embeddings without retraining them. Researchers working on retrieval, kNN classification, and OT in NLP can build on this.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean, well-motivated extension of WMD with strong empirical results and code.
- Solid computational contributions to make learning feasible and efficient.
- Broad comparison and clear gains on multiple datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Some theoretical and practical details (constraints on w, test-time distance, ablations) are missing.
- Evaluation limited to kNN; lack of comparison to strong supervised neural classifiers.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a substantive, well-executed contribution with clear impact for distance-based document modeling. Addressing the noted clarifications and minor issues would further strengthen it.
