

# Peer Review: Supervised Word Mover's Distance

## Summary

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to learn a better document distance metric. The authors recognize that while WMD is a powerful document distance measure, it's entirely unsupervised and not optimized for specific classification tasks. They address this limitation by learning two key components: a linear transformation of word embeddings (A) and word-specific importance weights (w) that emphasize words useful for distinguishing document classes. To make the computation feasible, they use a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and formulate a loss function that minimizes a stochastic approximation of the leave-one-out kNN classification error. The authors evaluate S-WMD on 8 document classification datasets, comparing against 26 baselines (including unsupervised WMD, WCD, and various document representations with supervised metric learning techniques), and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on 5 of the 8 datasets.

The work builds on Kusner et al.'s WMD (ICML 2015) and metric learning methods like NCA (NIPS 2005), but makes a significant contribution by directly incorporating supervision into the WMD framework without requiring a new document representation. The paper also makes a practical contribution by addressing the computational challenges of the WMD with the Sinkhorn distance approximation.

## Strengths

### Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-motivated mathematical formulations. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their approach, including the derivation of gradients for both the linear transformation A and the word importance weights w. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, and they provide a practical solution (Sinkhorn distance approximation) that addresses the cubic time complexity of the original WMD. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-structured, with results on 8 diverse datasets, detailed ablation studies (including S-WMD init.), and visualizations of learned word weights. The authors are honest about limitations (e.g., performance on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON) and provide reasonable explanations.

### Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The background section clearly explains word embeddings, WMD, and metric learning. The proposed method is described with sufficient mathematical detail while remaining accessible. The figures (t-SNE visualizations, top words with learned weights) effectively complement the text. The pseudocode for the algorithm is clear and well-structured. The authors provide sufficient detail to allow reproduction of their results, including code availability on GitHub.

### Originality
This work makes a clear and meaningful contribution to the field. While WMD was a significant advance, the unsupervised nature of the metric limits its performance on specific classification tasks. The authors' approach of incorporating supervision directly into the WMD framework is novel, and the specific combination of linear transformation of word embeddings with histogram re-weighting provides a flexible and powerful way to adapt the metric to specific tasks. The use of Sinkhorn distance to make the approach computationally feasible is a practical contribution that could be valuable for other optimal transport-based methods. The paper clearly differentiates itself from similar work (e.g., Wang et al. [35]) by highlighting the histogram re-weighting component.

### Significance
Document distance metrics are crucial for many text processing applications (search ranking, recommendation systems, news categorization), making this work highly relevant. The results demonstrate that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple datasets, with significant improvements over unsupervised baselines (e.g., 51% relative error reduction over LSI on BBCSPORT). The method is computationally efficient (training time comparable to baseline metric learning methods), making it practical for real-world applications. The paper advances the state of the art in document distance metrics in a demonstrable way, with clear improvements over existing methods.

## Weaknesses

### Reproducibility
While the authors provide code on GitHub, the paper doesn't include sufficient details about hyperparameters used in the experiments. For example, the learning rates η_A and η_w, the batch size B, the number of nearest neighbors N, and the value of λ used in the Sinkhorn approximation are not explicitly stated. These details would be crucial for reproducibility. The paper mentions using Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning but doesn't specify the search space or how the optimization was conducted.

### Analysis of Failure Cases
The paper notes that S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON datasets but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why this happens. For instance, on CLASSIC, the authors suggest that "S-WMD uses the squared Euclidean distance between word vectors, which may be suboptimal for this dataset," but they don't explore alternative distance metrics or provide more detailed analysis. Understanding these failure cases would be valuable for future work.

### Comparison with Alternative Approaches
The paper compares against 26 baselines, but it would be beneficial to include a more direct comparison with recent supervised text representation methods that use deep learning (e.g., BERT-based approaches). While the authors acknowledge that they're using pre-trained word2vec embeddings, the paper doesn't discuss how their approach might integrate with or compare to more recent contextualized embeddings.

### Initialization Analysis
The initialization method (S-WCD) is described but not thoroughly evaluated. It would be helpful to see a comparison between different initialization strategies (e.g., random initialization vs. S-WCD) to understand the impact on convergence and final performance.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document distance metrics. The authors successfully extend the WMD framework to a supervised setting with a novel combination of linear transformation and word weighting, while addressing computational challenges through the Sinkhorn distance approximation. The experimental results are thorough and convincing, demonstrating clear improvements over a wide range of baselines. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a practical problem with meaningful impact.

While there are some minor weaknesses in terms of reproducibility and analysis of failure cases, these do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work. The paper advances the state of the art in document distance metrics in a demonstrable way and provides a practical, efficient method that could be widely adopted.

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and makes a significant contribution to the field of text representation and document distances. The paper fits well within NIPS' scope, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel and practical solution. The results are significant, and the method has clear potential for practical application in text processing tasks.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include detailed hyperparameter settings used in the experiments (learning rates, batch size, number of nearest neighbors, λ for Sinkhorn approximation) to improve reproducibility.

2. Expand the analysis of failure cases (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON) to provide more insight into why S-WMD underperforms on these datasets.

3. Consider adding a brief comparison with recent deep learning-based text representation methods (e.g., using BERT embeddings) to provide context for the current state of the art.

4. Include a more detailed comparison of different initialization strategies to understand their impact on convergence and final performance.

5. Consider adding a discussion on how the method might scale to very large document collections or integrate with more recent contextualized embeddings.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Novel extension of WMD to supervised setting with clear theoretical foundation
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across 8 datasets with 26 baselines
- Practical computational solution (Sinkhorn distance) that makes the approach feasible
- State-of-the-art results on 5/8 datasets with significant improvements over baselines
- Well-written and clearly organized paper with strong technical content

**Con Acceptance:**
- Some details on hyperparameters are missing, affecting reproducibility
- Limited analysis of failure cases on specific datasets
- Does not compare with recent deep learning-based text representation methods

The pro arguments significantly outweigh the con arguments, and the paper makes a clear, valuable contribution to the field.
