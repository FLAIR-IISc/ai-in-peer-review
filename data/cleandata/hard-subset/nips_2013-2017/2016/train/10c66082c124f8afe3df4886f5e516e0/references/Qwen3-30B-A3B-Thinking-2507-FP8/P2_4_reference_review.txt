

# Review: Supervised Word Mover's Distance

## Summary

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of Kusner et al.'s Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to improve document classification performance. While WMD leverages word embeddings to compute document distances without learning document representations, S-WMD learns a linear transformation of word embeddings (A) and word importance weights (w) to make document distances align with class labels. The authors leverage the Sinkhorn distance approximation to efficiently compute gradients for optimization, making the approach computationally feasible. The paper demonstrates S-WMD's superiority over 26 baselines across 8 document datasets, achieving state-of-the-art results on 5 of them.

The work builds on two key threads: WMD (Kusner et al., 2015), which introduced optimal transport for document distances, and metric learning (e.g., NCA, LMNN), which learns distance metrics for classification. S-WMD uniquely combines these by applying metric learning directly to the WMD framework rather than to standard document representations.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a solid theoretical foundation. The authors correctly identify the limitations of unsupervised WMD and propose a principled solution using metric learning within the optimal transport framework. The mathematical formulation is well-justified, with proper use of optimal transport theory and metric learning concepts. The Sinkhorn approximation for computational efficiency is well-motivated and correctly applied.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed. The authors test on 8 diverse document datasets (news categorization, sentiment analysis, etc.) against 26 baselines (including BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA, WCD, WMD, and their supervised variants). They report kNN classification error rates with standard errors and provide t-SNE visualizations to illustrate the improved clustering. The results are convincing, showing S-WMD outperforms all baselines on average, with particularly strong improvements on datasets like TWITTER (10% better than TF-IDF+NCA), CLASSIC (42% better), and REUTERS (15% better).

The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations, noting S-WMD doesn't outperform on all datasets (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON) and providing plausible explanations. The training time analysis is also thorough, showing S-WMD's computational efficiency compared to baselines.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly establishes the problem and motivation. The background section provides sufficient context on word embeddings, WMD, and metric learning without overwhelming the reader. The method section is logically structured, with clear explanations of the loss function, gradient computation, and optimization.

The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including pseudo-code for the algorithm (Algorithm 1), parameter settings (λ=10, B=32, N=200), and code availability. The figures (t-SNE visualizations, word weight visualizations) effectively illustrate key results. The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon.

## Originality

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by extending WMD with supervision. While WMD itself was a significant advance, this paper shows how to make it even more effective for classification tasks by learning the metric directly from labels. The key novelty is the combination of optimal transport with metric learning for text documents, specifically using the Sinkhorn distance to handle computational complexity.

The paper properly distinguishes itself from related work. It's not merely applying metric learning to document representations (like NCA on TF-IDF), but rather learning a metric within the WMD framework. The authors cite relevant work on learning ground distances for EMD (Cuturi & Avis, Wang & Guibas) and clarify how their approach with word re-weighting provides additional flexibility.

The paper's novelty is significant enough to warrant publication at NIPS, as it represents a meaningful advance in document distance metrics that builds on a highly influential prior work (WMD).

## Significance

The results demonstrate clear significance. S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple document classification tasks, showing substantial improvements over unsupervised WMD (up to 51% relative reduction in error on OHSUMED) and traditional document representations. The computational efficiency (training time comparable to metric learning on baselines) makes the approach practical for real-world applications.

The paper addresses an important problem in text classification: how to leverage semantic information from word embeddings for better document distances. The method could be applied to numerous text-related tasks beyond classification, such as information retrieval, recommendation systems, and clustering.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Well-motivated problem extension of a highly influential prior work (WMD)
- Solid theoretical foundation with proper use of optimal transport and metric learning
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across 8 datasets and 26 baselines
- Computational efficiency through Sinkhorn approximation
- Clear writing and well-organized structure
- Practical significance for text classification tasks

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited analysis of why S-WMD underperforms on certain datasets (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON)
- Initialization method (using NCA on WCD) is somewhat ad-hoc without deeper justification
- Doesn't compare to more recent supervised text representation learning methods
- Minimal discussion of potential limitations in extremely long documents or low-resource settings

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of document distance metrics. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear improvements over existing approaches. The paper is well-written and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), represents a meaningful advance (Originality), and has significant potential impact (Significance).

While the weaknesses noted are worth considering, they don't detract from the paper's overall contribution. The authors have provided a clear, principled approach to improving WMD with supervision, and the results convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness.

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2016. It represents a significant, well-executed advance in the area of document distance metrics that will be of interest to the NIPS community. The paper's combination of theoretical rigor, computational efficiency, and strong empirical results makes it a strong candidate for publication.
