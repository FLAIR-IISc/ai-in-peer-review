### Summary

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which leverages pre-trained word embeddings to calculate the "travel cost" of words between two documents as a measure of their dissimilarity. The key insight of WMD is its ability to handle synonymy and conceptual similarity, overcoming a major limitation of sparse bag-of-words models. However, WMD is entirely unsupervised.

This paper's primary contribution is to make WMD supervised by learning two components: 1) a linear transformation matrix `A` applied to the word embedding space, which warps the space to make task-relevant word movements "cheaper", and 2) a word importance vector `w` that re-weighs word frequencies in the document histograms to emphasize discriminative words. A direct optimization of these parameters through the WMD objective is computationally intractable due to the cubic complexity of the underlying optimal transport problem. The authors overcome this significant technical hurdle by replacing the exact WMD with its entropically regularized, differentiable counterpart (the Sinkhorn distance). This allows for efficient gradient computation with respect to `A` and `w`. The overall objective is to minimize a stochastic version of the kNN leave-one-out classification error, following the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) framework. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation on 8 datasets, demonstrating that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results compared to an extensive list of 26 unsupervised and supervised baselines.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The core idea of learning a metric within the word embedding space for WMD, rather than in the high-dimensional document space, is both novel and elegant. It sidesteps the curse of dimensionality that plagues many metric learning algorithms when applied to text. The dual learning of a geometric transformation (`A`) and a feature re-weighting (`w`) is well-motivated and provides the model with powerful flexibility.

2.  **Significant Technical Contribution:** The main technical barrier to this work is the non-differentiability and computational cost of the WMD optimization. The paper's use of the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable proxy is a key contribution that makes the entire framework feasible. The derivations for the gradients with respect to `A` (via the primal solution) and `w` (via the dual solution) are non-trivial and technically sound. This provides a blueprint for how supervision can be incorporated into other optimal transport-based models.

3.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength. The authors compare S-WMD against an exhaustive list of 26 baselines across 8 diverse datasets. This includes classic text representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA), modern representations (mSDA), and the application of three strong metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML) to these representations. The use of Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning for all methods ensures a fair comparison. The results are compelling, showing S-WMD achieves the best average rank and outright wins on 5 of the 8 datasets.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It clearly explains the background concepts (word2vec, WMD, Sinkhorn distance, NCA), motivates the problem, and logically develops the proposed solution. The inclusion of practical optimization strategies like a smart initialization (using Supervised WCD) and nearest-neighbor-based batching demonstrates a thorough and practical approach.

### Weaknesses and Questions for Authors

1.  **Lack of Ablation Study:** The model learns two distinct components: the transformation matrix `A` and the word importance weights `w`. A crucial missing piece of the analysis is an ablation study to disentangle their respective contributions. It would be highly informative to see the performance of S-WMD with only `A` (i.e., `w` fixed to ones) and with only `w` (i.e., `A` fixed to identity). This would clarify whether both components are necessary and under what conditions one might be more important than the other.

2.  **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The Sinkhorn relaxation introduces a key hyperparameter `λ`, which controls the trade-off between approximation accuracy and computational stability/speed. The paper states `λ=10` is used as a good trade-off, but provides no analysis or sensitivity study. How does the final performance and training dynamic change with different values of `λ`? Similarly, the performance could be sensitive to the number of neighbors `N` used in the batch gradient approximation. A brief discussion on the sensitivity to these key parameters would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Comparison to End-to-End Neural Models:** The paper was written in 2016, and the set of baselines, while extensive, is reflective of that time. The strongest competitors are feature-based methods combined with metric learning. For a NIPS audience, a comparison to contemporary end-to-end neural document classifiers (e.g., CNNs on text, LSTMs/GRUs) would be necessary to truly claim state-of-the-art performance on document classification. While S-WMD is a distance metric and not a classifier, its evaluation is based on kNN classification error, making this comparison relevant and important for context.

4.  **Dependence on Word Embedding Quality:** The method's success is fundamentally tied to the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. The paper uses a single word2vec embedding. It would be interesting to know how performance varies with different embeddings (e.g., GloVe, fastText, or even task-specific fine-tuned embeddings). Does S-WMD compensate for lower-quality embeddings, or does it require a very strong initialization to work well?

### Arguments for Acceptance (Pros)

*   **High Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and practical method for supervised optimal transport on text, a significant contribution to both metric learning and NLP.
*   **Strong Technical Merit:** The use of the Sinkhorn relaxation to enable gradient-based learning is a key technical insight that solves a difficult problem.
*   **Convincing Empirical Results:** The exhaustive and well-executed experiments strongly support the claim that S-WMD is a superior document distance metric compared to a vast array of strong baselines.
*   **Potential for Broad Impact:** The methodology could be adapted to other domains where optimal transport distances are used, such as computer vision (e.g., comparing image feature distributions).

### Arguments Against Acceptance (Cons)

*   **Missing Ablation Study:** The lack of an ablation study makes it difficult to fully understand the contribution of the model's different components.
*   **Dated Baselines:** The absence of comparisons to end-to-end neural network models for document classification slightly limits the claims of being the "best performing" method for these tasks in a broader context.

### Final Assessment

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, technically sound, and highly effective method for learning a supervised document distance. The strengths, particularly the elegant formulation and the thorough empirical validation, far outweigh the weaknesses. The technical contribution of enabling gradient-based optimization of a WMD-like objective is significant and of high interest to the NIPS community. While an ablation study and a broader set of baselines would have made it even stronger, the work as presented is a complete and impactful contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
