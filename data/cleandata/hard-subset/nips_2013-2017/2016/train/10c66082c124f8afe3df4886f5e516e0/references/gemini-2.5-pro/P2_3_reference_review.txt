### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. It builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to become the words of the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. The key limitation of WMD is its unsupervised nature.

The authors propose to overcome this by learning two components from labeled data: 1) a linear transformation matrix `A` that projects the word embeddings into a new space where distances are more meaningful for the specific classification task, and 2) a vector `w` of word-specific importance weights that re-scales the document's bag-of-words representation to emphasize task-relevant words.

The primary technical challenge is that the WMD calculation involves solving a linear program, making it computationally expensive and non-differentiable to include in a gradient-based optimization loop. The authors cleverly circumvent this by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized variant, the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation provides a fast, differentiable approximation of the transport problem, enabling efficient gradient computation. The overall objective function is a stochastic version of the k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) leave-one-out error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). To further improve efficiency, the paper proposes a smart initialization scheme using a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batch-based training procedure that filters neighbors to reduce computation. The method is evaluated extensively on 8 datasets, showing state-of-the-art performance against 26 strong baselines.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of a popular and powerful document distance metric (WMD). By incorporating supervision, S-WMD makes the semantic power of word embeddings adaptable to specific tasks, which is a highly desirable property. Given the strong performance of the original WMD, this supervised extension is likely to be widely adopted and built upon by researchers and practitioners in text classification, retrieval, and clustering.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The core idea of learning a metric in the low-dimensional word embedding space, rather than the high-dimensional document space, is both elegant and practical. This avoids the scalability issues common to metric learning methods in high dimensions. The simultaneous learning of a geometric transformation (`A`) and a word importance weighting (`w`) is a novel combination that provides two complementary ways to tune the distance metric.

3.  **Technically Sound and Clever Solution:** The main technical contribution—using the Sinkhorn distance to create a differentiable and efficient proxy for WMD within the learning objective—is an excellent application of recent advances in optimal transport theory. The derivation of the gradients for both `A` and `w` (using the dual solution of the relaxed problem) is sound. This approach effectively solves the primary obstacle to creating a supervised WMD.

4.  **Extensive and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup is exceptionally thorough. The comparison against 26 baselines, including classic representations (TF-IDF, LSI, LDA), modern deep learning approaches (mSDA), and combinations with standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML), is comprehensive and leaves little doubt about the method's effectiveness. S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results on 5 of the 8 datasets and demonstrates the best average rank overall, providing strong evidence of its superiority. The inclusion of visualizations (t-SNE and word weights) further strengthens the paper by providing qualitative insights into *why* the method works.

5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the background section provides all necessary context, the proposed method is developed step-by-step, and the results are presented clearly. The authors are also honest about the few cases where their method does not achieve the top rank and provide plausible explanations.

### **Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement)**

1.  **Limited Ablation Study:** The paper convincingly shows that the full S-WMD model works very well. However, its two main components (`A` and `w`) are learned jointly. An ablation study would provide valuable insight into their relative contributions. For example, what is the performance if only `A` is learned (with `w` fixed to ones) or if only `w` is learned (with `A` fixed to identity)? This would help clarify whether the geometric transformation or the word re-weighting is the dominant factor in performance improvement.

2.  **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The paper states that hyperparameters were tuned using Bayesian optimization, which is a solid approach. However, there is no discussion about the sensitivity of S-WMD to its key parameters, particularly the entropy regularization parameter `λ` and the size of the nearest neighbor set `N` used in batch training. A brief analysis of how performance and training time vary with these parameters would add practical value for others seeking to implement or use the method. For instance, the choice of `λ = 10` is presented as a good trade-off, but it's unclear how critical this choice is.

3.  **Choice of Ground Metric:** The paper defaults to using the squared Euclidean distance between word vectors (`||A(xi - xj)||_2^2`) as the ground metric, which aligns with the NCA framework. The original WMD paper noted that the standard Euclidean distance (`p=1`) often performed better. It would be beneficial to briefly comment on whether the L1 norm was considered and why the L2 norm was chosen (presumably for its differentiability and compatibility with the NCA loss, but this should be stated).

### **Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant, novel, and well-executed contribution to the field of text analysis and metric learning. It identifies a clear shortcoming in an important existing method and proposes a technically sound and elegant solution. The empirical evidence for its effectiveness is overwhelming. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to providing deeper analysis rather than any fundamental flaws in the method or evaluation. The work is of high quality and is highly likely to have a significant impact on future research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
