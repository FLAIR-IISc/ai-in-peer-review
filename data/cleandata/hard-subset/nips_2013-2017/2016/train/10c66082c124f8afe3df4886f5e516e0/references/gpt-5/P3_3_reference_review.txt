Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of WMD that learns: (i) a linear transformation A of the pre-trained word embedding space and (ii) a global, nonnegative word-importance vector w used to reweight and renormalize document histograms. The learned ground cost is ||A(xi − xj)||^2, and the reweighting yields d̃ = (w ∘ d)/(w^T d). Training minimizes a Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA)-style stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss over pairwise document distances. To make learning tractable, the authors replace the exact OT with the entropically regularized Sinkhorn distance and exploit the Sinkhorn primal T* = diag(u) K diag(v) and dual potentials to derive efficient gradients w.r.t. A and w. They further accelerate training by minibatching and restricting NCA neighborhoods using a cheap WCD-based neighbor filter. Across eight datasets and 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised metric learners on standard representations), S-WMD achieves the best average rank and is often state-of-the-art.

Relation to Prior Work
The work builds on WMD (Kusner et al., 2015) and entropic OT (Cuturi, 2013) and adopts the NCA objective (Goldberger et al., 2004). It is related to learning ground metrics for EMD/OT (Wang & Guibas, 2009; Cuturi & Avis, 2014) and supervised uses of OT (e.g., Frogner et al., 2015). The novelty here is learning both a linear metric in embedding space and word-level weights specifically for WMD-style document distances, with practical training via Sinkhorn and an NCA loss.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: WMD is strong but unsupervised; learning a task-adapted ground metric and word weights is well-motivated for labeled tasks.
- Solid methodology: Using entropic OT and the envelope theorem to obtain efficient gradients is appropriate; the use of u,v to recover dual potentials is standard in Sinkhorn and provides a computationally feasible update for w.
- Practical engineering: WCD-based neighbor filtering for the NCA objective and minibatching address the quadratic/cubic costs that typically hinder OT-based learning.
- Empirical breadth: Evaluations on 8 datasets with a diverse and competitive set of baselines, including metric learning over standard features. The method often achieves the best errors and best average rank.
- Interpretability: Learned word weights are interpretable and qualitatively sensible.

Weaknesses and Questions
- Clarity on what distance is used where: The text suggests training uses the Sinkhorn relaxation for gradients, but it is not explicit whether the loss and the kNN distances during training and evaluation use the exact WMD or the regularized Sinkhorn distance. Please clarify training-time and test-time distances and report performance sensitivity to λ. If the loss uses exact WMD (Eq. 5) but gradients use Sinkhorn, this is a biased gradient; if the loss also uses Sinkhorn, please state it clearly.
- Constraints and regularization on w and A: Reweighted histograms must remain nonnegative and normalized. The paper defines d̃ using w ∘ d, but it does not state constraints on w. If entries of w can be negative, d̃ may leave the simplex. Please specify w ≥ 0 (or parameterize w = exp(θ)). Similarly, regularization on A and w (e.g., Frobenius/L2 or spectral norm penalties) is not discussed, yet the NCA objective is nonconvex and can overfit; please describe and ablate any regularization used.
- Dual potentials sign/derivation: The dual potentials recovered from Sinkhorn scaling are typically α = −(1/λ) log u + const and β = −(1/λ) log v + const. The paper writes α*λ = (log u)/λ − mean(log u)⋅1 and similarly for β. This seems to have the opposite sign; please verify the sign convention and correct if necessary.
- Objective approximation: The NCA denominator is approximated by summing over a restricted neighbor set Na chosen by WCD. This is a nontrivial approximation to the NCA probabilities. Please quantify the impact by varying |Na|, and report how sensitive accuracy is to this heuristic.
- Choice of squared vs. unsquared Euclidean cost: WMD typically uses ||xi − xj||2 (unsquared). The paper uses squared Euclidean in Eq. (5) and notes it may be suboptimal on some datasets. Please provide an ablation comparing squared vs. unsquared ground costs.
- Dimensionality r and initialization: The paper defines A ∈ R^{r×d} but does not specify how r is chosen or tuned. Please report r, its tuning protocol, and performance sensitivity. Likewise, training hyperparameters (learning rates, batch sizes, stopping criteria) should be detailed for reproducibility.
- Baseline fairness and breadth: Baselines are strong for distance-based kNN, but modern supervised classifiers (e.g., linear SVM/logistic regression on TF-IDF, or shallow neural document encoders available in 2016) could provide additional perspective. Since your target use is distance-based retrieval/classification, the current baselines are relevant, but including standard classifiers would strengthen claims of overall utility.
- Theoretical guarantees: The gradient formulas via the envelope theorem are correct under uniqueness/smoothness. For the exact WMD the solution may be non-unique and non-differentiable; the paper addresses this with Sinkhorn (strong convexity). It would be helpful to explicitly state that all gradients and updates are taken w.r.t. the entropic OT objective, and that in the limit λ→∞, the relaxation approximates WMD.
- Reproducibility details: Some datasets use five splits; others use a predefined split. Please specify k in kNN, validation protocols for hyperparameters (including λ), and whether early stopping used a validation set.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with correct use of entropic OT and NCA. Some derivation details (dual sign, constraints on w, exact training/eval distance) need clarification. Empirical evaluation is broad and competitive, though stronger ablations would improve confidence in design choices.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. However, crucial implementation details are missing (constraints/regularization, r, exact training/eval distances, k). Fix typos and notation (e.g., “p-dimensional all ones vector” where p earlier denotes unique words per document; should be n). Provide a self-contained description of hyperparameters and constraints.

Originality
The combination—learning a supervised ground metric and word weights for WMD using Sinkhorn and an NCA loss—is, to my knowledge, novel in NLP at the time. Related work on learning EMD ground metrics in vision is acknowledged.

Significance
Likely to be useful: supervised adaptation of WMD is a practical need, and the method delivers consistent gains with reasonable training costs. The approach may spur further work on supervised OT for text.

Suggestions for Improvement
- Clarify and correct the dual potentials; enforce and describe w ≥ 0 (e.g., exponential parameterization) and add regularization on A and w with ablations.
- Report sensitivity to λ, |Na|, and r; compare squared vs. unsquared ground costs.
- Explicitly state what distance is used at training and test time; if using Sinkhorn at test time, report also exact WMD performance to isolate the effect of the relaxation.
- Add a baseline with linear SVM/logistic regression on TF-IDF and report retrieval metrics if applicable.
- Expand the supplementary with full tables, training hyperparameters, and runtime/memory analyses.

Pros
- Well-motivated, principled, and efficient formulation.
- Strong empirical results across many datasets and baselines.
- Interpretable learned word weights; practical code release.

Cons
- Some derivational and implementation details unclear (constraints, dual signs).
- Limited ablations on key design choices and approximations.
- Baseline suite could include standard discriminative classifiers.

Overall, a strong submission pending clarifications and minor corrections.
