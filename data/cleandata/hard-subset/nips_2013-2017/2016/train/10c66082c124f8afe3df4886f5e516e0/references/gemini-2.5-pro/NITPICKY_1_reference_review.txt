**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance. The approach extends the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by incorporating supervision through metric learning. Specifically, the authors propose learning two components simultaneously: a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space and a word importance vector `w` that re-weights the bag-of-words histograms. The learning objective is to minimize a stochastic version of the k-NN leave-one-out classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A key technical contribution is overcoming the prohibitive computational cost and non-differentiability of the WMD optimization. This is achieved by replacing the exact Earth Mover's Distance with its entropy-regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance), which is differentiable and can be solved much more efficiently. The authors also propose several practical speed-ups, including a clever initialization scheme based on a supervised Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batch-based gradient descent with nearest-neighbor filtering. The method is evaluated on 8 datasets against a comprehensive suite of 26 baselines, demonstrating state-of-the-art or competitive performance on most tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a logical and important next step for the highly successful Word Mover's Distance: incorporating supervision. The idea of jointly learning a transformation of the embedding space and word-specific importance weights is well-motivated and provides a flexible framework for adapting the distance metric to a specific task.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The primary technical challenge—embedding a complex optimization problem (WMD) within a gradient-based learning framework—is elegantly addressed. The use of the Sinkhorn distance relaxation is a critical and well-justified choice that makes the entire enterprise computationally feasible. The derivation of the gradients with respect to both the matrix `A` and the weight vector `w` is a non-trivial contribution.
3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive. The comparison against 26 baselines, spanning classic representations (TF-IDF, LSI), modern ones (mSDA), and their combinations with various metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN), is commendable. The results are strong, with S-WMD achieving the best performance on 5 out of 8 datasets and the best average rank overall.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the strong core idea and impressive results, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor, yet collectively significant, flaws related to clarity, methodological detail, and presentation.

1.  **Lack of Polish and Formatting Issues:** The manuscript appears rushed and lacks the meticulousness expected of a top-tier conference submission.
    *   There are numerous formatting and typographical errors. For instance, the conference line on the first page contains a typo ("th Conference"). The footnote about author contributions is awkwardly placed. The reference list is in a state of disarray, with many entries incomplete (missing venues, years, or page numbers) and containing typos (e.g., "PdD Thesis" for [2]). Reference [21] has a question mark `[21? ]`, which is unacceptable in a final manuscript.
    *   Mathematical notation is inconsistent and often unclear. In Eq. (1), the distance is defined as `D(xi,xj)` on the left-hand side, which is the ground distance, but the text describes it as the distance between documents `da` and `db`. The norm `||xi-xj||p^2` is ambiguous; it should likely be `||xi-xj||_p^2`. The derivation of the dual variables in Section 3.1 is particularly opaque, with the equation `α∗λ = log(u) λ − log(u)>1 p 1` using an undefined variable `p` and unclear notation `1 p 1`. Consistency in using `q` versus `p` for the number of unique words is also lacking.

2.  **Methodological Gaps:** Several key details of the method are either glossed over or inadequately justified.
    *   The choice of the regularization parameter `λ=10` is presented as a "nice trade-off" without any supporting sensitivity analysis or more principled justification. The performance of the Sinkhorn approximation is highly dependent on `λ`, and this choice warrants more rigorous defense.
    *   The paper does not mention any form of regularization for the learned matrix `A` or the weight vector `w`. Is there a risk of `A` becoming ill-conditioned or the values in `w` exploding? Are the weights `w` constrained to be positive? These are important practical details for reproducibility and understanding the stability of the algorithm.
    *   The use of WCD to pre-filter neighbors for the NCA loss calculation is a heuristic that could introduce bias. There is no analysis of how this approximation affects the final learned metric or how sensitive the results are to the size of this neighbor set, `N`.

3.  **Insufficient Analysis of Results:** The discussion of the experimental results could be more insightful.
    *   The cases where S-WMD underperforms are not analyzed rigorously. On the CLASSIC dataset, it performs worse than the unsupervised WMD. The paper speculates this could be due to "a poor initialization" or the choice of distance, which is hand-wavy and unconvincing. A deeper investigation is needed to understand the limitations of the proposed method.
    *   Similarly, the superiority of TF-IDF+NCA on datasets with long documents (BBCSPORT, OHSUMED) is an interesting finding. The hypothesis that TF-IDF is a better starting point for long documents is plausible but remains an untested assertion. This weakens the claim of S-WMD's general applicability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, significant, and well-motivated supervised extension to the WMD.
*   The technical solution to make the problem tractable (using Sinkhorn distance) is elegant and effective.
*   The method achieves state-of-the-art results on a majority of datasets in a very thorough empirical comparison.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper suffers from a general lack of care in its presentation, including numerous typos, formatting errors, and an exceptionally sloppy reference list.
*   Key methodological details are missing or poorly explained (e.g., choice of `λ`, regularization, unclear mathematical notation), hindering reproducibility and confidence in the method.
*   The analysis of the method's failure modes and limitations is superficial.

**Recommendation:**

While the core contribution of this paper is strong and the results are impressive, the sheer number of minor flaws in presentation, clarity, and methodological detail is concerning. A paper submitted to NIPS should not only present novel ideas but also do so with clarity and precision. The current state of the manuscript, particularly the unclear notation and the messy reference list, suggests a lack of diligence that undermines the quality of the work. The methodological gaps (e.g., lack of discussion on regularization) are also non-trivial. For these reasons, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The paper has the potential to be a very strong contribution, but it requires a major revision to address these numerous issues.

**Decision:** Reject
