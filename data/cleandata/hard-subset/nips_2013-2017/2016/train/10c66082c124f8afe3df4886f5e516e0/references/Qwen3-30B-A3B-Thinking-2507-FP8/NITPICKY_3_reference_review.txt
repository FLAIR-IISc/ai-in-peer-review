

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes a supervised extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) for document classification, termed Supervised WMD (S-WMD). The authors aim to learn a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to improve the WMD metric. The approach leverages the Sinkhorn distance approximation to reduce computational complexity and uses a stochastic neighborhood relaxation similar to NCA for training.

## Strengths

The paper makes a reasonable extension to the WMD framework, which has shown significant promise in document classification. The computational efficiency gains through Sinkhorn approximation are well-justified, and the experimental results on 8 datasets demonstrate consistent improvements over unsupervised baselines, particularly on smaller document datasets. The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the relationship between the proposed method and existing work on metric learning.

## Weaknesses

The paper contains several technical inconsistencies and minor but important omissions that undermine its scientific rigor:

1. **Notational Inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for document representations (e.g., "d a i" vs. "d_a i" in equation (1), "d̃ a i" vs. "d̃_a i" in equation (5)). These inconsistencies make the mathematical presentation difficult to follow.

2. **Incomplete Technical Justification**: The claim that "the WCD always lower bounds the WMD" (Section 2) lacks a citation or proof. Similarly, the paper states "the WCD should be a reasonable approximation to the WMD" without justification, which weakens the proposed initialization approach.

3. **Ambiguous Loss Function**: Equation (6) uses "log" without specifying the base (natural log is standard in ML, but this should be explicit). The paper also fails to provide statistical significance testing for the reported 5/8 dataset improvements, making it difficult to assess whether these results are meaningful.

4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper uses λ=10 for the Sinkhorn approximation but provides no analysis of how this hyperparameter affects performance. The claim that "setting λ too large can make the algorithm converges slower" lacks supporting evidence or ablation studies.

5. **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper alternates between "S-WMD" and "SWMD" without consistent usage. The reference to "S-WMD init." (Section 4) is confusing since the initialization method is described as S-WCD in Section 3.1.

6. **Experimental Details**: The paper doesn't specify the word2vec model variant (Skip-gram vs. CBOW) or embedding dimensionality used in experiments. The claim that "S-WMD is surprisingly fast" lacks meaningful comparison with other metric learning approaches beyond the time to train the initial metric.

7. **Theoretical Gaps**: The paper states "the gradient is not always guaranteed to exist" but provides no examples or analysis of when this occurs, nor how the subgradient approach handles it in practice.

8. **Formatting Issues**: The reference list contains inconsistent formatting (e.g., "pp" in some entries but not others), and the citation "word2vec, although many word embeddings could be used [5, 21? ]" contains an odd question mark. The conference name "th Conference" should be "The Conference."

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting extension to the WMD framework with promising empirical results, the technical inconsistencies, lack of statistical significance testing, and incomplete justification of key claims prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for technical rigor. The minor methodological issues accumulate to a point where the paper's scientific contribution is not fully validated.

The authors have made a reasonable effort to extend WMD to a supervised setting, but the paper lacks the precision and completeness required for a top-tier NIPS submission. The most significant issue is the failure to properly justify and validate the key technical choices, particularly regarding the Sinkhorn approximation and the loss function.

I recommend rejection due to the accumulation of minor but significant technical flaws that undermine the paper's scientific contribution, despite its promising empirical results. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these technical inconsistencies before resubmission to a venue with less stringent standards.
