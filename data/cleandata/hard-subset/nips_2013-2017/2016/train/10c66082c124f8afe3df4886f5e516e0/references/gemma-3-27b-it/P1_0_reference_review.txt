## Peer Review: Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

**Summary:**

This paper presents Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel approach to learning a document distance metric by adapting word embeddings and re-weighting word frequencies. Building upon the recent success of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), the authors tackle the challenge of incorporating supervision into the distance calculation without incurring prohibitive computational costs. They achieve this by leveraging a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and a probabilistic filtering technique, enabling efficient gradient-based learning.  The method aims to minimize k-NN classification error, effectively aligning the distance metric with semantic similarity as defined by document labels. The authors demonstrate superior performance across 8 datasets and 26 baselines.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper positions itself well within the broader context of document distance metrics, starting from traditional methods like bag-of-words and TF-IDF, through embedding-based approaches like LSI and LDA, and culminating in the unsupervised WMD. It acknowledges the limitations of these methods and highlights the growing field of metric learning.  Critically, it distinguishes itself from existing metric learning approaches by operating directly within the word embedding space, rather than attempting to learn document representations *from scratch*.  The use of the Sinkhorn distance builds upon the work of Cuturi [6] to address the computational complexity of WMD.  The stochastic neighborhood descent approach for metric learning is inspired by NCA [15], with key adaptations for the WMD framework. The authors properly acknowledge and cite relevant literature throughout.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of learning a word embedding metric specifically tailored for the WMD framework is a significant contribution. Combining metric learning with optimal transport is a novel approach.
* **Efficiency:** The proposed use of the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering represents a clever solution to the computational bottleneck of supervised WMD.  The authors demonstrate efficiency gains and provide complexity analysis.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The paper presents compelling experimental results, consistently outperforming a comprehensive set of 26 baselines across diverse datasets. The averaging over multiple train/test splits adds robustness.
* **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of unsupervised methods and motivates the need for a supervised approach to document distance learning.
* **Detailed Technical Contribution:** The gradient derivation and optimization details are well-explained, providing sufficient information for reproducibility.
* **Visualization and Analysis:** The t-SNE visualizations and analysis of learned word weights offer valuable insights into the behavior of the S-WMD.
* **Well-written:** The paper is generally well-written, clearly organized, and relatively easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The reliance on Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning suggests potential sensitivity. The paper doesn't provide enough details on the ranges explored for these hyperparameters or a sensitivity analysis of key parameters.
* **Choice of Squared Euclidean Distance:**  While justified, the choice of squared Euclidean distance within the optimal transport problem feels somewhat arbitrary. A more thorough exploration of alternative distance metrics (e.g., Manhattan distance) might be warranted.
* **Scalability beyond current datasets:** While efficiency improvements are presented, scaling to *very* large document collections or extremely high-dimensional word embeddings remains an open question.  The complexity analysis, while present, does not provide concrete limits on the problem size that can be effectively handled.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses primarily on successes. A deeper discussion of the datasets where S-WMD *doesn’t* perform as well and potential reasons why would strengthen the analysis. For instance, the performance on BBCSPORT and CLASSIC is lower, and this requires a more detailed examination.
* **Initialization Dependence:** The performance gain from the initialization step (S-WCD) is mentioned, but its impact is not quantified beyond saying it "performs quite well". A more detailed comparison of S-WMD starting from random initialization vs. S-WCD initialization would be useful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Demonstrates a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in document distance learning.
* Achieves state-of-the-art results on a diverse set of datasets.
* Presents a practical and efficient solution that addresses the computational limitations of previous methods.
* The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.
* The contribution is significant and likely to be of interest to researchers in NLP, information retrieval, and machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Potential sensitivity to hyperparameters and lack of detailed sensitivity analysis.
* Limited discussion of failure cases and the scalability to extremely large datasets.
* While the complexity analysis is provided, the applicability to real-world very large corpora is not clearly demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling contribution. The authors have tackled a difficult problem and proposed a novel and effective solution. The experimental results are convincing, and the technical details are well-explained. While some minor weaknesses exist, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the following points:

1.  Provide more details on the Bayesian optimization process, including the ranges explored for hyperparameters and a brief sensitivity analysis.
2.  Discuss the limitations of the method and potential failure cases in more detail.
3.  Elaborate on the scalability of S-WMD to extremely large datasets or high-dimensional word embeddings.
4.  Quantify the impact of the S-WCD initialization with a comparison to random initialization.
