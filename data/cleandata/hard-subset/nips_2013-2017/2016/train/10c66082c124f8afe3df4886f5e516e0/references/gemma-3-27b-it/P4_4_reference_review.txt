The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embeddings and re-weighted word frequencies to align WMD distances with semantic similarity encoded in labels. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by exploiting a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering of the training set. They demonstrate superior performance on 8 datasets compared to 26 baseline methods.

This work builds upon Kusner et al.’s (2016) WMD, which leveraged word embeddings for document distance calculation. Prior to WMD, document distance research focused on representations like bag-of-words (Salton & Buckley, 1988) and latent semantic indexing (Deerwester et al., 1999). The authors also position their work within the broader context of metric learning (e.g., Goldberger et al., 2005; Davis et al., 2007), highlighting the quadratic scaling issues of many existing methods.  Recent advancements in fast Wasserstein distance computation (Cuturi, 2013; Cuturi & Doucet, 2016) also motivate their approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:**  The combination of learning a linear transformation of word embeddings *and* re-weighting word frequencies within the WMD framework is novel.  This offers a flexible approach to adapting WMD to specific tasks.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The authors effectively leverage the Sinkhorn distance to address the computational complexity of supervised WMD. The gradient computation and optimization details are well-explained. The use of a relaxed optimization problem and batch gradient descent appears well-motivated and practical.
*   **Empirical Results:** The extensive experimental evaluation across 8 datasets and comparison with 26 baselines provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD.  The consistent improvements over unsupervised WMD and other baselines are compelling.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The background section provides sufficient context for understanding the approach. The motivation for each design choice is clearly articulated.
*   **Efficiency:** The authors demonstrate that the proposed method is computationally efficient, addressing a key limitation of incorporating supervision into WMD.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning, the sensitivity of the results to specific hyperparameters (e.g., λ for the Sinkhorn distance, batch size, learning rates) is not thoroughly discussed.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** While the initialization step (S-WMD init) is evaluated, a more detailed ablation study investigating the individual contributions of the linear transformation and word re-weighting would be valuable.
*   **Choice of Squared Euclidean Distance:** The authors utilize the squared Euclidean distance in the optimization. A justification for this choice, compared to other distance metrics (e.g., L1 distance), could strengthen the paper.
*   **Connection to ITML:** Wang et al. (2017) proposes a similar approach learning a metric within a generalized Euclidean EMD ground distance using ITML. The paper should more explicitly position itself with respect to this closely related work and highlight the differences in their approaches.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to supervised document distance learning.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
*   The authors address a key practical challenge (computational complexity) in a clever and effective way.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters and the lack of a comprehensive ablation study are minor concerns.
*   The connection to related work (Wang et al. 2017) could be strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of document distance learning. The proposed S-WMD method is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated. Despite some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh the concerns. I recommend acceptance.
