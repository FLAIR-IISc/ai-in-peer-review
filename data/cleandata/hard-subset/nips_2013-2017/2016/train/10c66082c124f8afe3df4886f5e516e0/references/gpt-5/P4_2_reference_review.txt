Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised metric-learning framework that adapts the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) [Kusner et al., ICML 2015] for labeled document similarity. It learns (i) a linear transformation A of pre-trained word embeddings and (ii) a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms, so that distances align with label-based similarity. The training objective follows the stochastic neighborhood formulation of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [Goldberger et al., NIPS 2005], minimizing a smoothed leave-one-out kNN loss based on the learned WMD distance DA,w.

Computational challenges are addressed by replacing the exact optimal transport (OT) with the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance [Cuturi, NIPS 2013], enabling O(p^2) per pair (p = number of unique words per document) and stable, unique solutions. Gradients are computed via the envelope theorem: ∂DA,w/∂A uses the optimal transport plan T*λ; ∂DA,w/∂w uses the dual potentials (α*, β*), approximated from Sinkhorn scaling vectors (u, v). The authors introduce (a) an initialization via NCA in the word centroid space (S-WCD), (b) minibatch training, and (c) neighbor pruning using the fast Word Centroid Distance (WCD), to scale optimization.

Experiments on 8 datasets (news categorization, sentiment, etc.) compare against 26 baselines, including BOW/TF-IDF/BM25/LSI/LDA/mSDA, WCD/WMD, and applying NCA/LMNN/ITML on these representations (after PCA). S-WMD achieves the best average rank and the lowest kNN error on 5/8 datasets, with competitive results on the remainder. Training times are reported to be practical, and code is released.

Strengths
- Addresses a well-motivated gap: prior WMD is unsupervised; S-WMD learns task-specific document distances without re-learning document encodings, working in low-dimensional word-embedding space.
- Sound use of Sinkhorn regularization to obtain efficient and differentiable approximations of OT; gradient derivations leverage strong convexity and dual potentials appropriately.
- Joint learning of ground metric (A) and histogram weights (w) increases flexibility beyond prior EMD metric-learning work in vision (e.g., ITML/LMNN variants for EMD ground distances).
- Thoughtful engineering for scalability: NCA-style stochastic objective, S-WCD initialization, minibatching, and neighbor pruning through WCD.
- Extensive experimental comparison across diverse datasets and many baselines, with hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian optimization and public code.
- Clear empirical improvements over strong unsupervised (WMD) and supervised baselines on average.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Objective focuses solely on kNN classification. There is no evaluation on retrieval tasks (e.g., ranking), which are natural use cases for document distances, nor comparison to non-kNN classifiers leveraging the learned metric.
- Regularization and constraints are under-specified: nonnegativity of w is implied but not explicitly enforced; no explicit regularizers on A (e.g., Frobenius/trace constraints) or w are described, which risks overfitting, especially with small datasets.
- Sensitivity/ablation analyses are limited: no study on the effect of λ, the contribution of A vs. w alone, the neighbor-pruning parameter N, or the choice of squared Euclidean vs. other ground costs.
- Notational clarity issues: reuse of symbols (p for norm order vs. vocabulary/document size), typos (e.g., “.2 Optimization”), and missing explicit update of the Sinkhorn kernel K to reflect the transformed cost ‖A(xi−xj)‖^2.
- Computational scaling remains O(p^2) per pair; long documents can still be expensive despite approximations. This is briefly acknowledged but not deeply analyzed with respect to document lengths.
- While comparisons are broad, the study is confined to classical representation baselines and metric learning. No modern neural baselines for supervised text classification are included (though this is partly era-appropriate for the venues cited), and no cross-domain generalization tests are reported.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The core methodology is technically sound. Using the envelope theorem with unique Sinkhorn solutions justifies the gradient forms; approximating dual potentials from (u, v) is standard, acknowledging additive constants. However, the paper would benefit from explicit constraints/regularization for w and A, and more detailed algorithmic choices (e.g., ensuring K depends on A). The empirical study is careful, with tuned baselines and multiple datasets, but lacks ablations and tests outside kNN.
- Clarity: The exposition is generally understandable and well-structured (background → method → optimization → results). Some notation overload and typographical issues reduce clarity; tightening the presentation and explicitly stating constraints (e.g., w ≥ 0, normalization) would help reproducibility.
- Originality: The combination of supervised OT for text with joint learning of a ground metric in embedding space and histogram reweighting appears novel relative to WMD and prior EMD metric-learning for images. Leveraging Sinkhorn for end-to-end learning in this setting is a meaningful contribution.
- Significance: Results suggest consistent improvements over strong baselines across tasks, indicating practical utility. The approach is likely to influence supervised extensions of WMD/OT for text. Broader impact would be strengthened by demonstrating benefits in ranking/retrieval and providing ablation/sensitivity analyses.

Pros
- Principled supervised extension of WMD with efficient Sinkhorn-based training.
- Joint learning of embedding metric and word importance yields robust gains.
- Strong empirical performance across multiple datasets and baselines; code available.

Cons
- Limited to kNN evaluation; no retrieval or broader downstream tasks.
- Under-specified constraints/regularization; potential overfitting risks.
- Some clarity/notation issues; limited ablations and sensitivity studies.
- Computational cost may remain high for very long documents.

Recommendation
The paper presents a technically sound and practically useful supervised distance for documents, with solid empirical evidence and appropriate connections to OT and metric learning. With minor revisions to clarify constraints/notation and additional ablations, it would be a strong contribution to NeurIPS.
