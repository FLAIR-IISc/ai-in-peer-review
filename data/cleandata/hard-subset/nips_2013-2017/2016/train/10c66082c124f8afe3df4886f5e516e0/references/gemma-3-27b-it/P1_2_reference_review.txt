## Peer Review: Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel approach to improving the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by learning a metric specifically tailored to supervised classification tasks.  The authors propose learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weighting word frequencies to align WMD distances with semantic similarity as defined by class labels.  A key contribution is the efficient computation of gradients through the use of a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering of the training set, enabling practical application to reasonably sized datasets. The authors demonstrate strong empirical performance across 8 datasets and 26 baselines, showcasing the superiority of S-WMD.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds directly upon Kusner et al.’s (2016) WMD, leveraging the benefits of word embeddings for document distance. It also connects to the broader field of metric learning, specifically drawing inspiration from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) by Goldberger et al. (2005). The paper positions itself as improving upon unsupervised approaches (like WMD, LSI, and LDA) by incorporating supervision, and addresses the computational challenges that typically hinder metric learning in high-dimensional spaces through the Sinkhorn distance approximation.  The authors appropriately acknowledge relevant work in both optimal transport (Cuturi, 2013) and metric learning (Davis et al., 2007; Weinberger & Saul, 2009).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Learning a metric *within* the WMD framework, coupled with word re-weighting, is a genuinely novel contribution.  It moves beyond simply learning document representations and directly addresses the distance calculation itself.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical justification for using the Sinkhorn distance as a relaxation of the optimal transport problem is well-established, and the derived gradient formulations appear correct. The explanation of the gradient computation is detailed and the justification for approximations is reasonable.
* **Empirical Performance:** The extensive experimental evaluation across diverse datasets and a large number of baselines convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of S-WMD.  The consistent outperformance of state-of-the-art methods is impressive.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors cleverly address the cubic complexity of WMD computations with their efficient gradient approximation. The discussion of the complexity analysis is clear and provides valuable insight into the algorithm's scalability.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The background section provides sufficient context for understanding the key concepts.
* **Initialization Strategy:** The use of S-WCD for initialization appears to be a strong design choice, leading to faster convergence and potentially better performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors state they use Bayesian Optimization (BO) for hyperparameter tuning, the specifics of the BO setup (search space, acquisition function, etc.) are not detailed. A more thorough discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While the results are strong, there’s limited error analysis beyond the overall kNN error. Investigating *where* S-WMD fails (e.g., specific classes, document lengths) could offer insights for future improvement.  The discussion on BBCSPORT and OHSUMED is a good start, but could be expanded.
* **Squared Euclidean Distance:**  The choice of the squared Euclidean distance in the transport cost (eq. 5) is somewhat arbitrary.  Exploring other distance metrics might yield further improvements. While a justification is given, the impact of this choice isn’t thoroughly investigated.
* **Dependence on Word Embeddings:** The performance of S-WMD is inherently tied to the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings (word2vec). The paper doesn't explore the impact of using different embedding models (GloVe, FastText, etc.).
* **Lack of Ablation Study:** While the authors mention the contribution of both the linear transformation and word re-weighting, they don’t present a thorough ablation study that isolates the impact of each component.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong empirical performance and statistically significant improvements over numerous baselines.
* Novel and theoretically sound approach to learning a document distance metric.
* Addresses a key limitation of WMD (lack of supervision) while maintaining computational feasibility.
* Clear and well-written presentation of the method and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited error analysis and lack of investigation into hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Potential dependence on the choice of word embeddings.
* Absence of a dedicated ablation study to fully understand the contribution of each component.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of document similarity and metric learning. The proposed S-WMD algorithm is both novel and effective, and the authors have demonstrated its superiority through a comprehensive empirical evaluation.  While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I believe this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance to NIPS.

**Rating:**  Strong Accept (8/10)

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Provide more details on the Bayesian Optimization setup used for hyperparameter tuning.
* Conduct a more in-depth error analysis to identify potential failure modes.
* Explore the impact of different word embedding models on performance.
* Include an ablation study to evaluate the contribution of the linear transformation and word re-weighting separately.
* Briefly discuss the potential limitations of the method and avenues for future research.
