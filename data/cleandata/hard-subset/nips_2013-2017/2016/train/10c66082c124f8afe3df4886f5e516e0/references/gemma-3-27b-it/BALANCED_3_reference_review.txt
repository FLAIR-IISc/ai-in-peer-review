This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document similarity. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weight word frequencies to better align WMD distances with semantic similarity defined by document labels. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering. They demonstrate strong performance across 8 datasets and 26 baselines.

The paper builds upon the recent work of Kusner et al. [19] introducing WMD, which utilizes word embeddings to compute distances between documents. It also draws from the field of metric learning, citing relevant works like NCA [15] and LMNN [36]. The contribution distinguishes itself by adapting metric learning techniques *within* the word embedding space, avoiding the need to learn new document representations and instead focusing on refining the word-level relationships. This is a compelling approach given the strength of pre-trained word embeddings.  The efficient approximation via the Sinkhorn distance is a significant practical contribution, addressing a key bottleneck for supervised learning of WMD.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Applying supervised learning directly to the WMD framework is a novel and interesting approach. Learning word-specific importance weights alongside the embedding transformation is a valuable addition.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents comprehensive experimental results across a diverse set of datasets, consistently outperforming a large number of baselines. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering effectively addresses the computational complexity of supervised WMD, making it practical for real-world applications.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The background material is sufficient for understanding the proposed method, and the experimental setup is clearly described.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of relevant literature in metric learning and document distance measures.
* **Ablation Study**: Showing the performance of the method with just initialization (“S-WMD init.”) is very helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper presents a clear formulation of the loss function and gradient, there's limited theoretical justification for why learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weighting word frequencies is effective. A deeper analysis of the learned transformations could provide more insights.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors mention tuning hyperparameters with Bayesian optimization, but the details of the hyperparameter search and the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings are not thoroughly discussed.
* **Squared Euclidean Distance:** The choice of squared Euclidean distance in the objective function might not be optimal for all datasets. Exploring other distance metrics could potentially improve performance. This is hinted at in the discussion of the CLASSIC dataset.
* **PCA Usage in Baselines:**  The consistent use of PCA for dimensionality reduction in the baseline metric learning methods could introduce a potential bias in the comparison. The authors should discuss the impact of PCA on the baseline performance more thoroughly.
* **Discussion of Negative Results:** While the overall results are strong, a more in-depth discussion of the datasets where S-WMD *doesn't* perform as well (e.g., BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON) could be beneficial. Understanding the limitations of the method is crucial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to improving document similarity by learning a supervised metric within the WMD framework.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over a large number of baselines.
* The computational efficiency achieved through the use of the Sinkhorn distance makes the method practical for real-world applications.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of theoretical analysis limits our understanding of why the proposed method works.
* The limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and the potential impact of PCA on the baseline performance raise some concerns about the robustness of the results.
* While the overall performance is strong, a more detailed analysis of the datasets where the method performs less well would be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of document similarity. The proposed S-WMD method is novel, effective, and computationally efficient. While some theoretical limitations and minor concerns regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and baseline comparisons exist, the strong empirical results and clear presentation outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised in the weaknesses section.  The authors should elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of their method, provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process, and offer a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of S-WMD.
