

# Review: Supervised Word Mover's Distance

## Summary
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method to learn a supervised metric for document distances by extending the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) [19]. WMD computes document distances by finding the optimal transport cost of moving words from one document to another in word embedding space. The authors extend this framework by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings (A) and word importance weights (w) to improve document classification performance. The approach is framed as a metric learning problem, minimizing a stochastic version of leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric. The paper relates to prior work in metric learning (NCA [15], LMNN [36], ITML [10]) and document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA), distinguishing itself by applying metric learning directly to the WMD framework rather than to document representations.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness and Quality:** The paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound extension of WMD. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the supervised WMD, including the loss function, gradient computation, and optimization strategy. The use of the Sinkhorn distance approximation [6] to address the cubic time complexity of WMD is well-justified and effectively implemented. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with 8 datasets and 26 baselines, demonstrating consistent improvements over unsupervised WMD and other document representations. The paper carefully addresses computational challenges through efficient approximations and batch gradient descent.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with WMD or metric learning. The mathematical notation is consistent and clearly explained. The experimental setup, results, and visualizations (t-SNE embeddings, learned word weights) are presented in a clear and accessible manner. The paper meets the standard of providing enough information for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

**Novelty:** The paper makes a clear contribution by applying metric learning to the WMD framework. While there has been work on metric learning for document representations [10, 15, 36, 37], this is the first to extend the WMD itself with supervision. The paper distinguishes itself from related work, particularly Wang et al. [35], by incorporating histogram re-weighting through the importance vector w. The paper is significantly different from previous NIPS papers on WMD and metric learning.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate that S-WMD consistently outperforms unsupervised baselines and 25 other competitive methods across 8 diverse datasets. The method provides a practical way to leverage the power of unsupervised word embeddings while adding supervision for improved document classification. The learned word importance weights provide interpretability, showing which words are most discriminative for classification tasks. The computational efficiency (training times comparable to other methods) makes the approach practical for real-world applications.

## Weaknesses

**Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper uses λ=10 for the entropy regularization parameter without thorough exploration of its sensitivity. While the authors note that setting λ too large can slow convergence, they don't provide a systematic analysis of how performance varies with λ across different datasets.

**Justification of Distance Metric:** The paper states "we use the squared Euclidean word distance in Eq. (5)" but doesn't provide a justification for this choice. While squared Euclidean distance is commonly used in metric learning, it would be valuable to compare with alternative distance metrics for word embeddings (e.g., cosine similarity) to show the impact on performance.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper doesn't compare against the most recent supervised document distance methods that may have emerged after the WMD paper (2015). For example, recent work on supervised word embeddings (e.g., Tang et al. [32]) could provide additional context for the proposed approach.

## Pro/Con Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel extension of WMD with supervision, addressing a significant gap in the literature
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across 8 datasets with 26 baselines
- Technically sound approach with efficient computation using Sinkhorn approximation
- Clear writing and well-organized presentation
- Demonstrates consistent improvement over unsupervised baselines and state-of-the-art methods

**Con:**
- Limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity (λ)
- No justification for using squared Euclidean distance over alternatives
- Doesn't compare against the most recent supervised document distance methods

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant contribution to the field of document distances. The supervised extension of WMD addresses an important gap in the literature and demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple datasets. The method is well-motivated, properly evaluated, and efficiently implemented. While there are minor weaknesses regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and distance metric justification, these do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the contribution. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria and makes a valuable addition to the literature on text representation and metric learning. I recommend acceptance.
