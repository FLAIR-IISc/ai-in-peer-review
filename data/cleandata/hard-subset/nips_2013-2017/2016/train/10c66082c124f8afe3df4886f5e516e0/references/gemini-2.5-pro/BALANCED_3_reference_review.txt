### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific distance metric for documents. The work builds directly upon the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) [19], an effective unsupervised document distance that computes the minimum cost to "transport" words from one document to another in a pre-trained word embedding space (e.g., word2vec). The key insight of WMD is to frame this as an optimal transport problem.

The authors identify a major limitation of WMD: its unsupervised nature prevents it from adapting to the specific notion of similarity required by a given classification task. To address this, S-WMD introduces two supervised components: (1) a linear transformation `A` applied to the word embedding space, which learns a task-specific ground metric for the transport problem, and (2) a word importance vector `w` that re-weights the bag-of-words representation to emphasize task-relevant words.

The learning objective is formulated to minimize a differentiable proxy of the k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) leave-one-out error, following the framework of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [15]. A significant technical challenge is the high computational cost and non-differentiability of the standard WMD optimization. The authors overcome this by replacing the exact transport problem with its entropy-regularized version, known as the Sinkhorn distance [6]. This relaxation makes the objective strictly convex and allows for efficient, differentiable computation of the transport plan and its gradients. The paper also proposes several practical optimizations, including a clever initialization based on a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD), batch gradient descent, and filtering the training set to only consider nearby documents for gradient computation. This work sits at the intersection of metric learning [15, 36], optimal transport [6, 19], and natural language processing, and is closely related to prior work on learning ground metrics for the Earth Mover's Distance, which has been explored primarily in computer vision [34, 35].

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of the influential WMD algorithm. By introducing a principled way to incorporate supervision, S-WMD significantly advances the state of the art for kNN-based document classification. The joint learning of a linear transformation on the word embedding space and word-specific importance weights is a powerful and novel combination in this context.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The core difficulty—the computational burden and non-differentiability of optimizing through the WMD linear program—is elegantly solved by leveraging the Sinkhorn distance approximation. The derivations for the gradients with respect to both the matrix `A` and the weight vector `w` are well-presented and appear correct. The proposed optimization speed-ups (initialization, batching, neighbor filtering) are practical and demonstrate a thoughtful approach to making the algorithm efficient.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and compelling. S-WMD is benchmarked against an extensive set of 26 baselines across 8 diverse datasets. These baselines include classical document representations (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LDA), modern neural representations (mSDA), and the original WMD/WCD, with and without the application of leading metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The results are very strong, showing that S-WMD achieves the lowest error on 5 of the 8 datasets and the best average rank overall. The inclusion of visualizations (t-SNE plots and word clouds) adds valuable qualitative support for the method's effectiveness.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It provides the necessary background on word embeddings, WMD, and NCA before introducing the S-WMD framework. The motivation is clear, the technical sections are detailed, and the overall structure logically guides the reader through the problem, solution, and evaluation.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Limited Analysis of Learned Components:** While the word cloud visualization for the learned weights `w` is insightful, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of the learned linear transformation `A`. It would be highly beneficial to understand what semantic transformations `A` is learning. For example, the authors could visualize how the geometry of the embedding space changes by showing the nearest neighbors of a few key words before and after the transformation. This would provide more intuition into *how* S-WMD adapts the embedding space for a specific task.

2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method introduces several hyperparameters (e.g., the Sinkhorn regularization parameter λ, batch size `B`, neighbor set size `N`, learning rates). The paper states that λ=10 offers a good trade-off, but a more formal sensitivity analysis or ablation study (perhaps in an appendix) would strengthen the paper's claims of robustness and provide better guidance for practitioners. For instance, how does classification performance vary with different values of λ?

3.  **Context within Modern NLP Architectures:** The evaluation is squarely focused on the kNN classification paradigm. While this is the natural setting for a distance metric, the field of document classification is now heavily influenced by end-to-end deep learning models like CNNs, LSTMs, and particularly large pre-trained transformers (e.g., BERT). A brief discussion in the conclusion or related work section positioning S-WMD relative to these architectures would be valuable. S-WMD could be framed as a strong, data-efficient, and more interpretable non-parametric alternative, which is a valid and important research direction.

4.  **Choice of Ground Distance:** The paper notes that using a squared Euclidean distance might be suboptimal for one dataset. Since word2vec embeddings are often evaluated with cosine similarity, this raises the question of whether the choice of the L2 norm is a limitation. While exploring alternatives is likely beyond the scope of this paper, a short discussion on this point and potential future work exploring other base metrics would be a welcome addition.

### Summary of Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, technically sound, and empirically powerful method for supervised document distance learning. It successfully combines ideas from metric learning and optimal transport to significantly improve upon the highly-regarded Word Mover's Distance. The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. While the analysis could be deepened and the discussion broadened slightly, these are minor points that do not detract from the core contribution. The paper is a clear and significant advance for the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and significant contribution that solves a clear limitation of a popular method (WMD).
*   Technically strong, with an elegant and practical solution to the core computational challenge.
*   Extensive and convincing experimental results demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.
*   Clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   (Minor) Lack of in-depth analysis of the learned embedding transformation `A`.
*   (Minor) Hyperparameter sensitivity is not fully explored.

**Decision:** Accept.
