Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document similarity. Instead of learning a new document representation, S-WMD learns: (1) a linear transform A applied to pre-trained word embeddings (ground metric learning), and (2) a global word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms before computing transport. The learning objective directly optimizes a stochastic leave-one-out kNN criterion using the NCA framework, with pairwise distances defined by the (regularized) optimal transport cost between documents under the transformed embedding and reweighted histograms. To make learning scalable, the authors use the entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn distance) to obtain differentiable, O(q^2) approximations of both the primal transport and dual potentials, enabling efficient gradients w.r.t. A and w. They further accelerate training via (i) an NCA initialization learned on word-centroids (S-WCD), and (ii) neighbor subsampling based on a cheap WCD prefilter. Across 8 datasets and 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised), S-WMD achieves the best or near-best kNN classification performance, and the authors release code.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: Learning the OT ground metric and histogram weights specifically for supervised document similarity is a clear and meaningful extension of WMD. Using Sinkhorn both for speed and to retrieve dual variables for gradients is elegant.
- Practical and bold: Directly optimizing the kNN-LOO objective over an OT distance is ambitious and, to my knowledge, new in NLP; the combination with NCA is thoughtful and pragmatic.
- Technical contributions: Derivations for gradients w.r.t. A (via transport plan) and w (via dual variables) are useful; the approximations via Sinkhorn are sensible and make the problem tractable.
- Empirical breadth: Evaluation spans 8 datasets and 26 baselines, with consistent gains on 5/8 datasets and competitive results elsewhere. Initialization (S-WCD) is a solid baseline in its own right.
- Interpretability: Word weights learned by w are intuitive and support the claim that supervision discovers task-relevant vocabulary.

Weaknesses and questions
- Identifiability and constraints: The interaction between A and w may introduce scale ambiguities. Are there constraints (e.g., w ≥ 0, ||A|| regularization) and how are they enforced? What prevents degenerate solutions?
- Notation/clarity: There are several notational inconsistencies (e.g., p used for norm exponent, dimension, and 1p; q vs p for vocabulary size). Please clean up. Some equations (complexity) gloss over shapes/costs.
- Limited ablation: It would help to isolate contributions: only A, only w, and both; with/without Sinkhorn approximation; sensitivity to λ, batch size, neighbor set size M, and initialization quality.
- Test-time complexity: Training uses Sinkhorn; at inference, do you use exact WMD or the regularized distance? What is the end-to-end cost for kNN search on large corpora? Any use of approximate nearest neighbor or pruning during inference?
- Potential bias from neighbor subsampling: Using WCD to preselect neighbors could bias the NCA objective. A study of performance vs. M (and whether true informative negatives are missed) would increase confidence.
- Comparisons: Supervised neural baselines for text (circa 2016, e.g., CNN/RNN or doc2vec variants) are not reported. While your focus is on distances, such comparisons would strengthen claims of SOTA classification performance.
- Regularization and generalization: No explicit discussion of regularizers on A and w or early stopping; include details and a brief generalization analysis (e.g., overfitting on small datasets).
- Some references are incomplete/placeholder; ensure correctness.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a clever use of Sinkhorn-derived dual variables to differentiate through OT. The objective and optimization are reasonable; however, some details (constraints/regularization, inference-time protocol) should be clarified, and ablations would strengthen support for specific design choices. The empirical evidence is broad and generally compelling.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, but notation is occasionally confusing and a few typos persist. The training protocol (hyperparameters, λ, batch sizes) is described, code is available, and the method seems reproducible. Please add a clear statement of constraints on A and w, the exact distance used at test time, and a concise complexity comparison for training vs. inference.

Originality
High. While metric learning and ground-metric learning for EMD exist, applying it to WMD with learned histogram weights and optimizing an NCA-style kNN objective via Sinkhorn-based gradients in NLP is a novel and bold combination. The work opens a distinctive path for supervised OT-based document similarity.

Significance
High practical impact for retrieval/classification with interpretable distances. Results across many datasets and baselines suggest the approach advances the state of the art for distance-based methods, and the framework could inspire similar supervised OT formulations in other modalities.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel idea with solid technical execution.
- Strong empirical gains over many baselines; code released.
- Efficient training via Sinkhorn; interpretable word weights.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing ablations and some clarity issues.
- Inference-time complexity and choice of exact vs. regularized OT not fully specified.
- Limited comparison to supervised neural text models of the era.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a compelling, novel direction—supervising WMD via learned ground metric and histogram weights with scalable Sinkhorn-based training. Despite some presentation gaps and missing ablations, the contribution is significant and likely to spur follow-on work. I encourage acceptance contingent on clarifications and minor additions outlined above.
