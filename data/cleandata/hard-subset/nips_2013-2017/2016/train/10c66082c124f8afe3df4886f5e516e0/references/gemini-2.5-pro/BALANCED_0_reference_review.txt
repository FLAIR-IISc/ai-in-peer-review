### Summary

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. It builds upon the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) [19], which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. The authors propose to make this distance supervised in two ways: (1) by learning a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space to better suit the classification task, and (2) by learning a vector `w` of word-importance weights to re-scale the normalized bag-of-words representations.

The learning objective is formulated to minimize a differentiable proxy for the leave-one-out k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [15]. A key technical challenge is the non-differentiability and high computational cost of the WMD, which is an optimal transport problem. The authors overcome this by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized version, the Sinkhorn distance [6]. This relaxation makes the objective function differentiable and allows for a much faster, GPU-friendly computation. They derive the gradients for both the transformation matrix `A` and the word weights `w`. To further improve efficiency, they propose a practical optimization scheme that includes a clever initialization strategy (learning `A` on the simpler Word Centroid Distance), batch gradient descent, and filtering the training set to only consider nearby neighbors for each data point. The proposed S-WMD is evaluated on eight document classification datasets, where it is shown to outperform an extensive set of 26 baselines, including unsupervised WMD and various document representations combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of the highly successful WMD: its unsupervised nature. Document similarity is often task-dependent (e.g., similarity by topic vs. similarity by author style), and this work provides a principled and effective framework for learning a supervised, task-specific distance. The idea of simultaneously learning a transformation in the embedding space and re-weighting word importances within the optimal transport framework is novel and powerful. This contribution is likely to be influential and built upon by future work in text-based metric learning.

2.  **Strong Technical Quality:** The paper's technical contributions are solid and well-executed. Differentiating through the WMD optimization problem is non-trivial. The use of the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance is an elegant and effective solution that makes the problem tractable. The derivation of the gradients for both the linear transformation `A` and the word importance weights `w` appears correct and is a key contribution. Furthermore, the proposed optimization heuristics—initializing with a supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) and using a nearest-neighbor-based batch selection—are practical and clever, significantly improving the method's efficiency and making it applicable to real-world problems.

3.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD. The authors compare against a wide array of 26 competitive baselines on eight diverse datasets. These baselines include classic methods (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), modern neural approaches (mSDA), and the original WMD, with and without the application of leading metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results on 5 out of 8 datasets and demonstrates a superior average rank across all datasets. The inclusion of visualizations (t-SNE plots and word clouds of learned weights) provides valuable qualitative insights into why the method works.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The authors provide the necessary background on word embeddings, WMD, Sinkhorn distances, and NCA, making the paper accessible. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the proposed method is developed in a logical, step-by-step manner. The inclusion of pseudocode and a complexity analysis further enhances the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, there are a few areas that could be strengthened:

1.  **Lack of Ablation Study:** The model learns two components: the projection matrix `A` and the word importance weights `w`. It would be very insightful to understand the contribution of each component to the final performance. An ablation study that evaluates S-WMD with only `A` being learned (i.e., `w` fixed to ones) and only `w` being learned (i.e., `A` fixed to identity) would help disentangle their effects. This would provide a deeper understanding of the source of the performance gains.

2.  **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The method introduces several new hyperparameters, most notably the Sinkhorn regularization parameter `λ`. The paper mentions that `λ = 10` offers a good trade-off, but provides little justification or analysis of how sensitive the results are to this choice. A more detailed discussion or a sensitivity analysis regarding `λ` and other key parameters (e.g., batch size `B`, neighbor set size `N`) would strengthen the paper's practical value and reproducibility.

3.  **Discussion on the Ground Metric:** The paper formulates the learned distance based on the squared Euclidean distance in the transformed embedding space (`‖A(xi − xj)‖2^2`). The authors astutely note that on the CLASSIC dataset, where S-WMD does not perform best, this choice might be suboptimal compared to the L2 norm used in the original WMD paper. This is an interesting point that warrants further discussion. Could the framework be generalized to other norms? Acknowledging this limitation more formally and suggesting it as a direction for future work would be valuable.

### Decision

**Accept.**

This paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of document analysis and metric learning. It proposes a technically sound and well-motivated method, S-WMD, to learn a supervised version of the Word Mover's Distance. The core idea of using a differentiable, regularized optimal transport formulation to learn both a word embedding transformation and word importance weights is elegant and effective. The claims are strongly supported by a comprehensive and rigorous experimental evaluation against a large number of baselines, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on several benchmark datasets. The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to suggestions for additional analysis that would further strengthen the paper but do not detract from its core contributions. This work is of high quality and is likely to have a substantial impact on the community.
