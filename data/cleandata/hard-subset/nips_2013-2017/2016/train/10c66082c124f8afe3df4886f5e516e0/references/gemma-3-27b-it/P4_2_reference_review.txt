The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word representations and re-weighted word frequencies to align WMD distances with semantic similarity defined by class labels. The authors address the computational complexity of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and a probabilistic filtering of the training set. They formulate a loss function based on the kNN leave-one-out classification error and provide a gradient-based optimization procedure. The method is evaluated on eight datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to 26 baseline methods.

This paper builds upon existing work in document distance metrics (Salton & Buckley, 1988), word embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013), and optimal transport (Cuturi, 2013; Levina & Bickel, 2007). It connects to recent advances in metric learning (Weinberger & Saul, 2009; Davis et al., 2007; Goldberger et al., 2005) and supervised approaches to EMD/WMD (Wang & Guibas, 2016). Kusner et al. (2015) introduced the original WMD, which this paper extends with a supervised learning framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of learning a metric directly within the WMD framework, combined with word-specific importance weights, represents a novel contribution. Extending WMD with supervision is a significant step.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance to address computational complexity is a sound engineering choice, grounded in established work (Cuturi, 2013). The gradient derivation appears correct and the optimization strategy is well-defined. The discussion of the relaxed primal transport problem and the dual transport problem demonstrates technical depth.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The extensive experimental evaluation across eight datasets and comparison with 26 baselines provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD. The consistent improvement over existing methods is compelling.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The background section provides sufficient context for understanding the technical details. The explanation of the loss function and gradient computation is clear.
*   **Efficiency:** The authors have paid attention to computational efficiency through techniques like batch gradient descent and a probabilistic filtering approach, which is crucial for scaling the method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning, a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters would be valuable. What is the variance in the results when using different settings for λ?
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** The impact of each component of S-WMD (linear transformation *vs.* re-weighted frequencies) could be better analyzed through a more detailed ablation study. How much does each contribute to the overall performance gain?
*   **Dataset Choice & Domain Specificity:** The experimental results show variability across datasets. It's unclear if S-WMD performs consistently well across diverse document types and domains. A discussion of potential limitations regarding domain specificity is missing.
*   **Connection to Existing Metric Learning:** While the paper references metric learning techniques, the connection between S-WMD and the broader field could be strengthened. How does S-WMD relate to other prominent metric learning losses (e.g., triplet loss)?
*   **Implementation Details:** While the code is available, more detailed implementation specifics (e.g., learning rate schedules, optimization algorithm details beyond gradient descent) would enhance reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results demonstrate significant improvement over numerous baselines.
*   The proposed method offers a novel approach to incorporating supervision into the well-established WMD framework.
*   The authors address the computational challenges effectively, making the method practical.
*   The paper is technically sound and well-written.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a comprehensive ablation study limits the understanding of the contribution of each component.
*   The paper does not fully explore the limitations and potential domain-specific biases.
*   A deeper connection to the broader field of metric learning would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective method for supervised document distance learning. While some aspects could be further investigated (e.g., ablation study, hyperparameter sensitivity, domain specificity), the compelling empirical results and technical soundness warrant acceptance. The contribution is significant and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.
