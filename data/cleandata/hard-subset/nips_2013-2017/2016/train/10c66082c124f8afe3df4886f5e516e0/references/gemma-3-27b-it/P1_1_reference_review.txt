## Peer Review: Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document similarity. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weighted word frequencies, optimizing directly for classification performance. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed optimization problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering of the training set.  The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on 8 datasets, outperforming a comprehensive set of 26 baseline methods.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon the recent success of WMD (Kusner et al., 2016) as a powerful, embedding-based document distance metric. It also connects to the broader field of metric learning, specifically drawing inspiration from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) (Goldberger et al., 2005). The authors effectively position their work within this landscape, highlighting how S-WMD differs from existing approaches by learning *within* the embedding space and incorporating word importance weights, rather than directly learning document representations.  They correctly acknowledge the work in efficient Wasserstein distance computation (Cuturi, 2013) as crucial for feasibility.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Performance:** The results are compelling. Consistently outperforming 26 baselines across 8 datasets is a significant achievement, and the comprehensive comparison is commendable.
* **Novel Approach:** Learning a metric *within* the word embedding space, coupled with word re-weighting, is a genuinely novel contribution. This avoids the dimensionality reduction bottleneck common in traditional metric learning approaches for text.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  The use of the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering demonstrate a practical awareness of the computational challenges, enabling the method to scale to reasonably sized datasets.
* **Clear Motivation and Problem Statement:** The paper effectively articulates the limitations of unsupervised document distance metrics and motivates the need for task-specific learning.
* **Detailed Explanation & Implementation:** The paper provides sufficient detail to understand and potentially reproduce the results, with a link to the codebase. The gradient derivations are clearly presented.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The related work section is thorough and well-organized, placing the work in context.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention Bayesian optimization, the details are limited. The sensitivity of performance to hyperparameters (learning rates, λ, batch size, neighbor size) isn’t thoroughly explored or discussed.  A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter tuning would strengthen the results.
* **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks deeper theoretical analysis.  Understanding *why* S-WMD performs well – beyond the intuition of aligning distances with semantic similarity – would be valuable.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** The ablation study could be more extensive.  While the results for S-WMD initialization are presented, a systematic investigation of the contributions of the linear transformation (A) and the word weights (w) would be insightful. For example, what happens if you only learn A, or only w?
* **Squared Euclidean Distance:** The choice of the squared Euclidean distance in the objective (eq. 5 & 8) feels somewhat arbitrary. The paper doesn't explain why this was chosen over other distance metrics, and it’s potentially limiting. The authors acknowledge a possible suboptimal choice in the discussion of the CLASSIC dataset.
* **Potential for Overfitting:** The learned word weights could be prone to overfitting to the specific training data, particularly with smaller datasets. A discussion of regularization techniques or validation strategies to mitigate this risk would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective method for improving document distance metrics.
* The empirical results are consistently strong, demonstrating a significant advance over existing state-of-the-art techniques.
* The authors address the computational challenges effectively, making the method practical.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of deeper theoretical analysis leaves open questions about the underlying mechanisms driving the performance gains.
* The limited ablation study and discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity reduce confidence in the robustness of the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of document similarity. The empirical results are very compelling, and the approach is both novel and practical. While the lack of deeper theoretical analysis and a more extensive ablation study are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the overall strengths of the work.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Rating:** 8.5/10 (Excellent)

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Conduct a more thorough ablation study to assess the contributions of the linear transformation and word weights.
*   Provide a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter tuning process and the sensitivity of performance to different parameter settings.
*   Explore potential regularization techniques to prevent overfitting of the word weights.
*   Consider discussing the theoretical implications of using the squared Euclidean distance metric.
*   Elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of why the learned metric aligns with semantic similarity.
