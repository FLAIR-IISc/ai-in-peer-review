### Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach extends the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" words from one document to the other within a pre-trained word embedding space. The authors' key innovation is to make this distance metric supervised by learning two components simultaneously: 1) a linear transformation (matrix A) of the word embedding space to better align with the classification task, and 2) a vector of importance weights (w) to up-weight or down-weight specific words in the document's bag-of-words representation.

To learn these parameters, the authors formulate an objective function based on Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), which aims to minimize the stochastic leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor (kNN) classification error. A significant technical challenge is that the WMD calculation involves solving a linear program, making gradient-based optimization computationally prohibitive and potentially unstable. The authors address this by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized counterpart, the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation makes the objective function differentiable and allows for a much faster, GPU-friendly computation of the distance and its gradients. The paper proposes further practical speed-ups, including a smart initialization scheme based on a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) and a batch-based gradient descent that considers only a local neighborhood of documents. The method is validated through extensive experiments on 8 datasets, where it is shown to outperform 26 competitive baselines, often achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Critique**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the technical execution is sound. The primary difficulty in this work is making a supervised version of WMD tractable, and the authors' solution—using the Sinkhorn distance relaxation—is both elegant and effective. The derivation of the gradients for both the linear transformation `A` and the word weights `w` (leveraging the dual of the transport problem) is correct and clearly presented.

The experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough and convincing. The comparison against 26 baselines, including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with multiple metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN), provides strong evidence for the method's superiority. The authors are careful to report standard errors and follow robust evaluation protocols. The results in Table 2 clearly demonstrate that S-WMD is either the top-performing method or highly competitive across all datasets. The inclusion of qualitative results, such as the t-SNE visualizations (Figure 1) and the learned word weights (Figure 2), adds further confidence in the method's effectiveness and interpretability.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of providing the necessary background on word embeddings, WMD, the Sinkhorn distance, and NCA before introducing their own contributions. The structure is logical, flowing from the problem definition to the proposed solution and its empirical validation. The mathematical formulations are presented clearly, and the inclusion of Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary of the training procedure. The figures and tables are informative and well-designed. The explanation of the computational speed-ups is particularly clear and highlights the practical viability of the proposed approach.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While metric learning and the Earth Mover's Distance are not new concepts, their combination in this specific context is novel and impactful. Specifically:
1.  It is the first work, to my knowledge, to successfully learn a supervised metric directly for the Word Mover's Distance in a way that is computationally feasible for non-trivial datasets.
2.  The idea of simultaneously learning a transformation of the geometric space (via matrix `A`) and a re-weighting of the histogram masses (via vector `w`) within an optimal transport framework is a powerful and novel contribution. This sets it apart from prior work on learning EMD ground distances (e.g., Wang et al. [35]), which did not consider re-weighting the histograms.
3.  The use of the NCA loss with the Sinkhorn-relaxed WMD is a novel and effective combination of existing techniques to solve a new problem. The paper does a good job of positioning itself relative to related work, clearly delineating its unique contributions.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. The problem of defining a meaningful distance between documents is fundamental to information retrieval, document classification, and many other areas of NLP. WMD was already a significant step forward from traditional BOW models by leveraging the semantics of word embeddings. S-WMD takes this a crucial step further by allowing this powerful distance to be tailored to a specific supervised task.

Given the strong empirical results, S-WMD is likely to become a new state-of-the-art baseline for kNN-based document classification. The method is not just a theoretical curiosity; the authors have demonstrated that it can be trained efficiently, making it practical for real-world applications. Furthermore, the core idea of jointly learning a ground metric transformation and feature importance weights within a regularized optimal transport framework is a general one that could inspire similar models in other domains, such as computer vision for image retrieval based on feature histograms.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Impact:** Proposes a novel, principled, and highly effective method for supervised document distance learning that significantly advances the state of the art.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The use of the Sinkhorn relaxation to make the problem tractable is a strong technical contribution. The derivations are solid.
*   **Exceptional Empirical Validation:** The extensive experiments across 8 datasets and 26 baselines provide compelling evidence of the method's superiority.
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is clearly written, and the authors promise to release their code, which will benefit the community.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The problem it addresses is fundamental, and the proposed solution is likely to be widely adopted and built upon.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   (Minor) The paper notes that S-WMD performs slightly worse than WMD on the CLASSIC dataset, attributing this to a poor initialization or the use of squared Euclidean distance. While this honesty is commendable, a brief discussion or ablation study on the choice of the ground metric (e.g., L1 vs. L2) could have strengthened this point.
*   (Minor) The choice of the regularization parameter `λ` is set to 10 based on it being a "nice trade-off". A sensitivity analysis on this parameter would have been a useful addition, though its absence does not detract significantly from the main contribution.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of machine learning and natural language processing. It tackles a challenging and important problem with a novel and technically sound solution. The extensive and rigorous experimental evaluation demonstrates clear state-of-the-art performance. The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and poised to have a substantial impact. I strongly recommend acceptance.
