### Summary

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. While WMD has shown state-of-the-art performance, it is entirely unsupervised. This paper addresses this limitation by incorporating supervision.

The core idea is to learn two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, which adapts the ground distance for the transport problem, and (2) a word importance vector `w`, which re-weights the document's bag-of-words representation to emphasize task-relevant words. To learn `A` and `w`, the authors frame the problem as minimizing a stochastic approximation of the leave-one-out kNN classification error, following the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) framework.

A major challenge is the computational cost and non-differentiability of the WMD optimization. The authors overcome this elegantly by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance). This relaxation makes the problem strictly convex and differentiable, allowing for efficient gradient computation in O(q^2) time (where q is the number of unique words). The paper further proposes practical optimization strategies, including a clever initialization using a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batch gradient descent scheme that only considers a document's nearest neighbors. The method is evaluated on 8 document classification tasks, demonstrating superior performance over an extensive set of 26 baselines, including various document representations combined with leading metric learning algorithms.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a highly novel and significant contribution. Extending the powerful WMD framework to a supervised setting is a natural and important research direction. The approach of learning a metric in the low-dimensional word embedding space, rather than the high-dimensional document space, is both elegant and effective. This work successfully bridges the gap between recent advances in word embeddings (WMD) and the well-established field of metric learning. The resulting method is likely to have a substantial impact and inspire follow-up work in text classification, retrieval, and other related areas.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical execution is excellent. The key insight to use the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable proxy for WMD is clever and makes the proposed learning framework computationally feasible. The derivation of the gradients with respect to both the linear transformation `A` and the word weights `w` is sound. The proposed optimization heuristics—initializing with S-WCD and using a nearest-neighbor filtered batch gradient descent—are practical and crucial for making the training process efficient, as demonstrated by the reported training times.

3.  **Experimental Rigor:** The empirical evaluation is exceptionally thorough and convincing. The authors compare S-WMD against an extensive list of 26 competitive baselines on 8 diverse datasets. This includes classic representations (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), modern neural methods (mSDA), and the original WMD, with and without supervision from established metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The results consistently show that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance, providing strong evidence for the method's effectiveness. The t-SNE visualizations and word cloud analysis provide valuable qualitative insights into what the model learns.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient background on word2vec, WMD, Sinkhorn distances, and NCA, making the paper accessible to a broad audience. The motivation is clear, the proposed method is explained logically, and the experimental setup and results are presented transparently.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Sensitivity to Regularization:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance introduces a regularization hyperparameter `λ`. The authors state they use `λ=10`, but there is no discussion on the sensitivity of the model's performance to this choice. A larger `λ` yields a better approximation to the true WMD but can lead to numerical instability and slower convergence. A brief analysis of this trade-off would strengthen the paper's practical recommendations.

2.  **Scalability to Long Documents:** While the paper's optimizations significantly reduce training time, the core gradient computation still scales quadratically with the number of unique words in a document (p). For datasets with very long documents, this O(p^2) complexity could remain a bottleneck. The authors allude to this when discussing the performance on BBCSPORT, but a more explicit discussion of this limitation would be beneficial.

3.  **Choice of Ground Metric:** The method learns a linear transformation `A` but is ultimately based on a squared Euclidean distance in the transformed space (`||A(xi - xj)||^2_2`). The paper notes that this might be suboptimal for one dataset. While extending this is non-trivial, a brief discussion on the potential for learning more complex or non-Euclidean ground metrics could be a valuable addition for future work.

### Justification for Score

This paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound method for supervised document distance learning. It solves the non-trivial problem of incorporating supervision into the Word Mover's Distance by leveraging an elegant computational relaxation (Sinkhorn distance) and effective optimization techniques. The claims are strongly supported by an exceptionally comprehensive experimental evaluation, which demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on a wide range of tasks. The work is a significant advancement over prior art and is of high interest to both the metric learning and NLP communities. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
