This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by learning a linear transformation of word representations and re-weighted word frequencies. The core idea builds upon existing metric learning techniques but adapts them to the specific context of WMD, aiming to minimize kNN classification error. The authors tackle the computational challenge of applying metric learning to WMD by leveraging a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering.

The paper is well-written and clearly presents the motivation, methodology, and experimental results. It’s commendable that the authors address the computational complexity inherent in their approach and propose efficient solutions. The extensive experimental evaluation across eight datasets and comparison with 26 baselines demonstrates a thorough effort to validate their method. The connection to existing work in metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and optimal transport is well-established.

However, despite the comprehensive evaluation, I remain skeptical about the practical significance of the improvements. The gains over existing methods, while statistically significant in some cases, appear modest. The paper relies heavily on empirical results; a stronger theoretical justification for the proposed approach would be valuable. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of using the squared Euclidean distance, and potential issues with document length, these points are not deeply explored. The impact of the chosen regularization parameter (λ) on the Sinkhorn distance is mentioned but not systematically investigated, and the specific value (λ=10) feels somewhat arbitrary. Furthermore, the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance needs careful qualification, as several baselines, particularly those incorporating established metric learning techniques, perform competitively. The initialization procedure, while clever, is presented as crucial for performance, raising questions about the sensitivity of the method to initialization choices. The ablation study is lacking – a detailed analysis of the contribution of the linear transformation versus the word re-weighting would be helpful.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear and well-structured presentation.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation across multiple datasets and baselines.
*   Addresses a computationally challenging problem with a practical solution (Sinkhorn distance).
*   Combines ideas from metric learning and optimal transport effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Modest improvements over strong baselines.
*   Lack of strong theoretical justification.
*   Limited analysis of parameter sensitivity (e.g., λ).
*   Insufficient ablation study to understand the contribution of individual components.
*   The performance gains are not consistently observed across all datasets.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and well-executed contribution to the field of document distance metrics.
*   The experimental evaluation is extensive and demonstrates the potential of the proposed method.
*   The computational improvements achieved through the use of the Sinkhorn distance are valuable.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The improvements over existing methods are not substantial enough to warrant strong endorsement.
*   The lack of theoretical analysis and sensitivity analysis weakens the overall contribution.
*   The reliance on empirical results alone raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
*   The claim of "state-of-the-art" is overstated.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak accept**. While the work is technically sound and the experimental evaluation is thorough, the modest improvements and lack of deeper theoretical investigation prevent me from recommending a strong acceptance. The paper represents an incremental contribution that, while valuable, does not significantly advance the state of the art. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses identified above, particularly through a more thorough theoretical analysis and sensitivity study, to strengthen their claims and demonstrate the broader impact of their work.
