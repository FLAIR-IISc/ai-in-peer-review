Summary
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of WMD for document similarity. The method learns (i) a linear transformation A applied to pre-trained word embeddings and (ii) a nonuniform word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The learning objective minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out kNN error à la NCA: pairwise document distances are computed by an optimal-transport problem whose ground cost is ‖A(xi−xj)‖^2, and marginals use the reweighted histograms d̃ = (w ◦ d)/(wᵀd). To make training tractable, the authors use entropic OT (Sinkhorn) both to accelerate distance computation and to obtain approximate gradients, using primal transport matrices T*λ for ∂/∂A (via the envelope theorem) and dual potentials α*,β* for ∂/∂w. They further propose an initialization by learning A with NCA on word centroids (S-WCD), and a batch/neighbor-filtered training loop. Experiments on 8 datasets against 26 baselines report strong average ranking and best performance on 5 datasets.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on WMD (Kusner et al. 2015) and entropic OT/Sinkhorn (Cuturi 2013). It follows the metric learning tradition (NCA, LMNN, ITML) but moves the learning to the word embedding space and the histogram weights rather than the document vector space. Supervised EMD learning exists in vision (e.g., Wang & Guibas 2009; Wang et al. 2011), typically learning a Mahalanobis ground metric under ITML; this paper differs by (a) operating on pre-trained word embeddings for text, (b) coupling metric learning with learned word importance weights, and (c) optimizing a kNN/NCA objective using Sinkhorn-based gradients. The use of Sinkhorn dual/primal to obtain parameter gradients is consistent with recent optimal transport literature.

Strengths
- Clear and natural formulation: learning A and w to align OT-based distances with labels is well-motivated and leverages strong priors (pretrained embeddings).
- Solid computational strategy: using entropic OT for both efficiency and differentiability, plus envelope-theorem style gradients for A and w, is appropriate and elegant.
- Practical training recipe: good initialization (S-WCD), batch sampling, and neighbor filtering via WCD are well thought out and likely crucial for scalability.
- Empirical results: wide comparison set (unsupervised and supervised metric learning baselines), consistent average gains and SOTA on a majority of datasets; code release aids reproducibility.
- Interpretability: learned w highlights class-discriminative words.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Constraints/regularization on w and A: The method requires d̃ to remain a valid histogram (nonnegative and normalized). The paper does not state whether w is constrained to be nonnegative during optimization, nor does it describe any projection or parameterization (e.g., softplus). Negative components in w would invalidate the OT constraints. Similarly, no regularization (e.g., Frobenius norm on A, ℓ1/ℓ2 on w) is discussed; learning both A and w may overfit, especially with small datasets.
- Notational and clarity issues: p is overloaded (power in the ground metric, dimension of 1-vector, number of unique words per document). Section headers and equations contain typos (e.g., “.2 Optimization”). Please clean up variable naming (use q or n consistently for vocabulary/document support size) and correct the Sinkhorn kernel definition to use the cost C: Kij = exp(−λ Cij).
- Dual potentials sign/normalization: In entropic OT, dual potentials typically satisfy f = −(1/λ) log u and g = −(1/λ) log v up to additive constants. The paper uses +log(u)/λ and subtracts the mean. Because α,β are defined up to a constant, the centering may fix the ambiguity, but the sign matters for gradients. Please derive and clarify the exact relationship used and its effect on ∂D/∂w.
- Objective/gradient mismatch: Eq. (6) uses the unregularized DA,w, but gradients are computed with T*λ and entropic duals. This is a biased gradient unless the loss also uses the entropic OT value (or λ→∞). Either optimize the entropic transport cost in the loss or provide justification that the approximation is accurate enough and show sensitivity to λ. Reporting results with the Sinkhorn divergence would also be relevant.
- Scalability and test-time costs: Training complexity is analyzed, but test-time kNN with OT remains expensive. Do you use the relaxed distance (Sinkhorn) at test time? Is neighbor filtering via WCD used at inference? Please quantify test-time latency, especially on the larger datasets, and compare to WMD with the same filtering.
- Experimental scope: The comparisons are comprehensive for metric learning, but additional strong text baselines (linear SVM/logistic regression on TF-IDF; fastText) would contextualize gains for classification. Also include ablations: A-only (fixed w), w-only (A=I), effect of initialization, and effect of λ.
- Reproducibility details: Important hyperparameters (learning rates, number of iterations, early stopping, regularization) are not fully specified; results rely on BO for baselines but not for λ or A/w regularization. Provide full training details and report variance across runs.

Questions for the authors
1. How do you enforce w ≥ 0 (and possibly w > 0) during optimization? If you project or reparameterize, please describe it.
2. Do you regularize A or w? If so, how do you set penalties? If not, what prevents overfitting?
3. Is the loss in Eq. (6) computed with the entropic transport value or the unregularized WMD? What λ is used at inference?
4. What is the exact mapping from Sinkhorn scalings u,v to α,β used for ∂/∂w? Please reconcile sign conventions.
5. Provide ablations for A-only, w-only, and sensitivity to λ, batch size B, and neighbor count N.
6. What is the test-time cost per query on the largest dataset under your neighbor filtering strategy?

Minor comments and typos
- Fix section numbering (“.2 Optimization”), equation references, and consistent notation for q/n (support size) vs d (embedding dim) vs r (projection rank).
- Clarify the use of squared cost (p=2) vs L2; WMD often uses p=1. You speculate this might hurt on CLASSIC—please evaluate both.
- Some references are incomplete and several years/venues are missing; ensure accurate citations (e.g., Cuturi & Peyré, 2016; Wang et al., ECCV 2011).
- Provide or move key tables/figures to the main text if space allows; as is, the reader cannot verify numbers without the supplement.

Overall assessment
This is a timely and well-motivated contribution that thoughtfully combines metric learning with OT for document distances, offering practical training tricks and strong empirical performance. The main technical ideas are sound and incremental rather than groundbreaking, but the combination (learning A and w for WMD using Sinkhorn-based gradients) is novel in the text domain and practically useful. However, clarity issues, missing constraints/regularization for w, and the unaddressed approximation gap between the unregularized loss and entropic gradients need to be resolved.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the optimization details (constraints on w, dual potentials), aligning the objective with the entropic approximation, and adding ablations/sensitivity analyses. Confidence: high.
