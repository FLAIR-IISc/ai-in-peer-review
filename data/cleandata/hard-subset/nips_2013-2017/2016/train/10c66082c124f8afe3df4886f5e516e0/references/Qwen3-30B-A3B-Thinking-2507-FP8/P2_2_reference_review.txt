

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel approach to improving the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by incorporating supervision. The authors recognize that WMD, while effective, is entirely unsupervised and not optimized for specific classification tasks. They introduce two key innovations: (1) a linear transformation of word embeddings (A) to better capture task-specific semantic relationships, and (2) word importance weights (w) to emphasize words that are more discriminative for the classification task. 

The authors frame this as metric learning within the word embedding space, and they efficiently solve the computationally challenging optimization using the Sinkhorn distance (a relaxed version of optimal transport). They demonstrate that S-WMD outperforms 26 baselines across 8 document classification datasets, with the method achieving the lowest test error on 5/8 datasets and the best average performance.

## Strengths

### Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide solid theoretical foundations for their gradient computations (equations 8 and 10) and thoroughly justify their use of Sinkhorn distance for computational efficiency. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with 8 diverse datasets across different text classification tasks, and the authors appropriately compare against 26 baselines (including both unsupervised and supervised methods). The paper includes important limitations and discusses why S-WMD underperforms on certain datasets (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON), showing careful evaluation.

The computational efficiency claim is well-supported by the reported training times in Table 3, which demonstrate that S-WMD is competitive with other metric learning approaches. The authors also make their code publicly available on GitHub, enhancing reproducibility.

### Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The background section effectively introduces WMD and metric learning, providing the necessary context for readers unfamiliar with these concepts. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained throughout. The paper is structured logically, with each section building upon the previous one. The visualization in Figure 1 (t-SNE embeddings) effectively demonstrates the improvement of S-WMD over WMD, and Figure 2 provides insightful visualization of the learned word importance weights.

### Originality
The paper makes a clear and meaningful contribution to the field. While WMD was introduced by Kusner et al. [19], the authors are the first to incorporate supervision directly into the WMD framework. The idea of learning word-specific importance weights (w) to re-weight the bag-of-words representation is novel and provides additional flexibility beyond existing metric learning approaches. The authors properly distinguish their work from similar approaches like Wang et al. [35] by noting that their method considers histogram re-weighting, which they demonstrate provides additional benefits.

### Significance
The results demonstrate significant improvements over the state-of-the-art, with S-WMD outperforming all other methods on average across 8 datasets. The method is computationally efficient (as shown in Table 3), making it practical for real-world applications. The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature—unsupervised WMD is not optimized for specific classification tasks, and this work provides a principled way to incorporate supervision. The approach has clear potential for adoption in text classification and retrieval tasks.

## Weaknesses

### Limited Analysis of Failure Cases
While the paper acknowledges that S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON, it doesn't provide a deep analysis of why this occurs. The paper suggests possible reasons (long document lengths for BBCSPORT, poor initialization for CLASSIC), but doesn't explore these systematically. A more detailed analysis of failure cases would strengthen the paper and provide insights for future work.

### Incremental Nature of the Contribution
While the contribution is valuable, it's somewhat incremental—building on existing WMD and metric learning techniques rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach. The paper could better emphasize how S-WMD differs from recent advances in metric learning, particularly deep metric learning approaches.

### Limited Exploration of Word Embeddings
The paper uses word2vec throughout, but doesn't explore how different word embeddings (e.g., GloVe, ELMo, or BERT) might affect performance. This is a notable omission given that the quality of word embeddings is known to significantly impact text classification results.

### Limited Discussion of Hyperparameters
The paper mentions using λ=10 for the Sinkhorn regularization but doesn't thoroughly justify this choice or explore how different values of λ might affect performance. The paper also doesn't discuss the impact of the batch size (B=32) and neighbor set size (N=200) on the final results.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of document distances. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing approaches. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. While there are some minor weaknesses, they don't detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution.

The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature—improving unsupervised document distance metrics for specific classification tasks—and does so in a principled way that advances the state of the art. The experimental results are convincing, and the computational efficiency makes the approach practical for real-world applications.

## Specific Recommendations for Improvement

1. Add a more detailed analysis of the failure cases (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON) to better understand the limitations of the approach.

2. Explore the impact of different word embeddings (e.g., GloVe, ELMo) on S-WMD performance to demonstrate the robustness of the approach.

3. Provide a more thorough discussion of the hyperparameter choices (λ, B, N) and their impact on performance.

4. Briefly discuss how S-WMD might integrate with deep learning frameworks for end-to-end training, as this is an important direction for future work.

## Conclusion

"Supervised Word Mover's Distance" is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document distance metrics. The authors present a well-motivated approach that effectively combines metric learning with optimal transport, demonstrating clear improvements over the state-of-the-art. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and thoroughly evaluated. While there are some minor areas for improvement, these don't detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
