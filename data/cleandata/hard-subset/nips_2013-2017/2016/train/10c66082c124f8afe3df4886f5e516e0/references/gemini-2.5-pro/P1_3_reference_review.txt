### Summary

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other within a pre-trained word embedding space. While WMD has shown state-of-the-art performance, it is entirely unsupervised. This paper's key contribution is to incorporate supervision into the WMD framework.

The authors achieve this by learning two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, which adapts the word representations to the specific classification task, and (2) a word-importance vector `w`, which re-weighs the document's bag-of-words representation to emphasize task-relevant words. To make this learning tractable, the authors address a major computational hurdle: the high cost of computing the WMD and its gradient. They replace the exact WMD (an EMD problem) with its entropically-regularized, differentiable counterpart (the Sinkhorn distance), which can be solved much more efficiently. The learning objective is to minimize a stochastic version of the kNN leave-one-out classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). The paper includes several practical optimizations, such as a clever initialization scheme based on a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batch gradient descent procedure that uses a kNN-based approximation to further speed up training. The method is evaluated extensively on 8 datasets against 26 baselines, demonstrating state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of WMD: its unsupervised nature. Making WMD supervised is a natural and valuable research direction. The proposed method of jointly learning a linear transformation of the embedding space and word-importance weights is an elegant and effective way to incorporate label information. This is a novel combination of ideas from optimal transport (WMD, Sinkhorn distance) and metric learning (NCA).

2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The primary technical challenge—computing gradients through the WMD optimization—is solved convincingly. The use of the Sinkhorn distance is a key insight that makes the problem computationally feasible and the objective function smooth. The derivation of the gradients with respect to both the matrix `A` and the weight vector `w` (using the dual variables of the transport problem) is sound. The additional engineering efforts, including the S-WCD initialization and the efficient batching strategy, are practical and crucial for making the algorithm usable.

3.  **Extensive and Compelling Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is exceptionally thorough. The authors compare S-WMD against a comprehensive suite of 26 baselines across 8 diverse datasets. The baselines include classic document representations (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), modern neural methods (mSDA), and the original WMD, combined with multiple powerful metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN). The results are very strong, with S-WMD achieving the best performance on 5 out of 8 datasets and the best average rank overall. This provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It provides clear background on the necessary components (word2vec, WMD, Sinkhorn distance, NCA), logically presents the proposed S-WMD method, and details the optimization process. The inclusion of visualizations (t-SNE plots and a word cloud of learned weights) adds valuable intuition about what the model is learning.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** While S-WMD performs very well on average, it is outperformed on three datasets (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON). The discussion in Section 4 offers plausible but speculative hypotheses for this (e.g., long document lengths, poor initialization, suboptimality of squared Euclidean distance). A more in-depth analysis would strengthen the paper. For instance, could the authors investigate the properties of the learned matrix `A` or weights `w` on these datasets to provide more concrete evidence for these hypotheses?

2.  **Novelty in the Context of EMD Metric Learning:** The idea of learning a ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance is not entirely new (e.g., Wang et al. [35], cited by the authors). The paper would benefit from a slightly more detailed discussion in the related work section that more clearly delineates its contribution from prior work on learning EMD metrics, specifically highlighting the novelty of applying this to the text domain via WMD and the joint learning of histogram weights (`w`), which appears to be a unique contribution.

3.  **Dependence on Hyperparameters:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance introduces a key hyperparameter `λ`, and the optimization involves learning rates and batch sizes. The paper mentions using Bayesian optimization to tune hyperparameters for all methods, which is good practice. However, more discussion on the sensitivity of S-WMD to its own key parameters (especially `λ`) would be welcome. For instance, how does the trade-off between approximation accuracy and convergence speed manifest in practice?

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper proposes a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound solution to an important problem: supervising the powerful WMD metric.
*   **Pro:** The experimental results are state-of-the-art, validated by an extremely thorough comparison against a wide array of strong baselines.
*   **Pro:** The work successfully combines ideas from metric learning and optimal transport in a practical and effective way, with a high likelihood of influencing future research in document similarity.
*   **Pro:** The paper is clearly written and the authors have made their code available, facilitating reproducibility and adoption.
*   **Con:** The analysis of cases where the method does not achieve top performance is somewhat superficial.
*   **Con:** The paper could better position its novelty against prior work on learning ground metrics for EMD.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a significant contribution to the field of document analysis and metric learning. The problem is important, the solution is elegant and effective, and the experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. This is a high-quality paper that should be accepted at NIPS.
