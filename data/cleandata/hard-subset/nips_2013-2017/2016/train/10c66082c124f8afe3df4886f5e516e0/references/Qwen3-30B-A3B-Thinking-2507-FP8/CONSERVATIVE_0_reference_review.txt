

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes "Supervised Word Mover's Distance" (S-WMD), extending the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights. The authors claim their approach outperforms 26 baselines across 8 document classification tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths:**
The paper builds on solid foundations (WMD, metric learning, Sinkhorn distance) and presents a coherent framework for supervised document distance learning. The use of Sinkhorn distance to approximate the WMD computation is well-justified and aligns with recent literature. The experimental setup is comprehensive, evaluating on multiple document classification tasks with a wide range of baselines. The authors provide pseudocode, implementation details, and even training time comparisons, which is commendable for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1. **Insufficient Validation Against Established Baselines:** While the paper claims "the best performing distance metric for documents to date," the evaluation is limited to 8 datasets and 26 baselines. Crucially, the paper doesn't demonstrate why established metric learning methods (like NCA, LMNN, ITML) applied directly to standard document representations would fail to outperform their approach. The paper states "S-WMD achieves the lowest kNN test error on 5/8 datasets," but doesn't provide statistical significance tests for these differences. Without such analysis, the claimed superiority is difficult to verify.

2. **Questionable Novelty of Core Idea:** The paper's main contribution—applying metric learning to WMD—is not particularly novel. The authors cite Wang & Guibas [34] and Wang et al. [35] as similar work, yet fail to adequately differentiate their approach. Most significantly, they don't address why their re-weighting approach (which they claim provides "extra flexibility") is superior to existing methods that already incorporate similar concepts.

3. **Insufficient Analysis of Approximation Error:** The paper relies heavily on the Sinkhorn approximation of WMD (with λ=10) but provides minimal analysis of the approximation error introduced. They claim the approximation is "accurate" but don't quantify how much the relaxed transport affects the actual distance metric or classification performance. This is a significant oversight for a paper claiming to improve upon WMD.

4. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper makes claims about the effectiveness of their approach but provides no theoretical analysis to explain why their supervised learning method should outperform unsupervised WMD. They don't analyze the statistical properties of their loss function or provide convergence guarantees.

5. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison:** The paper compares against "6 document representations" with and without metric learning (totaling 26 baselines), but this comparison is problematic. The baselines are not all directly comparable—some are document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI), while others are distances (WCD, WMD). This makes it difficult to isolate the contribution of the supervised component.

6. **Questionable Initialization Claim:** The authors claim "S-WMD right after initialization (S-WMD init.) performs quite well" but don't explain why the initialization would be so effective. If the initialization is already good, the supervised component's contribution is questionable.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of being "the best performing distance metric for documents to date." The evaluation lacks statistical rigor, the novelty is questionable, and the paper doesn't adequately differentiate itself from existing work on metric learning for text.

The authors should address the following before resubmission:
1. Provide statistical significance testing for all performance comparisons
2. Conduct a more thorough comparison of their approach against established metric learning methods
3. Analyze the approximation error introduced by the Sinkhorn relaxation
4. Provide stronger theoretical justification for their approach
5. Clarify why their re-weighting approach provides meaningful improvements over existing methods

The paper makes a modest incremental contribution to the field of metric learning for text, but the claims of "state-of-the-art" performance are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented. The paper would be better suited for a more specialized venue with a narrower scope, rather than NIPS where the bar for novelty and rigorous validation is exceptionally high.
