Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns: (i) a linear transform A of pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec) to define the ground cost, and (ii) a nonuniform importance vector w to reweight term frequencies in the document histograms. The metric is trained to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification loss (via NCA) where distances between documents are computed as optimal transport in the transformed embedding space with reweighted histograms. Direct optimization over the exact WMD is computationally heavy, so the authors use the entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) relaxation to obtain smooth, fast approximations of the transport plan and the dual potentials, enabling approximate gradients with O(p^2) cost in the number of unique words per document. They also propose a strong initialization (S-WCD) by running NCA on document centroids in embedding space and accelerate training by batching and neighborhood pruning with a WCD-based prefilter. On 8 datasets, S-WMD outperforms 26 baselines on average and is best on 5/8 datasets. Code is provided.

The work is well-positioned with respect to WMD (Kusner et al., ICML’15), metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML), and entropy-regularized OT (Cuturi, NIPS’13). It is also related to supervised EMD/ground metric learning in computer vision (Wang & Guibas, ECCV’12; Cuturi & Avis), but differs by operating on text with pre-trained embeddings and by jointly learning histogram reweighting.

Quality:
- Methodology is sound and well-motivated. Using Sinkhorn OT to enable differentiable, efficient optimization of a kNN/NCA objective over a WMD-like distance is a solid idea. The gradient w.r.t. A using the current transport plan is standard; the use of the relaxed dual variables for gradients w.r.t. w is reasonable.
- The empirical study is broad, with diverse datasets and many baselines (including unsupervised and supervised metric learning on multiple representations). Average-rank improvements and several best-in-class results support the claims; training time measurements help establish practicality; code availability improves reproducibility.

That said, some technical and experimental details need clarification:
- Positivity/constraints on w: Eq. (4) requires w ≥ 0 to keep histograms valid. The paper does not state constraints or optimization strategy to enforce them (e.g., reparametrization via softplus, projected gradient, exponentiated update). Please clarify and report whether w is regularized (e.g., L2 or sparsity) to avoid overfitting.
- Objective vs. gradient consistency: The loss in Eq. (6) uses DA,w (unregularized WMD), but gradients are computed using the Sinkhorn plan and duals. Do you actually optimize the regularized OT cost Dλ,A,w in the NCA probabilities, or do you use a biased gradient estimator for the unregularized cost? Please make this consistent and explicit.
- Dual potentials sign/constants: For entropic OT with T* = diag(u)Kdiag(v), typical duals satisfy f = −(1/λ)log u, g = −(1/λ)log v up to additive constants. Your expressions use +log(u)/λ with a centering term. Please double-check signs and provide a short derivation or a citation to avoid confusion.
- Complexity accounting: The stated O(d^2(p + r)) term for assembling ∂/∂A seems pessimistic; with algebra (and symmetry) one can often reduce cost below forming all (xi − xj)(xi − xj)ᵀ terms. A brief note on implementation tricks would be useful.
- Sensitivity and ablations: It would strengthen the paper to include ablations of A-only vs. w-only learning; sensitivity to λ and to the choice of embedding (e.g., all results with the same word2vec model?); and the effect of the WCD neighbor pruning parameter N on both speed and accuracy.
- Fairness of baselines: Supervised baselines are run after PCA to 200 dims; it would be helpful to confirm whether this dimensionality is tuned and whether they also benefit from the same word embeddings (e.g., average word embeddings + metric learning). Including a simple supervised classifier on averaged embeddings (e.g., logistic regression/SVM) would provide a strong and commonly used baseline.
- Generalization: Results are limited to kNN classification. A brief exploration of retrieval or clustering metrics, or at least a discussion, would broaden the impact.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-structured, with a helpful background section and an intuitive narrative. A few issues to fix:
- Notation: p/q/n are used for different quantities; ensure consistency. Spell out constraints and domains (e.g., w ≥ 0).
- Minor typos and formatting: missing section label “3.2” header formatting; a stray “[5, 21?]”; ensure references are complete and properly formatted.
- Training details: learning rates, batch size, stopping criteria, and how neighborhood pruning is applied should be fully specified for reproducibility.

Originality:
The combination of (i) learning a supervised ground metric for WMD in the word embedding space, (ii) jointly learning word-importance weights, and (iii) leveraging Sinkhorn OT within an NCA objective for scalable training is novel and nontrivial. While related components exist in prior OT/EMD metric learning, the application to text with modern embeddings and the overall training pipeline appear original.

Significance:
Improving WMD via supervision is impactful: WMD is widely used and a learned variant that remains efficient is likely to be adopted. The empirical gains are meaningful across varied datasets, and the approach is modular (can plug in different embeddings). The code release increases the likelihood of follow-up work.

Pros:
- Clear, well-motivated problem; principled formulation.
- Efficient training via Sinkhorn, batching, and a strong initialization.
- Strong empirical results across many baselines; code available.
- Joint learning of A and w provides interpretability (word importance).

Cons:
- Missing constraints/regularization on w; potential overfitting.
- Ambiguity about regularized vs. unregularized objective and dual signs.
- Limited ablations/sensitivity analyses; evaluation limited to kNN.
- Some notational/clarity issues.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Explicitly enforce and state w ≥ 0 (e.g., w = softplus(z)) and add regularization terms on A and w; report ablations.
- Clarify whether you optimize Dλ or D and align the gradients accordingly; add a short derivation (or citation) for the dual potentials used.
- Add sensitivity to λ and to embedding choice; include A-only and w-only variants.
- Include a supervised baseline on averaged word embeddings and/or doc2vec/fastText (if feasible) for completeness.

Recommendation:
I lean toward acceptance. The paper addresses an important problem with a principled and practical method, and shows strong empirical improvements with available code. Resolving the noted technical clarifications and adding small ablations would further strengthen the work.
