

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) called S-WMD, which learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and word-specific importance weights to improve document classification. The authors claim their method outperforms 26 baselines across 8 datasets.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a clear motivation for supervised metric learning in text classification, acknowledges the limitations of unsupervised document distances, and provides a well-structured technical approach. The use of Sinkhorn distance for computational efficiency is reasonable, and the implementation details appear thorough.

**Major Weaknesses:** Despite these strengths, the paper falls short of NIPS' high standards for scientific contribution due to several critical issues:

1. **Lack of Novelty:** The core approach is a straightforward application of metric learning (specifically NCA) to the WMD framework. The authors cite NCA, LMNN, and ITML as related work but fail to clearly distinguish their contribution from these well-established methods. The "supervised" aspect is not novel, as most metric learning methods are supervised by definition. The paper doesn't adequately explain why their specific formulation (learning both a linear transformation and word weights) is fundamentally different from prior work.

2. **Inadequate Evidence for Claims:** The paper claims S-WMD "outperforms all other 26 methods" on average, but only achieves the best results on 5 out of 8 datasets. For BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON, it ranks 4th or lower. The paper fails to provide a rigorous statistical analysis of these differences or explain why the method performs poorly on certain datasets. The claim that S-WMD "is the best performing distance metric for documents to date" is unsupported by the evidence presented.

3. **Questionable Computational Claims:** The paper claims O(q²) complexity using the Sinkhorn approximation, but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the approximation error is acceptable for the classification task. The paper states the method is "surprisingly fast" compared to baselines, but provides no actual runtime comparisons beyond "comparable or even faster than the time it takes to train a linear metric on the baseline methods." Without concrete timing data, this claim is unsubstantiated.

4. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to determine the relative contribution of the linear transformation versus the word re-weighting component. This is critical for understanding what aspects of the method are actually driving performance improvements.

5. **Inadequate Theoretical Justification:** The paper lacks a strong theoretical argument for why their approach should work better than alternatives. The connection between the supervised learning objective and improved classification performance isn't rigorously established.

6. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations:** The paper fails to thoroughly discuss the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the trade-off between the Sinkhorn approximation's accuracy and computational efficiency. The paper doesn't address whether the approximation might harm performance on certain types of text data.

**Specific Concerns:**

- The paper claims "S-WMD achieves the lowest kNN test error on 5/8 datasets," but for BBCSPORT and CLASSIC, it's only 4th best. The paper doesn't explain why it performs so poorly on these datasets, especially since WMD itself performs reasonably well on these datasets.

- The paper states "S-WMD right after initialization (S-WMD init.) performs quite well," suggesting the main value of their method is the initialization rather than the learning procedure. This undermines the paper's main contribution.

- The paper's claim that "S-WMD outperforms NCA with TF-IDF relatively by 10%, 42%, and 15%, respectively" on TWITTER, CLASSIC, and REUTERS is misleading. The paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for these improvements.

- The paper's evaluation of "8 different document corpora" lacks detail about dataset characteristics that might explain varying performance. For example, the paper states that "on datasets with smaller documents such as TWITTER, CLASSIC, and REUTERS, S-WMD outperforms NCA with TF-IDF," but doesn't clarify what "smaller documents" means in quantitative terms.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a technically sound extension of WMD, it fails to meet NIPS' standards for a significant scientific contribution. The method is a straightforward application of existing metric learning techniques rather than a novel approach. The evidence supporting the method's superiority is insufficient, as the performance gains are modest and inconsistent across datasets. The paper doesn't adequately distinguish its contribution from prior work, and the claims about performance and efficiency aren't sufficiently supported by the evidence provided.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More rigorous statistical analysis of performance differences
2. Clear ablation studies to identify the key components driving performance
3. A thorough comparison with more recent metric learning methods
4. A detailed discussion of the trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational efficiency
5. Clearer explanation of why the method performs poorly on certain datasets

As it stands, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify its claims of being "the best performing distance metric for documents to date" or to establish a significant methodological contribution beyond existing metric learning approaches. The incremental nature of the contribution and lack of thorough validation against established baselines make it difficult to endorse as a significant advance for NIPS.
