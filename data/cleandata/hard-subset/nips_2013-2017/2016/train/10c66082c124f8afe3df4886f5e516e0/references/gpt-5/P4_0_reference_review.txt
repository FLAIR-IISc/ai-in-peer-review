Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) [19] for measuring distances between text documents. WMD uses optimal transport over pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec [22]) to compute document distances without learning a document representation. S-WMD learns (i) a linear transformation A of the word embedding space (a generalized Euclidean ground metric) and (ii) a word-importance vector w to reweight document histograms. The objective is a neighborhood-components-analysis (NCA)–style [15] stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss over document distances under the transport cost induced by A and w. To make optimization tractable, the authors replace exact WMD with its entropically regularized variant (Sinkhorn distance) [6], enabling O(q^2) transport computation and yielding dual potentials via Sinkhorn scaling that are used to compute gradients. They also introduce an initialization via supervised learning on the word centroid distance (S-WCD) and speed up training with WCD-based neighbor filtering.

Empirically, S-WMD is evaluated on eight datasets spanning categorization, sentiment, and product identification, against 26 baselines including BOW/TF-IDF/LSI/LDA/mSDA, WCD/WMD, and three metric-learning methods (NCA/LMNN/ITML) applied to reduced-dimensional representations. S-WMD achieves the lowest kNN error on 5/8 datasets and the best average rank, with training times comparable to or faster than baseline metric learning after PCA. Visualizations (t-SNE) and learned word-weight analyses illustrate the effect of supervision.

Strengths
- Methodological novelty: Learns both a ground metric in the embedding space and word-importance weights within an optimal-transport document distance, directly minimizing a surrogate of LOO kNN error. Prior metric learning for EMD exists in vision, but this instantiation for WMD with word reweighting and Sinkhorn-based differentiation is a new and timely contribution for text.
- Sound use of entropic OT: The switch to Sinkhorn makes the loss differentiable and computation practical, and the use of dual potentials to obtain gradients w.r.t. w is principled.
- Practical efficiency: Initialization via S-WCD and neighborhood filtering reduce cost substantially; complexity is carefully analyzed. The approach stays in the low-dimensional word-embedding space rather than operating in sparse BOW space.
- Strong empirical evaluation: Eight datasets, wide set of competitive baselines with hyperparameters tuned via Bayesian optimization, and released code improve credibility and reproducibility. Gains are consistent on average.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation scope: Results are reported only for kNN classification, which aligns with the training objective but does not test broader utility (e.g., retrieval, clustering) or other classifiers that would also rely on the learned distance.
- Ablations and sensitivity: Limited analysis of the relative contributions of A vs. w (beyond S-WCD and S-WMD init). No sensitivity analysis for λ (entropic strength), neighbor set size N, or batch size. The choice λ=10 is fixed without empirical justification across datasets.
- Notational/clarity issues: Several inconsistencies/typos hinder readability: reuse of symbols p/q for different quantities; “.2 Optimization” header; ambiguous dimension of the all-ones vector in dual formulas; a dangling reference “[5, 21?]”; and a stray file path in the text. Some details (embedding dimension d, which word2vec model, k in kNN) are not clearly specified.
- Theoretical justification boundaries: The gradient expression ∂/∂A using T* (Eq. 8) is consistent with envelope-theorem arguments for the smoothed problem, but this rationale is not explicitly stated. Likewise, the centering in the dual potentials (α*, β*) deserves explanation. These omissions do not invalidate the method but reduce clarity.
- Long-document behavior: The method appears less competitive on datasets with longer documents (BBCSPORT, OHSUMED), and the paper offers only a brief conjecture. More systematic analysis would be useful.

Assessment by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: Generally sound and technically well-motivated. The use of entropic OT and dual potentials for gradients is appropriate. Empirical evaluation is thorough across diverse baselines with fair tuning. Missing ablations and limited analysis for hyperparameters and failure modes modestly reduce the technical completeness.
- Clarity: The paper is mostly clear and well organized, with helpful algorithmic descriptions and complexity analysis. However, notation conflicts, typos, and a few broken references should be fixed; additional explanations for the dual-based gradients and parameter choices would strengthen clarity.
- Originality: The combination of NCA-style supervised training with a learnable WMD ground metric and histogram reweighting, enabled by Sinkhorn differentiation, is novel in the NLP setting. The relation to prior EMD metric learning in vision is acknowledged, and the differences are substantive.
- Significance: Likely to influence practitioners seeking robust document distances and inspire further supervised OT approaches in NLP. Average improvements across many strong baselines and efficient training suggest practical impact. Broader significance would be bolstered by additional tasks and ablations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations isolating the effect of A vs. w, and sensitivity to λ, N, and batch size.
- Report results with other pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe) to test robustness.
- Include retrieval metrics or clustering quality to demonstrate utility beyond kNN classification.
- Clarify and unify notation (avoid overloading p/q), fix typos/broken references, and specify all key hyperparameters (including k in kNN) and embedding details.
- Discuss the envelope-theorem justification for gradients under entropic OT and the centering constants in α*, β*.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated supervised extension of WMD with a principled, efficient learning procedure.
- Strong average performance across many datasets and baselines; code released.
- Method is general, modular, and computationally practical.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited evaluation scope (only kNN), with fewer gains on longer-document datasets.
- Missing ablations/hyperparameter sensitivity analyses.
- Some clarity/notation issues and minor technical explanations omitted.

Overall, the contribution is solid, relevant to NeurIPS topics (optimal transport, metric learning, NLP), and empirically compelling, with room for improved analysis and presentation.
