- Proposes S-WMD: a supervised extension of Word Mover's Distance that learns a linear embedding transform and word-specific importance weights.  
- Uses the Sinkhorn (entropy-regularized) distance to make supervised WMD training computationally efficient (O(q²) vs O(q³ log q)).  
- Evaluated on 8 diverse document classification datasets and outperforms 26 baselines on most tasks.  

Quality — Strengths:
- Mathematically sound with clear loss, gradient, and optimization derivations.  
- Thorough experimental evaluation across varied datasets and tasks.  
- Comprehensive comparisons against many baseline methods and metric learning variants.  
- Training time analysis supports computational efficiency claims.  

Quality — Weaknesses:
- Lacks theoretical justification for why supervision (linear transform and weights) improves WMD.  
- No ablation studies to quantify contributions of the transform vs. word weights.  
- Initialization (NCA on WCD) is not thoroughly justified or compared to alternatives.  
- Does not discuss failure cases or limitations (e.g., short documents, OOV words).  

Clarity — Strengths:
- Well-organized paper with clear notation and reproducible experimental setup.  
- Visualizations (embedding plots, learned weights) effectively illustrate benefits.  

Clarity — Weaknesses:
- Inconsistent terminology (S-WMD vs SWMD) causing reader confusion.  
- Ambiguous distinction between "re-weighted word frequencies" and "word importance weights."  
- Insufficient explanation of why the Sinkhorn approximation does not significantly degrade learned metric quality.  

Originality — Strengths:
- Novel integration of supervision into the WMD framework.  
- New combination of linear embedding transform and word-specific importance weighting.  
- Clever application of Sinkhorn distance for efficient supervised training.  

Originality — Weaknesses:
- Insufficient differentiation from Wang et al. [35] regarding metric learning in EMD ground distance.  
- Fails to cite or position relative to Tang et al. [32] on sentiment-specific supervised word embeddings.  

Significance — Strengths:
- Demonstrates substantial empirical gains (lowest kNN error on 5/8 datasets) and practical efficiency.  
- Provides an implementable method likely useful for text classification practitioners.  

Significance — Weaknesses:
- Improvements are not consistent across all datasets (e.g., BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON).  
- Limited argument for importance of supervised WMD relative to other trends (e.g., fine-tuning large pretrained models).  
- Applicability appears focused on text classification with unclear extension to other domains.  

Overall assessment:
- Well-written, technically solid, and novel contribution with strong empirical support but would benefit from theory and ablation analyses.  

Recommendation:
- Accept — solid methodological contribution with strong experiments and practical value.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- First to incorporate supervision into WMD, strong empirical results, and computationally efficient implementation.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited theoretical justification, terminology/confusion issues, incomplete differentiation from related work, and non-uniform gains across datasets.
