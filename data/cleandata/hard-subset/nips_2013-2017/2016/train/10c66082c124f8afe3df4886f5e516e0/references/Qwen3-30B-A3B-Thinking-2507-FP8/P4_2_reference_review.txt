

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to learn a metric optimized for classification tasks. WMD [19] computes document distances as the optimal transport cost of moving words between documents in the word embedding space, leveraging pre-trained word vectors. The authors recognize that WMD is unsupervised and propose to learn a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to better align with semantic similarity as defined by labels. They address the computational challenge of learning with WMD by using the Sinkhorn distance approximation [6], enabling efficient gradient computation and training.

The paper relates to previous work in metric learning [10, 15, 36, 37] and WMD [19], positioning S-WMD as a method that operates directly on the word embedding space rather than transforming document representations. This differs from prior supervised metric learning approaches that typically learn a linear transformation of the document representations themselves.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper presents a technically sound method with rigorous mathematical derivations for the loss function, gradients, and optimization. The authors provide a detailed explanation of the computational complexity and address the key challenge of making supervised WMD tractable. The experimental evaluation across 8 diverse document datasets (news categorization, sentiment analysis, etc.) is comprehensive, with 26 baselines including both unsupervised and supervised approaches. The use of kNN classification error as the evaluation metric is appropriate for the problem, and the results are statistically validated through multiple train-test splits for datasets without predefined splits.

*Clarity:* The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from background to method to experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility. The visualization in Figure 1 effectively demonstrates the improved clustering of documents using S-WMD compared to WMD.

*Originality:* The combination of supervised learning with WMD represents a meaningful extension to a previously unsupervised method. While metric learning has been applied to document representations [10, 15, 36], S-WMD is novel in operating directly on the word embedding space and learning both a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights. The paper adequately differentiates from related work, particularly noting that Wang et al. [35] learn a metric within EMD but do not consider histogram re-weighting.

*Significance:* The method achieves state-of-the-art results across multiple document classification tasks, with S-WMD outperforming all 26 baselines on average. The improvements are significant (e.g., 51% relative error reduction over LSI on BBCSPORT), and the computational efficiency (Table 3) makes the approach practical for real-world applications. The paper demonstrates how supervision can effectively enhance a powerful unsupervised distance metric, advancing the state of the art in document similarity learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*The paper does not sufficiently explore the limitations of the squared Euclidean distance assumption for word vectors. While the authors note that this choice may be suboptimal for some datasets (e.g., CLASSIC), they don't provide analysis on why this occurs or how to address it more generally.

*The initialization procedure (S-WCD) relies on the WCD, which is a lower bound of WMD [19]. While this is reasonable, the paper doesn't fully justify why this particular initialization leads to better convergence compared to other alternatives.

*The paper focuses on kNN classification, but doesn't address whether the learned metric generalizes to other downstream tasks beyond classification, such as clustering or retrieval, which would strengthen the significance claim.

*While the method is efficient relative to other metric learning approaches, the computational complexity (O(TBN[p² + d²(p + r)])) remains substantial for large vocabularies or very large datasets, and the paper doesn't provide concrete examples of the method scaling to extremely large document collections.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro: S-WMD is a well-motivated, technically sound extension of a significant prior work (WMD) that achieves state-of-the-art results across multiple document classification tasks with reasonable computational efficiency. The method is clearly described, thoroughly evaluated, and advances the state of the art in document distance metrics.

*Con: The paper doesn't fully address the limitations of the squared Euclidean distance assumption for word vectors, and the significance is somewhat limited to classification tasks without broader application discussion. The computational complexity, while improved, may still be prohibitive for some large-scale applications.

Overall, this paper makes a strong technical contribution to the field of document similarity learning by successfully incorporating supervision into the WMD framework. The method is well-justified, thoroughly evaluated, and demonstrates clear improvements over prior work. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
