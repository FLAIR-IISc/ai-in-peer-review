Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of WMD [19] that learns (i) a linear transformation A of the word embedding space and (ii) per-word importance weights w that reweight document histograms. The metric is trained to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss à la NCA [15]. To overcome the cubic cost and nondifferentiability of exact OT, the authors use entropic regularization (Sinkhorn) [6] to obtain a unique transport plan and dual potentials, enabling efficient O(q^2) computation and approximate gradients with respect to A and w. They further propose a strong initialization (S-WCD: NCA on word centroids) and WCD-based neighbor filtering during training. Across 8 datasets and 26 baselines (including strong metric learning methods on conventional representations), S-WMD attains the lowest kNN error on 5/8 datasets and the best average rank.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on WMD [19], entropic OT [6], and NCA [15]. It is related to supervised learning of EMD ground metrics in vision [34, 35], and to using Wasserstein losses in deep learning [9]. The contribution here is adapting metric learning to the OT ground cost in the word-embedding space, jointly learning a word-importance reweighting, and making the training computationally tractable for text.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: WMD is powerful yet unsupervised; different tasks require different notions of similarity.
- Methodological soundness: Formulating supervision via an NCA loss on pairwise OT distances is principled. Using entropic OT to obtain unique plans and dual potentials is a sensible and efficient approximation; the envelope theorem justifies gradients through the dual for the histogram weights.
- Practical engineering: S-WCD initialization and WCD-based neighbor pruning are effective and easy to implement. Complexity is analyzed; code is released.
- Empirical breadth: 8 datasets, many relevant baselines (including metric learning after PCA), and consistent gains on average. The visualizations help illustrate qualitative improvements.

Weaknesses and points requiring clarification
- Regularized vs. unregularized objective: The loss in Eq. (6) is written in terms of DA,w (unregularized WMD), but gradients are computed using the entropically regularized solver T*λ and duals α*λ,β*λ. Are you optimizing a surrogate where DA,w is replaced by the entropic OT cost, or are you using regularized objects only to approximate gradients of the unregularized objective? Please clarify which distance is used in the loss during training and at test time, and discuss the approximation error.
- Dependence of K on A: In the Sinkhorn solver, K = exp(−λ‖A(xi − xj)‖^2) depends on A. Do you recompute K for each document pair and current A at every iteration? The text in Section 2 defines K for fixed embeddings; with learned A this must be updated, which affects runtime.
- Constraints and regularization: The histogram reweighting requires w ≥ 0 for d̃ to remain a valid histogram. Is w constrained (e.g., positivity via softplus) and/or regularized (L1/L2) to avoid overly peaky or unstable weights? Similarly, A is unconstrained; in practice NCA often benefits from regularization or low-rank parameterization (choosing r < d). Please report whether you constrain A = L (r×d) and how r is chosen.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity and ablations: λ critically affects the transport and gradients; using a fixed λ=10 without normalization of costs is brittle across datasets. Please provide sensitivity analyses for λ, batch size B, neighbor count N, and r. Also ablate: only A, only w, both; with/without S-WCD init; with/without neighbor pruning.
- Fairness and scope of baselines: LDA is trained transductively (on train+test), which is unusual and may inflate its performance; clarify whether any other method benefits similarly. Also, since the focus is kNN with a distance, it’s fair not to compare to supervised neural classifiers, but a brief discussion of test-time cost versus standard linear/SVM baselines would improve context.
- Test-time efficiency: For kNN one must compute distances from each test doc to many training docs. Do you use the same WCD-based pruning at test time? What is the end-to-end query time?
- Theoretical rigor: While the use of dual potentials and envelope theorem is appropriate for exact OT, here you use entropic approximations. A short discussion of when α*λ,β*λ provide unbiased/sensible gradients for w (and any pitfalls) would strengthen the paper.
- Clarity/typos: Several minor issues hinder readability: missing/incorrect citations (e.g., “[5, 21?]”), section numbering (“.2 Optimization”), a stray file path, and some notation inconsistencies (p used both for Lp and for vocabulary size). Proofreading is needed.

Quality
The approach is technically reasonable, and the empirical results are largely supportive. However, several important implementation details and choices (noted above) are under-specified, and the regularized/unregularized mismatch should be clarified.

Clarity
The core idea is well presented, but the paper needs editing for precision and consistency. Adding pseudocode that explicitly states whether regularized distances are used in training/testing, and an ablation/ sensitivity figure, would aid reproducibility.

Originality
Learning a supervised ground metric for OT is not new, but applying it to WMD with joint histogram reweighting, NCA loss, and efficient Sinkhorn-based gradients for text is a novel and useful combination.

Significance
Given the popularity of WMD and word embeddings, a supervised and efficient variant is valuable. The gains over strong baselines across diverse datasets suggest the method will be adopted, especially for retrieval/classification with limited labeled data.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and practical problem with a principled, efficient method.
- Strong average empirical performance and open-source code.
- Sensible engineering (initialization, pruning) that others can reuse.

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguity about the exact objective being optimized; approximation gap not analyzed.
- Missing constraints/regularization details for w and A; limited ablations/sensitivity.
- Clarity issues and minor errors.

Questions for authors
1) Are w constrained to be nonnegative? Is any regularization applied to A or w?
2) Do you optimize and evaluate with entropically regularized OT distances, or only use Sinkhorn to approximate gradients for the unregularized objective?
3) Is K recomputed per iteration/pair as A changes?
4) Do you use neighbor pruning at test time? What is the end-to-end query time?
5) Please provide ablations (only A vs only w; with/without S-WCD init; sensitivity to λ and r).
