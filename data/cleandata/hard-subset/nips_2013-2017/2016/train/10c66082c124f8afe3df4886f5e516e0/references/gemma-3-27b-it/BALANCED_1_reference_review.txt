This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by learning a linear transformation of word representations and re-weighted word frequencies. The core idea is to directly minimize a stochastic version of the leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric. A key contribution is an efficient technique leveraging the Sinkhorn distance to address the computational complexity of WMD, combined with probabilistic filtering of the training set. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance across 8 datasets and 26 baselines.

This work builds on the recent success of WMD [Kusner et al., 2015] which offered a compelling alternative to traditional document distance measures. The paper correctly identifies the limitations of unsupervised approaches and frames the problem as a supervised metric learning task. The use of Sinkhorn distance for faster computation is a significant practical contribution, addressing a major bottleneck in applying WMD to larger datasets. The connection to Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [Goldberger et al., 2005] provides a sound theoretical foundation for the proposed method. Several related works in metric learning [Weinberger & Saul, 2008; Davis et al., 2007] and Wasserstein distance computation [Cuturi, 2013] are appropriately cited.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper convincingly demonstrates superior performance against a comprehensive set of baselines across diverse datasets. The ablation study with S-WMD initialization provides valuable insight.
* **Practical Contribution:** Addressing the computational bottleneck of WMD via the Sinkhorn distance makes the method applicable to more realistic problem sizes.
* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:**  The grounding in metric learning and the connection to NCA provide a strong theoretical basis.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the technical details are presented clearly, although the equations can be dense. The explanation of the gradient computation and its approximation are commendable.
* **Comprehensive Experimentation:**  The large number of datasets and baselines demonstrates thorough evaluation of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Insight into Weight Interpretation:** While the paper shows that learning word weights improves performance, it provides limited qualitative analysis of *what* the learned weights represent.  Are specific words consistently upweighted across datasets?  Is there a correlation between weight and TF-IDF? Providing more insight into the learned weights would strengthen the understanding of the method's behavior.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While Bayesian optimization is used for tuning, the paper doesn't explicitly discuss the sensitivity of performance to hyperparameters (e.g., λ in the Sinkhorn distance, learning rates). A brief discussion of hyperparameter robustness would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity Discussion:** While the authors mention the quadratic complexity with respect to the vocabulary size, a more detailed discussion of the actual runtime on the different datasets would be useful.  It would be helpful to compare the runtime to the original WMD implementation and to highlight the speedup achieved.
* **Comparison to Recent Advances:** The related work section could benefit from including more recent advancements in optimal transport and metric learning, especially those addressing computational efficiency. Recent diffusion-based approaches to optimal transport, for example, might be relevant.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to supervised document distance learning.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance.
* The computational efficiency improvements are significant and broaden the applicability of WMD.
* The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the method and its theoretical foundations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The limited insight into the learned word weights reduces the interpretability of the method.
* The lack of discussion regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost details.
* While thorough, the ablation study primarily focuses on the initialization, further ablation studies might provide deeper insights into the contributions of each component.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document distance learning. The proposed S-WMD method is novel, effective, and computationally efficient. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned word weights, including visualizations and qualitative examples.
2.  Discuss the sensitivity of performance to key hyperparameters.
3.  Include a more detailed discussion of runtime performance and scalability.
4.  Expand the related work section to include more recent advancements in optimal transport and metric learning.
