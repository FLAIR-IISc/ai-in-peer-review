

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

## Summary
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of Kusner et al.'s unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates label information to improve document classification. While WMD computes document distances by finding the optimal transport cost of moving words between documents in word embedding space, S-WMD learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to better align distances with semantic similarity encoded in labels. The authors frame this as minimizing a stochastic version of leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric, using the Sinkhorn distance for efficient computation. The work builds on prior metric learning methods (NCA, LMNN, ITML) but applies them specifically to the WMD framework, which is distinct from traditional metric learning that operates on document representations rather than word embeddings.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The mathematical formulation of S-WMD, including the loss function, gradients, and optimization procedure, is well-justified and carefully derived. The authors correctly identify that directly applying supervision to WMD would be computationally prohibitive, and they properly address this by using the Sinkhorn distance relaxation. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with results on 8 datasets across 26 baseline methods, including both unsupervised and supervised approaches. The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including implementation specifics and hyperparameter tuning procedures. The authors are appropriately cautious in their claims, noting that S-WMD does not consistently outperform all baselines on every dataset (e.g., it ranks 4th on BBCSPORT and CLASSIC).

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section effectively introduces necessary concepts (word2vec, WMD, metric learning) without overwhelming the reader. The mathematical formulations are clear and logically presented, with sufficient explanation for each component. The experimental section is thorough, with detailed results, statistical analysis, and visualizations (Figures 1 and 2) that effectively illustrate the method's benefits. The pseudo-code for S-WMD (Algorithm 1) provides a clear implementation roadmap. The writing maintains a professional tone throughout, avoiding unnecessary jargon.

## Originality
The paper makes a clear and meaningful contribution to the field. While WMD itself was a significant innovation (Kusner et al., 2015), incorporating supervision into this framework represents a novel direction. The authors carefully differentiate their work from prior metric learning approaches (NCA, LMNN, ITML), which operate on document representations rather than word embeddings. The dual contribution of learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights is particularly novel. The paper adequately references related work, including Wang & Guibas (2014) and Wang et al. (2015), which learn metrics within EMD frameworks but without histogram re-weighting. The novelty is well-justified and significant for the NIPS community.

## Significance
The results demonstrate clear significance. S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance on 5 out of 8 datasets, with statistically significant improvements over unsupervised baselines (WMD and WCD) and other document representation methods. The paper shows that supervision can substantially improve document distances, with relative error reductions of up to 51% compared to LSI on some datasets. The computational efficiency (Table 3) is impressive, with training times comparable to or faster than other metric learning approaches. The method's potential applications in text retrieval, sentiment analysis, and document classification make it broadly relevant to the NIPS community.

## Strengths and Weaknesses
*Strengths*: 
- Clear and novel contribution to the WMD framework
- Thorough experimental evaluation across multiple datasets and baselines
- Well-justified mathematical formulation
- Practical computational approach using Sinkhorn distance
- Strong empirical results with statistical significance

*Weaknesses*:
- The paper could better articulate why their supervised approach to WMD is superior to simply applying standard metric learning to the WMD embedding (which would require transforming the document representations rather than word embeddings)
- Limited discussion of limitations, particularly regarding scalability to extremely large datasets or different word embedding types
- The connection between the learned word importance weights and actual semantic relevance could be more deeply analyzed beyond the visualization in Figure 2
- The initialization method (S-WCD) is presented as effective, but the paper doesn't thoroughly explore how sensitive the final results are to this initialization

## Recommendation
The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of text representation learning. It addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by incorporating supervision into the WMD framework, with clear theoretical justification and compelling empirical results. The method is technically sound, well-executed, and demonstrates significant improvements over prior work. While there are minor limitations in the discussion of limitations and initialization sensitivity, these do not detract from the paper's overall quality.

**Pro Acceptance**: Strong theoretical contribution, clear novelty, comprehensive evaluation, practical efficiency, significant improvements over baselines.

**Con Acceptance**: Limited discussion of limitations, some aspects of the approach could be better contextualized relative to alternative supervised approaches.

Overall, this paper represents a significant advancement in document distance metrics that would be of interest to the NIPS community. It meets all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level and would be a valuable addition to the conference.
