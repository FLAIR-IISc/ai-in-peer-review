### NIPS Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. While WMD is powerful, it is entirely unsupervised. This work addresses that limitation by incorporating supervision.

The authors propose learning two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, and (2) a vector of word importance weights `w` that re-scales the document's bag-of-words representation. The learning objective is to minimize a differentiable proxy for the k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) leave-one-out classification error, adopting the stochastic neighborhood loss from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA).

A key technical challenge is the high computational cost of WMD and its non-differentiability, which makes direct gradient-based optimization intractable. The authors propose an elegant solution by replacing the exact WMD (an instance of the Earth Mover's Distance) with its entropically regularized counterpart, the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation yields a strictly convex and differentiable objective that can be solved efficiently, allowing for tractable gradient computation. The paper further proposes several practical optimizations, including a clever initialization strategy using a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD), batch gradient descent, and filtering the training set to only consider nearby neighbors. The method is validated through an extensive experimental comparison on 8 datasets against 26 baselines, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on the majority of tasks.

---

**Strengths**

1.  **High Quality and Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically very strong. The core problem—learning a metric *within* the WMD framework—is non-trivial. The central contribution of using the Sinkhorn distance to make the WMD objective differentiable and computationally tractable is both clever and well-justified. The derivations for the gradients with respect to the transformation matrix `A` and the word weights `w` appear sound and leverage established results from the optimal transport literature. The entire pipeline, from the WCD-based initialization to the batch-based optimization, presents a complete and well-thought-out solution.

2.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant and original contribution. While metric learning and WMD are existing concepts, their combination in this manner is novel. Unlike typical metric learning approaches that operate on fixed document-level feature vectors, S-WMD learns a metric on the underlying word-level space, which is a more fundamental and powerful approach. The simultaneous learning of word importance weights (`w`) adds another layer of task-specific adaptation that is missing from prior work. Given the impressive performance of the unsupervised WMD, this supervised extension is a natural and important next step. The strong empirical results, outperforming an extensive set of 26 baselines on average, convincingly demonstrate that this approach advances the state of the art in document classification. The method is likely to be influential and built upon by others.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established, the background on WMD and NCA is concise and sufficient, and the proposed method is described with a logical flow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining not just *what* they did, but *why* they made certain design choices (e.g., using Sinkhorn distance for efficiency). The inclusion of Algorithm 1, detailed experimental setup, and the promise of public code greatly enhance the paper's clarity and potential for reproducibility. The visualizations (t-SNE plots and word cloud) effectively support the quantitative results.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and compelling. The authors test their method on 8 diverse datasets, covering different domains and document characteristics. The comparison against 26 baselines—including classic methods (TF-IDF), modern deep learning approaches (mSDA), WMD itself, and supervised versions of all representation-based methods using standard metric learners (NCA, LMNN, ITML)—is exhaustive and leaves little room for doubt about the method's effectiveness. The authors are also intellectually honest, clearly reporting and discussing cases where S-WMD does not achieve the top rank.

---

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Justification for Some Hyperparameters:** While the overall method is well-motivated, the choice of certain hyperparameters feels slightly ad-hoc. For instance, the regularization parameter λ for the Sinkhorn distance is set to 10 based on it being a "nice trade-off between speed and approximation accuracy." A sensitivity analysis or a more principled discussion on the effect of λ on the final performance and gradients would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Dependence on Squared Euclidean Distance:** The formulation relies on the squared Euclidean distance as the ground metric between transformed word vectors (||A(xi - xj)||^2). The authors themselves note this may be suboptimal on the CLASSIC dataset, where the unsupervised WMD (which uses L2 distance) performs better. While this is a minor point and common in metric learning, a brief discussion on the potential of learning other types of metrics (e.g., non-linear transformations or different norms) could be a valuable addition for future work.

3.  **Potentially Overstated Conclusion:** The conclusion claims S-WMD "may well be the best performing distance metric for documents to date." While the results are indeed state-of-the-art on the tested benchmarks, this is a very strong and broad claim. A more measured statement reflecting the scope of the experiments would be more appropriate.

---

**Overall Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. It addresses a clear and important problem: how to supervise the powerful Word Mover's Distance. The proposed solution is technically sound, elegant, and original. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to enable efficient, gradient-based learning is a key methodological contribution. The paper is supported by an exceptionally thorough empirical evaluation that demonstrates a new state of the art in kNN-based document classification. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. This paper is a perfect fit for NIPS and is likely to be of high interest to the community.
