

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes an extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) to incorporate supervision, termed Supervised WMD (S-WMD). The authors aim to improve document classification by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to better align with semantic similarity encoded in labels. The method leverages the Sinkhorn distance for computational efficiency and frames the learning problem as minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out classification error.

The paper is well-organized and presents a technically sound extension of WMD. The background on WMD, metric learning, and the Sinkhorn relaxation is clear and well-motivated. The authors correctly position their work as building on prior metric learning approaches (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and the WMD framework.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear technical description of the method and its computational advantages.
- The empirical evaluation across 8 datasets is comprehensive, showing S-WMD outperforms 26 baselines on 5/8 datasets.
- The computational efficiency claims are reasonable, with training times comparable to other metric learning approaches.
- The visualization of learned word weights (Figure 2) provides intuitive evidence of the method's ability to capture meaningful word importance.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is its lack of sufficient theoretical justification for why this specific approach would outperform other established methods. While the authors correctly note that prior work (Wang & Guibas, 2015) has explored similar ideas, they don't sufficiently differentiate their contribution. The word importance weights (w) are introduced as a simple extension to standard metric learning, but the paper fails to demonstrate that this component is necessary for the observed improvements. An ablation study showing the impact of including w versus just learning the linear transformation A would have strengthened the claims.

The empirical results, while showing marginal improvements, don't convincingly demonstrate that these improvements are significant or practically meaningful. For instance, the paper reports S-WMD outperforms NCA with TF-IDF by 10-42% on certain datasets, but doesn't provide statistical significance tests or discuss whether these differences are large enough to warrant adoption in real-world applications. The paper also notes that S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT and CLASSIC, but doesn't analyze why these datasets are particularly challenging for the proposed method.

Most critically, the paper fails to establish why this approach is superior to the simpler strategy of applying standard metric learning techniques (NCA, ITML, LMNN) directly to the WMD or WCD representations. The authors mention using these as baselines (e.g., "WMD + NCA"), but don't provide a clear explanation for why their proposed method is fundamentally better than these straightforward combinations of existing techniques.

The paper also lacks sufficient discussion of limitations. The authors note that S-WMD underperforms on CLASSIC due to "a poor initialization" and "squared Euclidean distance between word vectors, which may be suboptimal," but don't explore how these limitations could be addressed or whether they represent fundamental weaknesses in the approach.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper presents a technically sound method that builds on established work in metric learning and optimal transport, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty and the empirical improvements are marginal. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that S-WMD represents a meaningful advance over simpler combinations of existing techniques. The authors' claim that S-WMD "may well be the best performing distance metric for documents to date" is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to the straightforward approach of applying standard metric learning to WMD/WCD, an ablation study of the word importance weights component, and more detailed analysis of the method's limitations and failure cases. Without these elements, the paper doesn't provide strong justification for why this specific approach should be adopted over existing methods.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. While the paper is technically sound and the empirical results are promising, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty and the claimed advantages aren't rigorously demonstrated. The marginal improvements over existing approaches don't justify the additional complexity of the proposed method without stronger theoretical justification and more thorough validation. The paper would be better suited for a more applied venue where the marginal improvements would be more meaningful than for NIPS, which prioritizes significant methodological advances.
