Summary and context
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document similarity. WMD measures the optimal transport cost to move one document’s word distribution to another’s in a pre-trained word embedding space. The authors introduce two learnable components: (i) a linear transformation A on the word embedding (learning a Mahalanobis ground cost), and (ii) a word-importance vector w that reweights the document histograms before transport. They learn A and w by minimizing a Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA)-style stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss using pairwise document distances computed via optimal transport. Because exact WMD is cubic in document vocabulary size and has non-smooth dependence on the ground cost, they use the entropy-regularized (Sinkhorn) relaxation to obtain both fast approximate distances and differentiable (or subgradient) estimates, including a neat derivation that recovers dual potentials from Sinkhorn scaling vectors to compute gradients w.r.t. w. They further accelerate training via: (a) initializing A by running NCA on fast word centroid distances (S-WCD), (b) minibatching, and (c) restricting the NCA neighborhood computation to a small WCD-based candidate set.

Experiments on 8 datasets comparing to 26 baselines (unsupervised representations/distances and three supervised metric learners applied to those representations) show that S-WMD achieves the best average rank and wins on 5/8 datasets, with code released. Visualizations suggest improved class clustering and interpretable upweighted words.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on WMD (Kusner et al., ICML 2015) and Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi, NIPS 2013). It is related to supervised metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and to prior work on learning ground metrics for EMD in vision (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.). The novelty here is to bring supervised ground-metric learning and histogram reweighting to the NLP/WMD setting, exploiting low-dimensional word embeddings and entropic OT to make training practical.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The learning objective is well-motivated (stochastic LOO kNN) and the use of entropic OT to obtain differentiable approximations and dual potentials is appropriate. The gradient w.r.t. A using the (regularized) optimal plan T*λ is standard; using log u, log v as dual potentials for the entropic problem is correct in principle. The overall optimization is nonconvex but standard for metric learning.
- Efficiency: The proposed accelerations (S-WCD initialization, minibatching, neighborhood filtering) are sensible and empirically effective.
- Empirics: The comparison is broad and includes both unsupervised and supervised baselines with hyperparameter tuning. Reported gains are consistent and sizeable on several datasets, and training times are reasonable.
- Potential issues:
  - Constraints/regularization: The paper does not clearly specify constraints on w. Since d̃ = (w ∘ d)/(wᵀd) requires nonnegativity, w should be constrained (e.g., w = exp(θ) or projected to R₊). As written, unconstrained gradient steps could make entries negative. Similarly, regularization on A and w (e.g., Frobenius/L1) would help generalization and interpretability; it is not discussed.
  - Approximate probabilities: The NCA probabilities are computed only over a restricted neighbor set Na. This is a practical heuristic, but the effect on optimization and sensitivity to |Na| should be reported.
  - Test-time distance: It is unclear whether kNN uses exact WMD with learned A,w or the Sinkhorn approximation. Clarifying this is important for both accuracy and runtime.
  - Notation: p is used both as an exponent in the ground cost and as the number of unique words; some formulas (e.g., centering of α,β with “p” in α*λ = log u / λ − …) could be clarified.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful intuition, algorithmic outline, and complexity discussion. The derivations are sketched at the right level for NIPS, though some details are terse (dual potentials, constraints on w). A few typos/formatting issues appear (section header “.2”, inconsistent reference formatting, overloading of “p”), and some implementation details (choice of r in A, positivity/regularization of w, test-time distance) should be made explicit to ensure reproducibility. The release of code is a plus.

Originality
The combination is novel in the NLP setting: learning a supervised ground metric and histogram weights for WMD using entropic OT and NCA, leveraging word embeddings to remain in a low-dimensional ground space. While related ideas exist in vision for EMD, the adaptation, the w reweighting, and the training pipeline are new and useful. The use of Sinkhorn dual variables to backpropagate through histogram reweighting is a nice technical contribution.

Significance
Document distance is a core problem with many downstream uses. This work advances the state of the art for kNN classification across diverse datasets and provides a practical recipe to inject supervision into WMD with interpretable weights and reasonable compute. Beyond kNN, learning A and w could benefit retrieval, clustering, and domain adaptation. The approach is likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., class-conditional metrics, sparsity on w, semi-supervised objectives).

Questions and requests for clarification
- How is w constrained during training to ensure nonnegativity? If projected or reparameterized, please specify. Any regularization on A and/or w?
- What value of r is used for A? Is A low-rank? How sensitive are results to r?
- Do you use exact WMD or Sinkhorn distances at test time? Please report both accuracy and runtime for the chosen option.
- Sensitivity analyses: λ (regularization), |Na| for neighborhood truncation, and effect of initialization (S-WCD) on final results.
- Ablations: learning only A, only w, neither (baseline WMD), to quantify contributions; effect of squared vs unsquared ground cost.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated extension of WMD that brings supervision effectively.
- Solid technical execution using entropic OT to enable learning; dual-based gradient for w is elegant.
- Strong empirical results against many baselines; code released; interpretable learned weights.
- Practical training pipeline and runtime.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing details on constraints/regularization for w and A; potential for negative weights or overfitting.
- Reliance on heuristic neighbor truncation without sensitivity study.
- Some clarity/notation issues and minor typos.
- Evaluation limited to kNN; broader downstream tasks would strengthen impact.

Minor comments
- Unify notation for p (exponent vs vocabulary size); clarify dimensions in dual formulas.
- Fix section header “.2 Optimization” and reference formatting inconsistencies.
- Report k selection and whether BO tuned k across methods.
- Consider L1 regularization on w for sparsity and interpretability.

Overall recommendation
This is a strong and timely contribution that advances supervised document distances via optimal transport. Despite some clarifications needed, the technical idea, practicality, and empirical gains justify acceptance. I recommend accept, contingent on addressing the noted clarifications and adding small ablations/sensitivity analyses.
