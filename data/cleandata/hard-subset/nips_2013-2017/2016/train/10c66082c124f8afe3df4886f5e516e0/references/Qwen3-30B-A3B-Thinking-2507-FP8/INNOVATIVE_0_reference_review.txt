

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel approach to learning a document distance metric by incorporating supervision into the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) framework. The authors build on Kusner et al.'s unsupervised WMD, which computes document distances as optimal transport costs in word embedding space, and propose to learn a linear transformation of word embeddings along with word-specific importance weights to improve classification performance.

The paper makes a clear and compelling contribution by addressing a significant gap in the literature: prior work on WMD and document distances is entirely unsupervised, while the optimal distance metric for a classification task depends on the specific task. The authors recognize that "text documents could be classified by topic or by author, which would lead to very different measures of dissimilarity," and propose a method to learn the metric directly for the task at hand. This is a bold and novel direction that extends the WMD framework in a meaningful way.

The technical approach is well-motivated and carefully developed. The authors leverage the Sinkhorn distance approximation to make the computation tractable, which is a practical and well-chosen solution to the cubic complexity of exact WMD computation. Their loss function directly minimizes a stochastic version of leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric, which is a principled approach to metric learning. The paper includes detailed derivations of gradients and an efficient optimization procedure using batch gradient descent with a clever neighborhood approximation.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering 8 diverse document datasets and comparing against 26 baselines. The results are impressive: S-WMD achieves the lowest kNN test error on 5/8 datasets and outperforms all other methods on average. The paper demonstrates a significant improvement over unsupervised WMD (e.g., 51% relative reduction in error on BBCSPORT compared to LSI, which was previously the best unsupervised method). The visualization in Figure 1 provides compelling evidence that S-WMD produces better class-separated embeddings than WMD.

The paper's novelty is substantial and clearly articulated. While there are related works in metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and in learning ground distances for EMD (Cuturi & Avis, Wang & Guibas), the authors' approach of learning a metric directly within the word embedding space with histogram re-weighting is distinct. As they note, "Wang et al. [35] learn a metric within a generalized Euclidean EMD ground distance using the framework of ITML for image classification. They do not, however, consider re-weighting the histograms, which allows our method extra flexibility."

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., "On CLASSIC WMD outperforms S-WMD possibly because of a poor initialization and that S-WMD uses the squared Euclidean distance between word vectors, which may be suboptimal for this dataset"). The complexity analysis and training time comparisons are particularly valuable for practitioners.

The significance of this work is high. It provides a principled way to incorporate task-specific supervision into the WMD framework, which has proven to be a powerful document distance metric. The method could be widely adopted in text classification and retrieval tasks. The paper's efficiency (training times comparable to or better than baseline methods) makes it practical for real-world applications.

The only minor weaknesses are that the paper doesn't explore using more advanced word embeddings beyond word2vec, and the performance on some datasets (CLASSIC, BBCSPORT) is not as strong as on others. However, these are not significant enough to detract from the paper's overall contribution.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS values. It takes an existing successful framework (WMD) and extends it in a meaningful way that addresses a clear limitation (unsupervised nature), with strong empirical results. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of document representation and metric learning.

I recommend acceptance. The paper introduces a significant new concept that advances the state of the art in document distance metrics, and the experimental results are compelling. The method is both theoretically sound and practically useful, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.
