## Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document similarity. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of word embeddings *and* re-weight word frequencies, supervised by document labels.  A key contribution is the efficient approximation of the optimal transport problem using the Sinkhorn distance, enabling practical training. The authors demonstrate strong performance across 8 datasets and 26 baselines, showcasing the effectiveness of their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results, consistently outperforming a large number of competitive baselines across diverse datasets. The magnitude of improvement is noteworthy.
* **Novel Combination:** The approach of simultaneously learning a transformation of word embeddings *and* re-weighting word frequencies within the WMD framework is a novel and interesting idea.  This moves beyond simply learning a global metric and tailors the distance to the specific classification task.
* **Efficient Approximation:**  The use of the Sinkhorn distance is a crucial contribution, making the supervised learning of WMD computationally feasible. This addresses a key limitation of applying metric learning to WMD directly.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The background section provides sufficient context, and the algorithm description is relatively clear. The inclusion of a Github link is excellent.
* **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:**  The authors evaluate against a comprehensive set of baselines, including strong methods like NCA and ITML.  The ablation study (showing the value of initialization) is also valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the authors mention speedups, implementing S-WMD appears non-trivial, requiring careful attention to detail with the Sinkhorn approximation and gradient computations. This might limit its accessibility to researchers without significant optimization expertise.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper briefly mentions tuning hyperparameters with Bayesian optimization, but doesn't provide details on the sensitivity of the results to these parameters. A more in-depth analysis of hyperparameter importance would be beneficial.
* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** The paper leans heavily on empirical validation.  A stronger theoretical justification for the proposed method and its convergence properties would be valuable.  While the connection to metric learning is drawn, a deeper understanding of *why* this approach works well would be helpful.
* **Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the authors acknowledge some datasets where S-WMD doesn’t perform as well, the discussion is limited.  A more thorough analysis of the failure cases and potential reasons for them would strengthen the paper.




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with clear justifications for the choices made (e.g., the Sinkhorn distance). The experimental results provide strong support for the claims. It’s a complete piece of work.  However, as mentioned above, more theoretical analysis would strengthen it.  **Score: 4/5**
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, although the mathematical notation could be slightly more streamlined. The explanations are relatively clear, though some background knowledge in optimal transport and metric learning is helpful. **Score: 4/5**
* **Originality:** The combination of learning word embedding transformations and word re-weighting within the WMD framework is novel. While building on existing work in metric learning and optimal transport, it presents a new and valuable approach. **Score: 4.5/5**
* **Significance:** The results are significant, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on document classification tasks. The approach has the potential to be impactful in areas like information retrieval and text mining. The efficient approximation makes it more practical than previous attempts at supervised WMD learning. **Score: 4.5/5**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Strong empirical performance across a diverse set of datasets.
* Novel and well-motivated approach that combines strengths of WMD and supervised metric learning.
* Efficient approximation technique that makes the approach practical.
* Thorough experimental evaluation against a large number of baselines.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*  Implementation complexity might limit adoption.
* Limited theoretical analysis.
* Relatively brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and failure cases.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document similarity and metric learning. The novelty of the approach, the compelling experimental results, and the efficient approximation technique outweigh the minor weaknesses.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.



**Recommendation:** **Accept**
