

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes "Supervised Word Mover's Distance" (S-WMD), which extends the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by incorporating supervision through metric learning. The authors claim their method learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and word-specific importance weights to improve document distance metrics for classification tasks. The approach leverages the Sinkhorn distance approximation to make the computational cost feasible.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a solid mathematical foundation. The authors provide a thorough background on WMD, metric learning, and optimal transport, demonstrating good understanding of the related literature. The methodological approach is technically sound, and the computational efficiency claims are supported by complexity analysis. The experimental results on 8 datasets are comprehensive, and the paper shows S-WMD outperforms 26 baselines on average.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness is the lack of sufficient evidence to support the paper's strong claims. While the paper reports that S-WMD achieves the lowest error on 5/8 datasets and outperforms all 26 baselines on average, it fails to provide statistical significance testing for these results. The paper mentions standard errors for some datasets but doesn't explicitly state whether the improvements are statistically significant, which is essential for making such strong claims.

The paper also lacks adequate comparison against more relevant baselines. The authors compare against WMD, WCD, and various document representations, but they don't properly compare against supervised metric learning applied directly to the word embeddings or other standard document representations (e.g., TF-IDF with NCA/ITML/LMNN). The paper states that "supervision seems to help all methods on average," but doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support this claim beyond the limited datasets they tested.

The paper makes a strong claim that "S-WMD may well be the best performing distance metric for documents to date," but this claim is not sufficiently justified by the limited evaluation scope. The results on 8 datasets are not enough to make such a broad claim, especially when the improvements on some datasets (like CLASSIC) are not substantial and sometimes worse than unsupervised WMD.

The paper also lacks sufficient analysis of the learned weights. While Figure 2 shows top-weighted words on 20NEWS, there's no deeper analysis explaining why these words are weighted more highly or how this aligns with the classification task. Without such analysis, it's difficult to assess whether the learned weights truly capture meaningful distinctions for the classification task.

The paper claims that S-WMD is "surprisingly fast" compared to other metric learning approaches, but the time comparisons are limited to training time and don't address the full pipeline (e.g., how long it takes to compute document distances for inference). The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that S-WMD is more efficient than standard supervised metric learning approaches applied to document representations.

**Originality:**
While the paper builds on WMD and metric learning, the specific combination of supervised metric learning with WMD is not sufficiently novel. The authors reference Wang et al. [35] which "learn a metric within a generalized Euclidean EMD ground distance," but don't adequately explain how their approach differs from this work. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish their method from existing supervised metric learning approaches applied to document representations.

**Significance:**
The paper makes a strong claim about being "the best performing distance metric for documents to date," but the evidence doesn't support this level of significance. The improvements over baselines appear marginal in many cases (e.g., only 10-15% better on some datasets), and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why these marginal improvements are practically significant. The paper also fails to address whether the computational cost of training S-WMD is justified by the modest improvements in classification performance.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents an interesting extension of WMD with supervised learning, but the evidence provided does not sufficiently support the strong claims made in the paper. The experimental validation is limited in scope, lacks statistical significance testing, and fails to provide adequate comparison against the most relevant baselines. The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of the learned weights, stronger statistical validation, and a clearer justification of why the proposed method is better than standard supervised metric learning approaches applied to document representations.

I recommend rejection. While the method is technically sound and the paper is well-written, the lack of sufficient evidence to support the paper's claims and the limited scope of the evaluation do not meet the high standards required for NIPS publication. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their experimental validation and provide more convincing evidence for their claims to warrant acceptance.
