

# Peer Review: Supervised Word Mover's Distance

## Summary

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to improve document distances for classification tasks. WMD, introduced by Kusner et al. [19] at NIPS 2015, computes document distances as the optimal transport cost of moving words between documents in the word embedding space. The authors extend this by learning a linear transformation of the word embeddings and word-specific importance weights to emphasize words that are more useful for distinguishing document classes.

The key contributions include: (1) a method to learn a supervised metric for WMD by directly minimizing a stochastic version of leave-one-out classification error; (2) an efficient computational approach using the Sinkhorn distance approximation to handle the cubic complexity of WMD; and (3) a probabilistic filtering technique to further reduce computation time. The authors demonstrate that S-WMD outperforms 26 baseline methods across 8 different document datasets, achieving the lowest kNN test error on 5/8 datasets and the best average rank across all datasets.

The paper builds upon the WMD framework while incorporating insights from metric learning literature, particularly Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [15]. It addresses a significant gap in the literature by moving from unsupervised to supervised document distance learning, which is particularly relevant for applications like document classification, web search ranking, and news categorization.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Completeness**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method, with well-derived mathematical formulations for the loss function and gradients. The authors carefully address the computational challenges of WMD by leveraging the Sinkhorn distance approximation [6], making the method practical for real-world applications. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with results on 8 diverse datasets and 26 baselines.

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The results are impressive, with S-WMD achieving the lowest error rate on 5 out of 8 datasets and the best average rank across all datasets. The authors provide detailed analysis of the results, including visualization of document embeddings using t-SNE (Figure 1) and analysis of the learned word importance weights (Figure 2). The comparison between S-WMD and its initialization (S-WMD init.) shows that the supervised training provides significant improvements.

3. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper makes a compelling case for the practicality of the method with its complexity analysis (O(TBN[p² + d²(p + r)])) and training time results (Table 3). The authors demonstrate that S-WMD training is surprisingly fast, comparable or even faster than training linear metric learning on baseline methods.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to proposed solution to experimental validation. The background section provides sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with WMD and metric learning, and the mathematical derivations are well-explained.

5. **Relevant to NIPS Community**: The paper addresses a fundamental problem in natural language processing that has received significant attention at NIPS (e.g., WMD was presented at NIPS 2015), and the approach combines advances in optimal transport, metric learning, and word embeddings in a meaningful way.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases**: The paper notes that S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON datasets, but the analysis is somewhat superficial. The authors suggest possible explanations (e.g., "long document lengths" for BBCSPORT), but a deeper analysis of *why* S-WMD fails on these specific datasets would strengthen the paper. For instance, it would be valuable to see if certain document characteristics (e.g., document length, vocabulary diversity, class imbalance) correlate with the performance gap.

2. **Lack of Comparison with Alternative Embeddings**: The paper exclusively uses word2vec embeddings. It would be beneficial to explore how the method performs with other word embedding techniques (e.g., GloVe, ELMo, or BERT-based embeddings) to demonstrate the generality of the approach. The authors mention that "many word embeddings could be used [5, 21?]" but don't provide evidence to support this claim.

3. **Insufficient Discussion of Initialization Impact**: While the paper describes the initialization procedure (S-WCD), it doesn't provide a thorough comparison between the initialized model and the fully trained model. The statement that "S-WMD right after initialization (S-WMD init.) performs quite well" is interesting, but the paper doesn't clearly articulate the marginal gains from full training versus the initialization.

4. **Scalability to Extremely Large Datasets**: The paper mentions the computational efficiency of the method, but it doesn't address how it would scale to extremely large datasets with millions of documents or very large vocabulary sizes. The complexity analysis suggests the method is scalable, but more concrete evidence would be valuable.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a significant advancement in supervised document distance learning. It builds upon the foundational WMD work with a well-motivated and technically sound extension that demonstrates clear empirical advantages. The method is both theoretically grounded and practically useful, with a compelling demonstration of its effectiveness across multiple datasets.

The strengths of the paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The authors have made a clear contribution to the field by extending WMD with supervision, and the results are convincing. While there are some areas for improvement (particularly regarding the failure cases and embedding comparisons), these do not undermine the overall quality of the work.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and makes a significant contribution to the field of document similarity and metric learning. The results are impressive, and the method has clear potential for practical applications in text classification tasks. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and thoroughly evaluated, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a deeper analysis of the datasets where S-WMD underperforms (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON), including specific characteristics of these datasets that might explain the performance gap.

2. Include experiments comparing S-WMD with different word embedding techniques to demonstrate the method's generality.

3. Provide a more detailed comparison between the initialized model (S-WMD init.) and the fully trained model, including how much improvement is achieved through training versus initialization.

4. Discuss how the method would scale to extremely large datasets (e.g., billions of documents) and potential challenges in such scenarios.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how S-WMD might be integrated with more recent deep learning approaches for document representation (e.g., BERT-based models), to position the work in the context of current trends.

## Final Thoughts

This is a strong paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of document distance learning. The extension of WMD with supervision is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated across multiple datasets. The authors have addressed the computational challenges effectively, making their method practical for real-world applications. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and should be accepted.
