Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of WMD that learns a task-specific ground metric over word embeddings and word-importance weights for documents. Concretely, the authors learn (i) a linear transform A over word embeddings, equivalent to a PSD Mahalanobis metric with optional low rank, and (ii) a nonnegative word-weight vector w that reweights BOW histograms before optimal transport. Supervision is injected via a Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA)–style loss that approximates leave-one-out k-NN error, using exp(−D) with D the OT-based document distance. To make learning tractable, they replace the exact WMD by its entropically-regularized Sinkhorn relaxation and use (a) Danskin’s theorem to obtain gradients w.r.t. A using the optimal transport plan and (b) dual potentials derived from the Sinkhorn scaling vectors for gradients w.r.t. w. They further accelerate training with (i) NCA pretraining on Word Centroid Distance (WCD) for initialization, (ii) mini-batching, and (iii) neighborhood truncation using WCD to limit NCA normalizers. Across 8 datasets and 26 baselines (unsupervised representations and supervised metric learning on those representations), S-WMD achieves the best average rank and state-of-the-art k-NN error on 5/8 datasets.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on WMD (Kusner et al., 2015) and Sinkhorn regularization for OT (Cuturi, 2013).
- Uses NCA’s stochastic neighbor loss (Goldberger et al., 2005) to learn a metric; comparable supervised ground metric learning for EMD exists in vision (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.) but not for text nor with reweighted histograms.
- Complements representation learning approaches (LSI/LDA/mSDA) by keeping documents in sparse BOW and learning in low-dimensional embedding space.

Strengths
- Technical soundness: Using entropic OT yields unique solutions; by Danskin’s theorem the gradient w.r.t. A is Σ T* ∂c/∂A, avoiding backprop through the solver. The dual-potential-based gradient for w is also principled for entropic OT.
- Efficient and practical: The Sinkhorn relaxation reduces per-pair complexity to O(p^2), and further heuristics (WCD neighborhood truncation, batching, pretraining) bring training to seconds per iteration.
- Clear algorithmic contribution: Jointly learning A and w with an NCA loss is a coherent objective; the histogram reweighting adapts the transport marginals to the task, going beyond prior ground-metric-only learning.
- Empirical breadth: Multiple datasets and a large set of baselines, including supervised metric learning on strong representations. S-WMD wins on most datasets and has best average rank.
- Reproducibility: Code is provided; most design choices are described; hyperparameters for baselines are tuned via Bayesian optimization.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Clarify dependence of Sinkhorn on A: In Section 3.1, K is defined as exp(−λ‖xi−xj‖2) in the background. For S-WMD, K must be exp(−λ‖A(xi−xj)‖2), recomputed as A changes. The text does not explicitly state this in the supervised setting; using a fixed K would be incorrect. Please clarify that K (and thus T*, u, v, α, β) are recomputed for the current A and w at each gradient step.
- Constraints and regularization for w: d̃ = (w◦d)/(w⊤d) requires w ≥ 0 to remain a valid histogram; the paper does not state constraints or projections. Without positivity and some regularization (e.g., ℓ2, entropy, or IDF prior), w could overfit by upweighting rare words. Please specify the parameterization (e.g., w = exp(θ)) and any regularization.
- Stability and scaling details: Entropic OT often needs log-domain stabilization and ε-additions; the paper omits numerical details. Also, the complexity expression includes O(d^2(p+r))—it would help to derive why d^2 appears (accumulating outer products can be O(r d p) if done carefully). For long documents, p^2 can still be large; more discussion on max p and practical runtimes per dataset is warranted.
- Evaluation scope: Results are only with k-NN. While appropriate for distance learning, it would strengthen the case to show downstream utility (e.g., retrieval MAP) or plug S-WMD distances into a simple kernel classifier. Comparisons to supervised text classifiers (fastText/doc2vec/CNN) are not included; a brief discussion of where S-WMD fits in this landscape would help.
- Notational/clarity issues: Several typos (section “.2 Optimization”), inconsistent use of p/q/n for vocabulary vs. document support, and some imprecise formulas (e.g., α, β centering terms, dimensions of 1). Related work citations are sometimes incomplete.

Quality
Overall technically solid, with a sound objective and reasonable approximations. The main theoretical step (gradients via optimal plans/potentials) is correct under entropic regularization. The empirical study is careful, with many baselines and sensible ablations (initialization vs. full S-WMD). Some implementation details and constraints (w ≥ 0, recomputing K) need to be clarified.

Clarity
Generally readable with a logical flow. The paper would benefit from tightening notation, explicitly stating all constraints, and detailing numerical stabilization and exact per-iteration computations. A figure summarizing the training loop would help.

Originality
The combination of NCA-style supervision with OT over word embeddings, jointly learning ground metric and marginal reweighting, is novel for NLP. Prior supervised EMD work in vision is acknowledged and extended meaningfully here.

Significance
Likely to be useful for practitioners needing task-specific distances for short/medium-length texts, k-NN classification, and retrieval. Demonstrates consistent gains over strong baselines. The approach may spur further work on supervised OT for NLP.

Questions for the authors
- Do you re-compute K = exp(−λ‖A(xi−xj)‖2) at each step? If so, how do you implement this efficiently across many pairs?
- How do you enforce w ≥ 0? Any regularization on w (e.g., entropy or IDF prior) to prevent overfitting to rare words?
- Sensitivity to λ, neighborhood size N, and batch size B? Any guidance or plots?
- Do you use log-domain Sinkhorn stabilization? What stopping criteria and typical iteration counts?
- How does performance change with r (rank of A), and does low-rank help speed without hurting accuracy?

Pros
- Principled objective with efficient optimization via Sinkhorn
- Joint learning of A and w; strong empirical gains
- Good initialization strategy and practical accelerations
- Code released

Cons
- Missing clarity on dependence of Sinkhorn kernel on A and on constraints for w
- Limited discussion of numerical stability and hyperparameter sensitivity
- Evaluation limited to k-NN; broader applicability not demonstrated

Recommendation
Lean accept. Solid, well-motivated contribution that advances supervised metric learning for document distances with clear empirical benefits. Addressing the clarity/implementation concerns would strengthen the final version.
