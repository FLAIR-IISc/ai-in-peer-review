This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a document distance metric by combining the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) with supervised learning. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weight word frequencies to better reflect semantic similarity based on document labels. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and a probabilistic filtering technique. The method is evaluated on 8 datasets, demonstrating superior performance compared to 26 baselines.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon recent advances in document distance metrics (WMD) and metric learning. The problem addressed is relevant – improving document similarity for tasks like information retrieval and classification is a long-standing challenge. The authors correctly identify the limitations of unsupervised methods and propose a principled approach to incorporate supervision. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to accelerate computation is a clever and crucial contribution, making the method practical. The extensive experimental evaluation, including comparison to a large number of baselines, strengthens the claims. The authors also provide a clear explanation of the gradients and optimization procedure. The inclusion of ablation studies (S-WMD init.) is also valuable, demonstrating the benefit of the full training process.

However, several weaknesses should be addressed. While the paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation, the improvement over the strong baseline of WMD isn’t always substantial. More detailed analysis of *where* S-WMD excels and *why* would be beneficial. For example, it would be helpful to understand which types of datasets or documents benefit most from the learned transformations. The authors mention that “supervision seems to help all methods on average”, but the magnitude of this help varies considerably. 

The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive. Specifically, the authors mention ITML and LMNN, but don't clearly position S-WMD relative to these methods in terms of strengths and weaknesses. For instance, how does learning a transformation in the word embedding space compare to learning a transformation directly in the document space as done in ITML/LMNN?  The connection to recent work on optimal transport for domain adaptation is also missing; specifically, the potential to view this as a domain adaptation problem with labeled documents as the source and unlabeled documents as the target. Furthermore, citing [1, 7] for gradient non-existence seems incorrect as the authors then proceed to use subgradient descent, suggesting existence/approximability.

The paper's clarity could be improved in a few areas. The notation is dense, and some equations (especially those involving gradients) could benefit from more detailed explanations. While the authors provide a link to their code, making the code more readily executable (e.g., with clear instructions and dependencies) would enhance reproducibility. The choice of hyperparameters (e.g., λ for the Sinkhorn distance, learning rates) is not fully justified, and a more thorough ablation study on these parameters would be valuable.  The explanation of the batch gradient descent with nearest neighbor selection feels rushed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to incorporating supervision into the WMD framework.
*   Effective use of the Sinkhorn distance to address computational challenges.
*   Strong experimental results on a diverse set of datasets, outperforming a large number of baselines.
*   Clear explanation of the method and optimization procedure.
*   Open-source code availability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The improvement over WMD is not always substantial, and further analysis is needed to understand *when* S-WMD excels.
*   The related work section could be more comprehensive and better contextualize S-WMD within the broader field of metric learning and optimal transport.
*   Clarity of some explanations and equations could be improved.
*   Hyperparameter selection lacks thorough justification.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of document distance metrics. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and empirically supported. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding analysis of the benefits and limitations of the approach and a more thorough discussion of related work. Specifically, I would like the authors to expand their discussion on where their method excels and why, and better contextualize it with existing metric learning techniques.
