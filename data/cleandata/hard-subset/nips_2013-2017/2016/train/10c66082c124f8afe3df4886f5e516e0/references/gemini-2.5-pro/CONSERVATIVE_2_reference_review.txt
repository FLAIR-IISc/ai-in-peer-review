### Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which calculates the optimal transport cost between documents in a pre-trained word embedding space. The authors introduce supervision in two ways: 1) by learning a linear transformation of the word embedding space to better suit the classification task, and 2) by learning a vector of importance weights for each word in the vocabulary.

To make this learning problem tractable, the authors propose several well-motivated approximations. They formulate the objective as minimizing a stochastic version of the k-NN leave-one-out error, similar to Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). To address the prohibitive computational cost and non-differentiability of the WMD, they replace the exact optimal transport problem with its entropy-regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance), which is faster to compute and yields a smooth, differentiable objective. The training is further accelerated through a clever initialization based on a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batch-based gradient descent that considers only a local neighborhood of documents. The method is evaluated on eight document classification datasets, showing superior performance over a comprehensive set of 26 baseline methods, including unsupervised WMD and various document representations combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this paper is its extensive and convincing empirical evaluation. The authors compare S-WMD against a wide array of established baselines, from classic bag-of-words models (BOW, TF-IDF) and topic models (LSI, LDA) to more recent approaches like mSDA and the original WMD. The application of standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN) to these baselines provides a robust point of comparison. S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results on 5 out of 8 datasets and demonstrates a very strong average rank, which provides compelling evidence for its effectiveness.

2.  **Sound Methodological Approach to a Difficult Problem:** The paper addresses the non-trivial challenge of incorporating supervision into the WMD framework. The decision to use the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance is a key insight, effectively converting a computationally expensive and non-differentiable linear program into a tractable optimization problem. This demonstrates a good understanding of the underlying mathematical machinery.

3.  **Well-Engineered for Practicality:** The authors have clearly invested effort in making their approach computationally feasible. The combination of the Sinkhorn relaxation, the WCD-based initialization, and the nearest-neighbor-filtered batch gradient descent makes the overall algorithm efficient enough for practical use, as demonstrated by the reported training times.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of Components:** While the combination is effective, the core components of the proposed method are largely drawn from existing work. The WMD is a prior method, the NCA-style loss is well-established in metric learning, and the use of Sinkhorn distances to approximate optimal transport is also known. The idea of learning a ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance has been explored previously in other domains. The main contribution lies in the specific combination and application to document distance, which, while valuable, is more of an incremental engineering advance than a fundamental one.

2.  **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper proposes learning both a linear transformation `A` and a word importance vector `w`. However, the individual contribution of each component is not analyzed. An ablation study that compares the performance of learning only `A`, only `w`, and both together would be necessary to understand the source of the performance gains. As it stands, it is unclear how much of the improvement is due to the novel word re-weighting scheme versus the more standard metric learning on the embedding space.

3.  **Incomplete Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The claim that S-WMD "may well be the best performing distance metric for documents to date" is not fully supported by the experiments. While the baseline comparison is broad, it notably omits a class of powerful and widely established methods for document classification: deep neural networks such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) or Recurrent Neural Networks (LSTMs) applied directly to text. These models have been shown to be highly effective and would constitute a crucial benchmark for any method claiming state-of-the-art performance in this area.

4.  **Absence of Deeper Analysis:** The paper is almost entirely empirical. There is little discussion of the method's limitations or sensitivity. For instance, the performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the initial pre-trained word embeddings, but this dependence is not explored. Furthermore, the choice of the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn distance is critical for the trade-off between approximation accuracy and computational speed, yet its impact is not analyzed. The non-convexity of the objective function is mentioned, but its implications on the stability and quality of the solution are not discussed beyond the proposed initialization scheme.

**Conclusion**

This paper presents a well-executed piece of work that successfully introduces supervision into the Word Mover's Distance framework. The proposed S-WMD method is technically sound, and its effectiveness is demonstrated through rigorous experiments against a large number of relevant baselines. The engineering choices made to ensure computational tractability are commendable.

However, the work's significance is somewhat tempered by its incremental novelty and a few key omissions in the analysis and evaluation. The lack of an ablation study makes it difficult to attribute the performance gains to specific components of the model, and the absence of comparisons to established deep learning models for text classification makes the claim of state-of-the-art performance premature. While the empirical results are strong, the paper would be more convincing with a deeper investigation into the method's properties and a more complete set of competitive baselines.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that refines an existing, powerful technique. It is likely to be useful for practitioners seeking a high-performing, k-NN-based classifier for text. My recommendation is borderline, but leaning towards acceptance due to the strength of the empirical results.
