This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance. The approach builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which leverages word embeddings to compute the optimal transport cost between documents. The key idea of S-WMD is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a set of word-importance weights to tailor the WMD to a specific classification task. The authors formulate this as a metric learning problem, minimizing a stochastic approximation of the leave-one-out kNN error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A significant technical challenge is the computational cost and non-differentiability of the WMD. The authors overcome this by using the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance, which provides a fast, differentiable approximation to the optimal transport problem. This allows for efficient gradient-based optimization. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation across eight text classification datasets, showing that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results compared to an extensive set of 26 baselines, including unsupervised WMD and various document representations combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a well-motivated and novel extension of the powerful WMD framework to the supervised setting. While metric learning is a mature field, its application to the ground metric of an optimal transport-based document distance is not commonplace. The dual learning of a transformation matrix `A` for the embedding space and a word importance vector `w` is a powerful combination that allows the model to capture both semantic relationships between words and the discriminative power of individual words for a given task. The strong empirical results suggest this is a significant contribution that advances the state-of-the-art in text classification.

2.  **Technical Contribution:** The main technical contribution is making the proposed learning problem tractable. Directly optimizing a loss over the WMD would be computationally prohibitive and involve non-differentiable sub-problems. The use of the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance is a clever and effective solution. It not only speeds up the computation of the distance itself (from O(q³ log q) to O(q²)) but also makes the objective differentiable, enabling efficient end-to-end training with gradient descent. The derivation of the gradients for both `A` and `w` using the dual variables of the relaxed transport problem is elegant and crucial for the method's success.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and rigorous. The authors compare S-WMD against a wide array of strong baselines, including classic methods (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LDA), deep learning approaches (mSDA), and the original WMD. Crucially, they also apply standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML) to the vector-based representations, providing a fair and challenging comparison. S-WMD's superior performance on 5 out of 8 datasets, and its strong average rank across all datasets, provides compelling evidence of its effectiveness. The inclusion of visualizations (t-SNE plots and word clouds of importance weights) adds valuable qualitative insight into what the model learns.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the necessary background on word2vec, WMD, Sinkhorn distances, and NCA before introducing their own method. The motivation for each component of S-WMD is clear, and the optimization strategy is well-described. This clarity makes the paper's contributions accessible and easy to appreciate.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Ablation Study:** The model learns two components simultaneously: the linear transformation `A` and the word importance weights `w`. While the combination is shown to be effective, the paper does not provide an ablation study to disentangle their individual contributions. It would be highly beneficial to see the performance of a model that learns only `A` (with `w` fixed to ones) and a model that learns only `w` (with `A` fixed to identity). This would provide a clearer understanding of which component is more critical and whether their combination is synergistic.

2.  **Impact of Initialization:** The method uses a clever initialization strategy by first training NCA on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD), which is a lower bound on WMD. The results for "S-WMD init." in Table 2 show that this initialization step is responsible for a very significant portion of the performance gain. While the final S-WMD model consistently improves upon this, it would strengthen the paper to discuss the sensitivity to this initialization. What is the performance if `A` is initialized to identity? This would help quantify the value added by the more complex, Sinkhorn-based optimization relative to the simpler, but powerful, initialization step.

3.  **Scalability and Limitations:** The paper effectively addresses the cubic complexity of the original WMD, but the O(q²) complexity of the Sinkhorn distance per pair can still be a bottleneck for documents with many unique words (`q`). The authors note that S-WMD is outperformed on BBCSPORT and OHSUMED, which may have longer documents. A more explicit discussion of the scalability limitations with respect to document length and vocabulary size would be welcome. How does the training time (and performance) degrade as `q` increases? This would provide practitioners with a clearer picture of when S-WMD is most applicable.

4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model introduces several hyperparameters, notably the regularization parameter `λ` for the Sinkhorn distance, which controls the trade-off between approximation accuracy and optimization smoothness/speed. The paper states `λ=10` is used, but a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the final results to this choice would be valuable. Does performance vary significantly for different values of `λ`?

### Summary of Arguments

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Proposes a novel, well-motivated, and powerful method for supervised document distance learning.
    *   Elegant technical solution to make a difficult optimization problem tractable using Sinkhorn distances.
    *   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance through an extensive and rigorous empirical evaluation.
    *   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

*   **Con-acceptance (Minor):**
    *   Lacks an ablation study to disentangle the contributions of the learned matrix `A` and weights `w`.
    *   The strong performance of the initialization method (S-WCD) slightly obscures the additional benefit of the full S-WMD optimization.
    *   Limited discussion on scalability limitations and hyperparameter sensitivity.

### Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the fields of metric learning and natural language processing. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and empirically powerful. The weaknesses are relatively minor and mostly relate to additional experiments that would further strengthen the paper, rather than fundamental flaws in the approach. I strongly recommend acceptance.
