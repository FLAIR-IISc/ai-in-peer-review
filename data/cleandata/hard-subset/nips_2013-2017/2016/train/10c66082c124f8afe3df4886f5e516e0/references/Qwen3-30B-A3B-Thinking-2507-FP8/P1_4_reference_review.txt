

# Peer Review: "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

## Summary
This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to learn a document distance metric optimized for classification tasks. Building upon Kusner et al.'s WMD [19], which computes document distances as optimal transport costs between word embeddings, the authors propose learning both a linear transformation of word embeddings (A) and a word importance vector (w) to re-weight document histograms. The method minimizes the kNN leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric, using the Sinkhorn distance approximation to make computation feasible. The approach is evaluated across 8 datasets against 26 baselines, demonstrating superior performance on 5 of the 8 datasets and overall best average performance.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-motivated contributions. The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation, including clear derivations of the gradient computations for both A and w, and properly justify their use of the Sinkhorn distance approximation to reduce computational complexity from O(q³ log q) to O(q²). The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, featuring 8 diverse document classification datasets (news categorization, sentiment analysis, etc.) and 26 baselines, including both unsupervised and supervised methods. The ablation studies (comparing S-WMD to its initialization S-WMD init.) provide valuable insights into the method's behavior. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as S-WMD's underperformance on the CLASSIC dataset, attributing it to "poor initialization" and the choice of squared Euclidean distance. The empirical results are convincing, showing significant improvements over unsupervised WMD (e.g., 51% relative error reduction on OHSUMED compared to LSI, which was previously the best unsupervised method).

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The background section effectively introduces WMD and metric learning concepts for readers unfamiliar with these topics. The mathematical derivations are presented logically, though some sections (particularly the gradient derivations) may be dense for readers without strong optimization backgrounds. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, with sufficient detail for replication. The visualization in Figure 1 effectively demonstrates how S-WMD improves class clustering compared to WMD across all datasets. Figure 2 provides useful insights into the learned word importance weights. A minor improvement could be adding a more intuitive explanation for the normalization of the re-weighted document histograms in equation (4).

## Originality
The core contribution of incorporating supervision into WMD is novel. While metric learning for text has been explored (e.g., NCA [15], LMNN [36], ITML [10]), this paper is the first to apply these techniques directly to the WMD framework. The authors properly distinguish their work from related approaches, particularly noting that Wang et al. [35] learn a metric within EMD but do not consider histogram re-weighting. The paper builds appropriately on the foundational WMD work [19], addressing a key limitation (unsupervised nature) that was explicitly acknowledged in the original paper. The combination of linear metric learning with histogram re-weighting represents a meaningful extension to the WMD framework.

## Significance
S-WMD makes a substantial contribution to the field of document distance metrics. The method demonstrates clear improvements over the state-of-the-art, outperforming all 26 baselines on average across 8 datasets. The approach is efficient (training time comparable to other metric learning methods) and practical for real-world applications. The work addresses a significant problem in text classification—improving document distances for classification tasks—and does so in a way that leverages the strengths of existing unsupervised word embeddings. The paper's practical impact is evident, with clear applications in information retrieval, text classification, and recommendation systems. The method also opens promising research directions for further improving the WMD framework with supervision.

## Strengths
1. Solves a clear limitation of WMD (unsupervised nature) with a well-motivated approach
2. Uses the Sinkhorn distance for efficient computation, making the method practical
3. Comprehensive experimental evaluation across 8 datasets and 26 baselines
4. Clear theoretical foundation with well-derived gradients
5. Efficient optimization with batch gradient descent and neighborhood filtering
6. Good ablation study showing the value of training (S-WMD vs S-WMD init.)

## Weaknesses
1. The explanation for S-WMD's underperformance on the CLASSIC dataset is somewhat superficial (attributed to "poor initialization" and squared Euclidean distance without deeper analysis)
2. The choice of squared Euclidean distance for word vectors lacks justification (Manhattan or other metrics might be more appropriate for WMD)
3. Limited analysis of the interpretability of the learned word importance weights beyond Figure 2
4. Does not compare against supervised approaches using more modern embeddings (e.g., BERT)

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a significant contribution to the field of document distance metrics. The method is novel, well-evaluated, and has clear practical significance. The minor weaknesses noted are addressable in a camera-ready version and do not detract from the paper's overall quality.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- The paper solves a clear, important problem in text classification
- The method outperforms all 26 baselines on average across 8 datasets
- The experimental design is comprehensive and rigorous
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized
- The method is efficient and practical for real-world applications

## Arguments against Acceptance
- The explanation for the CLASSIC dataset underperformance is not sufficiently detailed
- The choice of squared Euclidean distance for word vectors lacks justification
- The paper could provide more analysis of the learned word importance weights

In conclusion, "Supervised Word Mover's Distance" represents a significant advancement in document distance metrics, building effectively upon prior work while introducing novel supervised learning techniques. The paper is a strong contribution to the field with clear practical implications, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
