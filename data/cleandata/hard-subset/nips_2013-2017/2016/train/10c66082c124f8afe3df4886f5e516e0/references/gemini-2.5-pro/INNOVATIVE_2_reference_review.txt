This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific distance metric for documents. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which defines the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other within a pre-trained word embedding space. While WMD is entirely unsupervised, S-WMD introduces supervision by learning two key components: 1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, and 2) a vector of word-importance weights `w` that re-scales the document's bag-of-words representation. The learning objective, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), is to minimize a differentiable proxy for the leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor classification error.

The central technical challenge is the prohibitive computational cost of differentiating through the WMD optimization problem. The authors present a clever and effective solution by replacing the exact WMD (an instance of the Earth Mover's Distance) with its entropy-regularized counterpart, the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation makes the objective function strictly convex and differentiable, enabling an efficient matrix-scaling algorithm for both the distance computation and the gradient calculation. The authors further improve practical performance with a smart initialization scheme based on a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and an efficient batch training procedure.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The paper introduces a genuinely new and powerful idea. The standard paradigm for supervised document classification involves learning a fixed-dimensional document representation and then applying a metric. This paper breaks from that mold by learning a transformation of the underlying *word* space, which then induces a document distance via optimal transport. This is a much more flexible and sophisticated approach that respects the underlying structure of the documents. The combination of ideas from word embeddings, optimal transport, and metric learning is both novel and compelling.

2.  **Significant Technical Contribution:** The primary contribution is not just the high-level idea but making it computationally tractable. Identifying the Sinkhorn distance as the key to unlocking an efficient, gradient-based learning algorithm for this problem is a significant insight. The derivation of the gradients for both the linear transformation `A` and the word weights `w` is non-trivial and provides a practical recipe for optimizing what at first glance appears to be an intractable objective.

3.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The method is thoroughly evaluated on eight document classification datasets, and the results are outstanding. S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming a comprehensive suite of 26 baselines, including strong unsupervised methods like WMD and various document representations (TF-IDF, LSI, mSDA) paired with leading metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The fact that it consistently improves upon the already powerful unsupervised WMD demonstrates the effectiveness of the supervised learning framework.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Analysis of Learned Components:** While the paper demonstrates *that* the model works very well, it provides little insight into *why*. The learned word weights `w` are visualized as a word cloud for one dataset, which is illustrative but superficial. There is no analysis of the learned linear transformation `A`. It would have been more insightful to explore how `A` warps the semantic space of words to suit a particular task—for instance, by showing which word pairs become closer or further apart after the transformation.

2.  **Reliance on a Strong Initialization:** The paper commendably includes results for the model after just the initialization step ("S-WMD init."), which is based on the much simpler Supervised Word Centroid Distance. This baseline is already very strong. While the full S-WMD model consistently improves upon it, the delta is not always dramatic. This might suggest that a large portion of the performance gain comes from the simpler supervised centroid model, with the full optimal transport-based fine-tuning providing a more marginal, albeit important, improvement.

3.  **Approximation Trade-offs:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance is an approximation to the true WMD. The paper mentions that the approximation becomes more accurate as the regularization parameter `λ` grows, but it is fixed at `λ=10` as a trade-off between accuracy and convergence speed. A deeper discussion or empirical analysis of this trade-off would have strengthened the paper by clarifying the potential impact of this approximation on the final learned metric.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a bold, novel, and highly effective approach to learning a document distance metric.
*   The technical solution to the problem of differentiating through an optimal transport problem is elegant and impactful.
*   The method establishes a new state of the art in kNN document classification across a wide range of benchmarks.
*   The core idea of learning a metric in the component space (words) rather than the object space (documents) is likely to be influential and inspire future work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis of what the model learns is shallow, leaving the reader with a limited understanding of the learned transformations.
*   The marginal gain over the strong initialization baseline could be interpreted as diminishing returns for the full, more complex model.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The weaknesses identified are minor compared to the strengths. This work makes a significant conceptual and empirical contribution to the field. The core idea is innovative and pushes the boundary of how we think about document similarity. While a deeper analysis of the learned model would be welcome, the novelty of the framework and the strength of the results make this a standout paper. It is exactly the kind of creative and impactful research that NIPS should showcase.
