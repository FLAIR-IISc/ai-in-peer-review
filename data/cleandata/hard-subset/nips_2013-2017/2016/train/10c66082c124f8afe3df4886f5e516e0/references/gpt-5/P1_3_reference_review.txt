Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised metric-learning framework built on the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). Instead of learning document representations in the high-dimensional BOW space, S-WMD learns a linear transformation A of the low-dimensional word embeddings and a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The supervised objective is the NCA-style stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss, with pairwise document distances computed via optimal transport over the transformed embeddings and reweighted histograms. To make optimization tractable, the authors use the Sinkhorn (entropy-regularized) transport to approximate both the transport plan T and dual potentials (α, β), yielding efficient O(q^2) per-pair computations (q = unique words per document). They initialize A using NCA on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD), and then perform batch updates with a WCD-based neighbor filter. Across 8 datasets and 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised), S-WMD achieves the best average rank and best accuracy on 5/8 datasets. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on WMD (Kusner et al., 2015/2016) and entropy-regularized OT (Cuturi, 2013).
- Uses NCA (Goldberger et al., 2004) as the supervised objective, but with distances defined by OT over words.
- Related to supervised EMD ground-metric learning in vision (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.), but differs by operating in word-embedding space and jointly learning word weights w.
- Compared to classic text representations (BOW/TF-IDF/LSI/LDA/mSDA), S-WMD learns in a low-dimensional shared embedding space, avoiding quadratic scaling in vocabulary size.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: leverage powerful unsupervised word embeddings, and inject supervision by adapting the ground metric and word importances.
- Technical soundness: Using Sinkhorn for efficiency and dual potentials for gradients is principled; envelope theorem justifies the gradient form with unique solutions.
- Practicality: Efficient training via batch sampling, neighbor filtering, and a strong initialization (S-WCD). Reported training times are competitive.
- Strong empirical results across diverse datasets; code is provided for reproducibility.
- Insightful qualitative analysis (t-SNE with S-WMD distances; top words by learned w).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Notation/clarity issues:
  - The symbol p is overloaded (Lp norm exponent vs. number of words/vocabulary size in dual expressions). This is confusing.
  - The derivation of α*, β* from Sinkhorn u, v subtracts a mean term with “p-dimensional all-ones”; dimensionality should be stated precisely; rationale for centering should be explained.
  - Section numbering glitch (”.2 Optimization”) and some incomplete references (e.g., [21?]) and inconsistent symbols (M vs N for neighbor set size).
- Theoretical details:
  - The loss uses the unregularized DA,w, but gradients are computed with the Sinkhorn-regularized solution. Clarify whether training and evaluation consistently use the same (regularized) distance, and discuss the approximation error; a brief justification (e.g., envelope theorem + λ→∞) would help.
  - Complexity accounting seems off: computing ∑i,j Tij(xi−xj)(xi−xj)T is O(q^2 d^2), not O(d^2(p+r)). Please correct or explain the low-rank structure exploited.
  - Constraints/regularization on w are not stated. Since d̃ uses w◦d and requires normalization, w should be constrained to be nonnegative; what prevents negative entries or trivial/degenerate solutions (e.g., zeroing all words in a document)? Any sparsity or magnitude regularization?
  - Rank/regularization for A is not described (choice of r, penalties), and there is no sensitivity/ablation.
- Experimental protocol:
  - LDA is trained transductively on train+test vocabulary; this risks leakage and disadvantages methods trained strictly on training data. Consider training LDA only on training data for fairness.
  - The paper uses BO to tune baselines but seems to fix S-WMD’s λ, B, N and other hyperparameters; please clarify the tuning protocol for your method and provide sensitivity plots (λ, rank r, neighbor budget N, batch size).
  - Missing ablations: contributions of A-only, w-only, both; squared vs. unsquared Euclidean ground costs; effect of WCD neighbor filtering budget on accuracy/speed.
  - Scope of baselines: given the supervised setting, strong text classification baselines like linear SVM/logistic regression over TF-IDF, and fastText (Joulin et al., 2016) would contextualize significance beyond kNN. If your focus is strictly on metric learning for kNN, state that explicitly and motivate it.
- Practical issues:
  - k for kNN is not reported; sensitivity to k would be useful.
  - The choice λ=10 is justified empirically; report number of Sinkhorn iterations and stopping tolerance since runtime depends on these.

Quality
Overall technically solid and well-motivated. The optimization leverages known properties of entropy-regularized OT to obtain usable gradients. The main caveats are clarity of derivations, precise complexity claims, and some fairness/tuning issues in experiments.

Clarity
Writing is generally understandable, but the paper would benefit from cleaning notational overload, fixing typos/references, and explicitly stating constraints and hyperparameters. Add a concise algorithmic description of the gradient path (which quantities are differentiated through and which are held by envelope theorem).

Originality
The combination—learning a supervised ground metric and word importances for OT in the word-embedding space under an NCA loss—is novel in the NLP metric-learning context. Related ideas exist in vision but the adaptation and engineering for text are new and useful.

Significance
Likely to be impactful for retrieval/classification scenarios where distances matter and labeled data is available. The low-dimensional learning in embedding space is attractive. With stronger baselines/ablations and clarified theory, this could become a reference approach.

Questions for the authors
- Are w constrained to be nonnegative? Any regularization on w and A? How do you prevent degenerate solutions when w zeroes out words present in a document?
- Do you train/evaluate with the same (Sinkhorn-regularized) distance? If not, how large is the train-test mismatch?
- Please reconcile the complexity accounting of the gradient w.r.t. A.
- What is k in kNN? Sensitivity to k, λ, rank r, and neighbor budget N?
- How much does each component (A vs. w) contribute?

Suggestions
- Add ablations: A-only, w-only, both; with/without regularization; sensitivity to λ and r.
- Ensure fair training of LDA (no transductive use of test data), and consider adding strong supervised classification baselines.
- Clean notation and references; clarify dual variables and their dimensions.
- Report number of Sinkhorn iterations and stopping criteria; include runtime vs. accuracy trade-offs.

Minor issues
- Fix typos and incomplete citations (e.g., [21?]), and section numbering.
- Define all symbols at first use and avoid overloading p.
- Specify normalization and constraints for w explicitly.

Pros
- Well-motivated, principled, efficient, and empirically strong.
- Novel supervised adaptation of WMD with practical algorithmic contributions.
- Public code and comprehensive comparisons.

Cons
- Some theoretical/clarity gaps and questionable transductive baseline.
- Missing ablations and broader supervised baselines.
- Notational inconsistencies and complexity misstatement.

Overall: A strong and promising contribution; with addressed concerns, suitable for acceptance.
