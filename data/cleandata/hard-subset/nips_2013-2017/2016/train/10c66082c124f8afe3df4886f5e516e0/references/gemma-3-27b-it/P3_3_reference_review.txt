## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document similarity. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weight word frequencies, directly minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric.  A key contribution is the efficient computation of gradients leveraging the Sinkhorn distance as a relaxation of the optimal transport problem, making the approach scalable.  The authors demonstrate strong empirical performance across eight datasets and 26 baselines, showcasing the superiority of S-WMD.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the recent success of WMD [Kusner et al., 2016] as a powerful, embedding-based document distance measure. It correctly positions itself within the broader context of metric learning [Yang & Jin, 2017], noting the limitations of existing methods, particularly their quadratic scaling with dimensionality. The use of Sinkhorn distances [Cuturi, 2013] for efficient optimal transport is well-established, and the authors effectively integrate it into their supervised learning framework. They also draw inspiration from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [Goldberger et al., 2005] for the stochastic optimization objective. The authors appropriately reference related work in both document representation learning (LSI, LDA, autoencoders) and metric learning (LMNN, ITML).

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The extensive evaluation across a diverse set of datasets and a comprehensive set of baselines demonstrates the effectiveness of S-WMD. Consistently outperforming 26 other methods is a significant achievement.
* **Scalability via Sinkhorn Distance:** The efficient gradient computation using the Sinkhorn distance is a crucial contribution. This addresses a major computational bottleneck associated with WMD and makes supervised learning feasible.
* **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The combination of linear metric learning within the word embedding space *and* re-weighting word frequencies is a novel approach, offering increased flexibility and potentially capturing task-specific importance of words.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental results. The use of mathematical notation is appropriate and facilitates understanding.
* **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The paper demonstrates a meticulous experimental setup, including hyperparameter tuning with Bayesian optimization and reporting of standard errors.
* **Initialization Strategy:** Utilizing S-WCD for initialization demonstrates a good understanding of the underlying problem and provides a strong starting point for optimization.
* **Practicality:** The training times are reasonable, making the method practically applicable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Sensitivity to λ:** While the authors address the trade-off between accuracy and speed when choosing λ for the Sinkhorn distance, the sensitivity of performance to this parameter could be further explored. A more systematic investigation of λ’s influence would be valuable.
* **Squared Euclidean Distance:** The choice of squared Euclidean distance in the transport cost seems somewhat arbitrary. Exploring other distance metrics (e.g., p=1, cosine distance) might yield further improvements.  The reasoning for using squared Euclidean over other options isn't fully elaborated.
* **Limited Analysis of Word Weights:** While the visualization of learned word weights (Figure 2) is insightful, a more in-depth analysis of these weights across different datasets would strengthen the paper.  What patterns emerge? What types of words consistently receive high weights?
* **Potential for Overfitting:** The supervised learning approach could be susceptible to overfitting, especially with smaller datasets. While the authors use k-NN classification for evaluation, a discussion of regularization strategies or cross-validation approaches to mitigate overfitting would be beneficial.
* **Connection to Existing Metric Learning:** While the paper cites NCA, the connection to other metric learning techniques could be more explicitly drawn.  For example, how does the proposed approach relate to approaches that learn non-linear metrics?
* **Impact of Batch Size and Neighbor Set Size:**  The sensitivity analysis on the parameters B and N could be further explored to determine their impact on performance and training time.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* State-of-the-art performance on a wide range of datasets.
* Novel and technically sound approach.
* Addresses a significant computational challenge with a clever solution.
* Well-written and thoroughly evaluated.

**Con:**

* Sensitivity to the Sinkhorn regularization parameter.
* Limited analysis of the learned word weights.
* Potential for overfitting.
* Could benefit from a more detailed comparison to other metric learning approaches.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of document similarity and metric learning. The proposed S-WMD method achieves state-of-the-art results, addresses a key computational bottleneck, and offers a novel combination of techniques. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.  The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a compelling case for its value.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** I recommend this paper for acceptance to NIPS.  I believe it will be highly valuable to the research community and has the potential to inspire further work in this area.
