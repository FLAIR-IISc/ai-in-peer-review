Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD; Kusner et al., ICML 2015) for document comparison. The key idea is to learn (i) a linear transform A in the word-embedding space and (ii) a word importance vector w that reweights document histograms, so that WMD distances better reflect label-based similarity. The objective is an NCA-style stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss over document distances. To make optimization tractable, the authors use an entropically-regularized optimal transport (Sinkhorn distance; Cuturi, NIPS 2013) to compute approximate transport plans and dual potentials, enabling efficient gradients. They further introduce an initialization via supervised word centroid distance (S-WCD: NCA trained on averaged word vectors), mini-batch training, and neighbor filtering based on the cheaper WCD.

The method is related to metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML), supervised ground-metric learning for EMD (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.), and optimal transport relaxations (Cuturi; Cuturi & Doucet). The novelty here is combining supervised ground metric learning with histogram reweighting in the WMD setting for text, with efficient Sinkhorn-based gradients and a practical training recipe.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of Sinkhorn regularization to obtain unique transport plans and dual potentials for gradients is appropriate and well-motivated. The A-gradient via T* and the w-gradient via dual variables are reasonable. However, there is a conceptual mismatch: the loss is written in terms of the unregularized WMD, while gradients are computed using the Sinkhorn-regularized solution. This yields a biased gradient estimator unless the objective is explicitly the Sinkhorn cost. Clarifying whether the optimized distance is the entropic OT cost (and adopting its exact gradients) would strengthen the technical correctness.
- Optimization details are pragmatic: initialization via S-WCD, neighbor filtering, and batching. Complexity analysis is provided; code is released.
- Evaluation: Across 8 datasets and 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised variants with NCA/ITML/LMNN), S-WMD achieves best average rank and top performance on 5/8 datasets, with sensible interpretability via learned word weights. Hyperparameters of baselines are tuned via Bayesian optimization. However, the protocol for S-WMD hyperparameters (e.g., λ, M, batch sizes, learning rates) and whether a validation split is used is not fully specified, especially for datasets without predefined splits.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and well-structured. The motivation and pipeline are clear, and the role of Sinkhorn is well explained.
- There are several notation/typo issues: mismatched section numbers (e.g., “.2 Optimization”), inconsistent use of p and squared norms, incomplete citations (e.g., “[5, 21?]”), unclear expressions for dual variables (the “1/p 1” term), and occasional grammar. Tightening notation and explicitly stating constraints on w (nonnegativity) would improve clarity.

Originality
- Learning a supervised ground metric for OT is not new per se, but doing so for WMD on text, jointly with word-importance reweighting, and integrating Sinkhorn-based gradients within an NCA objective is a novel and useful combination. The initialization and neighbor filtering tailored to this setting are also practically novel.

Significance
- The work addresses a relevant and widely used distance for text and provides consistent improvements over strong baselines, including unsupervised WMD and supervised metric learning on common representations. The method is likely to be adopted in retrieval and kNN-style classification tasks and provides interpretable word weights.
- Limitations: evaluation is confined to kNN accuracy; scalability to very large corpora and long documents is not fully stress-tested; and comparisons to contemporary supervised text classifiers (e.g., fastText, CNNs) are absent (though the kNN focus is appropriate for assessing a metric).

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem: supervised adaptation of WMD for task-specific similarity.
- Sound and efficient use of entropic OT for differentiability and speed.
- Joint learning of A and w adds flexibility and interpretability.
- Strong empirical results across multiple datasets and many baselines; code available.
- Clear practical training recipe (initialization, batching, neighbor pruning).

Cons for acceptance
- Objective/gradient mismatch (WMD vs. Sinkhorn) is not fully justified; better to directly optimize the entropic OT cost.
- Constraints/regularization on w are not clearly specified; negative weights would be problematic after normalization.
- Limited analysis: no ablations (A-only vs. w-only), sensitivity to λ, or p=1 vs. p=2; limited discussion of initialization and neighbor filtering impact.
- Evaluation limited to kNN; no retrieval metrics or clustering; unclear hyperparameter tuning protocol for S-WMD on datasets without predefined splits.
- Clarity/notation issues and some citation gaps.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explicitly define the optimized distance as the entropic OT/Sinkhorn cost and use its exact gradients (or justify the approximation). Provide sensitivity to λ and a runtime/accuracy trade-off.
- Enforce and state constraints on w (e.g., w ≥ 0, possibly sum-to-one) and add regularization (L1/L2) to avoid overfitting; report sparsity/interpretability effects.
- Add ablation studies: A-only, w-only, different initializations, and the effect of neighbor filtering M and N.
- Expand evaluation: include retrieval metrics (e.g., mAP), clustering quality, and larger-scale datasets; consider additional embeddings (GloVe), and cosine vs. Euclidean ground costs.
- Clarify hyperparameter tuning and validation splits; avoid tuning on test data.
- Fix notation/typos and complete references.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper proposes a practically useful and novel supervised extension of WMD with solid empirical gains and an efficient training procedure. Addressing the objective-approximation mismatch and adding ablations/sensitivity analyses would make it stronger.

Confidence: High on OT/metric-learning aspects; moderate on NLP baseline coverage.
