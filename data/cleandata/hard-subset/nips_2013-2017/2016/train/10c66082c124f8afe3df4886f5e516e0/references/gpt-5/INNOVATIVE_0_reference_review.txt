Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of WMD that learns (i) a linear transformation A of the pre-trained word embedding and (ii) a word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms, to better align document distances with class labels. The learning objective is an NCA-style stochastic LOO kNN loss computed on pairwise WMD distances. Because exact WMD is expensive and non-smooth, the authors use the entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) relaxation to obtain fast, differentiable approximations: they leverage the Sinkhorn scaling vectors both to compute the approximate transport plan T* and to recover approximate dual variables for gradients w.r.t. A and w. Training further exploits a WCD-based neighbor filter and mini-batching. Across 8 datasets and 26 baselines (including unsupervised WMD/WCD and standard text representations with and without metric learning), S-WMD achieves the best average rank and is often the top performer. Code is provided.

Relation to prior work
WMD/Kusner et al. introduced document distances via OT over word embeddings. This paper is the first, to my knowledge, to supervise WMD for text by learning the ground metric and histogram weights jointly. There is relevant supervised EMD/OT work in vision and metric learning (Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; ITML/LMNN/NCA), which the authors cite and adapt: the novelty lies in bringing supervised ground-metric learning to the WMD/text setting, coupling it with word-importance reweighting, and making it practical with Sinkhorn-based gradients and an NCA objective.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and boldness: moving beyond unsupervised WMD to a supervised, learnable ground metric in word-embedding space plus adaptive word reweighting is a meaningful leap that can change how distances for text are built and tuned to tasks.
- Solid and efficient methodology: using entropic OT both to approximate the transport and to extract dual potentials for gradients is elegant and computationally effective; the neighbor filtering via WCD is pragmatic and well motivated.
- Strong empirical showing: thorough evaluation on 8 datasets with 26 baselines; S-WMD has the best average rank and often the best error. The visualization and word-weight inspection are insightful.
- Practicality: code available; training times appear reasonable; the approach remains in low-dimensional embedding space and avoids building new document representations.

Weaknesses / questions
- Objective mismatch and theoretical guarantees: the optimization uses gradients from the entropic OT relaxation to minimize an NCA loss defined in terms of (ostensibly) exact WMD. The paper acknowledges the approximation, but clearer discussion of the implications (e.g., are we effectively optimizing the Sinkhorn distance in the loss? how sensitive to λ?) would help. Some ablations varying λ and showing convergence/accuracy trade-offs would strengthen the case.
- Regularization and identifiability: A and w can in principle scale distances arbitrarily (e.g., global scaling of A), yet the NCA softmax is scale-sensitive. Are there explicit regularizers or constraints (e.g., Frobenius/trace norm on A, positivity and normalization for w) and projections during optimization? Please clarify and report their effect.
- Ablations: It would be useful to separate the contributions of A vs w: (i) only learn A, (ii) only learn w, (iii) both. Also report the impact of the WCD-neighbor filtering (N) and batch size (B) on accuracy/speed to demonstrate robustness.
- Choice of ground cost: the method fixes squared Euclidean between transformed embeddings. Did you try L2 vs L1 or cosine-derived distances? Given the mixed performance on CLASSIC, a brief sensitivity study would be informative.
- Evaluation breadth: Results are limited to kNN classification. Since distances are often used for retrieval/ranking, including IR-style metrics or author-identification tasks would broaden impact. Also, a comparison to strong supervised neural baselines for text classification (CNN/RNN on documents) would calibrate absolute performance; even if architectures differ, it helps position the contribution.
- Notational clarity: p is used both as the Lp exponent and later seemingly as the vocabulary size in an equation for α, β. Please disambiguate. The formulas for dual potentials derived from u, v could use a clearer derivation and dimensionality checks.

Quality
- Generally technically sound with a clever and well-argued approximation strategy. Empirical evaluation is strong. Some theoretical aspects (effect of λ, exact vs relaxed objective) are left informal; ablations and regularization details would improve rigor.

Clarity
- Clear and well organized overall. A few notational inconsistencies and minor typos should be fixed. Please expand the optimization section with constraints/regularization and give more detail on the neighbor-filter heuristic.

Originality
- High. While supervised ground-metric learning for EMD exists in vision, applying it to WMD with joint word reweighting and an efficient Sinkhorn/NCA training loop for text is novel and timely.

Significance
- High. WMD is widely used; a supervised, efficient extension that consistently improves performance is likely to be adopted. The idea of learning in the ground metric space rather than document space is compelling and extensible.

Pros
- Novel supervised extension of WMD with joint learning of ground metric and word weights.
- Elegant use of Sinkhorn for both speed and differentiability; practical training pipeline.
- Strong empirical gains across many datasets and baselines; code released.

Cons
- Limited theoretical discussion of approximation effects; lacks ablations (A vs w, λ, neighbor filtering).
- Evaluation confined to kNN classification; missing comparisons to strong supervised neural text classifiers.
- Minor clarity/notation issues; regularization details under-specified.

Recommendation: Accept
This is a bold and impactful step beyond WMD, with a well-engineered, efficient training method and convincing empirical gains. Despite some missing ablations and theoretical polish, the core idea is novel and significant enough to merit acceptance. I encourage the authors to add the suggested analyses and clarifications. Confidence: high.
