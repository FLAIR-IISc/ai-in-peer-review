### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific distance metric for documents. The work builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost of "transporting" words from one to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space (e.g., word2vec). While WMD has shown state-of-the-art performance, it is entirely unsupervised.

The core contribution of this paper is to incorporate supervision into the WMD framework. The authors propose learning two components simultaneously: 1) a linear transformation of the word embedding space to better align word relationships with the specific classification task, and 2) a set of "importance" weights for each word in the vocabulary, allowing the model to emphasize or de-emphasize words based on their discriminative power.

A major challenge is the prohibitive computational cost of embedding the WMD optimization (a linear program) within a gradient-based learning loop. The authors present an elegant and critical solution by replacing the exact WMD with its entropically regularized counterpart, the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation makes the optimal transport problem strictly convex and differentiable, admitting a fast iterative solution (Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm) and enabling efficient gradient computation. The overall model is trained to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments on 8 datasets that S-WMD significantly outperforms a wide range of baselines, including unsupervised WMD and various document representations combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments for Acceptance (Strengths):**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** This paper presents a bold and highly original idea. While supervising distance metrics is not new, the proposed approach of learning a metric *within* the optimal transport framework for documents is a significant conceptual leap. Instead of learning a Mahalanobis distance on fixed, high-dimensional document vectors, S-WMD learns to reshape the underlying low-dimensional word embedding space itself, along with word importance. This is a fundamentally new and powerful way to think about supervised document similarity and is likely to inspire a new line of research.

2.  **Elegant Technical Solution:** The primary obstacle to this idea is computational tractability. The authors' use of the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable proxy for WMD is an excellent technical contribution. It is a principled and effective solution that makes an otherwise intractable problem feasible. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the connection between optimal transport and machine learning and provides a practical blueprint for incorporating such distances into end-to-end learning systems.

3.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing. The method is evaluated against an extensive set of 26 baselines across 8 diverse datasets. S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance on the majority of these tasks, often by a significant margin. The comparison to strong baselines, such as traditional representations (TF-IDF, LSI) combined with powerful metric learners (NCA, LMNN), effectively demonstrates that the proposed approach is not just a minor improvement but a major step forward.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clear, the background is concise, and the proposed method is explained with precision. The derivation of the gradients and the optimization strategy are easy to follow. The connection to prior work in both metric learning and optimal transport is clearly articulated.

**Arguments for Rejection (Weaknesses):**

1.  **Reliance on Approximation:** The method's tractability hinges on the Sinkhorn relaxation. While empirically successful, a more thorough discussion or analysis of the impact of the entropy regularization parameter λ on the final task performance would have been beneficial. It is unclear how sensitive the final classification accuracy is to the quality of this approximation. However, given the intractability of the alternative, this is a pragmatic and well-justified choice rather than a critical flaw.

2.  **Simplicity of Transformations:** The paper learns a single, global linear transformation for the entire word embedding space. This is an elegant and simple starting point, but it's possible that a more expressive transformation (e.g., non-linear, or context-dependent) could provide further gains. Similarly, the word importance weights are global. This isn't a weakness of the current work so much as an obvious direction for future improvements that builds upon this strong foundation.

### Final Assessment

This is an excellent paper that exemplifies the kind of research NIPS should showcase. It takes a powerful, recent idea (WMD) and addresses its main limitation (being unsupervised) with a novel and technically sophisticated approach. The core idea of learning a metric by transforming the underlying word embedding space within an optimal transport framework is a significant contribution. The solution to the immense computational challenge via the Sinkhorn distance is both clever and effective.

The paper's strengths—its high degree of novelty, significant potential impact, strong technical contribution, and convincing empirical results—far outweigh its minor weaknesses. This work opens up a new, promising avenue for research in document understanding and metric learning. I am confident that other researchers will be eager to build upon these ideas. I strongly recommend acceptance.
