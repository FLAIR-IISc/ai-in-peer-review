### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), an unsupervised distance metric that computes the minimum cost to "transport" words from one document to another in a pre-trained word embedding space. The authors identify a key limitation of WMD: its unsupervised nature prevents it from adapting to the specific notion of similarity required by a given classification task.

To address this, S-WMD introduces two supervised components: 1) a linear transformation matrix `A` applied to the word embedding space, which learns to warp the geometry of word representations to be more suitable for the task, and 2) a word importance vector `w`, which re-weighs words in the document's bag-of-words representation to emphasize task-relevant terms.

The core technical challenge is that optimizing `A` and `w` by differentiating through the WMD calculation is computationally prohibitive due to the cubic complexity of the underlying optimal transport problem. The authors propose a clever and efficient solution by replacing the exact WMD with its entropically regularized counterpart, the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation is differentiable and can be computed much faster (in quadratic time). The parameters `A` and `w` are then learned by minimizing a stochastic approximation of the leave-one-out kNN classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). To further improve efficiency, the authors propose a smart initialization scheme (by first learning a metric on the much faster Word Centroid Distance) and a batch-based training procedure that only considers a small subset of nearest neighbors for gradient computation. The method is evaluated on 8 document classification tasks, demonstrating superior performance over 26 strong baselines, including WMD and various representation learning methods combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a highly novel and significant contribution. It elegantly combines ideas from optimal transport (WMD), word embeddings, and metric learning (NCA). Extending the powerful but unsupervised WMD framework to a supervised setting is a natural and important research direction, and this paper provides a principled and effective solution. The idea of learning both a transformation of the embedding space (`A`) and word-level importance weights (`w`) simultaneously within the transport framework is particularly insightful. This work significantly advances the state of the art in document distance metrics.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Quality:** The technical execution is excellent. The key insight to use the entropically regularized Sinkhorn distance to make the WMD objective tractable and differentiable is both clever and well-motivated. The derivations for the gradients with respect to `A` and `w` (leveraging the dual variables of the relaxed transport problem) appear sound. The proposed optimization strategy, including the S-WCD initialization and the nearest-neighbor-based batching, is practical and effectively addresses the significant computational hurdles, making the method viable.

3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare S-WMD against an extensive set of 26 baselines across 8 diverse datasets. These baselines include classical methods (TF-IDF, LSI), state-of-the-art representation learning (mSDA), and the original WMD, as well as combinations of these with powerful metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The fact that S-WMD achieves the best performance on 5 out of 8 datasets and the best average rank overall is strong evidence of its effectiveness. The inclusion of t-SNE visualizations and word weight analysis further strengthens the paper by providing qualitative insights into what the model has learned.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It provides a clear background on word2vec, WMD, Sinkhorn distances, and NCA, making the work accessible. The motivation for the problem is clearly articulated, and the proposed method is developed in a logical, step-by-step manner. The complexity analysis and the description of the optimization algorithm are clear and sufficient for understanding the implementation.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

1.  **Ablation Study on Initialization:** The paper proposes initializing `A` by training on the Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) and shows that this "S-WMD init." is already a strong baseline. However, the importance of this specific initialization is not fully ablated. How does the final performance of S-WMD change if initialized with a random projection or the identity matrix? Does the S-WCD initialization primarily speed up convergence, or is it critical for reaching a better local minimum of the non-convex objective? A more detailed analysis would strengthen the claim about the initialization's utility.

2.  **Sensitivity to the Regularization Parameter `λ`:** The Sinkhorn relaxation introduces a crucial hyperparameter `λ`, which controls the trade-off between approximation quality and computational speed/stability. The authors state they use a fixed `λ=10`, describing it as a "nice trade-off". However, the sensitivity of the model's performance to this parameter is not explored. A brief analysis showing how the final kNN error varies with `λ` would add to the paper's completeness and provide practical guidance for users.

3.  **Scalability of the Word Importance Vector `w`:** The word importance vector `w` has a dimension equal to the vocabulary size `n`, which can be very large. While the gradient updates are sparse, storing and manipulating this vector could be a concern for corpora with millions of unique words. A brief discussion on the scalability and memory implications of this component, especially in comparison to methods that produce fixed-size document vectors, would be beneficial.

4.  **Lack of Comparison with End-to-End Neural Models:** At the time of this work (NIPS 2016), neural models for text classification (e.g., CNNs, LSTMs/GRUs) were prominent. While S-WMD operates in a different paradigm (a non-parametric kNN-based method), these models represent the state-of-the-art for the *end task* of classification. A discussion of the relative advantages of S-WMD (e.g., data efficiency, interpretability of the learned metric and word weights) compared to these end-to-end trained parametric models would provide valuable context for the reader. This is not a major flaw, as the paper's focus is on document distance, but it would better position the work within the broader field.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the fields of machine learning and natural language processing. It proposes a novel, principled, and effective method for learning a supervised document distance. The technical approach is clever, addressing a significant computational challenge with an elegant relaxation. The experimental results are comprehensive and compelling. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to opportunities for additional analysis that would further strengthen an already outstanding piece of work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
