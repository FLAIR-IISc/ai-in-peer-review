**Summary**
This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which leverages pre-trained word embeddings to compute an optimal transport cost between documents. The authors incorporate supervision by learning two components: a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space and a vector `w` of word-importance weights. The learning objective is formulated to minimize a differentiable proxy for the kNN leave-one-out classification error, following the framework of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A significant technical challenge is the high computational cost of the WMD and its gradient. The authors address this by replacing the exact optimal transport problem with its entropy-regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance), which permits a much faster and differentiable approximation. The paper presents an extensive empirical evaluation across eight datasets, comparing S-WMD to a wide range of document representation and metric learning baselines, demonstrating strong performance.

**Strengths**
1.  **Clear Motivation and Sound Approach:** The paper addresses a well-motivated problem: adapting the powerful but unsupervised WMD for specific classification tasks. The proposed method of learning both a transformation of the embedding space and word-importance weights is a comprehensive way to incorporate label information.
2.  **Effective Technical Solution:** The primary contribution is a practical algorithm for a seemingly intractable optimization problem. The use of Sinkhorn distances to approximate the WMD gradient is a sensible and effective choice, successfully balancing computational feasibility with approximation accuracy. The proposed initialization strategy using a supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) and the nearest-neighbor filtering for batch updates are also clever engineering choices that improve efficiency.
3.  **Extensive and Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is a major strength of this work. The authors compare their method against 26 baselines on eight different datasets, which is commendably thorough. The results show that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art kNN classification error on a majority of these datasets and obtains the best average rank overall. This provides substantial evidence for the method's effectiveness.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides the necessary background on WMD, Sinkhorn distances, and NCA. The authors do a good job of explaining the problem, their proposed solution, and the steps taken to make it practical.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The proposed method is primarily a novel combination of several established techniques: WMD, NCA, and Sinkhorn distance approximation. While the integration is non-trivial and effective, the core conceptual components are not new. The work of Wang et al. (2012) [35] also explored learning a ground metric for EMD in a supervised setting, albeit with a different loss (ITML) and without the word-reweighting component. The contribution here is more empirical and practical than deeply theoretical or foundational.
2.  **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** The paper is entirely empirical. There is no analysis of the properties of the learned metric or convergence guarantees for the optimization procedure. The choice of the Sinkhorn regularization parameter (λ=10) is presented as a practical trade-off without a formal justification or sensitivity analysis. How does the choice of λ impact the quality of the learned metric and the gradient approximation? A more rigorous treatment of the approximation's effect on the learning process would strengthen the paper's methodological contribution.
3.  **Inconsistent Performance and Speculative Explanations:** While S-WMD performs very well on average, it does not outperform established baselines on all datasets. On CLASSIC, the unsupervised WMD performs better, and on BBCSPORT, LSI combined with NCA is superior. The authors offer brief, post-hoc explanations for these results (e.g., suboptimal squared Euclidean distance, long document lengths). These explanations are speculative and not supported by controlled experiments. For a new method to be compelling, it should be understood why and when it might fail. The lack of a clear win on all datasets suggests that the benefits of this complex model are not universal, and simpler, established methods remain competitive in certain domains.
4.  **Limited Insight into the Learned Metric:** The analysis of what is actually learned is somewhat superficial. The paper provides a word cloud visualizing the learned importance weights `w`, which is useful. However, there is no analysis of the learned linear transformation `A`. What kind of semantic shifts is `A` learning? Does it separate certain concepts or collapse others? Visualizing the effect of `A` on the word embedding space would provide valuable insight into the mechanism behind the method's success and move the contribution beyond simply demonstrating a lower error rate.

**Conclusion**
This paper presents a well-engineered and powerful method for supervised document classification. The authors successfully combine WMD with a metric learning objective, made practical through an efficient Sinkhorn-based approximation. The primary strength of the paper lies in its extensive and convincing empirical results, which demonstrate a clear advance in state-of-the-art for kNN document classification.

However, the contribution is more of a strong piece of engineering than a fundamental advance. The method synthesizes existing ideas, and its superiority over simpler, well-established methods is not consistent across all tested conditions. The lack of deeper analysis into the properties of the learned metric and the reasons for its occasional failures leaves some important questions unanswered.

Overall, the strong empirical performance and the clever solution to a difficult optimization problem make this a solid contribution. It is a complete and well-executed piece of work that is likely to be built upon by others. While I have reservations about the depth of the methodological novelty and analysis, the demonstrable significance of the results warrants acceptance.
