

# Peer Review: Supervised Word Mover's Distance

## Summary

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to learn a metric better suited for classification tasks. The authors introduce two key innovations: (1) a linear transformation of the word embeddings (A) and (2) word-specific importance weights (w) that re-weight the bag-of-words representation. To make the computation feasible, they leverage the Sinkhorn distance relaxation of the optimal transport problem. The method is evaluated across 8 document datasets against 26 baselines, with the authors claiming S-WMD outperforms all baselines on average.

The paper builds on two important strands of prior work: Kusner et al.'s WMD [19] and metric learning approaches like Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [15]. The authors convincingly argue that unsupervised document distances like WMD don't explicitly optimize for classification tasks, and their supervised approach should better capture the semantic relationships relevant to the classification task at hand.

## Quality

### Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method, with clear derivations of the gradient computations and a well-justified optimization strategy using the Sinkhorn relaxation. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to approximate the WMD is a well-established technique, and the authors correctly cite Cuturi [6] as the source.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experimental section is thorough, with evaluations on 8 diverse datasets (news categorization, sentiment analysis, product identification) and a comparison against 26 baselines. The authors provide detailed analysis of the results, including visualization via t-SNE and examination of learned word weights.

3. **Practical Considerations**: The authors address computational concerns by proposing batch gradient descent with neighbor filtering, which significantly speeds up training. The complexity analysis in Section 3.2 is particularly valuable for practitioners.

### Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: While the paper reports standard errors for some datasets, it doesn't provide statistical tests to confirm whether the performance improvements over baselines are statistically significant. This is a critical omission for a paper claiming to outperform all baselines on average.

2. **Limited Theoretical Analysis of Relaxation**: The paper uses the Sinkhorn relaxation without thoroughly analyzing its impact on the quality of the learned metric. The authors state that "setting λ too large can make the algorithm converges slower" but don't provide evidence that λ=10 is optimal for their specific task.

3. **Insufficient Justification for Key Design Choices**: The paper doesn't adequately explain why they chose the squared Euclidean distance in Equation (5) over the Manhattan distance used in the original WMD paper [19]. Additionally, the choice of KL-divergence loss (Equation 6) over other metric learning losses (e.g., LMNN's margin-based loss) isn't justified.

4. **Potential Negative Impact on Word Embeddings**: The paper doesn't address whether the linear transformation A might distort the semantic relationships between words that the unsupervised embedding captured. This could be particularly problematic if the transformation learns to collapse meaningful semantic distinctions.

## Clarity

### Strengths
1. **Well-Structured**: The paper is logically organized, with a clear progression from problem statement to background to proposed method and evaluation.

2. **Detailed Algorithm Description**: Algorithm 1 provides a clear step-by-step description of the S-WMD training procedure.

3. **Good Use of Visualizations**: The t-SNE visualizations (Figure 1) effectively demonstrate the improved clustering of documents using S-WMD compared to WMD.

### Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Intuitive Explanation for Key Steps**: The paper could benefit from more intuitive explanations for why certain technical choices were made (e.g., the Sinkhorn relaxation, the gradient computations).

2. **Ambiguous Terminology**: The paper uses "word importance vector w" (Equation 4) without clearly explaining how this differs from standard term weighting approaches used in information retrieval (e.g., TF-IDF).

3. **Insufficient Context for Related Work**: The related work section (Section 5) mentions Wang et al. [35] as similar work but doesn't clearly articulate how S-WMD differs from their approach in a way that justifies the novelty claim.

## Originality

### Strengths
1. **Novel Combination**: The paper successfully combines metric learning with optimal transport for text, creating a new supervised document distance that builds on the WMD framework.

2. **Word-Specific Importance Weights**: The introduction of word importance weights (w) to re-weight the bag-of-words representation is a novel contribution in the context of WMD.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Distinction from Prior Work**: The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from Wang et al. [35], who also learn a metric within a generalized Euclidean EMD ground distance. The authors mention that Wang et al. "do not consider re-weighting the histograms," but this doesn't fully capture the novelty.

2. **Incremental Nature of Contribution**: The core idea of using supervised learning to improve a distance metric is not novel (NCA [15] is a direct precursor). The paper's main contribution is applying this to the WMD framework, which is valuable but perhaps not sufficiently novel for a NIPS paper.

3. **Lack of Connection to Recent Work on Supervised Word Embeddings**: The related work section cites Tang et al. [32] on sentiment-specific word embeddings but doesn't connect this work to the proposed S-WMD method, missing an opportunity to position their contribution more precisely.

## Significance

### Strengths
1. **Practical Impact**: The paper demonstrates that supervised learning can significantly improve document distances for classification tasks, which is valuable for practitioners working on text classification problems.

2. **Computational Efficiency**: The proposed method is shown to be computationally efficient compared to exact WMD computation, making it more practical for real-world applications.

3. **Strong Empirical Results**: The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on 5 out of 8 datasets, which is a significant practical contribution.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**: The paper evaluates on only 8 datasets, all of which are relatively small (the largest is 20NEWS with 18,846 documents). The method's performance on much larger text classification tasks isn't explored.

2. **Lack of Discussion on Limitations**: The paper doesn't discuss scenarios where S-WMD might not work well (e.g., very long documents, extremely large vocabularies, or tasks where word order matters).

3. **Unclear Path to Broader Impact**: While the paper claims S-WMD "may well be the best performing distance metric for documents to date," it doesn't clearly articulate how this method could be integrated into larger NLP pipelines or how it might be extended to other tasks beyond classification.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add Statistical Significance Testing**: Include a formal statistical test (e.g., paired t-test) to confirm that the performance improvements over baselines are statistically significant.

2. **Justify Key Design Choices**: Provide more justification for using squared Euclidean distance instead of Manhattan distance, and for choosing KL-divergence loss over other metric learning losses.

3. **Expand Related Work**: Better distinguish the paper from Wang et al. [35] and Tang et al. [32] by clearly articulating the specific novel contributions.

4. **Address Potential Negative Impact**: Include an analysis of how the linear transformation A affects the semantic relationships between words in the embedding space.

5. **Add Discussion of Limitations**: Include a section discussing the limitations of the approach and scenarios where it might not be appropriate.

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a well-executed extension of the Word Mover's Distance that incorporates supervision to improve document classification performance. The method is theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and demonstrates strong empirical results across multiple datasets. However, the paper has several weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong candidate for NIPS:

1. The novelty of the approach is somewhat limited as it's primarily a direct application of metric learning to the WMD framework.
2. The lack of statistical significance testing for the performance improvements is a significant oversight.
3. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work on metric learning for text.

While the results are promising and the method has practical value, the paper would benefit from more thorough theoretical analysis, clearer justification of key design choices, and better differentiation from related work. These improvements would strengthen the paper's contribution and make it more suitable for NIPS.

**Recommendation**: **Weak Reject**

The paper is well-written and presents a useful extension of existing work, but the incremental nature of the contribution and the lack of sufficient theoretical justification and statistical validation prevent it from meeting NIPS's high standards for novelty and significance.
