- Presents Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), learning a document distance by adapting word embeddings and re-weighting frequencies using Sinkhorn relaxation and probabilistic filtering.  
- Aims to minimize k-NN classification error by enabling efficient gradient-based learning for an otherwise costly WMD.  
- Positions work well within prior document distance and metric-learning literature, distinguishing itself by operating in word-embedding space rather than learning document representations.  
- Leverages Sinkhorn distance (Cuturi) for computational efficiency and adapts NCA-style stochastic neighborhood descent for WMD.  
- Novelty: combines metric learning with optimal transport to create a supervised WMD — a significant conceptual contribution.  
- Efficiency: uses Sinkhorn relaxation and probabilistic filtering to reduce computational bottlenecks, with complexity analysis provided.  
- Empirical strength: outperforms 26 baselines across 8 datasets with averaged results over multiple splits for robustness.  
- Clear motivation and problem definition explaining limitations of unsupervised approaches and need for supervision.  
- Technical contribution: provides detailed gradient derivations and optimization details aiding reproducibility.  
- Visualization and analysis: includes t-SNE plots and learned word-weight analyses that offer insight into model behavior.  
- Writing and organization: paper is well-written, clear, and accessible despite technical complexity.  
- Weakness: potential sensitivity to hyperparameters and reliance on Bayesian optimization without detailed ranges or sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: choice of squared Euclidean distance in optimal transport is justified but not thoroughly compared to alternatives.  
- Weakness: scalability to very large corpora or extremely high-dimensional embeddings remains unclear despite efficiency claims.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of failure cases and datasets where S-WMD underperforms (e.g., BBCSPORT, CLASSIC).  
- Weakness: dependence on S-WCD initialization is mentioned but not quantitatively compared to random initialization.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound, practical, achieves state-of-the-art results, and is reproducible and well-presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: hyperparameter sensitivity, incomplete failure-case analysis, and unclear applicability to very large real-world datasets.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with convincing contributions and results; weaknesses are minor and addressable.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, contingent on addressing hyperparameter details, failure-case discussion, scalability elaboration, and quantification of S-WCD initialization impact.
