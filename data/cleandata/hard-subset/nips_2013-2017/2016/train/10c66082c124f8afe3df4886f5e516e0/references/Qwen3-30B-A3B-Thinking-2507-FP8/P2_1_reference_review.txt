

# Review: Supervised Word Mover's Distance

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a supervised variant of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD). WMD, introduced by Kusner et al. [19], computes document distances by finding the optimal transport cost of moving words from one document to another within a word embedding space. The authors extend this framework by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word-specific importance weights to improve classification performance. They address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by using the Sinkhorn distance (entropy-regularized optimal transport), which allows them to approximate the WMD computation in O(q²) time instead of the O(q³ log q) complexity of the original WMD. The authors evaluate their method on 8 different document classification datasets, showing that S-WMD outperforms 26 baseline methods across most datasets.

## Evaluation

### Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper is technically sound with well-justified mathematical formulations for the loss function, gradients, and optimization.
- The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation across 8 diverse datasets, including news categorization, sentiment analysis, and product identification tasks.
- They include comprehensive comparisons against 26 baseline methods (including 6 document representations with 3 metric learning algorithms each), which demonstrates the robustness of their approach.
- The computational efficiency claims are well-supported with training time analysis showing S-WMD is comparable or faster than baseline metric learning methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks theoretical analysis of why supervision improves WMD performance. While they show empirical improvements, they don't provide a theoretical justification for why the linear transformation and word weighting help.
- The paper doesn't include ablation studies to determine the relative contribution of the linear transformation versus the word importance weights. It's unclear whether one component is more important than the other.
- The initialization method (using NCA on WCD) seems reasonable but isn't thoroughly justified. The authors don't compare against alternative initialization methods.
- The paper doesn't adequately address potential failure cases or limitations of their approach (e.g., how it performs on very short documents or with out-of-vocabulary words).

### Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized with clear sections that build logically from background to proposed method to experiments.
- The mathematical formulations are clearly presented with proper notation and explanations.
- The experimental setup is described thoroughly, making it possible for readers to understand and potentially reproduce the results.
- The visualization of embeddings (Figure 1) and learned word weights (Figure 2) effectively communicate the benefits of their method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper uses "S-WMD" and "SWMD" interchangeably, which creates confusion for readers.
- The distinction between "re-weighted word frequencies" and "word importance weights" isn't always clear.
- The connection between the Sinkhorn approximation and the quality of the learned metric isn't fully explained. Why does using the Sinkhorn distance not degrade performance significantly?

### Originality

**Strengths:**
- The paper makes a clear and novel contribution by incorporating supervision into the WMD framework, which is not present in prior work.
- The combination of learning a linear transformation of word embeddings with word-specific importance weights is a novel approach to improving document distances.
- While metric learning has been studied extensively, applying it specifically to the WMD framework is original and addresses a gap in the literature.
- The use of the Sinkhorn distance for efficient training of supervised WMD is a clever application of existing techniques to a new problem.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from Wang et al. [35], which also learns a metric within a generalized Euclidean EMD ground distance. The authors mention this work but don't clearly explain how their approach differs (particularly regarding the histogram re-weighting component).
- The paper doesn't cite or build upon Tang et al. [32] (Learning sentiment-specific word embeddings), which is another approach to supervised word embeddings that could provide context for their work.

### Significance

**Strengths:**
- The paper demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods on multiple datasets (lowest kNN error on 5/8 datasets).
- The method is computationally efficient, with training times comparable or better than baseline metric learning approaches.
- The approach has practical value for many text classification tasks, as it leverages the powerful word embeddings that are commonly available.
- The paper provides a clear path for practitioners to implement and use their method.

**Weaknesses:**
- The improvements over WMD aren't dramatic on all datasets (e.g., S-WMD doesn't perform best on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON).
- The paper doesn't provide a compelling argument for why supervised WMD is needed beyond better classification results. Why should researchers care about this specific metric when other approaches (like fine-tuning BERT) are becoming more popular?
- The significance of the method is somewhat limited to text classification tasks, with less clear applicability to other domains.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a novel contribution to the field of document distance metrics. The authors successfully address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods on multiple datasets.

The paper meets the quality criteria with strong experimental validation, though it would benefit from more theoretical justification and ablation studies. The clarity is high, with well-structured content and good visualizations. The originality is strong, as this is the first paper to incorporate supervision directly into the WMD framework. The significance is moderate to high, as the method provides a practical and efficient solution for text classification tasks.

## Recommendation

**Accept** - This paper makes a solid contribution to the field of text representation learning and metric learning. It's technically sound, well-written, and demonstrates clear improvements over existing methods. While there are some limitations in the theoretical justification and experimental scope, these don't undermine the overall quality and contribution of the work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel application of supervision to WMD, a significant extension to an important existing method
- Strong empirical results across 8 diverse datasets
- Efficient implementation with reasonable training times
- Clear presentation of the method and results

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited theoretical justification for why the method works better
- Some confusion in terminology (S-WMD vs. SWMD)
- Doesn't fully differentiate from related work (Wang et al. [35])
- Improvements aren't dramatic on all datasets

The paper is a strong candidate for NeurIPS, as it addresses a meaningful problem with a clear methodological contribution that is well-supported by experiments. The authors have made a significant contribution to the literature on document distances that will likely be useful to researchers and practitioners working on text classification tasks.
