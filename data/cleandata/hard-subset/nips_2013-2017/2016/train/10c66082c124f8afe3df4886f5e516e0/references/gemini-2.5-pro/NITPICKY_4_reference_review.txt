This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance. Building upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which leverages word embeddings to compute an optimal transport cost between documents, this work introduces supervision. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space and a vector of word importance weights `w`. These parameters are optimized to minimize a stochastic version of the leave-one-out k-NN classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A significant contribution is a set of techniques to make this optimization computationally feasible. The authors replace the expensive WMD calculation with its entropy-regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance), enabling fast and differentiable gradient computations. They further accelerate training through a clever initialization scheme based on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batching strategy that considers only a local neighborhood of documents. The method is evaluated on 8 datasets against 26 baselines, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on a majority of them.

### Strengths

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses a well-motivated problem: adapting the powerful but unsupervised WMD for specific tasks. The proposed approach of learning a metric in the low-dimensional word embedding space, rather than the high-dimensional document space, is an elegant and effective solution. The simultaneous learning of word importance weights (`w`) provides additional flexibility and interpretability, as shown in the word cloud visualization.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is extensive, comparing S-WMD against a wide array of 26 baselines, including strong representation learning methods (LDA, mSDA) combined with popular metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). Achieving the best performance on 5 out of 8 datasets and the best average rank is a significant result that clearly advances the state-of-the-art in k-NN document classification.
*   **Computational Feasibility:** The authors successfully tackle the primary challenge of this work: the prohibitive computational cost of differentiating through the WMD. The combination of the Sinkhorn distance relaxation for gradients, an intelligent initialization using S-WCD, and a nearest-neighbor approximation for the NCA loss makes the overall algorithm practical and efficient, as supported by the reported training times.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising contributions, the paper suffers from a substantial number of flaws in technical clarity, presentation, and methodological detail.

1.  **Lack of Technical Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   The derivation of the optimal dual variables (`α*λ`, `β*λ`) from the Sinkhorn scaling vectors (`u`, `v`) is a critical component of the gradient calculation for `w`. However, the formulas are presented without any citation or derivation. The notation `log(u)>1 p 1` is particularly opaque; the vector `1` is defined as a "p-dimensional all ones vector," but `p` is not defined in this context. It was previously used for the `Lp` norm in Equation (1) and later appears in the complexity analysis as the number of unique words. This ambiguity severely hinders the reader's ability to understand and reproduce this key part of the method.
    *   The complexity analysis in Section 3.2 appears questionable. The time complexity is stated as `O(TBN[p^2 + d^2(p + r)])`. The origin of the `d^2(p+r)` term is not explained. A naive analysis of the gradient for `A` in Equation (8) would suggest a complexity closer to `O(p^2 d^2)` for constructing the `d x d` matrix sum. The provided formula is non-obvious and requires a more detailed justification.

2.  **Formatting and Presentation Issues:**
    *   The list of references is in a state of severe disarray. A large number of entries are incomplete, missing essential information such as the conference or journal venue, year of publication, and page numbers (e.g., references [1], [3], [4], [5], [10], [11], [15], and many others). This lack of scholarly diligence is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   There is a broken local file path (`file:///C:/Users/...`) in the main text of Section 4 where Figure 2 is referenced.
    *   A minor typo appears in the footer of the first page: "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems".

3.  **Methodological Concerns:**
    *   The authors note that on the CLASSIC dataset, unsupervised WMD outperforms S-WMD. Their explanation—"a poor initialization" or the suboptimality of the squared Euclidean distance—is speculative and highlights a potential brittleness in the method. The sensitivity of the non-convex optimization to initialization is a known issue, but its impact is not systematically explored.
    *   The method introduces the regularization parameter `λ` for the Sinkhorn distance. The authors state that `λ=10` offers a good trade-off, but no sensitivity analysis is provided to support this choice or to understand its effect on the final performance across different datasets.

### Decision

While the paper presents a novel and high-performing method for supervised document distance learning, the numerous deficiencies in technical clarity, reproducibility, and basic scholarly presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The unclear derivation of crucial gradient components and the questionable complexity analysis are significant technical weaknesses. Furthermore, the abysmal state of the reference list and other formatting errors fall well below the quality standards expected at NIPS. Although the core ideas and empirical results are strong, the paper in its current form is not a complete and carefully prepared piece of work. I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the manuscript to address these points before resubmission.

**Recommendation: Reject**
