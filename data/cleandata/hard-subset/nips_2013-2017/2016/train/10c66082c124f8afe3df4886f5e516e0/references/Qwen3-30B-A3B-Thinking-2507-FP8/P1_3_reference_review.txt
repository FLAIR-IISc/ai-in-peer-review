

# Peer Review: Supervised Word Mover's Distance

## Summary
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of Kusner et al.'s Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to improve document distances for classification tasks. The authors recognize that while WMD leverages word embeddings to compute document distances, it remains unsupervised and may not be optimal for specific classification tasks. S-WMD learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and word-specific importance weights to make the WMD metric more effective for classification. The key innovation is using the Sinkhorn distance (entropy-regularized optimal transport) to make the supervised learning computationally feasible, allowing efficient gradient computation. The authors demonstrate S-WMD's superiority across 8 datasets and 26 baselines in kNN classification tasks.

The paper builds upon prior work on WMD (NIPS 2015) and metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML), while introducing a novel combination of supervised learning with optimal transport for document distances. The work is well-motivated by the limitations of unsupervised document representations and the need for task-specific metrics.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a meaningful contribution by extending WMD to a supervised setting. The use of Sinkhorn distance to efficiently compute gradients is a practical and well-executed solution to the computational challenges of supervised WMD.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experimental section is thorough, evaluating the method on 8 diverse datasets across different text classification tasks (news categorization, sentiment analysis, product identification). The comparison to 26 baselines (including both unsupervised and supervised methods) provides strong evidence of the approach's effectiveness.

3. **Clear Methodology**: The paper clearly explains the mathematical formulation, optimization algorithm, and computational details. The pseudo-code (Algorithm 1) provides a useful implementation guide. The authors also provide helpful visualizations (Figures 1 and 2) that demonstrate the improved clustering and learned word weights.

4. **Practical Impact**: The training time results (Table 3) show that S-WMD is computationally efficient, with training times comparable or better than the time required to train linear metric learning on baseline methods. This makes the approach practical for real-world applications.

5. **Strong Baseline Comparisons**: The paper carefully compares against relevant baselines including BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA, WCD, and WMD. The inclusion of supervised variants of these methods (using NCA, ITML, LMNN) provides a comprehensive evaluation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases**: While the paper reports that S-WMD achieves the lowest error on 5/8 datasets, it doesn't sufficiently analyze why it underperforms on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON. The brief explanation about "poor initialization" for CLASSIC is insufficient. A more detailed analysis of failure cases would strengthen the paper.

2. **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices**: The paper uses squared Euclidean distance for word embeddings without justification. It would be helpful to understand why this choice was made over alternatives like cosine similarity or other distance metrics. The paper also doesn't discuss the relationship between the learned word weights and actual semantic importance.

3. **Initialization Could Be Better Motivated**: The initialization using NCA on WCD is presented as a heuristic. A more principled motivation for why this initialization works well would strengthen the paper.

4. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of S-WMD. For example, how does the method scale with larger vocabularies or longer documents? What are the constraints on the word embedding space?

5. **Comparison to Recent Advances**: The paper doesn't compare against more recent approaches to supervised text representation learning, such as those using transformers or contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT). This is a notable gap given the rapid advances in NLP.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document distances and metric learning. The technical approach is sound, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, and the results demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it accessible to the NIPS audience.

The main weaknesses are the limited analysis of failure cases and insufficient justification for certain design choices. However, these do not significantly undermine the paper's overall contribution. The method is novel in its combination of supervised learning with optimal transport for document distances, and the results convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of text classification and document distances. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, with thorough experiments and clear results. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses an important problem in natural language processing. While the weaknesses noted are valid, they don't outweigh the paper's strong technical contribution and compelling empirical results.

The paper is relevant to NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning (metric learning) with a novel approach that combines theoretical insights (optimal transport) with practical applications (text classification). The method could be of interest to both researchers working on metric learning and those working on text classification.

The paper would be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing more analysis of failure cases and better justification for key design choices. However, even as it stands, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in document distances.
