

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) called S-WMD, which learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to improve document classification performance. The authors claim S-WMD outperforms 26 baseline methods across 8 datasets by directly minimizing the leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric.

## Strengths
The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and properly contextualizes its work within existing literature on metric learning and document distances. The computational efficiency claim using Sinkhorn distance approximation is plausible and well-justified. The experimental section demonstrates systematic evaluation across multiple datasets and provides useful visualizations of the learned word weights.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

**1. Insufficient justification for supervision**: The paper fails to convincingly argue why supervision is needed for WMD, which was already shown to be state-of-the-art in its original formulation. Since WMD leverages pre-trained word embeddings that capture semantic relationships, the authors should demonstrate that supervision provides meaningful improvement beyond what the unsupervised embeddings already offer. The claim that "text documents could be classified by topic or by author, which would lead to very different measures of dissimilarity" is vague and doesn't establish a strong theoretical case for the need for supervision.

**2. Weak statistical evidence**: The paper claims S-WMD "outperforms all other 26 methods" on average, but provides no statistical tests to confirm these differences are significant. The standard errors reported for some datasets are not sufficient to establish statistical significance across all comparisons. Without proper statistical validation, the claimed superiority is unsubstantiated.

**3. Inadequate comparison to the most relevant baseline**: The paper compares against unsupervised WMD (which was already state-of-the-art), but fails to quantify how much better S-WMD performs than the original WMD. The paper states S-WMD "reduces the error of LSI relatively by 51% and 22%," but doesn't provide similar comparisons against WMD itself, making it impossible to judge the actual value of the proposed supervision.

**4. Limited analysis of failure cases**: The paper notes S-WMD fails to outperform baselines on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON, but provides no meaningful analysis of why these datasets pose particular challenges. Understanding these failure modes is crucial for assessing the method's limitations and potential.

**5. Questionable novelty**: The paper's core idea—learning a linear transformation of embeddings for metric learning—is closely related to existing approaches like NCA, LMNN, and ITML. The authors don't sufficiently differentiate their approach from these established methods. The "re-weighting" of word frequencies is also conceptually similar to techniques used in standard metric learning for sparse representations.

**6. Overstated claims**: The paper claims S-WMD "may well be the best performing distance metric for documents to date," but this is an unsubstantiated overstatement. The results show marginal improvements on most datasets, but the paper doesn't demonstrate that these improvements are practically significant or that the method is robust across diverse document types.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a technically sound extension of WMD with reasonable computational improvements, it falls short of demonstrating a meaningful advancement over established methods. The claimed superiority over 26 baselines is not sufficiently supported by statistical evidence, and the theoretical justification for supervision is weak. The paper would be significantly strengthened by:

1. Providing statistical significance testing for all performance claims
2. Quantifying improvements over the unsupervised WMD baseline
3. Offering a deeper analysis of failure cases
4. More clearly differentiating the approach from existing metric learning techniques

As a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and rigorous validation, I find the paper's claims overstated and the evidence insufficiently compelling. The approach appears to be a relatively minor extension of existing work rather than a significant advancement. While the computational efficiency claim is valid, it doesn't compensate for the lack of strong empirical evidence demonstrating the method's superiority.

The paper would be appropriate for a more applied venue where marginal improvements are valued, but for NIPS, it falls short of demonstrating a substantial scientific contribution that advances the field. I recommend rejection.
