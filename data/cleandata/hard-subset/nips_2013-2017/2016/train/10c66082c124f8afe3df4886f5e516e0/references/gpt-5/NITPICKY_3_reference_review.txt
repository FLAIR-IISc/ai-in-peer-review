Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of WMD that learns (i) a linear transform A on the word embedding space (equivalently a PSD Mahalanobis ground metric) and (ii) a global nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The supervision is injected via an NCA-style stochastic nearest-neighbor loss over document distances. To avoid the cubic complexity of exact WMD and to obtain differentiability, the authors use the entropic OT (Sinkhorn) relaxation and take gradients using the relaxed optimal transport T*λ and dual potentials (u, v). They initialize A by running NCA on word-centroid representations (S-WCD) and further reduce computation via mini-batches and a WCD-based neighbor shortlist. Experiments on eight datasets compare to many classical representations (BOW/TF-IDF/LSI/LDA/mSDA), WMD/WCD, and metric-learning methods; S-WMD is reported to achieve the best average rank and win on 5/8 datasets.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on WMD (Kusner et al.) and entropic OT (Cuturi). It is closely related to learning ground metrics for EMD/OT (Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al. supervised EMD learning) and to classification losses based on OT (Frogner et al.). The combination of entropic OT with an NCA loss to learn a Mahalanobis ground metric for text, plus global histogram reweighting, is a reasonable but incremental extension.

Strengths
- Sound high-level idea: learning an OT ground metric directly in the word-embedding space is natural and often more scalable than learning document representations; using entropic OT gives differentiability and practical runtimes.
- Sensible engineering: S-WCD initialization, neighbor shortlisting via WCD, and mini-batching are practical and likely effective.
- Potentially useful in practice: global word importance can mimic an IDF-like effect while remaining task-specific; learning a low-rank A is attractive.
- Empirical scope appears broad, with many baselines considered.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness
- Gradient w.r.t. w (Eq. 10) appears incorrect. For d̃a = (w ∘ da)/(w^T da), the Jacobian ∂d̃a/∂w yields a factor 1/(w^T da) for both terms: ∂D/∂w = (α* ∘ da)/(w^T da) − (α*^T d̃a) da/(w^T da) + same for β, db. The first denominator is missing in the paper’s Eq. (10). This is not a minor oversight; it changes the update magnitudes and possibly the direction.
- Constraints on w are unspecified. Since d̃ must be a valid histogram, w should be constrained nonnegative (w ≥ 0) and probably regularized (e.g., L1/entropy). No projection or parameterization (e.g., softplus) is described.
- Scale/regularization of A is unspecified. Without constraints (e.g., tr(M)=const, Frobenius penalty, or det(A)=1), NCA is sensitive to scaling, and learning may degenerate (either collapse or blow up distances). No regularization hyperparameters are discussed.
- The derivation of dual potentials from Sinkhorn is imprecise. α*λ and β*λ are defined up to an additive constant. The paper subtracts “log(u)>1 p 1”, which is dimensionally unclear (“p” re-used, and 1/p factor appears arbitrary). A principled centering (subtract mass-weighted means) should be stated cleanly; otherwise gradients may be biased.
- Notational inconsistencies: p is used for the Lp exponent, the dimensionality of the all-ones vector, and (elsewhere) for the number of unique words; q is also used for the same. This makes complexity and derivations difficult to follow.
- Complexity claims are questionable. Computing ∑ij Tij(xi−xj)(xi−xj)^T naively is O(d^2|supp(T)|); with |supp(T)|≈q^2, this is O(d^2 q^2), not O(d^2(p+r)) as stated. This matters for large vocabularies/doc lengths.

Experimental rigor
- Anonymity is violated (author footnotes and a public GitHub URL), which is unacceptable for a NeurIPS submission.
- Hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian optimization is claimed, but the protocol is unclear: are validation splits used strictly on training data? Is k in kNN tuned? The LDA baseline is trained “transductively” on train+test, which contaminates evaluation. These choices can unfairly affect comparisons.
- λ is fixed to 10 without sensitivity analysis; the effect of neighbor shortlist size N and batch size B is not studied. No ablations are provided to quantify the contribution of A vs w vs initialization vs entropic relaxation.
- The paper references tables/figures, but the draft contains broken references and even a stray local file path. As written, it is impossible to verify several claims.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typographical and formatting issues: “.2 Optimization,” broken equations, “word2vec [5, 21?],” inconsistent notations (S-WMD vs SWMD), “th Conference,” and many incomplete references.
- Missing important implementation details: rank r selection; whether M=A^T A is enforced PSD; projection onto constraints; stopping criteria; step-size schedules; how nearest-neighbor sets Na are chosen per iteration; exact k used in kNN.

Originality
- The core components (learned Mahalanobis ground metric, entropic OT, NCA objective, global histogram weighting) are all known; the contribution is a particular synthesis for text. Re-weighting histograms is very close to learning IDF-like global weights. Novelty is moderate.

Significance
- If fully correct and reproducible, the approach could be practically useful for supervised text distances and might improve over WMD in many settings. However, the technical and presentation issues significantly undermine confidence and reproducibility.

Pros
- Sensible and potentially impactful idea; practical speedups; promising empirical results.
- Uses supervision directly on distances without re-learning document embeddings.

Cons
- Anonymity violation.
- Apparent error in the gradient w.r.t. w; unclear dual potential handling.
- Missing constraints/regularization and key implementation details.
- Notational/formatting problems throughout; some references incomplete/incorrect.
- Questionable fairness in baselines (transductive LDA; unclear tuning protocol).

Recommendation
Reject. While the idea is promising and the empirical story is potentially strong, the draft has too many technical and presentation flaws for NeurIPS: a likely gradient error, missing constraints and regularization, ambiguous complexity, numerous clarity issues, and a clear anonymity violation. I encourage the authors to fix derivations (especially Eq. 10), enforce and describe constraints (w ≥ 0; PSD M; scale regularization), clean up notation and proofs, provide rigorous and fair experimental protocols with ablations/sensitivity analyses, and resubmit.
