Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of WMD for document comparison. It learns (i) a linear transformation A of the pre-trained word embedding (e.g., word2vec) to adapt the ground metric, and (ii) a per-word importance vector w to reweight document histograms. The learning objective minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss (NCA-style) where neighborhood probabilities are defined via the (supervised) WMD between documents. To make optimization tractable, the authors replace exact OT with the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance and exploit its scaling vectors to obtain approximate dual variables, enabling gradients w.r.t. both A and w. Initialization is done by running NCA on word centroids (S-WCD), and training is accelerated with mini-batches and neighbor filtering via fast WCD. Across 8 datasets and 26 baselines (including unsupervised WMD/WCD; TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA; and supervised metric learners like NCA/LMNN/ITML on those representations), S-WMD attains the best kNN accuracy on 5/8 datasets and the best average rank.

This work builds on: WMD (Kusner et al.), Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi), and supervised metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML). Related supervised EMD ground-metric learning in vision exists (e.g., Wang & Guibas; Cuturi & Avis; Wang et al.), but to my knowledge this is the first to bring supervised OT-ground metric learning to text with word embeddings and to jointly learn histogram reweighting with an NCA objective, plus a practical gradient computation via Sinkhorn dual variables.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is clear: supervised ground metric via A, and reweighted histograms via w, optimized with an NCA loss. Using entropy-regularized OT is appropriate for speed and differentiability; leveraging Sinkhorn scaling to approximate dual variables to differentiate through the reweighting is a neat and justified trick. The gradient for A follows standard results for OT ground metrics; the gradient for w via dual variables is reasonable, albeit approximate.
- Empirical support: The evaluation is broad (8 datasets) and careful: many baselines, supervised/unsupervised, Bayesian hyperparameter tuning, and some analysis (visualizations, training time). The reported gains are substantial on several datasets and average rank is best.
- Caveats:
  - There is no ablation showing the separate contributions of A vs w (e.g., learning A only, w only). This would clarify which component drives gains.
  - The approximation quality from using relaxed dual variables for ∂/∂w is not analyzed beyond stating λ=10 is a good trade-off; no sensitivity study on λ.
  - Fairness/strength of baselines: supervised baselines are PCA-reduced to 200 dims “if necessary,” which may disadvantage some; LDA is trained transductively (not entirely standard), though that generally helps baselines.
  - No statistical significance tests are reported; only standard errors across splits on some datasets.
  - Convergence properties and regularization are not discussed; constraints on w (positivity) are implied but not explicitly stated. Without constraints w could, in principle, be negative or ill-conditioned; in practice normalization may mitigate but this should be clarified.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, with clear motivation, problem setup, and algorithmic details. Pseudocode helps. The complexity discussion is useful.
- Minor issues: There are a few typos/formatting glitches (e.g., section “.2 Optimization,” indices p vs n, the expression for α*, β* with “1p” should be clearly dimensioned). The connection between Sinkhorn scaling and dual potentials could benefit from a short derivation or explicit citation to Cuturi & Peyré on the smoothed dual.
- Reproducibility: The code availability is a plus. Some hyperparameters are specified (batch size 32, N=200 neighbors, λ=10), but a more explicit list of training settings and the value of k in kNN would help replication.

Originality
- The combination is novel: applying supervised metric learning to the OT ground cost for text with pre-trained embeddings, jointly learning per-word reweighting, and using Sinkhorn dual variables for efficient gradients. Prior supervised EMD learning did not jointly reweight histograms nor target text/word-embedding settings. The NCA-based objective in OT space is an original and practical choice. The fast training scheme using WCD for neighbor filtering is also a pragmatic contribution.

Significance
- If robust, this is an impactful contribution: WMD has been influential, and a supervised variant that consistently improves kNN accuracy across diverse datasets is likely to be used. The approach leverages large unsupervised embeddings while tailoring distances to labels, addressing a common gap in text similarity. The method is general and could extend to other OT problems or to modern contextual embeddings.
- Limitations: The evaluation is exclusively kNN; showing improvements in other tasks (e.g., retrieval, clustering quality, or as a component in larger systems) would strengthen the case. Computationally, although Sinkhorn reduces complexity, scaling to very long documents or very large corpora may still be challenging.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Please clarify constraints and any regularization on w (positivity, norm). Do you use projection to enforce w ≥ 0?
- Provide an ablation: A-only, w-only, and full S-WMD. Compare learned w to IDF.
- Report sensitivity to λ and neighbor set size N; show runtime/accuracy trade-offs.
- State k used in kNN and how it was tuned; ensure all supervised baselines are tuned comparably (including with/without PCA).
- Discuss whether learning a non-linear transform (e.g., low-rank plus non-linearity) changes results; or comment on overfitting control.

Pros
- Clear and well-motivated formulation; principled use of OT and NCA.
- Efficient and clever gradient computation via Sinkhorn dual variables; practical training heuristics.
- Strong empirical results across many datasets and baselines; open-source code.
- Joint learning of ground metric and histogram weights is novel and useful.

Cons
- Missing ablations for component contributions and sensitivity analyses (λ, N).
- Limited discussion of constraints/regularization on w; potential degeneracies not analyzed.
- Evaluation confined to kNN; broader utility could be demonstrated.
- Minor clarity/notation issues.

Overall recommendation
Leaning accept. The paper is technically sound, original in its combination of ideas, and demonstrates meaningful gains with reasonable efficiency. Addressing the noted empirical ablations and clarifications would strengthen it further, but as it stands it meets NeurIPS quality, clarity (with minor fixes), originality, and significance criteria.
