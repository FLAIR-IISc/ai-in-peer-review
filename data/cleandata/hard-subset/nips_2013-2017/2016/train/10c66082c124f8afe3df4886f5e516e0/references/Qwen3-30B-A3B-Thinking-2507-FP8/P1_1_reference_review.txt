

# Peer Review: "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

## Summary

This paper proposes a supervised learning approach to improve the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) for document classification tasks. The authors introduce Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), which learns a linear transformation of word embeddings (A) and a word importance vector (w) to optimize document distances based on class labels. The key insight is to incorporate supervision into the WMD framework, which was previously unsupervised, by minimizing a stochastic version of leave-one-out classification error. The authors leverage the Sinkhorn distance to efficiently approximate the optimal transport problem, making the method computationally feasible. The approach is evaluated across 8 diverse document datasets against 26 baseline methods, demonstrating state-of-the-art results on 5/8 datasets.

The paper builds on the WMD framework introduced by Kusner et al. (2015) and extends it by incorporating supervision through metric learning, similar to Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). The work is well-positioned in the literature, with proper citation of related work including Cuturi's Sinkhorn distance and previous metric learning approaches.

## Strengths

**Quality:**
- The paper is technically sound with clear mathematical formulations of the problem and solution.
- The authors provide thorough theoretical derivations, including gradients for the loss function.
- The experimental design is comprehensive, evaluating the method across 8 diverse datasets with multiple baselines.
- The paper includes a detailed ablation study comparing S-WMD with its initialization (S-WMD init.) and demonstrates the value of the supervised component.
- The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., performance on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON datasets) and provide insightful analysis.

**Clarity:**
- The paper is well-organized with logical flow from problem statement to methodology to results.
- Mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained throughout.
- The experimental setup is clearly described, including dataset details, baselines, and evaluation metrics.
- The visualization of learned word weights (Figure 2) effectively demonstrates the model's ability to identify discriminative words.
- The pseudo-code (Algorithm 1) provides a clear implementation guide.

**Originality:**
- The core idea of incorporating supervision into WMD is novel and addresses a gap in the literature (WMD was previously unsupervised).
- The combination of optimal transport with metric learning represents a novel technical contribution.
- The authors properly position their work relative to related literature, citing Kusner et al.'s WMD and Cuturi's Sinkhorn distance.
- The paper's approach to handling the computational complexity of WMD through relaxation is well-motivated and novel.

**Significance:**
- The results demonstrate clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods across multiple datasets.
- The method provides a principled way to incorporate label information into the WMD framework.
- The computational efficiency (Table 3) makes the method practical for real-world applications.
- The work has potential for broader application beyond document classification (e.g., to other text processing tasks).

## Weaknesses

**Quality:**
- The paper doesn't fully explore the impact of the hyperparameter λ in the Sinkhorn distance. While they mention λ=10 as a good trade-off, a more thorough analysis of how λ affects performance would strengthen the paper.
- The analysis of why S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON datasets could be more detailed. The paper mentions "poor initialization" for CLASSIC but doesn't explore this thoroughly.
- The paper doesn't provide a detailed comparison of the computational complexity with other metric learning methods beyond the theoretical analysis.

**Clarity:**
- The initialization step (Section 3.2) could be better explained. The connection between WCD and NCA is somewhat unclear, and it's not fully evident why this initialization approach is beneficial.
- The relationship between the relaxed dual variables (α*λ, β*λ) and the dual problem could be explained more clearly.
- The paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the Sinkhorn relaxation is appropriate for the supervised WMD problem.

**Originality:**
- While the approach is novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate it from Wang et al. (2015) on "Supervised Earth Mover's Distance Learning," which also learns a metric within EMD. The authors mention this work in Section 5 but don't explicitly contrast their method (which includes histogram re-weighting) with this prior work.
- The paper doesn't explore whether their approach could be extended to other types of embeddings beyond word2vec (e.g., contextual embeddings like BERT), which would be an interesting direction for future work.

**Significance:**
- While the paper demonstrates improvements on 5/8 datasets, the absolute error reductions on the other 3 datasets aren't quantified in sufficient detail (e.g., how much worse is S-WMD compared to the best method on BBCSPORT?).
- The paper doesn't discuss the practical implications of the method for large-scale document classification systems beyond the experimental results.

## Recommendation

**Accept** - This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document representation and metric learning. The method is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear improvements over existing approaches. The computational efficiency and the comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets make the work particularly compelling. The paper is well-written and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by incorporating supervision into the WMD framework.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel integration of supervision into WMD, addressing a previously unsupervised approach
- Rigorous evaluation across 8 diverse datasets with 26 baselines
- Theoretical soundness with clear derivations of gradients and loss function
- Practical computational efficiency (Table 3)
- Insightful visualization of learned word weights (Figure 2)

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited analysis of hyperparameter λ in the Sinkhorn distance
- Some aspects of the initialization process could be clarified
- Not sufficiently differentiated from Wang et al. (2015) on supervised EMD learning

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a brief analysis of how the hyperparameter λ affects performance (e.g., a small table showing accuracy vs. λ for a representative dataset).

2. Expand the discussion of why S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON, particularly comparing to the initialization (S-WMD init.) to better understand the impact of training.

3. More explicitly contrast the method with Wang et al. (2015), highlighting how the histogram re-weighting provides additional flexibility not present in their approach.

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the method might generalize to contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT) as an interesting future direction.

5. Clarify the initialization process in Section 3.2 with a more detailed explanation of why learning A using NCA on WCD is a good initialization strategy.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation and metric learning by successfully incorporating supervision into the Word Mover's Distance framework. The authors demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods across multiple datasets while maintaining computational efficiency. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important gap in the literature. While there are some minor areas for improvement, the paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

The work is particularly relevant to NIPS readers interested in metric learning, optimal transport, and natural language processing. The combination of these areas represents a compelling intersection of current research trends in machine learning. The authors have made a clear contribution that will likely be of interest to the broader machine learning community.
