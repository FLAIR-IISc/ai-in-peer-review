## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document similarity. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of word embeddings *and* re-weight word frequencies, minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out classification error under the WMD metric. Critically, the authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering. They demonstrate strong empirical performance on eight datasets, consistently outperforming numerous baselines including unsupervised WMD and other metric learning approaches.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of work in document distance metrics (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA), the recent advancements in WMD by Kusner et al., and the broader field of metric learning. It correctly positions itself as bridging the gap between unsupervised document representation learning and supervised metric learning, addressing the limitations of both. The authors are thorough in citing relevant work in optimal transport, particularly Cuturi’s work on the Sinkhorn distance.  The connection to Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) is also well-articulated, and provides a clear motivation for the proposed loss function. The related work section is comprehensive and demonstrates a good understanding of the landscape.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Learning a metric *within* the WMD framework, while simultaneously re-weighting word frequencies, is a genuinely novel contribution. Most metric learning approaches focus on transforming the document representation itself, whereas S-WMD smartly operates on the word embeddings, preserving the benefits of pre-trained embeddings.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The application of the Sinkhorn distance is a key innovation. Addressing the cubic complexity of WMD with a scalable approximation is crucial for making supervised learning practical.  The probabilistic filtering technique further enhances efficiency.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides a compelling empirical evaluation, demonstrating superiority over a large number of strong baselines across diverse datasets. The comprehensive comparison (26 baselines!) strengthens the claim of improved performance.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details.  The explanations of the Sinkhorn distance and the gradient computations are particularly helpful.
* **Good Analysis & Ablation:** The inclusion of results for S-WMD after initialization (S-WMD init.) allows to understand the contribution of the full training process. The visualization of learned word weights adds valuable insight.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning, the sensitivity of the results to these parameters isn’t fully explored.  A more detailed ablation study focusing on the impact of λ (entropy regularization parameter) and the nearest neighbor size (N) would be valuable.
* **Squared Euclidean Distance:** The choice of the squared Euclidean distance within the optimal transport problem seems somewhat arbitrary. While justified, a discussion of the potential impact of using other distance metrics (e.g., L1 distance) could be beneficial.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:**  While the authors discuss the computational complexity, a more detailed analysis of scalability to extremely large datasets (e.g., billions of documents) would be helpful.  The current description is largely asymptotic and doesn’t provide concrete guidance for practical applications with massive datasets.
* **Potential for Local Optima:** Given the non-convex nature of the loss function, the paper doesn't delve into the risk of getting stuck in local optima. Exploring different initialization strategies or optimization algorithms might be warranted.
* **Interpretation of Word Weights:** While the visualization of word weights is informative, a deeper interpretation of *why* certain words are upweighted for specific tasks would be interesting.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* This paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to supervised document distance learning.
* The proposed method demonstrably improves upon state-of-the-art results on a variety of datasets.
* The computational challenges are addressed effectively with a clever application of the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering.
* The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and thoroughly evaluated.
* The contributions are significant and likely to be impactful in the field of information retrieval and document analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The sensitivity to hyperparameters could potentially limit the practical applicability of the method.
*  The lack of a detailed scalability analysis might raise concerns for applications involving extremely large datasets.

**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The contributions are significant, the technical approach is innovative and well-executed, and the empirical results are compelling. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. This paper represents a substantial advance in the field of document distance metrics and has the potential to be highly influential.
