

# Peer Review: "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

## Summary
This paper proposes "Supervised Word Mover's Distance" (S-WMD), an extension of Kusner et al.'s WMD that incorporates supervision to learn a document distance metric. S-WMD learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to make WMD distances align with class labels. The authors leverage Sinkhorn distance for computational efficiency and adapt NCA's loss function to the WMD framework. They demonstrate S-WMD's superiority over 26 baselines across 8 document classification tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Practical Contribution:** The paper addresses a genuine need in document distance learning - the unsupervised nature of WMD limits its performance for specific classification tasks. The authors successfully demonstrate that supervision significantly improves performance across most datasets.

2. **Computational Efficiency:** The use of Sinkhorn distance (entropy-regularized optimal transport) to approximate the WMD computation is well-justified and well-executed. The batch gradient descent with probabilistic filtering of nearest neighbors makes the method practical for large datasets.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across 8 diverse datasets with 26 baselines is thorough and well-designed. The results consistently show S-WMD outperforming unsupervised baselines and most supervised alternatives, with particularly strong improvements on smaller document datasets.

4. **Clear Technical Development:** The paper provides a well-structured derivation of the gradients for the loss function, which is crucial for the method's implementation. The initialization strategy using S-WCD is reasonable and well-motivated.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Theoretical Underpinnings:** The paper lacks theoretical analysis of why supervision should improve WMD. While the empirical results are strong, the authors don't provide conditions under which supervision will help, or when unsupervised WMD might suffice. The claim that S-WMD "outperforms all other 26 methods" on average would be stronger with statistical significance testing.

2. **Related Work Differentiation:** The paper cites Wang et al. [35] as "most similar" but doesn't adequately distinguish their contribution. Wang et al. also learn a metric within the EMD framework with histogram re-weighting, but they use a different ground distance (not based on word embeddings). The paper should more clearly explain how their approach differs from Wang et al., especially regarding the use of pre-trained word embeddings.

3. **Limited Analysis of Learned Weights:** The visualization of top words with largest weights on 20NEWS is intriguing but superficial. The authors don't analyze how these weights relate to class semantics or document content across multiple datasets. Without deeper analysis, it's hard to understand what the weights are actually capturing.

4. **Overfitting Concerns:** The paper doesn't address potential overfitting, especially given the small size of some datasets (e.g., CLASSIC has only 1,800 documents). The authors should provide details about their validation procedures and discuss whether the improvements generalize across different data splits.

5. **Complexity Claims:** While the paper claims "comparable or even faster" training times than baseline methods, it lacks detailed complexity comparisons with these baselines. A more thorough analysis of computational trade-offs would strengthen this claim.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable practical contribution to document distance learning, demonstrating that supervision can significantly improve WMD. The technical execution is sound, and the empirical results are compelling. However, the paper falls short in theoretical grounding, differentiation from related work, and depth of analysis of the learned components.

The method is well-suited for document classification tasks where semantic similarity matters, and the computational improvements make it practical for real-world applications. The paper's main limitation is that it doesn't represent a substantial theoretical advance, but rather a practical extension of existing techniques.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important practical problem with a well-executed solution that shows consistent empirical improvements. The minor revisions requested below would significantly strengthen the paper.

## Required Revisions

1. Add a brief theoretical analysis section discussing conditions under which supervision should improve WMD performance.

2. Expand the related work section to more clearly differentiate from Wang et al. [35], particularly regarding the use of word embeddings versus other ground distances.

3. Include statistical significance testing for the main results (e.g., using paired t-tests across datasets).

4. Provide deeper analysis of the learned word importance weights across multiple datasets, not just 20NEWS.

5. Clarify the computational complexity claims with more detailed comparisons to baseline methods.

The paper has strong empirical results and addresses a practical problem in text classification. With these revisions, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
