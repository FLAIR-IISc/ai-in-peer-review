Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of WMD that learns (i) a linear transformation A of pre-trained word embeddings and (ii) nonnegative word-importance weights w to reweight document histograms. The learned metric feeds into the optimal transport formulation underlying WMD. To make training tractable, the authors optimize an NCA-style stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective while approximating the transport via entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distances; they derive gradients by using the relaxed transport T* and approximate dual potentials from the Sinkhorn scalings. Training is further sped up via an initialization using NCA on word centroids (WCD), mini-batching, and pruning neighborhoods by WCD. Experiments on 8 datasets report kNN error reductions compared to 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised metric learning on various document representations), with S-WMD winning on 5/8 datasets and best average rank.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on WMD (Kusner et al. 2015) and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi 2013). It is related to metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and to learning ground metrics for EMD (Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.), extending these by (a) learning A within the embedding space rather than on document representations and (b) jointly learning a word-importance reweighting. The optimization objective follows NCA’s stochastic kNN formulation; the use of Sinkhorn for gradients is consistent with recent OT literature.

Strengths
- Clear and reasonably motivated objective: learn a supervised ground cost for document OT where supervision can shape semantic similarity beyond unsupervised embeddings.
- Pragmatic training recipe: Sinkhorn-based differentiable surrogate, NCA loss, WCD-based initialization and neighbor pruning; complexity is discussed and appears practical for medium-scale problems.
- Empirical breadth: 8 datasets, numerous baselines including both unsupervised and metric learning variants. Reported improvements are frequent and nontrivial; code availability is noted.
- Technique may be broadly useful: the idea of learning A and w over embeddings is conceptually simple and could transfer to other OT-based NLP distances.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical mismatch and missing guarantees: training optimizes an entropy-regularized surrogate, while the method is framed as learning WMD. The paper does not clarify whether test-time distances use exact WMD or the regularized distance with the learned parameters. There is no analysis of how λ (set to 10) affects the learned metric or of the consistency between relaxed training and the evaluation metric.
- Insufficient constraints/regularization on w and A: the histogram reweighting requires w ≥ 0 to remain in the simplex; this constraint and how it is enforced during optimization are not specified. There is no explicit regularization (e.g., L2, trace norm) or early stopping criterion described, raising overfitting concerns, especially with NCA’s known tendency to overfit.
- Neighbor pruning could bias learning: restricting gradients to WCD-nearest neighbors assumes that WCD correlates well with the learned supervised metric. This may exclude informative negatives/positives and trap optimization in poor local minima. No ablation quantifies the trade-off between speed and accuracy for this pruning.
- Evaluation scope and fairness: all results are for kNN classification. Many text classification tasks are dominated by linear classifiers (e.g., SVM/logistic on TF-IDF) or neural baselines; these are not reported. Some baselines are trained transductively (LDA on train+test), which can complicate fairness. Feature curation for mSDA (keeping top 20%) may handicap that method. There is no ablation separating the gains from A vs. w vs. Sinkhorn training, nor sensitivity to λ, batch size, N, or k.
- Clarity/technical details: several derivations are sketched but lack precision (e.g., using envelope theorem with the relaxed problem is reasonable but not discussed; the formulas for dual potentials from u,v deserve citation/derivation in the supervised setting). Some notation typos and missing details (dimension of A and r, positivity constraints, learning rate schedules).

Quality
Technically plausible and mostly sound at a high level, but important details are under-specified. Claims are largely empirical; without ablations and stronger baselines, it is hard to assess where the gains originate and how robust they are. The reliance on approximations (Sinkhorn, neighbor pruning) is sensible but needs more careful validation.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, but sections 3–3.1 need clearer statements of assumptions (w ≥ 0), what is used at test time, and how λ and other hyperparameters are chosen. Some references have placeholders and minor typographical issues. Including algorithmic pseudocode is helpful; however, complexity expressions could be justified more carefully.

Originality
Moderately novel. Learning a ground metric for EMD is not new; the contributions here are applying it in the word-embedding space for WMD with NCA loss and adding histogram reweighting. The use of Sinkhorn to make training efficient is now standard but appropriate. The combination is reasonable and practically useful, though incremental relative to prior OT + metric learning work.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners who rely on OT-based document distances and kNN retrieval/classification. However, the significance would be stronger with broader comparisons (beyond kNN), clearer robustness analyses, and demonstration at larger scales.

Questions for the authors
- Are w constrained to be nonnegative? How is this enforced during optimization?
- What distance is used at test time: exact WMD with learned A,w, or the Sinkhorn-regularized distance? If the former, what is the runtime impact?
- Sensitivity to λ and to the neighbor pruning parameter N? Please include ablations.
- What regularization is applied to A and w? Any early stopping or validation strategy to prevent overfitting?
- Can you report ablations for “A only,” “w only,” and “both,” and a comparison to training with exact WMD on small datasets?
- Please compare against strong non-kNN classifiers (logistic/SVM on TF-IDF; simple neural baselines) to contextualize the gains.

Pros
- Practical, efficient training procedure for supervised OT on texts
- Consistent improvements over many baselines; code provided
- Conceptually simple and potentially extensible

Cons
- Missing constraints/regularization details; potential overfitting
- Limited evaluation scope (only kNN); fairness concerns for some baselines
- Approximation-vs-evaluation mismatch not addressed; few ablations

Overall rating: 6 (weak accept if the above issues are clarified; otherwise borderline). The idea is practical and appears to improve over established baselines, but stronger experimental rigor and clearer methodological details are needed to fully endorse. Confidence: medium.
