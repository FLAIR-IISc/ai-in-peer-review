

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an innovative extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates task-specific supervision to learn a more effective document distance metric. The authors propose learning both a linear transformation of word embeddings (A) and word importance weights (w), while leveraging the Sinkhorn distance relaxation to make the computation feasible. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over 26 baselines across 8 document classification datasets, with S-WMD achieving the lowest test error on 5 out of 8 datasets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Integration of Supervision with WMD:** The paper makes a bold contribution by extending the unsupervised WMD framework to incorporate task-specific supervision. This addresses a key limitation of prior work, which has been entirely unsupervised. The idea of learning word-specific importance weights is particularly insightful and aligns with the intuition that different words may be more or less relevant for different classification tasks. This represents a clear conceptual advance over existing approaches.

2. **Practical and Efficient Approach:** The authors cleverly leverage the Sinkhorn distance relaxation to transform the cubic-time WMD computation into a quadratic-time problem, making the supervised learning feasible. This is a significant practical contribution that enables application to real-world document classification tasks.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper includes an extensive evaluation across 8 datasets, 26 baselines, and multiple metrics. The results consistently show S-WMD outperforming unsupervised baselines and many supervised alternatives, with particularly strong results on datasets with smaller documents (e.g., 42% improvement over TF-IDF+NCA on CLASSIC).

4. **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides solid theoretical derivations for the gradients of both the linear transformation and word weights, and clearly connects their approach to prior work in metric learning (NCA, LMNN, etc.).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Why Supervision Helps:** While the paper shows S-WMD outperforms unsupervised baselines, it doesn't deeply analyze *why* supervision helps. Understanding which datasets particularly benefit from supervision and why would provide deeper insights into the method's limitations and potential.

2. **Comparison to Recent Advances:** The paper compares against 26 baselines but doesn't include recent advances in contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT). It would be valuable to see how S-WMD compares to these state-of-the-art approaches, particularly since they've become the new standard for many NLP tasks.

3. **Initialization Sensitivity:** The paper acknowledges the loss function is nonconvex and depends on initialization, but doesn't thoroughly explore how sensitive the final performance is to different initialization strategies. The authors use a clever initialization via S-WCD, but it's unclear how robust this is across different datasets.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of document distance learning with a novel and well-executed approach. The authors have successfully extended the WMD framework to incorporate supervision in a way that addresses a key limitation of prior work, and their method demonstrates clear advantages across multiple datasets.

The novelty of combining supervision with WMD, the efficiency of the Sinkhorn-based relaxation, and the comprehensive evaluation strongly support the paper's contribution. While the analysis of *why* supervision helps could be deeper and the comparison to recent contextual embedding approaches would strengthen the evaluation, these limitations don't undermine the paper's core contribution.

The paper aligns well with NeurIPS' focus on novel and impactful approaches to machine learning problems. It represents a clear conceptual advance over existing work and provides a new direction for document distance learning that could influence future research.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a novel approach that is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. The authors have successfully extended the WMD framework to incorporate supervision in a way that addresses a key limitation of prior work, and their method shows strong performance across multiple datasets. The novelty and impact of this work clearly outweigh the minor limitations noted above.
