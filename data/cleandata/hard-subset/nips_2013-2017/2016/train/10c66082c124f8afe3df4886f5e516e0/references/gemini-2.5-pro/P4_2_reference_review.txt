This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) [19], an unsupervised distance metric that computes the minimum cost to "transport" words from one document to another in a pre-trained word embedding space. The authors identify a key limitation of WMD: its unsupervised nature prevents it from adapting to the specific notion of similarity required by a given task.

The core contribution of this paper is to introduce supervision into the WMD framework in two ways. First, it learns a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, effectively learning a Mahalanobis distance that modifies the ground cost of the transport problem. Second, it learns a word-importance vector `w` that re-weights the normalized bag-of-words document representations, allowing the model to emphasize words that are discriminative for the task at hand. The parameters `A` and `w` are learned by minimizing a proxy for the leave-one-out k-NN classification error, using the differentiable loss function from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [15].

A significant technical challenge is the computational complexity and non-differentiability of the original WMD formulation. The authors overcome this by replacing the exact optimal transport problem with its entropy-regularized version, known as the Sinkhorn distance [6]. This relaxation makes the problem strictly convex, differentiable, and solvable much more efficiently (O(q^2) vs O(q^3 log q) where q is the number of unique words). This enables the use of gradient-based optimization. The authors derive the gradients for both `A` and `w` and propose an efficient training scheme involving a clever initialization (learning a metric on the much faster Word Centroid Distance), batch gradient descent, and a nearest-neighbor filtering heuristic to reduce the number of pairwise distance computations. This work connects ideas from word embeddings [22], optimal transport [6, 19], and metric learning [15], and is related to prior work on learning the ground distance for Earth Mover’s Distance in computer vision [35].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality:** The paper presents a highly novel and well-motivated approach. While learning the ground distance for optimal transport has been explored before, its application to text by learning a transformation of the word embedding space is new. Furthermore, the simultaneous learning of word importance weights (`w`) is a powerful and original contribution tailored specifically for text data, providing an extra degree of flexibility that is absent in prior related work. This combination of learning a metric and re-weighting the inputs within a single framework is elegant.

2.  **Quality:** The technical quality of the paper is high. The formulation is sound, and the decision to use the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable surrogate for WMD is a key technical insight that makes the entire learning framework feasible. The gradient derivations appear correct and are thoughtfully presented. The proposed optimization strategy, including the initialization with a supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD), is practical and addresses the non-convex nature of the objective function. The experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough, comparing against 26 strong baselines across 8 diverse datasets. The consistently superior performance of S-WMD demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.

3.  **Significance:** This work has the potential for high impact. It addresses a fundamental problem in text analysis: creating task-specific document similarity measures. By demonstrating state-of-the-art results, S-WMD establishes itself as a powerful tool for kNN-based document classification and likely other retrieval tasks. The successful fusion of concepts from metric learning and optimal transport for natural language processing is a significant contribution that could inspire future research in this direction. The availability of code further increases its potential for adoption by the community.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the background section is concise and informative, and the proposed method is explained with sufficient detail. The inclusion of pseudo-code (Algorithm 1) and visualizations (Figures 1 and 2) significantly aids in understanding both the algorithm and its results.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Scalability:** While the authors have made commendable efforts to improve efficiency, the computational complexity still scales quadratically with the number of unique words in a document pair (O(q^2)). This could remain a bottleneck for tasks involving very long documents, where `q` can be large. A brief discussion on the practical limits of document length would be beneficial.

2.  **Choice of Ground Metric:** The formulation in Eq. (5) uses the squared Euclidean distance `||A(xi - xj)||_2^2` as the ground metric. This choice is convenient for deriving the gradient with respect to `A`. However, the original WMD paper [19] found the standard L2 norm (not squared) to be effective. The authors note that on the CLASSIC dataset, this choice might be suboptimal. A short justification for using the squared L2 norm and a discussion on whether the framework could accommodate other norms would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method involves several hyperparameters, notably the regularization parameter `λ` in the Sinkhorn approximation. The paper mentions `λ=10` provides a good trade-off between speed and accuracy, but it is unclear how this was determined or how sensitive the model's performance is to this choice. A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity would increase the paper's completeness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant method for supervised document metric learning that effectively combines ideas from multiple fields.
*   Technically sound, leveraging modern techniques in optimal transport to create a tractable and differentiable learning problem.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance through a comprehensive and rigorous experimental evaluation against a large number of competitive baselines.
*   The work is significant, addressing an important problem and providing a powerful new tool for practitioners and researchers.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The computational complexity, while greatly improved over a naive approach, may still limit applicability to very long documents.
*   The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of certain design choices (e.g., the squared Euclidean norm) and hyperparameter sensitivity.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field that is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.
