Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised metric-learning extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for comparing text documents. WMD frames document distance as an optimal transport (OT) problem over word embeddings, achieving strong kNN performance without learning document representations. S-WMD introduces supervision by learning (i) a linear transformation A of the word embedding space and (ii) a nonnegative word-importance reweighting vector w that rescales bag-of-words histograms before computing OT. The learning objective follows the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) stochastic leave-one-out formulation, optimizing a soft kNN loss over document distances computed via OT with the ground cost induced by A and the reweighted histograms via w.

Because exact OT is cubic in document vocabulary size, the paper employs an entropically regularized OT (Sinkhorn) approximation to both compute transport plans in O(p^2) (p = unique words per document pair) and to obtain approximate dual potentials for gradients. Gradients w.r.t. A are formed by summing transport-weighted quadratic forms in word differences, and gradients w.r.t. w leverage the fact that dual potentials are derivatives of the regularized OT objective w.r.t. the marginals; these are recovered from the Sinkhorn scaling vectors u, v. The optimization is accelerated by (a) initializing A via NCA on Word Centroid Distance (WCD)—yielding a supervised centroid metric (S-WCD)—and (b) batch training with a WCD-based neighbor filter to restrict the softmax normalization to the closest M candidates.

Experiments on 8 datasets against 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised metric learning atop several representations) show that S-WMD achieves the best average rank and the lowest kNN error on most datasets. Visualizations via t-SNE suggest tighter class clusters under S-WMD than WMD. Training times are competitive due to the Sinkhorn-based gradients and neighbor filtering. This work relates to prior NIPS/ICML work on WMD, entropic OT (Sinkhorn), NCA, and supervised EMD for images; its novelty is in learning a supervised OT ground metric and histogram reweighting specialized to text with word embeddings.

Quality
- Methodology: The approach is coherent and well-motivated. Using entropic OT to obtain both approximate transport plans and dual potentials is standard and computationally effective. The stochastic NCA loss is a reasonable surrogate for kNN leave-one-out error.
- Theoretical soundness: The gradients w.r.t. A using the (regularized) transport plan are standard; using Sinkhorn guarantees uniqueness and differentiability. The gradient w.r.t. w via dual potentials is appropriate for the regularized OT objective. One ambiguity is that the loss is written in terms of the unregularized distance DA,w but gradients are computed via the regularized solution; clarifying that the optimized objective is the entropic OT distance (or that a surrogate is used) would improve rigor. Also, squared Euclidean ground costs (‖A(xi−xj)‖^2) yield a transport “cost” that is not strictly a metric; that is acceptable for kNN but worth noting.
- Empirics: The evaluation is broad, includes many strong baselines, and uses Bayesian optimization for hyperparameters. The observed improvements are consistent across datasets. Training-time analysis and visualizations add credibility. Some experimental details could be clearer (e.g., the chosen k for kNN; the value of r; constraints/regularization).

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The background on word embeddings, WMD, entropic OT, and NCA is concise and sufficient. A few notational issues could be improved:
- The symbol p is overloaded (norm power vs. dimensionality of the all-ones vector in the dual expression; also used for number of unique words). Use distinct symbols.
- Explicitly state constraints on w (nonnegativity) and whether projection is used during updates.
- Specify r (the learned embedding dimension), any regularization on A and w, and the chosen k for kNN evaluation.
- Minor formatting issues (e.g., “.2 Optimization”).

Originality
Learning a supervised OT ground metric for document comparison that operates in the word embedding space is novel in the text domain. Prior work has learned EMD ground metrics in vision; combining this with pre-trained word embeddings, histogram reweighting, and NCA-style supervision appears new. Using Sinkhorn duals for gradients is aligned with recent OT literature but is a thoughtful integration here.

Significance
WMD is a widely used baseline for document similarity; a supervised variant that consistently improves kNN classification while remaining efficient is valuable. The approach is general, compatible with pre-trained embeddings, and likely to influence downstream tasks involving document distances (classification, retrieval). The gains are practical and the code availability supports impact.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation; principled incorporation of supervision into WMD via learned A and w.
- Efficient training using entropic OT and neighbor filtering; good empirical runtime.
- Strong empirical performance across diverse datasets and many competitive baselines.
- Sensible initialization (S-WCD) that also performs well on its own.
- Public code enhances reproducibility.

Cons
- Objective/gradient mismatch: the loss is written for unregularized OT but optimized with entropic OT-derived gradients; clarify or justify this approximation quantitatively (e.g., sensitivity to λ).
- Constraints and regularization are underspecified: nonnegativity of w, any norm constraints or penalties on A/w to prevent degenerate scaling and overfitting.
- Limited ablations: no results for learning only A vs only w, sensitivity to λ, batch size, and neighbor set size M/N.
- Evaluation limited to kNN; additional tasks (e.g., linear classifiers on learned features, retrieval metrics) would broaden significance.
- Minor notation/clarity issues (overloaded symbols, missing parameter details).

Suggestions for improvement
- State explicitly that the trained objective is the entropic OT distance; discuss the bias introduced by λ, and provide a sensitivity analysis (including λ→∞ trends if feasible).
- Enforce and report w≥0 (e.g., parameterize w = softplus(z) or project after each step). Consider regularizing w to avoid overemphasizing rare words; report the distribution of learned weights and an ablation learning only A vs only w.
- Add regularization on A (e.g., Frobenius norm) or constrain A to be low-rank to control capacity; report r and its effect.
- Clarify k in kNN, the choice of r, and the metric used in WMD baselines (L2 vs squared L2).
- Provide sensitivity to the neighbor filtering parameters and a brief analysis of computational vs. accuracy trade-offs.
- Consider reporting performance with alternative embeddings and OOV handling.

Overall evaluation
This is a solid, technically sound, and practically useful extension of WMD. The integration of metric learning and entropic OT is well-executed, and the empirical results are strong. Addressing the noted clarity and ablation gaps would further strengthen the contribution.
