### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds upon the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to transport words from one to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. The authors introduce supervision in two ways: 1) by learning a linear transformation `A` of the word embeddings to warp the underlying semantic space, and 2) by learning a word importance vector `w` to re-weight word frequencies based on their relevance to the classification task.

To make this learning problem tractable, the authors propose several key technical contributions. Recognizing that optimizing through the cubic-time WMD is infeasible, they replace it with the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance, which can be solved approximately in quadratic time. This allows for efficient gradient computation. The objective function minimizes a stochastic version of the kNN leave-one-out error, following the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) framework. The optimization is further accelerated by a clever initialization scheme where the transformation `A` is pre-trained on the much faster Word Centroid Distance (WCD), and by using a batch gradient descent strategy that considers only a small subset of nearest neighbors for each document. The authors conduct an extensive empirical evaluation on 8 text classification datasets, demonstrating that S-WMD outperforms 26 competitive baselines on average.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses a significant and natural problem: how to incorporate supervision into the powerful WMD framework. The proposed solution of jointly learning a transformation of the embedding space and word-specific importance weights is elegant and well-motivated. Given the success of WMD, a supervised variant that demonstrably improves performance is a valuable contribution to the field of text analysis and metric learning.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The core technical idea of using the differentiable Sinkhorn distance as a surrogate for WMD is clever and essential for making the proposed learning framework computationally feasible. The combination of this relaxation with the NCA loss function is a novel synthesis of ideas from optimal transport and metric learning.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against a wide array of 26 baselines, including various document representations paired with several metric learning algorithms. S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art kNN classification error on 5 out of 8 datasets and has the best average rank overall, which provides strong evidence for its effectiveness.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising core ideas, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, methodological details, and experimental analysis, which collectively undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript appears to have been prepared with a significant lack of care.
    *   The reference list is improperly formatted, with missing information (year, venue), inconsistent styling, and appears to be a raw dump from a bibliography manager. Reference `[21? ]` contains a literal question mark.
    *   There are numerous typos and formatting errors throughout the text (e.g., "th Conference...", `.2 Optimization`, a double-2 in the norm of Eq. (5) `‖A(xi − xj)‖22`, an absolute local file path for a figure).
    *   Mathematical notation is inconsistent and non-standard. The norm `‖xi − xj‖p2` in Eq. (1) is ambiguous. MATLAB-style notation `./` is used in the text. The symbol `p` is used to denote both a norm and the number of unique words, causing confusion. Section titles containing raw LaTeX like `∂DA,w(da,db)/∂(A,w)` are unconventional.

2.  **Omission of Key Methodological Details:** Several critical details are either missing or glossed over, hindering a full understanding and reproduction of the method.
    *   The paper does not specify any constraints on the word importance vector `w`. If entries of `w` become zero or negative during optimization, the normalization term `w>da` could become non-positive, leading to undefined behavior or numerical instability. This is a serious potential flaw that is not addressed.
    *   The derivation of the dual variables `α∗λ` and `β∗λ` is presented without justification or citation. The expression for these variables includes an unexplained term `p 1`.
    *   The optimization relies on two key approximations: the Sinkhorn regularization `λ` and the nearest neighbor set size `N`. Neither of these hyperparameters is analyzed. The paper simply states fixed values (`λ=10`, `N=200`) without any sensitivity analysis or justification for these choices. The impact of these approximations on the final performance is therefore unknown.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Analysis:**
    *   The paper fails to adequately explain S-WMD's failure cases. On the CLASSIC dataset, the unsupervised WMD outperforms S-WMD. The authors offer speculative reasons ("poor initialization", "squared Euclidean distance... may be suboptimal") without providing any empirical analysis to support these claims. A rigorous paper should investigate why and when its proposed method fails.
    *   The performance of the fully trained S-WMD is often only a marginal improvement over its initialization scheme (`S-WMD init.`), which is equivalent to applying NCA to the Word Centroid Distance. This raises the question of how much of the performance gain is attributable to the complex and costly WMD-based optimization, versus the much simpler supervised WCD approach.

### Justification for Recommendation

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and significant method for supervised document distance learning.
*   The core technical idea of using a relaxed WMD objective for gradient-based metric learning is sound and effective.
*   The method achieves state-of-the-art results on a majority of the tested datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper suffers from an unacceptable number of typos, formatting errors, and presentational flaws, most notably in the reference list. This demonstrates a lack of thoroughness unbecoming of a NIPS publication.
*   Critical methodological details regarding constraints on learned parameters and the derivation of key equations are missing, which compromises the paper's technical clarity and reproducibility.
*   The experimental analysis is incomplete, lacking a proper investigation of the method's failure cases and a sensitivity analysis of crucial hyperparameters.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

While the paper presents a powerful and promising idea, its current execution falls well below the high standards expected for NIPS. The sheer volume of minor flaws—ranging from trivial typos to significant methodological omissions—indicates a rushed and careless preparation. The work has the potential to be an excellent contribution, but it requires a major revision to address the numerous issues in its presentation, methodological exposition, and experimental analysis. As it stands, the paper is not ready for publication.
