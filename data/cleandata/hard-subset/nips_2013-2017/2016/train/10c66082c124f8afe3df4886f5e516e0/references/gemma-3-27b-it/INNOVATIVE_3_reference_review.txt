This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weighting word frequencies. The core idea is compelling: leveraging the power of WMD while incorporating supervision to tailor the distance metric to specific tasks. The authors cleverly address the computational challenges of supervised WMD by utilizing the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering. This is a significant direction, as prior work on document distance, including WMD, has been largely unsupervised. 

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The problem addressed is important – improving document distance metrics is crucial for numerous applications. The proposed approach is novel, moving beyond unsupervised methods to a supervised learning framework for WMD. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of existing literature in metric learning, optimal transport, and word embeddings. The experimental results are impressive, consistently outperforming numerous baselines across a diverse set of datasets (8 datasets, 26 baselines!). The efficiency improvements achieved through the Sinkhorn distance approximation and probabilistic filtering are crucial for making the method practical. The analysis, including the visualization of learned word weights, provides valuable insights. 

However, the paper isn't without weaknesses, though I am willing to overlook them given the novelty. The theoretical justification for the combination of linear transformation and re-weighting isn't deeply explored. While the authors motivate these choices, a more rigorous analysis of their interplay would be beneficial. The reliance on squared Euclidean distance in the optimization (eq. 5) is stated as potentially suboptimal for certain datasets (CLASSIC), but there's limited investigation into alternative distance functions. While the empirical results are strong, a more thorough ablation study, perhaps isolating the impact of the linear transformation versus the re-weighting, would strengthen the analysis. The discussion of hyperparameters, while mentioning Bayesian optimization, lacks specifics about the search space and computational cost. Finally, the paper is densely written, and some sections could benefit from increased clarity and more intuitive explanations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The concept of supervised WMD is a significant departure from existing work and opens up a new research direction.
*   **Strong empirical performance:** The consistent outperformance of numerous baselines provides compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness.
*   **Practicality:** The authors address the computational challenges and demonstrate reasonable training times.
*   **Sound technical foundation:** The use of Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering are well-motivated and effectively implemented.
*   **Potential impact:** The ability to learn task-specific document distances has the potential to significantly improve performance in various applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited theoretical analysis:** The rationale behind the combined linear transformation and re-weighting could be strengthened.
*   **Minor methodological limitations:** The choice of squared Euclidean distance and lack of a comprehensive ablation study.
*   **Dense writing style:** Impacts clarity in some sections.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a highly innovative and promising approach to learning document distances. While there are minor shortcomings in terms of theoretical depth and methodological exploration, the novelty, strong empirical results, and potential impact outweigh these concerns. The authors have successfully addressed a challenging problem with a creative solution, and the work is likely to inspire further research in this area. I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field and **recommend acceptance**. I prioritize the bold, novel direction taken by this paper over minor methodological imperfections.
