**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) [19], an unsupervised distance metric that leverages pre-trained word embeddings to calculate the minimum "cost" of transporting words from one document to another. The authors correctly identify that WMD, being unsupervised, is not optimized for any specific classification task. To address this, S-WMD incorporates supervision by learning two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, which effectively learns a new ground metric for the transport problem, and (2) a word importance vector `w` that re-weighs word frequencies in each document's representation.

The core technical challenge is the computational complexity of optimizing these parameters, as it requires differentiating through the optimal transport problem inherent in WMD. To make this feasible, the authors propose a key innovation: replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized version, known as the Sinkhorn distance [6]. This relaxation makes the objective strongly convex, enabling a fast iterative solver and, critically, allowing for the computation of unique and stable gradients. The authors derive the gradients for both `A` and `w`. The overall loss function is based on Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [15], a differentiable proxy for the k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) classification error. To further improve efficiency, the paper proposes a practical optimization scheme involving a smart initialization using a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD), batch gradient descent, and a nearest-neighbor filtering strategy to reduce the number of pairwise distance computations. The method is evaluated on eight document classification datasets, where it is shown to outperform an extensive set of 26 unsupervised and supervised baselines, achieving state-of-the-art results on the majority of tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a highly novel and significant contribution to the field of text classification and metric learning. It is the first work to successfully introduce supervision into the WMD framework. The idea of learning the ground metric (`A`) of an optimal transport problem jointly with a re-weighting of the marginal distributions (`w`) is an elegant and powerful concept that advances the state of the art in document distance learning. The strong empirical results, demonstrating superior performance over a very comprehensive set of baselines on diverse datasets, underscore the importance and effectiveness of the proposed approach.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The technical execution is excellent. The central challenge of differentiating through the WMD is solved effectively by leveraging the Sinkhorn distance. This is a non-trivial but well-motivated choice that makes the optimization problem tractable. The derivations for the gradients with respect to the transformation matrix `A` and the word weight vector `w` appear correct and are well-explained. The overall optimization algorithm, including the WCD-based initialization and the neighbor-based batching, is a clever and pragmatic solution that addresses the potential computational bottlenecks, making the method practical for real-world use.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and clear. The authors provide a concise yet thorough background on WMD, Sinkhorn distances, and NCA, making the paper accessible even to readers not deeply familiar with all of these concepts. The motivation for each component of the proposed method is clearly articulated, and the algorithmic details are presented with sufficient precision. The extensive experiments are well-structured, and the results are presented clearly in tables and figures, facilitating easy comparison and interpretation.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Ablation Study:** The two main contributions are the learned metric `A` and the word importance weights `w`. The experiments demonstrate the strong performance of learning both jointly. However, the paper would be strengthened by an ablation study that disentangles the contribution of each component. For instance, results for learning only `A` (with `w` fixed to ones) and learning only `w` (with `A` fixed to identity) would provide valuable insight into which component is more impactful and how they interact. While Figure 2 qualitatively shows the learned weights, it does not quantitatively assess their impact on classification accuracy.

2.  **Limitation to Squared Euclidean Distance:** The formulation of S-WMD relies on the squared Euclidean distance (`||A(xi - xj)||_2^2`) in the word embedding space. This choice facilitates the simple gradient update for `A`. However, the original WMD can use other norms (e.g., L1 or L2). The authors briefly mention that this limitation might explain why the unsupervised WMD outperforms S-WMD on the CLASSIC dataset. This is a reasonable limitation, but it would be beneficial to state it more explicitly and perhaps briefly discuss the challenges or potential avenues for extending the framework to other ground metrics.

3.  **Limited Discussion on Hyperparameters:** While the use of Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning is a good practice, the paper provides little discussion on the sensitivity of S-WMD to its own key hyperparameters, particularly the Sinkhorn regularization parameter `λ` and the optimization parameters (batch size `B`, neighbor set size `N`). A brief analysis or discussion of how these parameters affect the trade-off between approximation accuracy, convergence speed, and final performance would enhance the practical value and reproducibility of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pro):**
*   Presents a novel and powerful method for supervised document distance learning.
*   The technical approach for making the optimization tractable is elegant and sound.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance across multiple datasets against an exhaustive list of strong baselines.
*   The paper is very well-written and clear, making a complex topic accessible.
*   The ideas presented are likely to inspire follow-up work in both text analysis and other domains where optimal transport is used.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Con):**
*   The individual contributions of the two learned components (`A` and `w`) are not quantitatively evaluated through an ablation study.
*   The method is restricted to a squared Euclidean ground metric, which may be suboptimal for some tasks.
*   The sensitivity to key hyperparameters of the proposed method is not analyzed.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the approach and the strength of the empirical results, far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are primarily related to a lack of deeper analysis rather than fundamental flaws. The work is technically solid, clearly presented, and advances the state of the art in its field.
