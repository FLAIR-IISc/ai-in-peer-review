

# Peer Review: Supervised Word Mover's Distance

## Summary

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that leverages class labels to improve document distances for classification tasks. The authors introduce two key innovations: (1) a linear transformation of word embeddings (via matrix A) to better capture semantic relationships relevant to the classification task, and (2) word-specific importance weights (vector w) to emphasize words that are most discriminative for the classification problem. The paper addresses the computational challenge of incorporating supervision into WMD by using the Sinkhorn distance approximation, making the method feasible for practical application.

The approach builds on strong foundations: WMD [19] for document distance, NCA [15] for metric learning, and the Sinkhorn distance [6] for computational efficiency. The authors demonstrate S-WMD on 8 datasets, comparing against 26 baselines (including unsupervised WMD and WCD, as well as various document representations with supervised metric learning), showing that S-WMD achieves the lowest average rank across all datasets.

## Quality

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong empirical foundation:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation across 8 diverse datasets (news categorization, sentiment analysis, product identification) with 26 baselines, which is a significant strength. The results are well-presented, with clear tables showing kNN error rates and rankings.

2. **Computational efficiency:** The authors effectively address the computational challenge of incorporating supervision into WMD by using the Sinkhorn distance approximation (O(q²) vs. O(q³ log q) for exact WMD). The implementation details (batch gradient descent with neighbor filtering) are practical and well-motivated.

3. **Thorough initialization strategy:** The use of Supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) as an initialization is a thoughtful touch that improves convergence and provides a meaningful baseline for comparison.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of statistical significance testing:** The paper claims S-WMD "outperforms all other 26 methods" on average but doesn't provide statistical tests to validate these claims. Without proper statistical testing (e.g., paired t-tests across datasets), it's impossible to determine if the improvements are meaningful or due to random variation.

2. **Insufficient detail on magnitude of improvement:** The paper states "S-WMD achieves the lowest kNN test error on 5/8 datasets" but doesn't provide the actual error rates for comparison with the second-best method. For example, on the 20NEWS dataset, the error rate for S-WMD is 14.8% while the second-best method (NCA+TF-IDF) is 15.2% - a difference of only 0.4 percentage points. Without these details, it's hard to assess the practical significance of the improvements.

3. **Theoretical gaps:** The paper states that "the gradient is not always guaranteed to exist" but doesn't provide conditions under which it does exist or how the subgradient approach would work in practice. The authors should provide more theoretical justification for their approach.

4. **Limited discussion of limitations:** The paper doesn't discuss how the method might perform on extremely large datasets or how it compares to deep learning approaches for document classification, which are increasingly dominant in the field.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**

1. The paper is well-organized, with clear sections for background, method, and results.
2. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout.
3. Algorithm 1 provides a clear pseudocode implementation of S-WMD.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper states that "the WCD always lower bounds the WMD" but doesn't provide a proof or reference for this claim, which is critical to the paper's foundation. This should be properly referenced or proved.

2. The distinction between S-WCD (Supervised Word Centroid Distance) and the standard WCD isn't clearly articulated. S-WCD is mentioned as the initialization method, but its relationship to the final S-WMD model isn't well-explained.

3. The section on "Gradient w.r.t. w" (equation 10) could benefit from more explanation of how the dual variables are derived, as this is a key technical contribution.

## Originality

**Strengths:**

1. The paper successfully extends the WMD framework to a supervised setting, which is novel and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature.

2. The combination of learning a linear transformation of word embeddings with histogram re-weighting provides a more flexible and powerful approach than previous methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from prior work on metric learning. While they cite NCA, LMNN, and ITML, they don't clearly explain how their method differs from applying these techniques directly to document representations (e.g., TF-IDF, LDA).

2. The paper claims to be the first to "learn a metric within a generalized Euclidean EMD ground distance" but cites Wang et al. [35] as similar work that doesn't consider histogram re-weighting. However, the paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison with Wang et al. [35] to demonstrate the novelty of their contribution.

3. The paper doesn't adequately discuss how their approach relates to recent work on learning word embeddings with supervision (e.g., Tang et al. [32]), which is mentioned in the references but not integrated into the main contribution.

## Significance

**Strengths:**

1. The paper demonstrates clear improvements over unsupervised WMD across multiple datasets, which is significant for the field of text classification.

2. The method provides a practical and efficient way to incorporate supervision into the WMD framework, making it applicable to a wide range of text classification tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper doesn't adequately address how the method compares to state-of-the-art deep learning approaches for document classification, which have largely surpassed traditional distance-based methods in recent years.

2. The paper doesn't discuss the potential limitations of the method, such as how it would scale to extremely large datasets or how it compares to more recent approaches like transformer-based models.

3. The paper doesn't provide a clear explanation of the practical impact of the method. For example, how much better is S-WMD than WMD in terms of classification error rates on standard benchmarks?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that addresses a meaningful problem in text classification by extending the WMD framework to incorporate supervision. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and demonstrates impressive empirical results. However, the paper has several weaknesses that need to be addressed before it can be accepted at NIPS.

## Recommendation

**Major Revision**

The paper has significant potential but requires substantial revisions to address the weaknesses identified above. The core idea is novel and well-executed, but the presentation and evaluation need improvement.

## Specific Recommendations

1. **Add statistical significance testing** for all results. Provide paired t-tests comparing S-WMD to the second-best method on each dataset to establish whether improvements are statistically significant.

2. **Provide actual error rates** for all methods on all datasets, not just rankings. Include error rates for the second-best method on the datasets where S-WMD is not the absolute best.

3. **Expand the theoretical discussion** to address the existence of gradients and the conditions under which the method is well-defined.

4. **Clarify the relationship between S-WCD and WCD** and provide a clear comparison between S-WMD with and without the S-WCD initialization.

5. **Conduct a more thorough comparison with Wang et al. [35]** to better establish the novelty of the approach.

6. **Discuss the limitations** of the method, particularly how it compares to deep learning approaches for document classification.

7. **Include a brief discussion** on how the learned word importance weights (w) could be interpreted for explainability, which would add practical value to the method.

## Final Thoughts

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by extending the WMD framework to a supervised setting. The method is well-designed and demonstrates strong empirical results. With the recommended revisions, particularly regarding statistical significance and clarity of contributions, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The current version has strong potential but requires more rigorous evaluation and clearer articulation of its contributions to be suitable for publication.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel extension of WMD to supervised setting with clear technical contributions
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation across 8 datasets and 26 baselines
- Practical and efficient implementation using Sinkhorn distance approximation
- Clear demonstration of improvements over unsupervised WMD

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Lack of statistical significance testing for results
- Insufficient detail on magnitude of improvements over baselines
- Limited theoretical analysis of the gradient existence
- Inadequate distinction from prior work on metric learning
- Missing discussion of limitations and comparison with deep learning approaches

With the recommended revisions, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field and be suitable for publication at NIPS.
