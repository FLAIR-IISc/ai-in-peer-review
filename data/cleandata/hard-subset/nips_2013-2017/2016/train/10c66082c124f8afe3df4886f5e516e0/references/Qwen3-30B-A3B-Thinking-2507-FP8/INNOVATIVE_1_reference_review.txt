

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to improve document classification performance. The authors propose learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to make WMD more effective for classification tasks. The key insight is that while WMD leverages pre-trained word embeddings to compute document distances, these embeddings are unsupervised and not optimized for specific classification tasks. By learning a linear transformation and word weights within the WMD framework, S-WMD directly minimizes classification error while maintaining the conceptual advantages of WMD.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it introduces a supervised metric learning approach specifically designed for the WMD framework, which is a departure from traditional metric learning that operates on document representations rather than the word embedding space. Second, the authors cleverly leverage the Sinkhorn distance to efficiently compute gradients, making the method computationally feasible despite the cubic complexity of WMD. Third, they provide a comprehensive experimental evaluation across 8 datasets with 26 baselines, demonstrating consistent improvements over unsupervised WMD and other document representation methods.

The technical contributions are well-motivated and logically presented. The authors properly contextualize their work within the literature on metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and optimal transport (WMD, Sinkhorn distance), clearly differentiating their approach from prior work. The mathematical derivations for the gradient computation are sound, and the algorithm is clearly described. The experimental results are impressive, with S-WMD achieving the lowest error rate on 5/8 datasets and outperforming all 26 baselines on average. The visualization of learned word weights in Figure 2 provides compelling evidence of the method's ability to identify discriminative words for classification.

The paper's strengths are particularly notable in the context of NIPS. The core idea of learning a supervised metric within the WMD framework represents a bold conceptual extension of an important prior work (Kusner et al., 2015). While WMD itself was a significant contribution, this paper takes it further by making it task-specific. The approach is elegant in its simplicity—transforming the word embeddings rather than the documents—and the computational efficiency achieved through Sinkhorn distance makes it practical for real-world applications.

There are minor weaknesses, but they don't detract significantly from the paper's novelty and impact. The method doesn't outperform WMD on all datasets (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON), though the paper provides reasonable explanations for these cases. The theoretical analysis is limited to gradient derivations without convergence guarantees, but this is common in similar metric learning work. The paper could have more thoroughly explored the sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters, but the use of Bayesian optimization for tuning is appropriate.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The results are convincing, and the authors properly acknowledge the limitations of their approach. The method is directly applicable to numerous text classification tasks and represents a meaningful advancement over existing document distance metrics.

From the perspective of novelty and boldness, this paper stands out as a significant contribution. It takes an already influential idea (WMD) and extends it in a novel, principled way that directly addresses a key limitation (unsupervised nature). The method is both theoretically sound and practically effective, with strong empirical results that demonstrate clear advantages over prior work.

While the paper doesn't introduce a completely new mathematical framework, it demonstrates a novel application of existing techniques (optimal transport, metric learning) that leads to meaningful improvements in document classification. This is precisely the kind of work NIPS values: taking existing concepts and applying them in creative, impactful ways to solve important problems.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The novelty of the approach, the strong experimental results, and the elegance of the solution make it a compelling contribution to the field. The minor weaknesses identified are common in applied machine learning papers and don't undermine the paper's overall significance. This work represents a meaningful advance in document representation learning and should be of interest to the NIPS community.
