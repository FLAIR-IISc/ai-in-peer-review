Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) [Kusner et al., ICML’15] for measuring document similarity. WMD computes the optimal transport cost of moving the normalized bag-of-words of one document to another using distances in a pretrained word embedding space (e.g., word2vec). This work introduces two learnable components: (i) a linear transformation A applied to word embeddings (learning a ground metric) and (ii) a word-importance vector w to reweight document histograms before normalization. The parameters are learned by minimizing a Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA)–style stochastic leave-one-out k-NN loss over document distances.

Directly optimizing WMD is expensive and non-smooth. The paper leverages the entropically regularized OT (Sinkhorn distance) [Cuturi, NIPS’13] to obtain unique, smooth transport plans T∗λ and efficient O(q^2) solvers (q = number of unique words in a document). Gradients are derived for A using the envelope theorem and for w via dual potentials, approximated from Sinkhorn scaling vectors (u, v). To reduce computation, the method initializes A with an NCA metric learned on Word Centroid Distance (WCD) representations (document centroids in embedding space), then uses batch optimization with neighbor subsampling guided by WCD.

Experiments on eight datasets compare S-WMD against 26 baselines: classic sparse document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, BM25), LSI, LDA, mSDA, unsupervised WCD/WMD, and metric learning (NCA/ITML/LMNN) applied to reduced representations. S-WMD attains the best average rank and best k-NN accuracy on 5/8 datasets, with visualization and timing results supporting its effectiveness and practicality. Code is provided.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The formulation is coherent: learning A and w directly where WMD operates (word space) keeps the optimization in low dimension and targets the distance used for k-NN. The NCA-style objective is appropriate for metric learning.
- The use of the entropic OT relaxation and the envelope theorem justifies gradients with respect to the ground cost; strong convexity of the regularized problem ensures uniqueness and smooth dependence on costs. Approximating dual potentials from Sinkhorn scalings for ∂/∂w is standard and well motivated.
- Computational strategies (initialization with S-WCD, batch training, WCD-based neighbor filtering) are sensible and substantially lower runtime.
- Empirical evaluation is broad, with many relevant baselines and Bayesian optimization for hyperparameters. Improvements are consistent in several settings.

Potential technical gaps or ambiguities:
- Constraints and regularization on w and A are not clearly specified. Since d̃ = (w ◦ d)/(w^T d), nonnegativity of w seems required for a valid histogram; the paper does not state whether w is constrained (e.g., projected to R+ or parameterized via exp) or regularized (e.g., L1/L2). Without constraints, negative weights could break the probabilistic interpretation and induce overfitting.
- Notation is occasionally ambiguous (e.g., p used both for the norm exponent and number of unique words). Section numbering (“.2 Optimization”) and some reference entries are incomplete; clarifying these would improve reproducibility.
- The relaxation parameter λ is fixed (λ = 10); sensitivity analysis is not shown. Similarly, the effect of neighbor subsampling size N and batch size B on accuracy/convergence is not analyzed.
- The method optimizes a relaxed OT distance (Sinkhorn) but evaluates using k-NN with what distance? If evaluation uses the relaxed distance consistently, this should be stated; if using exact WMD at test time, the training–test mismatch should be discussed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized. The background on word embeddings, WMD, Sinkhorn OT, and NCA is concise and adequate. The algorithmic ideas are clearly conveyed, and the motivation for learning in word space rather than document space is compelling. Minor notational inconsistencies and typos should be corrected. More detail on constraints/regularizers and optimization hyperparameters would aid clarity.

Originality
Learning a ground metric for an OT-based document distance using supervision is a natural but nontrivial extension of WMD. Prior work has learned ground metrics for EMD in vision [e.g., Wang & Guibas, ECCV’09; Wang et al., 2011/2013], and Cuturi & collaborators introduced entropic OT and efficient solvers. This paper’s contributions are: applying supervised ground-metric learning to text via word embeddings, jointly learning histogram reweighting w, and making the training practical with Sinkhorn-based gradients, NCA loss, and WCD-based initialization/filtering. The combination and its effective execution in the text domain appear novel and useful.

Significance
Results indicate consistent gains over strong baselines, including unsupervised WMD and supervised metric learning on conventional document features. The approach is likely to be useful for k-NN classification and retrieval tasks where distances matter, and it showcases a principled way to inject supervision into OT-based text distances. Broader impact is somewhat bounded by the k-NN focus; comparisons to strong discriminative classifiers (e.g., linear SVM/LogReg on TF-IDF, doc2vec, or supervised fastText-style embeddings) and evaluation on retrieval would further strengthen the case.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Well-motivated formulation that directly improves a strong, widely used distance (WMD).
- Sound use of entropic OT for tractable, differentiable training; appropriate gradients.
- Practical and effective engineering (initialization, batching, neighbor filtering).
- Extensive empirical study with many baselines; consistent improvements on multiple datasets.
- Code availability.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Missing specification of constraints/regularization on w and A; potential overfitting or invalid weights.
- Limited ablations (contribution of A vs. w vs. initialization vs. Sinkhorn λ not isolated).
- Evaluation restricted to k-NN; no retrieval experiments or comparisons to strong supervised text classifiers.
- Minor clarity/notation issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clearly state and enforce w ≥ 0 (e.g., w = softplus(z)) and add regularization (e.g., ||A||F^2, L1/L2 on w). Report ablations showing their effect and the separate contributions of A and w.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for λ, neighbor set size N, and batch size B. Clarify whether test-time distances use the relaxed or exact OT.
- Compare to additional supervised document baselines (e.g., doc2vec; linear SVM/LogReg on TF-IDF) and include a retrieval evaluation.
- Fix notation and section numbering; complete references.

Overall, this is a solid and practically valuable contribution that advances supervised metric learning for document distances by combining WMD with learnable ground metrics and word weights via efficient Sinkhorn optimization.
