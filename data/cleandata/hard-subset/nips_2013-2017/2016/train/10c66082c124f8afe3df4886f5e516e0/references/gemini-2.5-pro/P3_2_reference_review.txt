### Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which leverages pretrained word embeddings to define the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to become the words of the other. While WMD has shown state-of-the-art performance in unsupervised settings, its utility is limited by its task-agnostic nature.

The authors propose to overcome this limitation by incorporating supervision in two key ways:
1.  **Learning a linear transformation (A)** of the word embedding space. This is a form of metric learning, aiming to reshape the semantic space so that distances better reflect the classification task at hand.
2.  **Learning a word importance vector (w)** that re-weights the normalized bag-of-words histograms for each document. This allows the model to up-weight words that are highly discriminative for a given task and down-weight common or irrelevant words.

A significant technical challenge is that optimizing these parameters through the WMD objective is computationally prohibitive and involves gradients through a non-differentiable linear program. The authors' key technical contribution is to address this by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized counterpart, the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation yields a strictly convex, differentiable objective that can be solved much faster (quadratically instead of cubically in the number of unique words). The authors formulate an objective function based on Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) to minimize a soft version of the leave-one-out kNN error and derive gradients for both the matrix A and the vector w. To further improve efficiency, they propose a smart initialization scheme using a supervised version of the faster Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and employ a batch gradient descent strategy that considers only a document's nearest neighbors.

The method is evaluated exhaustively on 8 document classification datasets, demonstrating superior performance against 26 strong baselines, including unsupervised WMD and various document representations combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of WMD. The idea of learning a task-specific distance by supervising the underlying optimal transport problem is a natural, yet powerful extension. The combination of learning both a geometric transformation of the embedding space (`A`) and a re-weighting of the document content (`w`) is elegant and provides two complementary mechanisms for adaptation. This work is a significant contribution to both metric learning and text analysis.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Elegance:** The main technical hurdle—differentiating through the WMD—is solved elegantly by leveraging the Sinkhorn distance. This is a very clever application of recent advances in computational optimal transport. The derivations for the gradients with respect to `A` and `w` (using the dual variables of the relaxed problem) appear sound and provide a principled way to optimize the objective.

3.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The authors do not just present a theoretical model but also pay close attention to making it practical. The combination of the Sinkhorn relaxation, a strong WCD-based initialization, and a nearest-neighbor filtered batch optimization scheme makes the overall algorithm computationally feasible. The reported training times are impressive and suggest the method is usable in practice.

4.  **Extremely Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup is exemplary. The comparison against 26 baselines across 8 diverse datasets provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness. By outperforming not just unsupervised methods but also combinations of strong document representations (like TF-IDF, LSI, mSDA) with powerful metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML), the authors convincingly establish a new state of the art in kNN-based document classification. The visualizations via t-SNE and the word-weight word cloud add valuable qualitative support for their claims.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Lack of Ablation Study:** The model introduces two learned components: the transformation matrix `A` and the word weight vector `w`. The paper does not disentangle their respective contributions. An ablation study would significantly strengthen the paper. For instance, experiments showing the performance of S-WMD with only `A` (i.e., `w` fixed to ones) and only `w` (i.e., `A` fixed to identity) would clarify how much each component contributes to the final performance boost. This is my primary criticism.

2.  **Sensitivity to Regularization Parameter `λ`:** The Sinkhorn distance relies on a regularization parameter `λ`, which balances the approximation quality against computational stability and speed. The paper fixes `λ=10` and mentions it's a trade-off, but provides no analysis of this choice. A brief discussion or sensitivity analysis showing how performance and training time vary with `λ` would be valuable for practitioners seeking to apply this method.

3.  **Scalability with Vocabulary Size:** The computational complexity is analyzed in terms of the number of unique words per document (`q`), but the word importance vector `w` has a dimension equal to the entire vocabulary size `n`. For very large vocabularies (millions of words), updating and storing this dense vector could become an issue. A short discussion on the scalability with respect to `n` and whether sparsity could be induced in `w` would be welcome.

4.  **Discussion of Failure Case:** The authors honestly report that unsupervised WMD outperforms S-WMD on the CLASSIC dataset and speculate this might be due to the squared Euclidean distance. This is an interesting finding that deserves slightly more investigation. Could this indicate that the NCA-style loss, which is tied to this distance, is sometimes a poor objective for text? Expanding this discussion slightly could provide useful insights into the model's limitations.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound method. It successfully marries the strengths of unsupervised word embeddings and optimal transport with the task-specificity of supervised metric learning. The proposed solution for making the optimization tractable is elegant, and the empirical results are comprehensive and convincing. While an ablation study is missing, the overall quality, originality, and significance of the work are very high. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. It is a strong candidate for acceptance and is likely to be influential.
