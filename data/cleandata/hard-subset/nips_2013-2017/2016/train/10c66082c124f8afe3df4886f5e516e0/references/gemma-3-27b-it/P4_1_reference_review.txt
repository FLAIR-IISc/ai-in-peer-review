The paper proposes a supervised variant of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), dubbed S-WMD, aiming to learn a metric that improves document distance computation for classification tasks. Leveraging word embeddings, S-WMD learns a linear transformation of the word representations and re-weighted word frequencies, minimizing the leave-one-out classification error. The key contribution lies in efficiently approximating the optimal transport problem using the Sinkhorn distance, enabling scalable learning. The authors demonstrate superior performance across eight datasets and comparisons with 26 baselines.

This work builds on Kusner et al.’s [19] WMD, which introduced the idea of using word embeddings for document distance. It also relates to the broader field of metric learning [10, 15, 36, 37], adapting concepts like Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [15] to the WMD framework. The paper acknowledges recent advances in fast Wasserstein distance computation [6, 7, 8, 12] and integrates the Sinkhorn distance [6] for computational efficiency.  Several prior works have also explored learning word embeddings with supervision [32], though S-WMD distinguishes itself by learning the *distance* rather than the embeddings themselves.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of metric learning with the WMD is a novel approach. Learning both a transformation of word embeddings *and* word weights provides a flexible framework for capturing task-specific similarity.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance for efficient approximation of the optimal transport problem is a strong technical contribution. The gradient calculations appear correct and the optimization strategy, including initialization and batch gradient descent, is well-considered. The mathematical formulation is clear and well-defined.
*   **Empirical Results:** The extensive experimental evaluation across diverse datasets and a large number of baselines convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of S-WMD. The ablation study examining the initialization phase strengthens the analysis.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors provide sufficient background on WMD, metric learning, and the Sinkhorn distance. The algorithm is presented clearly in pseudocode.
*   **Efficiency:** The authors explicitly address the computational challenges and propose a method that makes the algorithm practical.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention tuning hyperparameters with Bayesian optimization, the sensitivity of the method to these parameters is not thoroughly discussed. A more detailed analysis of hyperparameter importance would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on empirical results. A more rigorous theoretical analysis of the learned metric and its properties would be valuable. Specifically, exploring conditions under which the learned transformation is well-behaved would be useful.
*   **Squared Euclidean Distance:** The choice of the squared Euclidean distance in the linear program (Eq. 5) might be a limitation. Exploring other distance metrics could potentially yield further improvements.
*   **Comparison to Recent EMD Learning:** The related work section could benefit from a more detailed comparison to Wang et al. [35], which also learns a metric within a generalized EMD ground distance. A clearer differentiation of the approaches would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to supervised document distance learning.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
*   The efficient approximation using the Sinkhorn distance makes the method practical for real-world applications.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and its evaluation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a thorough theoretical analysis is a minor weakness.
*   The potential hyperparameter sensitivity could limit the method’s robustness in some scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document distance learning. The proposed S-WMD method is novel, technically sound, and empirically effective. While some minor weaknesses exist, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh them. I recommend acceptance.
