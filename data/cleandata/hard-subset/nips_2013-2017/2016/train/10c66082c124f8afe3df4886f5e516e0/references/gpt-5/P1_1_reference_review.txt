Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of WMD for document comparison. Instead of learning document representations, the method learns (i) a linear transformation A of the pre-trained word embedding space and (ii) word-specific importance weights w that reweight normalized bag-of-words histograms before computing the transport. Supervision is injected by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss in the spirit of NCA: a softmax over negative distances DA,w(da, db) replaces hard nearest neighbors. Because exact WMD is cubic in the number of unique words per document, the paper introduces an efficient training procedure that uses the entropically regularized OT (Sinkhorn) solution to approximate the inner transport plan and dual potentials, enabling O(p^2) scaling per pair (p = unique words). Additional accelerations include batch training and restricting the NCA soft neighborhood to the top-N nearest neighbors under the cheap WCD lower bound. Initialization uses NCA on word centroids (S-WCD) to provide a good A. Experiments on 8 datasets with 26 baselines show S-WMD achieves the best average rank and lowest error on 5/8 datasets. Code is publicly available.

Relation to prior work
- Builds directly on WMD (Kusner et al., 2015) and entropic OT (Cuturi, 2013), using Sinkhorn to make supervision feasible.
- Metric learning connection is via NCA and related methods (ITML, LMNN); here the novelty is learning the ground metric for OT in the word embedding space plus histogram reweighting, rather than a metric on document representations.
- Related EMD/OT ground metric learning in vision is cited (Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.), with a clear distinction: S-WMD additionally learns word weights and targets text.
- The approach contrasts with representation learning methods (LSI/LDA/mSDA) and supervised document encoders by preserving OT’s ability to align semantically similar but lexically different words.

Strengths
- Clear, practical idea: learn a task-specific ground distance and term weights for WMD; maintains low dimensionality by operating in embedding space.
- Solid optimization design: NCA loss, Sinkhorn relaxation for differentiability/efficiency, dual potential reuse for gradients, and simple yet effective batching/neighbor pruning.
- Strong empirical study: 8 datasets, 26 baselines including supervised metric learning on top of standard representations; consistent gains and best average rank.
- Sensible initialization (S-WCD) that already performs well; demonstrates the value of learning A even without full S-WMD training.
- Code release enhances reproducibility and impact.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Objective/gradient mismatch: The loss is written in terms of unregularized DA,w but training uses gradients derived from the entropic-regularized transport and dual potentials. This is a biased gradient estimator unless the objective is explicitly the regularized distance. The paper should clarify whether (a) the loss uses the regularized distance Dλ or (b) the authors rely on an envelope-theorem argument and show that using Tλ as a surrogate yields a descent method. Sensitivity to λ and its impact on accuracy/speed is not analyzed; λ is fixed at 10.
- Word weights w: There is no explicit constraint set or regularization. Eq. (4) implies w should be nonnegative; how is positivity enforced? Without regularization, w can overfit by upweighting rare words. A simple nonnegativity parameterization (w = exp(u)) and sparsity/variance regularization or class-wise normalization would help.
- Neighbor pruning: Limiting the NCA softmax to the top-N neighbors under WCD changes the training objective. While pragmatic, there is no analysis on whether the truncated objective remains a good surrogate (e.g., bounds on mass discarded, effect on convergence). An ablation on N would help.
- Notational clarity and minor errors: p is used for both Lp norm exponent and number of unique words; Section numbering glitches; some references incomplete; the derivation of dual potentials from u, v is only sketched. Reproducibility would benefit from fuller derivations and consistent notation.
- Complexity statements: The gradient cost is reported as O(d^2(p + r)); accumulating Σij Tij (xi − xj)(xi − xj)^T is O(p^2 d), so overall complexity appears understated; please clarify the exact computational steps and their costs.
- Missing ablations: The paper shows S-WCD and full S-WMD, but does not isolate the contribution of w vs A. A study with A-only, w-only, and joint would be helpful. Also, test-time speed and strategy (exact WMD vs Sinkhorn, any prefiltering) are not clearly stated.
- Comparison to neural supervised text encoders: While the focus is on distance-based kNN, a discussion/experiment vs strong supervised baselines (e.g., simple CNN/RNN at the time) would contextualize significance.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with appropriate optimization choices and a well-designed evaluation. The main technical caveats are the regularization mismatch and lack of discussion on constraints/regularization for w. Empirical results are convincing across diverse datasets.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, but the paper would benefit from:
- Explicitly stating the trained objective (regularized vs unregularized) and giving the derivation of dual potentials from Sinkhorn scaling.
- Cleaning notation (avoid reuse of p) and fixing section/reference typos.
- Clarifying test-time distance computation and runtime.

Originality
A novel and timely combination: supervised learning of the OT ground metric in word embedding space for document comparison, plus learned word importance via histogram reweighting, optimized with an NCA-style loss and efficient Sinkhorn-based gradients. Prior OT metric learning exists, but not in this form for text with word weighting.

Significance
High practical relevance: a drop-in supervised improvement to WMD that yields state-of-the-art kNN accuracy on multiple benchmarks and is efficient. Likely to be adopted by practitioners working on retrieval/classification with limited supervision and by researchers interested in supervised OT.

Questions for the authors
- Do you optimize the entropic-regularized distance Dλ in the loss, or the exact DA,w while using Tλ for gradients? Please clarify and provide sensitivity to λ.
- How do you enforce w ≥ 0, and do you regularize w? Any evidence of overfitting with large vocabularies?
- What distance is used at test time (exact WMD vs Sinkhorn)? Any prefiltering strategy, and what are the test-time runtimes?
- Can you provide ablations isolating A vs w, and sensitivity to N (neighbor cap) and batch size?
- Have you tried alternative embeddings (e.g., GloVe) to test robustness?

Pros
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated, and effective.
- Strong empirical gains with broad comparisons; code available.
- Efficient training via Sinkhorn and smart approximations.

Cons
- Objective/gradient mismatch and λ sensitivity not addressed.
- Missing constraints/regularization for w; potential overfitting.
- Some clarity and notation issues; limited ablations.

Recommendation
Lean toward accept, contingent on clarifying the objective vs approximation, adding a brief λ sensitivity analysis, and addressing constraints/regularization for w. The contribution is solid, useful, and advances supervised OT for text. Confidence: medium-high.
