**Summary**
This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance. Building upon the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which leverages word embeddings to calculate an optimal transport cost between documents, S-WMD introduces supervision in two ways: (1) by learning a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, and (2) by learning a vector `w` of word-importance weights that re-scales the document's bag-of-words representation. To make this learning problem tractable, the authors replace the computationally expensive WMD with its entropy-regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance), which is differentiable and faster to compute. The learning objective minimizes a stochastic approximation of the leave-one-out kNN classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). The authors propose an efficient training procedure involving a clever initialization based on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batch-based gradient descent with nearest-neighbor filtering. The method is evaluated on 8 datasets, demonstrating superior kNN classification performance against a comprehensive suite of 26 unsupervised and supervised baselines.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of the original WMD: its unsupervised nature. The proposed method of jointly learning a transformation of the embedding space and word-specific importance weights is a natural and powerful way to incorporate label information. This is a significant contribution to the field of document similarity and metric learning.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The core technical challenge—differentiating through the WMD linear program—is elegantly addressed by using the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance. This is a well-motivated choice that makes the optimization feasible. The derivation of the gradients for both the matrix `A` and the weight vector `w` appears correct.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is extensive. The comparison against 26 baselines across 8 diverse datasets is commendable. S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on most datasets, providing compelling evidence for its effectiveness.
4.  **Practicality:** The authors have clearly considered the computational aspects of their method. The WCD-based initialization and the approximate nearest-neighbor gradient computation are clever heuristics that significantly speed up training, making the method practical for real-world use.

**Weaknesses**
Despite the strong contributions, the paper suffers from a lack of rigor in its analysis and several instances of carelessness in its presentation, which collectively detract from its overall quality.

1.  **Methodological Details and Justification:**
    *   **The λ Parameter:** The choice of the regularization parameter λ in the Sinkhorn distance is crucial. A large λ approximates WMD better but can lead to numerical instability and slower convergence, while a small λ may overly smooth the transport plan. The authors state they use λ=10 as it "leads to a nice trade-off," but this is an empirical claim without any supporting evidence or analysis. A sensitivity analysis or a more principled discussion on the effect of λ is a notable omission.
    *   **Squared Euclidean Distance:** The paper uses the squared Euclidean distance `‖A(xi − xj)‖_2^2` in Equation (5). This choice is motivated by similarity to prior work, but the original WMD paper often uses the L2 norm, not the squared L2 norm. The authors themselves speculate that this choice might be suboptimal and responsible for the method's failure on the CLASSIC dataset. This suggests the choice was not thoroughly investigated or justified. A discussion on the implications of this choice (e.g., heavily penalizing large distances) is warranted.
    *   **Word Importance Weights (w):** The introduction of the weight vector `w` is an interesting addition. However, its relationship to existing word weighting schemes like TF-IDF is not discussed. Does `w` learn something complementary to TF-IDF, or does it simply relearn a similar concept? The strong performance of TF-IDF+NCA on some datasets suggests this is an important comparison to analyze, not just report.

2.  **Experimental Rigor:**
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** While the authors state they use Bayesian optimization, the process is opaque. Key hyperparameters for their own method, such as the learning rates ηA and ηw, the batch size B, the neighbor set size N, and the crucial λ, are not reported as having been tuned. B and N are simply set to 32 and 200. This lack of detail undermines the reproducibility and fairness of the experimental comparison.
    *   **Compounding Approximations:** The final algorithm relies on a series of approximations: Sinkhorn distance approximates WMD, NCA loss approximates kNN LOO error, and nearest-neighbor filtering approximates the full gradient. The paper lacks any discussion on how these approximations might interact or compound, and under what conditions they might fail.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper exhibits a concerning number of minor errors and inconsistencies that suggest a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   **Citations:** The reference list is poorly formatted. Several entries are incomplete (e.g., "[10] In ICML, pp, , ,"). Reference [1] is formatted as "D. Bertsimas, J.N. Tsitsiklis, Athena Scientific,, , ,", with extraneous commas. Reference [2] contains the typo "PdD Thesis". In the main text, a citation placeholder "[21? ]" remains in Section 2.
    *   **Notation:** The complexity analysis in Section 3.2 uses `p` for the maximum number of unique words, whereas Section 2 used `q` for the same concept. This is a needless inconsistency.
    *   **Formatting:** The header "th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2016), Barcelona, Spain." is an artifact of the camera-ready template and should have been removed for submission. The footnote `†` is also unconventional for a conference paper.
    *   **Grammatical Errors:** There are several minor grammatical issues, such as "proportional its learned weight" (should be "proportional to its"), and "the algorithm converges slower" (should be "more slowly").

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated method for supervised document distance learning.
*   The use of Sinkhorn relaxation to make the problem tractable is a strong technical contribution.
*   The empirical results are comprehensive and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The paper lacks a rigorous analysis of key methodological choices (e.g., the λ parameter, the choice of a squared distance).
*   Details on hyperparameter tuning are insufficient, hindering reproducibility.
*   The manuscript is marred by a substantial number of formatting errors, typos, and inconsistencies, which fall below the standard expected for a top-tier conference like NIPS.

**Decision:**
This paper presents a very promising research direction and strong initial results. However, the lack of thorough ablation studies for key parameters and the numerous instances of carelessness in the presentation prevent me from giving it a strong endorsement. The accumulation of many "minor" flaws casts doubt on the overall rigor of the work. While the core ideas are of NIPS quality, the execution in this manuscript is not. I recommend **Weak Reject**. A revised version that addresses the methodological weaknesses and presentation issues would likely be a very strong submission.
