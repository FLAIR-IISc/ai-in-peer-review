**Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The proposed approach extends the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which calculates the optimal transport cost between documents in a pre-trained word embedding space. The authors introduce supervision by learning two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation of the word embedding space to better align with class labels, and (2) a set of word importance weights to re-scale the document's bag-of-words representation. To make this learning problem tractable, the authors propose several key modifications. They formulate the objective as minimizing the leave-one-out kNN error using the differentiable Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) loss. Crucially, they replace the computationally expensive WMD calculation (a linear program) with its entropy-regularized, strictly convex counterpart, the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation allows for efficient and stable gradient computation. The optimization is further accelerated through a clever initialization scheme using the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batch gradient descent strategy that considers only a local neighborhood of documents. The method is evaluated on eight document classification datasets, where it is shown to achieve state-of-the-art kNN classification error compared to an extensive suite of 26 unsupervised and supervised baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper's primary strength lies in its rigorous experimental evaluation. The authors compare S-WMD against a comprehensive and challenging set of 26 baselines, including the original WMD, classic document representations (TF-IDF, LSI, LDA), and modern deep learning approaches (mSDA). Furthermore, they strengthen these baselines by pairing them with established metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN). The fact that S-WMD demonstrates superior or highly competitive performance across eight diverse datasets is compelling evidence of its effectiveness.

2.  **Well-Motivated Problem:** The work addresses a clear and sensible limitation of the original WMD: its purely unsupervised nature. The distance metric learned by WMD is task-agnostic, whereas document similarity is often context-dependent. Introducing supervision to tailor the metric for a specific classification task is a logical and valuable extension.

3.  **Pragmatic Approach to a Difficult Problem:** Directly optimizing a supervised version of WMD is computationally prohibitive. The authors make a series of well-justified and practical choices to render the problem tractable. The use of the Sinkhorn distance is a principled relaxation that has a strong theoretical basis and leads to significant speedups (from O(q^3 log q) to O(q^2)). The additional engineering, such as the WCD-based initialization and neighbor-based batching, further demonstrates a thoughtful approach to creating a usable algorithm.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of Components:** While the final algorithm is effective, its constituent parts are largely drawn from existing literature. The core ideas—WMD, NCA, and Sinkhorn distances for fast optimal transport—are all well-established methods. The concept of learning a ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance has also been explored previously, for instance in computer vision contexts (e.g., Wang & Guibas, 2012; Wang et al., 2013). The main contribution is the specific combination of these techniques for document classification, along with the addition of the word importance weights `w`. This feels more like a strong, novel application and integration of existing tools rather than a fundamental methodological advance.

2.  **Reliance on a Cascade of Approximations:** The method's tractability is achieved through several layers of approximation. The NCA loss is a soft proxy for the true kNN error. The Sinkhorn distance is an approximation of the true Wasserstein distance. Finally, the gradient calculation is further approximated by considering only a subset of nearest neighbors based on the WCD. While the empirical results suggest this combination works well, the paper lacks an analysis of the interplay between these approximations or the sensitivity to their hyperparameters (e.g., the regularization strength `λ`). It is unclear how much the final learned metric deviates from one that would be learned by optimizing the "true" (but intractable) objective.

3.  **Analysis of Learned Components:** The paper introduces two learned components, the matrix `A` and the vector `w`. While a word cloud is provided for `w`, a more in-depth analysis would strengthen the paper. For example, how do the learned importance weights in `w` compare to classic feature importance scores like information gain or TF-IDF weights? Similarly, is there any structure in the learned transformation `A` that provides insight into how semantic relationships are being warped for the specific task? The current analysis focuses almost exclusively on the final classification error, leaving the learned components as black boxes.

**Critique based on Review Criteria**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations appear correct, and the experimental methodology is rigorous and thorough. The claims of state-of-the-art performance are well-supported by the extensive results presented.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors provide sufficient background on WMD, Sinkhorn distances, and NCA, making the paper self-contained and easy to follow. The motivation for each technical decision is clearly explained.
*   **Originality:** This is the weakest aspect of the paper. As noted above, the work consists of a novel and effective combination of pre-existing, established techniques. While the application to document distance learning is new and the results are significant, the paper does not introduce a fundamentally new machine learning method.
*   **Significance:** The work is significant from a practical standpoint. It provides a new state-of-the-art method for kNN document classification that outperforms many strong baselines. Given the impressive performance and the practical computational approach, other researchers and practitioners in text classification are likely to use and build upon this method. It clearly advances the state of the art in its specific subfield.

In summary, this is a strong piece of empirical work. While its methodological novelty is incremental, the authors have skillfully integrated several powerful ideas to solve a well-motivated problem. The comprehensive experiments provide convincing evidence that S-WMD is a highly effective method for document classification. The paper is a solid contribution to the field.
