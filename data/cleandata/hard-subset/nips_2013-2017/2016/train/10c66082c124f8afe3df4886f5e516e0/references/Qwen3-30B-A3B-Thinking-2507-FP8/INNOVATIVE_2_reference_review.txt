

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel approach that extends the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by incorporating supervision to improve document classification performance. The authors propose learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word-specific importance weights to make WMD more effective for classification tasks. The key insight is that different classification tasks value words differently, and by learning these weights and the embedding transformation, they can better align the document distances with semantic similarity encoded in labels.

The paper's core innovation is the supervised adaptation of WMD, which was previously entirely unsupervised. This represents a significant conceptual shift from prior work, as the authors directly learn a metric to minimize classification error rather than using WMD as a fixed, unsupervised distance. The authors cleverly address computational challenges by using the Sinkhorn distance approximation to efficiently compute gradients, making the supervised approach feasible. The method is well-motivated and builds logically on WMD, metric learning, and the NCA framework.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, showing S-WMD outperforms 26 baselines across 8 datasets in kNN classification. Notably, S-WMD achieves the lowest error on 5/8 datasets and outperforms unsupervised WMD by significant margins (e.g., 51% relative error reduction on BBCSPORT). The visualization results (Fig. 1) convincingly show improved clustering of documents by class using S-WMD compared to WMD. The authors also provide a thoughtful analysis of why their method might underperform on certain datasets (e.g., CLASSIC), showing awareness of limitations.

The paper excels in originality, which is the most important criterion for NIPS. While WMD has been widely adopted, the supervised adaptation is genuinely novel and represents a bold new direction for document distance learning. The authors clearly differentiate their work from related metric learning approaches (e.g., NCA, LMNN) by emphasizing that they learn within the word embedding space rather than learning new document representations. The paper's theoretical foundation is sound, and the experimental validation is comprehensive.

Strengths:
- Strong novelty in incorporating supervision into WMD
- Clear conceptual contribution with a well-motivated approach
- Comprehensive experimental validation across 8 datasets
- Practical efficiency (Table 3 shows training times comparable to other methods)
- Thoughtful analysis of results and limitations

Weaknesses:
- Limited theoretical analysis of why the supervised approach works better than unsupervised WMD
- Some minor issues with initialization (mentioned in results section)
- The paper doesn't explore how S-WMD might scale to extremely large vocabularies

However, these weaknesses are relatively minor in the context of the paper's significant conceptual contribution. The paper doesn't claim to be a complete theoretical treatise but rather presents a novel, practical approach that advances the state of the art in document distance learning.

The significance of this work is substantial. Document distances are fundamental to many text retrieval tasks, and the authors demonstrate that incorporating supervision into WMD leads to meaningful improvements across multiple domains. Their approach could inspire new directions in metric learning for text, particularly for tasks where semantic similarity is crucial. The method is also computationally efficient, making it practical for real-world applications.

Compared to recent NIPS papers, this work stands out for its bold conceptual shift from unsupervised to supervised WMD. While metric learning has been studied extensively, the authors' specific adaptation to the WMD framework represents a fresh perspective that moves the field forward. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field. While the methodological details are sound, the true value lies in the novel idea of applying supervision to WMD, which the authors execute effectively. The paper's emphasis on a new conceptual direction rather than incremental improvements aligns perfectly with NIPS's preference for bold, novel approaches.

For the program committee, I recommend acceptance. The paper introduces a genuinely new concept that could influence future work in document representation and metric learning. The results are compelling, and the approach is both theoretically sound and practically useful. The minor weaknesses noted don't undermine the paper's significant contribution, which is what matters most for NIPS.
