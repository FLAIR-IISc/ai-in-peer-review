### **Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"**

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost of "transporting" words from one to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. The key limitation of WMD is its unsupervised nature, meaning the underlying word similarities are generic and not tailored to a specific classification task.

The authors propose to overcome this by simultaneously learning two components:
1.  A linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, which adapts the word representations to the task.
2.  A vector `w` of word importance weights, which re-weighs the document's bag-of-words representation to emphasize task-relevant words.

To learn `A` and `w`, the paper formulates an objective function based on Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), which aims to minimize the leave-one-out k-NN classification error in a differentiable manner. A major challenge is the high computational cost of the WMD, which makes gradient-based optimization intractable. The authors propose a clever and effective solution by replacing the exact WMD with its entropically regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance), which is significantly faster to compute and provides a well-defined gradient. This is further combined with an efficient initialization scheme (using the much simpler Word Centroid Distance) and a batch gradient descent strategy to make training practical. The authors conduct a comprehensive experimental evaluation across 8 datasets, showing that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results compared to 26 competitive baselines.

---

### **Evaluation**

**Quality: (Excellent)**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the technical execution is sound.

*   **Soundness:** The core idea of adapting the WMD ground metric and word weights using an NCA-style loss is well-founded. The main technical hurdle—the computational complexity and non-differentiability of the standard WMD—is addressed convincingly. The use of the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable and fast approximation is a key contribution that makes the entire framework feasible. The derivations for the gradients with respect to the transformation matrix `A` and the weight vector `w` appear correct, leveraging the properties of the relaxed optimal transport problem.
*   **Completeness & Evaluation:** The experimental validation is exceptionally thorough and convincing. The authors compare S-WMD not just against unsupervised WMD, but against a wide array of classic (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA) and modern (mSDA) document representation techniques. Crucially, they also pair these representations with powerful metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN), creating a very strong set of baselines. The consistent top performance of S-WMD across 5 of 8 diverse datasets strongly supports the paper's claims. The inclusion of visualizations (t-SNE and word clouds) and a training time analysis further strengthens the paper.

**Clarity: (Very Good)**

The paper is well-written and organized. The progression from the background on WMD and NCA to the proposed S-WMD method is logical and easy to follow.

*   Section 2 provides a concise and sufficient background on the necessary concepts.
*   Section 3 clearly lays out the proposed model (Eq. 5), the loss function (Eq. 6), and the optimization strategy. The descriptions of the fast gradient computation and the initialization/batching schemes are clear and motivate the design choices well.
*   The experimental setup and results are presented clearly in tables and figures, making it easy to assess the performance of S-WMD relative to the baselines.

A minor point for improvement would be to provide slightly more intuition or a brief derivation for obtaining the dual variables `α*` and `β*` from the scaling vectors `u` and `v` of the Sinkhorn algorithm. While this is likely standard in the optimal transport literature, a brief explanation would make the paper more self-contained.

**Originality: (Very Good)**

The paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of text classification and metric learning.

*   While metric learning itself is a well-established field, and learning the ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance has been explored before (e.g., Wang et al. [35] in computer vision), this paper is the first to successfully apply these ideas to the Word Mover's Distance for text documents.
*   The combination of learning a linear transformation of the embedding space (`A`) *and* a vector of word importance weights (`w`) is a novel and powerful idea. The importance weights add an extra degree of freedom that is particularly well-suited for text, where certain words (e.g., "excellent" vs. "the") carry much more discriminative information than others.
*   The specific combination of the WMD framework, NCA loss, and the Sinkhorn relaxation for efficient optimization represents a novel and well-engineered system that solves a previously intractable problem.

The paper does a good job of citing related work, including prior work on WMD, metric learning, and learning ground distances for EMD.

**Significance: (Excellent)**

The results presented in this paper are likely to be highly influential for researchers and practitioners working on text classification, retrieval, and other tasks involving document similarity.

*   **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper demonstrates a clear advance in the state of the art for k-NN based document classification. WMD was already a very strong baseline, and S-WMD significantly improves upon it, outperforming a large suite of powerful alternatives. This provides the community with a new, high-performing tool.
*   **Generalizability:** The framework is quite general. It can be applied with any pre-trained word embeddings and for any document classification task where labeled data is available. The method effectively shows how to inject task-specific knowledge into powerful, pre-trained, unsupervised representations.
*   **Future Work:** This work opens up several interesting avenues for future research, such as exploring non-linear transformations of the word embedding space, more sophisticated regularization on the weight vector `w`, or extending the framework to other optimal transport-based distances in NLP.

---

### **Arguments for Acceptance (Pros)**

1.  **Novel and Important Problem:** The paper tackles the important problem of adapting the powerful but unsupervised WMD for specific supervised tasks.
2.  **Technically Sound and Elegant Solution:** It provides a clever solution to the otherwise intractable optimization problem by using the Sinkhorn distance, enabling efficient, gradient-based learning.
3.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The evaluation is exemplary, comparing against a vast number of strong baselines on multiple datasets and demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.
4.  **Clear Contribution:** The dual learning of a metric on the embedding space and word importance weights is a novel contribution that is well-suited for text data.

### **Arguments for Rejection (Cons)**

1.  **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper briefly speculates why S-WMD is not the top performer on 3 of the 8 datasets, but a more in-depth analysis of its limitations would be beneficial. For example, are there specific data characteristics (e.g., document length, vocabulary size, class separability) where S-WMD is less effective?
2.  **Incremental Novelty Claim (Weak Argument):** One could argue that the work is a combination of existing ideas (WMD, NCA, Sinkhorn, metric learning). However, the combination is non-trivial, highly effective, and applied to a new and important domain, making this a weak argument against acceptance.

**Recommendation:** **Accept.**

This is a strong paper with a novel idea, solid technical execution, and outstanding experimental results. It solves a practical and important problem, advances the state of the art, and is likely to be highly cited and built upon. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the contribution.
