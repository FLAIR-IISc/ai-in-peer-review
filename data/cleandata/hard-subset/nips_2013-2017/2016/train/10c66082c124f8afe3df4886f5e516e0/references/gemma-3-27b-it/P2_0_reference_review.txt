## Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document similarity. Recognizing that WMD, despite its strengths, is unsupervised, the authors aim to incorporate supervision by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weighting word frequencies.  A key contribution is an efficient approximation using the Sinkhorn distance to make the computation tractable, and a clever initialization and optimization strategy leveraging the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and stochastic gradient descent. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results across 8 datasets and 26 baselines, consistently outperforming existing methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a relevant problem:** Improving document similarity is crucial for many NLP tasks.  The limitations of unsupervised approaches like WMD are well-motivated.
* **Novel approach:**  Learning a metric *within* the WMD framework, combined with word re-weighting, is a novel idea. It effectively leverages the strengths of word embeddings while incorporating task-specific supervision.
* **Computational efficiency:**  The use of the Sinkhorn distance is a significant contribution, addressing the cubic complexity of the original WMD and making supervised learning feasible. The efficient optimization strategy (WCD initialization, batch gradient descent) further enhances practicality.
* **Strong empirical validation:** The paper presents comprehensive experimental results across a diverse set of datasets, consistently demonstrating superior performance compared to a large number of competitive baselines. The ablation studies with S-WMD initialization are informative.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The background section provides a good overview of relevant concepts.
* **Detailed analysis:** Visualization using t-SNE and analysis of learned word weights provide valuable insights.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of implementation:**  While the authors address computational issues, implementing S-WMD appears complex, requiring familiarity with optimal transport, Sinkhorn distances, and metric learning.  Reproducibility, while claimed, might be challenging for those unfamiliar with these techniques.
* **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning using Bayesian Optimization, but doesn't fully detail the tuning process or sensitivity analysis.  It's unclear how robust the performance is to different hyperparameter settings.
* **Limited theoretical analysis:** The theoretical justification for the proposed method (beyond the connection to metric learning and optimal transport) is relatively limited.  A more in-depth analysis of the properties of the learned metric could strengthen the paper.
* **Comparison to more recent baselines:** While 26 baselines are used, some of these are relatively dated. Considering more recent advances in contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) and document representation learning would be beneficial. The authors acknowledge this in the related work.



**Assessment of Review Criteria:**

* **Quality (8/10):**  The paper is technically sound and well-supported by experimental results. The proposed method is a valid contribution, and the results are convincing. The theoretical analysis could be strengthened.
* **Clarity (9/10):**  The paper is generally very clear and well-organized.  The explanations of complex concepts like the Sinkhorn distance are accessible.
* **Originality (8/10):**  The combination of metric learning within the WMD framework and the efficient approximation using the Sinkhorn distance is novel and represents a significant advance.
* **Significance (9/10):**  The results are important and likely to be useful to researchers and practitioners in NLP. The potential for improved document similarity could have broad impact.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong empirical results consistently outperform a large number of baselines.
* Novel approach addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
*  Computational efficiency is addressed through a clever approximation.
* Well-written and clearly explained.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Implementation complexity may limit adoption.
* Limited theoretical analysis.
*  Comparison to more recent baselines (BERT, etc.) is lacking.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **accepting** this paper. While there are minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work – its novelty, strong empirical performance, and contribution to computational efficiency – significantly outweigh them. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of document similarity and is likely to be influential. I would encourage the authors to address the suggestions regarding theoretical analysis and comparison to more recent baselines in a future version.
