- Proposes S-WMD: a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance that learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weights word frequencies for improved document distance.
- Uses Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering to address computational challenges and enable efficient gradient-based training.
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance, outperforming many baselines across eight datasets.
- Novel contribution by incorporating supervision directly into the WMD framework, addressing a limitation of prior unsupervised WMD.
- Solid technical grounding in optimal transport theory with clear gradient computation and optimization strategies.
- Employs KL-divergence loss and stochastic neighborhood relaxation appropriately for metric learning.
- Very comprehensive experimental evaluation: 26 baselines, multiple datasets, ablations, t-SNE visualizations, and word importance analyses.
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear background and method sections.
- Implementation appears complex, requiring many hyperparameters and Bayesian optimization, which may hinder practitioner adoption.
- Training time comparable to alternatives, but initial setup and tuning can be substantial.
- Method shows sensitivity to initialization (S-WCD), indicating dependence on initial embeddings and unsupervised approximations.
- Limited analysis of failure cases and underperforming datasets (e.g., BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON); more investigation is needed.
- Lacks a strong theoretical justification for why learning word importance weights improves performance.
- Arguments for acceptance: significant empirical gains, technical novelty, sound methodology, and thorough evaluation.
- Arguments against acceptance: implementation complexity, sensitivity to initialization, limited failure analysis, and weak theoretical grounding for re-weighting.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions — strong contribution with manageable weaknesses.
- Suggested improvements: provide implementation guidance or code, analyze underperforming datasets, and explore theoretical benefits of learned word weights.
- Qualitative scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 8/10.
