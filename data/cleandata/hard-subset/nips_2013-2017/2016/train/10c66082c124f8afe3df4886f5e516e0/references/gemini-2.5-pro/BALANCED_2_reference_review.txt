### Summary

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) [19], which is a powerful but unsupervised metric based on optimal transport between word embeddings of two documents. The key insight of this paper is that for specific classification tasks, the underlying similarity measure can be significantly improved with supervision.

The proposed S-WMD learns two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, which effectively learns a new ground metric for the transport problem, and (2) a vector `w` of word-importance weights, which re-scales the document's bag-of-words representation to emphasize task-relevant words. To learn `A` and `w`, the authors propose minimizing a differentiable approximation of the k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) leave-one-out error, following the framework of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [15].

A major technical challenge is that computing the gradient of this loss function requires differentiating through the WMD optimization problem, which is computationally expensive and potentially ill-defined. The authors present an elegant solution by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized counterpart, the Sinkhorn distance [6]. This relaxation makes the optimization problem strictly convex and allows for a fast, differentiable solution via matrix scaling, enabling efficient gradient-based training. The paper further proposes practical optimization improvements, including a clever initialization scheme based on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batch training strategy that filters neighbors to reduce computational load. The method is validated through extensive experiments on 8 document classification datasets, where it is shown to outperform 26 strong baselines, including unsupervised WMD and various document representations combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of very high technical quality.
- The formulation of the problem is clear and well-motivated.
- The use of the Sinkhorn distance to make the learning problem tractable is a technically sound and clever solution that builds upon recent advances in optimal transport. The derivation of the gradients with respect to both the matrix `A` and the weights `w` appears correct.
- The experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough. The authors compare against a vast and appropriate set of 26 baselines across 8 diverse datasets. The use of Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning for all methods demonstrates a commitment to a fair comparison.
- The empirical results are very strong, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the majority of datasets and establishing S-WMD as a leading method for kNN-based document classification. The authors are also honest about the few datasets where their method does not rank first and offer plausible hypotheses for these results.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
- The motivation for supervising WMD is clearly articulated in the introduction.
- The background section provides all the necessary components (word2vec, WMD, Sinkhorn distance, NCA) in a concise and understandable manner, making the paper self-contained.
- The proposed method is described with sufficient detail for the reader to understand the model, the loss function, and the optimization procedure.
- The figures, particularly the t-SNE visualizations and the word cloud of learned weights, are effective in providing intuition about the method's behavior.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions.
- While the idea of learning a ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance has been explored before in computer vision (e.g., [35]), this paper is the first to successfully apply it to the WMD for text documents, which is a significant and non-obvious application.
- The joint learning of a linear transformation `A` and a word importance vector `w` is a key novelty. This provides two complementary ways to adapt the distance to the task and is particularly well-suited for text data, where word relevance is highly context-dependent.
- The combination of the NCA loss with the Sinkhorn-relaxed WMD for efficient, end-to-end training is a novel and effective learning framework.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact.
- It addresses a fundamental problem: how to adapt powerful, general-purpose (unsupervised) representations and distances for specific supervised tasks.
- It provides a practical and state-of-the-art method for document similarity learning, which is a core task in information retrieval, recommendation systems, and text classification.
- Given the strong performance of the original WMD, this supervised extension is likely to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners. The work opens up promising avenues for future research in supervised optimal transport for structured data.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel, principled, and highly effective method for learning a supervised document distance, significantly advancing the state of the art.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The technical approach is elegant, leveraging modern techniques in optimal transport to create an efficient and differentiable learning algorithm.
3.  **Empirical Strength:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and rigorous, with results that compellingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over a large suite of strong baselines.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, making a sophisticated method accessible and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Analysis of Learned Transformation `A`:** The paper provides a nice analysis of the learned word weights `w` (Fig. 2) but lacks a similar analysis for the learned linear transformation `A`. It would be insightful to understand what kind of structure this matrix learns (e.g., identifying or collapsing certain semantic directions).
2.  **Scalability Concerns:** While the O(q^2) complexity per pair is a significant improvement, it may still be a bottleneck for documents with very large numbers of unique words (`q`). A brief discussion on the limitations and potential scalability challenges would be beneficial.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

The paper is already excellent, but the following points could be considered to strengthen it further:

-   **Analysis of `A`:** As mentioned above, an analysis of the learned transformation matrix `A` would be a valuable addition. For instance, the authors could analyze its singular values to see which dimensions of the original word embedding space are emphasized or suppressed. Visualizing how the relative positions of groups of related words change after the transformation (`x -> Ax`) could also provide valuable insights.

-   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The Sinkhorn relaxation introduces a key hyperparameter `λ`. The authors state they use `λ=10`, noting it's a trade-off. A brief ablation study or discussion in the appendix on how performance varies with `λ` would add to the paper's completeness and help practitioners using the method.

-   **Non-squared Ground Metric:** The framework relies on a squared Euclidean ground metric. The original WMD [19] used the L2 norm. The authors hypothesize that this might explain the suboptimal performance on the CLASSIC dataset. It would be interesting to discuss whether the framework could be extended to non-squared distances and the potential challenges involved (e.g., differentiability at the origin). This could be framed as a direction for future work.

Overall, this is an outstanding paper that makes a significant and high-quality contribution to the field. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core strengths of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
