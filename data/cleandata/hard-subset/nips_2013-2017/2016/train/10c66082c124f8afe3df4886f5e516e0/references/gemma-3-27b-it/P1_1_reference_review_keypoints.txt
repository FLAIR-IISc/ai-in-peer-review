- Introduces S-WMD: learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and re-weighted word frequencies to improve Word Mover’s Distance for document similarity.  
- Uses Sinkhorn relaxation and probabilistic filtering to make supervised WMD computationally feasible.  
- Builds on WMD and metric learning (NCA), learning within embedding space and adding word importance weights rather than learning document representations.  
- Strong empirical performance: outperforms 26 baselines across 8 datasets.  
- Novel approach: metric learning inside embedding space with word re-weighting avoids dimensionality-reduction bottlenecks.  
- Computational efficiency: Sinkhorn distance and filtering enable scalability to reasonable dataset sizes.  
- Clear motivation, problem statement, and detailed implementation with gradient derivations and code link.  
- Comprehensive and well-organized related work situating the contribution.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity underexplored; limited details on Bayesian optimization and parameter effects.  
- Lacks deeper theoretical analysis explaining why S-WMD yields gains.  
- Ablation study is limited; unclear individual contributions of the linear transform (A) vs. word weights (w).  
- Choice of squared Euclidean distance is not well-justified and may be suboptimal.  
- Potential overfitting risk from learned word weights, especially on small datasets, and limited discussion of regularization.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, practical method with strong empirical results and reproducible details.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient theoretical grounding and limited robustness analysis (ablation/hyperparameter sensitivity).  
- Overall assessment: strong, practical contribution with convincing results; recommended for acceptance.  
- Recommendation: Accept; Rating: 8.5/10.  
- Suggested improvements: expand ablation study, detail hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity, add regularization discussion, justify distance choice, and provide theoretical insight into learned metric.
