**Summary**
This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds directly upon the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other within a pre-trained word embedding space. The authors' key insight is to make this process supervised by simultaneously learning two components from labeled data: 1) a linear transformation of the word embedding space, which adapts word relationships to the specific classification task, and 2) a vector of word-importance weights, which up-weights words that are discriminative for the task.

A major challenge is the computational complexity of differentiating through the optimal transport problem at the core of WMD. The authors overcome this by replacing the exact transport problem with its entropy-regularized version, the Sinkhorn distance. This makes the objective differentiable and computationally tractable. The model is trained to minimize a stochastic version of the kNN leave-one-out error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments on 8 datasets that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results for kNN classification, outperforming 26 competitive baselines, including WMD and various combinations of document representations with standard metric learning algorithms.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The primary strength of this paper is its conceptual novelty. Instead of following the standard pipeline of first creating a document representation and then applying metric learning, S-WMD integrates metric learning directly into the distance calculation itself. The idea of learning to warp the underlying word-embedding space and re-weight word histograms to optimize the WMD cost for a specific task is a bold and powerful new direction. This is a significant departure from prior work and opens up a new way to think about supervised similarity for structured objects.

2.  **Significant Performance Improvement:** The paper presents compelling empirical evidence of its effectiveness. S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art kNN classification error on 5 out of 8 benchmark datasets, and is highly competitive on the others, outperforming a very large suite of 26 baselines. This includes strong unsupervised methods like WMD and LSI, as well as powerful supervised combinations like TF-IDF+NCA. This clearly demonstrates that the proposed approach substantially advances the state of the art.

3.  **Clever Technical Solution to a Hard Problem:** The authors identify and solve a critical technical hurdle. Naively backpropagating through the WMD linear program is computationally prohibitive and faces issues with non-unique solutions. The use of the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance is an elegant and effective solution that makes the problem tractable, differentiable, and empirically fast. This, combined with a smart initialization scheme and efficient batch gradient descent, makes the entire framework practical.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance)**

1.  **Limited Analysis of the Learned Metric:** While the paper shows *that* the method works, it provides little insight into *why* it works. The learned components—the transformation matrix `A` and the importance weights `w`—are treated as black boxes. The word cloud in Figure 2 is a nice illustration, but a more quantitative analysis would be much more valuable. For example, how does the learned transformation `A` restructure the word embedding space? Does it pull task-relevant synonyms closer or push antonyms further apart? Deeper analysis here would elevate the paper from a strong engineering contribution to a more profound scientific one.

2.  **Reliance on Heuristics in Optimization:** The optimization procedure relies on several approximations and heuristics, such as the WCD-based initialization and the nearest-neighbor filtering for gradient computation. While these choices are well-motivated by efficiency and appear to work well, the paper does not explore their sensitivity. For instance, how does the quality of the WCD-based nearest neighbor set affect the final learned metric? A more thorough ablation study would strengthen the claims of robustness.

3.  **Squared Euclidean Distance Assumption:** The method inherits the squared Euclidean distance from the NCA loss function for its base word-to-word distance. The original WMD paper found L1 or L2 norms to be effective. The authors themselves note this might be suboptimal for the CLASSIC dataset. This is a minor limitation, but it does constrain the flexibility of the learned metric and may explain why the unsupervised WMD occasionally performs better.

**Critique and Recommendation**

This paper presents a highly novel and impactful idea. It successfully bridges the gap between the powerful, unsupervised Word Mover's Distance and the task-specific needs addressed by metric learning.

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The use of the Sinkhorn relaxation is well-justified and the derivation of the gradients appears correct. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and rigorous.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides clear background on the necessary components (word2vec, WMD, Sinkhorn, NCA) and builds its own contribution upon them in a logical and easy-to-follow manner.
*   **Originality:** The originality is outstanding. The core concept of learning a metric *within* the optimal transport framework for documents is, to my knowledge, new and represents a significant conceptual leap. It creatively combines ideas from several fields into a cohesive and powerful model.
*   **Significance:** The significance is high. The paper not only delivers a new state-of-the-art method for a standard NLP task but, more importantly, proposes a new research direction. The S-WMD framework could likely be adapted to other domains where Wasserstein-type distances are used, such as computer vision or computational biology.

While the analysis of the learned components could be deeper, this is a relatively minor flaw compared to the strength of the core contribution. The paper introduces a bold, elegant, and highly effective approach to a fundamental problem. It is exactly the kind of novel work that NIPS should champion.

**Recommendation: Accept**
