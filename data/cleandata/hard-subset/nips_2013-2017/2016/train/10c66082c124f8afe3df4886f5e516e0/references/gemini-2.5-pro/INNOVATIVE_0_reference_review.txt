### Summary
This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds directly upon the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), an unsupervised distance metric that leverages pre-trained word embeddings and optimal transport theory to calculate the "cost" of transforming one document into another. The key innovation is the introduction of supervision into the WMD framework. This is achieved in two ways: (1) by learning a linear transformation of the word embedding space to better align word-level semantics with the task, and (2) by learning a set of word importance weights to re-scale the document's bag-of-words representation, allowing the model to focus on task-relevant words.

To make this learning problem tractable, the authors address the prohibitive computational cost of differentiating through the WMD optimization. They propose a clever solution that replaces the exact optimal transport problem with its entropy-regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance), which can be solved and differentiated through much more efficiently. The model is trained to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). The paper provides an extensive empirical evaluation across eight datasets, demonstrating that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results compared to a large suite of 26 unsupervised and supervised baselines.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper is its **bold and highly novel approach** to learning a document metric. Instead of following the standard pipeline of creating a fixed document vector and then applying a metric learning algorithm, this work integrates metric learning directly into the structure of the WMD. This represents a significant conceptual advance.

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The core idea of learning a linear transformation *within* the word embedding space, guided by the optimal transport cost between documents, is exceptionally creative. It's a non-trivial and elegant fusion of ideas from word embeddings, metric learning, and optimal transport. This opens up a new and promising direction for supervised text similarity and is likely to inspire significant follow-up research.

2.  **Powerful and Intuitive Model:** The two components of the learned metric—the transformation matrix `A` and the word importance vector `w`—are well-motivated. `A` learns to warp the semantic space of words to suit a specific task (e.g., making "good" and "great" closer for sentiment analysis), while `w` provides an explicit mechanism to up-weight discriminative words and down-weight common or irrelevant ones. This combination provides both flexibility and interpretability, as shown by the word cloud visualization in Figure 2.

3.  **Strong Technical Contribution for Practicality:** The authors successfully tackle the main obstacle to their idea: the computational complexity of optimizing WMD. The use of the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable and efficient proxy for the true Wasserstein distance is a crucial technical contribution that makes the entire framework feasible. This, combined with pragmatic optimization strategies like a smart initialization and nearest-neighbor-based batching, demonstrates a thoughtful approach to both theory and implementation.

4.  **Thorough and Compelling Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art kNN classification error on 5 of 8 datasets and the best average rank overall against a very strong set of 26 baselines. This provides clear evidence that the proposed novel idea is not just theoretically interesting but also empirically powerful.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper's weaknesses are relatively minor and largely relate to the pragmatic approximations required to make the ambitious model work, which are acceptable trade-offs for the novelty gained.

1.  **Reliance on Approximations:** The method relies on the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance, which is an approximation of the true WMD. While the results are excellent, a deeper analysis of how the regularization parameter `λ` affects the quality of the learned metric would be beneficial. Similarly, the heuristic of using WCD to filter the nearest neighbors for gradient computation, while effective, lacks theoretical justification and its sensitivity is not explored.

2.  **Non-convex Optimization:** The objective function is non-convex, making the solution dependent on initialization. The authors propose a clever initialization scheme (S-WCD), but the final performance is still subject to the typical challenges of non-convex optimization. This is a common issue in modern machine learning and not a major flaw, but worth noting.

### Arguments for Acceptance/Rejection

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a fundamentally new and significant approach for supervised document distance learning.
*   The conceptual framework is elegant, powerful, and a departure from more incremental improvements in the field.
*   Overcomes significant computational challenges with clever and effective technical solutions.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance with a thorough and convincing set of experiments.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The method is a composition of existing ideas (WMD, NCA, Sinkhorn). However, their synthesis is highly non-trivial and creates a novel method that is far greater than the sum of its parts. This argument is not compelling.
*   The theoretical grounding for some of the optimization heuristics is light. This is a minor point, as the empirical success provides strong validation for these design choices.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, creative, and powerful method for supervised document similarity. It pushes the boundaries of how we think about learning text metrics by embedding the learning process within the optimal transport problem itself. The technical contributions that make this ambitious idea practical are significant, and the state-of-the-art empirical results provide strong validation. While there are minor points regarding the approximations used, they are well-justified trade-offs for achieving a practical implementation of a bold new idea. This work is a perfect fit for NIPS, prioritizing a significant new direction over incremental methodological refinement. I strongly recommend acceptance.
