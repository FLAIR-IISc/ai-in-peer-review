This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by Kusner et al. (2015), which defines the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to transport words from one to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. The authors correctly identify that WMD is entirely unsupervised and thus may not be optimal for specific classification tasks.

The main idea is to learn two components to make the WMD distance supervised: 1) a linear transformation matrix `A` that projects the word vectors into a new space where the ground distance is more meaningful for the task, and 2) a vector `w` of word-importance weights that re-scales the document's bag-of-words representation to emphasize task-relevant words. To learn `A` and `w`, the authors adopt a loss function from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) by Goldberger et al. (2005), which provides a differentiable proxy for the leave-one-out k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) classification error.

A key challenge is the prohibitive computational cost of differentiating through the WMD optimization problem. The paper's main technical contribution is a practical solution to this problem. Instead of the exact WMD, which is an instance of the Earth Mover's Distance, they use the entropy-regularized version proposed by Cuturi (2013), known as the Sinkhorn distance. This relaxation is not only significantly faster to compute (O(q^2) vs O(q^3 log q)) but also strictly convex, yielding a unique, differentiable solution. The authors derive the gradients of this relaxed distance with respect to `A` and `w`. To further improve efficiency, they propose a clever training scheme involving a fast initialization of `A` using the Word Centroid Distance (WCD), batch gradient descent, and a nearest-neighbor filtering strategy to reduce the number of pairs considered in each batch.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the supervised learning problem is well-motivated, and the use of the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable and efficient proxy for WMD is an appropriate and effective choice. The gradient derivations in equations (8) and (10) appear correct and are central to the method's feasibility. The claims of state-of-the-art performance are strongly supported by a comprehensive experimental evaluation. The authors compare their method against 26 strong baselines across 8 diverse datasets. The baselines include classic document representations (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), modern neural methods (mSDA), and the original WMD/WCD. Crucially, they also compare against these representations after applying standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN), which constitutes a very thorough and fair comparison. The use of Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning further strengthens the validity of the results. The work is a complete piece of research, presenting a novel algorithm, theoretical justification for its efficiency, and extensive empirical validation.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the background section provides the necessary context on word2vec, WMD, regularized optimal transport, and NCA, making the paper accessible. The proposed method is developed logically in Section 3, clearly explaining the objective function, the computational challenges, and the techniques used to overcome them. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 provides a concise summary of the training procedure. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, and the visualizations in Figure 1 (t-SNE) and Figure 2 (word cloud) effectively illustrate the benefits of the learned metric.

**Originality:**
The paper presents a novel and significant contribution. While the constituent components (WMD, NCA, Sinkhorn distance) are not new, their combination to create a practical, supervised document distance metric is highly original. Prior work has explored learning the ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance (e.g., Wang et al. 2012), but this paper is the first to successfully apply this concept to the WMD for text classification. Furthermore, the simultaneous learning of a transformation matrix `A` and word importance weights `w` is a novel aspect not present in previous work on learning EMD metrics. The efficient optimization scheme, which is crucial for making the method practical, is also a key part of the paper's original contribution. The work is clearly differentiated from prior work and related literature is adequately cited.

**Significance:**
The results presented are highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact. WMD has become a benchmark document distance metric, and this paper provides a powerful, task-specific extension. The method achieves state-of-the-art kNN classification error on 5 out of 8 datasets against an extensive suite of strong baselines, demonstrating a clear advance in the state of the art. The problem of learning task-specific document similarity is fundamental to many areas of NLP and information retrieval. Given the strong performance and the fact that the authors have made the code available, it is likely that other researchers and practitioners will use and build upon S-WMD. **Strengths:**
*   **Novelty and Impact:** Proposes a novel and effective method for learning a supervised version of the influential Word Mover's Distance, addressing a clear limitation of the original work.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The use of the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance to make the optimization problem tractable is an elegant and technically sound solution.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is shown to outperform a large number of competitive baselines on multiple datasets, demonstrating a significant improvement in the state of the art for kNN document classification.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, making a technically complex topic easy to understand.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The method's performance relies on a kNN-based objective function (NCA), which may not be optimal for tasks other than kNN classification (e.g., document retrieval or clustering).
*   The computational complexity, while greatly improved over a naive approach, is still quadratic in the number of unique words in a document pair. This could be a bottleneck for very long documents compared to methods that use a fixed-size vector representation.
*   The choice of the regularization parameter λ is mentioned as a trade-off, but no sensitivity analysis is provided to guide its selection.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound method for a significant problem. The strong empirical results, backed by a thorough evaluation, demonstrate a clear advance in the field. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the contribution.
