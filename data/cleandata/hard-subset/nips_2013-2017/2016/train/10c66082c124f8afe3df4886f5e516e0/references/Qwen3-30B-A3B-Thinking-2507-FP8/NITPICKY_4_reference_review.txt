

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), which extends the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by learning a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to improve document classification. The authors claim to achieve state-of-the-art results across 8 datasets with 26 baselines.

## Strengths
The paper presents a novel approach to incorporating supervision into the WMD framework, which is a significant contribution to the field of document distances. The use of Sinkhorn distance for efficient computation is well-motivated, and the experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over unsupervised baselines on several datasets. The paper is well-organized and provides sufficient technical details for reproduction.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Technical Inaccuracies (Major)
The paper contains several technical inaccuracies that undermine its credibility:
- Equation (5) uses "‖A(xi − xj)‖22" instead of "‖A(xi − xj)‖2^2" or "‖A(xi − xj)‖2^2", a clear typographical error.
- The claim that "the time complexity of solving the WMD optimization problem is O(q3 log q)" contradicts Kusner et al. [19], which states O(q^3) without the log q factor.
- The assertion that "T*λ is always unique as the relaxed transport is strongly convex" is incorrect; Cuturi [6] shows the Sinkhorn distance is convex but not strictly convex, so uniqueness is not guaranteed.
- The paper states "WCD always lower bounds WMD" but this relationship depends on the value of p used for WMD (the paper uses p=1 or 2), which is not properly qualified.

### 2. Formatting and Presentation Issues (Minor but Significant)
- "th Conference" in the header should be "The Conference" (typo).
- In equation (6), "m∑ a=1" should be "∑_{a=1}^m".
- "∂ ∂(A,w)" should be "∂/∂(A,w)" for proper notation.
- The claim that "S-WMD outperforms all other 26 methods" on average is not substantiated by the table, which doesn't show the average rank calculation.
- The WMD is incorrectly described as "a special case of the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD), also known more generally as the Wasserstein distance" - the correct relationship is that WMD is a special case of EMD, which itself is a special case of the Wasserstein distance.

### 3. Methodological Clarifications Needed
- The paper claims "S-WMD achieves the lowest kNN test error on 5/8 datasets" but doesn't clarify that it's tied for first on 20NEWS, making the claim somewhat misleading.
- The relationship between WCD and WMD depends on the p value used for WMD, which isn't properly addressed.
- The paper states "the WCD always lower bounds the WMD" but this is only true under specific conditions that aren't specified.
- The paper doesn't clarify whether word embeddings were fine-tuned or used as fixed (as claimed in the conclusion).

### 4. Related Work
The related work section fails to properly contrast with Wang et al. [35], which also learns a metric within a generalized Euclidean EMD framework. The paper should explicitly state that the key difference is the histogram re-weighting (which the authors claim is "extra flexibility"), but this isn't clearly articulated.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a novel idea that builds upon the WMD framework, the technical inaccuracies and formatting inconsistencies are substantial enough to raise concerns about the paper's rigor. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find it difficult to overlook these minor but significant flaws, especially when they undermine the technical claims of the paper.

The paper contains 12 distinct minor flaws that collectively indicate a lack of meticulous attention to detail. Given the stringent standards of NIPS, these issues outweigh the paper's strengths, particularly since the technical inaccuracies could mislead readers about the computational complexity and theoretical guarantees of the proposed method.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of minor but critical flaws that affect the paper's credibility, even though the core idea is interesting and the experimental results are promising. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmission.

The paper has significant potential but needs careful revision to fix the technical inaccuracies and presentation issues before it can be considered for publication.
