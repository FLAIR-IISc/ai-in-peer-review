Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of WMD that learns a linear transformation A of pre-trained word embeddings and a global word-importance vector w to better align document distances with labels. To make learning computationally feasible, the authors replace exact optimal transport with entropically regularized transport (Sinkhorn), use the resulting transport plan and dual potentials to form approximate gradients via envelope/Danskin-like arguments, and optimize a stochastic-NCA leave-one-out (LOO) objective using batch updates and a WCD-based neighbor filter. Experiments across 8 datasets and 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised, including NCA/LMNN/ITML on various document representations) show improved kNN classification accuracy on 5/8 datasets, with competitive run times and released Matlab code.

Relation to prior work
This work builds on (i) WMD (Kusner et al., ICML’15), (ii) entropic OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS’13), and (iii) NCA and metric learning methods (Goldberger et al.; LMNN; ITML). Prior supervised EMD/OT learning has been considered (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al. for images). The novelty here is to learn a linear ground metric in embedding space specifically for text and to introduce global word weights w, while employing Sinkhorn for efficient approximate gradients and a stochastic-NCA loss.

Strengths
- Tackles a sensible problem: task-adapted distances can materially improve WMD-based retrieval/classification.
- Reasonable technical design: learning A in low-dim word space avoids quadratic scaling in vocabulary, and entropic OT makes training practical.
- Clear empirical focus on strong baselines, including metric learning on TF-IDF/LSI/LDA/mSDA, WCD, and WMD.
- Code availability and some attention to computational efficiency (neighbor filtering, batching).
- The initialization via S-WCD is practical and seems to matter.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Gradient correctness and justification: The paper relies on replacing the exact optimal plan/dual with the entropically-regularized Sinkhorn plan and potentials, then applies envelope/Danskin-style arguments to ignore dependence of T*λ on A in ∂D/∂A (Eq. 8). This deserves a precise statement of conditions (differentiability, uniqueness, applicability of Danskin with entropic regularization) and a proper derivation. As written, it is heuristic.
- Gradient w.r.t. w (Eq. 10) uses approximated dual variables α*λ, β*λ derived from u, v. The given formula contains unclear terms (e.g., division by “p” where p has been used for multiple meanings, normalization by ones vector), and constraints on w (nonnegativity) are not specified. If w can go negative, the histogram d̃ may be invalid. A projection or parameterization (e.g., exp) should be reported.
- The objective actually optimized differs from the stated NCA-LOO: the neighbor filtering (restricting to Na via WCD) changes the loss without analysis of bias or convergence; no guarantees are provided.
- Regularization is not discussed. A and w could easily overfit, especially on small datasets, and there is no mention of Frobenius/L2 penalties, low-rank constraints on A, or sparsity on w.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity is absent. Performance likely depends on λ, batch size, N (neighbors), r (embedding dimension after A), and learning rates. No ablation or sensitivity analysis is provided.
- Experimental protocol details are insufficient for reproducibility: selection of validation sets for BO, controlling for test leakage (e.g., LDA trained “transductively”), and consistency across baselines are unclear.

Clarity
- Several notational and editorial issues: “p” denotes both Lp exponent and the number of unique words; missing/garbled references ([5, 21?]), typos, and incomplete equations undermine readability.
- Algorithmic details are compressed (e.g., constraints/enforcement on w, rank/shape of A, exact tuning ranges, stopping criteria).
- Tables/figures referenced are not shown in the text provided; the narrative alone makes it difficult to assess effect sizes.

Originality
- The combination—learning a linear ground metric with supervised signals for WMD using Sinkhorn—is incremental over established components. The introduction of a global word-importance vector w is straightforward (akin to supervised idf). Prior works have learned ground metrics for EMD and used Sinkhorn; the novelty lies in applying these ideas to word embeddings for text and in the practical training scheme. This is a reasonable, but not groundbreaking, step.

Significance
- Improvements on kNN classification across 8 datasets are potentially useful. However, the evaluation is limited to kNN; the community also expects comparisons to strong linear classifiers (e.g., logistic regression/SVM on TF-IDF) and contemporary neural baselines where appropriate. It is unclear whether S-WMD provides advantages in retrieval settings or beyond kNN. Without broader tasks and stronger baselines, impact is uncertain.
- The gains appear dataset-dependent; S-WMD is not consistently superior and sometimes trails simpler supervised baselines. An ablation showing the marginal benefit of w vs A, and the contribution of the Sinkhorn-based fine-tuning over S-WCD initialization, is essential to justify complexity.

Questions for authors
1. What constraints/parameterization are imposed on w? Is there a projection to w ≥ 0? Any regularization on w or A?
2. Can you provide a formal justification for the gradients via Danskin/envelope theorem under entropic regularization, and clarify the role of ∂T*λ/∂A?
3. How sensitive are results to λ, N (neighbor set size), and the dimensionality r of A?
4. What validation protocol was used for BO to avoid test leakage? Why is LDA trained transductively?
5. How much does S-WMD improve over S-WCD initialization on each dataset? Provide exact numbers and ablations (A-only, w-only).
6. Can you compare against linear SVM/logistic regression on TF-IDF and a simple neural baseline?

Pros
- Practical extension of a popular distance with a scalable training procedure.
- Broad baseline coverage; code release.
- Sensible use of word embeddings; low-dimensional ground metric learning.

Cons
- Insufficient theoretical justification for gradients; missing constraints/regularization.
- Clarity/notation issues; several editorial problems.
- Limited evaluation scope (kNN only), weak ablations, and no sensitivity analyses.
- Incremental novelty.

Recommendation: Weak reject
With stronger theoretical clarity (gradients and constraints), thorough ablations and sensitivity studies, and comparisons to stronger supervised baselines and tasks beyond kNN, this work could be compelling. As it stands, the contribution feels incremental and under-validated relative to established methods.
