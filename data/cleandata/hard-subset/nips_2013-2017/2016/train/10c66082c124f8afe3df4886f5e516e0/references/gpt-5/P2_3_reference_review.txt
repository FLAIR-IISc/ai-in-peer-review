Summary
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparison. Rather than learning a document representation, S-WMD learns (i) a linear transform A on pre-trained word embeddings that adjusts the ground cost in the optimal transport (OT) formulation, and (ii) a vector of word-importance weights w that reweights document histograms. The learning objective is an NCA-style stochastic leave-one-out k-NN loss, with distances computed via the WMD formulation but trained using an entropically regularized OT (Sinkhorn) relaxation to obtain tractable and differentiable approximations. Gradients w.r.t. A use the standard fact that the derivative of the optimal transport cost w.r.t. cost entries is the optimal plan; gradients w.r.t. w are obtained from the dual potentials, which the authors approximate from the Sinkhorn scaling vectors u, v. Training is accelerated by (a) initializing A via NCA on word centroid representations (WCD), (b) batch updates, and (c) restricting pairwise computations to a WCD-based nearest-neighbor candidate set. On eight text datasets, S-WMD reportedly achieves the best average rank across 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised, including NCA/LMNN/ITML on various representations) and is best on 5/8 datasets. Code is released.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The approach is well-motivated and leverages known properties of OT and its entropic relaxation. Using T* as the gradient of the regularized OT objective w.r.t. the cost is standard; using dual potentials to obtain gradients w.r.t. the marginals is also consistent with entropic OT. Approximating the true WMD’s subgradient by the Sinkhorn plan/duals is reasonable and provides a smooth surrogate for optimization.
- Claims vs. support: The empirical section is reasonably comprehensive for a distance-learning paper: multiple datasets, many baselines (including both representations and supervised metric learners), initialization ablations, training-time comparisons, and qualitative visualizations. The results indicate meaningful improvements on average and often per dataset.
- Caveats:
  - Constraints and regularization are under-specified. For w to define valid histograms, w should be constrained to be nonnegative; this constraint and how it is enforced is not clearly stated. Similarly, it would help to specify whether A is regularized (e.g., Frobenius norm, low-rank r ≤ d) to prevent overfitting and degenerate scalings.
  - Sensitivity and ablation: Limited analysis of λ, batch size, neighbor set size N, and the contributions of A vs. w. The method’s dependence on the WCD-based initialization is acknowledged but not fully quantified.
  - Evaluation scope: k-NN accuracy is a narrow endpoint. It would strengthen claims to include retrieval metrics or to demonstrate that S-WMD helps beyond k-NN (e.g., as a kernel/feature for linear classifiers).
  - Minor technical clarity: The stated complexity expression mixes terms in a way that is hard to verify; a clearer derivation or empirical scaling plot would help. Also, please clarify whether test-time distances for k-NN are computed with exact WMD under learned A, w, or with the Sinkhorn approximation.
Overall, the methodology is credible and the empirical evidence is solid, with some missing details that are fixable.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well-organized, with a helpful background section that situates word embeddings, WMD, Sinkhorn OT, and NCA.
- Positive: The objective and gradient expressions are explicit; the algorithmic accelerations are clearly stated; visualizations help intuition.
- Areas to improve:
  - Fix typos/formatting (e.g., “.2 Optimization”, some broken references like [21?], inconsistent notation for dimensions p/q/n; clarify p as vocabulary size in neighborhood sets vs. unique words per doc).
  - Explicitly state constraints on w (nonnegativity) and how enforced (e.g., parameterize w = softplus(z) or w = exp(z)).
  - Clarify whether A is full or low-rank and if there is any regularization or orthogonality.
  - Provide exact hyperparameters for M, N, batch size, learning rates, and convergence criteria; include sensitivity plots if possible.

Originality
- The paper builds on WMD, entropic OT (Sinkhorn), and metric learning (NCA). Learning ground metrics for EMD has precedent in computer vision (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; supervised EMD variants), but the combination for text with pre-trained word embeddings, joint learning of A and word weights w, and training via an NCA objective with efficient Sinkhorn-based gradients is novel and well-positioned for NLP.
- Related work is largely covered; a more explicit comparison to prior supervised EMD learning and a clearer articulation of what is technically new (dual-based gradient for w in the entropic setup, WCD-neighborhood filtering) would help.

Significance
- Practical significance is high: WMD has seen use in text; a supervised, learnable variant that remains computationally feasible is valuable. The empirical improvements over strong baselines across diverse datasets suggest impact.
- Scientific significance is moderate to high: It extends OT-based document distances with a principled supervised learning framework and contributes practical training tricks that others can build on.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and well-motivated formulation; principled use of Sinkhorn for tractable learning.
- Joint learning of ground metric and word importance is effective.
- Strong empirical results across many baselines and datasets; released code.
- Practical training accelerations and reasonable runtime.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing or unclear constraints/regularization for w and A; limited sensitivity analysis.
- Evaluation centered on k-NN only; broader downstream evidence would strengthen impact.
- Some clarity/formatting issues and minor technical ambiguities (complexity, test-time distance).

Recommendation
Overall, the paper meets the NeurIPS criteria well: technically sound with reasonable proofs/derivations, generally clear, incrementally original but with a compelling and useful combination, and significant empirically. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying constraints/regularization, test-time distance computation, and adding brief sensitivity/ablation analyses.
