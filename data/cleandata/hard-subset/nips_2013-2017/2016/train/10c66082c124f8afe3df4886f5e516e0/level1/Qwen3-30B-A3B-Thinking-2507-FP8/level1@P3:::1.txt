

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) framework that incorporates supervision to improve document classification performance. Building on Kusner et al.'s WMD (ICML 2015), which computes document distances as optimal transport costs between word embeddings, S-WMD introduces a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights to learn a metric that better aligns with class labels. The authors formulate a loss function that minimizes stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification error, and address the computational challenge of nested optimization through a Sinkhorn distance approximation, reducing complexity from O(q³ log q) to O(q²).

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it successfully extends WMD to a supervised setting, addressing an important gap in the literature. Second, the authors provide a technically sound solution to the computational bottleneck through the Sinkhorn relaxation, with clear justification for its use. Third, they conduct comprehensive experiments across 8 datasets (including news categorization, sentiment analysis, and product identification) against 26 baselines, demonstrating consistent improvement over unsupervised WMD and other document representations. The results show S-WMD achieves the lowest kNN error on 5/8 datasets and outperforms all other methods on average.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with an effective background section that connects WMD to related metric learning work. The algorithm description is precise, and the visualization in Figure 1 compellingly demonstrates S-WMD's improved document clustering. The authors also provide a thoughtful discussion of limitations, noting that S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON datasets, and attributing the CLASSIC result to potential initialization issues.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The authors do not sufficiently analyze why S-WMD underperforms on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON, despite these being important exceptions to the overall trend. While they mention a possible explanation for CLASSIC (poor initialization), they provide no empirical analysis for the other two datasets. The initialization approach (S-WCD) is described but not rigorously evaluated against alternatives. The theoretical analysis of the approximation error introduced by the Sinkhorn relaxation is limited—while the authors state that "as λ grows, the approximation becomes more accurate," they provide no bounds or empirical analysis of this error. Additionally, the paper does not compare with more recent metric learning methods published after WMD was introduced, which would provide better context for the claimed improvements.

The significance of the work is high, as document classification is a fundamental NLP task, and WMD has established itself as a strong baseline. S-WMD provides a simple yet effective way to incorporate supervision into WMD, with practical efficiency as demonstrated in Table 3. The method represents a clear step forward in document distance metrics with potential for broad application.

Strengths:
- Novel extension of WMD to supervised learning with clear motivation
- Effective computational solution to nested optimization problem
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across 8 datasets against 26 baselines
- Well-organized presentation with clear technical explanations
- Practical significance for document classification tasks

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient analysis of failure cases (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON)
- Limited theoretical analysis of approximation error
- Initialization method not rigorously evaluated
- No comparison with more recent metric learning methods

Pro/Con Acceptance:
Pro: This is a well-executed extension of a significant prior work that makes a clear contribution to the field. The method is theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and empirically validated across multiple datasets. It represents a natural and valuable progression from WMD.

Con: The paper would be stronger with more thorough analysis of the three datasets where S-WMD underperforms and a more comprehensive comparison with recent metric learning literature. The theoretical justification for the approximation error could be strengthened.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of document distances. While not perfect, the method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. The authors have addressed a natural extension of WMD that has significant practical value. I recommend acceptance.
