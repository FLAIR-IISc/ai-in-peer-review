

**Peer Review: Supervised Word Mover's Distance**

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel extension of Kusner et al.'s Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to learn document distances better aligned with class labels. While WMD leverages word embeddings to compute document distances as optimal transport costs between word embeddings, S-WMD learns an affine transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights to improve classification performance. The authors address the computational challenge of learning this metric by using a relaxed optimal transport approximation (Sinkhorn distance) and propose an efficient gradient update rule. The method is evaluated on 8 text classification datasets, consistently outperforming 26 baselines including unsupervised WMD, WCD, BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, and various metric learning approaches.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a well-motivated approach to a meaningful problem. The authors provide a rigorous derivation of the gradient approximation, addressing the computational challenge of learning the metric. The experimental design is comprehensive, with evaluation across 8 datasets and 26 baselines. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., performance on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON) and provides reasonable explanations for observed results. The implementation details and code availability enhance reproducibility. The authors carefully evaluate both strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with consistent mathematical notation. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by highlighting the limitations of unsupervised document distances. The technical sections provide sufficient detail for reproduction, though the gradient derivation in Section 3.1 may be challenging for non-experts. The t-SNE visualizations in Figure 1 effectively demonstrate the improvement over WMD. The paper would benefit from a more concise related work section (Section 5) to better highlight the novelty compared to Wang et al. (2016) who also learn a metric within EMD but without histogram re-weighting.

**Originality:** The paper builds directly on WMD (Kusner et al., NIPS 2015) and metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML), but the specific combination of supervised learning with WMD is novel. The authors clearly distinguish their work from related approaches, particularly noting that Wang et al. (2016) learn a metric within EMD but do not consider re-weighting the histograms, which provides S-WMD with extra flexibility. The use of Sinkhorn distance for approximation is not entirely novel but is applied effectively to address the computational challenge. The paper demonstrates that incorporating supervision into WMD is a meaningful extension with significant empirical gains.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant, with S-WMD achieving the lowest error rate on 5/8 datasets and outperforming all 26 baselines on average. The improvements are consistent across diverse domains (news categorization, sentiment analysis, product identification), suggesting broad applicability. The method is efficient (training times comparable to baselines), making it practical for real-world use. The paper provides a clear explanation of why supervision helps—particularly for capturing task-specific word importance—which adds theoretical value beyond just empirical performance.

**Strengths:**
- Clear problem statement and well-motivated solution
- Rigorous mathematical derivation of the gradient approximation
- Comprehensive evaluation across 8 datasets with 26 baselines
- Efficient implementation with reasonable training times (Table 3)
- Effective visualization of improvements via t-SNE embeddings
- Code availability for reproducibility

**Weaknesses:**
- The explanation for poor performance on BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, and AMAZON could be more thorough
- The initialization strategy (S-WCD) is not sufficiently motivated
- The paper could better distinguish its contribution from Wang et al. (2016) in the introduction
- The significance of the word importance weights could be more clearly articulated

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- S-WMD represents a meaningful and well-executed extension of WMD
- The method is both theoretically sound and practically efficient
- The evaluation demonstrates consistent and significant improvements
- The paper provides clear implementation details and code
- The approach has clear potential for broader adoption in text classification

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The novelty is incremental (building on WMD and metric learning)
- The explanation for performance variations across datasets is limited
- The paper could better highlight the unique contributions compared to similar work

This is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field. The authors address a meaningful problem with a technically sound solution that demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple datasets. While the novelty is incremental, it's a significant step forward with clear practical value. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better highlight the novelty compared to related work and to more thoroughly explain the performance variations across datasets.
