

# Review: Supervised Word Mover's Distance

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) introduced by Kusner et al. at NIPS 2015. While WMD leverages pre-trained word embeddings to compute document distances as optimal transport between word vectors, it remains entirely unsupervised. S-WMD introduces supervision by learning a linear transformation of the word embedding space (matrix A) and word importance weights (vector w) to minimize leave-one-out classification error. The authors address computational challenges by approximating the WMD using the Sinkhorn distance with an entropy regularizer, enabling efficient gradient computation. The method is evaluated across 8 text classification datasets against 26 baselines including unsupervised WMD, WCD, BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, and mSDA, with supervised metric learning applied to these representations.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The authors provide a clear theoretical derivation of the gradient for both the linear transformation and word importance weights, addressing the nested optimization challenge of WMD through the Sinkhorn approximation. The experimental design is comprehensive, with consistent performance improvements across 8 datasets. The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations, noting that S-WMD's performance can be affected by initialization (particularly on the CLASSIC dataset) and that the squared Euclidean distance may be suboptimal for certain datasets. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including implementation specifics and hyperparameter tuning procedures.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section effectively introduces word embeddings, WMD, and metric learning, establishing clear connections to prior work. The proposed method is explained with precise mathematical formulations, and the algorithm pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is clear. The experimental section provides thorough details about datasets, baselines, and evaluation metrics. The visualization in Figure 1 effectively demonstrates the improved clustering of documents after supervised training, and Figure 2 provides insightful examples of learned word importance weights.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by incorporating supervision into the WMD framework, which was previously unsupervised. While the Sinkhorn approximation was introduced by Cuturi at NIPS 2013, its application to supervised WMD represents a novel combination of techniques. The authors properly distinguish their work from related metric learning approaches (NCA, LMNN, ITML), noting that their method operates directly on the word embedding space rather than document representations. The paper demonstrates a significant advancement over the unsupervised WMD framework, with clear evidence of how their approach differs from prior work.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for the text classification community. S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple datasets, outperforming all 26 baselines on average. The paper demonstrates that supervision can substantially improve WMD, which was previously unsupervised. The method's efficiency (as shown in Table 3) makes it practical for large-scale applications, and the approach can be applied to any pre-trained word embedding without requiring additional training on labeled data. The results are particularly impressive given the competitive nature of text classification benchmarks.

**Strengths:**
1. Clear theoretical contribution to extending WMD with supervision
2. Efficient computational approach using Sinkhorn approximation
3. Comprehensive experimental evaluation across 8 datasets with 26 baselines
4. Strong empirical results showing consistent improvements
5. Well-written with clear mathematical derivations and visualizations

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper doesn't thoroughly explore why the squared Euclidean distance might be suboptimal for certain datasets (like CLASSIC), which could be a limitation for some applications.
2. The initialization approach (using NCA on WCD) might not be optimal for all datasets, as evidenced by the CLASSIC dataset results.
3. The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the full training process compared to alternative metric learning approaches.
4. The paper doesn't explicitly compare against other supervised extensions of WMD or related document distance metrics that might have been proposed after the NIPS 2015 WMD paper.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The paper makes a clear, meaningful contribution to the document distance literature by incorporating supervision into WMD
- The method is theoretically sound and practically efficient
- The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple datasets
- The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making it accessible to the NIPS audience
- The approach has clear practical applications for text classification tasks

*Con:*
- The novelty is somewhat incremental over the WMD framework (which was itself a significant contribution)
- The computational complexity analysis, while presented, could be more detailed
- The paper doesn't fully address why certain datasets (like CLASSIC) are exceptions to the general trend

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a strong technical contribution that advances the state of the art in document distance metrics. The combination of supervised learning with the WMD framework is novel and well-executed, with clear theoretical justification and empirical validation. The method is both theoretically sound and practically useful, with consistent improvements across multiple datasets. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. While the novelty is incremental relative to the WMD paper, it represents a significant improvement in the application of WMD to supervised text classification tasks.

The paper meets all four criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel combination of techniques (Originality), and demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods (Significance). The results are important for the text classification community and could influence future work in document similarity metrics. I recommend acceptance.
