## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised metric on top of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights by minimizing the leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by utilizing a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and a probabilistic filtering approach. They demonstrate strong performance on eight text classification datasets, consistently outperforming 26 competitive baselines.

This work builds directly on the recent success of WMD (Kusner et al., NIPS 2016) and addresses a key limitation – its unsupervised nature. The authors correctly identify the need for task-specific adaptation of document distances and position their work within the broader context of metric learning. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to enable efficient gradient computation is a clever and practical contribution, addressing a significant bottleneck in supervised WMD learning. The paper also appropriately references and compares against a comprehensive set of baselines, including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) and metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML).

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent outperformance across eight datasets and against a large number of baselines is compelling evidence of the effectiveness of S-WMD.
*   **Practical Efficiency:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering makes the method computationally feasible, a critical aspect for real-world applications.
*   **Clear and Well-Motivated:** The paper clearly articulates the problem, the proposed solution, and its advantages over existing approaches.
*   **Thorough Comparison:** The extensive comparison against a diverse set of baselines strengthens the validity of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors provide intuition for the method, a more rigorous theoretical analysis of the convergence properties and generalization bounds would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of metric learning algorithms is often sensitive to hyperparameter settings. A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and its impact on the results would be valuable.
*   **Squared Euclidean Distance:** The choice of squared Euclidean distance in the optimal transport problem might be suboptimal for certain datasets, as noted by the authors. Exploring alternative distance metrics could potentially improve performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective method for supervised document distance learning.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing approaches.
*   The proposed technique is practical and scalable due to the efficient gradient computation method.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is limited.
*   The sensitivity to hyperparameters and the choice of distance metric could be further investigated.

**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of document distance learning. The strong empirical results, practical efficiency, and clear presentation outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding the theoretical analysis and hyperparameter sensitivity.
