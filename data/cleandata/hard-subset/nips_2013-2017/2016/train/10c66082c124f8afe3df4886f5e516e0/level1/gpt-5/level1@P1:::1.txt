Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) of Kusner et al. (ICML 2015). WMD measures document distances as the optimal transport (OT) cost of moving normalized bag-of-words mass from one document to another in a pre-trained word embedding space (e.g., word2vec). S-WMD incorporates supervision by jointly learning (i) a linear transformation A applied to the word embedding space and (ii) a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. Learning minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out (LOO) kNN objective in the spirit of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), where the distance between documents is given by the (supervised) OT problem under ground cost ‖A(xi−xj)‖^2 and reweighted histograms d̃. Because differentiating through the exact WMD leads to a nested, cubic-time LP with non-smooth solutions, the authors use the entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) relaxation (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) to obtain efficient, differentiable approximations: the transport T*λ and the dual potentials α*, β* derived from the Sinkhorn scaling vectors u, v. They initialize A by running NCA on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) embedding (S-WCD), and optimize with mini-batch gradient descent while restricting neighborhoods using a fast WCD-based filter.

Empirically, on eight text classification datasets spanning topics, sentiment, and product identification, S-WMD with kNN consistently outperforms most of 26 baselines, including unsupervised WMD/WCD, BOW/TF-IDF/BM25/LSI/LDA/mSDA, and three classic metric learning methods (NCA/LMNN/ITML) applied to these representations. Visualizations show improved class clustering, and the learned word weights appear interpretable.

Quality:
- Methodological soundness: The formulation is well-motivated and leverages established components: NCA-style stochastic LOO loss, Sinkhorn-regularized OT for efficiency and differentiability, and metric learning of a linear ground cost. Gradients for A are derived via the envelope theorem using the optimal transport plan; gradients for w use dual potentials approximated from the Sinkhorn scaling. The approximation choice is reasonable and standard in modern OT.
- Computational considerations are thoughtfully addressed: Sinkhorn reduces per-pair cost to O(p^2), and the neighborhood filtering plus batching make training practical.
- Empirical evaluation is broad (8 datasets, 26 baselines), with hyperparameters tuned by Bayesian optimization and public code. Results are generally strong and consistent.

Potential issues:
- The paper optimizes a regularized OT objective while evaluating with the same objective (during training); it argues this approximates the unregularized WMD but does not quantify the bias introduced by λ or give sensitivity analyses. A discussion of convergence to the unregularized target or performance vs λ would strengthen the claims.
- Constraints/regularization on w and A are under-specified. Since d̃ depends on w and must remain a valid histogram, w should be nonnegative. The paper does not state how nonnegativity is enforced, nor whether A and w are regularized (e.g., L2/L1 penalties) to prevent overfitting or extreme scaling.
- The approach remains tailored to kNN; it would be informative to test whether the learned A and w also improve downstream classifiers (e.g., linear SVM on WCD after A, or retrieval tasks).
- Comparative coverage: while classic text baselines and metric learning methods are included, comparisons to strong supervised neural baselines circa 2014–2016 (e.g., CNNs for sentence/document classification, Paragraph Vector/doc2vec, fastText) are missing. Also closely related supervised OT/ground-metric learning works (e.g., Cuturi & Avis’s ground metric learning; Wang & Guibas’s supervised EMD) are cited but not compared experimentally.
- Some derivational details are terse. For example, the use of envelope theorem with entropy-regularized OT justifies ignoring dT*/dA, but a short justification would help clarity; similarly, explicit forms for the gradients of d̃ w.r.t. w and conditions ensuring uniqueness/stability of dual potentials would aid reproducibility.

Clarity:
The paper is largely clear and well-organized, with a helpful background section and practical algorithmic details (initialization, batching, neighbor filtering). A few notational/typographical issues remain: overloaded use of p (power vs dimension), a missing subsection number (“.2 Optimization”), and the lack of explicit constraints on w. Reporting the learned dimensionality r of A and any regularizers would improve transparency. Including an ablation (A-only, w-only, both) would clarify contributions.

Originality:
The combination of (i) learning a supervised ground metric within the word embedding space and (ii) jointly learning word-importance weights, optimized end-to-end with a stochastic kNN objective and Sinkhorn-based gradients, is novel in the NLP/WMD context. It draws from prior metric learning (NCA/LMNN/ITML) and OT (Sinkhorn) and adapts them thoughtfully to document distances. Related supervised EMD/ground metric learning existed in vision; the extension to word embeddings plus histogram reweighting is a meaningful advance.

Significance:
Given the popularity of WMD and OT, a practical supervised variant that yields consistent gains across diverse datasets is useful and likely to be adopted. The method is broadly applicable to retrieval and classification with minimal feature engineering and interpretable outputs (word weights). The empirical improvements over many strong baselines suggest real impact, although broader comparisons to supervised neural models would contextualize its standing in end-to-end classification.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated extension of WMD that leverages supervision effectively.
- Efficient and practical training via Sinkhorn and neighborhood filtering.
- Strong, consistent empirical gains across many datasets and baselines; code available.
- Interpretable word weights and sensible visualizations.
- Conceptually simple and likely to be used by practitioners familiar with WMD.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited theoretical discussion of the approximation gap (effect of λ) and lack of sensitivity analyses.
- Under-specified constraints/regularization on w and A; potential overfitting not analyzed.
- Evaluation limited to kNN; missing comparisons to strong supervised neural baselines of the era.
- Minor clarity/notation issues and missing ablations (A-only vs w-only).

Recommendation:
Lean accept. The paper presents a solid, practical, and impactful supervised variant of WMD with careful engineering and convincing empirical results. Addressing the noted clarity issues, adding ablations/sensitivity studies, and expanding comparisons to supervised neural baselines would further strengthen an already strong submission.
