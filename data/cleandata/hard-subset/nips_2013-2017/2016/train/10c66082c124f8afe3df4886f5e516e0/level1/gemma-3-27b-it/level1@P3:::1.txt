This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised metric for document similarity building upon the recent Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) framework. The core idea is to learn an affine transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights to minimize leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by using a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering. The paper presents extensive experiments on eight datasets, demonstrating that S-WMD consistently outperforms a large number of baselines.

The paper is well-motivated. WMD is a powerful, yet unsupervised, document distance metric. Addressing its limitation by incorporating supervision is a natural and important step. The problem is clearly defined, and the proposed approach is logically sound. The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck of directly incorporating supervision into WMD and provide a pragmatic solution using the Sinkhorn distance. The extensive experimental evaluation, comparing against 26 baselines across 8 datasets, is a significant strength and provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD. The inclusion of ablation studies (initialization results) further strengthens the analysis. The connection to, and clear explanation of, related work (metric learning, optimal transport) is well done. The mathematical derivation is relatively clear and the explanation of the gradient computation is helpful.

However, there are some weaknesses. The writing, while generally clear, is sometimes dense and could benefit from more concise explanations. The numerous equations contribute to this density.  While the authors mention the non-convexity of the loss function, a more detailed discussion of the optimization landscape and potential challenges (e.g., local optima) would be beneficial. The choice of λ=10 for the Sinkhorn distance is presented as a trade-off, but the rationale for this specific value isn't thoroughly explained. A sensitivity analysis of λ might be helpful. The probabilistic filtering of the training set is mentioned but lacks detail – how is this filtering performed, and what is the impact on the training process?  The paper states S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results, but often the improvements are marginal (e.g., 1-2% on some datasets).  While statistically significant, the practical impact of these gains in a real-world application needs consideration.  The limitations of using the squared Euclidean distance in the relaxed transport problem are not thoroughly discussed. The authors acknowledge the possibility of suboptimal performance on datasets like CLASSIC due to this choice, but do not explore alternative distance metrics within the framework. While the code is publicly available, reproducing the results requires a significant computational investment due to the large number of experiments and the complexity of the WMD computations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** S-WMD consistently outperforms a large number of competitive baselines across multiple datasets.
* **Novel Contribution:** The paper presents a novel approach to incorporating supervision into the WMD framework, addressing a significant limitation of the original method.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is theoretically grounded and the authors provide a detailed explanation of the underlying algorithms and optimization process.
* **Practical Relevance:**  Improved document distance metrics have broad applications in information retrieval, text classification, and related fields.
* **Well-written and Comprehensive:** The paper provides a thorough overview of related work and a clear presentation of the proposed method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Marginal Improvements:** The performance gains over existing methods are sometimes modest.
* **Computational Cost:** Replicating the experiments is computationally expensive.
* **Lack of Detailed Analysis:** Certain aspects of the method (e.g., probabilistic filtering, λ selection) lack detailed explanation and justification.
* **Dense Writing:** The paper is occasionally dense and could benefit from more concise explanations.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of document similarity and metric learning. The proposed S-WMD method is a valuable extension of the WMD framework, and the extensive experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding the lack of detailed analysis and potential for more concise writing. I encourage the authors to include a sensitivity analysis of the regularization parameter λ, provide more details on the probabilistic filtering process, and discuss the limitations of using squared Euclidean distance.
