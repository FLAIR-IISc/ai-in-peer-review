

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) introduced by Kusner et al. (ICML 2015) that incorporates supervision to improve document classification. While WMD leverages word embeddings to compute document distances through optimal transport, S-WMD learns an affine transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to minimize leave-one-out kNN classification error. The authors provide an efficient approximation to the gradient using Sinkhorn distance, avoiding the cubic complexity of exact WMD computation.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, consistently outperforming 26 baselines across 8 text classification tasks. The method builds upon NCA (Goldberger et al., NIPS 2005) and recent advances in optimal transport (Cuturi, NIPS 2013), extending them to the document level with a novel supervised learning framework. The authors provide a clear and well-structured exposition of the method, with careful experimental design and thorough evaluation.

Strengths include the practical and efficient gradient approximation, compelling empirical results showing consistent improvements over unsupervised baselines, and clear connections to prior work in metric learning and optimal transport. The paper is well-written with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The significance is high, as it addresses an important gap in the WMD framework by incorporating task-specific supervision, which could benefit numerous text classification applications.

Weaknesses include limited theoretical justification for the gradient approximation (beyond empirical speed), and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why S-WMD underperforms on three datasets (BBCSPORT, CLASSIC, AMAZON) despite its strong average performance. While the authors cite Wang et al. (ECCV 2015) as similar work, they could better differentiate their approach from this related EMD-based metric learning method.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a solid contribution to the field of text representation learning. The method is technically sound, well-validated, and represents a meaningful advancement over unsupervised WMD. The paper meets all four criteria: strong quality (empirical support), clarity (well-organized), originality (novel supervised extension of WMD), and significance (proven effectiveness on multiple datasets). The only minor concern is the lack of deeper theoretical analysis of the gradient approximation, but this doesn't detract from the strong empirical results. I recommend acceptance.
