

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension to the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision for improved document classification. The authors build upon Kusner et al.'s WMD, which computes document distance as optimal transport cost between embedded words, and introduce a method to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights to minimize leave-one-out classification error. The key innovation is an efficient approximation of the WMD gradient using Sinkhorn distance, avoiding the cubic time complexity of exact WMD computation.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-justified claims. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the problem, derive the necessary gradients for optimization, and validate their approach with thorough experiments. The experimental design is robust, evaluating on 8 diverse text classification datasets against 26 baselines, including both unsupervised methods (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, WCD, WMD) and supervised approaches (NCA, ITML, LMNN applied to document representations). The authors are careful to discuss limitations (e.g., S-WMD underperforms WMD on CLASSIC dataset, possibly due to initialization) and provide detailed computational complexity analysis. The approximation using Sinkhorn distance is well-justified and empirically validated as sufficiently accurate for the learning task.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The background section effectively contextualizes the work within the broader literature on word embeddings, document distances, and metric learning. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the pseudocode for Algorithm 1 provides sufficient detail for implementation. The experimental results are presented clearly in Table 2 and Figures 1-2, with appropriate visualizations showing the improvement of S-WMD over WMD. One minor weakness is the slightly abrupt transition to the gradient approximation section (Section 3.1); a brief intuitive explanation of why the Sinkhorn approximation is sufficient for learning would enhance clarity.

**Originality:** The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field. While metric learning is well-studied, the specific application of learning a supervised metric within the WMD framework is novel. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from prior work (e.g., Wang & Guibas [34], Wang et al. [35]) by introducing word importance weights, which provide additional flexibility. The paper properly cites relevant work, including the original WMD paper (Kusner et al., 2015) and metric learning methods (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The combination of optimal transport with supervised learning in the context of text classification represents a significant advancement over the unsupervised WMD.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate significant practical value. S-WMD achieves the lowest test error on 5/8 datasets, outperforms all 26 baselines on average, and shows substantial relative improvements (e.g., 42% on CLASSIC over TF-IDF+NCA). The computational efficiency (O(q²) vs O(q³ log q) for exact WMD) makes the approach practical for real-world applications. The paper convincingly demonstrates that supervision significantly improves document distances for classification tasks, particularly on datasets with smaller documents where WMD's advantages are most pronounced. The method builds on the widespread use of pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec), making it applicable to a wide range of text classification problems.

**Strengths:** 
- Clear, well-justified extension of the WMD framework to incorporate supervision
- Novel use of Sinkhorn distance for efficient gradient approximation
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across 8 diverse datasets
- Strong theoretical grounding with careful attention to computational complexity
- Practical implementation details and code availability

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of how the word importance weights relate to semantic meaning (beyond the visualization in Figure 2)
- The initialization approach (S-WCD) could be more thoroughly justified, particularly why it's better than random initialization
- The paper doesn't explore the impact of different word embedding models (e.g., GloVe vs word2vec), though this is acknowledged as a potential future direction

**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of text classification. It addresses a clear limitation of the WMD (unsupervised nature) with a technically sound and efficient solution. The experimental results are convincing, and the method has clear potential for practical application in document classification and retrieval tasks. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and advances the state of the art in document distances. 

The paper meets all the NIPS review criteria at a high level. It's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), makes a meaningful contribution to the field (Originality), and demonstrates significant practical impact (Significance). The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution.

I recommend acceptance with high confidence. The paper represents a significant advancement in document distance metrics and will likely be influential in the text classification community.
