This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of WMD that learns a task-adapted ground metric and word-importance weights for document comparison. Building on WMD (Kusner et al., ICML 2015), the authors learn (i) a linear transformation A of the word-embedding space and (ii) a histogram reweighting vector w, by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss à la NCA (Goldberger et al., NIPS 2004). To avoid the intractable nested optimization inherent in differentiating WMD, they adopt an entropy-regularized OT surrogate (Sinkhorn; Cuturi, NIPS 2013), using the primal transport T*λ for ∂/∂A and the associated dual potentials for ∂/∂w. They further initialize A via NCA on word centroids (S-WCD) and accelerate training with batch updates and WCD-based neighbor pruning. Across eight text classification datasets and 26 baselines (including LSI, LDA, mSDA, TF-IDF with NCA/LMNN/ITML, WCD, WMD), S-WMD attains the best average rank and wins on 5/8 datasets. Code is released.

Quality: The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. Using Sinkhorn yields efficient, differentiable approximations; the gradient derivations are reasonable, with uniqueness ensured by strong convexity. Empirical evaluation is thorough, with hyperparameter tuning and training-time measurements. However, more ablations would strengthen claims (e.g., contributions of A vs. w; sensitivity to λ and neighbor set size; effect of p=1 vs. p=2 ground costs). The reliance on kNN as the downstream classifier limits scope; comparisons to strong supervised neural document encoders (e.g., CNN/RNN/Doc2Vec) would contextualize gains.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. Some notation overload (p used for norm exponent and vocabulary/document size) and occasional typos make sections harder to parse. The pseudo-code and some parameter choices (e.g., M, λ) could be specified more precisely.

Originality: The combination of supervised metric learning in embedding space with histogram reweighting inside WMD, optimized via Sinkhorn-based gradients and NCA loss, is a novel and natural extension of prior supervised EMD work in vision (e.g., Wang & Guibas, ECCV 2007) to text with word embeddings.

Significance: If adopted, S-WMD provides a practical, interpretable, and strong default metric for text retrieval/classification, improving over WMD and many classical baselines.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation with efficient training via Sinkhorn
- Strong empirical performance across diverse datasets
- Interpretable word weights and released code

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses
- Scope confined to kNN; missing comparisons to modern supervised neural classifiers
- Some clarity/notation issues and minor editorial errors
