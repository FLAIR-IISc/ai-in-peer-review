### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a method to incorporate supervised information into the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a recently introduced and powerful document metric. The original WMD [19] formulates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other within a pre-trained word embedding space. While effective, WMD is entirely unsupervised and cannot adapt to a specific classification task.

The authors introduce the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), which learns a task-specific metric by optimizing two components: (1) a linear transformation matrix `A` applied to the word embeddings, which warps the embedding space to better suit the task, and (2) a word-importance vector `w` that re-weights the document's word histograms to emphasize discriminative words.

To learn `A` and `w`, the authors propose minimizing a stochastic version of the leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor (kNN) classification error, drawing inspiration from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [15]. A significant technical challenge arises because computing the gradient of the loss function requires differentiating through the WMD, which is a linear program. This nested optimization is computationally prohibitive and the gradient may not be unique. The core technical contribution of the paper is an efficient and practical solution to this problem. They replace the exact WMD with its entropically regularized counterpart, the Sinkhorn distance [6]. This relaxation makes the optimization problem strictly convex and differentiable, leading to an efficient `O(q^2)` algorithm for computing the transport plan and its gradient, where `q` is the number of unique words.

The optimization is further accelerated through a clever initialization scheme (pre-training `A` using the much faster Word Centroid Distance) and an efficient batch gradient descent procedure that limits neighborhood computations to a smaller subset of nearest neighbors. The authors perform a comprehensive evaluation on eight text classification datasets, comparing S-WMD against 26 baselines, including various document representations (e.g., TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) paired with and without metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper is technically strong. The central problem—differentiating through the optimal transport problem of WMD—is correctly identified as the main bottleneck. The proposed solution of using an entropically regularized approximation (Sinkhorn distance) is well-motivated, principled, and builds upon recent advances in computational optimal transport [6]. The derivations for the gradients with respect to the transformation matrix `A` and the word weights `w` appear correct and leverage the dual formulation of the transport problem effectively. The overall algorithm, including the initialization and batching strategies, represents a complete and practical piece of work. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with extensive baselines and proper hyperparameter tuning (Bayesian optimization), which supports the claims of the paper's effectiveness.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide clear background on word embeddings, WMD, regularized transport, and NCA, making the paper accessible. The motivation for the proposed S-WMD is laid out logically. The mathematical formulations are presented clearly, and the novel components of the method are explained step-by-step. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail, and the results are presented in a clean, interpretable format, including visualizations that effectively demonstrate the improved class separation achieved by S-WMD.

**Originality:**
The paper presents a novel combination of ideas. While metric learning for the Earth Mover's Distance has been explored in computer vision [35], this work is the first to successfully apply it to the WMD for document classification. The idea of jointly learning a transformation of the embedding space (`A`) and a re-weighting of the source distributions (`w`) is a novel contribution that provides additional flexibility. The main original contribution is the practical and efficient method for computing the gradient of the WMD-based loss function via entropic regularization, which makes the entire supervised learning framework feasible. The work clearly distinguishes itself from prior work in metric learning, which typically operates on fixed, high-dimensional document vectors, and from unsupervised document distances like WMD.

**Significance:**
The results presented are highly significant and likely to be impactful. S-WMD is shown to achieve state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance on kNN classification across a wide range of text datasets, outperforming a very large suite of competitive methods. This demonstrates that one can effectively combine the power of large-scale unsupervised pre-training (for word embeddings) with task-specific supervised fine-tuning of the distance metric itself. This work provides a powerful tool for practitioners who use kNN-based methods for text classification and retrieval. The approach of using differentiable, regularized optimal transport as a component within a larger learning system is a general idea that could inspire further research in other domains.

---

### Arguments For Acceptance:
1.  **Novelty and Soundness:** The paper introduces a novel and technically sound method to learn a supervised metric for the Word Mover's Distance, elegantly solving the difficult problem of differentiating through the WMD optimization.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is shown to outperform an extensive list of 26 strong baselines across 8 diverse datasets, demonstrating a significant advance in performance for kNN-based document classification.
3.  **High Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very clearly written, and the authors have made their code publicly available, which will facilitate adoption and further research.
4.  **Significant Contribution:** The work successfully bridges unsupervised word embeddings with supervised metric learning in a non-trivial way, offering a powerful new tool for text analysis.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  **Limited Discussion on Model Choices:** The paper uses a squared Euclidean distance for the ground metric `||A(xi-xj)||_2^2`, primarily for compatibility with the NCA framework. The impact of this choice, compared to the `L1` or `L2` norms often used in the original WMD, is not thoroughly discussed, although the authors briefly acknowledge it might explain a performance drop on one dataset.
2.  **Comparison to End-to-End Deep Models:** The evaluation focuses exclusively on kNN classification. By 2016, end-to-end deep learning models (e.g., CNNs, RNNs) were also state-of-the-art for text classification. While the comparison to metric learning baselines is fair and comprehensive, a discussion positioning S-WMD relative to these alternative architectures would have provided additional context on its applicability.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It is a high-quality contribution with a novel method, strong technical underpinnings, and significant empirical results.
