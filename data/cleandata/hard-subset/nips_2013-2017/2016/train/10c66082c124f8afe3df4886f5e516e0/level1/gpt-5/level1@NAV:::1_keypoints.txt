- Proposes S-WMD: a supervised extension of WMD that learns a linear embedding transform A and word-importance weights w for document comparison.  
- Optimizes A and w by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN (NCA-style) loss.  
- Uses entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) to obtain differentiable WMD surrogates, using primal transport for ∂/∂A and dual potentials for ∂/∂w.  
- Initializes A via NCA on word centroids (S-WCD) and speeds training with batch updates and WCD-based neighbor pruning.  
- Evaluates on 8 text classification datasets against 26 baselines (LSI, LDA, mSDA, TF-IDF variants, WCD, WMD, etc.).  
- Achieves best average rank and wins on 5 out of 8 datasets; code is released.  
- Technical approach is sound, well-motivated, and benefits from efficient Sinkhorn-based differentiation; gradient derivations are reasonable.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough (hyperparameter tuning, training-time measures), but more ablations are needed (contributions of A vs w; sensitivity to λ, neighbor size; p=1 vs p=2).  
- Primary limitation: reliance on kNN as downstream classifier; lacks comparisons to strong supervised neural document encoders (CNN/RNN/Doc2Vec).  
- Clarity is generally good but hampered by notation overload (p used twice), occasional typos, and underspecified pseudo-code/parameter choices (M, λ).  
- Originality: novel and natural extension of supervised EMD ideas to text by combining metric learning and histogram reweighting with Sinkhorn gradients.  
- Significance: offers a practical, interpretable, and strong default metric for text retrieval/classification that improves over WMD and many classical baselines.  
- Pros: principled formulation with efficient training, strong empirical performance, interpretable word weights, and released code.  
- Cons: limited ablations/sensitivity analyses, restricted to kNN scope, and minor clarity/editorial issues.
