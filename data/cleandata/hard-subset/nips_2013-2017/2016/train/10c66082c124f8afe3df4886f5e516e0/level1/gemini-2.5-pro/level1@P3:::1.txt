**Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. It builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) [19], an unsupervised metric that calculates the minimum cost to "transport" words from one document to another in a pre-trained word embedding space. The primary limitation of WMD is its inability to incorporate supervised information.

The authors address this by learning two components to adapt the WMD to a specific classification task: (1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, which learns a more suitable geometry for the task, and (2) a word-importance vector `w` that re-weighs the document's bag-of-words representation to emphasize task-relevant words. The learning objective is inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [15], minimizing a differentiable proxy for the leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor (kNN) classification error.

A major technical challenge is that the gradient of the WMD objective involves a nested linear program, making direct optimization computationally prohibitive. The core technical contribution of this paper is a clever and efficient approximation. The authors replace the exact WMD (a specific instance of the Earth Mover's Distance) with its entropically regularized version, also known as the Sinkhorn distance [6]. This relaxed problem is strictly convex and can be solved much faster, yielding a unique, differentiable solution. The authors derive the gradients for both `A` and `w` with respect to this relaxed objective. To further improve practicality, they propose an intelligent initialization scheme based on the faster Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and an efficient batch gradient descent procedure that only considers a small subset of nearest neighbors for each document.

**Strengths (Pros)**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of a highly influential, state-of-the-art unsupervised method (WMD). The idea of making WMD supervised is a natural and significant step forward. The proposed solution is both novel and elegant. While it combines ideas from metric learning (NCA), optimal transport (Sinkhorn), and word embeddings, the synthesis is non-trivial and results in a powerful new algorithm. Learning both a geometric transformation (`A`) and feature re-weighting (`w`) within the optimal transport framework is a particularly strong and original contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very solid. The central problem of the non-differentiable and slow WMD gradient is well-identified. The proposed solution—using the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable proxy—is an excellent and principled choice that makes the entire framework tractable. The gradient derivations for the transformation matrix `A` and the weight vector `w` (leveraging the dual variables of the transport problem) are correct and well-explained. The overall approach demonstrates a deep understanding of optimal transport and metric learning.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is exemplary. The authors compare S-WMD against an extensive set of 26 baselines across 8 diverse datasets. The baselines include classic document representations (BOW, TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), deep learning approaches (mSDA), and the original WMD/WCD, all combined with standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The results are compelling: S-WMD achieves the best performance on 5 out of 8 datasets and the best average rank overall, demonstrating a clear and substantial improvement over prior art. The inclusion of training time analysis and qualitative visualizations (t-SNE plots and word clouds) further strengthens the paper's claims.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It provides a clear background on word2vec, WMD, Sinkhorn distances, and NCA, making it accessible to a broad audience. The motivation for the method is clear, the technical details are presented logically, and the experimental setup and results are well-documented.

**Weaknesses (Cons)**

1.  **Scalability Concerns:** While the authors have made significant efforts to speed up the algorithm, the complexity of the gradient computation still scales quadratically with the number of unique words per document (`p`). For datasets with very long documents, this `O(p^2)` term could still be a bottleneck. A brief discussion on the practical limits of document length would be beneficial.

2.  **Fixed Ground Metric Form:** The formulation relies on a squared Euclidean distance in the transformed word embedding space (`||A(xi - xj)||_2^2`). The original WMD paper also considered the `L1` distance. The authors themselves note that this choice might explain the suboptimal performance on the CLASSIC dataset. While this choice is convenient for the NCA framework, a discussion on the potential for extending the method to other norms and the associated challenges would have strengthened the paper.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method introduces several hyperparameters (e.g., the regularization parameter `λ`, the neighbor set size `N`, batch size `B`, and learning rates). While the authors mention using Bayesian optimization for baselines, the sensitivity of S-WMD to its own specific hyperparameters is not fully explored. For instance, `λ` controls the trade-off between approximation quality and computational speed; providing more insight into its selection would be valuable for practitioners.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and high-quality contribution to the field. It presents a novel, technically sound, and empirically powerful method for supervised document similarity learning. The authors successfully tackle a challenging optimization problem with a clever and well-motivated approximation, leading to state-of-the-art results on a wide range of tasks. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the overall strength of the work. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and the ideas presented are likely to be influential.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and significant extension of a major unsupervised document distance (WMD).
*   Elegant technical solution to a difficult, non-differentiable optimization problem using Sinkhorn distances.
*   State-of-the-art empirical results demonstrated through a comprehensive and convincing evaluation.
*   High-quality presentation and clarity.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   (Minor) Scalability could be an issue for very long documents.
*   (Minor) Limited discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity and the choice of the underlying ground metric.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It represents a complete and impactful piece of research that was a clear asset to the NIPS 2016 program.
