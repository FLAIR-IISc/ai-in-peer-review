This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method to learn a supervised metric to improve the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector, minimizing the leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. The authors address the computational challenge of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering. They demonstrate strong performance on eight text classification tasks, outperforming numerous baselines.

The paper is well-motivated. WMD is a powerful, yet unsupervised, document distance metric. Incorporating supervision into this framework is a natural and valuable direction. The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck in naively applying supervised learning to WMD and offer a pragmatic and well-justified solution utilizing the Sinkhorn distance approximation. The extensive experimental evaluation, with 26 baselines across 8 datasets, is a significant strength, thoroughly demonstrating the effectiveness of S-WMD. The inclusion of ablation studies (initialization vs. full training) is also commendable. The presentation is generally clear and the related work is relevant and well-cited, referencing key papers in metric learning, optimal transport, and word embeddings.

However, there are some weaknesses. While the authors mention the non-differentiability of the LOO error, the discussion around the use of a *stochastic* approximation feels somewhat light. A more detailed explanation of why this stochastic approximation is valid and its potential biases would be helpful. The connection to NCA is briefly mentioned, but could be explored more thoroughly. It’s not immediately obvious why NCA was chosen as the inspiration for the stochastic optimization. The paper relies heavily on the parameter λ in the Sinkhorn distance for approximation. A more comprehensive sensitivity analysis or justification for the chosen value (λ=10) beyond it “leading to a nice trade-off” is needed. Although the authors claim their method is efficient, the complexity analysis presented seems abstract. Providing actual runtime numbers for different dataset sizes and dimensionality would be more convincing. Finally, while the visualizations are helpful, the discussion of *why* S-WMD produces better clusters compared to WMD is lacking. Is it due to the learned linear transformation, the word weighting, or a combination of both? 

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The core idea is logically presented and the approximation using the Sinkhorn distance is a reasonable approach. The extensive experimental results provide strong evidence to support the claims. However, the stochastic approximation and complexity analysis could be strengthened.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are reasonably clear. However, as noted above, certain sections (stochastic approximation, complexity analysis) would benefit from more detail.

**Originality:** The idea of incorporating supervision into WMD is novel and addresses a significant limitation of the original method. While metric learning is a well-established field, applying it to the WMD framework in this specific way, along with the efficient approximation, constitutes a valuable contribution.

**Significance:** The results are significant. Consistently outperforming 26 baselines across 8 datasets demonstrates the practical value of S-WMD. The method has the potential to be adopted by researchers and practitioners working on text classification, information retrieval, and related tasks. The insights into word importance weighting could also be beneficial.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical performance on a diverse set of datasets.
*   Novel and well-motivated approach to incorporating supervision into WMD.
*   Practical and efficient approximation for addressing the computational challenges.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation with numerous baselines.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of the stochastic approximation and its potential biases.
*   Insufficient justification for the choice of the Sinkhorn distance parameter (λ).
*   Abstract complexity analysis; lack of runtime numbers.
*   Superficial explanation of the improved clustering observed in visualizations.



**Overall Assessment:**  I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field and should be **accepted**. The strengths of the paper—strong empirical results, a novel approach, and a practical solution to a computational challenge—outweigh the weaknesses. Addressing the minor concerns outlined above would further strengthen the paper, but they do not fundamentally detract from its value.
