### Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. It builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) [19], which calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to become the words of the other, where cost is measured by Euclidean distance in a word embedding space (e.g., word2vec). While WMD has shown state-of-the-art performance for kNN classification, it is entirely unsupervised.

The key contribution of this work is to incorporate supervision into the WMD framework. The authors propose to learn two components jointly: 1) a linear transformation matrix `A` that reshapes the word embedding space to better suit the classification task, and 2) a word-importance vector `w` that re-weights word frequencies to emphasize task-relevant words. The learning objective is to minimize the stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification error, following the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) framework [15].

A major technical challenge is that computing the gradient of the WMD with respect to `A` and `w` is computationally prohibitive, as it involves differentiating through a linear program. To overcome this, the authors propose a clever and efficient approximation. They replace the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized version, also known as the Sinkhorn distance [6]. This relaxed objective is strictly convex and can be solved efficiently, yielding a unique, differentiable solution. This allows for a practical gradient-based optimization scheme. The authors further improve efficiency with a smart initialization strategy based on the faster Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batch-based training procedure that considers only a local neighborhood of documents. The proposed S-WMD is evaluated on eight text classification datasets, where it consistently outperforms an extensive suite of 26 baselines, including unsupervised methods and document representations combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

**Critique and Justification**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical motivation and extensive experimental results. The core technical problem—the intractability of differentiating through the WMD—is significant, and the proposed solution of using the Sinkhorn distance relaxation is both elegant and effective. The derivation of the approximate gradients for both the linear transformation `A` and the word weights `w` appears sound.

The experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough. The authors compare their method against a wide array of strong and relevant baselines, including classic methods (TF-IDF, LSI), modern deep learning approaches (mSDA), and the original WMD. Crucially, they also compare against supervised baselines by combining various document representations with established metric learning algorithms like NCA, ITML, and LMNN. This comprehensive comparison provides strong evidence for the superiority of S-WMD. The use of Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning across all methods is a commendable practice that ensures fair comparisons. The results are compelling, showing that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance on the majority of the datasets.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of providing the necessary background on word2vec, WMD, the regularized transport problem, and NCA, making the paper largely self-contained. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, and the proposed method is developed in a logical, step-by-step manner. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the entire training procedure. The figures, particularly the t-SNE visualizations and the word cloud of learned weights, effectively illustrate the benefits of the method. A minor point for improvement would be to include a citation or a brief explanation for the derivation of the dual variables from the Sinkhorn scaling vectors, which is currently stated without justification.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on existing concepts like WMD, NCA, and Sinkhorn distances, the synthesis of these ideas into a coherent framework for supervised document metric learning is novel and non-trivial. Prior work on learning ground metrics for the Earth Mover's Distance exists, primarily in computer vision [7, 34, 35], but this paper successfully applies and adapts these ideas to the domain of text classification via word embeddings. The simultaneous learning of both a geometric transformation (`A`) and histogram importance weights (`w`) is a distinctive and powerful feature of this work. The key innovation of using the entropy-regularized transport problem to enable efficient gradient-based learning for WMD is a significant contribution that was not, to my knowledge, previously explored in this context.

**Significance:** The paper has high significance. The WMD was an important advancement in document similarity, and this work addresses its most prominent limitation: its unsupervised nature. By providing an efficient and effective method to tailor the WMD to specific supervised tasks, S-WMD represents a substantial step forward. The demonstrated state-of-the-art results on multiple benchmarks make it a highly valuable contribution to the fields of text classification and metric learning. The approach of learning in the low-dimensional word embedding space, rather than the high-dimensional document space, is scalable and elegant. The ideas presented here are likely to inspire follow-up work on supervised metric learning for other structured data types where optimal transport distances are applicable. The public release of the code further enhances the potential impact and utility of this work for other researchers and practitioners.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Solves a well-motivated and important problem by adding supervision to the powerful WMD metric.
*   **Pro:** Presents a novel and technically sound solution to the challenging problem of computing gradients through an optimal transport problem, using the Sinkhorn distance approximation.
*   **Pro:** The experimental validation is exceptionally thorough and convincing, demonstrating consistent state-of-the-art performance against a large and diverse set of strong baselines.
*   **Pro:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and the contributions are easy to understand and appreciate.
*   **Pro:** The work is highly significant, likely to be influential, and provides a new benchmark for kNN-based text classification.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   (Minor) The method relies on an approximation to the true WMD gradient. While this is necessary for tractability and empirically successful, a brief analysis of the trade-offs associated with the choice of the regularization parameter `λ` would have strengthened the paper.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, well-executed, and significant contribution to the field. It successfully tackles a difficult technical problem and delivers state-of-the-art empirical results. It is a clear accept for NIPS.
