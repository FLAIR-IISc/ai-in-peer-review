### Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised document distance metric by extending the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The original WMD, introduced by Kusner et al. (ICML 2015), is an unsupervised metric that calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost of "transporting" words from one document to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. While effective, WMD cannot leverage labeled data.

The authors propose to learn two components to make the metric supervised: (1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space to tailor the word representations to the specific classification task, and (2) a word-importance vector `w` that re-weights word frequencies in the document's bag-of-words representation. The learning objective is to minimize a soft version of the leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor (kNN) classification error, drawing inspiration from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) (Goldberger et al., NIPS 2004).

A significant technical challenge is that computing the gradient of the WMD objective is computationally prohibitive, as it involves differentiating through a linear program. To overcome this, the authors make a key contribution by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized counterpart, known as the Sinkhorn distance (Cuturi, NIPS 2013). This relaxation makes the optimization problem strictly convex and allows for a fast, differentiable, and accurate approximation of the optimal transport plan. This enables the efficient computation of gradients for both `A` and `w`. The authors also propose practical optimization strategies, including a clever initialization of `A` using NCA on the much faster Word Centroid Distance (WCD) and a batch gradient descent scheme that only considers a small neighborhood of documents for each gradient update. The method is evaluated on eight text classification datasets, where it is shown to outperform an extensive suite of 26 baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of the WMD framework—its unsupervised nature. Extending WMD to incorporate supervision is a natural and valuable research direction. The proposed method of jointly learning a linear transformation of the embedding space and a word-importance vector is a novel and powerful way to adapt the distance metric to a specific task. This work is a direct and substantial advancement over the original WMD paper.

2.  **Technical Quality and Soundness:** The core technical contribution—the use of the Sinkhorn distance to make the gradient computation tractable—is both elegant and effective. This is a well-founded approach that leverages recent advances in optimal transport to solve a challenging nested optimization problem. The derivations for the gradients with respect to both `A` and `w` appear sound. The proposed optimization heuristics, such as the WCD-based initialization and nearest-neighbor filtering, are practical and well-justified, making the overall algorithm efficient enough for real-world use.

3.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength of this paper. The authors test their method on eight diverse datasets, covering various text classification scenarios. They compare against an impressive list of 26 baselines, which includes not only standard document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA) but also state-of-the-art methods like mSDA and the original WMD. Crucially, they also apply popular metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML) to these baselines, providing a very strong and fair comparison. The results are compelling, showing that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance on a majority of the datasets and the best average rank overall.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear background on word2vec, WMD, Sinkhorn distances, and NCA, making the paper accessible. The motivation for the S-WMD model is well-articulated, the technical challenges are clearly explained, and the proposed solution is presented in a logical and understandable manner. The inclusion of visualizations (t-SNE plots and word clouds) further aids in understanding the method's impact.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Lack of Ablation Study:** The S-WMD learns both a matrix `A` and a vector `w`. It would be highly informative to see an ablation study that disentangles the effects of these two components. For instance, what is the performance when learning only `A` (i.e., fixing `w` to all ones) versus learning only `w` (fixing `A` to identity)? This would provide valuable insight into which component provides more of the performance gain and how they interact.

2.  **Discussion on the Ground Metric:** The paper formulates the problem using the squared Euclidean distance between word vectors, which is convenient for the Sinkhorn approximation and gradient derivation. However, the original WMD can also use L1 distance. The authors briefly note that the choice of squared L2 might be suboptimal for one dataset (CLASSIC). A more detailed discussion on this limitation would be beneficial. Is it possible to extend this framework to other ground metrics, or is the reliance on squared L2 a fundamental requirement of the current approach?

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that the regularization parameter λ is set to 10 as a trade-off between speed and accuracy. While understandable for brevity, a small sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with λ would strengthen the paper. This would help practitioners understand how critical this parameter is and provide guidance for setting it on new tasks.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pro):**

*   Presents a novel, well-motivated, and significant extension of the state-of-the-art WMD metric to the supervised setting.
*   Introduces a clever and technically sound solution (using Sinkhorn distances) to overcome a major computational hurdle in the optimization.
*   The experimental evaluation is extremely thorough and provides compelling evidence of the method's superiority over a wide range of strong baselines.
*   The paper is very clearly written, well-organized, and the results are easily reproducible with the provided pseudocode and promise of public code.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Con):**

*   The contribution could be further strengthened with an ablation study to better understand the individual impact of the learned transformation `A` and the word weights `w`.
*   The discussion on the limitation to a squared Euclidean ground metric and the sensitivity to key hyperparameters like λ is somewhat brief.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of metric learning for text data. It successfully tackles the important problem of incorporating supervision into the powerful Word Mover's Distance framework. The technical approach is clever and sound, and the experimental results are comprehensive and convincing. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to additional analyses that would be nice to see rather than any fundamental flaws in the method. The paper is of high quality, original, significant, and clearly written. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
