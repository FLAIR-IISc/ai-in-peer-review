Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a learned document distance that incorporates supervision into Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by jointly learning (i) a linear transformation A of the underlying word embedding space and (ii) a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. Training minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out (LOO) kNN objective via the NCA loss, with document-to-document distances computed by a WMD-like optimal transport problem using the transformed ground cost ‖A(xi−xj)‖2 and reweighted histograms. Because differentiating through the original WMD is computationally impractical, the paper adopts Sinkhorn’s entropic regularization to obtain an efficient and smooth approximation: gradients w.r.t. A use the optimal plan T∗λ (envelope theorem), and gradients w.r.t. w use the dual potentials (α∗λ,β∗λ) extracted from Sinkhorn scaling vectors (u,v). The method is trained with mini-batches, neighbor subsampling using a cheap WCD filter, and initialized by running NCA on word centroids (S-WCD). Experiments on eight text datasets compare against 26 baselines (unsupervised distances and supervised metric learning on various representations), showing S-WMD achieves the best kNN error on 5/8 datasets and the best average rank.

This work builds on: WMD (Kusner et al., ICML’15), entropic OT/Sinkhorn (Cuturi, NIPS’13), and supervised metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML). It is close in spirit to prior attempts to learn ground metrics for EMD in vision (e.g., Wang & Guibas, ECCV’12; Cuturi & Avis; Wang et al.), but differs by (i) operating on word embeddings and (ii) jointly learning histogram reweighting tailored to labels.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated extension: bringing supervision into WMD addresses a widely-cited limitation, while keeping the compelling semantic alignment of OT over embeddings.
- Sound optimization strategy: using entropic OT enables efficient, differentiable approximations; deriving gradients via envelope/duality is appropriate, and the neighbor filtering is a practical speedup.
- Compact parameterization: learning A in the low-dimensional embedding space is far more tractable than learning a document-level metric; learning w adds task-specific salience.
- Strong empirical results: broad, carefully tuned baseline coverage (including supervised metrics on multiple representations) and consistent gains in average rank; code availability improves reproducibility.
- Sensible initialization (S-WCD): empirically effective and conceptually aligned with WMD lower bound.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Constraints/regularization on w: The method appears to require w≥0 to maintain valid histograms, but constraints are not explicitly stated, nor is any regularization (e.g., L1/L2) to prevent overfitting to rare words. Report enforcing nonnegativity (e.g., softplus or projected updates) and consider sparsity to improve interpretability and robustness. Ablate “A only” vs “w only” vs both.
- Choice of ground cost: The method uses squared Euclidean. WMD often performs well with ℓ2 or ℓ1; squared distance can overweight outliers. Provide an ablation (ℓ2 vs ℓ2-squared) and/or learn a Mahalanobis with unsquared cost, which interacts differently with Sinkhorn scaling.
- Fairness of evaluation protocol: All methods are evaluated with kNN in Euclidean space (after PCA+metric learning for some baselines). Many document representations (e.g., TF-IDF, mSDA, LSI/LDA) are typically paired with linear SVM/logistic regression and can perform substantially better in that regime. Including a standard linear classifier baseline on TF-IDF and mSDA would better contextualize practical significance.
- Sensitivity and hyperparameters: λ is fixed at 10; B, N, r (rank of A), learning rates, and stopping are not fully explored. Provide sensitivity plots for λ and N (neighbor subsampling), and clarify whether r<d is used (enforcing low rank could regularize M=AᵀA).
- Notation/clarity issues: Symbols p,q,n are overloaded (norm exponent, unique words per doc, vocab size), and there are minor typos (e.g., “.2 Optimization”). Clarify dimensions when defining 1 in dual potentials, and explicitly justify ignoring dT∗/dθ via the envelope theorem under strong convexity of the entropic objective.
- Scalability to long documents: Complexity is O(p^2) per pair with p unique tokens; long biomedical/news articles can push p high. Report train/test times vs document length and discuss options (e.g., vocabulary pruning, stochastic OT approximations, low-rank ground cost kernels) for further scaling.
- Missing contemporary supervised text baselines: Although the paper focuses on distances/metrics, simple supervised text classifiers (e.g., bag-of-n-grams + linear models) and doc2vec were available by 2016; fastText appeared around then. Including at least a linear classifier baseline would strengthen claims about state of the art.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound. Using Sinkhorn provides a principled approximation, and the gradient derivations are consistent with OT duality. The optimization is well thought out, though details on constraints/regularization and hyperparameter sensitivity should be expanded. The empirical study is broad for metric-learning baselines, and code is provided.

Clarity
Overall clear and well organized. Minor notation inconsistencies and typos should be corrected. More explicit statements on constraints (w≥0), initialization of r, and exact hyperparameters would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. Learning a ground metric for OT is not new, but applying it to WMD with word embeddings, jointly learning histogram weights, and integrating an NCA loss with Sinkhorn-based gradients in text is a novel and useful combination.

Significance
High for practitioners of distance-based text retrieval/classification and for the OT-in-NLP community. The approach is simple, efficient, and improves over strong baselines within the kNN framework. Broader impact would be strengthened by comparisons to standard supervised text classifiers.

Questions for the authors
- Are w constrained to be nonnegative? If so, how is this enforced during optimization? Any regularization on w or A?
- What rank r is used for A? Do low-rank constraints help generalization?
- How sensitive are results to λ and to the neighbor set size N? Any guidelines?
- Ablation: performance with only A, only w, and both?
- How are OOV words at test time handled in terms of w and embeddings?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a clear gap: supervised WMD with an efficient and principled optimization.
- Strong empirical gains across diverse datasets with extensive baselines.
- Practical method with open-source code; likely to be adopted.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited analysis of constraints/regularization and sensitivity.
- Evaluation restricted to kNN; lacks standard supervised text classifier baselines.
- Some clarity/notation issues.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution that extends WMD in a principled and effective manner. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarity and experimental concerns.
