Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised metric for document comparison that extends the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD; Kusner et al., ICML’15). WMD computes the optimal transport cost of moving the normalized bag-of-words mass of one document to another under a ground cost derived from word embeddings (e.g., word2vec). The key novelty is to incorporate supervision by (i) learning a linear transformation A applied to the word embedding space and (ii) learning a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The metric is trained by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective in the spirit of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA; Goldberger et al., NIPS’04). Direct gradients through the exact WMD entail nested LPs; the authors therefore use the entropic regularized transport (Sinkhorn distance; Cuturi, NIPS’13) to obtain a unique transport plan and associated dual potentials, enabling efficient approximate gradients with O(q^2) per pair (q = unique words per doc). Initialization uses NCA on the fast Word Centroid Distance (WCD), and training uses batch updates with neighbor pruning. On eight text classification datasets and against 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised metric learning on various representations), S-WMD achieves the best average rank and the best or near-best performance on most datasets.

This work connects to metric learning (LMNN, ITML, NCA), supervised EMD/OT works in vision (Wang & Guibas, ECCV’12; Cuturi & Avis), and entropic OT computation (Cuturi; Cuturi & Peyré). Applying supervised ground-metric learning and histogram reweighting in the WMD setting for text is new and practically significant.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is sensible. Using entropic OT to obtain stable and differentiable surrogates for the transport plan and dual potentials is appropriate. The gradient w.r.t. A via T* and the gradient w.r.t. w via dual potentials (α, β) are correct for the regularized problem and practical. However, the paper optimizes an entropic approximation while claiming to learn “S-WMD”: the mismatch between training objective (regularized OT) and the evaluation metric (is it exact WMD or the regularized distance?) should be clarified. Guarantees on the bias induced by finite λ (λ=10 in experiments) and its effect on kNN accuracy are not provided.
- Efficiency: The complexity analysis and training tricks (WCD-based neighbor filtering, batch updates) make the approach feasible. Reported training times are reasonable; still, the quadratic dependence on per-document vocabulary size may be costly for very long documents.
- Empirical support: Experiments span diverse tasks with careful hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian optimization and many baselines. Results show consistent improvements. Ablations partially exist (S-WCD and S-WMD init.), but further disentangling the contributions of A vs. w would strengthen the case. Sensitivity analyses for λ, neighbor set size, and initialization quality would be useful.
- Potential pitfalls: The model appears to lack explicit regularization on A and w. Without constraints (e.g., nonnegativity and norm control on w, Frobenius/orthogonality constraints on A), overfitting or degenerate reweighting toward rare words is conceivable. The paper implicitly requires w ≥ 0 to keep histograms valid; this constraint and any regularizers should be stated clearly.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written with a clear problem setup, derivations, and algorithmic description. The use of standard references for OT and NCA helps situate the method. Some minor issues: inconsistent notation (p used for both power and dimension in places), a few typos, and unclear details about test-time metric (regularized vs. exact WMD) and constraints/regularization on w. Adding a short discussion on how the approximate gradients relate to optimizing the exact WMD would improve clarity.

Originality
Learning a supervised ground metric in an OT-based document distance with both a linear transform of the embedding space and learned word-importance weights is novel in the NLP/WMD setting. While supervised EMD learning exists in vision, the adaptation to word embeddings, the NCA-based objective on documents, and the practical gradient scheme using Sinkhorn duals are original and nontrivial.

Significance
WMD has been influential; a supervised, efficient extension that consistently improves kNN classification across many datasets is impactful. The approach is general (any pretrained embeddings), interpretable (word weights), and likely to be adopted for retrieval/classification. That said, comparisons to contemporary supervised document models (e.g., doc2vec; fastText; CNN/RNN sentence classifiers) are missing and would better position the contribution relative to strong end-to-end baselines available by 2016. Demonstrations beyond kNN (e.g., retrieval metrics, clustering quality) would broaden the impact.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated formulation marrying supervised metric learning with OT/WMD.
- Efficient and practical training via entropic OT and neighbor pruning; code released.
- Strong empirical performance across many datasets and baselines; good initialization strategy.
- Interpretability via learned word weights; sensible visualizations.

Cons
- Objective mismatch and limited discussion of approximation error from finite λ; unclear test-time metric.
- Lack of explicit regularization/constraints on A and w; potential overfitting not analyzed.
- Missing comparisons to prominent supervised document/sentence models (doc2vec, fastText, CNNs).
- Limited ablation/sensitivity analyses (λ, role of w vs. A, neighbor set size).
- Scalability to very long documents not thoroughly evaluated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify whether test-time distances use exact WMD or the entropic surrogate; report sensitivity to λ and the trade-off between accuracy and speed.
- State and enforce constraints on w (nonnegativity) and consider regularization (e.g., L1/L2 on w, Frobenius or orthogonality on A); include ablations showing their effect.
- Add ablations disentangling gains from A vs. w; report robustness to initialization and neighbor-pruning parameters.
- Include comparisons to doc2vec and fastText, and to simple supervised neural baselines (e.g., Kim-style CNN) to contextualize gains.
- Discuss memory/time scaling for very large vocabularies/long documents; consider sparse Sinkhorn variants.

Overall recommendation
A strong, well-executed contribution with clear novelty and practical significance. Despite some missing analyses and clarifications, the technical approach and empirical results meet NIPS criteria well. I lean toward acceptance.
