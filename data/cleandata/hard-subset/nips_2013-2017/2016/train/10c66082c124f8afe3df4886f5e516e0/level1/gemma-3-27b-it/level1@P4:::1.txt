The paper proposes a supervised variant of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), termed S-WMD, aiming to incorporate supervision into the distance metric learning process. WMD leverages word embeddings to compute distances between documents by solving an optimal transport problem. S-WMD learns a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights by minimizing the leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. The authors address the computational challenges posed by the original WMD formulation using a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and a probabilistic filtering technique. They demonstrate superior performance on eight real-world text classification tasks compared to 26 baselines.

This work builds upon the recent success of WMD (Kusner et al., 2016) and addresses a key limitation: its unsupervised nature. The idea of incorporating supervision into document distance metrics is not new; significant work exists in metric learning (e.g., Weinberger & Saul, 2008; Davis et al., 2007). However, applying metric learning directly to the high-dimensional word embedding space, while avoiding dimensionality reduction, is a novel approach.  The use of the Sinkhorn distance for efficient gradient computation is a clever adaptation of existing work (Cuturi, 2013) to this specific problem. Related work also includes approaches learning ground distances for EMD (Wang & Guibas, 2015), although the context and specific techniques differ.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of learning a supervised metric directly within the word embedding space, combined with word importance weighting, is a significant contribution. This avoids the limitations of applying traditional metric learning techniques to potentially less informative, dimensionality-reduced representations.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper presents compelling results across a diverse set of datasets, consistently outperforming a large number of competitive baselines.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is well-motivated and technically sound. The use of the Sinkhorn distance and the probabilistic filtering technique effectively address the computational challenges. The derivation of the gradients appears correct and the optimization procedure is clearly described.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the method. The motivation and related work are adequately discussed. The algorithmic steps are clearly outlined.
*   **Efficiency:** The authors highlight the computational efficiency of the proposed approach, making it practical for real-world applications. The comparison of training times in Table 3 is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** While the authors mention tuning hyperparameters with Bayesian optimization, the sensitivity of the results to these parameters is not extensively discussed. A more detailed analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters would be beneficial.
*   **Squared Euclidean Distance:** The choice of the squared Euclidean distance within the transport cost in eq. (5) is not rigorously justified. Exploring other distance metrics might lead to further performance improvements.
*   **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors provide ablation studies and visualizations, a more thorough error analysis, identifying specific types of instances where S-WMD fails, could provide valuable insights for future work.
*   **Connection to Existing Metric Learning:** The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how S-WMD relates to existing metric learning methods beyond NCA.  Specifically, discussing the differences and potential advantages of learning both a linear transformation *and* word weights compared to other approaches.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to supervised document distance learning.
*   The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over a strong set of baselines.
*   The computational efficiency of the method makes it practical for real-world applications.
*   The clear writing and detailed explanation of the method enhance the paper’s value.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The sensitivity to hyperparameters and lack of detailed error analysis are minor weaknesses that could be addressed with further experimentation.
*   The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the connections to and distinctions from broader metric learning literature.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document distance learning. The proposed S-WMD method is novel, technically sound, and empirically effective. While some minor weaknesses exist, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
