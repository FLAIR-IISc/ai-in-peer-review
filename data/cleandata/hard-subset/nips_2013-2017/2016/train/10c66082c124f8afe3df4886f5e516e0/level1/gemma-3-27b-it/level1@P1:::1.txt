This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised metric on top of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights by minimizing the leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. The authors address the computational challenge of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and probabilistic filtering. They demonstrate strong performance on eight text classification tasks, consistently outperforming 26 baseline methods.

This work builds directly on the recent success of WMD [Kusner et al., 2015] which provided a compelling way to measure document distance using word embeddings. Prior work in document distance focused heavily on unsupervised representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA). S-WMD distinguishes itself by leveraging the power of pre-trained word embeddings and then *adapting* them for a specific task via supervised learning, rather than learning a document representation from scratch. This aligns with a growing trend in metric learning [Davis et al., 2011; Weinberger & Saul, 2009] but crucially addresses the scalability issues when applied to text data, by operating within the lower-dimensional word embedding space.  The use of the Sinkhorn distance for efficient approximation is a smart choice, building on the work of Cuturi [2013].

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistently strong performance across a diverse set of datasets and a large number of competitive baselines. The consistent outperformance is a significant strength.
* **Novel Approach:** Learning a supervised metric *on top of* WMD, rather than learning a new document representation, is a novel and effective approach.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering effectively addresses the computational challenges associated with incorporating supervision into WMD. The authors present a practical and efficient algorithm.
* **Clear Motivation and Explanation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of unsupervised methods and motivates the need for a supervised approach. The explanation of the algorithm and the mathematical derivations are generally clear.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The extensive comparison with numerous baselines provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Availability of Code:** The public availability of the code promotes reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors provide a gradient derivation, the theoretical properties of the proposed method (e.g., convergence guarantees, generalization bounds) are not explored in detail. A more in-depth theoretical analysis would strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of metric learning algorithms is often sensitive to hyperparameter settings. While the authors use Bayesian optimization, a discussion of the sensitivity to different hyperparameters and their impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Initialization Dependence:** The authors acknowledge the importance of initialization, but a more thorough investigation into different initialization strategies and their impact on performance could be valuable.  The description of the WCD initialization is somewhat brief and could benefit from a more detailed justification.
* **Squared Euclidean Distance Choice:** The choice of using squared Euclidean distance in the WMD calculation is mentioned as potentially suboptimal for some datasets, but not rigorously explored.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the overall performance is strong, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the cases where S-WMD performs poorly and potential reasons for these failures.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to supervised document distance learning.
* The empirical results are strong and consistently outperform numerous baselines.
* The computational challenges are addressed effectively through a clever approximation technique.
* The code is publicly available, promoting reproducibility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis is limited.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameters and initialization is not fully explored.
* The justification for certain design choices (e.g., squared Euclidean distance) is brief.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document distance learning. The proposed S-WMD method is novel, effective, and computationally efficient. The thorough evaluation and strong empirical results provide compelling evidence for its effectiveness. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened, the overall quality of the work warrants acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
