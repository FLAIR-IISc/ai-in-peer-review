## Review of “Supervised Word Mover’s Distance”

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised document distance by applying a linear transformation and reweighting word distributions on top of the existing Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) framework. Building upon the strong performance of WMD, the authors aim to incorporate supervision by learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space and a word importance vector, optimized via a stochastic leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and mathematical foundations. The literature review is thorough, placing the work in context with existing metric learning techniques and optimal transport methods. Importantly, the authors provide extensive experimental results across eight datasets, comparing S-WMD against a considerable 26 baseline methods. The results consistently demonstrate superior performance, showcasing the efficacy of the proposed approach.

However, the contribution feels incremental. While the application of NCA-style optimization to WMD is reasonable, the core idea of applying a linear transform and reweighting isn't a significant conceptual leap. A major concern is the justification for the word reweighting. The authors acknowledge it *could* alter document semantics, but provide insufficient reasoning or analysis to demonstrate it *improves* label separation without doing so. How does the reweighting specifically contribute to better document representations, and what guarantees are there that it doesn’t introduce noise? 

Furthermore, a practical concern arises regarding the computation of the reweighting vector *w* for completely new documents not seen during training. The paper doesn't address this crucial aspect.  Finally, the use of “semantic difference” in the abstract is ambiguous and reads as label/topic/authorship similarity rather than genuine semantic understanding.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results on multiple datasets.
*   Clear writing and thorough presentation.
*   Well-motivated problem building on a successful existing method (WMD).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty – the approach is a relatively straightforward application of existing techniques.
*   Weak justification for the reweighting component and its impact on document semantics.
*   Lack of discussion on how to handle new/unseen documents.
*   Ambiguous terminology.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a clear presentation of a reasonably effective technique. However, addressing the concerns regarding novelty, the reweighting strategy, and the generalization to unseen documents is crucial. I do not object to acceptance *provided* these issues are adequately addressed by the authors.
