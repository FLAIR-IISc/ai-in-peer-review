This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), an unsupervised metric that calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" words from one document to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. The key contribution of this paper is to incorporate supervision into this framework. This is achieved by learning two components: (1) a linear transformation, A, of the word embedding space, and (2) a vector of word-importance weights, w, that re-scales the document's bag-of-words representation. The learning objective, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), is to minimize the stochastic leave-one-out k-NN classification error. A significant technical challenge is that the gradient of the WMD is computationally prohibitive. The authors overcome this by proposing a clever and efficient approximation based on the regularized Sinkhorn distance, which makes the end-to-end training of S-WMD practical.

**Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is sound and the core technical contribution—the use of the Sinkhorn distance to enable efficient gradient-based optimization of the WMD—is both novel and effective. The claims of state-of-the-art performance are well-supported by a comprehensive experimental evaluation on eight real-world datasets against a large suite of 26 competitive baselines, including various document representations paired with standard metric learning algorithms. The authors are careful in their experimental setup, using Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning, which adds credibility to the results. The inclusion of a fast initialization strategy based on the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) is also a thoughtful practical consideration.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The background on word2vec, WMD, and NCA is clear, and the motivation for S-WMD is well-articulated. The authors also helpfully promise to release their code, enhancing reproducibility. However, there are a few areas where clarity could be improved:

*   The intuition behind the word importance vector `w` and its interaction with the linear transform `A` could be elaborated upon. While the formulation in Eq. (5) is clear, a more detailed discussion of why re-weighting the document histograms *before* the optimal transport problem is beneficial would be insightful.
*   It is unclear from the experiments which of the two learned components (`A` or `w`) is the primary driver of the performance gains. An ablation study (e.g., training with only `A` or only `w`) would be a valuable addition to disentangle their respective contributions.
*   There is a minor ambiguity regarding the regularization parameter `lambda`. It is introduced in the context of the regularized transport problem in Eq. (2), but it is not explicitly stated whether this is the same `lambda=10` referenced on line 170 during the gradient approximation. Clarifying this would be helpful.
*   The procedure for selecting the number of neighbors, `k`, for the k-NN classification in the final evaluation is not described. It is important to know if `k` was tuned per dataset or per method, as this can impact the results.
*   A small correction: the text on line 257 mentions outperforming baselines "on average across all datasets," which seems to refer to the "average rank" shown in Table 2, not an "average error". Aligning the text with the table header would improve precision.

**Originality**
The paper presents a novel and significant contribution. While metric learning and document distance are well-established fields, this is the first work to successfully incorporate supervision directly into the powerful WMD framework. The combination of WMD with an NCA-style loss is new, and the technique for deriving a tractable gradient is a key original contribution. The work is well-situated within the literature and clearly distinguishes itself from prior work in metric learning (which typically operates on fixed, high-dimensional document vectors) and supervised embedding methods.

**Significance**
The results presented are highly significant and likely to be impactful. S-WMD establishes a new state of the art for k-NN based document classification, outperforming a wide array of strong baselines. Given the success of the original WMD, this supervised extension is likely to be of great interest to both researchers and practitioners working on text classification, retrieval, and ranking tasks where labeled data is available. The problem it addresses—learning a task-specific metric that leverages the semantic richness of word embeddings—is important, and this work provides a robust and effective solution.

The authors’ rebuttal clarified that k-NN classification is the most natural evaluation for a new distance metric, which is a reasonable justification for the experimental design.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** Presents a novel, well-motivated, and powerful method for supervised document metric learning that significantly advances the state of the art.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is extensively validated on 8 diverse datasets against 26 baselines, demonstrating consistent and substantial performance improvements.
3.  **Solid Technical Contribution:** The use of the Sinkhorn distance to create a practical optimization algorithm for WMD is a clever and important technical contribution that makes the approach feasible.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is clearly written, and the authors' commitment to releasing code is a strong positive for the community.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Missing Ablation Study:** The paper would be strengthened by an analysis that disentangles the effects of the learned word-embedding transformation `A` and the word-importance weights `w`.
2.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** Several minor details regarding the experimental setup (e.g., selection of `k` for k-NN) and model parameters (`lambda`) could be specified more clearly.

**Final Assessment:**
This is a strong paper with a novel and significant contribution. The proposed S-WMD method is technically sound, highly effective, and backed by a thorough experimental evaluation. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to opportunities for deeper analysis and clarification, which do not detract from the core contribution. The paper is an excellent fit for NIPS and I recommend acceptance.
