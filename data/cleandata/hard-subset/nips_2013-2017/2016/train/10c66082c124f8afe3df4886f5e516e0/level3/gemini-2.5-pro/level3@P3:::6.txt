This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the recently introduced Word Mover's Distance (WMD) for document classification. The original WMD is a powerful but unsupervised metric that calculates the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. The key contribution of this work is to incorporate supervision into this framework. The authors propose learning a linear transformation, `A`, of the word embedding space and a vector of word-importance weights, `w`, to tailor the distance metric for a specific classification task.

The learning is framed as an optimization problem that minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out k-NN classification error, following the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) loss function. A major technical challenge is that the gradient of the WMD involves a nested optimization problem, which is computationally prohibitive. The authors cleverly circumvent this by using the Sinkhorn distance, an entropy-regularized and differentiable approximation of the WMD, which allows for efficient gradient computation. The method is evaluated extensively on eight text classification datasets, where it consistently outperforms a large suite of 26 competitive baselines.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of the original WMD—its unsupervised nature. The proposed method of learning a task-specific metric by transforming the underlying word embedding space and re-weighting words is a novel and well-motivated approach. It effectively bridges the gap between powerful unsupervised document distances and supervised metric learning, which is a significant contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The technical approach is solid. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to create a differentiable and efficient approximation for the WMD optimization is a crucial and elegant solution to a difficult problem. The overall framework, which combines this with an NCA-style loss function and a practical batch-based training scheme, is well-conceived.
*   **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare S-WMD against a wide array of 26 baselines, including classic document representations (TF-IDF, LSI, LDA), modern methods (mSDA), and combinations of these with standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The consistent state-of-the-art or near-state-of-the-art performance of S-WMD across eight diverse datasets provides strong evidence of its effectiveness.
*   **Reproducibility:** The authors' promise to release their Matlab code is commendable and will be a great asset to the community, facilitating further research and application of their method.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

While the paper is strong, there are several points that could be clarified or improved to strengthen the final manuscript. I appreciate the authors' careful clarifications in their rebuttal, and the following points are intended to help improve the paper for publication.

1.  **Intuition and Ablation:** The two main components of the learned metric are the linear transform `A` and the word importance weights `w`.
    *   Could the authors provide more intuition behind the definition and usage of `w` in Equation (5)? Re-weighting the normalized BoW vector `d` before it enters the optimal transport constraints is an interesting choice. A more detailed explanation of how this encourages the model to focus on important words would be beneficial.
    *   A key unanswered question is which of these two components (`A` or `w`) drives the reported improvements. An ablation study that evaluates the performance of S-WMD with only `A` (i.e., `w` fixed to ones) and only `w` (i.e., `A` fixed to identity) would be extremely valuable. This would provide deeper insight into the method's behavior and the relative importance of transforming the geometric space versus re-weighting word contributions.

2.  **Clarifications on Method and Results:**
    *   There was some confusion regarding the use of the regularized transport problem from Equation (2). While it is clear this is used to approximate the gradient, it might be helpful to explicitly state that the `λ` in the dual variable calculation (ln 170) is the same entropy regularization parameter from Equation (2).
    *   The paper claims the method is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words" (ln 316). This is a known strength of the original WMD. Could the authors elaborate on how the supervised learning of `A` and `w` specifically enhances this property, rather than just inheriting it?
    *   The text mentions an "average error across datasets" (ln 257), but I was unable to find this metric in Table 2 or elsewhere. Table 2 reports "Avg. Rank". Please clarify where this average error is reported or correct the text.
    *   In Table 2, the columns "n" and "ne" are not defined. Please add a clarification to the caption.
    *   The experimental setup for k-NN classification is missing a description of how the hyperparameter `k` was chosen. Was a fixed `k` used for all methods and datasets, or was it tuned for each? This is an important detail for reproducibility and fair comparison.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and empirically strong method for supervised document metric learning. It builds intelligently on previous work (WMD, NCA, Sinkhorn distances) to create a significant contribution to the field. The weaknesses are primarily related to a need for deeper analysis and clarification, which can be addressed in the final version. I recommend acceptance.
