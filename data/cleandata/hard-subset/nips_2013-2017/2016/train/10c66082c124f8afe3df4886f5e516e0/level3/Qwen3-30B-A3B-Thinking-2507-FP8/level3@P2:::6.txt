

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to improve document classification. The authors learn a linear transformation A of word embeddings and word importance weights w to minimize the leave-one-out kNN classification error. The key innovation is efficiently approximating the computationally expensive WMD optimization using the Sinkhorn distance, enabling practical training. This work builds directly on Kusner et al.'s WMD (2015) and metric learning approaches like NCA, but applies them to the word embedding space rather than document representations.

## Strengths

The paper makes a compelling case for the value of supervision in document distances, which is a natural extension of the WMD framework. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with results on 8 diverse text classification datasets and 26 competitive baselines. The authors provide a clear initialization strategy (S-WCD) that makes training efficient, and the method consistently outperforms all baselines across most datasets, achieving the lowest error on 5/8 datasets and the best average performance. The technical approach is sound, with a well-justified approximation using the Sinkhorn distance (Eq. 2) that makes the optimization tractable. The paper is clearly written with a logical structure, and the authors have promised to release their implementation code.

## Weaknesses

The paper would benefit from greater clarity on several key aspects. First, the role of the word importance weights w (Eq. 5) needs better explanation. It's unclear how the weights affect the document representation and whether they're simply reweighting word frequencies or performing a more sophisticated transformation. The authors should clarify which component (A vs. w) contributes more to the performance gains, as this would help readers understand the method's core innovation.

The claim that the method is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words" (ln316) requires stronger justification. The paper lacks concrete examples or analysis demonstrating this property. Additionally, there's confusion regarding the lambda parameter in Eq. 2 and its relation to the lambda referenced at ln170—these should be explicitly connected.

The paper mentions "average error across datasets" at ln257 but fails to show this in the results (Table 3 appears to show training times, not error). The reader must infer the average performance from Table 2, which would be clearer with explicit mention. The paper also doesn't explain how k was chosen for kNN classification (is it dataset-dependent or method-dependent?), which is crucial for reproducibility.

## Overall Assessment

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, though the justification for the "forgiving" property and the lambda parameter need strengthening.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written but would benefit from clarifying the role of w, the lambda parameter, and k selection. The intuition behind Eq. (5) requires better explanation.

**Originality:** The work extends WMD to supervised learning in a novel way, leveraging the word embedding space rather than document representations. This is sufficiently distinct from prior work (WMD, metric learning) to be considered original.

**Significance:** The method consistently outperforms 26 baselines across 8 datasets, demonstrating significant practical value for text classification tasks. The efficient training makes it suitable for real-world applications.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro:** The paper presents a significant extension of the WMD framework with clear empirical advantages across multiple datasets. The efficient approximation using Sinkhorn distance makes the method practical, and the comprehensive evaluation demonstrates strong performance. The work advances the state of the art in document distance metrics.

**Con:** The paper would benefit from greater clarity on key methodological aspects (w, lambda, k selection) and stronger justification for the "forgiving" claim. Some results (e.g., "average error across datasets") are not clearly presented.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of document distance metrics with clear empirical advantages. The minor weaknesses regarding clarity can be addressed in the camera-ready version. The authors have already shown responsiveness to reviewer comments (as noted in the key points), and the work aligns well with NIPS' focus on novel, high-quality contributions to machine learning. The method represents a meaningful advance over unsupervised WMD and other metric learning approaches for text classification.
