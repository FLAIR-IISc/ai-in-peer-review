This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which leverages pretrained word embeddings to define the distance between two documents as an optimal transport problem. While WMD has shown state-of-the-art performance in an unsupervised kNN setting, it cannot incorporate label information. This work addresses this limitation by introducing a supervised learning framework. Specifically, S-WMD learns two components: 1) a linear transformation of the word embedding space to adapt the word representations to the specific task, and 2) a word-importance vector to up-weight discriminative words.

The authors formulate the learning problem as the minimization of a stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A key technical challenge is that differentiating through the WMD objective is computationally prohibitive and the gradient may not be unique. To overcome this, the paper proposes an efficient and elegant approximation based on the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance, which makes the optimization practical. The method is evaluated on eight text classification datasets, where it is shown to consistently and significantly outperform an extensive set of 26 competitive baselines.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a well-motivated and timely contribution. WMD was a significant advance in unsupervised document similarity, and this work provides a natural and powerful supervised extension. Applying discriminative training to learn a domain-specific ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance in the context of text is a novel and important idea. The ability to fine-tune the powerful representations from large, unsupervised corpora (via word2vec) for specific supervised tasks is a key direction for the field, and this paper makes a strong contribution in that vein. The results demonstrate a clear advance in the state-of-the-art for kNN-based document classification.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The authors correctly identify the primary challenge in their formulation—the computational complexity and non-differentiability of the gradient of the WMD loss. Their solution, which leverages the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance to create a smooth and efficiently computable approximation, is both clever and practical. The derivation of the gradients for both the linear transformation matrix A and the word-weight vector w is clearly presented. The overall approach, combining an NCA-style loss with the approximated WMD gradients, is coherent and well-executed.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear background on the necessary components (word2vec, WMD, NCA), which makes the paper self-contained. The motivation for the problem is articulated clearly, and the proposed S-WMD method is explained with appropriate mathematical detail. The algorithmic details, including the initialization strategy and batch optimization, are described sufficiently for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is a major strength of this work. The experiments are exceptionally thorough and convincing.
    *   **Datasets:** The evaluation is performed on eight diverse, real-world datasets, covering a range of classification tasks, document lengths, and class numbers.
    *   **Baselines:** The comparison against 26 baselines is comprehensive. It includes classic methods (TF-IDF), topic models (LSI, LDA), modern neural approaches (mSDA), and the original WMD. Crucially, the authors also compare against strong baselines where standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML) are applied to these document representations, providing a fair and challenging benchmark.
    *   **Results:** The proposed S-WMD method achieves the best performance on 5 of the 8 datasets and the best average rank overall, often outperforming the next-best method by a large margin. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The qualitative visualizations using t-SNE and the word-weight clouds further strengthen the paper by providing intuition for why the method works.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While this is a very strong paper, there are a few minor points that could be addressed to improve it further:

1.  **Analysis of Failure Case:** The paper notes that the unsupervised WMD outperforms S-WMD on the CLASSIC dataset. While a plausible hypothesis is provided (poor initialization or suboptimality of the squared Euclidean distance), this result warrants a bit more investigation. A brief ablation study, perhaps exploring the effect of the initialization or using a different p-norm for the word distance, could provide more insight and further strengthen the paper's conclusions.

2.  **Impact of Initialization:** The proposed initialization strategy (S-WCD) is very effective and performs impressively on its own (S-WMD init. in Table 2). It would be beneficial to include a more direct discussion or analysis of the marginal benefit of the full S-WMD optimization over this strong starting point. While the results are present in the table, a dedicated paragraph discussing when and why the full optimization provides the largest gains would be insightful.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper sets key hyperparameters (e.g., batch size B=32, neighbor set size N=200, regularization λ=10) to fixed values. While the training times reported are impressive, a brief discussion on the sensitivity of the model's performance and training time to these choices (perhaps in the supplementary material) would be valuable for practitioners looking to apply this method.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, significant, and technically sound contribution to the field of document classification and metric learning. The idea of learning a supervised metric for the Word Mover's Distance is compelling, and the proposed solution is both elegant and practical. The paper's claims are strongly supported by an extensive and rigorous set of experiments that demonstrate a new state-of-the-art for kNN-based text classification. The work is clearly presented and should be of high interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
