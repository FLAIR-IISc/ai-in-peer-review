This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method to learn a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds directly upon the unsupervised Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) proposed by Kusner et al. (2015), which frames document distance as an optimal transport problem between word distributions using pre-trained word embeddings. The authors extend this by learning two components: a linear transformation (A) of the word embedding space and a word-importance vector (w) that reweights the document’s word distribution. The parameters are optimized to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification error, drawing inspiration from established metric learning techniques like Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA).

The paper's primary strengths lie in its clarity and empirical rigor. It is exceptionally well-written, with a clear motivation and a thorough literature review that situates the work effectively within the context of both document representation and metric learning. The experimental evaluation is a significant highlight, demonstrating consistently strong performance across eight real-world datasets against an impressive suite of 26 baselines. This provides compelling evidence for the method's practical utility in kNN-based document classification.

Despite the strong results, the paper has several weaknesses. The originality of the contribution is somewhat limited; applying a linear transform and an NCA-style objective to learn a distance metric is a well-established paradigm. The main novelty is its specific application to the WMD framework. Furthermore, the motivation for learning the word reweighting vector `w` is weak. Reweighting could fundamentally alter a document's semantic content, and the paper lacks justification for how this process improves label separation without corrupting the document's meaning. A practical concern is the lack of discussion on how to compute `w` for a new document with unseen words. Finally, the use of "semantic difference" in the abstract is ambiguous, as the method learns a task-specific similarity (e.g., topic, author) rather than a general semantic one.

In summary, this is a solid piece of empirical work with impressive results. I would not object to its acceptance, but I strongly recommend that the authors address the concerns regarding the motivation, semantic implications, and practical application of the word reweighting vector `w` in the final version.
