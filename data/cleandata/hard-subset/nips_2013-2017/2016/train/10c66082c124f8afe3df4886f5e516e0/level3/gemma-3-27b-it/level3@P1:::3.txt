This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a document distance metric by incorporating supervision into the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD leverages word embeddings and optimal transport to define a distance between documents, achieving strong results in kNN-based document classification. However, WMD is inherently unsupervised. S-WMD addresses this limitation by learning a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector, minimizing leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. A key contribution lies in efficiently computing gradients for this optimization, utilizing a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and a probabilistic filtering technique. The authors demonstrate superior performance on eight real-world text classification tasks, outperforming 26 competitive baselines.

The work builds upon a rich history of research in document distance metrics, beginning with bag-of-words and TF-IDF representations and evolving to dimensionality reduction techniques like LSI and LDA. More recently, WMD offered a significant advancement by leveraging the semantic richness of word embeddings. This paper positions itself within the growing field of metric learning, specifically addressing the challenge of applying these techniques to high-dimensional text data. While many metric learning methods scale poorly with dimensionality, S-WMD cleverly operates within the lower-dimensional word embedding space. Related work such as Cuturi & Avis’s entropy regularization of the optimal transport problem, and the reweighting schemes proposed by Lebanon and Le/Cuturi, are acknowledged but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of their nuances in relation to the proposed approach. 

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The core idea – extending WMD with supervision – is intuitive and well-motivated. The proposed method is technically sound, with a clear derivation of the gradient and a practical approach to overcoming the computational complexity of the WMD calculation. The extensive experimental evaluation, encompassing eight datasets and a comprehensive comparison against a large number of baselines, provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD. The consistent outperformance of these baselines is a strong indicator of the contribution's significance.  The authors also address practical considerations like initialization and batch gradient descent, further enhancing the method’s usability.

However, there are areas for improvement. While the paper describes the interplay between regularized and unregularized optimal transport, a more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs and implications of this relaxation would be beneficial. The mathematical derivations, while generally clear, could benefit from greater rigor and consistency in notation.  Specifically, the use of both Euclidean and squared Euclidean distances (p-Wasserstein vs 2-Wasserstein) within the equations creates some ambiguity; defining WMD as a p-Wasserstein distance from the outset would resolve this.  An algorithmic box summarizing the key steps of S-WMD would also improve clarity and reproducibility. 

Furthermore, the paper could provide more insights into the learned word-importance weight vector `w`.  Analyzing which words receive higher weights and how these weights vary across datasets would strengthen the understanding of the model's behavior.  While the authors acknowledge the work of Wang et al., a more detailed comparison and discussion of the limitations of their approach, especially regarding the lack of histogram reweighting, would be valuable. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong empirical results:** S-WMD consistently outperforms a large number of strong baselines across diverse datasets.
*   **Novel combination:** The combination of WMD with supervised metric learning and efficient gradient computation is a novel and effective approach.
*   **Technical soundness:** The method is well-motivated, clearly explained, and supported by rigorous mathematical derivations.
*   **Practical relevance:**  The method addresses a practical problem in document similarity and offers a potentially valuable tool for various NLP tasks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Notational inconsistencies:** The mix of Euclidean and squared Euclidean distances can be confusing.
*   **Limited analysis of learned weights:** Further investigation into the word-importance weights could provide deeper insights.
*   **Discussion of related work could be expanded:**  A more detailed discussion of related work on optimal transport and metric learning would enhance the paper’s context and impact.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document similarity. While some minor improvements could enhance its clarity and impact, the strong empirical results and novel technical approach warrant acceptance to NIPS 2016.
