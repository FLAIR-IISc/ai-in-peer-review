## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a document distance metric by parameterizing the cost matrix of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) with a Mahalanobis distance and also learning word importance weights. The core idea leverages recent work on efficient approximation of optimal transport (Sinkhorn distance) to enable practical gradient-based optimization of the WMD with respect to a loss function derived from k-Nearest Neighbors classification. 

The work builds logically upon the existing WMD framework, recognizing its unsupervised nature as a limitation and addressing it with a supervised learning approach. The authors correctly position their contribution within the broader context of metric learning and highlight the challenges of applying traditional methods to high-dimensional text data. The use of the Sinkhorn distance for efficient gradient computation is a clever adaptation, and the experimental results across eight datasets are convincingly strong, consistently outperforming a comprehensive set of 26 baselines. This empirical validation is a major strength of the paper. 

However, the paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the interplay between the regularized and unregularized optimal transport formulations. While the authors mention the regularization via entropy, a deeper exploration of how the regularization parameter (λ) influences performance and the trade-off with accuracy would be valuable.  The mathematical derivations, while generally clear, could be more rigorous, and an algorithmic box summarizing the S-WMD procedure would significantly improve clarity. Additionally, Eq. 1 uses Euclidean distance while Eq.6 uses squared Euclidean distance (essentially a 2-Wasserstein distance); describing WMD more generally as a p-Wasserstein distance would eliminate this confusion.

The paper also overlooks relevant prior work on reweighting schemes in optimal transport. The reweighting in Eq. 5 is similar to work by Guy Lebanon and Le/Cuturi, which should be cited. Further, while the authors demonstrate strong performance, they offer limited insight into the learned weight vector *w*.  Providing analysis of which words receive higher weights and their relationship to the classification task would enrich the empirical evaluation. Finally, the paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of how S-WMD differs from Cuturi/Avis (particularly regarding entropy regularization, continuous vs. discrete cost, and linear parameterization) and clearly articulating the limitations of Wang et al.’s approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper presents a simple, straightforward, yet effective idea that combines existing techniques in a novel way. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines. 

**Arguments against Acceptance:** The paper lacks in-depth discussion of certain theoretical aspects and relevant prior work. The analysis of the learned word weights is superficial.

**Overall:** I support acceptance of this paper. The strengths in empirical performance and the generally sound methodology outweigh the relatively minor weaknesses. Addressing the suggested improvements would further solidify the contribution.
