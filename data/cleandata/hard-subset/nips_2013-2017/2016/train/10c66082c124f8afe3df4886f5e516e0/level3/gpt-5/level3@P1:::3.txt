This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of WMD that learns (i) a linear transformation of the word embedding space (a Mahalanobis ground metric parameterized by A) and (ii) a word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. Supervision is injected by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective (NCA), with pairwise distances computed via the optimal transport problem underlying WMD. Because differentiating through the exact WMD is impractical, the authors use an entropic OT relaxation (Sinkhorn) both to compute approximate transports T* and to obtain surrogate dual variables for gradients w.r.t. w, yielding an efficient training algorithm with batch sampling and WCD-based neighbor filtering. Empirically, across 8 datasets and 26 baselines (including unsupervised WMD/WCD and supervised LMNN/ITML/NCA on various representations), S-WMD achieves the best average rank and wins on 5/8 datasets, with reasonable training times. The paper situates S-WMD with respect to WMD (Kusner et al.), Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi), and classic metric learning (NCA/LMNN/ITML), and it relates to supervised EMD work in vision (e.g., Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The learning objective is standard (NCA). The parameterization of the ground cost via A and the histogram reweighting via w are natural. Using Sinkhorn to obtain T* and dual variables for efficient gradients is well-motivated; strong convexity of the entropic objective ensures uniqueness and stable differentiation. That said, some derivations are only sketched; the approximation gap introduced by replacing exact WMD and its (sub)gradient with entropic OT and surrogate duals is not analyzed, and choices like λ=10 are not justified beyond empirical convenience. Constraints/regularization on w (e.g., nonnegativity) and A are not fully specified.
- Empirical evaluation: Strong. Many competitive baselines, careful hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian optimization, and consistent improvements on most datasets with fair experimental protocol. Visualizations (t-SNE) and learned word-weight wordclouds are helpful. Ablations could be deeper (see below).
- Reproducibility: Code is released; algorithmic details are mostly present, but additional clarity would help.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly well written and easy to follow. However, there are notational inconsistencies: the base WMD definition switches between Euclidean and squared Euclidean costs, and p-Wasserstein versus 1- or 2-Wasserstein is not consistently specified. Eq. 1 and Eq. 6/8 should be reconciled by explicitly framing WMD as p-Wasserstein and fixing p throughout.
- Some minor typos (e.g., “.2 Optimization”), and it would help to have a cleaner, self-contained algorithm box with variable shapes, constraints (A, w), and the neighbor-filtering strategy.
- The derivations for the gradients (especially wrt w using dual variables via u,v) deserve more detail and explicit assumptions.

Originality
- The core idea—learning the WMD ground metric—builds on existing lines of work: supervised EMD/OT (Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.), learning ground costs/feature reweighting in OT (Cuturi and collaborators), and NCA-style objectives. The contribution here is to instantiate this for document distances by leveraging pretrained word embeddings (thus keeping the ground space low-dimensional), jointly learning A and w, and providing practical, efficient training via an entropic relaxation with a sensible initialization (S-WCD) and neighbor filtering. The combination is simple but effective. Novelty is moderate but meaningful for NLP+OT.

Significance
- The method improves a widely used and conceptually elegant distance (WMD), and the gains over strong baselines across diverse text tasks make it likely to be adopted. Learning A and w directly in the word-embedding space is a practical design with low computational footprint relative to classic metric learning on high-dimensional BOW. The work should influence subsequent supervised OT applications in NLP.

Specific strengths
- Clear, task-relevant parameterization (A, w) that aligns OT costs with labels.
- Efficient training using Sinkhorn, dual-variable approximations, and neighbor filtering.
- Thorough empirical study with many baselines and datasets; public code.
- Sensible initialization via S-WCD that already performs well.

Specific weaknesses and suggestions
- Regularized vs. unregularized OT: Provide a more systematic discussion and empirical study of λ. How does λ affect accuracy, runtime, and stability? Is there a noticeable bias relative to exact WMD at larger λ? Consider reporting accuracy as a function of λ and an ablation that uses exact WMD in the inner problem on small subsets for reference.
- Derivations and constraints: Be more rigorous about the gradient with respect to w. Clarify the assumptions under which the surrogate dual variables yield correct gradients for the relaxed objective and articulate how this approximates the original (unregularized) WMD gradient. Clearly state constraints on w (nonnegativity?) and A, and any regularizers to avoid degenerate solutions (e.g., w concentrating on a few rare words). Discuss whether A is equivalent to learning a PSD Mahalanobis metric (M = A^T A) and whether you constrain r<d for regularization.
- Notation: Unify the cost as p-Wasserstein with explicit p, and be consistent about squared vs. unsquared Euclidean distances. Define all variables (e.g., shapes of A, w) in the algorithm box.
- Prior work: The reweighting in Eq. 5 is related to earlier supervised term weighting and discriminative learning over multinomials (e.g., work by Guy Lebanon) and to OT-based learning of feature weights/ground costs (Cuturi and collaborators). Please add citations and clarify the novelty. Also more clearly distinguish your setting from Cuturi & Avis and from Wang et al.: your cost is learned in a pretrained embedding space; you jointly learn histogram weights; and you optimize an NCA objective rather than margin-based or constraint-based formulations. Briefly discuss limitations of these prior approaches and why yours is preferable for text.
- Ablations: Quantify the contributions of A vs. w (A-only, w-only, both). Provide statistics/interpretation on learned w beyond a word cloud (e.g., sparsity, overlap across datasets, correlation with IDF). Report sensitivity to the number of neighbors N and batch size B.
- Scope limitations: Discuss behavior on very long documents (p large) and very large vocabularies; report memory/time scaling. Consider results with alternative embeddings (e.g., GloVe) to demonstrate robustness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, effective improvement over a popular metric with solid empirical gains and open-source code.
- Reasonable technical novelty in combining supervised metric learning with entropic OT for WMD, with efficient training.
- Clear potential impact in NLP applications relying on document distances.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical depth is limited; reliance on heuristic approximations (fixed λ) without principled analysis.
- Some missing citations and notational/derivational clarity issues.
- Ablation and sensitivity analyses could be more comprehensive.

Overall recommendation: Accept. The idea is straightforward but well executed, the empirical results are compelling, and the method is likely to be useful to the community. Strengthening the clarity of the mathematics, the discussion of regularization, and the related-work positioning would further improve the paper.
