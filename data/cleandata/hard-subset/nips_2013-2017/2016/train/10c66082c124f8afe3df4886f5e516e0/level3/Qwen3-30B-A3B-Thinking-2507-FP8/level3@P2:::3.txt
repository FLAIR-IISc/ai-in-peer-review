

# Review for "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to improve document classification performance. The authors build upon Kusner et al.'s WMD (ICML 2015, which was presented at NIPS 2015), which defined document distances as optimal transport problems between word embeddings, and combine it with metric learning techniques like Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights. The key innovation is learning a supervised metric that directly minimizes the stochastic leave-one-out classification error under the WMD framework.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, showing consistent improvements over 26 baselines across 8 document classification datasets. This represents a significant contribution to the field of document representation and metric learning, as it effectively leverages the strengths of both word embeddings and supervised learning for document distances.

**Strengths:**
1. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, with 8 diverse datasets and 26 competitive baselines. The results clearly demonstrate the superiority of S-WMD over existing methods.
2. The authors provide a clever approximation to the computationally expensive WMD gradient using entropy regularization (Cuturi's Sinkhorn distance), making the approach practical.
3. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the relationship between WMD, metric learning, and the proposed method.
4. The initialization strategy using Supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) is insightful and improves convergence.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Notational inconsistency:** The paper uses both Euclidean (Eq.1) and squared Euclidean (Eq.6) distances without clear explanation. The authors should consistently describe WMD as p-Wasserstein (with p=1 or 2) to avoid confusion.
2. **Mathematical rigor:** The derivation of gradients (Eq.8-10) lacks sufficient detail. The paper should provide more rigorous justification for the approximations made, particularly regarding the relationship between the regularized and unregularized optimal transport formulations.
3. **Prior work:** The reweighting approach in Eq.5 is not novel (see Lebanon's work on histogram reweighting in metric learning, as well as Cuturi's work on continuous vs. discrete cost functions). The paper should more clearly distinguish its contribution from these prior works.
4. **Algorithm presentation:** The paper would benefit from including a formal algorithm box to clarify the optimization procedure, which would improve reproducibility.
5. **Analysis of learned weights:** The paper doesn't sufficiently analyze the meaning of the learned word importance weights w. Providing insights into which words are weighted more heavily and why would strengthen the paper.

**Improvement suggestions:**
1. Clarify the relationship between regularized and unregularized optimal transport formulations in the theoretical section. Explain why the entropy regularization approximation is valid for the supervised learning objective.
2. Include a formal algorithm box for S-WMD that clearly outlines the optimization steps.
3. Expand the related work section to better distinguish the paper from Cuturi & Avis (2014), Wang et al. (2015), and other metric learning approaches that parameterize the ground distance.
4. Add a section analyzing the learned weight vector w, showing examples of important words for different classification tasks and discussing their relevance to the classification problem.

**Overall assessment:**
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to document representation learning. The proposed S-WMD method is simple, effective, and well-justified with comprehensive experimental results. While the paper has some minor weaknesses in mathematical rigor and related work discussion, these are easily addressable with minor revisions. The empirical results are compelling, and the method has clear potential for practical application in text classification tasks.

The paper satisfies all four NIPS criteria:
- **Quality:** The method is technically sound, with well-supported experimental results.
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, though could be improved with a formal algorithm box.
- **Originality:** The combination of WMD with supervised metric learning is novel, though the reweighting component has prior work.
- **Significance:** The results demonstrate consistent improvement over 26 baselines across 8 datasets, representing a meaningful advance in document classification.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the mathematical rigor, notational consistency, and related work issues. The paper's empirical strength and practical value make it a valuable contribution to the field.
