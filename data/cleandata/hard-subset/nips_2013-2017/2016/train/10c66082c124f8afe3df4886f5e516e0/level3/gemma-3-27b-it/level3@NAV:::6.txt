## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

This paper presents Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) to incorporate supervised learning for document similarity. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation *A* of the word embedding space and word importance weights *w* to minimize k-NN classification error. This is a valuable contribution, building upon the recent success of WMD and addressing its limitation of being purely unsupervised. The approach leverages the efficient Sinkhorn distance approximation to make the optimization tractable.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation and methodology. The experimental setup is comprehensive, evaluating S-WMD on eight datasets against a large number of baselines (26 total). The authors consistently outperform most baselines and provide visualization (t-SNE) and analysis of the learned word weights, adding credibility to their findings.  The promise to release Matlab code is a strong positive aspect, furthering reproducibility and impact. I particularly appreciated the authors’ thorough responses in the rebuttal, addressing concerns and clarifying details. 

However, several points require further attention. While the authors mention that learning both *A* and *w* is crucial, it’s unclear *which* component drives the reported improvements. A more detailed ablation study dissecting the contribution of each element would be helpful. The intuition behind Eq. (5), specifically the usage of *w* in re-weighting the word histograms, could be elaborated upon. The explanation provided is somewhat concise and a clearer example would be beneficial. 

There are also some minor concerns regarding clarity. The connection between Eq. (2) and the lambda referenced at line 170 isn’t explicitly stated and should be clarified. The justification for the claim that S-WMD is “particularly forgiving” of syntactic variation (line 316) is weak; more concrete evidence or explanation is needed. Furthermore, the “average error across datasets” mentioned at line 257 is not found in Table 3; confirming its location (or removing the reference) would improve readability. Finally, Table 2’s columns “n” and “ne” require clarification - their meaning isn't immediately apparent. While the description of *k* for k-NN classification is satisfactory now after the rebuttal, it was initially unclear how *k* was determined (dataset- or method-dependent).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant extension of WMD.
*   Strong empirical results on multiple datasets, consistently outperforming many baselines.
*   Clear writing and well-organized presentation.
*   Commitment to code release.
*   Responsive and thorough rebuttal.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of ablation study to isolate the contributions of *A* and *w*.
*   Insufficient justification for claims regarding syntactic robustness.
*   Minor clarity issues regarding equation references and table descriptions.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution. Addressing the noted concerns would further strengthen the work and make it a more compelling addition to the NIPS proceedings. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
