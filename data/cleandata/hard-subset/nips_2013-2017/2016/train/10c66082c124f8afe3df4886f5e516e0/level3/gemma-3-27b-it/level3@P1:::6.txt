This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), an extension to the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision into document distance learning. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space (A) and word-specific importance weights (w) to minimize leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. The authors address the computational complexity of incorporating supervision by leveraging a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem – the Sinkhorn distance – and a probabilistic filtering technique. Extensive experiments on eight real-world text classification tasks demonstrate that S-WMD consistently outperforms a comprehensive set of 26 baseline methods.

The work builds upon a rich history of document distance metrics, beginning with bag-of-words and TF-IDF representations, evolving through dimensionality reduction techniques like LSI and LDA, and culminating in the embedding-based approach of WMD.  Recent work has also focused on metric learning, though many of these methods struggle to scale to the high dimensionality of text data without pre-processing.  S-WMD distinguishes itself by directly operating on the low-dimensional word embedding space, enabling efficient supervised learning. Related work by Cuturi and Avis and Wang & Guibas explore learning ground distances within the Earth Mover's Distance framework, though they do not incorporate word re-weighting.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling evidence of S-WMD’s effectiveness. The consistently superior performance across eight datasets and against a large number of baselines is a significant achievement.
* **Novel Approach:** Extending WMD to a supervised setting is a valuable contribution. The combination of learning a transformation of the word embedding space and re-weighting word frequencies offers a flexible and powerful approach.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors cleverly address the computational challenges of supervised WMD through the use of the Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering. This makes the method practical for real-world applications.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the algorithm. The inclusion of pseudocode enhances reproducibility.
* **Code Availability:**  The authors state their intention to release Matlab code, which is a positive step towards facilitating further research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Intuition for Word Weights:** While the concept of word importance weights (w) is intuitive, the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of *how* these weights contribute to improved performance.  It would be helpful to see examples of words that receive significantly increased or decreased weights, and the rationale behind these changes.
* **A vs. w Contribution:** It remains unclear which component, the linear transformation A or the word weights w, is primarily responsible for the observed performance gains. Disentangling their contributions through ablation studies would strengthen the analysis.
* **Lambda Consistency:** The use of lambda in Eq. (2) and at line 170 is somewhat confusing.  A clearer explanation of whether these refer to the same parameter, and how the value is chosen, would improve clarity.
* **Claim Justification:** The claim that S-WMD is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words" (line 316) feels somewhat unsubstantiated and could be bolstered with examples or further analysis.
* **Table Clarity:** The columns "n" and "ne" in Table 2 are not defined, hindering the reader's understanding of the results.
* **k-NN Parameter:**  The choice of *k* for the k-NN classifier is not discussed. Is *k* a fixed parameter across all methods and datasets, or is it tuned? Providing this information is crucial for evaluating the fairness of the comparison.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective method for supervised document distance learning.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing state-of-the-art techniques.
* The authors address the computational challenges in a clever and practical manner.
* The clear presentation and commitment to code release enhance the value of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed analysis regarding the contribution of the individual components (A and w) and the intuition behind the learned word weights limits the depth of understanding.
* Some aspects of the presentation (e.g., lambda ambiguity, table clarity) could be improved for greater clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document distance learning. The S-WMD method is both theoretically sound and empirically effective, and the authors have addressed the computational challenges in a practical manner. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2016.  I recommend minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly providing further insight into the learned word weights and the relative contributions of the A and w components.
