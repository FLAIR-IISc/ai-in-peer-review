## Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns word-specific weights and leverages the Sinkhorn distance as a faster approximation to the Wasserstein distance. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and re-weight word frequencies to minimize k-NN classification error. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results, achieving the best average performance across eight datasets and 26 baselines. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical foundations of WMD, the Sinkhorn approximation, and the proposed learning algorithm. The derivation of the gradients, while complex, is presented with commendable detail. The connection to prior work in metric learning, particularly NCA, is well-established. The inclusion of an ablation study with S-WMD initialization is helpful in understanding the contribution of the supervised learning component.

However, the novelty of the work is somewhat limited. While the application of supervised learning to WMD is a logical extension, it builds heavily on existing WMD research and standard metric learning techniques. The authors acknowledge this, but the contribution feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. A more thorough discussion of how S-WMD specifically improves upon or diverges from existing approaches beyond simply adding supervision would be beneficial.

A significant weakness is the limited discussion of the entropic regularizer parameter, lambda. The authors briefly mention its impact but don’t provide sufficient guidance on choosing its value. The influence of lambda on the approximation quality and the resulting behavior (closer to pure Wasserstein vs. entropic/Sinkhorn) is crucial, and a more detailed exploration—perhaps with sensitivity analysis—is necessary. The references to Word Centroid Distance (WCD) appear repeatedly but feel somewhat tangential and occasionally confusing; clarifying their role or removing them would improve clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical performance on a diverse set of datasets.
*   Excellent clarity of writing and mathematical exposition.
*   Well-motivated extension of existing work with a clear and practical approach.
*   Efficient implementation utilizing the Sinkhorn approximation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty beyond applying supervised learning to WMD.
*   Insufficient discussion of the lambda parameter and its impact.
*   Irrelevant and confusing references to WCD.



Overall, this is a solid and well-executed work. While it lacks substantial novelty, the strong empirical results and clear presentation make it a valuable contribution. Addressing the concerns regarding lambda and the WCD references would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
