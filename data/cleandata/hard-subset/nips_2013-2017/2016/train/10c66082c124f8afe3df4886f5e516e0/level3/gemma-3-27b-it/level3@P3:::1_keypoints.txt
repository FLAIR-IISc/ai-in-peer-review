- Proposes S-WMD: learns a supervised metric for WMD via a linear projection of embeddings and document-specific word re-weightings, minimizing kNN error.  
- Addresses computational challenges by using the Sinkhorn (relaxed OT) distance and a probabilistic filtering strategy.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance on eight text classification tasks, outperforming many baselines.  
- Builds on WMD (Kusner et al., 2016) and connects to metric learning literature (ITML, LMNN, NCA).  
- Strength: natural and insightful extension of WMD that incorporates supervision effectively.  
- Strength: clear, well-written, and well-organized presentation with useful visualizations and ablation analysis.  
- Strength: practical and efficient optimization via Sinkhorn approximation and batch gradient descent.  
- Weakness: full supervised learning shows limited impact—S-WCD (word centroid init) achieves comparable performance (Table 3).  
- Weakness: missing baseline evaluating word-centroid document embeddings with standard metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA).  
- Weakness: lacks an ablation isolating contributions of word-specific weights versus the linear projection matrix A.  
- Novelty is somewhat limited to the optimization approach and embedding metric learning within EMD/WMD rather than a wholly new metric learning algorithm.  
- Suggestion: deeper analysis of why S-WCD performs so well to clarify the marginal gains from full supervision.  
- Minor clarifications needed: explicitly cite Kusner et al. (2016) where “The authors” is used; mark optimal dual variables in Eq.11; explicitly note Sinkhorn scaling around line 170.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong empirical results, clear presentation, efficient optimization, and a natural WMD extension.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited additional impact over simpler variants, missing baseline comparisons, missing targeted ablations, and somewhat incremental novelty.  
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed paper recommended for acceptance contingent on addressing the missing baselines, ablations, and minor clarifications.
