Summary
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparison. The method learns two components jointly: (1) a linear projection A that transforms the underlying word embedding space, and (2) a nonnegative word-importance vector w to reweight document histograms. Supervision is incorporated by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective in the spirit of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). Because differentiating through the original WMD is computationally prohibitive and potentially non-smooth, the authors replace the WMD with a strongly convex, entropy-regularized optimal transport objective (Sinkhorn distance), enabling an efficient and differentiable approximation. They further introduce a practical training recipe with S-WCD (NCA on word centroids) for initialization, batch optimization with WCD-based neighbor pruning, and approximate gradients derived from the optimal transport primal and dual solutions. On eight text classification datasets, S-WMD reportedly outperforms 26 baselines, including traditional document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) with and without standard metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML), as well as unsupervised WCD and WMD.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on WMD (Kusner et al., ICML’15), entropic optimal transport (Cuturi, NIPS’13), and metric learning for kNN such as NCA, LMNN, and ITML. Prior work on learning ground metrics for EMD in vision (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.) is acknowledged; the main difference here is learning both a ground metric via a linear projection on word embeddings and a histogram reweighting w, tailored to text classification with word embeddings and kNN.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is coherent and the use of Sinkhorn regularization to obtain differentiable, efficient gradients is appropriate. The gradient derivations with respect to A (using the primal T) and w (using dual potentials α, β) are reasonable under the entropic approximation and uniqueness. The training acceleration via WCD-based neighbor pruning is practical.
- Empirical support: Experiments span eight datasets and a broad set of baselines; S-WMD generally improves kNN accuracy. However, several ablations and baselines that would isolate where the gains come from are missing or underemphasized (see below).
- Reproducibility: The paper is clearly written and code is promised; key hyperparameters (e.g., λ, batch size, neighbor set size) are specified, though sensitivity analyses are limited.

Clarity
The manuscript is very well organized and pleasant to read. The connections among WMD, Sinkhorn, and NCA are clearly explained, and the training algorithm is described concretely. Minor clarifications are suggested below.

Originality
The idea of learning a ground metric for EMD has precedents, but applying entropic OT with an NCA-style objective to learn a linear transform in the embedding space plus word reweighting for text is a novel and interesting combination. The main novelty lies in the efficient gradient approximation via Sinkhorn scaling and how it is used to train a supervised variant of WMD for kNN.

Significance
Supervising WMD is practically valuable: it adapts a strong unsupervised distance to task-specific notions of similarity and shows consistent improvements on kNN text classification. That said, impact may be tempered by two observations:
- The reported gains over the initialization (S-WCD / S-WMD init.) appear modest on some datasets, suggesting a significant portion of the benefit may already be captured by learning on word centroids with NCA.
- The method is evaluated primarily for kNN classification; exploring retrieval or clustering could broaden impact.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation that marries metric learning with OT.
- Efficient training through entropic regularization and neighbor pruning.
- Strong empirical performance across diverse datasets against many baselines.
- Insightful visualization and interpretability via learned word weights.

Weaknesses and suggested improvements
- Missing/understated baselines:
  - Word centroid embeddings with standard metric learning beyond NCA (e.g., ITML/LMNN on centroids) should be reported to fairly assess how much S-WMD improves over simple supervised centroids.
  - A setting with A-only (w fixed to TF-IDF or uniform) and w-only (A = identity) ablations would clarify the contribution of each component.
  - Compare against simple supervised reweighting baselines (e.g., class-conditional TF-IDF, supervised term weighting) to contextualize w.
- Analysis/gains over initialization:
  - Report per-dataset deltas between S-WMD and S-WMD init., and discuss when full training helps most. A sensitivity study on λ, neighbor set size N, and batch size B would also be helpful.
- Theoretical aspects:
  - While the entropic approximation is standard, a brief discussion of bias introduced by λ and its effect on the learned A and w would strengthen the paper.
- Scope of evaluation:
  - Results are limited to kNN; a retrieval evaluation (e.g., MAP, nDCG) would demonstrate broader utility for ranking.
- Efficiency:
  - Provide wall-clock comparisons versus running WMD kNN directly at test time to quantify train-then-test cost vs. compute-on-demand.

Minor comments
- Please specify which work is referenced by “the authors” at line ~152 (context suggests Kusner et al. [19]).
- Equation (11): α and β should have stars (α*λ, β*λ) to indicate use of relaxed dual variables; clarify the connection to Sinkhorn scaling explicitly at first mention in this section.
- Clarify constraints and regularization on w (nonnegativity? normalization? any sparsity/entropy penalty?). Discuss whether w can overfit frequent/rare terms.
- State the dimensionality r of A used in experiments and how it is selected.
- Small typos/notation: ensure consistent use of p (embedding dim vs. document vocabulary size vs. power in norms), and define all symbols where they first appear.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and practically useful supervised extension of a widely used document distance.
- Solid empirical improvements over strong baselines across many datasets.
- Methodology (Sinkhorn-based gradients for supervised OT over words) is likely to be adopted.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete ablation/baseline analysis obscures the specific sources of improvement (A vs. w vs. initialization on centroids).
- Gains over S-WMD initialization can be modest; the incremental contribution beyond supervised centroids is not fully quantified.
- Evaluation focus is narrow (kNN only).

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a well-motivated, efficient, and empirically strong supervised variant of WMD. Addressing the ablation and baseline gaps would make the contribution more compelling.

Confidence
High: familiar with OT, metric learning, and text representation literature.
