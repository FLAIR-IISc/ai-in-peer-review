**Summary of the Main Ideas**

The paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by learning word-specific weights and using the Sinkhorn distance as a faster proxy for the Wasserstein distance. The authors evaluate their method, called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), on eight datasets against 26 baselines and report the best average performance. The paper provides a clear and in-depth mathematical exposition of the method.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon the previous work on Word Mover's Distance (WMD) and Sinkhorn distance. The authors acknowledge the limitations of the unsupervised WMD and propose a supervised extension to improve its performance. The method is also related to other metric learning algorithms, such as Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) and Information-Theoretic Metric Learning (ITML).

**Strengths**

1. **Well-written paper**: The paper is very well written, with clear and in-depth mathematical exposition.
2. **State-of-the-art performance**: The proposed method, S-WMD, achieves the best average performance on eight datasets against 26 baselines.
3. **Efficient computation**: The authors propose a fast gradient computation process and batch gradient descent to speed up the training.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited novelty**: The paper builds upon previous work, and the novelty of the method is limited.
2. **Unclear discussion of lambda**: The role and choice of lambda (entropic regularizer) are poorly discussed in the manuscript, and its value dramatically impacts the behavior and results.
3. **Confusing references**: The references to Word Centroid Distance are not relevant and are confusing.
4. **Lack of analysis**: The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the method's behavior and performance under different settings of lambda.

**Originality**

The paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance, which is a novel contribution to the field of natural language processing. However, the novelty of the method is limited, as it builds upon previous work on WMD and Sinkhorn distance.

**Significance**

The paper's contribution is significant, as it achieves state-of-the-art performance on eight datasets against 26 baselines. The proposed method, S-WMD, has the potential to be widely adopted in natural language processing tasks.

**Quality**
