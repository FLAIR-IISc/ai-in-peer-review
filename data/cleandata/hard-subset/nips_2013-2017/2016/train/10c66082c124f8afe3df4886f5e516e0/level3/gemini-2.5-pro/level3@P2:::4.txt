This paper proposes a method to learn a supervised document distance metric based on the Word Mover's Distance (WMD). WMD, a recently proposed metric, measures the dissimilarity between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other in the space of pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec). While highly effective, WMD is entirely unsupervised. This work, termed Supervised WMD (S-WMD), introduces supervision by learning two components: 1) a linear transformation, A, of the word embedding space, and 2) a word-importance vector, w, that re-weights the word frequencies in each document. The learning is framed as a metric learning problem that minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor classification error, following the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) objective. A key technical contribution is an efficient gradient computation method that approximates the WMD calculation using the entropic-regularized Sinkhorn distance, making the optimization process practical.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. It provides a thorough background on WMD, word embeddings, and metric learning, making the work accessible. The motivation is clear, and the proposed method is explained in sufficient detail.

2.  **Quality & Significance of Results:** The experimental evaluation is a major strength. The authors benchmark their method on eight different datasets against an extensive set of 26 competitive baselines, including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with multiple metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN). S-WMD demonstrates state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance on almost all datasets, providing strong empirical evidence for its effectiveness. The inclusion of visualizations and training time analysis further strengthens the paper.

3.  **Technical Soundness:** The approach to making the gradient computation tractable is clever and well-motivated. Directly calculating the gradient of the WMD loss is computationally prohibitive. By using the Sinkhorn distance as an arbitrarily close, differentiable approximation, the authors develop a practical and efficient algorithm. The initialization strategy using a supervised version of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) is also sensible.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Originality:** The primary weakness is the limited conceptual novelty. The proposed method is a novel and effective combination of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new approach. It combines a known distance (WMD), a standard metric learning technique (linear transformation learned via NCA), and a common heuristic (feature re-weighting). While the synthesis is successful, the individual components are well-established.

2.  **Conceptual Justification for Word Re-weighting (w):** The motivation for the word-importance vector `w` is not well-justified from a conceptual standpoint. The transformation from the original document BoW vector `d` to the re-weighted vector `d̃` (Eq. 4) effectively changes the document's content before the distance is computed. This raises the question of whether the learned metric is a distance between the original documents or between task-specific abstractions of them. An argument could be made that this alters the document's semantics to better fit the labels, which might be undesirable. The paper would be stronger if it provided a more rigorous justification for this design choice beyond its empirical utility. For example, how does this learned `w` relate to other word-importance schemes like TF-IDF?

3.  **Practicality and Generalization of `w`:** It is unclear how the learned vector `w` would generalize. Since `w` is a global vector learned on a specific training set's vocabulary and labels, it is tailored to that particular classification task. Its applicability to new documents is straightforward (element-wise product), but its utility on a different task or domain is questionable. Furthermore, the importance of a word is often highly context-dependent, but `w` assigns a single static importance score. A discussion of these limitations would be welcome.

4.  **Minor Issue with Terminology:** In the abstract and conclusion, the paper suggests that S-WMD captures differences in "semantic meaning" based on supervision. This phrasing is slightly ambiguous. The model learns to align the distance metric with task-specific labels (e.g., topic, author, sentiment), which is not necessarily the same as general semantic similarity. Using more precise language, such as "task-specific similarity," would improve clarity.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   The paper addresses the important and practical problem of incorporating supervision into the powerful WMD framework.
    *   The empirical results are extensive and very strong, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance across multiple datasets and against many baselines.
    *   The method is technically sound, with a practical solution to a difficult optimization problem.
    *   The paper is extremely clear and well-written.

*   **Arguments for Rejection:**
    *   The contribution is somewhat incremental, as it combines several well-known techniques in a straightforward manner.
    *   The conceptual foundation for the word re-weighting component `w` is weak and could benefit from a more thorough justification and analysis.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a very good paper that presents a practical and highly effective method for supervised document classification. The strengths, particularly the comprehensive and impressive experimental results and the clarity of the presentation, are significant. While the conceptual novelty is not groundbreaking, the work represents a solid engineering contribution that is likely to be impactful and useful for practitioners. I would not object to the paper's acceptance, but I strongly encourage the authors to address the conceptual questions surrounding the word re-weighting vector `w` in their final version. A more robust justification for this component would elevate the paper from a strong empirical piece to a more complete scientific contribution.
