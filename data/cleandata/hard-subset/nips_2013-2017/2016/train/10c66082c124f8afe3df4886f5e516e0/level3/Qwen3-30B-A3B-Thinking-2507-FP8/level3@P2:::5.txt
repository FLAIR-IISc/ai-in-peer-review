

# Review for "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

## Summary
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) [19] that incorporates supervision through learning word-specific importance weights and a linear transformation of the word embedding space. The authors address the computational challenge of incorporating supervision into WMD by using a Sinkhorn approximation to the Wasserstein distance, enabling efficient gradient-based optimization. The method is evaluated on 8 text classification tasks against 26 baselines, with S-WMD achieving the lowest average error rate.

The work builds on Kusner et al.'s WMD, which leverages word embeddings to compute document distances as optimal transport problems between embedded words. While WMD was unsupervised, S-WMD introduces supervision to better align document distances with semantic similarity encoded in labels. The paper also references Word Centroid Distance (WCD) as a baseline, though this is somewhat misleading as WCD is a simpler approximation of WMD rather than a directly comparable metric learning approach.

## Quality
The technical content is sound with thorough mathematical derivations of the gradient computation and algorithm design. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering 8 diverse datasets and 26 baselines, with appropriate statistical reporting. The authors provide a clear empirical analysis of performance across different tasks.

However, a critical weakness is the insufficient discussion of the lambda parameter in the Sinkhorn approximation. The choice of lambda=10 is not justified, and the paper fails to explore how performance varies with different lambda values. Since lambda dramatically affects whether the approximation behaves like the pure Wasserstein distance (large lambda) or the entropic/Sinkhorn distance (small lambda), this omission significantly impacts the reproducibility and reliability of the results. The authors should have conducted ablation studies on lambda's effect on performance.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear mathematical exposition. The algorithm is presented in a logical flow, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The figures (t-SNE visualizations, word weight rankings) effectively support the claims. The writing is professional and accessible to NIPS readers.

The only clarity issue is the confusing reference to Word Centroid Distance (WCD) as a baseline in the main text, when WCD is actually a simpler approximation of WMD rather than a distinct metric learning approach. This could mislead readers about the relationship between the methods.

## Originality
The core contribution of learning a supervised metric for WMD is reasonable, but the novelty is limited. The use of Sinkhorn distance as a computational proxy for Wasserstein is not new (Cuturi [6]), and the authors don't sufficiently distinguish their approach from Wang et al. [35], who also learn metrics within EMD. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with prior work on learning supervised word embeddings and metric learning in text.

The paper doesn't clearly articulate how S-WMD differs from the unsupervised WMD in terms of the underlying mathematical properties, especially regarding the role of the entropic regularizer. The relationship between the supervised metric and the original WMD is not sufficiently clarified.

## Significance
The empirical results are compelling, with S-WMD achieving the best average performance across 8 datasets against 26 baselines. This represents a meaningful advance in document distance metrics. The method's efficiency (training time comparable to baseline metric learning approaches) is particularly valuable for practical applications.

However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the lack of discussion about the lambda parameter's critical impact on results. Without clear guidance on choosing lambda for different tasks, the practical utility of the method is limited. The paper would be significantly more impactful if it provided a principled approach to selecting lambda based on dataset characteristics.

## Recommendation
**Accept**

Pros:
- Strong empirical results across diverse text classification tasks
- Well-written with clear mathematical exposition
- Efficient implementation using Sinkhorn approximation
- Consistently outperforms 26 baselines on most datasets

Cons:
- Limited novelty compared to prior WMD work
- Insufficient discussion of lambda parameter's critical impact on performance
- Confusing references to Word Centroid Distance
- Doesn't adequately distinguish from similar metric learning approaches

While the novelty is somewhat limited, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to document distance metrics with strong empirical validation. The well-written nature of the paper and the consistent performance gains across multiple datasets justify acceptance. The authors should address the lambda parameter discussion in the camera-ready version to strengthen the paper's technical contribution.
