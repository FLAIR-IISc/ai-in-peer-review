This paper proposes a supervised extension to the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), dubbed Supervised-WMD (S-WMD), by learning word-specific weights and utilizing the Sinkhorn distance as a computationally efficient approximation to the Wasserstein distance. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space alongside re-weighted word frequencies, minimizing leave-one-out kNN classification error. The method is evaluated extensively on eight datasets, achieving strong performance compared to 26 baselines.

The paper builds directly on the recent success of WMD (Kusner et al., 2016) in leveraging word embeddings for document similarity. Prior work focused on unsupervised document representations (LSI, LDA, mSDA) or supervised metric learning applied *after* dimensionality reduction. S-WMD distinguishes itself by directly learning a metric *within* the word embedding space, avoiding the representational bottleneck and retaining low dimensionality. The paper also draws from the metric learning literature (NCA, LMNN, ITML) but addresses the computational challenges posed by applying these methods to document distances. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to approximate the WMD is a key contribution, drawing on the work of Cuturi (2013).

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Performance:** The extensive evaluation across eight datasets and comparison to 26 baselines demonstrate the effectiveness of S-WMD. Achieving the best average performance is a significant result.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a clear and in-depth mathematical exposition of the method. The derivation of the gradients and the explanation of the Sinkhorn approximation are well-articulated. The connection to optimal transport theory is properly established.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivations are clearly stated, and the methodology is explained in a logical and accessible manner. The figures and tables are informative and well-presented.
* **Practicality:** The use of the Sinkhorn approximation and the batch gradient descent optimization make the method computationally feasible for real-world applications. The reported training times are reasonable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of supervised learning with WMD is a valuable contribution, the core ideas (supervised metric learning, Sinkhorn approximation) are not entirely novel. The paper largely builds upon existing work rather than introducing fundamentally new concepts.
* **Confusing Reference to WCD:** The discussion of the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) feels tangential and, at times, confusing. The paper highlights that WCD lower bounds WMD, but the connection to the overall method isn’t clearly articulated. Using WCD for initialization is reasonable, but repeatedly referencing it in the discussion detracts from the core contribution.
* **Poor Discussion of Lambda:** The role and choice of the entropic regularizer parameter, lambda (λ), are inadequately addressed. The paper mentions that larger lambda values approximate the Wasserstein distance, while smaller values correspond to the entropic/Sinkhorn distance, but lacks a thorough discussion of the trade-offs.  
* **Lambda’s Dominant Effect:** The paper does not sufficiently emphasize that lambda can have a dominant effect on eq(2) and significantly impacts the method's behavior and results.  The choice of λ dictates whether the method behaves as a true Wasserstein distance approximation or a more computationally efficient, but potentially different, entropic regularized distance. Most users will likely opt for smaller lambda for speed, which alters the fundamental nature of the metric being learned. This needs explicit acknowledgement and potentially sensitivity analysis.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a practically useful and theoretically sound method for learning a supervised document distance metric.
* The extensive empirical evaluation demonstrates the superior performance of S-WMD compared to a wide range of baselines.
* The clear and well-written presentation makes the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The paper’s novelty is somewhat incremental, building heavily on existing work.
* The discussion of lambda is insufficient and potentially misleading.
* The relevance of the WCD discussion is questionable.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a solid technical foundation and compelling experimental results. While the novelty is not groundbreaking, the method is a valuable addition to the toolkit for document similarity analysis. The weaknesses regarding the discussion of lambda and the WCD are addressable and should be rectified before publication. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the role of lambda and streamline the discussion of WCD. The authors should provide more guidance on selecting an appropriate lambda value and discuss the implications of different choices.
