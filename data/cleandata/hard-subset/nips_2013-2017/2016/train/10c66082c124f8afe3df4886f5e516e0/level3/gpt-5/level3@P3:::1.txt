Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for text. WMD computes document distances via an optimal transport (OT) problem over pre-trained word embeddings, and has been shown to be strong for kNN classification. S-WMD injects supervision by learning: (1) a linear projection A applied to the word embedding space (i.e., learning the ground metric), and (2) a global word-importance vector w that induces document-specific reweighting after normalization. The objective is an NCA-style stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss on document distances. Direct differentiation through WMD would entail an impractical nested LP; instead, the paper uses an entropy-regularized OT relaxation (Sinkhorn distance) to obtain efficient, smooth gradients, and further accelerates training via mini-batching and neighbor pruning using a fast lower bound (word centroid distance, WCD). The method is evaluated on eight datasets against 26 baselines, showing competitive or state-of-the-art kNN accuracy. This work builds on WMD (Kusner et al.), Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi), and classic metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML), and relates to prior attempts at learning ground metrics within EMD for images (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is sound, and the use of Sinkhorn scaling for both transport and dual potentials yields tractable gradients. The stochastic kNN loss is standard and appropriate. The initialization with a supervised WCD metric (NCA on word centroids, “S-WCD”) is sensible and empirically effective.
- Empirical support: Results across eight datasets and many baselines are thorough. S-WMD generally improves over WMD and most learned baselines. Training times are reasonable given the OT component.
- Concerns: (i) Based on the presented results, the initialization (S-WCD) performs very close to the full S-WMD on several datasets, which raises questions about how much the learned OT contributes beyond a strong centroid-based metric. (ii) Missing ablations to isolate the contribution of A versus w. (iii) A baseline applying existing metric learners (ITML, LMNN, NCA) directly to word-centroid embeddings is incomplete: S-WCD covers NCA, but LMNN/ITML on centroids are absent.

Clarity
The paper is very well written, organized, and pleasant to read. The motivation, algorithmic choices, and computational tricks are explained clearly. Code availability is a plus. Minor clarifications are listed below.

Originality
The core idea—learning a linear ground metric within WMD plus global word reweighting—is a natural extension of earlier EMD metric-learning work to text. The main novelty is the efficient optimization: using Sinkhorn to get tractable gradients (both primal and dual) and combining this with NCA-style objectives, batching, and neighbor filtering. While interesting and useful, the contribution is incremental in terms of modeling (linear A + global w), with the originality residing primarily in the optimization pathway and its application to WMD.

Significance
The approach is practically useful: it improves strong kNN baselines and makes supervised OT feasible at document scale. However, if S-WCD (or centroids + off-the-shelf metric learning) performs similarly, the incremental impact of the full OT-based learning may be limited. Clarifying when and why S-WMD significantly outperforms centroid-based methods would strengthen the paper’s significance.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated extension of WMD to supervised settings.
- Effective and efficient optimization via Sinkhorn-based gradients and NCA objective.
- Strong empirical results on multiple datasets with many baselines; code availability.
- Simple modeling that leverages pre-trained embeddings; interpretable word weights.

Cons
- S-WMD init (S-WCD) is often close to full S-WMD; added complexity may yield modest gains on several datasets.
- Missing baselines: LMNN and ITML on word-centroid embeddings.
- Missing ablations: effect of A vs w (learn one while fixing the other); analysis of λ and neighbor-pruning hyperparameters.
- Scope limited to linear A and global w; no exploration of nonlinearity or class-specific weights.

Requests for clarification and additional experiments
- Provide an ablation: (a) learn A with w fixed to 1; (b) learn w with A fixed to identity; (c) both. Report accuracy and training times.
- Add baselines: LMNN and ITML on word centroids (to parallel S-WCD with NCA).
- Quantify the incremental gain of S-WMD over S-WCD per dataset, and discuss when OT learning matters most (e.g., short vs long documents, vocabulary size, label granularity).
- Sensitivity analyses: λ in Sinkhorn; number of neighbors M for pruning; impact of initialization quality; different embeddings (e.g., GloVe/fastText).
- Report memory and time scaling with document length q and vocabulary size; include per-iteration wall-clock and convergence plots.

Minor comments and typos
- Please specify which paper “The authors” refers to around line 152.
- In Equation 11, α and β should carry stars (α*, β*) to match the optimal dual potentials referenced earlier.
- Around line 170, explicitly state that the method uses Sinkhorn scaling to obtain T*, u, v and the corresponding dual potentials; make the connection to the earlier Sinkhorn reference explicit.
- Consistency: clarify symbols p and q (power of the norm vs number of unique words) to avoid overload; fix minor typos (e.g., “imporance” → “importance”).
- Briefly discuss whether w is global or class-specific, and whether class-specific variants were tried.

Overall assessment
A solid and well-executed paper that advances supervised document distances by marrying WMD with efficient metric learning and word reweighting. The work is likely to be useful to practitioners who rely on kNN with document distances. To maximize impact, the authors should add the missing centroid+metric baselines and ablations to pinpoint where OT-based learning delivers clear, reproducible gains over simpler, faster alternatives. My recommendation is borderline accept, contingent on addressing the missing comparisons and ablations.
