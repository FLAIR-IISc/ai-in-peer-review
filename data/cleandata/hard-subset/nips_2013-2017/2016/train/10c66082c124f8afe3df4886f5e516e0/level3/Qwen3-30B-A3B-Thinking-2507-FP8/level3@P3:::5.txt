

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance" (NIPS 2016)

This paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by learning word-specific importance weights and using the Sinkhorn distance as a computational approximation. The authors aim to improve document classification performance by incorporating label information into the WMD framework, which was previously unsupervised. The approach builds upon the foundation of WMD (Kusner et al., 2015), which computes document distances as the optimal transport cost between word embeddings, and extends it with a metric learning procedure that minimizes the leave-one-out classification error.

The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field by addressing an important limitation of the unsupervised WMD - its inability to incorporate task-specific supervision. The authors develop a method to learn a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights, using the Sinkhorn approximation to make the optimization tractable. The method is evaluated on 8 standard text classification datasets against 26 baselines, with the authors reporting superior average performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clarity and Mathematical Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear, in-depth mathematical exposition. The authors provide thorough derivations of the gradient updates and the Sinkhorn approximation, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with optimal transport and metric learning.

2. **Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive, covering 8 diverse text classification datasets and 26 competitive baselines. The results demonstrate consistent improvement over unsupervised baselines and other metric learning approaches, with the proposed method achieving the best average performance across datasets.

3. **Practical Implementation:** The authors provide clear implementation details, including an efficient batch gradient descent procedure with a clever initialization using the Word Centroid Distance. The training time analysis shows the method is practical for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of extending WMD with supervision is not significantly novel compared to prior work. The paper builds directly on Kusner et al.'s WMD framework, and the supervised extension resembles standard metric learning approaches (like NCA) applied to a novel distance metric. The authors do not clearly articulate how their method differs from applying standard metric learning directly to the WMD.

2. **Poor Discussion of Lambda:** The role and choice of the entropic regularizer λ are poorly discussed. The paper states λ = 10 is used as "a nice trade-off between speed and approximation accuracy" without justification. However, λ critically impacts the behavior of the method: small λ yields the Sinkhorn approximation (which is not the true Wasserstein distance), while large λ approaches the true Wasserstein distance. The authors do not clarify which regime their method operates in or how this choice affects results. This is a significant omission since most users would choose small λ for speed, which substantially changes the behavior from the intended Wasserstein distance.

3. **Confusing References to Word Centroid Distance:** The paper repeatedly references the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) as a baseline and initialization method, but the relationship to WCD is confusing. The WCD is a lower bound of WMD (as noted in Kusner et al.), but the authors' use of WCD for initialization and comparison is not well-justified and creates confusion about the relationship between their method and prior work.

4. **Incomplete Evaluation of the Method's Behavior:** The paper does not clearly establish whether S-WMD behaves like pure Wasserstein (with large λ) or like Sinkhorn (with small λ). Given that the Sinkhorn approximation is used for computational efficiency, the method is effectively a variant of the Sinkhorn distance, not the true Wasserstein distance. This distinction is important for understanding the method's properties and limitations.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported experimental results. However, the lack of discussion about the critical role of λ weakens the theoretical foundation. The experimental results are robust but do not sufficiently address how the method's behavior changes with different λ values.

**Originality:** The novelty is limited. The core contribution is applying standard metric learning techniques to the WMD framework, which is a natural extension but not a significant conceptual advance. The paper does not clearly differentiate itself from other metric learning approaches applied to text.

**Significance:** The method provides a practical improvement to document classification, and the results are impressive across multiple datasets. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the limited novelty and the fact that the approach is essentially a standard metric learning technique applied to a new distance metric.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a well-executed and practical extension to the WMD framework with strong empirical results. The writing is excellent, and the technical content is clearly presented. However, the limited novelty and poor discussion of the critical λ parameter significantly impact the paper's scientific contribution. The method is essentially a standard metric learning approach applied to the WMD, with the choice of λ being a major unaddressed issue that affects the interpretation of results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Strong empirical results across 8 datasets with 26 baselines
- Excellent writing and mathematical exposition
- Practical implementation details and training time analysis
- Clear demonstration of the value of incorporating supervision into the WMD framework

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited novelty compared to prior work (WMD and standard metric learning)
- Critical omission in discussing the role and choice of the entropic regularizer λ
- Confusing references to Word Centroid Distance
- Failure to clarify whether the method behaves like pure Wasserstein or Sinkhorn approximation

Given the conference's emphasis on novelty and scientific contribution, the paper's limited novelty and significant omission regarding λ make it difficult to recommend acceptance. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before the paper can be considered for publication at NIPS.
