This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach based on the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD). The authors aim to improve the WMD by incorporating supervision into the metric learning process. The proposed method learns a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector to capture the semantic meaning of similarity encoded in the labels.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear introduction to the problem and the proposed solution, as well as a thorough review of related work. The technical details of the method are well-explained, and the experimental results are comprehensive and convincing.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to leverage the strengths of the WMD while addressing its limitations. The WMD has been shown to be effective in capturing semantic relationships between words, but it is an unsupervised method that does not take into account the specific task or labels. The proposed S-WMD method addresses this limitation by incorporating supervision into the metric learning process.

The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms a wide range of competitive baselines, including unsupervised WMD, WCD, and several document representation methods with and without supervised metric learning. The results are consistent across multiple datasets and tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness and robustness of the proposed method.

One of the concerns is that the impact of the proposed method may be limited, as the results in Table 3 show that EMD with metric learning initialized via a "word centroid distance" performs similarly to the full proposed learning. This suggests that the contribution of the proposed method may be incremental, rather than revolutionary.

Another concern is the lack of evaluation of word-centroid document embeddings with existing metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA). This would provide a more comprehensive comparison of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art approaches.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the contributions of the word-specific weights versus the projection matrix A. The authors mention that the word-specific weights are important for distinguishing between classes, but a more thorough analysis of their impact on the results would be helpful.

In terms of originality, the main novelty of the paper lies in the optimization approach (lines 156-175), which is an interesting and useful extension of the WMD. However, the contribution is somewhat limited, as the authors build on existing work on metric learning and optimal transport.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed method is effective and robust. However, the concerns mentioned above suggest that the impact of the paper may be limited, and further analysis and evaluation are needed to fully understand the contributions of the proposed method.
