

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes an effective extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by incorporating supervised learning to improve document classification performance. The authors build upon Kusner et al.'s unsupervised WMD (ICML 2015), which computes document distances as optimal transport between word embeddings, and introduce Supervised WMD (S-WMD), a discriminative metric learning approach that jointly optimizes a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights. The key innovation lies in efficiently approximating the gradient of the WMD distance to enable supervised training, making the method computationally feasible.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results across eight diverse text classification datasets, outperforming 26 baselines including standard document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA), unsupervised metrics (WCD, WMD), and supervised metric learning methods (NCA, ITML, LMNN applied to various representations). The authors provide a thorough evaluation including ablation studies (showing the value of both the linear transformation and word weighting), visualization of learned word weights, and detailed runtime analysis.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with a clear and well-justified approach. The authors properly address the computational challenges of learning a supervised metric by leveraging the Sinkhorn distance approximation (Cuturi, 2013) and provide an efficient gradient approximation that makes the method practical. The experimental design is comprehensive, with multiple datasets, careful hyperparameter tuning, and statistical significance testing. The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations (e.g., S-WMD's performance may depend on the quality of the underlying word embeddings and the initialization). The method is complete and not merely a position paper, with clear theoretical foundations and empirical validation.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background section provides sufficient context for both the WMD and metric learning communities, and the method is explained with appropriate mathematical detail. The pseudo-code for the S-WMD algorithm is clear and helpful. The presentation of experimental results is thorough, with detailed tables, visualizations (t-SNE embeddings), and analysis of the learned word weights. The paper would be easily reproducible by experts.

Originality: The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by applying supervised learning to the WMD framework, which was previously unsupervised. While metric learning has been extensively studied, the authors effectively adapt these techniques to the WMD setting in a novel way. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from related work (e.g., Wang et al.'s EMD metric learning, which doesn't include histogram reweighting) and provides appropriate citations. The work is significantly different from previous NIPS papers on WMD (Kusner et al., 2015) and metric learning.

Significance: The results are highly significant, with S-WMD achieving state-of-the-art performance across multiple text classification tasks. The method provides a practical way to incorporate domain-specific supervision into the WMD framework, which was previously not possible. The improvements over unsupervised WMD are substantial (e.g., 51% relative error reduction on OHSUMED), and the method is efficient enough to be practical for large-scale text classification. The paper demonstrates that even modest supervision can significantly improve document distance metrics, which has broad implications for text retrieval and classification tasks.

Strengths: 
- The method is sound and well-motivated
- The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing
- The approach is practical with reasonable computational requirements
- The paper clearly explains how the method differs from and improves upon prior work
- The visualization of learned word weights provides valuable insight into the model's behavior

Weaknesses: 
- The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the impact of different pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., GloVe vs. word2vec) on S-WMD performance
- The method's performance on extremely short documents (e.g., tweets) isn't explicitly analyzed
- The paper doesn't discuss how S-WMD might integrate with modern deep learning approaches to text classification

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated across multiple datasets. The authors have provided a valuable extension to the WMD framework that enables supervised learning for document distances, with demonstrable improvements over a wide range of baselines.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Significant extension of the WMD framework with clear empirical validation
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets and baselines
- Practical and efficient algorithm with reasonable computational requirements
- Clear presentation and well-justified technical approach
- Demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on text classification tasks

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited exploration of the impact of different word embedding models
- No discussion of potential failure cases or limitations beyond what's mentioned in the paper

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning and metric learning. The proposed S-WMD method is a significant improvement over unsupervised WMD and outperforms all competing baselines across multiple text classification tasks. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides a practical solution to a meaningful problem in text analysis. I recommend acceptance to NIPS 2016.
