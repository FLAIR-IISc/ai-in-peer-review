This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach, called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), which extends the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by learning a linear projection and document-specific word re-weighting. The approach is evaluated on eight real-world text classification tasks, where it consistently outperforms 26 competitive baselines.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed approach, including the optimization method and the initialization technique. The experimental results are also well-presented, with a detailed comparison of S-WMD with other methods.

The main contribution of the paper is the proposal of a supervised metric learning approach that can effectively learn a document distance metric from labeled data. The approach is based on the WMD, which is a powerful document distance metric that can capture the semantic meaning of words. By learning a linear projection and document-specific word re-weighting, S-WMD can adapt the WMD to different classification tasks and datasets.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of S-WMD in improving the performance of kNN-based document classification. The approach outperforms all other methods on five out of eight datasets, and achieves the lowest kNN test error on average across all datasets.

One of the strengths of the paper is the thorough evaluation of the proposed approach. The authors compare S-WMD with a wide range of competitive baselines, including unsupervised and supervised methods. They also provide a detailed analysis of the results, including a comparison of the performance of S-WMD with other methods on different datasets.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the concerns is the limited impact of the proposed approach, as the results in Table 3 show that EMD with metric learning initialized via a "word centroid distance" performs similarly to the full proposed learning. This suggests that the contribution of the paper may be limited to the optimization approach, which is a somewhat minor contribution.

Additionally, the paper lacks a comparison with other supervised metric learning methods, such as ITML, LMNN, and NCA, which are commonly used in the literature. This makes it difficult to assess the novelty and impact of the proposed approach.

Furthermore, the paper does not provide an isolated assessment of the contributions of word-specific weights versus the projection matrix A. This makes it difficult to understand the relative importance of each component of the proposed approach.

In terms of originality, the main novelty of the paper lies in the optimization approach, which is an arbitrarily close approximation of the original WMD distance. However, this contribution is somewhat limited, and the paper does not provide a significant new insight or perspective on the problem of document distance metric learning.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach. However, the limited impact and novelty of the contribution, as well as the lack of comparison with other supervised metric learning methods, are concerns that need to be addressed.
