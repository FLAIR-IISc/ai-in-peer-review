The paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised metric based on the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD leverages word embeddings to define a distance between documents as an optimal transport problem. While WMD has shown promise in kNN-based document classification, it’s entirely unsupervised. This work addresses this limitation by learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights to minimize leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. A key contribution is an efficient optimization technique exploiting a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) to enable practical updates.  The authors demonstrate consistent improvements over 26 competitive baselines across eight real-world text classification tasks.

This paper builds upon a significant body of work in document distance metrics. Early approaches focused on sparse representations like bag-of-words and TF-IDF, which often struggle with semantic similarity due to orthogonality issues. Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) and Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) were introduced to address this, learning lower-dimensional representations. More recently, WMD capitalized on the power of pre-trained word embeddings to provide a compelling document distance measure. The authors correctly position their work within the context of metric learning, acknowledging that existing methods often scale poorly with dimensionality and require dimensionality reduction.  The connection to the Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) and the related work on efficient approximation via Sinkhorn distances is also well established.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The idea of incorporating supervision into WMD is natural and valuable. The proposed approach is technically sound, and the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the underlying optimization challenges. The empirical evaluation is extensive, comparing S-WMD to a large number of baselines across diverse datasets. The results convincingly show that S-WMD consistently outperforms many state-of-the-art methods. The writing is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making the paper a pleasure to read.  The code release is a definite plus, facilitating reproducibility and further research.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The impact of the proposed method, while positive, feels somewhat limited. The results in Table 3 reveal that the initialization using Supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) achieves performance comparable to the full S-WMD learning process. This suggests that the supervised learning component might not be adding substantial value beyond a good initialization. A crucial missing baseline is the evaluation of word-centroid document embeddings *with* existing metric learning algorithms like ITML, LMNN, or NCA. This would provide a clearer comparison of the benefit gained from jointly learning the word transformation and re-weighting within the WMD framework, versus applying metric learning to a simpler word-centroid representation.  

Furthermore, an ablation study is needed to disentangle the contributions of the word-specific weights and the linear projection matrix A.  Is the performance gain primarily driven by the re-weighting of words, the transformation of the embedding space, or a combination of both? Knowing this would provide deeper insights into the mechanism driving the improvement. While the originality of the overall approach is good, the main novelty lies in the optimization technique presented in lines 156–175. While clever, this is a relatively incremental contribution given the existing literature on approximating optimal transport.

Finally, a few minor clarifications are necessary. The phrase "The authors" on line 152 is ambiguous and should be more specific. Equation 11 appears to be missing stars on the α and β terms, which is standard notation. Also, around line 170, it would be helpful to explicitly mention that the method leverages the Sinkhorn scaling described earlier in the paper, reinforcing the connection between the theoretical framework and the implementation details.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results across multiple datasets.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem (incorporating supervision into WMD).
*   Technically sound and novel optimization approach.
*   Code release promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited incremental improvement beyond a strong initialization (S-WCD).
*   Missing baseline comparisons with metric learning applied to word-centroid embeddings.
*   Lack of an ablation study to isolate the contributions of individual components.
*   Optimization technique, while clever, may not be groundbreaking.



Overall, this is a solid paper with valuable contributions. While the impact might be somewhat limited by the strong performance of the initialization and the lack of certain baseline comparisons, the technical soundness, extensive evaluation, and clear writing make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS proceedings. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns regarding baseline comparisons and ablation studies in a revised version.
