This paper proposes a supervised extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) metric, which has shown state-of-the-art results in document classification tasks. The authors introduce a linear transformation A and word importance weights w to learn a supervised metric, dubbed Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD). The method is evaluated on eight real-world text classification tasks, where it consistently outperforms 26 competitive baselines.

The main contribution of this paper is the extension of WMD to supervised metric learning, which allows the method to incorporate label information and improve its performance on document classification tasks. The authors provide a clear and well-structured explanation of their method, including the mathematical formulation and the optimization procedure.

One of the strengths of this paper is its thorough evaluation of the proposed method. The authors compare S-WMD to a wide range of baselines, including unsupervised WMD, Word Centroid Distance (WCD), and six document representation methods with and without supervised metric learning. The results show that S-WMD achieves the lowest kNN test error on five out of eight datasets and outperforms all other methods on average across all datasets.

The paper also provides some insights into the behavior of the proposed method. For example, the authors note that S-WMD is particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words. This is because the word embedding gives rise to a very good document distance, which is able to capture the semantic meaning of words.

However, there are some aspects of the paper that could be improved. For example, the reviewer requests clearer intuition behind Eq (5), which defines the usage of the word importance weights w. The authors could provide more explanation on how w is used to re-weight the word histogram values and how it affects the document distance.

Additionally, the reviewer asks for justification of the claim that S-WMD is particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words. The authors could provide more evidence or analysis to support this claim.

The reviewer also notes some minor issues, such as the lack of description of how k is chosen for k-NN classification and the missing explanation of the columns "n" vs "ne" in Table 2.

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of document classification and metric learning. The proposed method, S-WMD, shows promising results and has the potential to be widely adopted in various applications.
