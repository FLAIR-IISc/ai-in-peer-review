## Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns word-specific weights and utilizes the Sinkhorn distance as a faster approximation to the Wasserstein distance. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space alongside word importance weights, minimizing leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. The authors demonstrate strong performance on eight datasets, achieving the best average performance compared to 26 baselines.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical foundations of WMD, the Sinkhorn approximation, and the proposed supervised learning framework. The motivation for the work is well-articulated, building upon the success of WMD and addressing its limitation of being entirely unsupervised. The inclusion of a fast gradient computation technique leveraging the Sinkhorn distance is a practical contribution, making the method scalable. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with a comprehensive comparison against a large number of baselines. 

However, while technically sound and well-executed, the novelty of the work feels incremental. WMD itself was a significant contribution, but S-WMD feels more like a natural and expected extension. The core idea of learning weights to adapt WMD to specific tasks isn’t entirely new in the broader metric learning landscape. Furthermore, the paper’s repeated references to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) feel misplaced and occasionally confusing; its role seems primarily to serve as an initialization strategy, but it's given undue prominence in the discussion. 

A significant weakness lies in the discussion of the entropic regularizer parameter, lambda. The authors acknowledge its importance (Eq. 2) but provide insufficient guidance on its selection. The behavior of the method drastically changes with lambda – large values approximate Wasserstein distance, while small values lean towards entropic/Sinkhorn – and the paper doesn’t clearly articulate the implications of this choice or provide a rationale for their chosen value. It’s unclear whether the observed performance is due to the learned weights or simply the effect of a specific lambda setting.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical performance, consistently outperforming numerous baselines.
*   Clear and well-written exposition of complex mathematical concepts.
*   Practical contribution of using Sinkhorn distance for efficient computation.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty compared to the original WMD work.
*   Confusing and unnecessary emphasis on Word Centroid Distance.
*   Insufficient discussion and justification for the choice of lambda.



**Overall:** This is a solid paper with a clear contribution, but its incremental nature and the lack of detailed discussion around the lambda parameter prevent it from being a truly outstanding submission. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the concerns regarding lambda and streamlining the discussion of WCD.
