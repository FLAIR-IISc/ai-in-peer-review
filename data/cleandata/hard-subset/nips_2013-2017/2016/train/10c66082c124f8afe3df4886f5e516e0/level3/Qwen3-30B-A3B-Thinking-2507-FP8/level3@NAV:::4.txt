

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision through a linear transform A and word importance weights w. The authors learn these parameters to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out classification error, using an efficient Sinkhorn approximation to address computational challenges. The work builds on Kusner et al.'s WMD and NCA metric learning, applying them to document distances.

The paper excels in clarity and organization, with a thorough literature review placing the work in context of prior metric learning approaches and WMD. The extensive experimental evaluation across eight novel datasets demonstrates consistent improvements over 26 baselines, showing strong empirical results that validate the approach's effectiveness.

However, several significant concerns limit the paper's contribution. The motivation for reweighting word distributions (w) is weak and poorly explained; as noted, this operation could fundamentally alter document semantics by changing the underlying word distribution. The paper fails to justify how reweighting improves label separation without distorting document meaning. The core contribution appears limited—applying linear transform and reweighting with NCA objective is not sufficiently novel compared to existing metric learning work. Additionally, the practical concern regarding how to compute w for unseen documents remains unaddressed, which is a critical limitation for real-world deployment.

The terminology issue with "semantic difference" in the abstract is particularly problematic, as it ambiguously conflates label/topic similarity with true semantic meaning. This undermines the paper's claims about preserving semantic content.

While the technical execution is sound and experiments well-designed, the limited novelty and unresolved conceptual issues prevent this from being a significant advancement. The paper would benefit from addressing these concerns thoroughly before acceptance. With proper clarification of the reweighting mechanism, justification of semantic preservation, and discussion of unseen document handling, this could be a strong contribution. As it stands, the current version does not meet the threshold for significant novelty expected at NIPS.
