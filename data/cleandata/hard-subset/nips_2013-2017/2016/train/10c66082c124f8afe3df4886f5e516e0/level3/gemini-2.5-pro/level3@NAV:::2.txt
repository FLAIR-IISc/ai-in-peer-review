This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. This work is a direct and well-motivated extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) introduced by Kusner et al. at ICML 2015. While WMD leverages unsupervised word embeddings to create a powerful document distance, this paper incorporates supervision by learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space and a set of word-importance weights. The authors formulate this as a metric learning problem, adapting the stochastic leave-one-out error minimization from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) to the WMD framework.

The paper has many significant strengths.
**Pros:**
*   **Quality & Clarity:** The proposed method is technically sound and very clearly explained. The authors identify the key computational challenge—differentiating through the WMD optimization—and present a clever and practical solution by using an entropy-regularized version of the transport problem. The methodology is presented with appropriate detail for reproducibility.
*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant. The experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough, covering eight diverse classification datasets and comparing against 26 strong baselines. S-WMD is shown to outperform these baselines by a large margin on most tasks, clearly advancing the state-of-the-art for kNN-based text classification.
*   **Originality:** The application of discriminative training to learn a domain-specific metric on top of WMD is a novel and important contribution. While metric learning for the Earth Mover's Distance ground metric has been explored in computer vision, its application in this context, combined with learning word weights, is a valuable and original extension for the NLP community.

**Cons:**
*   The paper is very strong, and weaknesses are minor. The authors note that S-WMD is not the top performer on every single dataset. A slightly more detailed analysis of these specific cases could provide deeper insight into the model's limitations or scenarios where the learned metric might not be optimal. This, however, is a small suggestion for improvement rather than a significant flaw.

Overall, this is a solid and complete piece of work that addresses a relevant problem with an effective and well-evaluated solution. The paper is well-written, the experiments are compelling, and the contribution is a clear step forward. I consider it a high-quality paper well-suited for presentation at NIPS.
