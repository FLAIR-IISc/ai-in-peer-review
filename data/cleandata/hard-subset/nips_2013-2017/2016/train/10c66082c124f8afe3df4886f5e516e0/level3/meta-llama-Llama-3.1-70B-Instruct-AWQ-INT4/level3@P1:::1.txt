The paper "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance" proposes a novel extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by incorporating supervision into the metric learning process. The authors introduce a technique to learn a supervised metric, called Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), which leverages an affine transformation of the underlying word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector. The proposed method is evaluated on eight real-world text classification tasks and consistently outperforms 26 competitive baselines.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the background and related work. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of the optimization approach. The experimental results are impressive, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the performance of S-WMD on various datasets.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to effectively leverage the strengths of WMD while addressing its limitations. The authors provide a clear explanation of how S-WMD improves upon WMD, particularly in terms of its ability to incorporate supervision into the metric learning process. The use of an affine transformation of the underlying word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector is a novel contribution that enables S-WMD to outperform WMD on various tasks.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One concern is that the impact of the proposed method may be limited, as the authors note that EMD with metric learning initialized via a "word centroid distance" performs similarly to the full proposed learning (see Table 3). Additionally, the authors do not provide an evaluation of word-centroid document embeddings with existing metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA), which would have been a useful comparison. Furthermore, the authors do not provide an isolated assessment of the contributions of word-specific weights versus the projection matrix A, which would have helped to better understand the role of each component in the proposed method.

In terms of originality, the main novelty of the paper lies in the optimization approach (lines 156–175). While the idea of linear metric learning within EMD is interesting, it is a somewhat limited contribution. The authors could have provided more context on how their work relates to existing research in metric learning and optimal transport.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed method is a useful extension of WMD. However, the authors could have addressed some of the limitations mentioned above to strengthen the paper.
