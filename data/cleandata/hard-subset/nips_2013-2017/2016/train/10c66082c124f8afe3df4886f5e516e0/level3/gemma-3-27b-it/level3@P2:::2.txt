This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised metric based on the recently introduced Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD leverages word embeddings to define a distance between documents as an optimal transport problem. The authors address the limitation of WMD being entirely unsupervised by learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space and word-importance weights, minimizing leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. A key contribution lies in an efficient approximation to the gradient computation, enabling practical training. The paper presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation across eight datasets and against 26 baselines, demonstrating consistently strong performance.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in document distance metrics, tracing a lineage from bag-of-words and TF-IDF representations [30] through dimensionality reduction techniques like LSI [11] and LDA [2], to the more recent WMD [19]. The paper correctly situates itself within the broader context of metric learning [10, 15, 36, 37], highlighting the challenges of scaling these methods to high-dimensional text data. Importantly, the authors acknowledge and build upon the work of Cuturi [6] on efficient computation of optimal transport, which is crucial for the practicality of their approach. Related work in supervised word embeddings [32] is also appropriately discussed.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically well-motivated and sound. The derivation of the gradient approximation, leveraging the Sinkhorn distance [6], appears justified and necessary for efficient training. The combination of learning a transformation of the word embedding space *and* re-weighting word frequencies is a novel and promising approach.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are thorough and accessible. The authors effectively convey the core ideas of the paper without sacrificing technical detail.
* **Originality:** While building on existing work, the S-WMD metric represents a novel combination of techniques. Applying discriminative training to refine the WMD distance, and the specific approach to gradient computation, are significant contributions.
* **Significance:** The extensive experimental results, consistently outperforming a large number of strong baselines, demonstrate the significance of this work. The improvement over WMD, particularly on datasets where WMD already achieves state-of-the-art results, is noteworthy. The efficiency of the training process, as highlighted in Table 3, further enhances the practical impact of the method. The visualization in Figure 1 provides compelling evidence of the improved clustering achieved by S-WMD.

**Weaknesses:**

* The paper does not extensively discuss the sensitivity of the method to the choice of hyperparameters (learning rate, regularization, batch size, etc.). While Bayesian optimization is mentioned, a more detailed analysis of the impact of these parameters would be beneficial.
* The discussion of why S-WMD underperforms on the CLASSIC dataset feels somewhat incomplete. Further investigation into this specific case could provide valuable insights.
* While the authors mention the squared Euclidean distance in the transport problem, it might be insightful to briefly discuss the impact of using other distance metrics (e.g., p=1).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem (supervised document distance learning) with a novel and technically sound approach.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to understand and appreciate the contributions.
* The method is practical and efficient, as demonstrated by the reported training times.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* While the analysis is thorough, a more in-depth investigation of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the paper.
* The underperformance on the CLASSIC dataset warrants further scrutiny.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of document distance learning. The S-WMD metric is a novel and effective approach, and the experimental results are compelling. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. I believe this paper is well-suited for presentation at NIPS and recommend acceptance. I was particularly pleased to see someone apply discriminative training to learn a domain-specific metric from WMD – it’s a natural and valuable extension of the original work.
