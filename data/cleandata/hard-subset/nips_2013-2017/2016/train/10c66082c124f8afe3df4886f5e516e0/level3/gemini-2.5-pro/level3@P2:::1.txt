This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD). WMD is a powerful, unsupervised distance metric for documents that computes the minimum cost to "transport" words from one document to another in a pre-trained word embedding space. The key limitation of WMD is that it cannot leverage supervised information. This paper addresses this gap by introducing a method to learn a task-specific document metric. S-WMD learns two components: a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a vector of word-importance weights. The learning objective is to minimize a differentiable proxy for the leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor (kNN) classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA). A significant technical challenge is that differentiating through the WMD objective is computationally prohibitive. The authors overcome this by replacing the exact optimal transport problem with its entropy-regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance), which has an efficient and differentiable solution. The proposed method is evaluated on kNN classification across eight text datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance against a large suite of 26 baselines.

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound. The main contribution—devising an efficient, differentiable approximation to the WMD for the purpose of metric learning—is well-motivated and appears correct. The use of the Sinkhorn distance relaxation is a clever way to make an otherwise intractable optimization practical. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using multiple datasets and a wide range of competitive baselines, and the results strongly support the claim that S-WMD is an effective document distance metric.

However, the evaluation is missing some key components that would be necessary to fully assess the paper's contributions:
1.  **Missing Ablation Study:** The model learns both a linear transformation `A` and a word-weighting vector `w`. The paper does not provide an ablation study to analyze the independent contributions of these two components. It is unclear whether the performance gains come primarily from transforming the space, re-weighting the words, or if both are essential. This analysis would provide deeper insight into why the method works.
2.  **Marginal Improvement over Initialization:** The initialization method ("S-WMD init."), which is the Supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD), is a very strong baseline in its own right. As shown in Table 2, the final S-WMD model offers consistent but sometimes modest improvements over this initialization (e.g., 33.15% to 32.13% on OHSUMED). This raises a question about the practical significance of the full, more complex S-WMD optimization compared to the much simpler S-WCD method. A more detailed discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the paper.

**Clarity**
The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the background is explained succinctly, and the proposed method is described in a logical flow. The figures, particularly the t-SNE visualizations, are helpful for understanding the impact of the learned metric.

A few minor points could further improve clarity:
*   In Section 2, line 90, "The authors also introduce..." is slightly ambiguous. It would be clearer to specify "Kusner et al. [19] also introduce...".
*   In Section 3.1, the discussion of the dual variables for the gradient w.r.t `w` could be slightly clearer. While Equation 10 correctly uses `α*` and `β*`, ensuring this notation for optimality is used consistently would be helpful.
*   Around line 170, when introducing the solution for the dual variables using `u` and `v`, it might be beneficial to explicitly state that the matrix-scaling algorithm used to find them is the Sinkhorn algorithm, reinforcing the link to the background section.

**Originality**
The idea of learning a ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) is not entirely new (e.g., Wang et al. [35]). However, this paper's contribution is distinct and novel in several ways. First, it applies this idea to the text domain via WMD, which is a significant application area. Second, it introduces the learning of word-importance weights (`w`), which is a novel component not present in prior work on learning EMD metrics. Most importantly, the optimization approach is novel. The combination of an NCA-style loss with an efficient gradient computation based on the Sinkhorn distance approximation is a new and valuable contribution that makes the overall approach practical.

**Significance**
This work is significant as it addresses a key shortcoming of the influential WMD metric, making it applicable to supervised classification tasks. The demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on several benchmarks suggests that S-WMD could become a widely used tool for practitioners and a strong baseline for researchers working on document classification and retrieval. The efficient optimization technique may also be of independent interest to researchers working on metric learning for other structured objects where distances can be framed as optimal transport problems.

However, the significance is somewhat tempered by a missing baseline comparison. The paper's initialization method, S-WCD, is equivalent to applying NCA to word-centroid document representations. A crucial missing baseline is the application of other standard metric learning algorithms, such as LMNN and ITML, to these same word-centroid representations. Without this comparison, it is difficult to conclude whether the full S-WMD framework is truly superior to the simpler two-stage approach of first creating document embeddings (via word centroids) and then applying off-the-shelf metric learning techniques. If the simpler approach performs comparably, the significance of the more complex end-to-end learning approach would be reduced.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, well-motivated, and elegant method for supervising the powerful Word Mover's Distance.
*   The technical contribution of using the Sinkhorn distance for efficient gradient computation is clever and makes the method practical.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on kNN classification across a wide range of datasets and baselines.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The lack of an ablation study makes it difficult to understand the source of the model's performance gains (projection `A` vs. weights `w`).
*   A critical set of baselines is missing: applying standard metric learning methods (LMNN, ITML) to the word centroid document representations. This comparison is essential for judging the contribution of the full S-WMD optimization.
*   The performance improvement over the simpler initialization scheme (S-WCD) is marginal on some datasets, questioning the cost-benefit of the full optimization.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a novel and effective method. While the core ideas are strong and the results are impressive, the missing baselines and ablation study represent a significant gap in the evaluation. I would be inclined to accept the paper if these issues were addressed.
