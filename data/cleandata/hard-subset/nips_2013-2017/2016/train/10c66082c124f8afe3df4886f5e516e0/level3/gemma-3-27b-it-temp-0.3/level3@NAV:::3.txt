## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

This paper proposes a method, Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), to incorporate supervision into the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) framework. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights to minimize k-nearest neighbor classification error. The authors address the computational challenges of optimizing the parameterized WMD by leveraging a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and a probabilistic filtering approach. 

The work builds upon recent advances in optimal transport and metric learning, specifically Kusner et al.’s WMD and Cuturi’s entropy-regularized optimal transport. It distinguishes itself by learning both a transformation of the embedding space *and* word-specific weights, offering a more flexible approach than simply learning a global metric. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results on eight text classification datasets, consistently outperforming a comprehensive set of 26 baselines. This strong empirical validation is a significant strength of the paper.

The method is conceptually straightforward and well-motivated. The proposed gradient approximation is a key contribution, enabling efficient optimization. The experimental results are compelling and thoroughly presented, showcasing the effectiveness of S-WMD across diverse datasets. The inclusion of a detailed comparison against numerous baselines strengthens the claims. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the interplay between the regularized and unregularized OT formulations. While the authors utilize the regularized version for efficiency, a clearer explanation of the trade-offs and potential impact on accuracy would be valuable. The mathematical derivations, while present, could be more rigorous and benefit from clearer notation. An algorithmic box summarizing the S-WMD procedure would also enhance clarity. 

Specifically, Eq. 1 uses Euclidean distance while Eq. 6 uses squared Euclidean distance (effectively a 2-Wasserstein distance). Describing WMD as a p-Wasserstein distance would avoid this confusion. Furthermore, the reweighting scheme in Eq. 5 is not novel and should cite prior work by Guy Lebanon and Le/Cuturi. Finally, the paper would be strengthened by providing insights into the empirical importance and relevance of the learned weight vector *w* – what patterns emerge, and how do these weights contribute to improved performance? The authors should also more clearly distinguish their work from Cuturi/Avis (focusing on entropy regularization, continuous vs. discrete cost, and linear parameterization) and discuss the limitations of Wang et al.’s approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results across diverse datasets.
*   Efficient and practical optimization approach.
*   Clear and well-motivated idea.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed discussion regarding the regularization trade-offs.
*   Mathematical derivations could be more rigorous.
*   Missing citations to related work on reweighting schemes.



Overall, this is a solid contribution that addresses an important problem with a practical and effective solution. I support acceptance, contingent upon addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above.
