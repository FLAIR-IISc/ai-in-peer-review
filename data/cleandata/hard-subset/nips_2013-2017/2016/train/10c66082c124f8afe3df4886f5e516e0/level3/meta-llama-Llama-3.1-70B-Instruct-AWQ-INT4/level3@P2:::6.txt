The paper proposes an extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) to supervised metric learning for documents, which is a novel and interesting contribution to the field. The authors introduce a linear transformation A and word importance weights w to improve the WMD distance between documents. The proposed method, called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), is evaluated on eight real-world text classification tasks and outperforms 26 competitive baselines.

The authors provide a clear and thorough explanation of the background and related work, including the Word Mover's Distance and linear metric learning. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise. The authors also provide a detailed description of the algorithm and the optimization process, which is helpful for understanding the method.

One of the strengths of the paper is its evaluation on a large number of datasets and baselines. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of S-WMD with other methods, including unsupervised WMD, WCD, and various supervised metric learning algorithms. The results show that S-WMD consistently outperforms other methods on most datasets, which demonstrates its effectiveness.

However, there are a few areas that require clarification or improvement. The reviewer requests clearer intuition behind Eq (5) and the definition/usage of w. Specifically, it would be helpful to understand which component (A vs w) drives the reported improvements. The authors should provide more insight into the role of w and how it contributes to the overall performance of S-WMD.

Additionally, the reviewer notes that the evaluation is focused on classification tasks, and it would be interesting to compare S-WMD with simple classifiers on the same datasets. However, the authors clarify that this is not necessary, and the focus on metric learning is sufficient.

The reviewer also points out some minor issues, such as the confusion about when Eq (2) is used and whether the lambda there is the same as the lambda referenced at ln170. The authors should clarify this point to avoid confusion.

Furthermore, the reviewer asks for justification of the claim that S-WMD is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words." The authors should provide more evidence or explanation to support this claim.

Finally, the reviewer seeks clarification on Table 2, specifically the columns "n" vs "ne," and requests more information on how k (for k-NN classification) is chosen. The authors should provide a clear explanation of these points to improve the clarity of the paper.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel extension of WMD to supervised metric learning, which is a significant contribution to the field. The method is also evaluated on a large number of datasets and baselines, which demonstrates its effectiveness.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in natural language processing, namely, document classification. The proposed method has the potential to improve the state-of-the-art in document classification tasks, which is a significant contribution to the field.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and thorough explanation of the method and the evaluation. However, there are a few areas that require clarification or improvement, as mentioned above.

Overall, the paper is a strong contribution to the field of natural language processing, and with some minor revisions, it has the potential to be a top-tier paper.
