This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The work extends the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), which frames document similarity as an optimal transport problem between word embeddings. The key innovation is to introduce supervision by jointly learning a linear transformation `A` on the word embedding space and a word-importance vector `w`. This approach connects the fields of document representation and supervised metric learning, building on principles from methods like Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). To make the nested optimization problem tractable, the authors use an efficient, differentiable approximation based on the regularized Sinkhorn distance.

The paper has several significant strengths. The primary contribution is a well-motivated and logical extension of a powerful unsupervised metric to the supervised domain. The technical approach is sound, employing a necessary relaxation to ensure the optimization is computationally feasible. The experimental evaluation is thorough, demonstrating state-of-the-art k-NN classification performance on eight datasets against a comprehensive suite of 26 baselines. Finally, the authors’ promise to release their code is a major asset for reproducibility and future work.

Despite the strong results, the paper could be improved with several clarifications and deeper analysis. My main concerns are:
1.  **Ablation Study:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by an ablation study that disentangles the contributions of the linear transformation `A` and the word weights `w`. It is currently unclear which component is the primary driver of the reported improvements.
2.  **Clarity and Intuition:** While the mechanics are described, a clearer intuition behind the word importance weights (`w` in Eq. 5) and their interaction with the matrix `A` would be beneficial.
3.  **Experimental Details:** Key details are missing. The paper does not state how `k` for the k-NN classifier was chosen (e.g., cross-validation, fixed value). There is also some confusion about the use of the regularization parameter `lambda` from Eq (2) and its relationship to the value mentioned later in the text.
4.  **Unsupported Claims:** The claim that S-WMD is “particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words” should be justified specifically in the context of the learned parameters, rather than simply inheriting the claim from unsupervised WMD.

**Update after author response:** I thank the authors for their careful and clarifying rebuttal. Their explanation regarding the focus on k-NN specific metric learning addressed my initial concern about comparisons to other classifier types.
