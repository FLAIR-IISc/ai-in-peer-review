This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised learning approach to refine the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) metric for document similarity. WMD, introduced by Kusner et al., leverages word embeddings and optimal transport to calculate distances between documents, achieving state-of-the-art results in kNN-based document classification. However, WMD is inherently unsupervised. This work addresses this limitation by learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space and word-importance weights to minimize leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. A key technical contribution is an efficient approximation to the gradient calculation, circumventing the computational bottleneck of the original WMD formulation.

The work builds on a substantial body of research in document distance metrics, starting with traditional approaches like bag-of-words (BOW) and TF-IDF, and evolving through dimensionality reduction techniques like LSI and LDA, and more recently, leveraging word embeddings with WMD.  It also connects to the broader field of metric learning, acknowledging the quadratic scaling issues of many existing methods when applied to high-dimensional text data. The authors explicitly position their work as learning a transformation *of the word embedding space* rather than learning a new document representation, a critical distinction. Relevant prior work on applying learned metrics to the EMD (Earth Mover’s Distance, a generalization of WMD) is also acknowledged.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. First, the technical soundness is compelling. The motivation for learning a supervised metric on top of WMD is clear, and the proposed approach of simultaneously learning a transformation of the word embedding space and word weights is well-justified. The efficient gradient approximation, using the Sinkhorn distance, is a significant contribution that makes the method practical. The derivation and explanation of the gradients are thorough. Second, the experimental evaluation is exceptionally strong. The authors evaluate S-WMD on eight real-world text classification tasks and compare it against a vast array of 26 competitive baselines, including WMD itself, various document representations, and metric learning techniques applied to those representations. The consistent and substantial performance gains across these datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of S-WMD. The inclusion of statistical significance testing (standard errors) and careful hyperparameter tuning with Bayesian optimization further strengthens the results. The visualization using t-SNE convincingly illustrates the improved clustering of documents based on the learned S-WMD metric.  Finally, the paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors provide sufficient detail to allow for reproduction of the results, and the connections to related work are well-established.

However, there are a few minor weaknesses. The discussion of the limitations is somewhat brief. While the authors mention potential issues with document length and the squared Euclidean distance in the word embedding space, a more in-depth exploration of these factors would be beneficial. Additionally, the complexity analysis, while provided, could benefit from a clearer discussion of the practical implications of the O(q^2) term, particularly with respect to vocabulary size. Further, it would be interesting to see an ablation study detailing the contributions of the word importance weighting *vs.* the transformation of the word embeddings. Although the authors mention this is beneficial, a direct comparison could provide further insight.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results: consistently outperforms a large number of competitive baselines.
*   Technically sound and novel approach to incorporating supervision into the WMD metric.
*   Efficient gradient approximation addresses a key computational challenge.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation with rigorous methodology.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of potential limitations and failure cases.
*   Lack of detailed ablation study.
*   Complexity analysis could be more insightful.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of document similarity and metric learning. The proposed S-WMD method is technically sound, empirically well-supported, and clearly explained. While there are a few minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses.  I believe it is a solid paper suitable for presentation at NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
