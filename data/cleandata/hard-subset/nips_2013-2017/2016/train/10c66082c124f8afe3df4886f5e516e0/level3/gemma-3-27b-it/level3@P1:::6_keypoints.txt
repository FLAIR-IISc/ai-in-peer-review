- Proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD) that learns a linear embedding transform (A) and word importance weights (w) to minimize leave-one-out NN classification error.
- Uses Sinkhorn distance and probabilistic filtering to make supervised WMD computationally feasible.
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance: consistently outperforms 26 baselines across eight datasets.
- Novelty: first practical supervised extension of WMD combining embedding transformation and word re-weighting.
- Operates directly in low-dimensional embedding space, avoiding heavy pre-processing and scaling issues of prior metric-learning methods.
- Related prior work on learning ground distances (Cuturi/Avis, Wang & Guibas) did not include word re-weighting, distinguishing this work.
- Clear presentation with pseudocode and intention to release Matlab code enhances reproducibility.
- Weakness: insufficient intuition and concrete examples showing how learned word weights (w) improve performance.
- Weakness: unclear which component (A vs w) drives gains—needs ablation studies to disentangle contributions.
- Weakness: ambiguity in the use of lambda (Eq. 2 vs line 170) and lack of explanation for how it’s chosen.
- Weakness: claim that S-WMD is forgiving for syntactically different but conceptually similar words is under-supported and needs examples/analysis.
- Weakness: Table 2 columns "n" and "ne" are undefined, reducing clarity of reported results.
- Weakness: choice of k in k-NN is not discussed—unclear whether k was fixed or tuned across methods/datasets, affecting fairness.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, effective method with strong empirical support, practical computational solutions, clear write-up, and code release.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited analysis of individual component contributions and minor presentation ambiguities.
- Overall assessment: strong paper meriting acceptance with minor revisions, especially providing insight into learned word weights and the relative roles of A and w.
