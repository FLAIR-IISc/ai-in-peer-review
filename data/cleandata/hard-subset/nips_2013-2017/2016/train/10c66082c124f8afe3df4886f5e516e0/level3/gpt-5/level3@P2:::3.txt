Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised variant of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The key idea is to learn the ground metric used by WMD by parameterizing the word–word cost with a Mahalanobis distance A and to learn a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The learning objective is an NCA-style stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss over documents, where pairwise document distances are computed via (regularized) optimal transport between reweighted histograms with costs induced by the transformed word embeddings. Because differentiating through the exact WMD is expensive and non-smooth, the authors adopt the Sinkhorn-regularized transport to obtain efficient, unique primal and dual solutions (T*, α, β) that yield practical gradient approximations. Training is further accelerated by initializing A with a metric learned on word centroid representations (S-WCD) and by restricting pairwise computations to WCD neighbors.

This work builds directly on WMD [Kusner et al., ICML’15], Sinkhorn distances for OT [Cuturi, NIPS’13; Cuturi & Peyré], and metric learning with NCA/LMNN/ITML [Goldberger et al., NIPS’04; Weinberger & Saul; Davis et al.]. It is related to supervised EMD/OT ground metric learning in computer vision [Wang & Guibas, ECCV’09; Cuturi & Avis; Cuturi & Peyré] and to work that reweights or deforms histograms/words in the simplex (e.g., Lebanon’s Riemannian metrics on the multinomial simplex). The paper reports extensive experiments across eight text classification datasets, comparing to 26 baselines, and shows consistent gains.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The learning formulation is sensible: an NCA loss on document pairs with a learned cost A and weights w, optimized via SGD with gradients approximated using the Sinkhorn solution. The gradients for A (via the primal) and w (via the dual) are standard in OT and look correct given the regularization. The approximation—using α*, β* from Sinkhorn (u, v) to backprop into w and T* to backprop into A—is reasonable and efficient, though it is an approximation to the unregularized WMD gradients.
- Empirical support: Strong. Eight datasets, 26 baselines, careful hyperparameter tuning, and consistent improvements. The visualization and timing results support the claims.
- Caveats: The paper fixes λ=10 and does not quantify the impact of regularization strength on both accuracy and the faithfulness of the gradient to the unregularized WMD objective. An ablation on the roles of A vs. w would strengthen the empirical story.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-written, but several notational and presentation issues should be addressed:
- Notation inconsistencies: Eq. (1) uses ‖xi−xj‖p2 (with p=1 or 2), whereas Eq. (5) fixes squared Euclidean. Later, p is also used as a symbol for dimensionality/word counts. Please unify notation, e.g., describe WMD as the p-Wasserstein distance with cost ‖·‖q (and fix separate letters for exponent vs. dimensions), and be explicit when switching from unregularized to entropy-regularized OT.
- Regularized vs. unregularized OT: The paper alternates between exact WMD and Sinkhorn distances for training. Clearly state throughout which objective is being optimized and which distance is used at test time. A short discussion of the bias introduced by regularization and how λ trades off bias/variance and computational cost would be valuable.
- Algorithmic clarity: You already include Algorithm 1; please make it fully self-contained (inputs, outputs, constraints on A and w, choice of r, regularization/constraints on w≥0, learning-rate schedules, stopping criteria, and neighbor-filtering details).
- Derivation details: The mapping from Sinkhorn scaling vectors u, v to approximate dual variables α*, β* deserves a brief derivation or an explicit citation to Cuturi & Peyré’s smoothed dual formulation. A short appendix showing these steps would improve rigor.

Originality
Learning the OT ground cost is not new per se, and supervised EMD has been explored in computer vision (e.g., Wang & Guibas) and in OT metric learning (Cuturi & Avis; Cuturi & Peyré). The novelty here lies in: (i) applying this idea to document distances grounded in pre-trained word embeddings, (ii) jointly learning a linear word embedding transform A and histogram weights w tailored to labels, and (iii) integrating Sinkhorn-based gradients into an NCA objective for efficient end-to-end training. While conceptually straightforward, the combination is well-motivated and practically impactful.

Significance
Given the popularity of WMD and OT in NLP and ML, a supervised, efficient variant that materially improves kNN classification is valuable. The approach is simple enough to be adopted broadly and can benefit retrieval, classification, and visualization. The empirical results suggest state-of-the-art performance among non-neural document metrics; the learned word weights may also provide interpretability.

Reasons to accept
- Clear, practical method that extends a widely used distance (WMD) with supervision.
- Solid experimental evidence across diverse datasets and strong baselines.
- Efficient training via Sinkhorn approximations and neighbor filtering; code released.
- Interpretability via learned w and linear A; good visualizations.

Reasons to reject
- Limited analysis of the regularization–accuracy trade-off and approximation gap between Sinkhorn and true WMD.
- Some mathematical and notational sloppiness; derivations are sketched but not fully rigorous.
- Missing ablations to isolate contributions of A vs. w; unclear empirical importance of w.
- Related work on histogram reweighting/ground metric learning could be cited and contrasted more carefully.

Questions and suggestions
1) Regularization/approximation: Please discuss and, if possible, quantify how λ affects (a) classification accuracy, (b) optimization stability/speed, and (c) the gap to unregularized WMD. A small study varying λ and reporting accuracy and runtime would help.
2) Ablations: Report variants with (i) w fixed to 1 (only A), (ii) A fixed to identity (only w), and (iii) both learned (full S-WMD). This would clarify the relative contribution of reweighting vs. ground metric learning.
3) Constraints/regularization: Are there constraints on w (nonnegativity, normalization) and A (e.g., low rank, Frobenius norm penalties)? Please specify and discuss overfitting controls.
4) Neighbor filtering: Provide evidence that WCD-based neighbor pruning does not harm performance (e.g., recall of true nearest neighbors under S-WMD vs. N, sensitivity to N).
5) Notation: Use distinct symbols for norm exponent, vocabulary size, document support size, embedding dimension, and neighbor probabilities (p is overloaded).
6) Related work: In addition to Cuturi & Avis and Wang & Guibas, please cite and contrast with prior work that reweights or deforms multinomial histograms (e.g., Lebanon’s information-geometric distances on the simplex) and with Le/Cuturi-style OT variants that learn feature weights. Clarify your differences: discrete word support, entropy regularization, linear ground metric parameterization, and supervised NCA objective.
7) Test-time distance: Clarify whether you use the learned A and w with exact WMD or with Sinkhorn distance at test time; report any accuracy/runtime differences.
8) Sensitivity to initialization: You note S-WCD performs well—please report convergence plots and final gains relative to initialization. Also specify k in kNN and selection protocol.
9) Interpretation of w: Provide an analysis of the learned weights (beyond a word cloud), e.g., top up/down-weighted words per class and their effect on errors.

Minor comments and typos
- Section 3.1: consistently denote the document support size (previously q, later p).
- Clarify that D′(da,db)=‖Xda−Xdb‖2 is a lower bound to WMD under the same ground cost and that this remains true after applying A.
- Provide details on stopping criteria and number of Sinkhorn iterations.
- Some references are incomplete; ensure full bibliographic details.

Overall assessment
A simple but effective supervised extension of WMD with strong empirical results and reasonable computational efficiency. With improved rigor, clearer notation, and additional analyses on regularization and ablations, this would be a solid NIPS contribution. I support acceptance.
