

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) introduced by Kusner et al. (NIPS 2015). While WMD leverages pre-trained word embeddings to compute document distances through optimal transport between word embeddings, S-WMD incorporates supervision to learn task-specific document metrics. The authors address the computational challenge of incorporating supervision into WMD by introducing an efficient approximation using Sinkhorn distance, enabling the learning of both an affine transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights. This approach minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out classification error, directly optimizing for classification performance rather than relying on unsupervised document representations.

The paper makes a significant contribution by applying discriminative training to learn a domain-specific metric from WMD, which has not been done previously. The method is technically sound, with a clear and well-justified approximation that avoids the cubic complexity of exact WMD gradients. The authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation for their approach, including detailed derivations of the gradients with respect to both the transformation matrix and word importance weights.

The experimental evaluation is exceptionally strong, covering eight diverse text classification datasets across news categorization, sentiment analysis, and product identification. The authors compare against 26 competitive baselines, including standard document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA), distance metrics (WCD, WMD), and supervised metric learning approaches (NCA, ITML, LMNN) applied to various representations. S-WMD consistently outperforms all baselines, achieving the lowest error rate on 5/8 datasets and the best average performance across all datasets. Notably, the method improves upon WMD by significant margins (e.g., 51% relative reduction on BBCSPORT, 22% on OHSUMED), demonstrating the value of supervision for document distance learning.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, with well-organized sections that provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors carefully explain the connections between their work and prior metric learning approaches (NCA, LMNN, ITML), while highlighting the unique aspects of their method. The visualization results (t-SNE embeddings, learned word weights) further support the effectiveness of their approach.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with rigorous experimental validation. The authors thoughtfully address both the strengths and limitations of their approach, noting that S-WMD's performance is particularly strong on smaller documents where unsupervised methods struggle. The method's efficiency (training time comparable to baseline metric learning approaches) is also well-documented.

For originality, S-WMD represents a meaningful extension of WMD that addresses a significant gap in the literature. While WMD was introduced at NIPS 2015, the supervised variant presented here is novel and distinct from previous metric learning work. The authors correctly reference prior NIPS work on WMD and metric learning, demonstrating awareness of the field's context.

The significance of this work is substantial. Document distances are fundamental to numerous text retrieval applications, and the consistent improvements demonstrated across multiple datasets suggest broad applicability. The method's ability to leverage pre-trained word embeddings while learning task-specific metrics provides a practical and effective solution that could benefit both researchers and practitioners. The results represent a clear advancement over the state-of-the-art, with improvements that are both statistically significant and practically meaningful.

A minor limitation is the paper's focus on word2vec embeddings without exploring other embedding approaches, though the authors acknowledge this is possible. The method's scalability to extremely large text corpora could also be explored further, though the current training times are reasonable for most applications.

Overall, this is a strong, well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The technical approach is sound, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, and the results are compelling. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS contribution: it is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. The method represents a meaningful advancement in document distance learning that will likely influence future work in text representation and metric learning.

Arguments for acceptance: Novel supervised extension to WMD with significant performance gains across multiple datasets; technically sound method with efficient approximation; comprehensive experimental evaluation; clear writing and organization; strong potential impact on text classification and retrieval applications.

Arguments against acceptance: None that are significant; the paper's contributions are clear and well-supported, with no major technical or methodological flaws.
