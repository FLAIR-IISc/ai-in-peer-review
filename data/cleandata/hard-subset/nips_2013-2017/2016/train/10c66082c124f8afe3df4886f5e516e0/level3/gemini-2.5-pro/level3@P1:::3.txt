### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. The approach builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD), which models the distance between two documents as an optimal transport (or Earth Mover's Distance) problem between their respective word embeddings. The key limitation of WMD is its unsupervised nature. This work addresses this limitation by introducing supervision in two ways: 1) by learning a linear transformation (a Mahalanobis metric) of the underlying word embedding space, and 2) by learning a word-importance vector that re-weighs the document's bag-of-words representation.

To learn these parameters, the authors propose minimizing a stochastic k-Nearest Neighbor leave-one-out classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A significant technical challenge is that computing the gradient of the WMD is computationally prohibitive and potentially ill-defined. The authors provide a practical and efficient solution by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized counterpart (the Sinkhorn distance), which is differentiable and can be computed much faster. The resulting gradients for the linear transformation and the word-importance weights are then used in a batch gradient descent procedure. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation across eight text classification datasets, demonstrating that S-WMD consistently outperforms a large suite of 26 competitive baselines, including WMD and various document representations combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

### Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance

The paper has several significant strengths that argue for its acceptance.

1.  **Clear Motivation and Simple, Effective Idea:** The work addresses a clear and important limitation of the original WMD: its inability to incorporate supervision. The proposed solution is a straightforward yet powerful combination of existing techniques: WMD for the base distance, NCA for the supervised objective, and entropy-regularized optimal transport for efficient optimization. This combination is well-motivated and elegant.

2.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** The main technical contribution—deriving an efficient and differentiable approximation for the gradient of the WMD—is crucial for making the approach practical. By leveraging the Sinkhorn distance and its dual variables, the authors circumvent the need for a complex and inefficient nested optimization loop, which would render the learning process intractable.

3.  **Compelling and Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is a major strength of the paper. The authors evaluate S-WMD on eight diverse datasets and compare it against an extensive list of 26 baselines. These baselines include not only standard text representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA) but also state-of-the-art methods like mSDA and the original WMD. Furthermore, they combine these representations with strong metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML), providing a robust benchmark. S-WMD's superior performance across the board provides convincing evidence of its effectiveness and significance.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

Despite the strong results, there are several areas where the paper could be improved in terms of clarity, rigor, and its connection to prior work. These points should be addressed for the final version.

1.  **Regularized vs. Unregularized OT:** The paper's use of the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance is a clever practical solution to make the optimization tractable. However, the connection between the gradient of this regularized objective and the original unregularized WMD objective could be discussed more thoroughly. The paper is learning a metric for the regularized distance, but evaluating its performance using the (presumably) unregularized distance at test time. The authors should clarify this and discuss the implications of this approximation, particularly how the choice of the regularization parameter `λ` might affect the quality of the learned metric for the true WMD.

2.  **Rigor and Algorithmic Clarity:** The mathematical exposition could be tightened. For instance, the derivation of the gradient with respect to `w` in Equation (10) relies on the dual variables from the relaxed transport problem. A more detailed derivation or a pointer to supplementary material would strengthen the paper's technical quality. Additionally, the overall algorithm is only described in prose and summarized briefly in Algorithm 1. Including a more detailed algorithm box that outlines the complete training procedure (initialization, batch selection with neighbor filtering, gradient computation, and parameter updates) would significantly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

3.  **Notational Inconsistency:** There appears to be a notational inconsistency regarding the distance used. In Equation (1), the WMD is defined with the Euclidean norm `||xi - xj||_p^2`, with `p` typically 1 or 2. However, the loss function and gradients (e.g., Eq. 5, 8) use the squared Euclidean distance. This corresponds specifically to the 2-Wasserstein distance. To avoid confusion and improve precision, the authors should be consistent throughout and perhaps frame WMD as the p-Wasserstein distance from the outset, clarifying that they focus on the case where p=2.

4.  **Novelty of Histogram Reweighting:** The idea of learning weights for histogram re-weighting within an EMD framework is not entirely novel, and this should be acknowledged. For example, work by Guy Lebanon on "Metric learning for text documents" (JMLR 2006) and more recent work by Le and Cuturi on Wasserstein barycenters also involve learning weights for distributions. Citing and briefly discussing this related work would better situate the paper's contribution regarding the learned vector `w`.

5.  **Analysis of Learned Parameters:** The experiments convincingly show *that* the method works, but provide limited insight into *why*. Specifically, the contribution of the learned word importance vector `w` is not fully explored. The word cloud in Figure 2 is a nice visualization, but a more quantitative analysis would be valuable. An ablation study comparing S-WMD with and without learning `w` (i.e., only learning `A`) would help disentangle the relative importance of the metric transformation versus the histogram re-weighting.

6.  **Clarity on Related Work:** The discussion of related work could be more precise in its distinctions. For example, while the paper cites Cuturi & Avis (2011), it should be clarified that their work learns a general, unconstrained ground distance matrix, whereas this paper learns a more constrained (and lower-dimensional) linear parameterization via `A`. Similarly, the work of Wang et al. (2012) is highly relevant as it also learns a Mahalanobis metric for EMD. The authors should distinguish their work more clearly, for instance by highlighting that this paper's contribution includes the novel histogram re-weighting component, which Wang et al. do not consider.

### Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that presents a simple, well-motivated, and effective method for supervised document metric learning. It combines several existing ideas in a novel way and solves the associated technical challenges with a practical and efficient approximation. The extensive and compelling experimental results demonstrate a significant advance over a wide range of strong baselines. While there are several opportunities to improve the paper's clarity, rigor, and discussion of related work, these points are addressable. I support the acceptance of this paper.
