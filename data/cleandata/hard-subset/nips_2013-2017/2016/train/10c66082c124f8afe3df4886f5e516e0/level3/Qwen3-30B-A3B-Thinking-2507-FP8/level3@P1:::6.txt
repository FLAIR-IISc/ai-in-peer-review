

This paper extends the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) proposed by Kusner et al. (NIPS 2015) to a supervised setting, introducing Supervised-WMD (S-WMD) for document classification. The authors address a significant limitation of unsupervised WMD by incorporating label information through a linear transformation A of word embeddings and word importance weights w. The method efficiently approximates the gradient of the leave-one-out classification error using Sinkhorn distance (Cuturi, NIPS 2013), enabling practical optimization. The authors evaluate S-WMD on 8 diverse text classification datasets, demonstrating consistent outperformance over 26 baselines, including both unsupervised document representations and supervised metric learning approaches.

The paper's main strengths include a technically sound approach to incorporating supervision into WMD, a well-justified computational approximation, and comprehensive empirical evaluation. The authors carefully address the computational challenges of directly optimizing WMD with supervision through the Sinkhorn relaxation, providing a practical solution that maintains the conceptual elegance of WMD while adding supervision. The experimental results are compelling, with S-WMD achieving the lowest error on 5/8 datasets and outperforming all baselines on average. The method's efficiency (as shown in Table 3) is particularly impressive, with training times comparable to other metric learning approaches.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on document distances, building directly on Kusner et al.'s WMD while addressing a critical limitation. The connection to previous work on metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) is well-established, and the authors appropriately situate their method within this context. The idea of learning word importance weights w alongside the linear transformation A is particularly novel and well-motivated by the observation that different classification tasks may value words differently.

However, several aspects of the paper require clarification. The intuition behind Equation (5) and the specific role of w in the document distance definition is not sufficiently explained. It's unclear whether the improvements primarily stem from the linear transformation A or the word importance weights w, which would be valuable for understanding the method's mechanics. The paper mentions a "particularly forgiving" property regarding syntactically different but conceptually similar words (line 316), but provides no justification for this claim beyond the general properties of word embeddings.

There are also several technical ambiguities that need addressing. The relationship between Equation (2) and the λ referenced at line 170 is unclear, as is the exact role of λ in the gradient computation. The paper claims to report "average error across datasets" at line 257, but this metric is not present in Table 2 or 3, and the meaning of the columns "n" and "ne" in Table 2 is not specified. The choice of k for k-NN classification is not described, leaving unclear whether it's dataset-dependent or method-dependent.

The authors' clarification in the rebuttal about the "n" vs "ne" columns (which refers to normalized vs non-normalized word histograms) was helpful, but this should be explicitly stated in the paper. Similarly, the justification for the "forgiving" property could be strengthened with specific examples from the visualization results (Figure 1) or the top-weighted words in Figure 2.

The paper's significance is high as it provides a powerful new document distance that achieves state-of-the-art results across multiple text classification tasks. The method's ability to leverage pre-trained word embeddings while incorporating task-specific supervision represents an important advance in document representation learning. The authors' commitment to releasing code (as mentioned) will facilitate reproducibility and further adoption.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of document distances. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. The minor ambiguities and lack of certain explanations are common in NIPS submissions and can be addressed in the camera-ready version. The paper significantly advances the state of the art in document representation and classification, with clear potential for adoption in various text analysis applications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the points mentioned above.
