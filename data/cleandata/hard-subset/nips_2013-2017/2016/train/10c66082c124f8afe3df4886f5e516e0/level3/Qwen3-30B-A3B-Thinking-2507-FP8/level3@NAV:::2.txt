

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a significant extension to the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) framework. While WMD (Kusner et al., 2015) established a powerful document distance metric by leveraging word embeddings through optimal transport, S-WMD innovatively incorporates supervision to learn domain-specific metrics. The authors propose learning both an affine transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights through discriminative training that minimizes stochastic leave-one-out classification error. The key technical contribution is an efficient approximation of the WMD gradient using Sinkhorn distance, enabling practical optimization without the prohibitive cubic complexity of exact WMD computation.

The paper excels in quality with a technically sound method that is clearly explained and rigorously validated. The authors provide thorough theoretical motivation, including the derivation of efficient gradient approximations, and conduct an exceptionally comprehensive experimental evaluation across eight diverse text classification datasets. They compare against 26 competitive baselines spanning traditional document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI), deep learning approaches (mSDA), and metric learning techniques (NCA, ITML, LMNN). S-WMD consistently outperforms all baselines by significant margins, achieving state-of-the-art results on five of eight datasets with strong average performance.

The paper demonstrates exceptional clarity in presenting the method and experimental results. The writing is well-organized with appropriate technical detail, making it accessible to readers familiar with WMD and metric learning. The approach shows clear originality by uniquely combining optimal transport with discriminative metric learning for document distances, addressing a gap in the literature where previous metric learning approaches required dimensionality reduction before applying to text.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it provides a practical, efficient solution for learning domain-specific document metrics that substantially improves upon the state-of-the-art. The method leverages existing word embeddings without requiring additional training, making it broadly applicable to various text classification tasks. The thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets and baselines convincingly demonstrates the value of supervised learning for document distances.

Overall, this is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It is clearly suitable for presentation at NIPS.
