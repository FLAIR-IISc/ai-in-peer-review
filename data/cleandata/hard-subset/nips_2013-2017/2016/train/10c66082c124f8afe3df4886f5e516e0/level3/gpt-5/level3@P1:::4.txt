This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised metric over documents that builds directly on the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The authors learn (i) a linear transform A of the underlying word embedding space and (ii) a global word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms, with both optimized to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective à la NCA. To avoid nested differentiation through the optimal transport solver, they adopt an entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) relaxation to obtain efficient, differentiable approximations for both the transport plan and the dual potentials, enabling practical gradient updates. They initialize A by applying NCA to word centroid representations (S-WCD), then refine with minibatch optimization and a WCD-based neighbor filter, reporting consistent improvements over numerous unsupervised and supervised baselines across eight text classification tasks.

Relation to prior work: The paper stands at the intersection of WMD/OT for document distances (Kusner et al.; Cuturi’s Sinkhorn distances) and supervised metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML). Earlier works learn generalized Euclidean metrics for vector representations; here the supervision is injected at the level of the ground metric for OT and via a learned vocabulary weighting. There is also related work on learning ground metrics for EMD in vision, but less so for words/documents. The approach is thus a natural supervised extension of WMD with computational leverage from Sinkhorn OT and with an NCA-style loss.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and well-written exposition, with a thorough literature review that situates the contribution among WMD, OT, and metric learning.
- Methodologically coherent: learning a task-aligned ground metric A and a vocabulary weight vector w directly targets kNN performance, and the Sinkhorn-based approximation makes optimization practical.
- Sensible initialization (S-WCD) and engineering choices (minibatch training, neighbor filtering) that materially improve efficiency.
- Strong empirical study: eight datasets, 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised, including NCA/LMNN/ITML on various representations), consistent gains on most datasets, plus t-SNE visualizations and timing analyses.
- Public code, suggesting reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Motivation and interpretation of w: A global vocabulary reweighting that rescales each document and renormalizes may change document semantics in ways not clearly justified. Why is this preferable to learning only A? Under what constraints (e.g., nonnegativity, bounded dynamic range, sparsity) does w improve label separation without effectively altering documents into different ones? A principled argument or empirical analysis is needed.
- Novelty is incremental: The pieces—linear transform on embeddings, global reweighting, NCA objective, Sinkhorn relaxation—are all known. The contribution lies in combining them for WMD with careful engineering. This is useful but not conceptually deep.
- Generalization to new documents: It is not fully clear how w is applied at test time for unseen vocabulary or domain shift. Since w appears global, how are OOV terms handled? What is the effect if the test distribution shifts (e.g., different word frequency spectrum)?
- Terminology: The abstract’s “semantic difference” reads as label/task-specific similarity (topic, authorship), not semantics in the linguistic sense. This could be clarified to “task-aligned similarity.”
- Limited ablations: The paper would benefit from explicit A-only vs w-only vs A+w comparisons and sensitivity to λ (regularization strength), the number of neighbors used in the gradient, and the choice of ground cost (L1 vs L2 vs squared L2).

Quality
- Generally sound and careful. The use of Sinkhorn provides differentiable, unique solutions, and the gradients with respect to A and w are derived cleanly. However, a brief justification of the quality of the dual potentials approximation for gradient w.r.t. w (and its sensitivity to λ) would strengthen the technical claims. Regularization of A and w (e.g., Frobenius/L1 penalties) is not discussed and could be important to avoid overfitting.

Clarity
- Very clear overall. Suggested clarifications: explicitly state that w is global and nonnegative; define how OOV words are handled; replace “semantic” with “task-aligned” in the abstract; add a short discussion on constraints/priors on w and A.

Originality
- Moderate. The idea of supervising the WMD ground metric with NCA and accelerating with Sinkhorn is natural but still a meaningful extension to an influential unsupervised method.

Significance
- Practical significance is good: S-WMD improves kNN classification across diverse datasets and provides an efficient supervised alternative to WMD. Conceptual significance is modest but the impact as a tool could be high given the popularity of WMD-like distances.

Questions for the authors
1. Is w constrained (nonnegativity, bounds, sparsity)? How sensitive are results to such constraints, and what prevents pathological reweighting?
2. How does S-WMD perform with A-only, w-only, and A+w? Please add ablations and discuss when w helps most.
3. How are OOV tokens handled at test time? If w is global, what is the default for unseen words, and how robust is performance to domain shift?
4. How sensitive are results to λ in the Sinkhorn relaxation, and to the choice of p (L1 vs L2 vs squared L2) in the ground cost?
5. What regularization is used on A and w? Any early-stopping criteria or validation-based model selection?
6. Can you report inference-time cost compared to vanilla WMD and to vector-based kNN baselines?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical or empirical justification for w that preserves document semantics while improving task discrimination (e.g., constrain w to be close to IDF, or add a KL penalty to keep reweighted histograms close to originals).
- Add ablations (A-only, w-only), sensitivity analyses (λ, neighbor set size), and robustness tests (OOV handling).
- Clarify terminology and test-time procedure; include a short discussion on generalization across domains/vocabularies.
- Consider comparing against alternative ground metrics (cosine, Mahalanobis) and alternative embeddings (GloVe, fastText) to show robustness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, well-executed supervised extension to a widely used distance (WMD), with strong empirical gains and practical efficiency.
- Solid engineering contributions (initialization, Sinkhorn gradients, batching) and public code.

Arguments against acceptance
- Conceptual novelty is limited; the method is largely a composition of known components.
- The role and justification of w remain underspecified, with potential semantic distortion and unclear generalization to new vocabularies.

Overall, I would not object to acceptance, especially if the authors address the concerns around w, add ablations, and clarify terminology and test-time behavior.
