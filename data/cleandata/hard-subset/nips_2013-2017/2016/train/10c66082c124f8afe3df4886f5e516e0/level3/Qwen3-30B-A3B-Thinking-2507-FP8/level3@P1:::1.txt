

The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that learns a linear transformation of word embeddings and word importance weights to improve document classification. Building on Kusner et al.'s WMD (ICML 2015), which computes document distances as optimal transport between word embeddings, S-WMD incorporates supervision through stochastic leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error minimization. The authors introduce an efficient Sinkhorn distance approximation to handle the computationally challenging nested optimization problem inherent in WMD gradient computation, enabling practical training. The method is evaluated across 8 text classification datasets, demonstrating consistent improvements over 26 baselines.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution that extends the WMD framework to incorporate task-specific supervision. The authors properly acknowledge the limitations of unsupervised metrics in document classification tasks, where semantic similarity should align with class labels rather than raw word co-occurrence. The optimization approach using Sinkhorn distance (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) is well-motivated and effectively addresses the computational bottleneck. The experimental results are thorough, showing S-WMD outperforms unsupervised WMD and multiple document representation methods across 5/8 datasets, with particularly strong results on smaller document collections like TWITTER and CLASSIC.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear exposition of the mathematical foundations and a well-structured presentation of the methodology. The technical details are precise, and the connection to related work (NCA, metric learning) is appropriately contextualized. The authors provide a clear justification for their approach to gradient computation, which is a significant contribution to making supervised WMD practically feasible.

However, several concerns limit the paper's impact. The most significant issue is that the initialization method (S-WCD) using the word centroid distance already achieves results very close to the final S-WMD model, as shown in Table 2 (S-WMD init. vs. S-WMD). This suggests that the primary benefit of the supervised learning component may be marginal, as the initialization itself is already a strong baseline. The authors should explicitly address why the supervised refinement is necessary if the initialization performs so well.

Additionally, the paper misses a critical baseline: evaluating standard metric learning methods (NCA, LMNN, ITML) on top of word centroid embeddings. This would provide crucial context for whether the improvement over unsupervised WMD is due to the supervised learning framework or simply the quality of the word centroid representation. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the S-WMD framework offers a genuinely novel contribution.

The paper also lacks an ablation study isolating the contributions of the linear projection matrix A versus the word importance weights. Understanding which component drives the performance improvement would strengthen the technical contribution and provide clearer guidance for future work.

While the optimization technique (lines 156-175) is technically interesting and well-executed, the core metric learning approach within the EMD framework represents a somewhat incremental contribution. The main novelty appears to be the practical implementation rather than a fundamentally new theoretical approach. The authors could better position their contribution by more clearly differentiating it from Wang et al.'s EMD metric learning work (ECCV 2015), which also learns a metric within the EMD framework but doesn't incorporate word weighting.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the focus on kNN classification, which is a narrow application of document distances. While the results are impressive on the evaluated tasks, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how the method would benefit other text processing tasks where document distances are used (e.g., information retrieval, clustering).

The paper is well-suited for NIPS due to its clear connection to recent advances in optimal transport for text and its practical contribution to making supervised document distances feasible. The writing is excellent, and the implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproducibility.

Pro: Well-written, technically sound, strong empirical results on multiple datasets, efficient optimization using Sinkhorn approximation.
Con: Limited impact due to strong initialization performance, missing critical baseline comparisons, lack of ablation study, incremental contribution in metric learning concept.

Overall, the paper presents a useful extension to the WMD framework that improves document classification accuracy, but its significance would be substantially enhanced with more thorough comparisons to relevant baselines and a clearer demonstration of the relative contributions of the different components. The method is well-executed and practical, but the novelty appears somewhat limited given the strong performance of the initialization. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the missing baseline comparisons and ablation study.
