This paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by learning word-specific weights, which is referred to as the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD). The authors leverage the Sinkhorn distance as a faster proxy for the Wasserstein distance to alleviate the cubic time complexity of the original WMD computation. The proposed method is evaluated on 8 real-world text classification tasks against 26 competitive baselines, and the results show that S-WMD consistently outperforms almost all of the baselines, achieving the lowest kNN test error on 5 out of 8 datasets.

The paper is well-written, with clear and in-depth mathematical exposition. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the background and related work, including the Word Mover's Distance and linear metric learning. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the mathematical derivations are sound.

However, the novelty of the paper is somewhat limited compared to the prior Word Mover's Distance work. The main contribution of the paper is the extension of WMD to a supervised setting by learning word-specific weights. While this is a useful extension, it is not a major departure from the original WMD work.

There are a few areas where the paper could be improved. The references to Word Centroid Distance are not relevant and are confusing. The authors should clarify the relationship between Word Centroid Distance and the proposed method. Additionally, the role and choice of lambda (entropic regularizer) are poorly discussed in the manuscript. Lambda can have a dominant effect on the regularized transport problem, and its value dramatically impacts the behavior and results of the method. The authors should provide more discussion on the choice of lambda and its impact on the method.

Furthermore, it is unclear whether the method behaves like pure Wasserstein (large lambda) or like entropic/Sinkhorn (small lambda). Most users choose small lambda for speed, which yields different behavior. The authors should provide more insight into the behavior of the method under different values of lambda.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed method on 8 real-world text classification tasks, and the results are convincing.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the mathematical derivations are clear and easy to follow. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the background and related work, which makes the paper accessible to a wide range of readers.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a useful extension of the Word Mover's Distance to a supervised setting. However, the novelty of the paper is somewhat limited compared to the prior WMD work.

In terms of significance, the paper provides a useful contribution to the field of natural language processing and text classification. The proposed method has the potential to improve the state-of-the-art in text classification tasks, and the results on 8 real-world datasets are convincing.
