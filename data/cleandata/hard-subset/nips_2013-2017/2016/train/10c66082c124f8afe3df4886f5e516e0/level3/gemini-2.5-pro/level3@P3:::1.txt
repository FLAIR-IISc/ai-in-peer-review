**Review of "Supervised Word Mover’s Distance"**

**Summary**

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised document metric built upon the recently proposed Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD computes the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the word embeddings of one document to match the other, a formulation of the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD). While powerful, WMD is entirely unsupervised. This work extends WMD by incorporating supervision to tailor the distance metric to a specific classification task. The proposed method learns two components: (1) a linear projection of the word embedding space to better align word semantics with the task, and (2) a vector of word-specific importance weights to up-weight discriminative words.

The main technical contribution is an efficient optimization procedure. A direct gradient-based optimization of the WMD metric is computationally prohibitive and non-differentiable. The authors overcome this by replacing the exact EMD with its entropy-regularized Sinkhorn approximation, which is differentiable and can be solved much faster. The model parameters (the projection matrix and weight vector) are then learned by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification error, similar to Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments on eight text classification datasets that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art kNN classification error, outperforming 26 competitive baselines on average.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Useful Extension:** The paper addresses a clear limitation of the original WMD—its unsupervised nature. Extending WMD to a supervised setting is a logical and valuable contribution, allowing the powerful document distance to be adapted to specific tasks. The proposed method of jointly learning a transformation of the embedding space and a re-weighting of word importance is intuitive and well-motivated.

2.  **Technically Sound and Clever Optimization:** The core challenge of this work lies in optimizing through the EMD. The paper's solution—using the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable proxy for WMD to enable efficient gradient-based learning—is elegant and effective. This makes an otherwise intractable problem practical.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is thoroughly evaluated on eight datasets against a comprehensive set of 26 baselines, including various document representations (TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN). S-WMD achieves the best average rank and sets a new state-of-the-art on 5 of the 8 datasets for kNN classification.

4.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, well-organized, and pleasant to read. The background is explained clearly, the proposed method is motivated and derived logically, and the experimental setup is detailed. This high level of clarity makes the contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Improvement Over a Simpler Baseline:** The most significant concern is that the performance gains of the full S-WMD method over its own initialization step are marginal. The initialization, termed "S-WMD init." or S-WCD, is simply NCA applied to word-centroid document representations. As shown in Table 2, this much simpler method achieves a large portion of the performance gains. For example, on the RECIPE dataset, unsupervised WMD has an error of 2.1, S-WCD achieves 1.6, and the full S-WMD only slightly improves this to 1.5. This pattern holds across several datasets. While the full S-WMD is technically novel, its relatively small improvement over a simple and fast alternative questions its practical significance and impact.

2.  **Missing Baselines:** The experimental evaluation, while broad, is missing a key comparison. The authors apply NCA, ITML, and LMNN to several document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, etc.), but not to the word-centroid distance (WCD) representation. Given the outstanding performance of S-WCD (which is NCA applied to WCD), it is crucial to compare it against ITML and LMNN applied to the same WCD representation. This comparison is necessary to properly contextualize the performance of the S-WCD initialization and determine if the NCA-style loss is uniquely suited for this problem.

3.  **Lack of an Ablation Study:** The proposed S-WMD learns both a projection matrix `A` and a word-importance vector `w`. However, the paper does not include an ablation study to analyze the individual contributions of these two components. It is unclear whether the performance gain comes primarily from transforming the embedding space, re-weighting the words, or if both are essential. Such a study would provide valuable insight into the method's behavior and justify the inclusion of both learned components.

4.  **Incremental Originality:** While the application to WMD for text and the optimization scheme are novel, the core idea of learning a linear metric within an EMD framework has been previously explored in computer vision (e.g., Wang et al. [35]). The main novelty thus lies more in the clever and efficient adaptation of these ideas to the text domain rather than in a fundamentally new concept.

**Minor Comments and Questions for Clarification**

*   In Section 2, last paragraph of "Word Mover's Distance" (around line 90), the sentence begins "The authors also introduce...". For clarity, it would be helpful to specify that "The authors" refers to Kusner et al. [19].
*   Equation 10 uses optimal dual variables α* and β*. For consistency and clarity, the α and β used to derive the gradient in Equation 11 should likely also have stars (α*, β*) to indicate they are the optimal dual variables from the relaxed transport problem.
*   In Section 3.1, the paper describes how to obtain the dual variables from the vectors u and v. It would be slightly clearer to explicitly state here that u and v are found via the Sinkhorn scaling algorithm referenced in Section 2.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a solid contribution. It is technically sound, empirically strong, and exceptionally well-written. The main weakness is that the practical significance of the full, complex S-WMD model is somewhat undermined by the strong performance of its much simpler initialization method (S-WCD). This, combined with the missing ablation study and key baseline comparisons, makes the conclusions about the superiority of the full S-WMD model less convincing than they could be. Despite these concerns, the paper introduces an interesting and powerful method for supervised document distance learning and the efficient optimization technique is a valuable contribution in its own right. The paper would be significantly strengthened by experiments that better isolate the source of its performance gains.
