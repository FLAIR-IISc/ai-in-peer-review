This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for incorporating supervision into the powerful but unsupervised WMD document metric. The core idea is to learn a task-specific distance by parameterizing the ground metric between word embeddings with a Mahalanobis distance (a linear transformation `A`) and simultaneously learning a vector `w` of word-importance weights. Building on prior work in metric learning, the learning objective is inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), minimizing a soft version of the kNN leave-one-out classification error. To overcome the computational challenge of differentiating through the optimal transport problem, the authors leverage the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance, which provides an efficient and differentiable approximation for computing the gradients of `A` and `w`.

**Strengths:**
- **Significance and Quality:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of WMD, a highly influential metric in text analysis. The proposed solution is elegant, practical, and yields impressive results.
- **Originality:** The work presents a novel and effective combination of existing techniques—namely WMD, NCA-style metric learning, and efficient regularized optimal transport. This synthesis is well-motivated and straightforward.
- **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are a significant strength of the paper. S-WMD is benchmarked on eight datasets against a comprehensive suite of 26 baselines, demonstrating state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance consistently. The thoroughness of this evaluation provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
- **Clarity of Exposition:** The mathematical derivations and notation could be more rigorous. For instance, there is an inconsistency between the use of Euclidean distance in the WMD definition (Eq. 1) and the squared Euclidean distance used in the supervised formulation (Eq. 6). Explicitly framing the distance as a p-Wasserstein metric would add clarity. While Algorithm 1 is helpful, a more detailed algorithmic box would improve reproducibility.
- **Related Work:** The histogram re-weighting (Eq. 5) is not entirely new and should cite related work by Guy Lebanon and Le/Cuturi. The paper should also more clearly distinguish its approach from previous work on learning EMD ground metrics, such as Cuturi & Avis (e.g., entropy regularization, continuous vs. discrete cost parameterization) and discuss the limitations of Wang et al. that are addressed here.
- **Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a deeper discussion of the interplay between the unregularized EMD and its entropy-regularized approximation. Furthermore, while the visualization of the learned weight vector `w` is insightful, a quantitative analysis or ablation study showing its specific empirical contribution to the performance gain would strengthen the paper's claims about its relevance.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a solid paper with a well-executed idea that leads to significant empirical gains. The combination of established techniques is clever and results in a practical algorithm for supervised document distance learning. While there are minor weaknesses in the mathematical exposition and positioning with respect to prior work, these are addressable. The strong experimental results clearly demonstrate an advance in the state of the art. I support acceptance of this paper.
