This paper proposes a novel approach to learning a supervised document distance metric, building upon the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by introducing a linear transformation and a reweighted word distribution. The authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their work, including a thorough literature review and extensive experimental results on several novel datasets.

The main idea of the paper is to learn a supervised metric on top of the WMD, which is a document distance metric that leverages word embeddings to compute the optimal transport cost between word distributions. The authors propose to learn a linear transformation A and a reweighted word distribution w to improve the label separation of documents. The linear transformation A is learned using the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) objective, which is a popular metric learning algorithm.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed approach and its components. The experimental results are extensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on several datasets. The authors also provide a thorough literature review, which helps to situate their work within the broader context of document distance metrics and metric learning.

However, there are some limitations and concerns with the paper. Firstly, the motivation for reweighting the word distribution w is not well-explained, and it is unclear how this reweighting improves label separation without altering the document semantics. The authors should provide more justification for this design choice and explain how it relates to the underlying semantics of the documents.

Secondly, the contribution of the paper is limited, as applying a linear transform and reweighting with an NCA objective is not seen as a significant novelty. The authors should provide more context and explanation for why this approach is necessary and how it improves upon existing methods.

Thirdly, there is a practical concern regarding how to compute the reweighted word distribution w for a completely new or unseen document. The authors should provide more guidance on how to handle this scenario and ensure that the proposed approach can be applied in practice.

Lastly, there is a terminology issue with the use of "semantic difference" in the abstract, which appears to mean label/topic/authorship rather than true semantics. The authors should clarify this terminology and ensure that it is consistent throughout the paper.

Overall, while the paper has some limitations and concerns, it is well-written and provides a clear presentation of a novel approach to learning a supervised document distance metric. With proper addressing of the above questions and concerns, the reviewer would not object to acceptance.
