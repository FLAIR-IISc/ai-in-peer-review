

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), extending the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance to a supervised metric learning framework for document classification. The authors introduce a linear transformation A of word embeddings and word importance weights w to learn a document distance that better aligns with class labels. The key technical contribution is an efficient gradient approximation using the Sinkhorn distance, enabling practical training while maintaining theoretical connections to the original WMD formulation. The method is evaluated across 8 document classification tasks, demonstrating consistent improvements over 26 baselines including WMD, WCD, and various document representations with supervised metric learning.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the literature on document distances, building upon Kusner et al.'s WMD (ICML 2015) and metric learning approaches like NCA. The connection to supervised learning is particularly relevant for practical document classification tasks where labeled data is available.

Strengths include the clear motivation for supervised metric learning, the elegant approximation technique for efficient training, and the comprehensive experimental evaluation across diverse datasets. The authors' careful clarifications during rebuttal demonstrated thorough understanding of the work.

However, several aspects require clarification. The intuition behind Eq. (5) (particularly the role of w) is unclear, and it's uncertain whether the improvements stem primarily from A or w. The paper claims S-WMD is "particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words" (ln316), but provides no justification for this claim. The reference to "average error across datasets" at ln257 cannot be located (Table 3 appears to show average rank, not error). Table 2's "n" vs "ne" columns need explanation, and the paper doesn't specify how k (for k-NN classification) was chosen. There's also confusion regarding whether the lambda in Eq. (2) matches the one referenced at ln170.

The quality is strong with sound methodology and thorough evaluation, though the missing details about k and the ambiguous claim about syntactic similarity weaken the argument. The clarity would be greatly improved by addressing these points. The originality is solid, building on WMD with a novel supervised extension. The significance is high, as document distances are crucial for many text applications, and the method shows consistent improvements over multiple baselines.

Overall, this is a strong paper with important contributions, but requires clarification on several key points before acceptance.
