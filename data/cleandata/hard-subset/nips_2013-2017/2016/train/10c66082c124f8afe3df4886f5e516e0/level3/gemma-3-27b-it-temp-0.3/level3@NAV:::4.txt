## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised document distance by learning a linear transformation *A* and a reweighted word distribution *w* on top of the existing Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) framework. The core idea is to minimize k-NN classification error by adapting the word embedding space and word importance, offering a way to incorporate supervision into the previously unsupervised WMD. The authors provide a thorough literature review, positioning their work within the context of metric learning and document distance measures.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The writing is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making the method easy to follow. The extensive experimental evaluation across eight datasets and comparison against 26 baselines is a significant undertaking and demonstrates a strong commitment to rigorous evaluation. The reported results consistently show S-WMD outperforming many baselines, suggesting a tangible benefit to the proposed approach. The authors also address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging the Sinkhorn distance for efficient gradient computation.

However, the paper also has notable weaknesses. The motivation for reweighting the word distribution *w* feels weak. While the authors suggest it allows for task-specific emphasis on words, the potential for fundamentally altering the document’s semantic meaning is not adequately addressed. A stronger justification is needed, along with analysis demonstrating that the reweighting preserves core document semantics while improving label separation. Furthermore, the contribution feels incremental. Applying a linear transform and reweighting with an NCA objective, while effective, doesn't represent a substantial novelty in the field of metric learning. A practical concern arises regarding the computation of *w* for completely new, unseen documents – the paper doesn’t offer a clear solution. Finally, the use of “semantic difference” in the abstract is ambiguous; it appears to refer to label/topic/authorship distinctions rather than true semantic changes.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Clear writing, extensive experiments, consistently strong performance, and a practical approach to incorporating supervision into WMD.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Limited novelty, weak motivation for reweighting, practical concerns regarding unseen documents, and ambiguous terminology.

Overall, this is a solid paper with a clear presentation and strong empirical results. However, addressing the concerns regarding the motivation for reweighting and the novelty of the approach would significantly strengthen the contribution. I would not object to acceptance if these issues are properly addressed.
