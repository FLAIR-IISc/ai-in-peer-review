This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the recent Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision into the document distance calculation. The authors address the limitation of WMD being entirely unsupervised by learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space and word-importance weights. This supervised training minimizes leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. A key contribution is an efficient approximation leveraging the Sinkhorn distance to address the computational complexity of the optimal transport problem inherent in WMD. The method is evaluated on eight real-world text classification tasks, demonstrating superior performance compared to 26 competitive baselines.

The work builds upon a considerable body of research in document distance metrics, tracing its roots from bag-of-words and TF-IDF representations to more sophisticated methods like LSI and LDA. It correctly positions WMD as a significant advancement by utilizing high-quality word embeddings and formulating distance as an optimal transport problem. The paper also appropriately contextualizes its approach within the broader field of metric learning, acknowledging existing techniques and their limitations, particularly in handling high-dimensional text data. The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of prior work, correctly citing relevant papers and situating their contribution within the existing literature.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. First, the technical presentation is exceptionally clear and detailed. The mathematical formulation is rigorous and well-explained, making the approach accessible to readers familiar with optimal transport and metric learning. The efficient approximation using the Sinkhorn distance is a significant practical contribution, making the supervised learning of WMD feasible. The extensive evaluation across eight datasets and comparison to 26 baselines provides strong empirical support for the effectiveness of S-WMD. The inclusion of ablation studies, such as the “S-WMD init.” results, further strengthens the analysis. The authors’ attention to optimization details, including the initialization strategy and batch gradient descent, also contributes to the practical value of the work.

However, the paper does have weaknesses. While the extension of WMD to a supervised setting is valuable, the novelty relative to the original WMD work itself is somewhat limited. The core idea of incorporating supervision into a distance metric is not entirely new, and the primary contribution lies in the efficient adaptation of WMD. The discussion regarding the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) feels tangential and, at times, confusing, and its relevance isn’t sufficiently justified. The reference to WCD seems more like an initial step and could be removed or better integrated into the motivation.

Critically, the choice and role of the entropic regularizer parameter, λ, are insufficiently discussed. The authors mention its influence but fail to provide sufficient guidance on selecting an appropriate value. Given that λ dramatically affects the behavior of the Sinkhorn distance – shifting the approximation between a true Wasserstein distance (large λ) and a faster, entropic approximation (small λ) – this is a significant omission. It's unclear whether the reported results were obtained using a value of λ that prioritizes accuracy (approaching the true WMD) or speed (leveraging the benefits of the Sinkhorn approximation). Most users opt for smaller lambda for speed, which results in drastically different behavior. This lack of clarity hinders reproducibility and limits the interpretability of the results.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical performance on a diverse set of datasets.
*   Clear and technically sound presentation.
*   Practical contribution through the efficient approximation using the Sinkhorn distance.
*   Thorough comparison with a large number of baselines.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty compared to the original WMD.
*   Insufficient discussion of the entropic regularizer parameter (λ) and its impact.
*   The relevance of WCD within the context of the paper is unclear.



Overall, this is a solid and well-executed paper. While the novelty is incremental, the authors make a significant contribution by adapting WMD to a supervised learning framework and providing a computationally efficient implementation. Addressing the concerns regarding the discussion of λ would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily focusing on clarifying the role and choice of λ and streamlining the discussion of WCD.
