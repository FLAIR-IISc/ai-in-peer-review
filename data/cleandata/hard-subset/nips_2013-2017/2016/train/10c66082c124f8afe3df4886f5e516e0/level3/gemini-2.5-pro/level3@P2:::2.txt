This paper proposes a supervised extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a recently introduced and powerful metric for measuring the distance between text documents. The original WMD leverages pre-trained word embeddings (like word2vec) to calculate the minimum "cost" to transport words from one document to another, but it is entirely unsupervised. This work, called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), addresses this limitation by learning a task-specific distance metric. The method learns two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation of the word embedding space to adjust word similarities, and (2) a vector of word-importance weights to re-scale word frequencies based on their relevance to the classification task.

The authors formulate the learning problem as minimizing a stochastic version of the leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor (kNN) classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Component Analysis (NCA). A key technical challenge is that computing the gradient of the loss function with respect to the learned parameters involves differentiating through the WMD optimization problem, which is computationally prohibitive and can lead to subgradient issues. The main contribution of this paper is a practical and efficient solution to this problem. The authors approximate the WMD with its entropy-regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance), which is strictly convex and differentiable, leading to an efficient and stable update rule. The optimization is further accelerated through a clever initialization scheme (using a supervised Word Centroid Distance) and a batch gradient descent strategy that considers only a local neighborhood of documents.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of the original WMD. While WMD provides excellent performance out-of-the-box, its unsupervised nature means it cannot adapt to the specifics of a given task (e.g., distinguishing topic vs. sentiment). This work provides a principled and effective method for incorporating supervision, which is a natural and valuable extension. Learning a domain-specific metric for a transport-based distance is a strong contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The identification of the gradient computation as the key bottleneck is astute, and the use of the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance as an approximation is an elegant and effective solution. The derivation of the gradients for both the linear transformation `A` and the word weights `w` is clearly explained. The entire optimization pipeline, including the initialization and batching strategy, is well-thought-out and makes the approach practical for real-world use.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear background on word2vec, WMD, the regularized transport problem, and NCA, which makes the paper self-contained. The motivation is well-established, and the proposed method is described with the appropriate level of detail.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical validation is a major strength of this work. The authors conduct a very extensive set of experiments on eight diverse document classification datasets. They compare S-WMD against an impressive suite of 26 baselines, including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with multiple metric learning algorithms (NCA, ITML, LMNN), as well as the unsupervised WMD and WCD. The results are compelling: S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art performance on the majority of datasets, often outperforming all other methods by a significant margin. The inclusion of visualizations and training time analysis further strengthens the experimental section.

**Weaknesses:**

This is a very strong paper with few weaknesses. The following are minor points for potential improvement:

1.  The authors note that S-WMD performs slightly worse than the unsupervised WMD on the CLASSIC dataset and suggest this might be due to the choice of the squared Euclidean distance as the ground metric. It would be interesting to briefly discuss the rationale for this choice (beyond differentiability) and whether the framework could be extended to learn metrics beyond the generalized Euclidean one.

2.  The approximation of WMD relies on the Sinkhorn distance, which has a regularization parameter `λ`. The authors set `λ=10`, noting it's a trade-off. A brief discussion or experiment showing the method's sensitivity to this hyperparameter would add completeness to the analysis.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, and the claims are very well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation.
*   **Clarity:** High. The paper is well-organized and clearly written.
*   **Originality:** High. The paper presents a novel and non-trivial extension to an important existing method, with a clever technical contribution to make the approach computationally feasible.
*   **Significance:** High. The strong empirical results demonstrate a clear advance in the state-of-the-art for kNN-based text classification. Given the success of WMD, this supervised variant is likely to be widely adopted and built upon by other researchers.

Overall, this is a solid and impressive paper that is well-suited for presentation at NIPS. It takes a successful, recently proposed method, identifies a key limitation, and proposes a technically sound, practical, and effective solution. The extensive and convincing experimental results seal this as a high-quality contribution to the field.
