Summary
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of WMD that learns a document metric by (i) applying a linear transformation A to the word embedding space and (ii) learning word-importance weights w that reweight document histograms. Training minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective (NCA-style) where pairwise distances are the (regularized) optimal transport cost between reweighted documents under the transformed embedding. To avoid nested, cubic-time differentiation through WMD, the authors use an entropically regularized transport (Sinkhorn distance), enabling O(q^2) computations and yielding approximate dual variables for gradients. Initialization is via NCA on Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD), followed by mini-batch training with nearest-neighbor pruning. Across 8 datasets and 26 baselines, S-WMD delivers strong kNN classification performance and competitive training times. Matlab code is promised.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on WMD (Kusner et al., ICML’15) and on entropic regularization of OT (Cuturi, NIPS’13). The learning objective follows the stochastic kNN approach of NCA (NIPS’05). Prior metric learning for EMD/OT (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; ITML-based ground-metric learning for images) generally learns a Mahalanobis metric in the ground space; this paper adapts that idea to text by transforming pre-trained word embeddings and additionally learning per-word histogram weights. Compared to standard text representation + metric learning (e.g., TF-IDF + LMNN/ITML/NCA), S-WMD operates in the word-embedding space and keeps the OT geometry that makes WMD effective.

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated idea: learning A and w aligns OT distances with labels while staying in a low-dimensional ground space.
- Solid engineering: Sinkhorn relaxation for fast gradients, NCA objective, practical batching and neighbor filtering lead to efficient training.
- Comprehensive evaluation: 8 datasets, 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised, with careful hyperparameter tuning via BO). Consistent gains on most datasets.
- Good initialization via S-WCD; ablation-like evidence that training improves over init on most tasks.
- Code release promised.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Ablation: It is unclear how much of the gain comes from A vs w. Please report an ablation (only A learned with w=1; only w learned with A=I; both) across datasets.
- Intuition for Eq. (5) and w: Provide more intuition and examples. Reweighting with w shifts probability mass toward discriminative words (after renormalization), so OT pays less to move uninformative words and more to align class-relevant words; a small illustrative two-document example would help.
- Use of entropic regularization (Eq. 2) and λ: Please clarify earlier that gradients always use the entropic relaxation; confirm that λ here is the same λ referenced later. You set λ=10; include a brief sensitivity analysis and discuss the trade-off between bias (smoother transport) and variance/efficiency.
- “Particularly forgiving for syntactically different but conceptually similar words”: Justify by noting that (i) WMD’s ground cost relies on word embeddings where semantically related words are close; (ii) learning A can further warp the space so class-relevant synonymy is emphasized; (iii) reweighted histograms w increase the influence of such words in the transport. A compact empirical illustration (e.g., nearest-neighbor qualitative examples) would strengthen the claim.
- Average error across datasets (ln257): I could not find a single “average error” number; Table 3 appears to be training times. If you meant “average rank across datasets,” please fix the pointer and/or add a proper average/median error summary with confidence intervals.
- Table 2 columns “n” vs “ne”: Please define these explicitly in the table caption and text. If “n” is dataset size and “ne” is standard error (or something else), make it unambiguous.
- k for kNN: State how k is chosen (per-dataset? per-method?), and whether it is tuned via the same BO protocol with a validation split. For fairness, k should be selected per method/dataset using only training/validation data.
- Constraints/regularization on w and A: Explicitly state constraints on w (non-negativity?) to preserve histogram semantics, and whether A is regularized (e.g., Frobenius penalty, rank r) to avoid overfitting. Discuss sensitivity to r.
- Minor clarity issues: Typos (e.g., “.2 Optimization” → “3.2”), equation references, and the explanation of when the relaxed dual variables (α*, β*) are used could be streamlined.

Quality
The method is technically sound. Using Sinkhorn for differentiable, efficient gradients is appropriate, and the dual-variable approximation is standard in entropic OT. The NCA objective is a reasonable surrogate for kNN LOO. Experimental protocol is generally strong (many baselines, BO tuning, multiple splits where needed), though ablation and sensitivity to λ and k should be added. Theoretical discussion about approximation error due to entropy (impact of λ on learned A,w) would be a plus.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized, but several notational and table/caption ambiguities should be fixed, and more intuition for w and the role of λ would help readers. The algorithmic flow is otherwise easy to follow.

Originality
Moderate but meaningful. The combination—learning a ground metric and per-word weights for WMD with NCA, using entropic OT for differentiable, efficient training—is novel in the NLP/WMD context, even if each component builds on known pieces.

Significance
High practical significance. Strong empirical results across diverse datasets suggest S-WMD is a robust, general-purpose document metric. The approach should be useful to practitioners and may stimulate further work on supervised OT for text.

Pros for acceptance
- Effective, general method with consistent empirical gains over strong baselines
- Elegant use of OT and metric learning with practical efficiency
- Relevance to NIPS topics (representation learning, optimal transport, NLP)
- Code release

Cons for acceptance
- Missing ablation (A vs w) and limited sensitivity analysis (λ, k, r)
- Some clarity issues in tables/references
- Focus only on kNN; although appropriate for metric learning, a brief note on transfer to other retrieval tasks would broaden impact

Questions for authors (to address in rebuttal/camera-ready)
- Provide A vs w ablation.
- Clarify λ usage and add sensitivity.
- Define “n” and “ne” in Table 2; fix the “average error” pointer.
- Specify how k is chosen.
- State constraints/regularization on w and A.
- Add a short intuitive example for w’s effect.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a well-motivated, empirically strong contribution that extends WMD with supervised learning in a principled and efficient way. With minor clarifications and an ablation, it would be a solid addition to the program. Confidence: high.
