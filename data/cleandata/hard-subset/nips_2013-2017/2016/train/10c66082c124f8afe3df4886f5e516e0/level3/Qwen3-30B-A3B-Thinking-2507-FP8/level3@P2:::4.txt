

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper extends the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) proposed by Kusner et al. (ICML 2015) to a supervised setting, introducing Supervised WMD (S-WMD). WMD computes document distances as the optimal transport cost between word embeddings, representing a significant advance over traditional bag-of-words approaches. The authors propose learning a linear transformation of the word embedding space (matrix A) and a word importance weight vector (w) to improve document classification performance. The method minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error using an efficient approximation of the WMD gradient, avoiding the computationally prohibitive nested optimization.

The paper is clearly written with a thorough literature review that properly contextualizes the work within metric learning and document distance methods. The authors effectively connect their approach to Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) while addressing the unique challenges of applying metric learning to the WMD framework. The experimental section provides comprehensive evaluation across eight text classification datasets, comparing against 26 baselines including traditional document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) and unsupervised variants (WCD, WMD).

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a strong technical contribution by addressing the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD through the Sinkhorn approximation, enabling practical training.
2. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating consistent improvement over unsupervised baselines and many supervised competitors across diverse text classification tasks.
3. The implementation details, including the efficient initialization via S-WCD and batch gradient descent with neighbor filtering, are well-explained and contribute to the method's practicality.
4. The clarity of writing and organization makes the paper accessible to the NIPS audience, with sufficient mathematical detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The motivation for the word importance weight vector (w) is underdeveloped. The paper fails to convincingly justify why reweighting word frequencies improves label separation without fundamentally altering document semantics. This could potentially transform a document into a different semantic entity.
2. The contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. Applying linear metric learning to the WMD framework with an NCA objective builds directly on existing work, and the novelty is somewhat limited.
3. The paper does not adequately address how to compute the word importance weights for completely new, unseen documents, which is a critical practical concern for real-world application.
4. The abstract and some sections use "semantic difference" ambiguously to refer to label/topic differences rather than true semantic meaning, which could confuse readers.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The method is technically sound and well-motivated for improving document classification performance.
- The extensive experimental validation across eight datasets provides strong evidence of the approach's effectiveness.
- The computational improvements make the supervised WMD practical for real-world applications.

*Con:*
- The contribution appears incremental rather than groundbreaking for the NIPS community.
- The motivation for the key component (word importance weights) is weak and requires significant clarification.
- The practical concern about applying the method to unseen documents is not addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a well-executed extension of the Word Mover's Distance to a supervised setting with compelling empirical results. While the method is technically sound and the experiments are thorough, the novelty is somewhat limited. The paper would be significantly stronger with a more convincing justification for the word importance weighting mechanism and a clear explanation of how to handle unseen documents. The current presentation leaves some important questions unanswered, particularly regarding the semantic interpretation of the reweighting operation.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of document representation and metric learning, but the novelty does not quite reach the level expected for acceptance at NIPS. If the authors can adequately address the concerns about the word importance weights and the practical application to unseen documents, this would become a much stronger paper. As it stands, it represents a good incremental improvement rather than a significant advancement in the field. I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing these concerns.
