This paper presents Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document metric based on the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD). WMD measures the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space, an instance of the Earth Mover's Distance. The key limitation of WMD is that the distance is entirely defined by the fixed word embeddings and is not adapted to any specific classification task.

The authors propose to overcome this by introducing supervision. S-WMD learns two components from labeled data: (1) a linear transformation (matrix A) of the word embedding space to make the word-level distances more relevant to the task, and (2) a word-importance vector (w) that re-weighs word frequencies to emphasize more discriminative words. The learning is framed as minimizing the stochastic leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor classification error, following the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) framework. A core technical contribution is the method for efficiently computing the gradient of the WMD. As differentiating through the exact WMD linear program is computationally prohibitive, the authors use the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance as a smooth, differentiable, and much faster approximation. The method is evaluated on eight text classification tasks, where it is shown to achieve state-of-the-art performance against a large suite of 26 baselines.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Strong Empirical Results:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of a popular and powerful document distance metric. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing S-WMD against an extensive set of baselines across eight datasets. The results are very strong, showing that S-WMD achieves the best average performance and sets a new state-of-the-art on the majority of the tested benchmarks. This demonstrates the practical value and effectiveness of the proposed method.

2.  **Technical Contribution for Tractability:** The main technical challenge is making the learning process feasible. The authors' proposal to use the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable proxy for the WMD is an elegant and effective solution. This allows for an efficient gradient-based optimization scheme that would otherwise be intractable, making the idea of supervising WMD a practical reality.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and well-organized. The background material on word embeddings, WMD, and NCA is explained concisely, and the derivation of the proposed S-WMD method and its gradients is presented in a thorough and easy-to-follow manner. This high level of clarity makes the work accessible and its contributions easy to appreciate.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Discussion of the `lambda` Hyperparameter:** The most significant weakness is the insufficient treatment of the entropy regularization hyperparameter, `lambda`. This parameter governs the trade-off between the exact Wasserstein distance (WMD) and the computationally efficient Sinkhorn distance. A large `lambda` approximates WMD more closely but is slow to converge, while a small `lambda` is fast but results in a qualitatively different, "blurrier" transport plan. The authors simply state that `lambda=10` provides a "nice trade-off" without any further justification, sensitivity analysis, or discussion of its impact on the results. This is a critical omission, as the behavior and performance of the algorithm are highly dependent on this choice. It leaves the reader wondering whether the reported success is due to supervising a metric that behaves like WMD or one that behaves more like the Sinkhorn distance, which has different properties. A more thorough analysis of this parameter is needed to strengthen the paper's technical claims and aid reproducibility.

2.  **Moderate Novelty:** While the engineering and results are impressive, the conceptual novelty is somewhat incremental. The work is a supervised extension of an existing method (WMD) using a standard metric learning objective (NCA). The ideas of learning a ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance and learning feature/word weights are not new in themselves. The primary novelty lies in the specific combination of these ideas for text documents and the use of the Sinkhorn approximation to make the gradient computation efficient.

3.  **Clarity on the Role of WCD:** The paper frequently refers to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD). It is used to initialize the matrix A and to find approximate nearest neighbors to speed up the batch gradient computation. While these are valid and clever implementation heuristics, the repeated emphasis on WCD can be confusing and may distract the reader from the main contribution, which is about supervising the WMD (via its Sinkhorn relaxation), not the WCD. The manuscript could be improved by more clearly positioning WCD as a supporting heuristic for efficient training, rather than a central component of the S-WMD model itself.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and powerful extension to the Word Mover's Distance. The strong empirical results and the clear presentation are major strengths. The primary weakness is the lack of analysis concerning the crucial `lambda` hyperparameter, which should be addressed to make the work technically more complete. Despite this, the significance of the results and the practical utility of the method make it a strong contribution to the field.
