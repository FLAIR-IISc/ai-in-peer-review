## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

This paper presents a compelling extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) to a supervised learning setting, termed Supervised-WMD (S-WMD). The core idea – learning a linear transformation *A* and word importance weights *w* to minimize k-NN classification error – is well-motivated, building upon the success of WMD and the broader field of metric learning. The authors effectively address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into the WMD framework through a relaxed optimization approach using the Sinkhorn distance. The promise of releasing Matlab code is a significant positive aspect, fostering reproducibility and further research.

The strengths of this work lie in its technical soundness and strong empirical results. The authors demonstrate consistent performance improvements across eight datasets and against a comprehensive set of 26 baselines. The ablation study, showing the value of both the linear transformation and word weighting, is particularly insightful. The visualizations (Figure 1) provide intuitive support for the learned distance metric. The authors also responded thoughtfully to questions in the rebuttal, clarifying several points.

However, some areas require further clarification. While the authors mention that S-WMD is “particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words” (line 316), the mechanism behind this remains unclear. A more detailed explanation, perhaps with examples, would strengthen this claim. Furthermore, it would be valuable to understand which component – *A* or *w* – contributes more significantly to the observed improvements. 

I also encountered some minor inconsistencies. The usage of lambda in Eq (2) and line 170 is unclear – are they the same? Additionally, the “average error across datasets” mentioned in line 257 is not readily found in Table 3. Finally, Table 2’s columns “n” and “ne” require explanation. The description of *k* selection for k-NN classification is also missing; is it dataset-dependent, method-dependent, or cross-validated?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results consistently outperforming numerous baselines.
*   Technically sound approach leveraging a well-established metric (WMD) and addressing computational challenges effectively.
*   Clear writing and thoughtful responses to reviewer questions.
*   Promise of code release.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed explanation regarding the mechanism behind the method’s robustness to syntactic variations.
*   Minor inconsistencies and missing details regarding experimental setup (lambda, Table 2 columns, k selection).



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. Addressing the minor clarifications requested would further enhance its quality and solidify its contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
