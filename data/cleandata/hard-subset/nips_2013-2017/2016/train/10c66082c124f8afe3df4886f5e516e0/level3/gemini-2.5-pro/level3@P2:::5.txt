This paper proposes the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document distance metric. This work builds directly upon the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by Kusner et al. (2015), which frames the distance between two documents as an optimal transport problem between their respective word embeddings. While WMD is entirely unsupervised, S-WMD introduces supervision by learning two components: a linear transformation of the underlying word embedding space and a vector of word-importance weights. The learning objective is to minimize a stochastic version of the k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) leave-one-out classification error, inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A key technical contribution is a method to make this optimization computationally feasible. As the gradient of the WMD is intractable, the authors substitute the exact optimal transport problem with its entropy-regularized version, also known as the Sinkhorn distance (Cuturi, 2013). This yields a differentiable and efficient approximation, enabling gradient-based optimization. The proposed method is evaluated on eight text classification tasks, where it demonstrates state-of-the-art performance, outperforming an extensive set of 26 competitive baselines on average.

**Strengths and Weaknesses (Qualitative Evaluation)**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The motivation for using the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable proxy for WMD is well-founded, and the derivation of the gradients for both the linear transformation and the word weights appears correct. The empirical evaluation is a significant strength; it is comprehensive, comparing S-WMD against a wide array of unsupervised and supervised methods on eight diverse datasets. The results are compelling, showing that S-WMD achieves the lowest average kNN error across all tasks.

However, a notable weakness is the lack of discussion and analysis regarding the crucial hyperparameter λ, the entropic regularization coefficient. The authors state that λ=10 is used as a "nice trade-off between speed and approximation accuracy," but this choice is not justified further. The value of λ can have a dramatic effect on the behavior of the transport problem. A large λ makes the regularized problem closer to the original WMD, while a small λ results in a much smoother objective that converges faster but may yield a very different, dense transport plan. The paper would be substantially stronger with a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with λ, which would provide insight into whether the method is operating closer to a true Wasserstein distance or in a highly regularized Sinkhorn regime.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and well-organized. The background section effectively introduces the necessary concepts (word2vec, WMD, Sinkhorn distance, NCA), and the proposed method is described with detailed mathematical exposition. The logic flows well from the problem formulation to the final algorithm. The inclusion of visualizations in Figure 1 and Figure 2 adds to the paper's clarity, effectively illustrating the impact of the learned metric and the nature of the learned word weights.

One minor point of confusion is the repeated reference to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD). While its use for initialization and neighbor filtering is a practical choice for efficiency, its role feels somewhat disconnected from the core method. A clearer motivation for why an NCA-trained metric on word centroids is a suitable starting point for the full S-WMD optimization would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The work represents a novel and logical combination of existing techniques to solve an important problem. While the core components (WMD, NCA-style loss, Sinkhorn distance) are not new, their synthesis into a supervised document metric learning framework is original. The idea of jointly learning a transformation on the embedding space and re-weighting the document histograms is a valuable contribution. However, the overall novelty is somewhat incremental, as it is a natural supervised extension of the WMD paper. Similar ideas of learning a ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance have been explored in other domains, such as computer vision (e.g., Wang et al., 2014), and the paper could do more to position itself relative to that line of work.

**Significance:** The results presented are highly significant. WMD established itself as a state-of-the-art unsupervised document distance. This paper convincingly demonstrates that incorporating supervision into this framework can yield substantial further improvements, setting a new state of the art for kNN-based text classification. Given the strong performance, the clarity of the presentation, and the availability of code, the S-WMD method is likely to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners as a powerful tool and a strong baseline for future work in supervised document similarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of learning a supervised document distance.
*   **Pro:** It proposes an effective and computationally feasible solution by cleverly combining WMD with an NCA loss and using the Sinkhorn distance as an efficient, differentiable proxy.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Pro:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and the results are state-of-the-art, demonstrating clear superiority over a large number of strong baselines.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The methodological treatment of the crucial entropic regularization parameter λ is superficial, lacking justification or sensitivity analysis.
*   **Con:** The novelty is somewhat incremental, as it is a well-executed but fairly direct extension of prior work.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**
My primary concern is the handling of the regularization parameter λ. Could the authors please provide more justification for the choice of λ=10? An ablation study showing the effect of λ on both training time and final classification error across a range of values would significantly strengthen the paper and provide valuable guidance for practitioners. It would clarify whether the strong performance relies on being very close to the original WMD (large λ) or on the properties of the entropic regularization itself (small λ).
