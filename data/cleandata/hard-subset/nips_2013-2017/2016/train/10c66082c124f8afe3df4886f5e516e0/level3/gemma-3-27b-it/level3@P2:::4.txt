This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised document distance based on the recent Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) [Kusner et al., 2016]. The authors address the limitation of WMD being entirely unsupervised by learning an affine transformation of the word embedding space (matrix A) and a word-importance weight vector (w). The objective is to minimize leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error, utilizing a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance [Cuturi, 2013]) to enable efficient gradient computation. The authors demonstrate strong performance on eight real-world text classification tasks, consistently outperforming 26 competitive baselines.

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in document distance metrics, starting from bag-of-words and TF-IDF representations [Salton & Buckley, 1988] and progressing through LSI [Deerwester et al., 1999] and LDA [Blei et al., 2003]. It appropriately positions itself within the context of recent advancements leveraging word embeddings, particularly WMD, and metric learning [Weinberger & Saul, 2009; Davis et al., 2007]. The thorough review of related work, including relevant techniques like Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) [Goldberger et al., 2005] which inspires their approach, is a strength of the paper.

**Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented. The motivation is well-defined, and the technical details are explained in a logical and accessible manner. The proposed method is conceptually sound, building upon existing techniques in a sensible way. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using a diverse set of datasets and comparing against a large number of baselines. The reported results are compelling, demonstrating consistent improvements over existing methods. The authors also provide a practical discussion of computational complexity and optimization strategies, making the method more readily applicable. The release of code further enhances the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:** While the technical execution is strong, the justification for the word-importance reweighting (w) feels somewhat weak. The paper acknowledges that this reweighting can alter the document representation, but doesn't fully address the potential impact on document semantics. There's a lack of detailed explanation on *why* certain words should be emphasized for a given classification task, and whether this reweighting might inadvertently distort the underlying meaning of the documents. While the experimental results support the approach, a deeper understanding of *how* the word weights contribute to improved performance would be valuable. Further, it is not immediately clear how to compute 'w' for a completely new document not seen during training. The contribution, while incremental, is potentially limited; applying a linear transform and reweighting within an NCA objective, while effective, isn't necessarily a fundamentally novel approach. Finally, the abstract’s use of “semantic difference” is ambiguous – it appears to refer to label/topic/authorship distinctions rather than inherent semantic changes within the text.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the underlying concepts and provide a rigorous analysis of their method. The experimental validation is strong, with statistically significant results reported across multiple datasets. The gradient approximation is a key contribution, enabling the efficient training of the model. The work appears complete and well-polished.

**Clarity:** The paper is remarkably clear and easy to follow. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are concise and informative. The organization is logical, progressing smoothly from background material to the proposed method and experimental results.

**Originality:** The originality lies in the combination of WMD with supervised metric learning, specifically learning both a transformation of the word embedding space *and* word-importance weights. While the individual components are not novel, the integration within the WMD framework and the efficient optimization strategy represent a valuable contribution.

**Significance:** The results are significant, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on several text classification tasks. The method has the potential to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners working in areas such as text retrieval, document classification, and information retrieval. The work advances the state-of-the-art by providing a more accurate and flexible document distance metric.



**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Strong empirical results across multiple datasets.
*   Clear and well-written paper with a thorough literature review.
*   Efficient implementation and practical optimization strategies.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in text analysis.
*   Code release promotes reproducibility and adoption.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Weak justification for the word-importance reweighting and its impact on semantics.
*   Incremental contribution – combining existing techniques.
*   Unclear procedure for applying the method to entirely new documents.
*   Ambiguous terminology in the abstract.



**Overall Assessment:** I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. While the concerns regarding the justification for word reweighting and the incremental nature of the contribution are valid, they do not fundamentally detract from the value of the work. I would not object to acceptance provided the authors address these points adequately in a revision, particularly by providing more intuition and empirical evidence supporting the effectiveness of the word-importance weighting and clarifying its potential impact on document semantics.
