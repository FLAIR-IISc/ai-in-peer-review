## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

This paper proposes a supervised extension to the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD), learning weights on the distance function itself – a linear transformation of the word embedding space and word importance weights – to improve performance on document classification tasks. The core idea is sound and directly addresses a limitation of the original WMD, which is inherently unsupervised. The authors effectively leverage the recent advancements in efficient optimal transport computation (Sinkhorn distance) to make the proposed Supervised-WMD (S-WMD) practical and scalable. 

The paper is very well-written and clearly explains the technical details with appropriate depth. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the method is logically presented, building from the foundation of WMD and metric learning. The authors carefully address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into the WMD framework and offer a clever approximation based on the Sinkhorn distance. The experimental evaluation is exceptionally comprehensive. The authors evaluate S-WMD on eight real-world text classification datasets, comparing it against a vast array of 26 baselines, including various document representations and combinations with established metric learning techniques. The results consistently demonstrate that S-WMD significantly outperforms all baselines, often by a substantial margin. 

It’s particularly pleasing to see someone applying discriminative training to learn a domain-specific metric from WMD. This work builds nicely on Kusner et al.’s initial contribution and addresses a critical next step in leveraging the power of word embeddings for document analysis. The inclusion of a visualization component (t-SNE embeddings) further strengthens the empirical validation and provides intuitive insight into the learned representations. The authors also thoughtfully discuss limitations and avenues for future work, demonstrating a careful and honest assessment of their contributions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** Consistently outperforms a large number of competitive baselines.
*   **Sound Methodology:** The proposed approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and effectively addresses the challenges of supervised WMD learning.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Extensive experimentation across diverse datasets and baselines.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, organized, and easy to understand.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:** 

*   While extensive, the experiments primarily focus on kNN classification. Exploring the performance of S-WMD in other downstream tasks (e.g., information retrieval, document clustering) would further strengthen the paper.



Overall, this is a solid and significant contribution to the field. The combination of a novel and well-motivated approach, a rigorous experimental evaluation, and a clear presentation makes this paper highly suitable for presentation at NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
