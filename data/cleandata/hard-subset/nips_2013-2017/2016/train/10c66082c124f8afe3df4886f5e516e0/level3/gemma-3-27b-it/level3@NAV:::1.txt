## Review of Supervised Word Mover’s Distance

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised metric built on the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear projection of the word embedding space alongside document-specific word re-weighting, optimizing via an efficient approximation to the gradient. The authors demonstrate strong kNN classification performance on several text datasets, outperforming a large number of baselines.

The work builds upon the recent success of WMD by addressing its inherent unsupervised nature. This is a natural and valuable extension, as incorporating supervision can significantly improve performance in downstream tasks. The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, presenting a clear explanation of the method and its theoretical underpinnings. The extensive experimental evaluation, including comparisons to 26 baselines, is commendable and provides strong evidence of the method's effectiveness. The focus on efficient optimization, especially the use of the Sinkhorn distance, is a crucial contribution, making the method practical for larger datasets. 

However, the impact of the full proposed learning process is somewhat diminished by the observation in Table 3: EMD with metric learning initialized via a "word centroid distance" performs similarly. This raises the question of whether the complexity of learning both the projection matrix *A* and word weights is fully justified. Furthermore, the paper lacks a comparison to existing metric learning methods (e.g., ITML, LMNN, NCA) applied *directly* to word-centroid document embeddings. This baseline would provide a clearer understanding of the benefit derived from using WMD as opposed to simpler document representations with powerful metric learning algorithms. 

Finally, an ablation study is missing. Isolating the contributions of the word-specific weights versus the projection matrix *A* would help clarify which component is driving the performance gains. While the optimization approach (lines 156–175) is indeed a notable contribution and a clever solution to a challenging problem, the overall originality lies primarily in this optimization aspect, rather than a fundamentally new approach to metric learning within EMD. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results on multiple datasets.
*   Well-written and clear presentation.
*   Efficient optimization makes the method practical.
*   Interesting extension of WMD with a clear motivation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited performance gain over a simpler baseline (S-WCD).
*   Missing baseline comparison with standard metric learning methods on word-centroid embeddings.
*   Lack of ablation study to isolate the contributions of different components.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Addressing the missing baselines and ablation study would significantly strengthen the paper and clarify the contribution of S-WMD. The minor clarifications regarding references and equation notation should also be addressed.
