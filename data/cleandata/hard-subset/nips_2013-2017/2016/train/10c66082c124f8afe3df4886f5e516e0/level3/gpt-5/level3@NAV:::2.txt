This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a discriminatively trained extension of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns both an affine transformation of the word embedding space and a word-importance vector. The learning objective follows the NCA-style stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss, while gradients through the optimal transport layer are made practical via Cuturi’s entropy-regularized (Sinkhorn) relaxation and corresponding dual variables. The authors further accelerate training with an S-WCD (supervised centroid) initialization, batch optimization, and neighbor truncation. Empirically, S-WMD is evaluated on eight classification datasets against 26 baselines (including WMD, TF-IDF/LSI/LDA/mSDA with and without metric learning via NCA/LMNN/ITML) and achieves the best average rank, often by a large margin. This work sits at the intersection of WMD/OT for document distances [Kusner et al.; Cuturi], classic metric learning (NCA/LMNN/ITML), and supervised variants of EMD in vision [Wang & Guibas], advancing them by learning a task-specific ground metric in the embedding space jointly with histogram reweighting.

Quality: The method is technically sound, with clear derivations of approximate gradients for A and w via Sinkhorn primal/dual solutions. The experimental evaluation is extensive, with careful baselines and reasonable engineering choices; code availability is a plus. Some analyses could be deepened (see below), but claims are well supported.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized, with sufficient detail to reproduce the approach, including complexity analysis and training heuristics.

Originality: While building on established ingredients (WMD/OT, NCA), the combination—discriminatively learning both the embedding-space metric and word weights for WMD—is novel and well motivated.

Significance: Results are strong across diverse datasets, showing consistent gains over a wide suite of baselines. The approach is likely to be useful to practitioners seeking domain-specific document distances.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Provide ablations isolating the contributions of A vs. w, and sensitivity to λ, neighbor set size N, and initialization.
- Discuss memory/runtime scaling for long documents (T and K are O(p2)); report peak memory.
- Consider regularization on w (e.g., sparsity or boundedness) to mitigate overfitting; report learned weight distributions.
- Clarify robustness to different embeddings (e.g., GloVe/fastText) and to k in kNN.
- Add statistical significance tests on key improvements and comment on the transductive setting for LDA.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, consistent empirical gains over many strong baselines
- Sound method with clear exposition and practical efficiency
- Bridges OT/WMD with supervised metric learning elegantly
- Relevance and likely impact for document similarity and retrieval

Cons:
- Limited ablation/sensitivity analysis
- Scalability concerns for very long documents not fully quantified
- Focused on kNN; limited exploration of downstream tasks

Overall: A solid, well-executed paper suitable for presentation at NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
