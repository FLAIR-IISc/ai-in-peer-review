This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), which learns a task-specific document distance atop WMD by optimizing a linear transform A in the word embedding space and a global word-importance vector w, using an NCA-style stochastic leave-one-out objective. To make training practical, the authors replace exact WMD with an entropy-regularized Sinkhorn relaxation and derive efficient gradients via the primal transport T* and dual potentials (α, β) obtained from the Sinkhorn scaling vectors. The work relates to WMD (Kusner et al.), Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi), and supervised metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML), as well as supervised EMD ground-metric learning in vision (e.g., Wang & Guibas). The contribution is an instantiation of these ideas for document distances with an efficient training pipeline and extensive evaluation.

Quality: The method is technically sound and the gradient approximations are reasonable; the experiments are broad (8 datasets, 26 baselines) and generally support the claims, with code available. However, I miss an ablation isolating A vs w, sensitivity to λ, and a clear inference description.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well organized, and contains a thorough literature review. The notion of “semantic difference” in the abstract is ambiguous; it appears to mean supervised label/topic/author similarity rather than semantics per se.

Originality: Moderate. Learning a linear ground metric with an NCA objective and using Sinkhorn for efficiency are known; learning a global reweighting over words within WMD is a natural but incremental extension. The combination is well executed.

Significance: Empirical gains are consistent and sometimes large; the approach is practical and likely useful for kNN-style retrieval/classification. The impact would be stronger with clearer justification and guidance around w.

Main weaknesses/questions:
- Motivation and semantics of reweighting w are underdeveloped. Since w rescales the empirical word histogram, it can, in principle, alter the document distribution. Please justify why this improves label separation without compromising document semantics, and consider regularizing w toward uniform (e.g., KL to 1, positivity and box constraints) with an ablation.
- Inference for unseen documents: is w global and fixed at test time (applied to any histogram), and how are OOV words handled? Please make the pipeline explicit.
- Provide ablations: (i) A-only (w=1), (ii) w-only (A=I), (iii) combined; and report training/inference costs and λ sensitivity.
- Terminology: replace “semantic difference” with “label-informed similarity” or similar.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, well-executed method with efficient training via Sinkhorn.
- Strong empirical performance across diverse datasets; code released.
- Useful, practical extension of WMD.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited novelty; core components are known.
- Weak theoretical/empirical justification for reweighting; missing ablations and inference clarity.

I would not object to acceptance if the above issues—especially the role and deployment of w—are addressed.
