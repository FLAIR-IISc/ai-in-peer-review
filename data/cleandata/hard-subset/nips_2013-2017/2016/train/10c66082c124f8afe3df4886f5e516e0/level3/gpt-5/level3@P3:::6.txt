Summary and relation to prior work
This paper extends Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) to a supervised setting by learning (i) a linear transform A of the pre-trained word embedding space and (ii) a global word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The supervised distance DA,w is optimized with an NCA-style stochastic leave-one-out kNN objective. To make training tractable, the paper replaces exact optimal transport with the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn relaxation (Eq. 2), enabling efficient gradients via the relaxed primal/dual solutions. The method, S-WMD, is evaluated on eight text classification datasets against 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised, including NCA/LMNN/ITML on various representations). The approach relates to metric learning (NCA/LMNN/ITML), supervised EMD ground-metric learning in vision, and the original WMD. The novelty lies in learning a low-dimensional ground metric directly in word-embedding space plus a discriminative global reweighting over words, trained end-to-end with a Sinkhorn-based approximation.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated extension of WMD with supervision; elegant use of A and w to tailor the ground metric and the document mass distribution.
- Sound optimization strategy: NCA-style objective with efficient, scalable Sinkhorn-based gradients and smart neighbor filtering.
- Strong empirical performance across many datasets; visualizations and learned word-weight cloud add interpretability.
- Practical contribution: code promised/released in Matlab; training times reported; initialization via S-WCD is sensible.
- Authors’ rebuttal was careful and helpful; many clarifications improved my understanding.

Weaknesses and questions (requests for clarification)
- Intuition for w in Eq. (5) and its usage: Please expand the intuition. As defined, d̃ = (w ◦ d)/(w>d) rescales each word’s mass globally and renormalizes the histogram, akin to a discriminatively learned, task-specific IDF. It downweights ubiquitous/uninformative words and upweights class-discriminative ones so that the transport focuses on salient mass. Stating this explicitly (and contrasting with static IDF) would help readers.
- Which component drives improvements (A vs w): Please add an ablation: (1) A-only (fix w=1), (2) w-only (fix A=I), (3) both. This will clarify how much gain comes from reweighting vs ground-metric adaptation, and whether they are complementary across datasets.
- When is Eq. (2) used, and is λ the same as at ln170? My reading is that Eq. (2) (Sinkhorn) is used during training to compute an approximate DA,w and its gradients via relaxed T*, α*, β*, and you set λ=10 (ln170) for the relaxation. Please clarify: (a) do you also use the Sinkhorn relaxation at test time, or revert to exact WMD with A,w? (b) is there any other λ in the paper (e.g., a regularizer) or are all mentions of λ the Sinkhorn smoothness? (c) is λ tuned or fixed across datasets?
- Justifying the claim at ln316 (“particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words”): This is intuitive for WMD because mass can move between semantically close word vectors, and S-WMD should amplify this by learning A to shrink meaningful directions and w to suppress uninformative mass. Please add quantitative evidence: e.g., a controlled experiment that replaces words with synonyms/paraphrases and shows S-WMD degrades less than baselines; or an analysis of transport mass between synonym sets before vs after training A.
- “Average error across datasets” at ln257: I suspect you meant “average rank across datasets,” which you note is shown on the right of Table 2. Please correct the wording and/or point precisely to the column. Table 3 appears to be training times, so it likely does not contain average error.
- Table 2 columns “n” vs “ne”: I could not infer these column meanings from the text. Please define them clearly in the caption or main text.
- Choice of k for kNN: How is k selected? Is it tuned per dataset (e.g., via validation/BO) and then fixed across methods, or tuned per method? Please state the selection protocol and whether k is method-dependent.
- Sensitivity/robustness: Please include sensitivity to λ, the neighbor-filter size N and batch size B, and to the choice of squared vs unsquared Euclidean in the ground cost; and report whether performance saturates with A’s output dimension r.
- Approximation accuracy: You argue λ=10 offers a good speed/accuracy trade-off. A small plot showing correlation between Sinkhorn DA,w and exact WMD under the learned A,w on a subset would bolster confidence in the relaxation during training.
- Evaluation scope: The current focus is kNN classification; your rebuttal clarified prior concerns about comparing to simple classifiers, and I’m satisfied that those comparisons were not the point of the paper. For completeness, a retrieval task (nearest-neighbor document search) would further showcase the learned distance.

Quality
- The method is technically sound; the use of relaxed primal/dual variables for gradients is standard in OT with entropy, and you note subgradient caveats. The optimization and complexity analysis are reasonable. Empirical results are thorough with many baselines and BO hyperparameter tuning.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The requested clarifications (w intuition, λ/Eq. 2 usage, table labels, “average error” phrasing, k-selection) would further improve readability. Please also make explicit whether you use the relaxed or exact distance at test time.

Originality
- A novel and well-integrated combination: supervised learning of both a word-level ground metric and global word weights for WMD using an NCA objective and Sinkhorn approximations. Distinct from prior supervised EMD work in vision by operating in word-embedding space and adding learned histogram reweighting.

Significance
- Likely to be useful for practitioners who rely on distance-based document retrieval/classification; the approach advances state of the art across diverse datasets, with interpretable learned weights and a practical training recipe.

Pros
- Strong empirical gains across many baselines
- Efficient and principled optimization with Sinkhorn
- Interpretable components (A, w) and nice visualizations
- Code release

Cons
- Missing ablations (A vs w), sensitivity to λ/N/B
- A few clarity issues (λ/Eq. 2 use, table labels, k selection)
- Evidence for “forgiving” claim could be made quantitative

Overall assessment
A solid, well-executed contribution that meaningfully advances supervised metric learning for documents by building on WMD with learnable A and w. With the requested clarifications and ablations, I would be in favor of acceptance.
