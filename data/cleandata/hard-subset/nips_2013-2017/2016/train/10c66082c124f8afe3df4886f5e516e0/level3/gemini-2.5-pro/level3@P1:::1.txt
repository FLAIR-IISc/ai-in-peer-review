### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a task-specific document metric based on the recently proposed Word Mover's Distance (WMD). WMD computes the distance between two documents as the minimum cost to "transport" the words of one document to the other in a pre-trained word embedding space. While effective, WMD is entirely unsupervised. S-WMD extends this by incorporating supervision to learn a metric that is better suited for a specific classification task.

The proposed method learns two components simultaneously: (1) a linear transformation (matrix A) of the word embedding space to adjust word similarities, and (2) a word-importance vector (w) to re-weight word frequencies in documents. The learning objective is to minimize a stochastic version of the leave-one-out k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) classification error, following the framework of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A key technical contribution is an efficient method for computing the gradient of the objective function. Since differentiating through the WMD's optimal transport problem is computationally prohibitive, the authors propose using an entropy-regularized version (the Sinkhorn distance) to obtain a smooth and differentiable approximation, leading to a practical optimization algorithm. The method is evaluated on eight text classification datasets, where it generally outperforms a wide range of unsupervised and supervised baselines in kNN classification.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The paper's quality, clarity, originality, and significance are evaluated below.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The primary technical contribution—the derivation of an efficient gradient for the WMD-based loss function using an entropy-regularized relaxation—is clever and well-justified. This successfully addresses the main obstacle to creating a supervised WMD. The experimental evaluation is extensive, comparing against 26 baselines across 8 datasets, and the reported results demonstrate state-of-the-art performance for kNN-based document classification.

However, the experimental analysis has two notable weaknesses:
1.  **Missing Ablation Study:** The model learns both a projection matrix `A` and a word-weighting vector `w`. The paper does not provide an ablation study to disentangle the contributions of these two components. It would be highly informative to see experiments where only `A` is learned (with `w` fixed to ones) and where only `w` is learned (with `A` fixed to identity). This would provide valuable insight into which aspect of the learned metric is more crucial for the performance gains.
2.  **Incomplete Baselines:** While the authors compare against a very large set of methods, a direct comparison is missing. They apply NCA to the Word Centroid Distance (WCD) to initialize their model, calling this S-WCD. However, they do not report results for applying other strong metric learners like LMNN and ITML to the WCD representation, even though these learners are used on other baseline representations (e.g., TF-IDF, LSI). Including these would provide a more complete picture of how S-WMD compares to applying standard metric learning techniques to a simple word-embedding-based document representation.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the background on WMD and NCA is concise and helpful, and the proposed method is explained logically. The authors do an excellent job of detailing the technical challenge (differentiating through WMD) and their proposed solution. The figures and tables are informative and support the paper's claims effectively.

**Originality:**
The idea of learning a supervised metric for document comparison is not new, nor is the idea of learning a ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD), as the authors acknowledge (e.g., Wang et al. [35]). The novelty of this work lies in:
1.  The specific application of metric learning to the Word Mover's Distance for text.
2.  The joint learning of a linear transformation and word-importance weights within this framework.
3.  The efficient optimization scheme based on the Sinkhorn distance approximation, which is the paper's main technical innovation.
While the contribution is more of an elegant and effective combination of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new paradigm, the formulation and its application to document classification are novel and interesting.

**Significance:**
The work addresses a significant problem: adapting the powerful WMD framework to supervised settings. The reported results suggest that S-WMD is a state-of-the-art method for kNN-based text classification.

However, the paper's main weakness lies in the practical significance of its core technical contribution. Table 2 shows that the performance of the full S-WMD model is only marginally better than its initialization scheme, "S-WMD init." (which is S-WCD, i.e., NCA applied to the word centroid distance). Across the eight datasets, the final model offers minimal, and in one case even worse, performance compared to the initialization. For instance, the error on BBCSPORT and OHSUMED is identical (3.6 and 40.5), and the improvement on other datasets is often just 0.1-0.2 percentage points. This finding significantly undermines the impact of the proposed complex optimization procedure. If a much simpler approach (applying NCA to document centroids) yields nearly identical results, the additional complexity of optimizing through the relaxed WMD seems hard to justify. The authors should address this point directly, as it raises questions about the value of the main contribution beyond the strong initialization scheme.

### Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance

**Pro:**
*   Proposes a novel and principled method for supervising the powerful Word Mover's Distance.
*   The technical approach for enabling efficient optimization is clever and sound.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on kNN classification across numerous datasets and a wide array of competitive baselines.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Con:**
*   The primary weakness is that the full, complex S-WMD optimization yields only marginal performance gains over its much simpler initialization procedure (S-WCD). This questions the practical necessity of the main technical contribution.
*   The lack of an ablation study makes it difficult to understand the relative importance of the learned projection matrix vs. the word weights.
