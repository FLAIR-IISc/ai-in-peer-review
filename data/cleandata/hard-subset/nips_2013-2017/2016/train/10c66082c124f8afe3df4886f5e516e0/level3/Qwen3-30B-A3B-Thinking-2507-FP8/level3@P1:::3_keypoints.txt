- Proposal: Introduces Supervised WMD (S-WMD) that incorporates supervision into Word Mover’s Distance to improve document classification by learning a metric and word importance weights.
- Formulation: Frames the method as learning a generalized Euclidean metric in embedding space while reweighting document histograms with word-specific importance.
- Objective: Directly minimizes stochastic leave-one-out classification error under the WMD framework.
- Efficiency: Provides an approximate WMD gradient using Sinkhorn regularization to avoid the original cubic-time nested optimization.
- Optimization: Efficiently learns a linear transform A and a word importance vector w via the Sinkhorn-based approximation.
- Empirical performance: Evaluated on eight datasets and outperforms 26 baselines, achieving lowest test error on 5/8 datasets and best average performance.
- Experimental rigor: Strong experimental design with multiple train/test splits, statistical significance reporting, and detailed ablation studies.
- Interpretability: Learned word weights are meaningful and highlight class-relevant words (demonstrated on 20NEWS).
- Clarity issue: Notational inconsistencies (Euclidean vs squared Euclidean) conflict with p-Wasserstein interpretation; recommend explicitly describing WMD as p-Wasserstein.
- Presentation suggestion: Algorithm 1 is fragmented; include a clear algorithmic box summarizing the S-WMD procedure.
- Originality: Moderate—novel combination of existing techniques (WMD, metric learning, Sinkhorn), but core ideas like histogram reweighting have prior work.
- Prior literature: Needs a more thorough discussion of related work on histogram reweighting (Lebanon, Cuturi, etc.) and clearer distinction from Wang et al. (ECCV 2015) on EMD learning.
- Regularization concern: Should rigorously discuss how Sinkhorn regularization vs unregularized WMD affects the learned metric and final performance.
- Weight analysis request: Provide more insight into how learned weights vary across datasets and classes and their empirical impact.
- Strengths summary: Compelling empirical results, efficient implementation, clear experiments, and practical applicability.
- Weaknesses summary: Notational inconsistencies, insufficient prior-work discussion, and inadequate treatment of regularized vs unregularized OT.
- Recommendation: Recommend acceptance based on strong empirical validation, practical effectiveness, and significance to text representation learning.
