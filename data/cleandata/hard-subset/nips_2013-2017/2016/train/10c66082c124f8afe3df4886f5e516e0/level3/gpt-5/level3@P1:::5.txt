This paper proposes a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparison. The method, Supervised WMD (S-WMD), learns (i) a linear transformation A of the underlying word embedding (e.g., word2vec) to adapt the ground cost and (ii) a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights bag-of-words histograms before transport. Supervision is injected by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out kNN loss, in the style of NCA, over pairwise document distances computed via optimal transport. Because differentiating through exact WMD is computationally prohibitive, the paper uses the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance to obtain a unique and efficient transport plan and its dual potentials, which in turn give practical gradients for A and w. Training is accelerated further by (a) initializing A via NCA on Word Centroid Distance (WCD) representations (S-WCD), and (b) restricting neighborhood computations using WCD to preselect candidate neighbors. Experiments on eight datasets against 26 baselines show S-WMD achieves the best average rank and state-of-the-art performance on most datasets, with competitive training times and informative visualizations.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds directly on WMD (Kusner et al., ICML 2015) and on Sinkhorn regularization for OT (Cuturi, NIPS 2013). The learning objective borrows from metric learning, especially NCA (NIPS 2005) and is related to supervised learning of EMD ground metrics in computer vision (e.g., Wang & Guibas, ECCV 2009). The contribution is to adapt these ideas to learned document distances by simultaneously learning a ground metric in embedding space and term weights, with an efficient training strategy.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The derivations for gradients w.r.t. A (via the transport plan) and w (via dual potentials) are standard and plausible under the entropy-regularized OT approximation. Using Sinkhorn scaling to approximate both the primal plan and dual potentials is a practical and elegant choice. Assumptions about uniqueness are acknowledged. However, the consequences of using the regularized plan for training while evaluating with (presumably) unregularized or regularized distances are not clearly specified and should be clarified.
- Empirical evaluation: Broad and solid. Eight datasets spanning topics and sentiment; 26 strong baselines including unsupervised document models and supervised metric learning on those representations. S-WMD wins on 5/8 and is second/competitive otherwise; average rank is best. The use of Bayesian optimization for hyperparameters, published code, training-time comparisons, and t-SNE visualizations are positives. Missing pieces include sensitivity analyses (see below), ablations (A-only vs w-only), and statistical tests.
- Efficiency: The use of Sinkhorn, batch training, and WCD-based neighbor filtering is well justified and appears effective.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally very well written, with clear motivation, notation, and exposition of the optimization. The role of WCD (as a lower bound, for initialization, and for neighbor filtering) is operationally relevant, but the frequent references and notation around WCD occasionally distract from the main contribution; consider consolidating and clarifying its limited roles to avoid confusion.
- The discussion of the entropy regularization parameter (lambda) is insufficient. Given its centrality, readers need guidance on behavior across regimes and on default choices.

Originality:
- The work combines known components—WMD, Sinkhorn regularization, and NCA-style objectives—but applies them in a novel and useful way to text distances by jointly learning a ground metric and term weights. Relative to prior supervised EMD/OT work in vision, adding learnable word reweighting and adapting the approach to document embeddings are appreciable but incremental innovations. Overall novelty is moderate.

Significance:
- Practical impact is high. WMD has seen broad adoption; providing a supervised, efficient variant that consistently improves kNN classification is valuable and likely to be used.
- The learned word weights provide interpretability (e.g., class-discriminative terms), which is attractive in text applications.

Major weaknesses and suggestions:
- Entropic regularization (lambda): The paper fixes lambda=10 without a principled rationale or sensitivity analysis. Lambda controls both computational behavior and the geometry of the learned distance (from nearly independent histograms at small lambda to approximating true WMD at large lambda). Please:
  - Report performance and training stability as a function of lambda across datasets.
  - Clarify whether lambda used for gradient computation matches the lambda (or unregularized WMD) used at test time.
  - Analyze how transport sparsity, classification accuracy, and runtime vary with lambda; include plots or tables.
- Behavior relative to pure WMD vs Sinkhorn-WMD: It is unclear whether the learned metric tends to “true WMD” behavior or remains in the entropic regime chosen for efficiency. Many practitioners default to small lambda for speed; guidance on accuracy-speed tradeoffs is essential.
- Ablations and robustness:
  - A-only vs w-only vs both; show their individual contributions.
  - Different embedding backbones (e.g., GloVe) and ground costs (L2 vs cosine/L1).
  - Regularization of A (e.g., Frobenius norm or constraining M=A^T A to be PSD) to assess overfitting, especially on small datasets.
  - Sensitivity to neighbor set size and batch size in training.
- Constraints on w: Ensure nonnegativity and discuss whether additional priors (e.g., L1 or IDF-informed initialization) help stability/performance.
- Evaluation breadth: Include significance testing and report whether performance differences are statistically meaningful; clarify whether test-time distances are exact WMD with learned parameters or the Sinkhorn approximation.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong empirical results across many baselines and datasets with code availability.
- Clear, well-motivated method that is practical and efficient.
- Interpretable learned weights and compelling visualizations.
- Bridges an influential unsupervised distance (WMD) with supervision in a principled way.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited conceptual novelty relative to prior WMD/Sinkhorn and metric learning.
- Insufficient treatment of the entropy regularization parameter and its implications.
- Missing ablations/sensitivity analyses that would bolster understanding and reproducibility.
- Some confusion around the role and necessity of WCD references.

Overall, this is a well-executed, clearly presented paper with strong empirical value to the community. Addressing the lambda sensitivity, clarifying test-time behavior, and adding ablations would significantly strengthen the work.
