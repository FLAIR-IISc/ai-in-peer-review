This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) to incorporate supervised learning for document similarity. The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space (A) and word importance weights (w) to minimize k-NN classification error. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed version of the optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and a probabilistic filtering approach. They demonstrate strong performance on eight real-world text classification tasks, consistently outperforming numerous baselines.

The work builds upon the recent success of WMD [Kusner et al., 2015] by addressing its inherent limitation of being unsupervised. It connects to the broader field of metric learning [Yang & Jin, 2017], but distinguishes itself by operating directly on word embeddings rather than learning new document representations, and by jointly learning both a transformation of the embedding space and word weights. The proposed approach addresses a clear need for task-specific document distance metrics and offers a practical solution for incorporating supervision into the WMD framework.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates compelling results across a diverse set of datasets, consistently outperforming a large number of competitive baselines (26 in total). This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Technical Soundness:** The authors provide a clear and detailed mathematical formulation of the S-WMD metric, including a derivation of the gradient and a discussion of efficient computation via the Sinkhorn distance approximation. The use of established techniques like NCA [Goldberger et al., 2005] for initialization and batch gradient descent further strengthens the technical foundation.
* **Practicality:** The authors address the computational complexity of incorporating supervision into WMD with a clever approximation, making the method practical for real-world applications. The reported training times (Table 3) are reasonable.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the motivation, approach, and results. The code availability is a significant positive.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Intuition for 'w':** While the concept of word importance weights (w) is intuitively appealing, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of *how* these weights influence the learned metric and *why* they are beneficial. It’s not immediately obvious what kind of patterns are being captured by these weights.
* **A vs. w Contribution:** The paper states that both A and w contribute to performance, but does not quantify the relative contribution of each component. An ablation study or sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of each component individually would be valuable.
* **Lambda Consistency:** The use of lambda (λ) in Eq. (2) (relaxed transport problem) and referenced later (ln 170) requires clarification. Are they the same lambda? A clear explanation would enhance readability.
* **Justification of "Forgiving" Property:** The claim regarding the method being “particularly forgiving when two documents use syntactically different but conceptually similar words” (ln 316) lacks sufficient justification. While the use of word embeddings inherently addresses synonymy, the authors need to explain *how* S-WMD specifically enhances this property compared to the standard WMD.
* **Table Clarity:** Table 2’s columns “n” and “ne” are not defined within the paper and require clarification.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with careful consideration given to computational efficiency and theoretical justification. The experimental setup is comprehensive, with a large number of baselines.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, although certain aspects (as noted in the weaknesses) could benefit from further clarification.
* **Originality:** The work presents a novel extension of WMD to the supervised setting. While the individual components (metric learning, optimal transport) are not new, their combination is a valuable contribution.
* **Significance:** The demonstrated performance improvements across multiple datasets suggest that S-WMD has the potential to be a significant advancement in document similarity and text classification. The method’s efficiency and scalability make it applicable to real-world problems.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Strong empirical performance on a variety of datasets.
* Technically sound and well-motivated approach.
* Addresses a practical problem in document similarity.
* Clear and well-organized presentation.
* Code availability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of detailed analysis of the individual contributions of A and w.
* Some areas require clarification (lambda consistency, justification of forgiving property, table labels).




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document similarity. The authors have addressed a significant limitation of WMD and developed a practical and effective solution. While there are a few areas that could benefit from further clarification, the overall quality of the work and the compelling results warrant acceptance.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**
