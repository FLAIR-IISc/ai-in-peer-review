

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), which extends the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by incorporating supervision to improve document classification performance. The authors frame their approach as learning a generalized Euclidean metric within the word embedding space while simultaneously learning word-specific importance weights to reweight document histograms. The method builds upon the recent WMD work of Kusner et al. (ICML 2015) and the metric learning framework of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), making it a natural progression in the field of text representation learning.

The paper's main contribution is a practical and efficient technique for supervised metric learning that directly minimizes the stochastic leave-one-out classification error under the WMD framework. The authors provide an approximate gradient for the WMD distance that avoids the cubic-time nested optimization problem inherent in the original formulation, leveraging Cuturi's Sinkhorn distance approximation. This allows them to efficiently optimize the linear transformation matrix A and the word importance vector w. The approach is evaluated across eight real-world text classification tasks, where it consistently outperforms 26 competitive baselines, including various document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) and metric learning approaches (NCA, ITML, LMNN).

The quality of the paper is strong, with technically sound methodology and thorough experimental validation. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison across multiple datasets, with clear statistical significance (averaged over five train/test splits where appropriate) and detailed ablation studies. The empirical results are compelling, with S-WMD achieving the lowest test error on 5 out of 8 datasets, and outperforming all baselines on average. The paper also includes a thoughtful analysis of the learned word importance weights, visualizing how they highlight class-relevant words in the 20NEWS dataset.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though there are some notational inconsistencies that could confuse readers. The authors use Euclidean distance in Equation (1) but squared Euclidean in Equation (6), which is inconsistent with the p-Wasserstein framework (where p=2 would correspond to squared Euclidean). I recommend explicitly describing the WMD as p-Wasserstein to avoid confusion. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a clear algorithmic box summarizing the S-WMD procedure, as the current pseudo-code in Algorithm 1 is somewhat fragmented.

The originality of the work is moderate. While the authors combine existing techniques (WMD, metric learning, Sinkhorn approximation) in a novel way for text classification, the core idea of histogram reweighting (Equation 5) has been explored in related work by Lebanon, Cuturi, and others. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of this prior work to better position their contribution. The paper also doesn't sufficiently distinguish their approach from Wang et al.'s (ECCV 2015) work on Earth Mover's Distance learning, particularly regarding the reweighting aspect.

The significance of the paper is high. Document distances are a fundamental component of many text retrieval and classification tasks, and the authors demonstrate that their supervised approach significantly improves performance across multiple datasets. The method is efficient enough to be practical for real-world applications, with training times comparable to baseline metric learning approaches. The insights into how the learned weights correspond to meaningful class-relevant words (Figure 2) provide valuable interpretability, which is often lacking in deep learning approaches to text classification.

I have two key suggestions for improvement. First, the paper should more rigorously discuss the relationship between the regularized (Sinkhorn) and unregularized (WMD) optimal transport formulations, particularly regarding how the approximation affects the final metric. Second, the authors should provide more insight into the empirical importance of the learned weight vector w—how much do the weights vary across different datasets and classes, and what do they reveal about the text classification problem?

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of text representation learning by providing a practical, efficient, and effective method for incorporating supervision into the Word Mover's Distance framework. While the core idea is a combination of existing techniques, the thorough empirical evaluation and clear presentation make it a valuable addition to the literature. The method is simple yet effective, and the authors have done a good job of demonstrating its superiority across multiple text classification tasks.

Strengths: Compelling empirical results, efficient implementation, clear experimental design, practical application.
Weaknesses: Notational inconsistencies, insufficient discussion of prior work on histogram reweighting, need for more rigorous treatment of the regularized vs. unregularized OT formulations.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field with strong empirical validation, and the proposed method has clear potential for adoption in real-world text classification systems. The authors have addressed the key challenges of learning a supervised metric within the WMD framework, and their approach is both theoretically sound and practically effective.
