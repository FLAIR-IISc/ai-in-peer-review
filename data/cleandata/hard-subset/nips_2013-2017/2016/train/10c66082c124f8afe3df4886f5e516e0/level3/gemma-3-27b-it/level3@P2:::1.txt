This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a novel approach to document distance learning that builds upon the recently introduced Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) by incorporating supervision. The authors address the limitation of WMD being an unsupervised metric by learning a linear transformation of the word embedding space and document-specific word importance weights. A key contribution lies in an efficient optimization technique leveraging the Sinkhorn distance to approximate the underlying optimal transport problem, enabling practical training. The method is evaluated on eight text classification tasks, demonstrating consistent outperformance over a comprehensive set of 26 baselines.

The core idea of extending WMD with a supervised learning component is interesting and potentially impactful. WMD has already proven to be a strong baseline for document similarity, and incorporating task-specific information has the potential to significantly improve performance. The extensive empirical evaluation, with comparisons to a large number of competitive baselines, is a significant strength of this work. The paper is also exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its experimental setup. The authors appropriately situate their work within the existing literature on document distances, metric learning, and optimal transport.

However, despite its strengths, the paper has some weaknesses that impact its overall contribution. A primary concern is that the improvement gained from the full S-WMD learning process, compared to the initialization via the Supervised Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD) – essentially a metric learning approach applied to word centroid representations – appears limited (Table 3). This raises questions about the true contribution of the more complex S-WMD optimization. While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth analysis of why the full learning process doesn’t yield a more substantial gain would be valuable.

A significant omission is the lack of evaluation of word-centroid document embeddings *with* existing, standard metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA). Establishing a stronger baseline using these methods on the word-centroid representation would provide a more robust comparison point for the proposed S-WMD.  The authors do include NCA as a baseline, but only after applying PCA, obscuring a direct comparison to S-WCD.

Furthermore, an ablation study is needed to isolate the contributions of the learned word-specific weights versus the linear projection matrix A. Determining the relative importance of each component would provide valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Is the improvement primarily driven by the re-weighting of words, the transformation of the embedding space, or a combination of both?

The originality of the work is somewhat tempered by the fact that the core concept – applying metric learning within the Earth Mover’s Distance framework – has been explored previously (e.g., Wang et al., ECCV 2017, cited in the related work). While the authors’ optimization approach is novel and efficient, the overarching idea is not entirely new. The optimization contribution (lines 156–175) is indeed significant, allowing for efficient training, but the broader idea of learning a metric within the EMD falls short of a truly groundbreaking advancement.

Several minor clarifications are needed. On line 152, the phrase "The authors" is ambiguous and should be explicitly stated (e.g., "Cuturi et al."). In Equation 11, the variables α and β likely require stars (α*, β*) to indicate they are optimal values from the dual problem. Finally, around line 170, the authors should explicitly state that the method utilizes the Sinkhorn scaling previously discussed in Section 2, to clearly connect the optimization procedure to the theoretical foundation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on multiple text classification tasks.
*   Well-written and clearly presented paper.
*   Efficient optimization technique enabling practical training of the proposed method.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in document similarity learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited performance gain from the full S-WMD learning process compared to the S-WCD initialization.
*   Lack of a strong baseline using standard metric learning methods on word-centroid representations.
*   Absence of an ablation study to isolate the contributions of individual components.
*   Limited novelty beyond the efficient optimization technique.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of document similarity learning. However, the limitations regarding the magnitude of the improvement and the lack of thorough baselines and ablation studies prevent it from being a truly outstanding submission. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically addressing the points raised regarding baseline comparisons, ablation analysis, and clarification of the optimization details. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.
