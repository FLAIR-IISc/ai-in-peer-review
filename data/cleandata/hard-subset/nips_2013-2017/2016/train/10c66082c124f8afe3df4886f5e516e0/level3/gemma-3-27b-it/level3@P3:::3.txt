This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised metric within the word embedding space to improve document distance calculations. The core idea is to parameterize the cost matrix of the WMD with a Mahalanobis distance and learn word importance weights, optimizing via gradients for NCA-type objectives and an efficient approximation of the parameterized EMD. The authors demonstrate compelling experimental results across eight datasets, consistently outperforming a large number of baselines.

The work builds upon recent advancements in document distance metrics, particularly Kusner et al.’s WMD, which leverages word embeddings for optimal transport. It also connects to the broader field of metric learning, addressing the limitations of unsupervised methods by incorporating supervision. The paper acknowledges and builds upon Cuturi's work on entropy regularization for efficient optimal transport, and positions itself within the context of NCA for stochastic nearest neighbor classification. 

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper’s most significant strength lies in its extensive experimental validation. The authors comprehensively evaluate S-WMD against 26 baselines across eight datasets, consistently demonstrating superior performance. This robust empirical evidence strongly supports the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Efficient Implementation:** Addressing the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD, the authors cleverly leverage Sinkhorn distances and probabilistic filtering for efficient gradient computation. The reported training times are reasonable, making the method practical for real-world applications.
* **Clear and Focused Contribution:** The paper presents a relatively simple, yet effective, idea—combining existing techniques (WMD, metric learning, Sinkhorn distances) in a novel way. The focus on learning both a linear transformation and word importance weights adds flexibility and contributes to the observed performance gains.
* **Well-Motivated Problem:** The limitations of unsupervised document distance metrics are clearly articulated, motivating the need for a supervised approach. The authors convincingly demonstrate how S-WMD addresses these limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Mathematical Rigor & Clarity:** While the paper presents the core ideas, the mathematical derivations could be more rigorous. The connection between the different approximations and the theoretical guarantees of their accuracy would benefit from further elaboration. An algorithmic box summarizing the S-WMD procedure would significantly improve clarity.
* **Regularization Discussion:** The interplay between the regularized (Sinkhorn) and unregularized OT formulations deserves more detailed discussion. A clear explanation of how the regularization parameter (λ) affects the trade-off between accuracy and efficiency would be valuable.
* **Notational Inconsistency:** The use of Euclidean distance in Eq. 1 and squared Euclidean distance (2-Wasserstein) in Eq. 6 is confusing. Describing the WMD as a *p*-Wasserstein distance would provide a more general and consistent framework, clarifying the role of the distance metric.
* **Related Work Gap:** The reweighting scheme in Eq. 5 is not novel and should be explicitly cited. Lebanon and Le/Cuturi’s earlier work on related concepts should be acknowledged.
* **Weight Vector Insights:** The paper lacks detailed insights into the empirical importance of the learned weight vector *w*. Further analysis of the learned weights – e.g., identifying the most influential words for different classes – would strengthen the conclusions and provide valuable interpretability.
* **Distinction from Cuturi/Avis & Wang et al.:** The paper needs to more clearly distinguish its work from Cuturi/Avis (focusing on the difference between entropy regularization, continuous vs discrete cost, and linear parameterization) and discuss the specific limitations of Wang et al.’s approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant empirical improvement over a wide range of baseline methods.
* The proposed approach is efficient and scalable.
* The combination of existing techniques is novel and effective.
* The problem addressed is relevant and important within the field of text retrieval and document analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The mathematical derivations lack rigor and clarity.
* There are minor gaps in the related work discussion.
* Further analysis of the learned weight vector would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear and well-motivated contribution. The extensive experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of S-WMD. While some improvements to the mathematical rigor and related work discussion would be beneficial, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the reviewer's comments regarding clarity and completeness.
