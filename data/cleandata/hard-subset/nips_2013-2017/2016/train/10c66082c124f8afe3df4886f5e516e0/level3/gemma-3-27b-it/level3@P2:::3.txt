This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a document distance metric by adapting the recently introduced Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space alongside word-specific importance weights, optimizing these parameters to minimize leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by utilizing a relaxed, entropy-regularized version of the optimal transport problem, allowing for efficient gradient computation. The empirical evaluation across eight datasets demonstrates consistently strong performance, outperforming a comprehensive set of 26 baselines.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work in document distance metrics, starting with bag-of-words and TF-IDF representations and progressing to more recent approaches like LSA, LDA, and, crucially, WMD. The authors correctly position their contribution as extending WMD by incorporating supervision, a key limitation of the original unsupervised method. The connection to metric learning is also well established, referencing NCA, LMNN, and ITML. However, the authors should acknowledge related work on reweighting schemes in optimal transport. Specifically, the reweighting of word histograms in Eq. 5 is reminiscent of techniques explored by Lebanon & Cuturi, and this connection should be explicitly discussed and cited.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The proposed method is conceptually simple and intuitively appealing – leveraging the power of pre-trained word embeddings while adapting them to specific classification tasks. The technical contribution of devising an efficient gradient-based update rule for the supervised WMD is significant, circumventing the computational bottleneck of nested optimization. The thorough empirical evaluation is a major strength, demonstrating robust performance gains across a diverse set of datasets and consistently outperforming a large number of competitive baselines. The inclusion of ablation studies (S-WMD initialization) provides valuable insight into the contribution of each component.

However, there are also some weaknesses. While the authors mention using the Sinkhorn distance (entropy regularization) for efficiency, the interplay between the regularized and unregularized formulations of the optimal transport problem isn’t fully explored. A more in-depth discussion of how the regularization parameter affects the approximation accuracy and computational cost would be beneficial. The mathematical derivations could be more rigorous and clarified; for instance, a more careful treatment of the gradient calculations would enhance the paper’s clarity. Furthermore, including an algorithmic box summarizing the S-WMD learning procedure would improve readability. There’s a notational inconsistency between Eq. 1 (Euclidean distance) and Eq. 6 (squared Euclidean distance). It would be clearer to consistently refer to WMD as the *p*-Wasserstein distance, where *p* specifies the distance metric used (1 or 2).

Regarding the experimental results, while compelling, more analysis of the learned weight vector *w* would be valuable. Understanding which words are being upweighted and why, and how this relates to the specific classification task, could provide further insight into the behavior of the model. Are certain types of words (e.g., sentiment-laden words, topic-specific keywords) consistently receiving higher weights? This kind of analysis would strengthen the paper’s claims about the benefits of learning word importance. While the paper distinguishes S-WMD from related work like Cuturi & Avis (focusing on continuous vs. discrete costs and linear parameterization), a clearer articulation of the specific limitations of Wang et al.’s work (compared to S-WMD) would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical performance: consistently outperforms numerous baselines on diverse datasets.
*   Technically sound: provides an efficient and theoretically motivated approach to supervised WMD learning.
*   Addresses a relevant problem: extends a state-of-the-art unsupervised method to a supervised setting.
*   Clear and well-written (with minor notational inconsistencies).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of the regularization parameter in the Sinkhorn distance.
*   Mathematical derivations could be more rigorous.
*   Missing analysis of the learned weight vector *w*.
*   Lack of explicit citation of related work on reweighting in optimal transport.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of document distance learning. The proposed S-WMD method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically successful. While there are some minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
