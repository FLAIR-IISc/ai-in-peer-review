

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance that learns a linear projection and document-specific word re-weighting for improved text classification. The authors formulate a supervised metric learning problem that minimizes stochastic leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error, using Sinkhorn scaling to efficiently approximate the WMD gradient. The method demonstrates strong performance across eight text classification tasks, consistently outperforming 26 baselines.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it a pleasure to read. The authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation and experimental validation, demonstrating that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results on multiple text classification benchmarks. The technical approach is sound, and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive.

However, several concerns limit the paper's impact. The most significant issue is the limited novelty demonstrated in Table 3: the authors' initialization approach (S-WCD) performs nearly as well as the full S-WMD method, suggesting the proposed learning framework may not be substantially better than simpler alternatives. The paper lacks critical baselines that would properly contextualize the contribution, specifically missing evaluations of standard metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA) applied to word-centroid document embeddings. Additionally, the authors fail to conduct an ablation study isolating the contributions of the word-specific weights versus the projection matrix A.

While the optimization approach (lines 156-175) is technically interesting, the core contribution of linear metric learning within EMD is somewhat limited in scope. The paper would have been strengthened by addressing these missing comparisons and ablations to better establish the relative value of their approach.

In terms of quality, the technical content is sound but the evaluation lacks depth in critical areas. The clarity is excellent, and the significance is moderate - the method provides a useful extension to WMD but doesn't represent a breakthrough in the field. Originality is somewhat limited, as the core metric learning approach builds on established techniques with only incremental modifications.

Overall, this is a solid technical contribution that advances the application of WMD to supervised learning, but its impact is constrained by the limited differentiation from simpler baselines and the absence of critical ablation studies. The paper would benefit from addressing these concerns before publication.
