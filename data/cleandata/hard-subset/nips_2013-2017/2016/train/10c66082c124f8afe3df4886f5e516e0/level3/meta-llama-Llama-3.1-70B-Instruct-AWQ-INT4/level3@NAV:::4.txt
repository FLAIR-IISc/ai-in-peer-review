This paper proposes a novel approach to learning a supervised document distance metric, called Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD). The authors build upon the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) metric, which has shown state-of-the-art results in k-nearest neighbor (kNN) document classification. The key innovation of S-WMD is the incorporation of supervision into the WMD metric, allowing it to learn a linear transformation of the word representations and a word importance vector that captures the semantic meaning of similarity encoded in the labels.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a thorough literature review that provides context and motivation for the proposed approach. The authors also provide extensive experimental results on several novel datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of S-WMD in improving document classification accuracy.

The strengths of the paper include:

* A clear and concise presentation of the proposed approach and its underlying principles
* A thorough literature review that provides context and motivation for the proposed approach
* Extensive experimental results on several novel datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of S-WMD in improving document classification accuracy

However, there are also some weaknesses and limitations of the paper:

* The motivation for reweighting the word importance vector w is not well-explained, and it is unclear how this reweighting affects the document semantics
* The contribution of the paper is limited, as applying a linear transform and reweighting with an NCA objective is not seen as a significant novelty
* The practical concern of computing the reweight w for a completely new/unseen document is not addressed
* The terminology "semantic difference" in the abstract is ambiguous and appears to mean label/topic/authorship rather than true semantics

To address these concerns, the authors could provide more explanation and justification for the reweighting of the word importance vector w, and demonstrate how this reweighting affects the document semantics. Additionally, the authors could explore more significant novelties in the proposed approach, and provide more guidance on how to compute the reweight w for new/unseen documents.

Overall, I would not object to acceptance of this paper, provided that the above questions are properly addressed. The paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of document distance metrics, and the authors have demonstrated the effectiveness of their approach through extensive experimental results.
