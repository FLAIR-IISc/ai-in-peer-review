

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes an extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) metric by incorporating supervised learning. The authors introduce Supervised WMD (S-WMD), which learns a linear transformation A of the underlying word embedding space and a word importance weight vector w to improve document distances for classification tasks. The approach builds upon Kusner et al.'s WMD (NIPS 2015), which computes document distances as optimal transport between word embeddings, and adapts the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) framework to this setting. The authors provide an efficient gradient approximation using the Sinkhorn distance, enabling practical training.

The paper makes a clear connection to prior work on metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA). It properly situates its contribution within the literature, noting that while most metric learning approaches operate on document representations, S-WMD operates directly on the word embedding space, avoiding the need for document-level dimensionality reduction.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with a thorough literature review that properly contextualizes the work.
2. The authors provide extensive experimental results on eight diverse text classification datasets, comparing against 26 baselines (including unsupervised WMD, WCD, and multiple document representations with supervised metric learning).
3. The method is clearly motivated and the technical approach is sound, with a well-justified approximation to the gradient computation.
4. The results demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines, with S-WMD achieving the lowest error on 5/8 datasets and outperforming all other methods on average.

**Weaknesses and concerns:**
1. The motivation for reweighting word distributions (w) is weak and not well-explained. The authors do not adequately address the concern that reweighting could fundamentally alter a document's semantics (e.g., by emphasizing words that are not representative of the document's true meaning). The paper would benefit from a more rigorous analysis showing that the reweighted words remain semantically meaningful for the classification task.

2. The paper lacks justification for why reweighting improves label separation without altering document semantics. It would be valuable to include examples showing how the learned weights align with meaningful linguistic distinctions for the classification task.

3. The contribution appears limited in novelty. Applying a linear transform and reweighting with an NCA objective is a relatively straightforward adaptation of existing metric learning techniques to the WMD framework. The paper does not sufficiently highlight how this differs from previous work, particularly Wang et al.'s supervised EMD learning (which the authors cite but don't contrast with).

4. There is a significant practical concern: the paper does not explain how to compute the reweighting vector w for completely new, unseen documents. This is a critical gap for any practical application of the method.

5. The terminology "semantic difference" in the abstract is ambiguous and appears to mean label/topic/authorship rather than true semantic meaning. This should be clarified throughout the paper to avoid confusion.

**Overall assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound approach that consistently improves upon previous document distance metrics in classification tasks. The method is well-motivated, well-executed, and thoroughly evaluated. The writing quality and experimental validation are strong points of the paper.

However, the novelty is somewhat limited, and the paper would benefit from addressing the concerns regarding the reweighting mechanism, its semantic validity, and its practical application to unseen documents. The authors should also clarify the distinction between their approach and prior work on supervised EMD.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, provided the authors address the concerns outlined above. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of document distances, and the improvements over existing methods are significant. The reviewers would not object to acceptance if the authors properly address the questions about reweighting and its relationship to semantic meaning.

The paper's main strength is its comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and baselines, which demonstrates the practical value of the approach. The weaknesses are primarily related to the explanation of the reweighting mechanism and the novelty of the approach, not to the technical soundness or empirical results.

This work is well-suited for NIPS, as it combines insights from optimal transport, metric learning, and natural language processing to address an important practical problem in text classification. The authors' careful attention to experimental validation and thorough comparison with existing methods aligns with NIPS' standards for quality and significance.
