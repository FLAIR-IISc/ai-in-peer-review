

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance" (NIPS 2016)

This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a novel extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision to learn a domain-specific metric. The authors build upon Kusner et al.'s WMD (NIPS 2015), which established a powerful document distance metric by formulating document similarity as an optimal transport problem between word embeddings. While WMD demonstrated impressive results in unsupervised document classification, it lacked a mechanism to incorporate task-specific supervision. The authors address this gap by proposing an efficient technique to learn an affine transformation of the word embedding space and a word-importance weight vector that minimizes the stochastic leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a principled way to incorporate supervision into the WMD framework, which was previously unsupervised. Second, it develops an efficient approximation to the gradient of the WMD that avoids the computationally prohibitive nested optimization. Third, it conducts an extensive empirical evaluation across 8 diverse text classification datasets, comparing against 26 competitive baselines including traditional document representations (BOW, TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA), distance metrics (WCD, WMD), and various supervised metric learning approaches (NCA, ITML, LMNN).

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, with S-WMD outperforming all baselines on 5 out of 8 datasets, and achieving the best average performance across all datasets. The authors provide detailed visualizations showing how S-WMD produces more clustered class representations than WMD, and they analyze the learned word importance weights to demonstrate their relevance to the classification task.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains both the theoretical underpinnings and practical implementation details. The authors carefully address the computational challenges of learning a supervised metric by leveraging the Sinkhorn distance approximation and introducing batch gradient descent with a clever neighbor filtering strategy. The experimental design is thorough, with careful hyperparameter tuning using Bayesian optimization and comprehensive analysis across multiple datasets and baselines.

The paper is highly original in its approach to incorporating supervision into the WMD framework. While metric learning has been studied extensively in the literature (e.g., NCA, LMNN, ITML), the authors' approach of learning a linear transformation within the word embedding space while also learning word importance weights represents a novel application of metric learning to the text domain. This approach is particularly well-suited to the text domain because it leverages the semantic relationships captured in word embeddings rather than learning a new document representation.

The significance of this work is substantial. The authors demonstrate that supervised learning can significantly improve upon the already strong unsupervised WMD baseline, achieving state-of-the-art results across multiple text classification tasks. The method is efficient enough to be practical, with training times comparable to or better than other metric learning approaches. The authors' implementation is publicly available, which will facilitate further research and adoption.

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide detailed theoretical derivations, including the gradient computation for the supervised metric learning problem, and demonstrate through extensive experiments that their approach consistently outperforms strong baselines. The paper also appropriately acknowledges limitations, noting that on some datasets (like CLASSIC) the unsupervised WMD slightly outperformed S-WMD, which they attribute to initialization issues.

The only minor weakness is the paper's reliance on word2vec embeddings without exploring whether other embedding techniques might yield even better results with S-WMD. However, this is a minor point given the strong results achieved with the standard word2vec embeddings.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of text classification by providing a principled way to incorporate supervision into the Word Mover's Distance. The method is sound, clearly explained, and demonstrated to be highly effective across multiple text classification tasks. The extensive experimental evaluation provides strong evidence for the approach's superiority over existing methods. The paper is well-suited for presentation at NIPS as it advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, addresses a difficult problem (learning a task-specific document distance metric), and provides a unique theoretical and practical approach.

The paper meets all four criteria for quality:
- Technical soundness: The method is well-justified and the experimental results strongly support the claims.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with sufficient detail to reproduce the results.
- Originality: The approach of learning a supervised metric within the WMD framework is novel and represents a significant extension of prior work.
- Significance: The method achieves state-of-the-art results across multiple text classification tasks and provides a practical, efficient solution to an important problem.

Given the paper's technical quality, clarity, originality, and significance, I strongly recommend acceptance for NIPS 2016.
