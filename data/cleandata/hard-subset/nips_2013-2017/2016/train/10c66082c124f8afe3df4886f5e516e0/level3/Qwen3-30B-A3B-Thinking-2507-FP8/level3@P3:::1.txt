

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance" for NIPS 2016

This paper proposes an extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) framework for text classification, introducing a supervised metric learning approach called Supervised WMD (S-WMD). The authors recognize that while WMD has shown excellent performance in kNN text classification, it is entirely unsupervised and lacks a mechanism to incorporate task-specific supervision. Their approach learns both a linear transformation of the word embedding space and document-specific word importance weights to improve the WMD metric. The key innovation lies in their efficient optimization strategy that approximates the gradient of the WMD distance, avoiding the computationally prohibitive nested optimization problem.

The authors build upon the foundational work of Kusner et al. [19] who introduced WMD as a special case of Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) that leverages word embeddings to compute document distances. They extend this with a supervised learning framework that directly optimizes the leave-one-out kNN classification error. The paper also draws inspiration from metric learning techniques like Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), adapting them to the WMD framework.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong empirical results across 8 text classification datasets, consistently outperforming 26 competitive baselines. The technical approach is sound, with a clear explanation of the optimization strategy using Sinkhorn scaling to approximate the WMD gradient. However, the paper falls short in several important aspects. The most significant limitation is the lack of a proper baseline comparison: the authors fail to evaluate the word-centroid document embeddings (which form the basis of their initialization) with standard metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA). This omission prevents proper assessment of whether their approach truly adds value beyond standard metric learning applied to the word centroid representation. Additionally, the paper lacks an ablation study isolating the contributions of the word-specific weights versus the projection matrix A, making it difficult to assess the relative importance of these components. The claim of "strong kNN classification results" is somewhat undermined by Table 3, which shows that the initialization (S-WCD) performs nearly as well as the full S-WMD method.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain the background, motivation, and technical details of their approach. The pseudo-code (Algorithm 1) and mathematical formulations are well-presented. The visualization of t-SNE embeddings (Figure 1) effectively demonstrates the improvement of S-WMD over WMD.

**Originality:** While the core idea of learning a metric within the WMD framework is interesting, the main novelty appears to be in the optimization approach (lines 156-175), not in the metric learning aspect itself. The linear metric learning within EMD is a relatively straightforward extension, and the paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from related work like Wang et al. [35] who also learn metrics within EMD. The paper would benefit from a more precise statement of how their contribution differs from previous work.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a relevant problem in text classification, but the significance is limited by the concerns noted above. The consistent improvement over baselines is promising, but the lack of proper baselines and ablation studies reduces the confidence in the claimed significance. The approach does not fundamentally advance the state of the art in metric learning, but rather applies existing metric learning techniques to a new distance metric.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a useful extension to WMD that is well-motivated and demonstrated to be effective in practice. The optimization approach is clever and efficiently implemented. The writing is clear and engaging, and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive across multiple datasets.

**Weaknesses:** The most critical weakness is the absence of the missing baseline (word-centroid embeddings with standard metric learning methods). Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine whether the S-WMD approach offers a genuine advantage over simpler alternatives. The paper also fails to conduct the necessary ablation study to determine which component (projection matrix A or word weights) contributes more to the performance gain. The limited impact of the full method compared to the initialization (Table 3) further weakens the case for the proposed approach.

**Recommendation:** While the paper presents a technically sound and well-written extension to WMD, the lack of proper baseline comparisons and ablation studies significantly weakens its contribution. The authors should conduct the missing experiments before the paper can be considered for acceptance. Without these additional analyses, the paper's significance is questionable, as it's unclear whether the proposed method provides a meaningful advancement beyond standard metric learning applied to the word centroid representation.

**Pro Acceptance:** Well-written, interesting extension to WMD, strong empirical results across multiple datasets, efficient optimization approach.

**Con Acceptance:** Missing critical baseline comparisons, lacks ablation study, limited impact compared to simpler initialization approach, novelty is primarily in optimization rather than the metric learning concept itself.
