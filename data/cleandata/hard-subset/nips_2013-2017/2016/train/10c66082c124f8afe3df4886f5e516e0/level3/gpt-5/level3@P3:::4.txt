Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised document distance built on Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). WMD measures the optimal transport cost between two documents viewed as word distributions, using word2vec distances as the ground metric. S-WMD incorporates supervision by (i) learning a linear transform A of the word embedding space and (ii) learning a global word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms before computing WMD. The objective approximates leave-one-out kNN classification via an NCA-style stochastic neighborhood loss. To make training tractable, the paper replaces exact WMD with an entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) approximation and derives efficient gradients for A and w, leveraging primal/dual quantities from the Sinkhorn scaling. Experiments on eight datasets against 26 baselines show consistent improvements over WMD and most alternatives.

This work connects two streams: (1) WMD/Wasserstein distances for text and (2) supervised metric learning (NCA/LMNN/ITML) that typically learn Euclidean metrics on vector representations. Prior work has learned ground metrics for EMD in vision; here the authors adapt that idea to text with word embeddings, add supervised reweighting of histograms, and use Sinkhorn to enable scalable training.

Strengths
- Clear, well-structured paper with a thorough literature review; mathematical choices are motivated and derivations are readable.
- Pragmatic and elegant: learning in the low-dimensional embedding space avoids the curse of dimensionality typical in text metric learning.
- Solid engineering: the Sinkhorn relaxation yields efficient, differentiable updates; the initialization via S-WCD (NCA on word centroids) is sensible and empirically helpful.
- Extensive evaluation: eight datasets, many strong supervised and unsupervised baselines, systematic hyperparameter tuning, runtime reporting, and visualizations; consistent gains across several tasks.
- Reproducibility: code availability is a plus.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Motivation and semantics of histogram reweighting w are underexplained. A global multiplicative w followed by renormalization can substantially alter a document’s distribution—potentially changing its semantics rather than merely emphasizing discriminative features. The paper needs to justify that this operation improves label separation without meaningfully changing document content.
- Practicality at test time: it is not fully clear how w is applied to unseen documents and how OOV words or domain shift are handled. If w is global, this should be stated explicitly; otherwise, a test-time estimation procedure must be provided.
- Limited novelty. Learning a linear ground metric A and weights with an NCA loss on top of OT is a straightforward combination of existing components. The main novelty is in the specific marriage to WMD for text and the efficient gradient via Sinkhorn; the conceptual contribution beyond prior EMD metric learning is incremental.
- Terminology: the abstract’s “semantic difference” is ambiguous; the method appears to optimize label/topic/author-related similarity rather than general semantic similarity.
- Missing ablations: the paper does not isolate the benefits of A vs w vs both, nor quantify the impact of the Sinkhorn approximation parameter λ on accuracy and runtime.

Quality
- Generally sound. The derivations for gradients through the Sinkhorn-form OT are sensible; the use of dual potentials for w is standard. Some claims would benefit from more careful justification (e.g., when and why reweighting preserves semantics).
- Strong empirical support with competitive baselines. Suggested additional ablations (below) would further strengthen the conclusions.

Clarity
- Very clear and easy to follow; the optimization pipeline and complexity are well explained.
- Needs explicit clarification on whether w is global or document-specific; how inference works for new documents; and what constraints/regularization are applied to w.

Originality
- Moderate. Applying linear metric learning and supervised reweighting to the OT ground cost in text is natural, and the Sinkhorn-based training is known. The paper’s originality lies in adapting these ideas cohesively to WMD with an efficient training recipe.

Significance
- Practical significance is good: a drop-in supervised upgrade to a popular unsupervised metric that improves kNN classification on diverse datasets.
- Conceptual significance is modest: the field may view the contribution as an effective integration rather than a fundamentally new method.

Questions for the authors
1. Is w a single global vocabulary-level vector shared across all documents and classes? If so, please state clearly and explain test-time application on unseen documents and OOV handling. If not, describe how per-document or per-class weights are inferred at test time.
2. What constraints or regularizers are imposed on w to prevent semantic drift (e.g., ||w−1|| penalties, bounds, sparsity)? Can you report the distribution of learned weights, and a measure of divergence between original and reweighted document histograms?
3. Ablations: performance with only A, only w, and both; sensitivity to λ and number of Sinkhorn iterations; impact of the WCD-based neighbor filtering (N) and batch size (B).
4. Can you validate the Sinkhorn-gradient approximation by comparing against exact WMD gradients on small problems?
5. Please clarify “semantic difference” and align terminology with the supervised objective (e.g., “task- or label-informed similarity”).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical or empirical safeguards that reweighting does not materially change document meaning (e.g., bounding w, regularizing toward IDF or w=1, reporting KL divergence pre/post reweighting, qualitative examples).
- Clarify and formalize the inference procedure for new documents, including OOV handling and domain shift.
- Include ablation studies and sensitivity analyses as above.
- Consider per-class or group-structured weights, or priors centered at IDF, and discuss trade-offs.
- Reframe the abstract to avoid ambiguity around “semantic difference.”

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical gains over a wide set of baselines; clean, efficient method with available code.
- Clear exposition and good engineering choices; relevant to many NLP and IR applications.
- Builds meaningfully on WMD by enabling supervision.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to existing metric learning and OT literature.
- Insufficient justification and analysis for the reweighting mechanism and its semantic implications.
- Minor clarity gaps about test-time application and terminology.

Recommendation
Leaning weak accept, contingent on clarifying and justifying the reweighting design and test-time inference. I would not object to acceptance if the above questions are adequately addressed.
