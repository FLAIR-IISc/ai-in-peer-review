This paper proposes a supervised metric learning approach built on the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) to improve the performance of k-nearest neighbor (kNN) text classification tasks. The authors introduce the Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD) metric, which learns a linear projection and document-specific word re-weighting through an efficient optimization process. The proposed method is evaluated on eight real-world text classification tasks, where it consistently outperforms 26 competitive baselines.

The main idea of S-WMD is to learn a generalized Euclidean metric within the word embedding space and a word importance vector. The authors assume that the word embedding matrix X defines a d-dimensional word vector for each word in the vocabulary. They then transform the word embedding using a linear transformation xi→Axi and introduce a histogram importance vector w to re-weight the word histogram values. The WMD distance between documents da and db is then computed using the transformed word embeddings and the re-weighted histogram values.

The authors use a stochastic neighborhood relaxation to approximate the leave-one-out kNN error and minimize the KL-divergence loss. They also propose an efficient technique to compute the gradient of the loss function with respect to the linear transformation A and the word importance vector w. The gradient computation involves relaxing the modified linear program using the entropy regularizer, which allows for an efficient approximation of the optimal transport problem.

The experimental results show that S-WMD achieves the lowest kNN test error on 5/8 datasets and outperforms all other 26 methods on average across all datasets. The authors also provide a visualization of the test split of each dataset using t-Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE), which shows that S-WMD improves upon WMD on almost all datasets.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes an interesting and useful extension of WMD, which is shown to be effective on several kNN text classification tasks.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method on eight real-world text classification tasks, which demonstrates its superiority over 26 competitive baselines.
* The paper is well-written and pleasant to read.

Weaknesses:

* The impact of the proposed method is limited, as EMD with metric learning initialized via a "word centroid distance" performs similarly to the full proposed learning (see Table 3).
* The paper lacks an evaluation of word-centroid document embeddings with existing metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA).
* There is no isolated assessment of the contributions of word-specific weights versus the projection matrix A.
* The originality of the paper lies mainly in the optimization approach, which is a somewhat limited contribution.
* Minor clarifications are needed, such as specifying which paper "The authors" refers to (line 152) and explicitly noting around line 170 that the method uses Sinkhorn scaling referenced earlier.

Quality: 8/10

The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

Clarity: 9/10

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides enough information for the expert reader to reproduce its results. The authors use proper citations and provide a thorough explanation of the background and related work.

Originality: 7/10

The main novelty of the paper lies in the optimization approach, which is an interesting but somewhat limited contribution. The idea of learning a linear projection and document-specific word re-weighting within the word embedding space is not entirely new, but the authors provide a unique and efficient optimization process.

Significance: 8/10

The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research, and the results are important for the field of natural language processing. The proposed method has the potential to advance the state of the art in kNN text classification tasks.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and efficient optimization approach for supervised metric learning within the word embedding space.
* The experimental results demonstrate the superiority of S-WMD over 26 competitive baselines on eight real-world text classification tasks.
* The paper is well-written and provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method.

* The paper lacks an evaluation of word-centroid document embeddings with existing metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA).
