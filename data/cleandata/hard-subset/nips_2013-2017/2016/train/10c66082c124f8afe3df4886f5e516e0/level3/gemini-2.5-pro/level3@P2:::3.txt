This paper proposes a method to learn a supervised version of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a recently introduced and powerful document distance metric. The original WMD is entirely unsupervised, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compute an optimal transport cost between the words of two documents. This work, termed Supervised WMD (S-WMD), extends WMD by learning a task-specific distance. The supervision is incorporated in two ways: (1) by learning a linear transformation (a Mahalanobis metric) on the underlying word embedding space, and (2) by learning an importance weight for each word in the vocabulary, effectively re-weighting the document's bag-of-words representation.

To learn the transformation matrix and the word-importance vector, the authors propose to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor classification error, following the framework of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A key challenge is that the gradient of the WMD involves a nested optimization problem, which is computationally prohibitive. The authors propose a clever and practical solution by using the entropy-regularized version of the optimal transport problem (the Sinkhorn distance), which admits a fast, differentiable approximation to the WMD. This allows for an efficient gradient-based optimization procedure. The paper presents a compelling empirical evaluation on eight text classification datasets, showing that S-WMD consistently outperforms a large suite of 26 competitive baselines, including WMD and various document representations combined with standard metric learning algorithms.

### Quality
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The core idea of learning a ground metric for WMD is well-motivated, and the proposed method for doing so is practical and effective. The main technical contribution—the use of the Sinkhorn distance to derive an efficient and differentiable approximation for the gradient—is crucial for making the approach feasible and is well-executed.

The experimental evaluation is a significant strength of the paper. The authors are careful to compare against a very wide range of strong baselines, including unsupervised methods like WMD, LSI, and LDA, as well as supervised versions of these methods using established metric learning algorithms like NCA, LMNN, and ITML. The consistent state-of-the-art performance of S-WMD across multiple datasets strongly supports the authors' claims.

A minor weakness is the lack of an ablation study. While the results for S-WMD are excellent, it is unclear how much of the performance gain is attributable to the learned metric `A` versus the learned word weights `w`. An experiment comparing S-WMD with a version that only learns `A` (or only `w`) would provide valuable insight into the relative importance of these two components.

### Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the background on WMD and NCA is helpful. However, the clarity of the technical exposition could be improved in a few places:

1.  **Regularized vs. Unregularized OT:** The objective function in Eq. (6) is defined using the unregularized WMD, `D_{A,w}`. However, the gradients are computed using the solution to the entropy-regularized (Sinkhorn) version, `T*_\lambda`. The paper presents this as an approximation but could be more explicit about the theoretical relationship. Is the gradient of the regularized distance an approximation of a subgradient of the original problem? A more rigorous discussion of this approximation would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Inconsistent Notation:** There is a slight notational inconsistency between the definition of WMD and its use in the supervised setting. Eq. (1) defines WMD with a general p-norm `||xi - xj||_p`, while the learned metric in Eq. (5) and the NCA loss implicitly rely on a squared Euclidean distance `||A(xi - xj)||_2^2`. For clarity, it would be better to introduce WMD as a p-Wasserstein distance and then explicitly state that the squared 2-Wasserstein distance is being used for the supervised learning objective.

### Originality
The paper combines several existing ideas—WMD, NCA-style metric learning, and Sinkhorn distances—in a novel and effective way. While learning a ground metric for the Earth Mover's Distance is not a new idea (e.g., [7, 35]), this paper is the first to apply it to the WMD for document classification. The simultaneous learning of a linear transformation `A` and a histogram re-weighting `w` within this framework is a novel contribution.

However, the authors should better contextualize their work with respect to prior art.
*   The idea of re-weighting histograms (your vector `w`) has been explored previously in the context of EMD. The authors should cite and discuss related work, for instance, from Guy Lebanon or Le and Cuturi, to clarify their specific contribution.
*   The distinction from related work could be sharper. For instance, Cuturi & Avis [7] learn a continuous cost function via entropy regularization, which is different from learning a parameterized discrete ground metric. Wang et al. [35] learn a Mahalanobis distance for EMD in vision but do not learn the histogram weights, which is a key difference and advantage of the proposed method. A more detailed discussion would better highlight the paper's novelty.

### Significance
The results presented in this paper are significant. WMD is a state-of-the-art unsupervised metric for document similarity. This paper provides a principled and computationally efficient way to adapt this powerful metric to supervised tasks, leading to substantial performance improvements. Given the strong empirical results, S-WMD is likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners working on document classification and retrieval tasks. The ideas presented could also inspire further research into learning supervised optimal transport metrics in other domains.

### Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and natural problem of incorporating supervision into the WMD metric.
*   The proposed method is a novel and elegant combination of WMD, metric learning, and efficient optimal transport solvers.
*   The use of the Sinkhorn relaxation to enable efficient gradient-based training is a key technical contribution that makes the method practical.
*   The experimental evaluation is exceptionally thorough and provides compelling evidence that S-WMD achieves state-of-the-art results for kNN-based document classification.

**Arguments against Acceptance (and areas for improvement):**
*   The technical presentation could be more rigorous, particularly regarding the approximation of the WMD gradient via the regularized Sinkhorn distance.
*   The originality could be better contextualized by a more detailed discussion and citation of prior work on learning EMD ground metrics and histogram re-weighting.
*   The paper would benefit from an ablation study to disentangle the contributions of the learned metric `A` and the word weights `w`.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel idea, a practical algorithm, and impressive results. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed in a final version. I recommend acceptance.
