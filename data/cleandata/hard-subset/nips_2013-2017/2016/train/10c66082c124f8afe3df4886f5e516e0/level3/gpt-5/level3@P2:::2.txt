Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns, from labeled data, both (i) a linear transformation of the underlying word embedding space and (ii) a word-importance reweighting of document histograms. The objective is a stochastic leave-one-out (LOO) kNN criterion à la Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), but the distance between documents is defined by an optimal transport (OT) problem over transformed word embeddings and reweighted term frequencies. To make training practical, the authors optimize an entropically-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) relaxation and derive efficient gradients with respect to both the metric and word weights, using dual variables recovered from Sinkhorn scaling.

The method is closely related to:
- WMD (Kusner et al., ICML 2015), which introduced OT over word embeddings for document distances, but remained unsupervised.
- Entropic OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013), used here to obtain fast, differentiable approximations to the WMD and its dual.
- Classical metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML), which typically learns generalized Euclidean metrics in vector spaces; S-WMD differs by learning the “ground” metric inside OT plus word weights for histograms.
- Prior work on learning EMD/ground metrics in vision (e.g., Wang & Guibas), which did not consider text-specific reweighting or leveraging pretrained word embeddings.

Algorithmically, S-WMD initializes the linear transform A by running NCA on Word Centroid Distance (WCD)—a lower bound to WMD—then performs batch gradient descent with neighbor filtering (using WCD to preselect M candidates) and Sinkhorn-based gradients. The authors evaluate on 8 datasets against 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised with NCA/LMNN/ITML after PCA), and report consistent improvements, often by a large margin.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The formulation is well-motivated and mathematically coherent. Using entropic OT to obtain unique transport plans and dual potentials for gradient computation is appropriate and standard. The derivations for ∂/∂A and ∂/∂w are sensible; the use of dual variables for w is elegant.
- Efficiency: The adoption of Sinkhorn scaling, neighbor filtering via WCD, and minibatching makes optimization practical. Complexity is discussed and bounded in terms of vocabulary per document and batch/neighbor sizes.
- Empirics: The experimental protocol is extensive: 8 diverse tasks, 26 baselines including strong supervised metric learners, hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian optimization, and visualizations (t-SNE) and training-time reports. Code availability supports reproducibility.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written and well-structured, with sufficient algorithmic details to reproduce results. The role of initialization (S-WCD) is articulated and empirically justified. Figures and tables (as described) are informative.

Originality:
- The key novelty is learning a supervised ground metric and word-importance weights within WMD for text, with a principled, efficient training approach using Sinkhorn-based gradients and an NCA-style objective. While metric learning and supervised EMD exist, applying them in this way to word-embedding OT with histogram reweighting appears new and well-differentiated from prior work.

Significance:
- Results are strong across a broad suite of baselines and datasets, suggesting practical impact for document retrieval/classification where WMD is applicable. The method advances state of the art for kNN-based classification and provides a general recipe for task-adaptive OT over embeddings.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean integration of supervision into WMD via ground metric learning and word reweighting.
- Sound, efficient training using entropic OT and carefully designed approximations.
- Very thorough empirical evaluation with strong gains over robust baselines.
- Clear writing and open-source implementation.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Sensitivity/ablation: It would be helpful to report ablations isolating the contributions of A vs. w (e.g., learn only A, only w) and the impact of initialization, λ, and neighbor set size M on accuracy and training time.
- Choice of ground distance: The squared Euclidean ground metric is assumed; some datasets might benefit from L2 or cosine. A brief study would strengthen claims.
- Approximation bias: The neighbor filtering (computing probabilities only within M nearest WCD neighbors) is a biased approximation to the NCA objective. Please quantify the effect on final performance and whether M can be small without loss.
- Dual variable approximation: The mapping from Sinkhorn scaling vectors to dual variables and choice of λ=10 are reasonable, but a sensitivity analysis would reassure readers about stability vs. accuracy.
- Scalability for very long documents: While faster than exact WMD, complexity remains O(p^2) per pair for Sinkhorn; discuss behavior for corpora with very large p, and whether vocabulary truncation/stop-word handling is needed.
- Fairness of baselines: Some baselines are reduced with PCA to 200 dims before metric learning; consider reporting results without PCA for methods that can handle higher dims or providing justification that 200 is near the knee of the curve.

Questions for authors:
- How do performance and learned weights differ across tasks (e.g., topic vs. sentiment)? Can you provide per-class or per-word analyses beyond the top-100 word cloud?
- Does learning class-specific weights w_c or a low-rank class-conditional A improve performance?
- How robust is S-WMD to domain shift between the corpus used to train word2vec and the downstream labeled data?
- Did you try fine-tuning the word embeddings jointly (beyond an affine transform) or using alternative embeddings (e.g., GloVe) to test generality?

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, well-motivated methodological contribution that extends a popular distance (WMD).
- Solid theory-inspired engineering with practical efficiency.
- Extensive, carefully tuned empirical evaluation with consistent improvements.
- Clear presentation and available code.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited ablation/sensitivity analyses on approximation choices (λ, M) and the relative roles of A vs. w.
- Assumption of squared Euclidean ground metric without alternatives evaluated.
- Remaining computational burden for very long documents.

Overall recommendation:
A solid and timely contribution that brings discriminative, task-adaptive learning to WMD with a well-executed, efficient training procedure and compelling empirical gains. I recommend acceptance for presentation at NIPS. My enthusiasm is high; addressing the suggested ablations and sensitivities would further strengthen the paper.
