This paper proposes a method to learn a supervised version of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a recently introduced and powerful document metric. The original WMD is entirely unsupervised, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to calculate an optimal transport (Earth Mover's Distance) cost between the words of two documents. The authors' proposal, Supervised WMD (S-WMD), introduces supervision by learning two components: (1) a linear transformation `A` of the word embedding space, which corresponds to learning a Mahalanobis distance as the ground metric for the transport problem, and (2) a vector of weights `w` that re-scales the word histograms to reflect word importance for the given classification task.

To learn `A` and `w`, the authors formulate an objective function based on Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), which aims to minimize a differentiable proxy for the leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor classification error. A key technical challenge is that differentiating through the WMD objective is computationally prohibitive and ill-defined. The authors overcome this by replacing the exact WMD with its entropy-regularized counterpart (the Sinkhorn distance), which is differentiable and can be computed much more efficiently. This allows for practical optimization via stochastic gradient descent. The paper presents a strong set of experiments on eight text classification datasets, showing that S-WMD consistently outperforms a large suite of 26 competitive baselines.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of the original WMD—its inability to incorporate supervision. Given the strong performance of WMD, developing a supervised variant is a natural and valuable contribution. The proposed S-WMD method is likely to be widely adopted and built upon by researchers working with document distances.

2.  **Novelty and Soundness:** While the method combines several existing ideas—WMD, metric learning via NCA, and entropy-regularized optimal transport—the synthesis is novel, effective, and well-motivated. The core idea of learning a ground metric and histogram weights simultaneously within an NCA framework for documents is a solid contribution. The use of the Sinkhorn distance as a differentiable surrogate for WMD is a clever and pragmatic choice that makes the approach computationally feasible.

3.  **Empirical Strength:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and compelling. The authors compare S-WMD against an extensive list of 26 baselines, including various document representations (TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with several metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML), as well as the unsupervised WMD. The consistent state-of-the-art performance of S-WMD across five of the eight datasets, and strong performance on the rest, provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is strong, but could be improved in terms of mathematical rigor, clarity of presentation, and positioning with respect to prior work.

1.  **Mathematical Rigor and Clarity:**
    *   **Regularized vs. Unregularized OT:** The derivation of the gradient in Eq. (8) relies on the uniqueness of the transport plan `T`, which is only guaranteed for the entropy-regularized problem. The paper acknowledges this but could better explain the relationship between the gradient of the regularized objective and the subgradient of the unregularized one. A brief discussion on whether the approximation becomes more accurate as the regularization parameter `λ` increases would strengthen the theoretical grounding.
    *   **Notational Inconsistency:** There is a notable inconsistency in the definition of the distance. Equation (1) defines the WMD cost using `||xi − xj||p^2`, which is ambiguous. Typically, the p-Wasserstein distance uses a ground cost of `c(x,y)^p`, where `c` is a metric. Equation (5), however, uses `||A(xi − xj)||_2^2`, which corresponds to the squared 2-Wasserstein distance. To avoid confusion, I suggest the authors clarify this from the beginning, perhaps by framing WMD generally as a p-Wasserstein distance and then specifying that the supervised version focuses on the squared 2-Wasserstein distance, which is common in metric learning.
    *   **Algorithm Clarity:** While Algorithm 1 provides a high-level overview, it omits key details of the gradient computation. Including the gradient update rules from equations (8) and (10) within a more detailed algorithmic box would significantly improve the paper's self-containment and reproducibility.

2.  **Positioning and Prior Work:**
    *   **Histogram Reweighting:** The idea of learning importance weights for histograms in the context of optimal transport (Eq. 5) is not new. Similar ideas have been explored, for example, in work by Guy Lebanon on metric learning for distributions and by Cuturi & Doucet in the context of Wasserstein barycenters. The authors should cite and briefly discuss this related work to better contextualize their contribution.
    *   **Comparison to Related Work:** The "Related Work" section could be sharpened. The distinction from Cuturi & Avis (2011), who also learned a ground distance for EMD, should be made more explicit (e.g., they learn a continuous cost function via kernels, not a discrete Mahalanobis metric, and use a different objective). When discussing Wang et al. (2012), beyond noting their lack of histogram reweighting, it would be useful to contrast their ITML-based framework with the NCA-based one used here.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   The paper demonstrates that learning both the matrix `A` and the weight vector `w` is effective. Could you provide some insight into the relative importance of these two components? An ablation study showing the performance of S-WMD when learning only `A` (i.e., `w` is fixed to all ones) or only `w` (i.e., `A` is fixed to identity) would be very informative. This would help quantify how much of the performance gain comes from transforming the space versus re-weighting the words.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a simple, well-executed, and effective idea. It elegantly combines concepts from optimal transport and metric learning to create a supervised document distance metric with state-of-the-art performance. The experimental results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed method. While there are opportunities to improve the mathematical rigor and better contextualize the work, these are addressable points. The core contribution is significant and sound. I recommend acceptance.
