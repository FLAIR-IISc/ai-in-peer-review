This paper proposes a supervised extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD), a recently introduced and powerful document distance metric. The core idea is to learn task-specific transformations that improve the performance of WMD for k-Nearest Neighbor (kNN) classification. WMD operates by finding the minimum cost to "transport" the word distribution of one document to another, where the cost is defined by distances between word embeddings (e.g., word2vec). The proposed method, Supervised WMD (S-WMD), learns two components via a supervised objective: (1) a linear transformation matrix `A` applied to the word embedding space, which adapts the underlying word-level geometry, and (2) a word importance vector `w` that reweights the word frequencies in each document's bag-of-words representation.

The learning framework is inspired by Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), minimizing a differentiable surrogate for the leave-one-out kNN classification error. A key technical contribution is an efficient gradient computation method that approximates the otherwise intractable nested optimization problem within WMD by using the entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance. This work builds directly upon WMD (Kusner et al., 2015) and draws its optimization objective from the metric learning literature (NCA, Goldberger et al., 2005).

**Strengths:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the background of WMD and NCA, motivate the problem of adding supervision, and detail their proposed method and approximations. The structure is logical, guiding the reader from the unsupervised baseline to the proposed supervised extension and its efficient implementation.

*   **Technical Quality & Experimental Rigor:** The proposed method is technically sound. The use of the Sinkhorn distance to enable efficient gradient computation is a clever and necessary approximation to make the approach practical. The experimental evaluation is a major strength. The authors test their method on eight diverse datasets and compare it against an extensive set of 26 competitive baselines, including various document representations (TF-IDF, LSI, LDA, mSDA) combined with standard metric learning algorithms (NCA, LMNN, ITML). The consistent outperformance of S-WMD across most datasets provides strong evidence for its effectiveness.

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem: adapting powerful, unsupervised document distance metrics for specific tasks. WMD is a strong baseline, and this work provides a principled and effective way to inject supervised information into it. Learning a transformation in the low-dimensional word embedding space is an elegant solution that avoids the scalability issues associated with applying metric learning directly to high-dimensional document representations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Motivation and Justification for Word Reweighting (`w`):** The motivation for learning the word importance vector `w` is weak and its implications are not sufficiently discussed. The goal is to emphasize task-relevant words, but the mechanism—reweighting the document's normalized bag-of-words vector—could fundamentally alter a document's representation into something that no longer reflects its original content. For example, if `w` assigns very low weights to key nouns and high weights to stopwords, the resulting document distribution could become semantically meaningless. The paper needs to provide a stronger justification or analysis showing that this reweighting improves class separation *without* destroying the document's semantics.

*   **Practicality of Applying `w` to Unseen Documents:** A critical practical detail is missing: how is the learned weight vector `w` applied to a new test document? Since `w` is learned over the training vocabulary, it is unclear how to handle words in a test document that were not seen during training. Furthermore, the normalization of the reweighted document vector (`d̃a = (w ◦ da) / (w>da)`) seems to depend on the specific words present in `da`. This needs to be clarified to ensure the method is well-defined for out-of-sample data.

*   **Originality/Contribution:** While the results are strong, the novelty of the technical contribution is somewhat limited. The core components are a combination of existing ideas: applying a linear transformation for metric learning and using an NCA objective are standard practices. The main novelty lies in applying these ideas to the WMD framework and deriving an efficient gradient approximation. This is a valuable engineering contribution but may not be seen as a fundamental algorithmic advance.

*   **Ambiguous Terminology:** The abstract claims the method learns transformations to make distances "match the semantic meaning of similarity encoded in the labels." This phrasing is imprecise. The labels in the experiments (e.g., newsgroup topic, author, sentiment) represent task-specific categories, not "semantic meaning" in a general linguistic sense. Using a more accurate term like "task-specific similarity" or "class-based dissimilarity" would better reflect what the model is actually learning.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically solid and the claims are well-supported by extensive experiments.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is very well-written and organized.
*   **Originality:** Moderate. The work is a novel and effective combination of existing techniques (WMD, NCA, metric learning) rather than a completely new paradigm.
*   **Significance:** High. It provides a state-of-the-art method for supervised document distance learning and is likely to be used by practitioners and built upon by researchers.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that presents a well-executed and effective method for supervised document classification. The clarity and extensive experimental validation are commendable. However, the work would be significantly strengthened by addressing the conceptual and practical questions surrounding the word reweighting vector `w`. I would not object to this paper's acceptance, provided the authors can satisfactorily address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the justification and out-of-sample application of the word reweighting and the clarification of the "semantic meaning" claim.
