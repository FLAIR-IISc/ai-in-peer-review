This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of WMD that learns (i) a linear transform A on the word embedding space and (ii) a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms before computing the transport. Supervision is injected via a Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA)-style stochastic leave-one-out objective defined on WMD distances. Since differentiating through exact WMD is computationally prohibitive, the authors use entropic regularization (Sinkhorn) to obtain a smooth transport plan and corresponding dual variables, enabling efficient approximate gradients with O(q^2) cost per pair (q = unique words per doc). Training uses an NCA-initialized projection learned on word centroids (S-WCD), mini-batches, and neighbor filtering based on WCD. Across eight text classification benchmarks and 26 baselines (unsupervised and supervised over various representations), S-WMD attains the best average rank and the lowest error on 5/8 datasets. Code is promised.

Relation to prior work: The approach builds on WMD/EMD (Kusner et al.; Rubner et al.; Wasserstein distance), entropic OT (Cuturi), and metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML). It is closest to supervised ground-metric learning for EMD in vision (e.g., Wang & Guibas; Cuturi & Avis) but differs by (a) operating in word-embedding space with low dimensionality, (b) learning both a ground metric (A) and feature weights (w) jointly for text, and (c) integrating Sinkhorn-based gradients into an NCA loss over document-level OT. The evaluation is broader than previous WMD work and includes strong supervised baselines.

Quality: The technical development is generally sound. The use of entropic OT to obtain unique transport and dual variables for gradient computation is appropriate, and the initialization via S-WCD is well motivated given WCD’s lower-bound property. The training-time complexity and practical accelerations are clearly discussed, and reported wall-clock times suggest feasibility. The empirical study is extensive, with careful hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian optimization and comparisons against both distances (WCD/WMD) and representations (BOW, TF-IDF, BM25, LSI, LDA, mSDA) with and without supervised metric learning.

However, several clarifications would strengthen the paper:
- Intuition and role of w in Eq. (5): Please give a clearer explanation for the renormalized reweighting d̃ = (w ◦ d)/(w^T d), the constraints/regularization on w (nonnegativity? sparsity?), and an ablation disentangling the contributions of A vs w (A=I, w=1; A learned only; w learned only; both).
- Use of Eq. (2) and λ: The paper uses entropic regularization (Eq. 2) to obtain T*λ and the dual variables via u, v. Please confirm that the same λ controls both the primal regularization and the dual approximations for α*, β*, and detail how λ is selected (fixed to 10 or tuned per dataset). A brief sensitivity analysis would help.
- Gradient expressions: The formulas for α*, β* from u, v include terms like “log(u)>1/p 1.” Please correct notation and dimensions (define 1 as the appropriate all-ones vector; clarify the centering term).
- Claim about “forgiving when syntactically different but semantically similar”: While this is a known benefit of WMD, it would be useful to substantiate it for S-WMD (e.g., robustness to synonym substitutions, paraphrase experiments, or nearest-neighbor qualitative examples showing A and w adapting semantics beyond raw embeddings).
- Table/reporting clarity: The text references “average error across datasets”; I could not find this value—perhaps you intended average rank, which appears in Table 2? Please add a clear aggregate summary (mean error and rank) with standard errors. In Table 2, define the “n” vs “ne” columns. Also, specify how k for k-NN is chosen (per dataset via validation? fixed across datasets? per method?) and whether it is included in BO.
- Training details: Specify early stopping/convergence criteria; whether neighbor set size N and batch size B were tuned; whether WCD neighbor filtering introduces bias (and sensitivity of results to N); handling of OOV words, preprocessing, and which word2vec model/dimension was used.

Clarity: The paper is readable and well organized, but notation could be tightened. “p” is used both for norms and for dimensions; unify symbols and define all shapes. Clearly state constraints on w and A (e.g., A unconstrained vs low-rank, any regularization). Fix minor typographical issues in the dual formulas and algorithm pseudocode. Explicitly distinguish exact WMD from its entropic approximation whenever gradients are used.

Originality: The combination of supervised metric learning directly inside WMD via a learned ground metric and word weights, trained with an NCA objective and efficient Sinkhorn-based gradients, is novel in the NLP/OT context. The histogram reweighting w is a simple but effective addition not commonly explored in prior supervised EMD works.

Significance: The empirical gains over strong baselines and the practical efficiency make this a useful contribution to supervised document similarity. The approach should be of interest for classification, retrieval, and clustering where distances are central. The release of code further enhances impact.

Pros:
- Clear, principled extension of WMD to the supervised setting with efficient training.
- Strong, broad empirical results across 8 datasets and 26 baselines.
- Sound use of entropic OT for tractable, differentiable approximations; practical accelerations.
- Sensible initialization via S-WCD; good training-time profile.

Cons:
- Lack of ablations disentangling the specific gains from A vs w and from initialization vs training.
- Notation/λ usage could be clearer; dual formulas need careful presentation.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to λ, k, neighbor set size, and choice of ground cost (L1 vs L2).
- The claim about semantic robustness would benefit from targeted experiments.

Questions for authors:
- Please provide ablation studies (A only, w only, both), and report their impact on accuracy and training time.
- How are constraints/regularizers on w enforced? Are weights constrained to be nonnegative? Any sparsity penalty?
- Confirm whether the same λ is used in Eq. (2) and in deriving α*, β*, and how λ is chosen; add a sensitivity plot.
- Define “n” vs “ne” in Table 2; add aggregate mean error and rank across datasets.
- How is k chosen for k-NN? Is it per dataset and tuned on validation? Is it method-dependent?
- Can you report sensitivity to N (neighbor filtering) and show that results are stable?
- Did you try alternative ground costs (L1, cosine) or adding a translation term (affine transform) to A?
- Please clarify preprocessing (tokenization, stopwords, stemming), word2vec source/dimension, and OOV handling.

Overall, this is a strong paper that, with the above clarifications and ablations, would make a solid NIPS contribution. I appreciate the authors’ helpful rebuttal clarifications and the promise to release code.
