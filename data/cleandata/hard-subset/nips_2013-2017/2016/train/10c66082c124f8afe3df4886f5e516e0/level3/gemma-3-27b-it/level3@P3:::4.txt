This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised document distance metric on top of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a reweighted word distribution to better align document distances with labeled data, minimizing k-NN classification error. The authors leverage the Sinkhorn distance to address computational challenges associated with the optimal transport problem inherent in WMD. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the method, providing a thorough review of relevant literature in metric learning, word embeddings, and optimal transport. Extensive experimental results on eight datasets and comparison to 26 baselines demonstrate consistent performance improvements.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The clarity of the presentation is noteworthy, making a complex topic accessible. The literature review is comprehensive, situating the work effectively within the broader context of document distance metrics and metric learning. The extensive experimental evaluation is a significant strength, providing convincing evidence of the method’s effectiveness across diverse datasets. The motivation for exploring supervised learning on top of WMD is sound – unsupervised metrics may not optimally capture task-specific notions of similarity. The introduction of a fast approximation based on the Sinkhorn distance is a practical contribution, addressing a key computational bottleneck.

However, the paper also has notable weaknesses. The justification for reweighting the word distribution (w) is weak. While the authors state the goal is to emphasize useful words, it's unclear *why* this reweighting improves performance without fundamentally altering the semantic meaning of the document. Re-weighting can easily change a document's representation significantly, potentially leading to misclassification if not carefully controlled. A stronger theoretical or empirical argument is needed to demonstrate that the reweighting maintains document semantics while improving label separation. The contribution feels somewhat incremental. Applying a linear transformation (a common technique in metric learning) and reweighting within an NCA objective function, while effectively implemented, doesn’t represent a substantial conceptual leap. The novelty lies primarily in applying these techniques specifically to the WMD framework.

A practical concern arises regarding the computation of the reweight vector *w* for completely new, unseen documents. The training process relies on the labeled training set to learn *w*. How is this vector generalized to documents outside of this set? Is a separate estimation procedure required, and if so, how does it impact performance? The use of the term “semantic difference” in the abstract is ambiguous. In the context of the experiments, this appears to refer to differences in label, topic, or authorship, rather than a more fundamental alteration of document meaning. This imprecise language detracts from the clarity of the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results consistently outperform numerous baselines.
*   Clear and well-written presentation with a comprehensive literature review.
*   Practical approximation using the Sinkhorn distance addresses a computational challenge.
*   Addresses a relevant problem: incorporating supervision into a powerful unsupervised metric.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Weak justification for the word reweighting and its impact on document semantics.
*   Incremental contribution – application of established techniques to a new domain.
*   Practical concern regarding generalization of the reweight vector to unseen documents.
*   Ambiguous terminology ("semantic difference").



Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field. However, addressing the concerns regarding the justification for reweighting, the practical generalization issue, and clarifying the terminology would significantly strengthen the work. I do not object to acceptance, provided these points are adequately addressed in a revision.
