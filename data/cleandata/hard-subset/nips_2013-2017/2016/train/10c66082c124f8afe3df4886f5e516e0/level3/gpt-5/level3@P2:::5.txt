Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) (Kusner et al., ICML’15) for document comparison. WMD measures document distances as a Wasserstein/optimal transport cost over pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec). S-WMD incorporates supervision by (i) learning a linear transformation A of the embedding space (a ground metric) and (ii) learning a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The parameters are trained to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification loss in the spirit of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA), where the distance between documents is computed via optimal transport under the transformed ground cost and reweighted histograms.

Because computing exact WMD is costly and non-differentiable in practice, the paper adopts the entropy-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) relaxation to obtain fast, unique transport plans and dual potentials, which are used to derive efficient approximate gradients with respect to A and w. The training is further accelerated via (a) initialization by learning A with NCA on Word Centroid Distance (WCD) representations (S-WCD), and (b) neighbor filtering using WCD to limit the pairs contributing to the NCA probabilities.

Empirically, S-WMD is evaluated on eight datasets against 26 baselines including BOW/TF-IDF/Okapi, LSI, LDA, mSDA, WCD, WMD, and the same features with metric learning (NCA/LMNN/ITML). S-WMD achieves the best average rank and wins on 5/8 datasets; code is released. This work is close to prior EMD/OT metric learning (e.g., Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al. on supervised EMD for vision), but adapts these ideas to text with word embeddings and histogram reweighting.

Quality
- Soundness: The objective and use of NCA are standard and appropriate. The Sinkhorn relaxation is a reasonable and widely used surrogate for WMD, and the use of dual variables from Sinkhorn scaling to approximate gradients is technically sensible. The derivations are sketched clearly.
- Approximations: The method hinges on the entropy regularization parameter λ. The paper fixes λ=10 with minimal discussion. Because λ controls both statistical behavior (bias toward diffuse transports) and computational behavior, this choice can materially change the learned metric. A deeper analysis (or at least an ablation) of λ’s impact on performance, stability, convergence, and on the gap between relaxed and exact WMD is necessary. It is also unclear whether evaluation uses exact WMD with learned A,w or the Sinkhorn distance with the same λ (and whether results change across λ). This affects interpretability: with small λ, the method behaves quite differently from Wasserstein.
- Implementation details: The initialization via S-WCD is helpful but the paper places substantial emphasis on WCD throughout (for neighbor filtering and initialization), which can obscure the core contribution. Please clarify when WCD is merely an engineering device and disentangle its impact via ablations (e.g., random init vs S-WCD init; with/without WCD neighbor filtering).
- Empirics: The evaluation is broad and thorough, with strong average performance. However, the novelty is incremental relative to prior OT metric learning. A few additional analyses would strengthen the claims: (i) sensitivity to λ; (ii) effect of learned w vs fixing w=1; (iii) whether using exact WMD at test time changes accuracy; (iv) sensitivity to k in kNN and to the number of neighbors N used for gradient estimation; (v) comparison of p=1 vs squared L2 ground costs.

Clarity
- The paper is very clearly written, with solid mathematical exposition and helpful algorithmic details.
- The discussion of λ is insufficient. Please explain the limiting behaviors (λ→∞ approximates unregularized Wasserstein; λ small yields smoothed, entropic transport), expected impact on sparsity of T, gradient variance, and classification behavior. Provide guidance for selecting λ beyond “λ=10 worked.”
- The frequent references to WCD (for bounds, initialization, and neighbor pruning) are confusing in places and sometimes feel tangential to the core S-WMD method. Consider moving some WCD material to the supplement and clearly separating what is used only for speed/initialization versus what defines the final metric.
- Minor: specify constraints/regularization on A (e.g., Frobenius penalty or low-rank r) and w (nonnegativity? sparsity?), as this affects stability and interpretability.

Originality
- The idea of learning a ground metric for EMD/OT and using entropy-regularized OT for efficiency has precedents (Cuturi; Cuturi & Avis; Wang & Guibas; Wang et al.). The paper’s main novelty is adapting these ideas to document distances built on word embeddings and jointly learning histogram weights w. This is a meaningful but incremental contribution.

Significance
- The empirical gains over strong baselines across many datasets suggest practical utility, especially for kNN-style retrieval/classification with limited labeled data. The approach is likely to be adopted due to its simplicity and code release.
- The lack of analysis on λ and the reliance on approximations somewhat limit the generality and interpretability of the conclusions. Still, the empirical performance is strong and the method is efficient.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear formulation; principled use of Sinkhorn for efficient training.
- Strong, broad empirical results; best average rank among 26 baselines.
- Joint learning of ground metric and word-importance weights; interpretable weights.
- Good writing and released code.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to prior OT/EMD metric learning.
- Insufficient discussion and analysis of λ, which can dominate behavior.
- Overreliance on WCD for initialization/filtering; confounds contributions.
- Unclear whether final evaluation uses exact WMD or Sinkhorn; no study of the approximation gap.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an ablation on λ: performance vs λ, convergence speed, sparsity of T, approximation gap to exact WMD on a subset.
- Clarify test-time distance: exact WMD vs Sinkhorn with the learned A,w; report both.
- Ablate the role of w (learned vs fixed), initialization (S-WCD vs random), and neighbor filtering (with/without WCD).
- Discuss and possibly regularize A and w; report sensitivity to rank r and any penalties.
- Consider experiments with p=1 vs squared L2 ground costs.
- Streamline the WCD narrative to avoid confusion.

Overall recommendation
- Recommendation: Weak Accept
- Confidence: Medium-high
- Rationale: While the conceptual advance over prior OT metric learning is moderate, the paper is technically sound, clearly written, and demonstrates strong and consistent empirical benefits on a relevant NIPS topic. Addressing the λ analysis and clarifying the role of WCD would make the contribution more compelling.
