This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), which learns a supervised ground cost for WMD by jointly optimizing a linear projection of word embeddings (A) and document-specific word re-weighting (w), trained via a stochastic LOO kNN objective. To make training tractable, the authors replace exact EMD with entropy-regularized OT and derive efficient gradients using Sinkhorn scaling, leading to O(q^2) per-pair costs and strong results on eight kNN text classification tasks. The work builds on WMD (Kusner et al., ICML’15), Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS’13), and kNN metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML), situating itself at the intersection of optimal transport and metric learning.

Quality: The method is technically sound, with careful derivation of approximate gradients for A via the relaxed transport plan and for w via the relaxed dual variables. Empirical evaluation is broad and generally favorable. However, the gains over the initialization (S-WMD init. via NCA on word centroids) are modest in several datasets, suggesting limited incremental benefit of the full optimization. Missing baselines (see below) and an ablation on A vs. w hinder causal attribution.

Clarity: The paper is very well written and pleasant to read. The optimization section (lines 156–175) is clear and likely the main technical contribution. Minor clarifications: specify which prior work “The authors” refers to (line 152); in Eq. 11, α and β appear to require stars to denote relaxed duals; explicitly state near line 170 that the gradients use the Sinkhorn scaling introduced earlier.

Originality: Learning a linear ground metric within EMD for text and coupling it with learned word weights is interesting, but the conceptual novelty lies mostly in the efficient optimization and integration with NCA-style objectives. Related work on learning EMD ground metrics (e.g., Wang & Guibas, ECCV’11) should be discussed more directly in the text domain.

Significance: Results are strong for kNN classification and the approach is practical and reusable. That said, the closeness of S-WMD to its WCD-based initialization tempers impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Useful, well-engineered extension of WMD with solid empirical gains and released code.
- Clear optimization that may interest OT/NLP practitioners.
- Broad evaluation across datasets and baselines.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing baselines: apply ITML and LMNN to word-centroid embeddings (and compare to S-WCD/NCA).
- Missing ablations: isolate effect of A vs. w (A=I, w=1, and each alone).
- Limited incremental gains over initialization on some datasets; analyze when/why full S-WMD helps.

Overall: A solid, well-presented paper with practical value; recommend acceptance contingent on adding the missing baselines/ablations and minor clarifications.
