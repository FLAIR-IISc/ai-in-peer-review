- Proposes S-WMD: a supervised WMD that learns a linear transform A and a global word-importance vector w, trained with an NCA-style loss and Sinkhorn OT for efficiency, yielding consistent gains over baselines.
- Situates work at the intersection of WMD/Wasserstein text distances and supervised metric learning, adapting prior EMD metric-learning ideas to word-embedding-based text.
- Strength: clear, well-structured paper with motivated derivations and readable math.
- Strength: learning in low-dimensional embedding space avoids typical text metric-learning curse of dimensionality.
- Strength: pragmatic engineering — Sinkhorn relaxation gives efficient differentiable updates and sensible initialization via S-WCD.
- Strength: extensive empirical evaluation on eight datasets against many baselines, with hyperparameter tuning, runtimes, and visualizations.
- Strength: code released, aiding reproducibility.
- Weakness: semantics and motivation of the global histogram reweighting w are underexplained and may alter document meaning rather than just emphasize discriminative features.
- Weakness: unclear test-time procedure for applying w to unseen documents and handling OOV words or domain shift; needs explicit statement if w is global.
- Weakness: limited novelty — combination of linear ground-metric learning and supervised reweighting on OT is straightforward and incremental over prior EMD metric-learning.
- Weakness: ambiguous wording such as “semantic difference” which conflates label/task similarity with general semantic similarity.
- Weakness: missing ablations isolating the contributions of A vs w, and sensitivity analysis for Sinkhorn parameter λ and number of iterations.
- Quality: generally sound derivations for Sinkhorn-gradient backpropagation; some claims (e.g., semantic preservation under reweighting) need stronger justification.
- Clarity: overall clear, but needs explicit clarification on whether w is global or per-document, inference on new documents, and constraints/regularization on w.
- Originality: moderate — method coherently integrates known components but is not a radical conceptual leap.
- Significance: practically useful as a supervised upgrade to WMD with good empirical gains; conceptual impact is modest.
- Question 1: Is w a single global vocabulary vector — if so, explain test-time application and OOV handling; if not, describe inference for per-document/class weights.
- Question 2: What constraints/regularizers are applied to w to prevent semantic drift, and can you report the distribution of learned weights and divergence pre/post reweighting?
- Question 3: Provide ablations: only A, only w, both; sensitivity to λ and Sinkhorn iterations; effect of WCD neighbor filtering (N) and batch size (B).
- Question 4: Can you compare Sinkhorn-gradient training to exact WMD gradients on small problems to validate approximation?
- Question 5: Clarify and align the term “semantic difference” with the supervised objective (e.g., “task- or label-informed similarity”).
- Suggestion: add theoretical/empirical safeguards (e.g., regularization toward IDF or w=1, KL divergence reports, qualitative examples) to show reweighting preserves meaning.
- Suggestion: formalize inference for new documents, including OOV and domain-shift handling.
- Suggestion: include the recommended ablations and sensitivity analyses, consider per-class/group weights or IDF priors, and reframe the abstract to avoid ambiguous phrasing.
- Pros for acceptance: strong empirical gains, clean efficient method, good engineering and code availability, broad applicability.
- Cons for acceptance: limited novelty, insufficient analysis/justification of reweighting, and minor clarity gaps on test-time use and terminology.
- Recommendation: leaning weak accept contingent on clarifying and justifying the reweighting design and test-time inference; acceptance acceptable if authors address reviewer questions.
