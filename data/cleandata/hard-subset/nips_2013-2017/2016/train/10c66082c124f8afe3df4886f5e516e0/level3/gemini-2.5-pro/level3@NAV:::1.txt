This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that incorporates supervision into the document distance metric. Building upon the work of Kusner et al. (2015), the authors propose learning a linear projection of the underlying word embedding space and a vector of word-importance weights. This is framed as an optimization problem that minimizes the stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification error, drawing inspiration from Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA). A key technical contribution is an efficient gradient computation method that uses an entropy-regularized, or Sinkhorn, version of the optimal transport problem to make the nested optimization tractable.

The paper's strengths are clear. It presents an interesting and useful extension of WMD, a powerful unsupervised text distance metric. The proposed S-WMD method is shown to be effective, achieving strong results on a variety of kNN text classification benchmarks. The paper itself is exceptionally well-written, clear, and pleasant to read.

However, several concerns limit the paper's overall impact. The most significant weakness is that the full, complex optimization of S-WMD provides only marginal gains over its simpler initialization step (S-WCD), as shown in Table 3. This initialization, which applies NCA to word-centroid document representations, achieves a large portion of the performance improvement. This raises questions about the practical significance of the more computationally intensive S-WMD formulation.

This issue is amplified by missing experimental comparisons. The authors do not evaluate the straightforward baseline of applying existing metric learning methods (like ITML or LMNN) directly to the word-centroid embeddings. It is plausible that this simpler approach would perform comparably to S-WMD. Additionally, the paper lacks an ablation study to isolate the respective contributions of the learned projection matrix A and the word-specific weights w, making it difficult to understand the source of the improvements.

While the optimization approach is novel, the core idea of learning a linear metric within the Earth Mover’s Distance framework is an interesting but somewhat limited contribution.
