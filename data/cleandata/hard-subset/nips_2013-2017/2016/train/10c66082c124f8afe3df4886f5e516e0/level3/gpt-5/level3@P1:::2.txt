This paper proposes a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) for document comparison. The method, Supervised WMD (S-WMD), learns (i) an affine transformation of the word embedding space and (ii) a word-importance weighting vector, with the goal of aligning the distance to label-based semantic similarity. Training minimizes a stochastic leave-one-out (LOO) kNN objective, à la NCA, but uses an entropy-regularized optimal transport (Sinkhorn) relaxation to obtain efficient and differentiable approximations of both the transport plan and dual variables, which are used to compute gradients. The approach is initialized by learning a supervised metric over the Word Centroid Distance (S-WCD), then refined with batch updates using approximate neighborhoods filtered by the fast WCD lower bound. Experiments on 8 text classification datasets against 26 strong baselines show consistent improvements, with S-WMD obtaining the best average rank and the best accuracy on most datasets. Visualizations and learned word weights corroborate the interpretability and task-adaptivity of the learned metric.

Relation to prior work: WMD (Kusner et al., ICML) lifted word embeddings (Mikolov et al.) to document distances via Earth Mover’s/Wasserstein distance. This paper builds on that by introducing supervision into the ground metric and histogram weights. It leverages the Sinkhorn relaxation (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) for efficiency and NCA (Goldberger et al., NIPS) for the stochastic LOO objective. Compared to supervised EMD/ground-metric learning in vision (e.g., Wang & Guibas; Cuturi & Avis), this work addresses text, learns both a linear ground metric and word reweighting, and exploits pre-trained embeddings for a low-dimensional, scalable setup.

Quality:
- Methodological soundness: The objective, approximations, and gradients are clearly derived. Using Sinkhorn to obtain a unique transport plan and dual potentials for gradient computation is well-motivated and avoids nested non-differentiable optimization. The neighborhood filtering by WCD and the S-WCD initialization are sensible and helpful in practice.
- Empirical support: The evaluation is extensive (8 datasets, 26 baselines including BOW/TF-IDF/LSI/LDA/mSDA, WCD, WMD, and supervised metric learning atop representations), with consistent improvements and analysis (visualizations, timing).
- Limitations: The approach relies on an entropy-regularized approximation; while λ=10 is a reasonable choice, a more systematic sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims. The ground cost is squared Euclidean; alternative costs (cosine, L2 without squaring, p=1) could be considered. Some datasets with long documents show smaller gains; discussing when histogram reweighting vs. ground metric learning drives improvements would be useful. Regularization on A and w (e.g., to avoid degenerate scaling) is not discussed in depth.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient algorithmic detail (pseudo-code, complexity, implementation choices such as batch size B and neighbor set size N). The gradient derivations and the connection to dual variables via Sinkhorn are explained. Minor suggestions:
  - Explicitly state constraints/regularization on w (non-negativity, normalization) and A (full vs. low-rank r, any weight decay).
  - Summarize sensitivity to λ, B, N, and k in kNN in the main text.
  - Clarify whether p=2 is always used for the transport ground cost and discuss the effect of p.

Originality:
- While supervised metric learning and supervised EMD have precedents, learning a supervised ground metric and word weights for WMD in text, with efficient Sinkhorn-based gradients and an NCA objective, is a novel and timely combination. The S-WCD initialization is a practical contribution.

Significance:
- The improvements are substantial across diverse datasets, and the approach is likely to be useful in kNN classification, retrieval, and clustering scenarios where distances matter. Code availability increases impact. The learned word weights offer interpretability. This work meaningfully advances supervised optimal transport for NLP.

Pros:
- Clear, principled formulation that marries NCA with Sinkhorn OT to supervise WMD.
- Strong, extensive empirical evaluation with many baselines; consistent and often large gains.
- Efficient training via approximations, batching, and neighbor filtering; practical runtimes.
- Interpretability via learned word weights and improved class separation in visualizations.
- Released code; method likely to be adopted.

Cons:
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to the Sinkhorn regularization λ and the choice of ground cost p.
- Ablations on the separate contributions of A vs. w could be expanded.
- Comparisons to neural text classifiers from the same era (e.g., CNNs/fastText) are not reported; while the task is distance-based kNN, such baselines would contextualize absolute accuracy.
- Potential overfitting risks for w and full A are not discussed; details on regularization would help.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Provide ablations: only-A, only-w, and S-WCD vs. full S-WMD across datasets; report how often each component drives gains.
- Include sensitivity plots for λ, neighbor set size N, batch size B, and the dimension r of A; discuss runtime-accuracy trade-offs.
- Explore alternative ground costs (cosine/L2, p=1 transport) and report whether squared L2 is consistently best.
- Clarify constraints on w (non-negativity) and any regularization on A,w; report how you prevent trivial scaling solutions.
- Add results with different embeddings (e.g., GloVe) to show robustness.
- Report performance for varying k in kNN and for retrieval tasks to highlight broader utility.

Recommendation: Accept. This is a solid, well-executed paper that introduces a practical and effective supervised extension to WMD, grounded in sound optimization and demonstrated with a thorough empirical study. It fits well within NIPS topics (optimal transport, metric learning, NLP) and is likely to influence subsequent work on supervised OT for text and beyond.
