**Summary**

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method to learn a task-specific document distance metric by extending the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear transformation of the word embedding space and a vector of word-importance weights. This is achieved by minimizing a stochastic leave-one-out k-nearest neighbor classification loss, following the Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) framework. A key technical contribution is the use of the entropically regularized Wasserstein distance (Sinkhorn distance) as a differentiable and computationally efficient proxy for the WMD. This avoids a prohibitively expensive nested optimization problem when computing gradients. The authors conduct a large-scale empirical study on eight text classification datasets, comparing S-WMD against 26 baselines, and report state-of-the-art results, achieving the best average performance across all datasets.

**Strengths and Weaknesses (Qualitative Evaluation)**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the gradients for the learned transformation and word weights using the Sinkhorn approximation is correct and clearly presented. The claims of superior performance are very well-supported by extensive experiments on a wide range of datasets and a comprehensive set of competitive baselines. The empirical methodology, including hyperparameter tuning via Bayesian optimization, appears rigorous. However, the work's completeness is undermined by a poor discussion of the critical hyperparameter λ (the entropic regularizer), whose impact on the model's behavior is significant but left unanalyzed.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical exposition is clear, detailed, and easy to follow, from the background on WMD and Sinkhorn distance to the derivation of the S-WMD loss and its gradients. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the experimental setup is well-described. This high level of clarity is a major strength of the manuscript.

**Originality:** The originality of the paper is somewhat limited, as it primarily combines several existing, powerful techniques: WMD, NCA-style metric learning, and the Sinkhorn distance approximation. The main novel contribution lies in the specific formulation that jointly learns a linear transformation and word-importance weights within the WMD framework and the practical demonstration of its effectiveness. While not a fundamental breakthrough, this is a novel and clever synthesis of ideas that successfully addresses the important problem of adding supervision to WMD.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and impactful. WMD is a powerful but unsupervised baseline for document similarity. This paper provides an effective and relatively efficient method to adapt it for supervised classification tasks, demonstrably advancing the state of the art in kNN-based text classification. Given the strong empirical results, other researchers and practitioners are likely to use and build upon S-WMD.

**Detailed Comments**

The paper presents a compelling method with impressive empirical results. The strengths are numerous, particularly the clarity of the presentation and the thoroughness of the evaluation. However, there are several key weaknesses that should be addressed.

1.  **The Role and Choice of the Regularization Parameter λ:** This is the most significant weakness of the paper. The Sinkhorn distance is used as a proxy for the true Wasserstein distance, and the parameter `λ` controls the strength of the entropic regularization. A large `λ` yields a distance closer to the true WMD, while a small `λ` results in a smoother, but different, distance that is much faster to compute. The paper dedicates only one sentence to this crucial choice: "In our experiments, we use λ = 10, which leads to a nice trade-off between speed and approximation accuracy." This is insufficient.
    *   The value of `λ` can have a dominant effect on the objective function in eq(2). It directly scales the ground metric term `||A(xi - xj)||^2`, meaning its value dramatically impacts the behavior of the distance and the resulting gradients.
    *   It is unclear whether the method, with `λ=10`, is learning a metric for the WMD (the large `λ` regime) or for the Sinkhorn distance (the small `λ` regime). These are different objectives. Most users, motivated by the paper's claims of efficiency, will likely use a small `λ`, but the paper does not characterize the behavior of the learned metric in this regime.
    *   A proper scientific contribution requires an analysis of this parameter. An ablation study showing how performance, training time, and the learned parameters (A, w) change with `λ` would have made the paper much stronger and more complete.

2.  **Confusing References to Word Centroid Distance (WCD):** The paper uses the WCD for two purposes: initializing the matrix `A` ("S-WCD") and finding an approximate nearest neighbor set for minibatch training. While these are reasonable heuristics for speeding up the algorithm, the paper's description is potentially confusing. It introduces WCD as a lower bound on WMD and a fast approximation but does not clearly motivate why learning a metric for this simpler distance provides a good initialization for the much more complex S-WMD objective. A clearer explanation of the relationship and the motivation for this specific initialization strategy would be beneficial.

3.  **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of methods is effective, the paper builds heavily on prior work. The contribution is more of an engineering/integrative success than a fundamental algorithmic advance. This is not necessarily a reason for rejection, as the resulting system is powerful and useful, but it is an important aspect to consider when evaluating the paper's overall contribution.

In summary, this is a very well-executed paper presenting a high-performing system for document classification. The clarity and empirical results are top-notch. The primary weaknesses are the lack of analysis of the crucial `λ` hyperparameter and the limited conceptual novelty.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a practical and effective supervised extension to the popular Word Mover's Distance.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on 8 datasets against 26 baselines in a rigorous evaluation.
*   The paper is extremely well-written, with clear mathematical exposition.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The crucial role of the entropic regularization parameter `λ` is almost entirely ignored, making the results difficult to interpret and reproduce reliably.
*   The novelty is limited, as the work is a combination of existing techniques (WMD, NCA, Sinkhorn).
*   Minor confusion regarding the role and justification of the Word Centroid Distance in the algorithm.
