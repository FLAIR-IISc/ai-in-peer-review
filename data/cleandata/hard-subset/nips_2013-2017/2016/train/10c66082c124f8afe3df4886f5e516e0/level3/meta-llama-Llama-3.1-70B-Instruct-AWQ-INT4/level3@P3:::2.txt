The paper "Supervised Word Mover's Distance" proposes an extension to the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) metric, which learns weights on the distance in a supervised manner. This work builds upon the original WMD paper by Kusner et al., which introduced a new document metric that leverages word embeddings to compute distances between documents.

The authors' main contribution is the development of a method to incorporate supervision into the WMD metric, which they call Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD). The S-WMD metric learns a linear transformation of the word representations and a word-importance weight vector to make the WMD distances match the semantic meaning of similarity encoded in the labels. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms a wide range of baselines, including unsupervised WMD and other document representation/distance methods, on eight real-world text classification tasks.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method, including the optimization procedure and the computation of the gradients. The experimental evaluation is extensive, covering several classification datasets and a wide range of baselines and features. The results show that S-WMD consistently outperforms all baselines by a large margin, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to bridge the gap between unsupervised word embeddings and supervised document classification. By learning a supervised metric, the authors are able to capture the semantic meaning of similarity encoded in the labels, which is not possible with unsupervised methods. The use of word-importance weights also allows the method to emphasize the usefulness of certain words for distinguishing the document class.

The paper is also well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of why their method is necessary.
