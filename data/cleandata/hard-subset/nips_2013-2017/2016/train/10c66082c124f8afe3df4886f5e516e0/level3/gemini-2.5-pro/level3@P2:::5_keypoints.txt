- Proposes S-WMD: a supervised extension of Word Mover’s Distance that learns a linear transform of embeddings and per-word importance weights.  
- Uses an NCA-style kNN leave-one-out classification loss to train the task-specific document metric.  
- Key technical contribution: substitutes WMD with entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distance to obtain a differentiable, computationally feasible objective.  
- Derives gradients for both the linear transformation and word weights and enables gradient-based optimization.  
- Empirical evaluation across eight datasets compares to 26 baselines and reports state-of-the-art average kNN performance.  
- Paper is technically sound; motivation for using Sinkhorn as a differentiable proxy is well-founded and derivations appear correct.  
- Writing and organization are excellent; background, method exposition, and figures effectively clarify the approach.  
- Visualizations (Figures 1–2) help illustrate learned metric effects and word-weight behavior.  
- Major weakness: no justification or sensitivity analysis for the entropic regularization hyperparameter λ (authors use λ=10 without evidence).  
- The behavior of the method can change dramatically with λ (closer to WMD for large λ vs. dense/smoother transport for small λ); analysis is needed.  
- Minor confusion: the role and motivation for initializing/filtering with Word Centroid Distance (WCD) feels disconnected and needs clearer justification.  
- Originality is incremental—logical synthesis of existing components (WMD, Sinkhorn, NCA), and related work on learning ground metrics in other domains should be discussed more.  
- Significance is high: demonstrates clear empirical gains and provides a strong, practical baseline likely to be adopted.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses important problem, computationally feasible solution, clear presentation, thorough empirical validation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: superficial treatment of λ and somewhat incremental novelty.  
- Main requested experiment: ablation/sensitivity study on λ showing its effect on training time and final classification error to clarify whether gains rely on near-WMD behavior or entropic regularization.
