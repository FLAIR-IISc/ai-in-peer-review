This paper introduces Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a document distance metric by building upon the recent Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) framework. The core idea is to learn both a linear transformation of the word embedding space (A) and a word importance weighting vector (w) to improve performance on supervised tasks.  The optimization minimizes leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error, utilizing a computationally efficient approximation to the WMD gradient based on the Sinkhorn distance. The authors demonstrate strong performance across eight real-world text classification tasks, consistently outperforming a comprehensive set of 26 baselines.

The work builds upon a rich history of research in document distance metrics, starting with bag-of-words and TF-IDF representations, evolving through dimensionality reduction techniques like LSI and LDA, and culminating in the recent success of WMD which leverages the power of word embeddings.  The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of metric learning, acknowledging related methods such as NCA, LMNN, and ITML, while highlighting the advantages of operating directly in the low-dimensional word embedding space.  The connection to the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) and the introduction of the regularized transport problem (Cuturi’s work) are also well presented, demonstrating a clear understanding of the underlying theory.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The writing is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making the complex concepts accessible. The authors provide a thorough literature review, properly situating their work within the existing body of knowledge and acknowledging related contributions.  The experimental evaluation is extensive and convincing.  The comparisons to 26 baselines on 8 different datasets provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of S-WMD. The inclusion of a discussion of training time is also valuable, demonstrating the practical efficiency of the proposed approach. The code availability is a significant plus, allowing for easy reproducibility and further research.

However, there are also several weaknesses that need to be addressed.  The motivation for introducing the word importance weighting vector (w) is relatively weak. The authors claim it emphasizes “the usefulness of certain words for distinguishing the document class,” but this explanation lacks depth.  Furthermore, reweighting the word frequencies can fundamentally alter the underlying document representation, and it's not immediately clear how to guarantee that this modification preserves the core semantics of the document while enhancing label separation.  The authors need to provide a more rigorous justification for this reweighting step and demonstrate that it doesn’t simply distort the document’s meaning.  How does this differ from simply removing irrelevant words?

The core contribution of applying a linear transform and reweighting with an NCA-like objective feels somewhat incremental. While the application to the WMD framework is novel, the underlying approach draws heavily from existing metric learning techniques.  The authors should more clearly articulate the specific advantages of this combination and highlight what makes it truly distinct from prior work.

A practical concern arises regarding the computation of the reweighting vector (w) for entirely new, unseen documents. The training process requires labeled data to learn w. How is this vector generalized to documents outside of the training set?  The authors should address this issue, perhaps by proposing a mechanism for estimating w based on the document’s word composition and the learned embedding space.

Finally, the use of the phrase “semantic difference” in the abstract is ambiguous. The authors appear to be equating semantic difference with differences in labels or topic assignments, which is a narrower interpretation of semantics. Clarifying this terminology would improve the precision of the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results consistently outperform a large number of competitive baselines.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of a complex method.
*   Thorough literature review and proper context within the field.
*   Practical efficiency demonstrated through training time analysis.
*   Code availability facilitates reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak justification for the word importance weighting vector (w).
*   Incremental contribution - applying existing techniques to a new framework.
*   Practical concern regarding generalization to unseen documents.
*   Ambiguous terminology ("semantic difference").



Overall, this is a solid paper with a clear presentation and compelling results. While the contribution isn't groundbreaking, the empirical performance is strong, and the method appears promising. I would not object to acceptance if the authors adequately address the concerns regarding the motivation and justification for the word reweighting, the generalization to unseen documents, and the clarity of the terminology. A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach would also be beneficial.
