

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), a method to learn a metric that improves upon the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) by parameterizing the cost matrix with a Mahalanobis distance and learning histogram reweighting coefficients. The authors build on NCA-type objectives to efficiently optimize the parameterized Earth Mover's Distance, providing practical gradient updates that avoid the cubic complexity of exact WMD computation.

The paper makes a strong contribution to text classification by combining existing techniques in a simple yet effective manner. The experimental section is particularly compelling, with thorough evaluations across eight real-world datasets and 26 competitive baselines, consistently demonstrating S-WMD's superiority. The authors provide clear empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness, with detailed analysis of results and visualizations of learned weights.

The main strengths lie in the methodological clarity of the approach and the robust experimental validation. The paper effectively shows how supervised learning can enhance WMD without requiring document-level representation learning, maintaining the low-dimensional advantage of WMD.

However, several improvements would strengthen the paper. First, there's a notational inconsistency: Equation 1 uses Euclidean distance while Equation 6 uses squared Euclidean (2-Wasserstein), which should be clarified by consistently describing WMD as p-Wasserstein. Second, the paper should better address the interplay between regularized (using Sinkhorn) and unregularized OT formulations, which is crucial for understanding the approximation quality. Third, the reweighting in Equation 5 is not novel—Guy Lebanon and Le/Cuturi's work on histogram reweighting should be properly cited. Fourth, the paper would benefit from deeper analysis of the empirical importance of the learned weight vector w, beyond just showing it improves performance. Finally, the related work section should more clearly distinguish the authors' approach from Cuturi/Avis (entropy regularization, continuous vs discrete cost) and Wang et al. (limitations of their linear parameterization).

Overall, this is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The simple idea combines existing techniques effectively, and the strong empirical results support its value. With minor revisions addressing the notational issues, related work clarity, and deeper analysis of the weight vector, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
