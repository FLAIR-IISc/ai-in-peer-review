Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a supervised extension of the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD) that learns a domain-specific ground metric and per-word importance weights for document comparison. Concretely, the method learns (i) a linear transformation A of the word embedding space and (ii) a nonnegative word-importance vector w that reweights document histograms. The parameters are trained to minimize a stochastic leave-one-out kNN classification loss (NCA-style softmax over neighbors) where distances are computed via optimal transport between transformed embeddings and reweighted histograms.

Directly differentiating WMD would entail nested, potentially nondifferentiable linear programs. The paper circumvents this by using the Sinkhorn-regularized transport (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) to obtain efficient O(q^2) approximations of both primal transport plans and dual potentials. These enable practical gradients with respect to A and w. Training uses: (1) an initialization learned via NCA on word centroids (S-WCD), (2) minibatch optimization, and (3) neighbor pruning using fast centroid distances. Complexity is analyzed and code is released.

This work sits at the intersection of document similarity (WMD; Kusner et al., ICML 2015), metric learning (NCA; LMNN; ITML), and entropy-regularized optimal transport (Cuturi, NIPS 2013). Related vision work on learning EMD ground metrics is acknowledged, but the joint learning of a word-space transform and word weights for text is novel. I am pleased to see discriminative training applied to WMD to learn a domain-specific metric.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is solid. The NCA-style objective is appropriate for kNN, and the use of Sinkhorn to obtain unique transport plans and approximate dual potentials for gradients is well motivated. Gradients with respect to A and w are derived clearly, including the role of the reweighted, renormalized histograms. While the gradients correspond to the entropy-regularized OT rather than the exact WMD, the authors discuss the λ trade-off and report good empirical behavior.
- Empirical support: Extensive experiments on eight classification datasets with 26 baselines (unsupervised representations, distances, and their combinations with LMNN/ITML/NCA) demonstrate that S-WMD achieves the best performance on most datasets and is highly competitive on the rest, often by a large margin. Training times are reasonable; initialization via S-WCD is effective; visualizations corroborate the improved class clustering. The evaluation protocol (kNN) aligns with the training objective, and hyperparameters are tuned via Bayesian optimization.
- Caveats: The method is nonconvex and relies on good initialization; while S-WCD helps, sensitivity analyses (λ, neighbor set size N, batch size) are limited in the main text. The theoretical link between optimizing the Sinkhorn-regularized surrogate and improvements in the unregularized WMD is not formalized. The study focuses on kNN classification; comparisons to strong end-to-end neural classifiers (e.g., fastText, CNN/RNN contemporaneous to 2016) are not included. An ablation to quantify the separate contributions of A vs. w would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The description of WMD, Sinkhorn, the NCA objective, and the resulting gradients is concise yet detailed enough for reproduction. Pseudocode and complexity analysis are helpful. Minor suggestions:
- Provide a short sensitivity plot for λ and neighbor pruning parameters.
- Clarify constraints on w (nonnegativity) and any regularization.
- Expand ablation reporting (A-only, w-only, and both).
- Collect a few scattered typos/formatting issues.

Originality
The key novelty lies in learning a supervised, task-specific ground metric and per-word weights for WMD in text, leveraging entropy-regularized OT to enable efficient training under an NCA objective. While metric learning and OT regularization are known, their combination in this way for document distances is new and well executed. Related work on supervised EMD in vision is properly cited, and the differences are articulated.

Significance
The contribution is practically important. WMD is widely used, but unsupervised; S-WMD provides a principled, efficient way to inject supervision and yields large accuracy gains across diverse datasets and features. The approach is likely to be adopted for supervised retrieval and classification tasks, and the released code increases impact. The paper advances the state of the art for metric-based document comparison and will interest both the OT and NLP communities.

Pros
- Clear, principled supervised extension of WMD via learned A and w.
- Sound use of Sinkhorn OT for efficient, differentiable training.
- Very extensive empirical evaluation with many strong baselines.
- Consistent accuracy improvements, often substantial; code released.
- Good practical considerations: initialization, batching, neighbor pruning, complexity analysis.

Cons
- Limited theoretical guarantees connecting the surrogate to exact WMD optimization.
- Nonconvex training with some sensitivity; hyperparameter/λ analyses could be expanded.
- Evaluation centered on kNN; no comparison to strong neural classifiers of the era.
- Missing ablation on the relative gains from A vs. w.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to λ and the neighbor set size N? Any guidance for new datasets?
- What is the impact of learning only A vs. only w vs. both?
- Have you tried alternative ground costs (e.g., cosine or Mahalanobis with p=1 vs. p=2)?
- How does S-WMD perform as a retrieval metric beyond kNN classification?
- Any observations about learned w across domains (e.g., interpretability, sparsity)?

Recommendation
Accept. This is a solid, well-executed contribution that extends a popular document distance with discriminative training, is technically sound and clearly explained, and demonstrates strong, consistent gains across many datasets and baselines. It is well suited for presentation at NIPS.
