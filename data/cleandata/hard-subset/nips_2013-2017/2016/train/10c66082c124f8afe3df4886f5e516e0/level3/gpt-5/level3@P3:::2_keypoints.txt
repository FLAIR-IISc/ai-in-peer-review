- Proposes S-WMD: a supervised extension of WMD that learns a linear embedding transform A and nonnegative per-word importance weights w for document comparison under a kNN/NCA objective.  
- Uses Sinkhorn-regularized optimal transport to obtain differentiable, efficient O(q^2) approximations of transport plans and dual potentials for gradient-based training.  
- Trains parameters with a stochastic leave-one-out kNN (NCA-style) softmax loss, with practical tricks: S-WCD initialization, minibatching, and neighbor pruning via centroids.  
- Technical formulation is sound; gradients w.r.t. A and w are derived clearly and justified despite using the entropy-regularized surrogate.  
- Extensive empirical evaluation on eight datasets against 26 baselines shows S-WMD achieves the best or highly competitive performance in most cases.  
- Training time and implementation details are practical; code is released and visualizations support improved class clustering.  
- Main caveats: nonconvex optimization relying on initialization, limited sensitivity analyses (λ, neighbor size, batch size), and no formal link between Sinkhorn surrogate improvements and exact WMD.  
- Evaluation focuses on kNN classification and omits comparisons to strong end-to-end neural text classifiers contemporaneous to 2016.  
- Ablation analysis is missing: the separate contributions of learning A versus w are not quantified.  
- Paper is generally clear, well organized, reproducible, with helpful pseudocode and complexity analysis; minor typos/formatting and clarity on w constraints/regularization suggested.  
- Originality: novel combination of supervised metric learning and entropy-regularized OT for learned ground metrics and word weights in text, distinct from supervised EMD work in vision.  
- Significance: practically important extension of WMD that yields large accuracy gains and is likely useful for supervised retrieval and classification.  
- Pros summarized: principled supervised extension, effective use of Sinkhorn OT, very extensive experiments, consistent accuracy gains, and released code.  
- Cons summarized: limited theoretical guarantees, sensitivity/nonconvexity concerns, kNN-centric evaluation, and missing A vs. w ablation.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to λ and neighbor set N; impact of A-only vs w-only vs both; experiments with alternative ground costs; retrieval performance beyond kNN; interpretability/sparsity of learned w.  
- Recommendation: Accept — solid, well-executed contribution with strong empirical results and community relevance.
