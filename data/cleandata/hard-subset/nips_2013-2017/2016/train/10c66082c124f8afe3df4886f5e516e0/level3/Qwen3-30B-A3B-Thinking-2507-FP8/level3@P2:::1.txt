

# Review of "Supervised Word Mover's Distance"

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the Word Mover's Distance (WMD) metric that incorporates supervision through learning a linear transformation of the underlying word embedding space and word importance weights. The authors build upon Kusner et al.'s WMD, which computes document distances as optimal transport costs between embedded words. S-WMD improves upon WMD by learning a metric that better aligns with document class labels, using a stochastic leave-one-out nearest neighbor classification error as the objective. The key technical contribution is an efficient approximation of the gradient computation through Sinkhorn distance, avoiding the cubic complexity of exact WMD optimization.

The paper makes a clear connection to prior work in metric learning (NCA, LMNN, ITML) and optimal transport (EMD, Sinkhorn distance), positioning S-WMD as a natural extension that leverages the strengths of both domains. It extends the WMD framework to incorporate supervision, which is a significant gap in the literature as prior document distances are entirely unsupervised.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical soundness and evaluation:** The paper presents a well-motivated approach with thorough experimental evaluation across 8 text classification datasets. S-WMD consistently outperforms 26 baselines, including both unsupervised document representations and supervised metric learning methods applied to those representations. The results are convincing, with strong improvements on multiple datasets.

* **Clarity and writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The mathematical formulations are precise, the problem statement is well-motivated, and the paper is pleasant to read. The authors effectively explain the connections between WMD, metric learning, and their proposed solution.

* **Practical impact:** The method provides a meaningful improvement to an already strong baseline (WMD) with practical computational benefits. The use of Sinkhorn distance enables efficient training, and the paper demonstrates that S-WMD can be trained in reasonable time compared to baseline metric learning approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited incremental contribution:** The most significant concern is that the initialization method (S-WCD, which uses the word centroid distance with NCA) performs nearly as well as the full S-WMD method, as shown in Table 2 and Table 3. This suggests that the additional learning of the word importance weights and linear transformation provides minimal improvement over the initialization. The paper lacks ablation studies to isolate the contributions of the word weights versus the projection matrix, making it difficult to assess the value of each component.

* **Missing baseline:** The paper fails to evaluate word-centroid document embeddings (which the authors use for initialization) with existing metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA), which would provide a more meaningful comparison point. This omission makes it difficult to assess whether the improvement is due to the S-WMD framework or simply the combination of word centroids with standard metric learning.

* **Originality:** While the optimization approach (using Sinkhorn distance) is novel, the core idea of learning a metric within the WMD framework is not highly original. The paper builds upon existing metric learning techniques and applies them to a specific document distance, which is a reasonable but not particularly groundbreaking extension.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper presents a well-executed extension of the WMD framework with strong empirical results
- The writing is excellent and clearly communicates the method
- The approach provides a practical and efficient way to incorporate supervision into WMD
- The method achieves state-of-the-art results on multiple text classification tasks

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The marginal improvement over the initialization method (S-WCD) significantly reduces the perceived impact of the work
- The lack of ablation studies makes it difficult to determine which components of the method contribute most to the performance gains
- The missing baseline comparison (word centroids + metric learning) undermines the significance claim
- The core contribution is incremental rather than transformative

**Overall assessment:**

This is a solid technical contribution that extends the WMD framework with a supervised metric learning approach. While the method is well-executed and produces good results, the limited incremental gain over the initialization (S-WCD) and the lack of ablation studies reduce the paper's significance. The paper would be strengthened by including the missing baseline comparison and providing a more detailed analysis of the contributions of the word weights versus the projection matrix.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but the marginal improvement over the initialization method makes it difficult to justify the additional complexity. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the missing baseline and ablation studies, as these would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution. As it stands, the paper is a good but not exceptional contribution to the field. The work is clearly relevant to NIPS, as it advances the state of the art in document representation and metric learning.
