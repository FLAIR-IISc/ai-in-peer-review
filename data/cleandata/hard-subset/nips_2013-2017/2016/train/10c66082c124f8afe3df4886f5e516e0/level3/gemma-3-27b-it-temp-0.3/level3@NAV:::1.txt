## Review of “Supervised Word Mover’s Distance”

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover’s Distance (S-WMD), a method for learning a supervised metric built upon the Word Mover’s Distance (WMD). The core idea is to learn a linear projection of the word embedding space *and* document-specific word re-weighting, minimizing kNN classification error. The authors address the computational challenges of incorporating supervision into WMD by leveraging a relaxed optimal transport problem (Sinkhorn distance) and a probabilistic filtering approach. The paper is well-written and clearly presents the method, its theoretical basis, and experimental results on eight text classification datasets, demonstrating strong performance against a comprehensive set of baselines.

The work builds on the recent success of WMD by extending it to a supervised setting, a natural and valuable direction. The proposed optimization approach, particularly the use of the Sinkhorn distance for efficient gradient computation (lines 156-175), is a significant contribution. The empirical results are compelling, consistently showing S-WMD outperforming many baselines, including those employing metric learning. The ablation study, while not isolating the contributions of the projection matrix and word weights, does demonstrate the effectiveness of the overall approach. 

However, the impact of the full S-WMD learning process is somewhat diminished by the observation in Table 3 that initializing the metric learning via a “word centroid distance” (S-WCD) yields performance comparable to the full S-WMD learning. This raises the question of whether the added complexity of learning the full S-WMD metric provides a substantial benefit over a simpler initialization strategy. A key missing baseline is the evaluation of word-centroid document embeddings *with* existing metric learning methods (ITML, LMNN, NCA). This would help establish whether the gains are due to the WMD framework itself or the specific learning procedure proposed. Furthermore, a more detailed ablation study is needed to disentangle the contributions of the linear projection (A) and the word-specific weights (w). 

The originality primarily lies in the optimization technique. While linear metric learning within the EMD framework is interesting, it’s a relatively limited contribution given the existing body of work on metric learning. A few minor clarifications are needed: the paper should specify which paper “The authors” refers to on line 152, and Equation 11 likely requires stars on α and β to denote their optimality. It should also explicitly state around line 170 that the method utilizes the Sinkhorn scaling referenced earlier.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results on multiple datasets.
*   Well-written and clearly explained method.
*   Novel and efficient optimization approach.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in text classification.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited impact due to comparable performance with S-WCD initialization.
*   Missing baseline: word-centroid embeddings with standard metric learning.
*   Lack of ablation study to isolate contributions of A and w.
*   Incremental originality.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a valuable contribution. Addressing the missing baselines and providing a more detailed ablation study would significantly strengthen the work. I lean towards acceptance, contingent on minor revisions addressing these points.
