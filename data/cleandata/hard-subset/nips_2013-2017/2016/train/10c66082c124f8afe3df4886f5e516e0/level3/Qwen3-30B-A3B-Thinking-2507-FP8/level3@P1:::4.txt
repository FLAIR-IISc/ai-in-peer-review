

This paper proposes Supervised Word Mover's Distance (S-WMD), an extension of the unsupervised Word Mover's Distance (WMD) that incorporates label information to learn a document distance metric. The authors learn a linear transformation matrix A and a word importance weight vector w to optimize the leave-one-out kNN classification error under the WMD framework. The approach leverages the Sinkhorn distance approximation to make the optimization tractable, and evaluates on eight text classification tasks.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, outperforming 26 competitive baselines across multiple datasets. The experimental section is thorough, with detailed analysis on eight different document corpora, including news categorization, sentiment analysis, and product identification tasks. The writing is clear and well-organized, with a comprehensive literature review that properly contextualizes the work within metric learning and document distance research.

The primary strengths of the paper include the clear exposition of the method, the extensive empirical validation, and the practical implementation details. The authors effectively demonstrate that supervised learning on top of WMD provides consistent improvements over unsupervised baselines. The technical approach is sound, with appropriate use of the Sinkhorn approximation to handle the computational complexity of the optimal transport problem.

However, several concerns limit the paper's contribution. The motivation for the word importance weight vector w is weakly justified. The authors claim that certain words are more important for distinguishing document classes, but they fail to provide evidence that this reweighting preserves semantic meaning while improving label separation. Crucially, reweighting could effectively transform a document into a different one, potentially altering its semantic content. The paper needs to address this fundamental concern by demonstrating that the learned weights correspond to meaningful linguistic features rather than arbitrary document modifications.

The novelty of the approach is questionable. Learning a linear transformation for document distances is a standard technique in metric learning, and the authors' use of Neighborhood Components Analysis (NCA) with the WMD as the base distance does not constitute a significant advance. The contribution appears more like an application of existing metric learning techniques to a novel distance metric rather than a fundamental methodological innovation. The authors should more clearly articulate how their approach differs from Wang et al. (2015) who also learn a metric within a generalized Euclidean EMD framework.

The paper also contains a terminology issue: the abstract claims to capture "semantic difference" but the method actually optimizes for label separation (topic, authorship, etc.). This is misleading, as semantic difference refers to the meaningful relationships between words and concepts, not document classification labels. The authors should clarify this distinction throughout the paper.

A significant practical concern is how to compute the word importance weights for unseen documents. The paper describes a training process but does not explain how to handle new documents that might have words not present in the training set or with different contextual importance. This limitation affects the method's applicability in real-world scenarios where documents are continuously added.

Regarding quality, the technical approach is sound, but the paper lacks theoretical justification for the reweighting mechanism. The experimental results are convincing, but the paper would be stronger with ablation studies showing the individual contribution of the linear transformation versus the word reweighting.

The clarity is excellent, with well-structured exposition and good figures. The significance is somewhat limited by the incremental nature of the contribution, though the consistent performance improvements across diverse datasets suggest practical utility.

In summary, the paper makes a solid empirical contribution that demonstrates the benefits of incorporating supervision into the WMD framework. However, the methodological novelty is limited, the motivation for key components is weak, and the terminology is imprecise. The paper would be significantly improved by addressing the concerns about word reweighting, clarifying the relationship to prior work on metric learning, and resolving the terminology issues.

Pro: Strong empirical results, clear writing, thorough experiments, practical implementation details.
Con: Limited novelty, weak justification for word reweighting, terminology issues, unresolved practical concerns about unseen documents.

The paper is well-written and demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines, but the contribution does not advance the state-of-the-art in a sufficiently novel way. The authors should address the concerns about word reweighting and terminology before the paper can be considered for acceptance. With these clarifications, the paper would be a strong candidate for NIPS, but as written, it lacks the necessary theoretical depth and novelty to warrant acceptance.
